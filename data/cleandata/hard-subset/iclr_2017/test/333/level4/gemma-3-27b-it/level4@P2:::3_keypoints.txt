- Core finding: Single-layer filter banks with random weights can synthesize natural textures effectively, sometimes outperforming VGG-19-based methods.  
- Insight: Shallow, simple architectures can challenge the assumption that deep, pre-trained networks are necessary for texture generation.  
- Optimization issue: Optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 objective is difficult; shallow-network outputs can yield lower VGG-19 loss.  
- Empirical support: Supplementary results show initializing VGG-19 optimization with shallow-network outputs improves VGG-based results.  
- Experimental breadth: Thorough exploration of single-layer variants (random, hand-crafted, unsupervised filters) is provided.  
- Evaluation metric: The authors introduce a VGG-based quantitative texture quality measure, offering partial objectivity.  
- Perceptual vs. variability tradeoff: Discussion highlights interplay between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture synthesis.  
- Limitations: Objective evaluation remains difficult and the VGG-based loss may not fully capture perceptual quality, especially for regular textures.  
- Suggested improvement: Explore alternative quantitative metrics beyond VGG loss and discuss entropy/diversity measures more concretely.  
- Open question: Clarify why random filters work—due to many feature maps or specific properties of randomness.  
- Theory connection: The link to the maximum entropy framework is noted and could be further emphasized to explain constraints balancing similarity and variability.  
- Request for clarity: Provide sensitivity analysis of optimization hyperparameters (e.g., L-BFGS-B learning rate, iterations) and whether they were systematically tuned.  
- Request for alternatives: Identify other perceptual metrics that could complement VGG-based evaluation for regular textures.  
- Empirical examples: Supply specific texture examples where VGG loss diverges from visual perception and include detailed visual analysis.  
- Extension query: Report any preliminary results using shallow multi-layer (2–3 layer) architectures to assess gains in efficiency or quality.
