This work proposes a compelling and surprisingly strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that textures of high perceptual quality can be generated using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, often rivaling the performance of state-of-the-art methods relying on deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG. This challenges the prevailing assumption that depth and learned features are indispensable for natural image generation, specifically in the context of texture synthesis.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core finding – that surprisingly simple networks can achieve competitive texture synthesis results – is a significant and potentially impactful contribution to the field. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by thorough experimentation.

The paper effectively addresses the question of which components of existing texture synthesis approaches are truly necessary. The authors convincingly demonstrate that neither the hierarchical multi-layer representation nor supervised training of features are essential, offering a minimalist approach that performs remarkably well. The extensive comparison against a wide range of single-layer architectures, along with the introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG reconstruction loss, strengthens the validity of the claims. The discussion relating their work to the MaxEnt framework and the importance of perceptual distance metrics provides valuable theoretical context.

Several avenues for future work could further strengthen this research. It would be insightful to explore the limitations of the approach on more complex textures or image types. A deeper investigation into the interplay between perceptual similarity and variability, perhaps through more rigorous psychophysical experiments, would be valuable. Examining the computational efficiency trade-offs between the proposed multi-scale approach and hierarchical networks is also worthwhile.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify certain aspects of the work:

1.  The paper mentions that samples generated by both the VGG-based model and the single-layer models are “not perfect.” Could you elaborate on the specific artifacts or limitations observed in the generated textures, and how these differ between the two approaches?
2.  You note that the VGG-based model appears to perform better in the inpainting task presented in Figure 7. Could you discuss potential reasons for this observation, and whether it suggests limitations of the proposed approach in capturing long-range dependencies?
3.  While the multi-scale approach is shown to be effective, could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with using numerous filters of varying sizes compared to a similarly performing deep hierarchical network? What are the memory requirements of the multi-scale model, particularly with a large number of filters?
4.  The optimization procedure seems crucial to the success of the single-layer models. Could you discuss the sensitivity of the results to the scaling factor (10^7) applied to the loss and gradients, and whether a systematic optimization of this parameter was performed?
