This work proposes a simple yet strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis. In empirical experiments, samples generated by the baseline – composed of multi-scale and random filters – sometimes rival the VGG-based model, which employs multi-layer and pre-trained filters. The authors conclude that texture synthesis does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps.

This is an interesting and insightful contribution. However, the conclusions could benefit from further investigation, particularly regarding the role of deep hierarchical representations. Firstly, the quality of all generated samples, from both the VGG-based and single-layer models, appears limited and notably worse than results achievable with non-parametric methods. Furthermore, the VGG-based model seems to perform better in the inpainting task shown in Figure 7. Finally, it would be valuable to know if a hierarchical model (rather than numerous filters of differing sizes) would handle multi-scale analysis more efficiently.

**1. Summary**

The paper tackles the question of what is essential for generating natural textures. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained convolutional neural networks are crucial for this task. They demonstrate that surprisingly good texture synthesis can be achieved using a single convolutional layer with random filters, even rivaling the performance of more complex models like the VGG network. The key contributions include a minimal baseline for texture synthesis, a comprehensive comparison of different single-layer architectures, a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based model, and a discussion of generalizations of maximum entropy models to account for texture variability.

**2. Decision**

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The authors present a counter-intuitive and well-supported argument that challenges current thinking in texture synthesis. The results are compelling and the experimental setup is thorough.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper clearly identifies a significant question in the field: what components of deep learning architectures are truly necessary for natural texture generation? The authors systematically investigate this question by stripping down the complexity of existing methods and demonstrating that a simple, single-layer network with random filters can achieve surprisingly good results. 

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with comparisons against a wide range of single-layer architectures and the state-of-the-art VGG-based model. The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric, based on the VGG-based loss, is a valuable contribution, as it moves beyond subjective human inspection.  The fact that the proposed method often matches or surpasses the performance of more complex models is a strong indication of its effectiveness.  The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of the theoretical implications of their findings, linking them to maximum entropy models.

**4. Additional Feedback**

*   **Computational Efficiency:** The authors mention the computational inefficiency of their multi-scale model. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the computational cost of the different architectures in terms of training and synthesis time.
*   **Filter Initialization:** While the authors explore random filters, it could be interesting to briefly discuss the impact of different random filter initializations (e.g., different distributions, orthogonal initialization).
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The optimization procedure seems crucial to the success of the method. A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, regularization) would be helpful.
*   **Visual Examples:** While many visual examples are provided, including a more diverse set of textures could further strengthen the argument.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   You mention that the VGG-based loss is not perfect and can sometimes fail to capture perceptual advantages. Could you elaborate on the types of textures for which this is particularly true, and why you believe the VGG-based loss struggles in these cases?
*   You state that deeper hierarchical models do not improve texture quality compared to the single-layer model. Were these deeper models trained from scratch, or were they initialized with pre-trained weights? If the latter, did you fine-tune the weights specifically for texture synthesis?
*   In the discussion section, you mention the possibility of extending the MaxEnt framework to allow for variability in the summary statistics. Could you provide a high-level overview of how this extension might be implemented, and what potential benefits it could offer?
*   You mention a "finite size effect" related to the constraints in the MaxEnt framework. Could you elaborate on how this effect might be mitigated in practice?
