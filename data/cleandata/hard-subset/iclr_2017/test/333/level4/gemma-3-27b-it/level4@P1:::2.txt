This paper provides an interesting analysis of the conditions required for generating natural-looking textures. The results are quite surprising, and the analysis is thorough. 

I recommend accepting this paper. My primary reasons are the novelty of the findings – demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated with surprisingly simple networks – and the rigor with which the authors explore the design space of single-layer architectures.

The authors tackle the question of what is essential for natural image generation, specifically for natural texture synthesis. They challenge the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are indispensable, and convincingly demonstrate that high-quality textures can be generated from networks with a single layer, no pooling, and random filters. This is a significant finding, as it suggests that the core mechanisms for texture generation may be simpler than previously thought. The extensive experimentation with various single-layer architectures – Fourier bases, K-means filters, PCA filters, and random filters – and their comparison to state-of-the-art methods, strengthens their claim.  The introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure using the VGG-based model is also a valuable contribution, moving beyond purely qualitative human evaluation.

While the paper is strong, some aspects could be clarified. The discussion of the relationship between the VGG-based loss and perceptual quality is intriguing, particularly the observation that the VGG loss sometimes *favors* images less similar to the reference texture. It would be helpful to elaborate on this point further. Additionally, the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model is acknowledged, but a more detailed discussion of potential trade-offs between efficiency and quality would be beneficial.  

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide more intuition behind why random filters can perform so well, and what properties of the random filters might be crucial for this success?
*   You mention a discrepancy between your results and earlier reports (Gatys et al., 2015a) regarding the performance of networks with random weights. Beyond the more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure, could there be other factors at play, such as differences in implementation or hardware?
*   Regarding the VGG-based loss, could you provide specific examples of textures where the VGG loss and human perception diverge significantly, and speculate on the reasons for this divergence?
*   You briefly mention the potential for extending the MaxEnt framework to account for variability in summary statistics. Could you elaborate on how this could be implemented, and what the potential benefits would be?
