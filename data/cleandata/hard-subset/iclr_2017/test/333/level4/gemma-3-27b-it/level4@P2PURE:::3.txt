This work builds on the finding by Gatys et al. that correlation statistics – specifically, empirical Gram matrices – of deep feature responses effectively characterize visual textures. The paper investigates the performance of various network architectures, both deep and shallow, within this framework. A key result is the surprising effectiveness of very shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights; for simple, regular textures, these can even produce visually superior results compared to those generated using complex, data-adapted filters like those in VGG-19. More generally, the paper offers a valuable and insightful discussion of the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

As demonstrated in Figure 4, optimizing images based on shallow filter banks can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss than direct optimization against the VGG-19 objective. This is plausibly attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. The authors further support this with new supplementary material showing that initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from the shallow network optimization improves performance – a useful addition to the original experimental findings. 

The paper’s primary limitation lies in the lack of a systematic comparison against quantifiable objectives. While it is straightforward to define image statistics that would perfectly reproduce a reference texture, achieving high visual quality this way would be trivial and rely on shallow statistics. The core challenge, however, is to capture a broader texture distribution, and a robust metric for evaluating success in this regard remains an open problem. Consequently, although the empirical results suggest that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), definitive confirmation is difficult to achieve.

The authors propose entropy as a measure of diversity, acknowledging the practical challenges of its implementation. Moreover, entropy alone does not address the issue of visual quality. They also suggest a psychophysical assessment as a potentially practical solution, but consider this to be beyond the scope of the current work.

Overall, given the inherent difficulties in evaluating image generation, I believe the paper presents sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, some form of psychophysical evaluation would be valuable in corroborating the intuitions currently supported only by visual inspection of the figures within the paper and its supplementary material.
