## Review for ICLR 2017 - “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper investigates the necessary components for natural texture synthesis using deep convolutional neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are crucial, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated from networks with a single layer, no pooling, and random filters. This is a surprising and compelling result, achieved through a careful experimental setup and a detailed analysis of different network architectures. The work provides valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms of texture generation and suggests that the complexity of existing approaches may not be strictly necessary.

**Accept** – This paper presents a well-motivated and thoroughly investigated study with significant findings that challenge current understanding in the field of texture synthesis. The results are compelling and the analysis is rigorous.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper directly addresses an important question: what minimal components are required for effective natural texture synthesis? Prior work heavily relies on deep, pre-trained networks (e.g., VGG). The authors convincingly demonstrate that such complexity isn’t fundamental, showcasing surprisingly good performance with significantly simpler architectures. The methodical exploration of different filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) and network configurations is commendable. The introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure—using the VGG-based model’s synthesis loss—is a significant step towards more objective evaluation in this field, typically reliant on subjective human inspection. The authors acknowledge the limitations of this metric and discuss potential improvements, demonstrating a critical awareness of their methodology. The ablation studies, particularly comparing multi-scale models to single-scale counterparts, provide crucial insight into the importance of receptive field size and combining different scales. Furthermore, the discussion around the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis is insightful and highlights directions for future research. The detailed explanation of the optimization procedure, including the scaling factor for the loss, and its impact on results demonstrates a strong understanding of the practical considerations of this task.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Clarity of Notation:** While generally well-written, some of the notation could be clearer. For instance, a more explicit definition of variables in equations (2) and (3) would be helpful.
*   **Visualizations:** While Fig. 1 provides a good overview, including more examples, perhaps with different texture types, would strengthen the visual impact. A side-by-side comparison of the VGG-based synthesis and the best single-layer model results for each texture could be particularly illustrative.
*   **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the inefficiency of the multi-scale model, a more quantitative comparison of the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) between the single-layer and multi-layer models would be valuable.
*   **Further Exploration of Variability:** The discussion about the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. Could the authors briefly speculate on potential methods to explicitly increase variability within their framework, beyond adjusting the optimization stopping criterion?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions the optimization procedure being more elaborate than in previous work. Could the authors elaborate on the specific changes made to the optimization procedure, and how these changes contributed to the observed improvement in performance with random filters?
*   The authors utilize a VGG-based loss function for quantitative evaluation. While acknowledged as imperfect, could they provide further details on why they believe it performs better than alternatives like SSIM (as mentioned in the appendix)? Were other quantitative metrics considered and rejected, and if so, why?
*   Regarding the potential for reconstructing the reference texture in deeper random CNNs, is this behavior dependent on the size of the input image, or other parameters? Could this effect be mitigated with regularization techniques or other modifications to the network architecture?
*   In the discussion section, the authors mention a potential generalization of the MaxEnt framework. Could they briefly sketch out a potential approach to incorporating variability directly into the MaxEnt formulation?
