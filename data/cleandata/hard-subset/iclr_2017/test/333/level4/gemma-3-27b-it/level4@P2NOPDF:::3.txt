This paper investigates the effectiveness of different network architectures—both deep and shallow—within the Gatys et al. framework, which leverages correlation statistics (Gram matrices) of deep feature responses for texture synthesis. A key finding is that surprisingly strong results can be achieved with very shallow networks consisting of a single, randomly initialized filter bank. For simple and regular textures, these shallow networks even outperform more complex, data-adapted filters like those found in VGG-19. The work provides a valuable and insightful discussion of the strengths and limitations of this approach to texture synthesis.

The authors demonstrate, with Figure 4 as illustrative evidence, that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss compared to direct optimization against the VGG-19 objective. This is plausibly attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. Further strengthening this observation, the supplementary material shows that initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from the shallow network optimization leads to improved performance – a useful and insightful extension of the original experiments.

A primary limitation of this work is the absence of a systematic, quantifiable evaluation against competing methods. While it's straightforward to create shallow statistics that perfectly replicate a reference texture, achieving high visual quality in this manner isn't the core challenge. The true goal is to capture the underlying *distribution* of a texture, and objectively measuring how well different methods achieve this remains an open problem. Consequently, while the empirical results support the intuition that simpler statistics are sufficient for both quality and diversity in texture synthesis (compared to more complex statistics), a definitive conclusion is difficult to draw.

The authors consider entropy as a potential metric for diversity, acknowledging its practical challenges. Furthermore, they recognize that entropy alone does not address the crucial aspect of visual quality. They propose a psychophysical assessment as a potential solution, but rightly categorize this as future work. 

Overall, given the inherent difficulties in evaluating image generation techniques, I believe this paper presents sufficient merit for publication at ICLR. However, incorporating some form of psychophysical evaluation – even a limited study – would considerably strengthen the conclusions and provide more robust support for the compelling intuitions derived from visual inspection of the results presented in the paper and supplementary material.
