The framework introduced by Gatys et al. demonstrates that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterisation of visual textures. This paper investigates in detail which network architectures, both deep and shallow, perform well within this framework. A key finding is that surprisingly effective texture synthesis can be achieved with very shallow networks – specifically, a single filter bank with random weights – and that, for simple and regular textures, these networks can produce results visually superior to those obtained using complex, data-adapted filters such as those found in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper presents an interesting and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 illustrates that optimising images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield texture images with a lower VGG-19 loss than direct optimisation of the VGG-19 objective. This is attributed to the difficulty of optimising the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, a reasonable explanation. The authors bolster this point in the new supplementary material by demonstrating that improved optimisation results can be obtained by initialising VGG-19-based optimisation with the results from shallow network optimisation, providing a valuable complement to the original experiments.

The main limitation of this work is the lack of a systematic comparison of methods against a quantifiable objective. While it is straightforward to define image statistics that would allow for the exact replication of a reference texture – achieving high visual quality – such trivial statistics would also be inherently shallow. The goal here is to capture a texture *distribution*, and measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Therefore, while the empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), conclusively confirming this is challenging.

The authors suggest evaluating diversity in terms of entropy, which is reasonable, but as they acknowledge in their responses to questions, difficult to implement practically. Moreover, this would not fully address the other crucial aspect of the problem: visual quality. They also propose a psychophysical assessment as a potential solution, acknowledging it as the most practical approach but deferring it to future work.

Overall, given the inherent difficulty in evaluating image generation, I believe the paper possesses sufficient merit to warrant publication in ICLR. However, some form of psychophysical assessment would be beneficial to validate the intuitions currently supported only by visual inspection of the figures in the paper and supplementary material.
