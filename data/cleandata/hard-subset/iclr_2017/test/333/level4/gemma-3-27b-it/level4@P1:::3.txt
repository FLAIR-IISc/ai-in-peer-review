The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterisation of visual textures. This paper investigates in detail which kinds of deep or shallow networks perform well within this framework. One of the main findings is that very shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, work surprisingly well, and for simple and regular textures may produce results visually superior to those of complex, data-adapted filters such as those in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks may result in texture images that have a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. This is attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the supplementary material, the authors show that better optimization results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the shallow network optimization results, a useful complement to the original experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is straightforward to define image statistics that would allow for the simple generation of an exact copy of any reference texture, achieving very good visual quality. Such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The goal, however, is to capture a texture *distribution*, and measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. Hence, while the empirical results seem to confirm the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), it is difficult to conclusively confirm this.

The authors indicate that diversity could be measured in terms of entropy. This is reasonable, but, as they acknowledge, difficult to do in practice. Furthermore, this would still not account for the other aspect of the problem – namely, visual quality. They also suggest a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way of addressing this problem, but deem that to be material for future work.

Overall, given the inherent difficulty in evaluating image generation, I believe the paper has sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, some form of psychophysical assessment would be useful to confirm the intuitions that, at present, can only be obtained by inspecting the figures in the paper and the supplementary material.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a surprising and well-supported result demonstrating that complex deep networks are not strictly necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The experiments are thorough and the discussion is insightful.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty and Surprise:** The finding that a single-layer network with random filters can achieve competitive results challenges the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are crucial for natural image generation.
*   **Thoroughness:** The authors explore a wide range of single-layer architectures and compare them rigorously against the state-of-the-art.
*   **Justification of Results:** The explanation for the discrepancy with earlier work (better optimization procedure) is plausible and supported by experiments.
*   **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the technical details are clearly explained.

**Additional Feedback (for improving the paper):**

*   **Discussion of Filter Size:** While the paper touches on the importance of multi-scale filters, a more in-depth discussion of the trade-offs associated with different filter sizes could be beneficial.
*   **Clarify "Stationary Structure":** The phrase "stationary structure" is used repeatedly. A more precise definition or explanation of this concept would improve clarity.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational costs associated with the multi-scale model relative to the state-of-the-art, as this may be a practical limitation.
*   **Diversity Metric:** Expand on the difficulties of quantifying texture diversity. Perhaps briefly mention alternative approaches beyond entropy, even if they are also impractical.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The optimization procedure seems critical. Can you provide more details on the sensitivity of the results to the optimization parameters (learning rate, number of iterations, box constraints)?
*   You mention that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages. Could you provide specific examples where the VGG loss is misleading, and perhaps analyze *why* it fails in those cases?
*   Given that the multi-scale model often rivals the VGG-based results, do you see any potential for combining the strengths of both approaches – for example, using a shallow network to initialize the optimization for a deeper network?
