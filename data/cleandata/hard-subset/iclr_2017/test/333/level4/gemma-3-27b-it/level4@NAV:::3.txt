This work builds upon the foundation laid by Gatys et al., which demonstrated the effectiveness of correlation statistics – specifically, empirical Gram matrices of deep features – in characterizing visual textures. The paper thoroughly investigates the performance of various network architectures, both deep and shallow, within this framework. A key finding is the surprisingly strong performance of very shallow networks comprised of a single, randomly-weighted filter bank; for simple and regular textures, these can even surpass the visual quality achieved by complex, data-adapted filters found in networks like VGG-19. More generally, the paper offers a valuable and insightful discussion of the strengths and limitations of this approach to texture synthesis.

The results presented in Figure 4 highlight that optimizing images based on shallow filter banks can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss compared to direct optimization using the VGG-19 objective. The authors attribute this to the challenges inherent in optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function – a compelling and logical explanation. The inclusion of supplementary material demonstrating improved VGG-19 optimization through initialization with results from the shallow network optimization is a positive and relevant addition to the original experimental analysis. 

A primary limitation of the study is the lack of systematic comparison against quantifiable metrics. While it is straightforward to create statistics that perfectly reproduce a reference texture, resulting in high visual quality, such approaches are inherently shallow and don’t address the core challenge of capturing a broader texture *distribution*.  Quantifying success in this regard remains an open research question. Consequently, while the empirical results support the idea that simple statistics are sufficient for both quality and diversity in texture synthesis (when compared to more complex statistics), definitive confirmation remains elusive.

The authors propose entropy as a potential measure of diversity, acknowledging its practical difficulties. They also suggest psychophysical evaluation as a potentially definitive, though currently deferred, avenue for future work. This assessment would address not only diversity, but also the crucial aspect of visual quality, which is currently evaluated subjectively. 

In conclusion, given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation techniques, I believe this paper presents a valuable contribution and warrants publication in ICLR. However, incorporating some form of psychophysical assessment would strengthen the conclusions and provide more robust support for the observed intuitions, which currently rely on visual inspection of the figures presented in the paper and supplementary materials.
