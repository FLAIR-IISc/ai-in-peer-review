- Introduces a simple single-layer convolutional network with random filters as a strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis.  
- Demonstrates high perceptual-quality textures that often rival deep pre-trained methods like VGG.  
- Challenges the assumption that depth and learned features are necessary for natural image texture generation.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Contribution judged significant, well-motivated, clearly presented, and supported by thorough experiments.  
- Shows that neither hierarchical multi-layer representations nor supervised feature training are essential for good texture synthesis.  
- Provides extensive comparisons across single-layer architectures and proposes a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG reconstruction loss.  
- Places results in theoretical context by relating to the MaxEnt framework and emphasizing perceptual distance metrics.  
- Suggests future work to probe limitations on more complex textures or other image types.  
- Recommends psychophysical studies to examine the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.  
- Proposes evaluating computational efficiency trade-offs between the multi-scale approach and hierarchical networks.  
- Asks authors to clarify specific artifacts or limitations in generated textures and how they differ between VGG and single-layer outputs.  
- Requests discussion on why VGG appears better in the inpainting task and whether this implies limitations in capturing long-range dependencies.  
- Seeks a detailed analysis of computational cost and memory requirements of the multi-scale model versus similarly performing deep networks.  
- Asks about sensitivity to the optimization scaling factor (10^7) and whether its selection was systematically optimized.
