This paper investigates the effectiveness of different network architectures—ranging from shallow, randomly initialized filter banks to deep, data-trained networks like VGG-19—within the framework of texture synthesis based on Gram matrices, as originally demonstrated by Gatys et al. A key finding is that surprisingly shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, can perform remarkably well, even surpassing the visual quality achievable with complex, data-adapted filters in certain cases, particularly for simple and regular textures. The work provides a valuable and insightful discussion of the strengths and limitations of this approach to texture synthesis.

The authors demonstrate in Figure 4 that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective. This is plausibly attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, a reasonable and well-articulated explanation. The inclusion of new supplementary material showcasing improved VGG-19 optimization results when initialized with outputs from the shallow network optimization is a particularly useful addition, strengthening the empirical analysis.

The primary limitation of this work lies in the absence of a systematic, quantifiable evaluation against competing methods. While it's straightforward to define image statistics for perfect replication of a reference texture (achievable with shallow methods), the core challenge lies in capturing the underlying *distribution* of a texture. Defining metrics to accurately assess a method's success in this regard remains an open problem. Consequently, while the empirical results support the intuition that simpler statistics are sufficient for achieving both quality and diversity in texture synthesis—potentially even outperforming more complex statistics—drawing definitive conclusions proves challenging. 

The authors acknowledge the potential of entropy as a measure of diversity but recognize its practical difficulties in application. They also propose psychophysical assessment as a potential solution, and arguably the most practical approach, but correctly identify this as future work. This acknowledgement is appropriate, given the scope of the current investigation. 

Overall, evaluating image generation remains a notoriously difficult problem. Despite this inherent challenge, this paper presents a strong contribution with sufficient merit to warrant publication in ICLR. However, incorporating some form of psychophysical evaluation, even on a limited scale, would further bolster the authors’ compelling intuitions, which are currently largely supported by visual inspection of the results presented in the paper and supplementary material. The insights gained regarding the surprisingly effective performance of shallow networks, and the difficulties in optimizing more complex models, are significant contributions to the field.
