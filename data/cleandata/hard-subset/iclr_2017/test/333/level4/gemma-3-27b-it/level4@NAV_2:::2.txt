This paper presents a compelling investigation into the requirements for generating natural-looking textures. The central finding – that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer network with random filters – is quite surprising and challenges prevailing assumptions in the field. The analysis is thorough, exploring a wide range of single-layer architectures and comparing their performance against state-of-the-art methods.

I recommend accepting this paper. The core contribution is significant: demonstrating that depth and pre-training, previously considered essential, are not indispensable for natural texture generation. This insight shifts the focus and opens new avenues for research in this area. Additionally, the quantitative texture quality measure introduced provides a valuable, objective alternative to traditional human evaluation.

The primary support for this decision lies in the robust experimental results. The paper convincingly demonstrates that the proposed baseline model can generate textures competitive with, and in some cases surpassing, those produced by significantly more complex networks. The detailed ablation studies, comparing various filter types and sizes, strengthen the validity of the conclusions. The careful consideration of optimization procedures also appears to be a crucial factor contributing to the success of the simpler models, addressing a discrepancy with prior work.

While the paper is strong, several points could further enhance its impact. The discussion of the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality could be expanded. Further investigation into scenarios where the VGG-loss *fails* to accurately reflect perceptual differences would be valuable. It would be helpful to understand the limitations of the proposed quantitative evaluation metric and how it might be improved. Finally, while the ablation studies are comprehensive, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with the multi-scale model would be useful.

I have a few questions for the authors: Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the scaling factor (10^7) used in the optimization procedure (Section 4)? How does varying this parameter affect the quality of the synthesized textures? Additionally, the paper mentions that deeper, multi-layer random CNNs sometimes exactly reconstruct the reference texture. Could you provide more details on the conditions under which this occurs, and how it relates to the discussion of the MaxEnt framework in Section 7? Finally, regarding the limitations of the VGG-based loss, do you have any preliminary thoughts on alternative perceptual metrics that might better capture the nuances of texture quality, particularly for textures with regular, man-made structures?
