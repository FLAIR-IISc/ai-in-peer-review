This paper investigates the requirements for generating natural textures, building upon the framework introduced by Gatys et al. which demonstrates that correlation statistics (Gram matrices) of deep feature responses effectively characterize visual textures. The core finding is that surprisingly simple, shallow networks – specifically, single-layer filter banks with random weights – can perform remarkably well in this framework, even surpassing more complex, data-adapted filters like those in VGG-19 for certain textures. The paper offers a valuable and insightful discussion of the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

A key observation is that optimizing images with respect to these shallow filter banks can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss compared to directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective itself. The authors attribute this to the challenges of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, a plausible explanation supported by their supplementary material showing improved VGG-19-based optimization when initialized with results from the shallow network approach.

**Decision: Accept.** The paper presents a compelling argument, supported by empirical evidence, that challenges existing assumptions about the necessity of deep, pre-trained networks for natural texture generation. Despite the inherent difficulties in objectively evaluating generative models, the authors provide sufficient justification for their claims.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper directly addresses a relevant and important question within the field of natural image generation. It effectively deconstructs the assumptions underlying state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods, demonstrating that depth and pre-training are not indispensable. The authors provide a thorough exploration of various single-layer architectures, including those with random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned filters. The comparison against the established VGG-based model is well-executed and the observed results are intriguing. Furthermore, the inclusion of a quantitative texture quality measure, based on the VGG-based model, is a step towards more objective evaluation, even if not fully definitive. The discussion regarding the interplay between perceptual similarity and variability is insightful and highlights important considerations for future research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

While the paper acknowledges the difficulty of objective evaluation, strengthening this aspect would be beneficial. Exploring alternative quantitative metrics beyond the VGG-based loss, even if imperfect, could further bolster the claims. The authors mention entropy as a potential measure of diversity, and while acknowledging its practical challenges, a more detailed discussion of these challenges and potential workarounds would be valuable. 

Consider expanding the discussion of why random filters perform so well. Is it simply the large number of feature maps, or is there a specific property of random filters that contributes to their effectiveness in capturing texture statistics?

The connection between the findings and the maximum entropy framework is well-articulated, but could be further emphasized. How do the results inform our understanding of the constraints necessary for achieving a good balance between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the potential for the optimization procedure to influence results. Could you provide more details on the sensitivity of the results to the L-BFGS-B hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, number of iterations)? Were any attempts made to systematically optimize these parameters across different architectures?
2.  You mention that the VGG-based loss may not perfectly capture perceptual quality, particularly for textures with regular structures.  Are there any readily available alternative perceptual metrics (beyond those mentioned) that could be explored to provide a more comprehensive evaluation?
3.  You note a discrepancy between the VGG-based loss and visual perception for certain textures.  Could you provide a few specific examples illustrating this discrepancy, alongside a more detailed visual analysis of the generated textures?
4. The paper investigates single layer architectures. Have you performed any preliminary experiments with shallow multi-layer architectures (e.g. 2-3 layers) to see if they offer any advantages over the single layer models, perhaps in terms of computational efficiency without sacrificing quality?
