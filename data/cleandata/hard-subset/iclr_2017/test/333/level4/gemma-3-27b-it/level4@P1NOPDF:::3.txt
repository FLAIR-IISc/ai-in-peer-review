The work of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics – specifically, empirical Gram matrices – of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper investigates in detail which types of deep or shallow networks perform well within this framework. A key finding is that surprisingly effective texture synthesis can be achieved with very shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights. For simple and regular textures, these shallow networks can even produce results visually superior to those generated using complex, data-adapted filters such as those found in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an interesting and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 illustrates that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield texture images with a lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, a reasonable explanation. The newly added supplementary material demonstrates that improved optimization results can be obtained by initializing VGG-19-based optimization with the results from shallow network optimization, a useful addition to the original experiments.

The main limitation of this paper is the lack of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is straightforward to define image statistics that would allow for the exact reproduction of a reference texture, achieving very good visual quality – and such statistics would necessarily be shallow. However, the goal here is to capture a texture *distribution*, and measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Therefore, while the empirical results appear to support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis, both in terms of quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), definitively confirming this is challenging. 

The authors suggest measuring diversity in terms of entropy, which is reasonable, but as they acknowledge in their response to questions, difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, even entropy measurements wouldn't fully address the other crucial aspect of the problem: visual quality. They also propose a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to resolve this issue, but consider this to be a direction for future work. 

Overall, given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, I believe this paper possesses sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, some form of psychophysical assessment would be valuable to corroborate the intuitions currently based on visual inspection of the figures presented in the paper and supplementary material.
