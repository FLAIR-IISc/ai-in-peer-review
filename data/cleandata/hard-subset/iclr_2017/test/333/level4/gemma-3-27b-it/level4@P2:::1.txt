This work presents a compelling baseline for parametric texture synthesis, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated using a surprisingly simple model: a single-layer convolutional network with random filters. The authors show that these textures often rival those produced by the more complex, state-of-the-art VGG-based model, leading to the insightful conclusion that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features may not be as crucial for natural texture generation as previously believed.

**Reject** with a request for major revisions. While the presented work is interesting and challenges common assumptions in the field, the conclusions drawn are not fully supported by the presented evidence, and the overall quality of synthesized textures, even with the proposed method, remains limited.

The core argument – that depth and pre-training are not *indispensable* – is plausible, and the single-layer random filter baseline is a valuable contribution. The results demonstrably show that good textures *can* be generated without these elements. However, the comparison to the VGG-based model is not consistently favorable. The authors acknowledge this, noting the VGG model sometimes performs better, particularly in inpainting tasks (Figure 7). This casts doubt on the claim of rivalling the state-of-the-art, and suggests the simpler model may lack crucial capabilities for capturing complex texture features. Furthermore, the overall visual quality of generated textures, even by the multi-scale random model, appears limited when compared to real-world examples or even some non-parametric methods, as hinted at in the text. The assertion that the presented results “surpass parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000)” feels overstated given the limited visual comparison provided.

The paper’s quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss is a positive step towards more objective assessment. However, the authors themselves acknowledge its limitations, particularly for textures with regular, man-made structures (section 7). The reliance on this metric, which is inherently biased towards the features learned by VGG, weakens the claim of objective superiority. The discussion of balancing perceptual similarity and variability is also valuable, but the proposed solutions are largely left for future work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Strengthen the comparison with the VGG model:** Provide a more rigorous and comprehensive comparison, perhaps using larger texture datasets and/or more detailed quantitative metrics. A head-to-head user study (human evaluation) would greatly strengthen the claims.
*   **Address the limitations of the VGG-based loss:** Explore alternative metrics for texture quality that are less biased towards VGG features.
*   **Investigate hierarchical models more thoroughly:** The authors briefly mention that hierarchical models are not an improvement in terms of quality but are more efficient. Provide more details on the architectures tested and the reasons for their lack of improvement in perceptual quality. Specifically, address the question of whether a well-designed hierarchical model could address the multi-scale issue more efficiently than the current approach.
*   **Expand visual comparisons:** Include comparisons to a wider range of texture synthesis methods, including non-parametric approaches, to provide better context for the presented results. Show more diverse texture examples.
*  **Clarify the optimization process:** Provide more details about the sensitivity of the optimization procedure to hyperparameters, particularly for the single-layer models.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   You state that the single-layer model's success might stem from a more elaborate optimization procedure (Introduction). Could you elaborate on the specific tuning parameters and techniques employed to achieve this, and how they differ from those used with the VGG-based model?
*   What specific architectural choices were made when testing hierarchical models, and why did they not yield improvements in texture quality? What learning rates and optimization methods were used?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where the VGG-based model outperforms the proposed method, particularly regarding inpainting tasks (Figure 7)? What characteristics of those textures might explain this difference?
*   How sensitive are the results to the specific random seed used for initializing the filters? Did you observe significant variation across different random initializations?
*   While you discuss the limitations of the VGG-based loss, what alternative metrics are you considering for future work, and what are the potential challenges in implementing them?
