## Review for ICLR 2017 - WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

This paper presents a compelling investigation into the requirements for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly strong results can be achieved with a minimal baseline – a single-layer convolutional network utilizing random filters – challenging the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are essential for high-quality texture generation. The work provides valuable insights into the core components necessary for this task and proposes a novel quantitative evaluation metric based on synthesis loss in the VGG-based model.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses an important question within the field of generative modeling and delivers convincing evidence supporting its claims. The experimental results are well-presented and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, often matching or exceeding the performance of the established VGG-based method. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper’s strength lies in its clear articulation of the research question and its systematic exploration of different network architectures. The authors effectively isolate and demonstrate the surprisingly limited contribution of depth and pre-training in the context of texture synthesis. The comparison across a wide range of single-layer architectures – Fourier basis filters, K-means learnt filters, PCA filters, and random filters – provides a comprehensive evaluation of the impact of filter choice. 

The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric, utilizing the VGG-based loss, is a significant contribution. While human evaluation remains the gold standard, this metric provides a more objective and repeatable method for assessing texture quality, allowing for more rigorous comparisons between models. The ablation study comparing single-scale vs. multi-scale models, revealing the importance of combining filters across different scales, is particularly insightful.  

The paper is well-written and thoroughly references relevant prior work, positioning the research effectively within the existing literature. The authors acknowledge limitations and provide a thoughtful discussion of future research directions, specifically concerning the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in generated textures.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Clarification on Optimization:** The paper mentions a more “elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” as a factor contributing to the discrepancy with earlier reports. Providing more detail regarding this tuning – specific parameters, schedules, or techniques employed – would strengthen the reproducibility and impact of the work.
*   **Comparison to Non-Parametric Methods:** The reviewer correctly points out the generated samples are not perfect and may be worse than non-parametric methods. Elaborating on the limitations of both parametric and non-parametric methods and discussing where the proposed approach positions itself would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Inpainting Results:** Addressing the reviewer's observation regarding the VGG-based model’s superior performance in the inpainting task (Figure 7) would be beneficial. Is this a limitation of the single-layer model’s representation, or an artifact of the evaluation metric?
*   **Computational Efficiency:** While acknowledging the inefficiency of the multi-scale approach, further discussion on potential optimizations or alternatives for achieving similar receptive fields in a more computationally tractable manner would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more specifics regarding the “elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” mentioned in the paper? What specific parameters or techniques were employed that differed from previous work and contributed to the improved results?
2.  The paper highlights the success of random filters. Do different initialization schemes for these random filters (e.g., Glorot/Xavier vs. He initialization) significantly impact the quality of the generated textures?
3.  Regarding the inpainting task (Figure 7), can you offer any insight into why the VGG-based model appears to perform better than the single-layer models in this scenario? Does this suggest limitations in the representational capacity of the single-layer approach for capturing long-range dependencies?
4.  You mention the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability. What specific modifications to the current framework (e.g., adjustments to the optimization process or the loss function) would you consider to better control this trade-off and increase the diversity of generated samples?
