This paper presents a compelling investigation into the requirements for generating natural-looking textures. The central finding—that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer network with random filters—is both surprising and thoroughly analyzed. The authors effectively challenge the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are indispensable for this task.

**Decision: Accept** – The paper offers a significant and counterintuitive result that challenges established practices in texture synthesis. The thorough experimental evaluation and clear presentation of findings support the claims made.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors directly address a critical question in the field of natural image generation: what components of existing deep learning approaches are truly essential for success? By systematically demonstrating that single-layer networks with random filters can achieve competitive results, they effectively dismantle the perceived necessity of deep hierarchical representations and pre-training on natural images. This finding has important implications for understanding the underlying mechanisms of texture synthesis and potentially for designing more efficient generative models. The comprehensive comparison against various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) and the state-of-the-art VGG-based model provides strong evidence for their claims. The introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG loss, while acknowledged as imperfect, represents a step towards more objective evaluation in this field. The ablation studies, particularly those focusing on filter size and multi-scale approaches, provide valuable insights into the factors contributing to successful texture generation.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

While the results are convincing, the evaluation primarily relies on the VGG-based loss. Although the authors acknowledge its limitations, a more robust evaluation incorporating human perceptual studies would further strengthen the paper. Exploring different distance metrics beyond Euclidean distance for comparing Gram matrices might also reveal further insights. The discussion section raises important points about the interplay between perceptual similarity and variability, and a brief exploration of methods to explicitly control this trade-off could be valuable. Finally, clarifying the computational cost associated with the multi-scale model and a more detailed comparison with the efficiency of deeper networks would be beneficial. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the optimization procedure was more elaborately tuned compared to previous work. Could you provide more details on these tuning strategies? Specifically, what modifications were made, and how did they impact the results?
2.  The VGG-based loss is used as a proxy for perceptual quality. While acknowledged as imperfect, you state it's a step towards objective evaluation. Were any preliminary human studies conducted to validate the correlation between VGG loss and perceived quality, even on a small scale?
3.  In the discussion, you mention that deeper networks could improve efficiency. Could you elaborate on any initial experiments with deeper architectures and why they didn’t yield improved perceptual quality compared to the single-layer model?
4.  Regarding the “Minimax entropy principle,” could you provide a more intuitive explanation of how allowing a “small mismatch” in summary statistics impacts the generated textures, and why this might lead to better results?
5.  The code for the presented models is not available. Will the authors consider releasing the code to facilitate reproducibility and further research?
