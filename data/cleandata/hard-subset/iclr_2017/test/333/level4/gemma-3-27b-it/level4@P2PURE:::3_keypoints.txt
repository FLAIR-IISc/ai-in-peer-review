- Builds on Gatys et al.: uses correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses to characterize visual textures.  
- Compares performance of various network architectures, both deep and shallow, within the Gram-matrix framework.  
- Key finding: very shallow networks (single filter bank with random weights) are surprisingly effective for texture synthesis.  
- For simple, regular textures, shallow random filters can produce visually superior results compared to complex, data-adapted filters like VGG-19.  
- Paper provides an insightful discussion of the strengths and limitations of these texture synthesis methods.  
- Empirical result (Fig. 4): optimizing images against shallow filter banks can yield lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG-19 optimization, likely due to optimization difficulty of VGG-19’s non-linear objective.  
- Supplementary material shows initializing VGG-19 optimization with shallow-network results improves VGG-19 performance.  
- Main limitation: lack of systematic comparison against quantifiable objectives and metrics for texture synthesis.  
- Trivial reproduction of reference textures via shallow statistics is easy, but capturing broader texture distributions remains the core challenge.  
- Authors propose entropy as a diversity measure but note practical implementation challenges and that entropy alone doesn’t ensure visual quality.  
- Suggestion of psychophysical evaluation as a practical solution, but this is considered beyond the current work’s scope.  
- Overall: paper is strong and publishable at ICLR, though psychophysical evaluation would strengthen the claims.
