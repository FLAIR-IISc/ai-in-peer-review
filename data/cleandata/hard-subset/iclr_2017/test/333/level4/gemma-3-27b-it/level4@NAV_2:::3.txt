The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterisation of visual textures. This paper investigates in detail which kind of deep or shallow networks may work well in this framework. One of the main findings is that very shallow nets, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, work surprisingly well, and for simple and regular textures may produce results which are visually superior to complex data-adapted filters such as the ones in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strength and limitations on such methods for texture synthesis.

I recommend accepting this paper. The work challenges a prevailing assumption in texture synthesis – that depth and pre-training are indispensable – and demonstrates a surprisingly effective minimal baseline using single-layer networks with random filters. This is a significant and counterintuitive finding. Furthermore, the authors provide a comprehensive comparison of various single-layer architectures, offering valuable insights into the components crucial for texture generation.

The paper convincingly shows that high-quality textures can be generated without the complexity of deep, pre-trained networks. Figure 4 demonstrates that optimization with shallow filter banks can even yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss compared to directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective, a result the authors reasonably attribute to the optimization landscape of the deeper network. The inclusion of supplementary material detailing improved VGG-19 optimization initialized with single-layer results strengthens the findings. The thorough ablation studies and comparative analyses presented provide strong support for the claims made.

While the subjective evaluation of texture quality remains a challenge, the authors acknowledge this limitation and propose avenues for future work, including psychophysical assessments. It would be beneficial to understand the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices within the optimization procedure (e.g., the scaling factor mentioned in Section 4). Additionally, exploring the generalizability of these findings to more complex and less structured textures would be valuable. Could the authors comment on the computational cost associated with the multi-scale model compared to VGG-based synthesis, and whether optimizations could be made? Finally, what is the role of ReLU non-linearities – do they truly provide an indispensable component, or are other non-linearities equally effective?
