This work presents a simple yet surprisingly effective baseline for parametric texture synthesis. Empirical results demonstrate that textures generated using this multi-scale, random filter approach often achieve comparable quality to those produced by a more complex VGG-based model employing multi-layer, pre-trained filters. The authors argue this suggests that successful texture synthesis doesn’t necessarily require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps.

This is an interesting and insightful contribution. However, the conclusions regarding the necessity of deep hierarchical representations warrant further investigation. First, the quality of samples generated by *both* the VGG-based model and the single-layer baseline is noticeably imperfect and generally inferior to results obtained from non-parametric texture synthesis methods. Second, the VGG-based model appears to perform better in the inpainting task illustrated in Figure 7. Finally, it's worth considering whether a truly hierarchical model – rather than a large collection of filters with varying sizes – would offer a more efficient and robust approach to handling multi-scale textures. 

Specifically, the current multi-scale approach relies on a large number of independent filters, which could become computationally expensive as the resolution or complexity of the textures increases. A hierarchical architecture might learn to represent different scales in a more compact and generalizable manner. Further experiments comparing the proposed method to hierarchical parametric models would strengthen the paper's argument and better clarify the role of hierarchical representations in texture synthesis.
