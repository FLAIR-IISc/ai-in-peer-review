- Central finding: high-quality textures can be synthesized with a single-layer network using random filters, challenging prevailing assumptions about required depth and pre-training.  
- Surprise and significance: result is unexpected and shifts focus in texture generation research.  
- Core contribution: demonstrates depth and pre-training are not indispensable for natural texture synthesis.  
- New metric: introduces a quantitative texture quality measure as an objective alternative to human evaluation.  
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends accepting the paper.  
- Experimental strength: robust experiments show the baseline model is competitive with, and sometimes surpasses, more complex state-of-the-art networks.  
- Ablation studies: thorough comparison across filter types, sizes, and architectures strengthens conclusions.  
- Optimization importance: careful optimization procedures are highlighted as a key factor enabling simple models to perform well, addressing prior discrepancies.  
- VGG-loss discussion: suggests expanding analysis of VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality and investigating failure cases.  
- Metric limitations: requests deeper examination of limitations of the proposed quantitative evaluation and ways to improve it.  
- Computational cost: asks for a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the multi-scale model.  
- Question on scaling factor: asks how sensitive results are to the 10^7 scaling factor in optimization and how varying it affects texture quality.  
- Multi-layer behavior: requests details on conditions where deeper, multi-layer random CNNs exactly reconstruct textures and how this relates to the MaxEnt framework.  
- Alternative perceptual metrics: seeks preliminary thoughts or alternatives to VGG-based loss for capturing perceptual nuances, especially for regular, man-made textures.
