## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper investigates the essential components for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks, challenging the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are crucial. The authors demonstrate surprisingly strong results can be achieved with single-layer convolutional networks employing random filters, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). The work provides valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms of texture generation and suggests that simpler models can be surprisingly effective. The accompanying discussion regarding the limitations of current evaluation metrics and the need for considering both perceptual similarity and variability is insightful and well-articulated.

**Reject.** While the paper presents intriguing findings and a thoughtful discussion, the lack of a robust, quantitative evaluation beyond the VGG-based loss makes it difficult to confidently assess the claimed improvements. The core claim – that single-layer networks with random filters can rival state-of-the-art performance – requires stronger evidence than primarily visual inspection, despite the compelling examples provided.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its counter-intuitive finding that complex, pre-trained networks are not strictly *necessary* for high-quality texture synthesis. The thorough exploration of different single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) and the comparison to the established VGG-based model are commendable. The observation that optimization of simpler models is less prone to local minima and can even provide a better initialization for more complex models (VGG) is a significant and practical contribution. The discussion of the interplay between constraints, entropy, and perceptual similarity is valuable and points toward important future research directions. However, the reliance on qualitative assessment, even supplemented by the VGG loss, weakens the support for the central claim. The VGG loss, as the authors acknowledge, is not a perfect proxy for human perception, and the observed lower loss for the multi-scale model in some cases doesn’t definitively translate to superior perceptual quality. The explanation regarding optimization difficulties is plausible, but requires more rigorous validation.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** A psychophysical experiment, even a small-scale user study, would substantially strengthen the paper. Presenting synthesized textures alongside ground truth examples and asking participants to rate quality or distinguish between them would provide invaluable data. Alternatively, exploring other quantitative metrics that correlate better with human perception (beyond VGG loss or SSIM) would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the authors compare various single-layer architectures, further ablation studies could dissect the contributions of filter size, the number of feature maps, and the ReLU non-linearity more precisely. Quantifying the impact of each component would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Discussion of Failure Cases:** The paper primarily showcases successful syntheses. Including a discussion of cases where the single-layer models *fail* to generate realistic textures would provide a more balanced and nuanced assessment. What types of textures are particularly challenging for the proposed approach?
*   **Clarify “Diversity”:** The paper touches upon the importance of variability/diversity in generated textures. However, defining and measuring this aspect remains somewhat vague. Providing a more concrete discussion of how diversity is assessed (even if a definitive metric isn’t available) would be helpful.
*   **Optimization Details:** More detail regarding the optimization process (L-BFGS-B parameters, sensitivity to initialization) beyond the scaling factor would be useful for reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail on the sensitivity of the results to the scaling factor (10<sup>7</sup>) used during optimization? Does the performance vary significantly with different scaling values?
2.  Regarding the VGG loss being lower for the multi-scale model in certain cases – are there any specific characteristics of those textures that might explain this phenomenon? Is it solely attributable to differences in optimization, or could it suggest that the multi-scale model captures different aspects of texture statistics?
3.  Given the computational cost of the multi-scale model, have you explored any techniques to reduce its complexity without sacrificing performance, such as filter pruning or dimensionality reduction?
4.  You mention the limitations of current evaluation metrics. Would you consider reporting the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) score as a complementary metric, despite its limitations in the context of texture synthesis?
5.  You discuss the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability. Do you have any preliminary thoughts on how to incorporate a measure of variability directly into the loss function during optimization?
