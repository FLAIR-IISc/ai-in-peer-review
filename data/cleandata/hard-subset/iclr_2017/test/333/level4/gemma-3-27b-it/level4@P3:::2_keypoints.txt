- Paper shows high-quality natural texture synthesis can be achieved with single-layer, no-pooling networks and random filters.
- Challenges prevailing belief that depth and pre-training are necessary for texture generation.
- Careful experimental setup and detailed analysis across architectures make the result surprising and compelling.
- Systematic exploration of filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) and configurations is thorough and informative.
- Introduces a quantitative texture-quality measure using a VGG-based synthesis loss to reduce reliance on subjective evaluation.
- Authors acknowledge metric limitations and discuss possible improvements.
- Ablation studies reveal the importance of receptive field size and multi-scale combinations over depth.
- Highlights the trade-off between perceptual similarity to reference textures and variability in generated samples.
- Detailed optimization procedure (including loss scaling) is important to achieved results with random filters.
- Suggests improving clarity of notation, especially definitions in equations (2) and (3).
- Recommends additional visualizations, including side-by-side comparisons of VGG-based and best single-layer results across textures.
- Requests quantitative comparison of computational cost (training/inference) between single-layer and multi-scale models.
- Encourages further exploration of methods to explicitly increase variability beyond adjusting optimization stopping criteria.
- Asks authors to detail the specific optimization changes that enabled strong performance with random filters.
- Requests justification for preferring VGG-based metric over alternatives like SSIM and whether other metrics were considered.
- Asks whether reconstruction behavior in deeper random CNNs depends on input size or can be mitigated by regularization or architectural changes.
- Requests a brief sketch on how variability could be incorporated into a MaxEnt framework.
