The structure of Gatys and others has shown that deep feature response correlation statistics (empirical gram matrices) provide an excellent feature of visual texture. This paper explores in detail what kind of deep or shallow network can work well in this structure. One of the main findings is that a very shallow net, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, works surprisingly well and can produce results for general and regular textures that are visually superior to complex data-adapted filters in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, this paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such approaches for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that the optimization of images relative to shallow filter banks can produce texture images with less VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing for VGG-19. This is considered a disadvantage of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the new supplement, the authors show that better optimization results can be obtained by starting VGG-19-based optimization with shallow network optimization results, which is a useful complement to the main experiment.

The main limitation of this paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable goal. Defining image statistics that allow for a precise copy of a reference texture, therefore with very good visual quality, is trivial, so such trivial statistics will also be very shallow. Instead, the goal remains an open problem of capturing a texture distribution and measuring how well a method copes with this challenge. Thus, the empirical results confirm the idea that general statistics are good enough for texture synthesis in terms of quality and diversity (compared to more complex statistics), but it is difficult to definitively confirm that this is true.

The authors suggest that diversity can be measured in terms of entropy. This is reasonable, however, they acknowledge in response to the question that, in practice, it is difficult to do. Moreover, it will not yet account for the other side of the problem, which is visual quality. They suggest a psychophysical evaluation, which may be the only practical way to solve this problem, but they believe that this is a material for future work.

Overall, since the evaluation of image generation is such a difficult problem, I think this paper still has enough strength to be published in ICLR. Nevertheless, some kind of psychophysical evaluation would be helpful in confirming the ideas that can currently only be obtained by inspecting paper images and supplementary materials.
