Gatti and others' framework demonstrates that deep feature response correlation statistics (empirical gram matrices) provide an excellent feature of visual texture. This paper explores in detail what kind of deep or shallow network can work well in this framework. One of the main findings is that a very shallow net, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, works surprisingly well and can produce results for general and regular textures that are visually better than complex data-adapted filters in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, this paper contains an interesting and informative discussion about the strengths and limitations of such approaches for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that image optimization compared to shallow filter banks can produce texture images with less VGG-19 loss than direct optimization for VGG-19. This is considered a disadvantage of optimizing highly non-linear VGG-19 cost functions, which is a reasonable explanation. In a new supplement, the authors show that better optimization results can be obtained by starting VGG-19-based optimization with shallow network optimization results, which is a useful complement to the main test.

The main limitation of this paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable goal. Defining image statistics that allow a specific copy of a reference texture, therefore very good visual quality, is trivial, so such trivial statistics will also be very shallow. Instead, the goal remains an open problem of capturing a texture distribution and measuring how well a method copes with this challenge. Thus, the empirical results confirm the idea that general statistics are good enough for texture synthesis in terms of quality and diversity (compared to more complex statistics), but it is difficult to definitively confirm whether this is true.

The authors suggest that diversity can be measured in terms of entropy. This is reasonable, but they acknowledge in the answer to the question that, in practice, it is difficult to do. Moreover, it will not yet account for the other side of the problem, which is visual quality. They suggest a psychophysical evaluation, which may be the only practical way to solve this problem, but they believe that it is an element for future work.

Overall, since image generation evaluation is such a difficult problem, I think this paper still has enough strength to be published in the ICLR. Nevertheless, some kind of psychophysical evaluation concepts will be helpful in confirming what can currently only be obtained by inspecting paper images and complementary materials.
