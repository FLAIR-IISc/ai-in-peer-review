The framework introduced by Gatys et al. showed that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper examines in detail which kinds of deep or shallow networks are effective within that framework. A central finding is that very shallow networks—consisting of a single filter bank with random weights—perform surprisingly well and, for simple and regular textures, can even produce visually superior results compared to complex, data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an interesting and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter-bank objectives can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the new supplementary material, they further show that initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the shallow-network results leads to better outcomes, a useful complement to the original experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is the lack of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. One can trivially define image statistics that simply reproduce an exact copy of any reference texture, thereby achieving very high visual quality; such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The true aim, however, is to capture a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. Thus, while the empirical results appear to support the intuition that simple statistics suffice for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (relative to more complex statistics), it is difficult to confirm this conclusively.

The authors indicate that diversity could be measured in terms of entropy. This is reasonable but, as they acknowledge in their responses to questions, difficult to implement in practice. Moreover, it would still not address the other key aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also suggest conducting a psychophysical assessment—which may be the only practical way to address this issue—but consider it material for future work.

Overall, because evaluating image generation is such a hard problem, I believe the paper nevertheless has sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. Still, some form of psychophysical assessment would be useful to substantiate the intuitions that currently can only be drawn from inspecting the figures in the paper and in the supplementary material.
