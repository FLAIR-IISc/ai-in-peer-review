- Main claim: high-quality textures can be synthesized with a single convolutional layer + ReLU + random filters + Gram-matrix constraints, without depth, pooling, or supervised pretraining.  
- The paper provides a strong minimal baseline that challenges the assumption that depth and supervised features are necessary.  
- Extensive single-layer ablations (Fourier, k-means/PCA, random; varying channel counts; whitening; per-texture sampling) underpin the claims.  
- A multi-scale random-filter variant markedly improves quality; multi-scale receptive fields and nonlinearity are critical.  
- Optimization matters: initializing deep (VGG) synthesis from single-layer samples improves results, indicating landscape/optimization effects.  
- Evaluation: quantitative proxy uses VGG-based Gram losses, supplemented by extensive qualitative comparisons.  
- Positioning: work is carefully related to Gatys et al., Portilla–Simoncelli, and contemporaneous texture-synthesis advances.  
- Decision: Accept — contributions are well-motivated, thoroughly ablated, and of broad community value.  
- Evidence: qualitative results are compelling; VGG loss is reasonable though imperfect.  
- Rigor/transparency: authors openly discuss metric limitations, similarity–variability trade-offs, and finite-size MaxEnt effects.  
- Caveat: Heavy reliance on VGG-based Gram losses can mis-rank highly regular textures and fails to capture sample variability.  
- Caveat: Multi-scale models with large spatial filters are computationally expensive; limited runtime/memory comparisons are provided.  
- Caveat: Statistical robustness is limited—claims (e.g., random vs learned filters) need quantitative summaries across textures and seeds.  
- Suggestion: Add a small human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC or inpainting tests) to complement VGG-based evaluation.  
- Suggestion: Quantify diversity across random initializations (feature-space dispersion) and report aggregate metrics (means, variances, significance).  
- Suggestion: Provide runtime/memory comparisons and specify filter counts per scale; detail hyperparameter sensitivities.  
- Suggestion: Discuss failure cases and textures where VGG loss misaligns with perception.  
- Suggestion: Compare quality and speed with fast feed-forward texture networks; consider other nonlinearities or mild pooling.  
- Suggestion: Release code and evaluation scripts to establish the method as a community baseline.  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the number of feature maps per scale and the chosen set of scales—any compute-quality “sweet spot”?  
- Question: How consistent are outcomes across optimization seeds and alternative optimizers (e.g., Adam)? Any mode collapse/low variability observed?  
- Question: Can you report aggregate VGG-loss statistics over all textures and seeds (with error bars) for random vs learned filters?  
- Question: For textures where VGG loss misaligns with perception, which layers or statistics drive the discrepancy?  
- Question: Were other nonlinearities or light pooling/normalization tested, and do core conclusions persist?  
- Question: Can the single-layer random approach extend to style transfer, or is depth required for content preservation?  
- Question: What are typical runtimes and memory footprints for the multi-scale model vs VGG synthesis at common resolutions, and will code/pretrained configs be released?
