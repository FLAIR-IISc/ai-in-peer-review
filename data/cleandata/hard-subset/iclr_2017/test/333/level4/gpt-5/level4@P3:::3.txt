1. Summary of contributions
This paper investigates which aspects of convolutional representations are truly necessary for natural texture synthesis within the Gram-based framework popularized by Gatys et al. The authors present a strong and surprisingly simple baseline: single-layer CNNs with random filters (no pooling, ReLU nonlinearity) can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality, sometimes rivaling or even surpassing models based on deep, pretrained networks. They systematically compare a range of single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA learned from ImageNet or from the target texture, different channel couplings and numbers of feature maps) and introduce a multi-scale random model that further improves results by combining filters across scales. They propose a quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based Gram loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, and they offer a thoughtful discussion of variability versus fidelity, relating the practical synthesis problem to maximum entropy modeling for finite images.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and clarifying finding: depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not necessary for high-quality Gram-based texture synthesis; a minimal, random, single-layer (especially multi-scale) model is often sufficient.
- Solid empirical evidence and careful analysis that provide a useful baseline and sharpen our understanding of what matters for this class of models.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: The paper asks what properties of feature representations (depth, pooling, training on natural images) are crucial for Gram-based texture synthesis. This question is well-motivated given the success of deep pretrained features in prior work.
- Approach and placement in literature: Building on Gatys et al., the authors keep the synthesis objective (match Gram matrices) and vary the representation (shallow/random vs deep/pretrained), covering random filters, analytically defined filter banks, and unsupervised filters. This isolates the role of architecture/training from the synthesis mechanism and is well positioned against classical models (Portilla–Simoncelli) and recent deep approaches.
- Evidence supporting claims:
  - Single-layer random models (with 11×11 filters) capture small to mid-scale structure; increasing the number of feature maps improves quality; random filters perform on par with or better than learned filters.
  - A multi-scale random model significantly improves long-range structure, supporting the claim that a mix of receptive field sizes (not hierarchy per se) is crucial. Ablations show: small filters reproduce color marginals; medium filters capture mid-scale structures; very large filters capture long-range layout but may miss fine texture—hence the benefit of combining scales.
  - Nonlinearities are essential: removing ReLU degrades synthesis quality.
  - Quantitative evaluation using VGG-based loss generally correlates with visual assessment; in some cases, shallow synthesis even achieves lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization, plausibly due to optimization difficulty in the deep objective. Initializing VGG-based synthesis from shallow outputs improves results, substantiating this explanation.
  - The discussion of fidelity–variability trade-offs is insightful. The paper argues that exact constraint matching in finite images can collapse variability, motivating soft constraints and metrics that define an ε-neighborhood of acceptable textures.

Limitations:
- Evaluation relies primarily on VGG-loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity; while reasonable and commonly used, it can be biased and does not measure diversity. The authors acknowledge the lack of a robust diversity/entropy metric and suggest psychophysical tests or inpainting-based protocols as future directions.
- Computational efficiency: large spatial filters in the single-layer multi-scale model are expensive; the authors note that deeper, hierarchical implementations can recover efficiency without improving quality.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help, not required for the decision)
- Quantitative diversity: Report variability across multiple samples per texture (e.g., distributions of VGG-loss to the reference and between synthesized samples). Even if entropy is intractable, pairwise distances in feature space or coverage metrics across multiple runs would give a sense of sample diversity.
- Psychophysical evaluation: A small-scale two-alternative forced choice test (e.g., real vs synthesized, or real patch vs inpainted patch) on a subset of textures would substantiate the perceptual claims and the fidelity–variability discussion.
- Optimization details: Provide a brief sensitivity analysis for key optimization choices (e.g., L-BFGS-B scaling factor 1e7, iteration count, stopping criteria), and discuss how these influence the fidelity–variability trade-off.
- Fairness of comparisons: Clarify whether all methods (shallow and VGG-based) were optimized under comparable budgets (iterations, compute time), and whether multi-start strategies change outcomes.
- Multi-scale design: Detail the exact filter sizes, counts per scale, and normalization across scales. Consider reporting results with dilated/separable convolutions as an efficiency baseline.
- Reproducibility: Release code, random seeds, and texture lists used in Figures to facilitate replication.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the results to the number of feature maps versus filter size? Is there a clear scaling law (e.g., more channels can compensate for smaller spatial filters)?
- How many independent synthesis runs per texture were used to assess variability, and how variable are the outputs visually and under the VGG-based metric?
- Did you try average vs max pooling within the shallow networks, or other nonlinearities (e.g., leaky ReLU)? How do they affect performance?
- How does image resolution affect the perceived advantage of multi-scale random models? Do results degrade/improve with larger textures?
- What is the impact of different input normalizations (e.g., per-image mean subtraction vs dataset mean) on synthesis quality?
- Can you quantify the computational cost differences between the single-layer multi-scale model and a hierarchical implementation that achieves similar receptive fields?
- In cases where the multi-scale random model outperforms VGG under VGG-loss, do you observe over-smoothing or reduced diversity? Any diagnostics beyond the loss values?

Overall, this paper provides a clear, well-motivated analysis with compelling empirical results and a valuable minimal baseline. Despite the known challenges of evaluating generative models, the work advances understanding and practice in texture synthesis and merits acceptance.
