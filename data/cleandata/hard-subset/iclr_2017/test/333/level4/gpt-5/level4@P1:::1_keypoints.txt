- Paper claim: a single convolutional layer with random filters and multi-scale receptive fields can synthesize textures with perceptual quality competitive with deep models.
- Systematic comparisons: authors test single-layer models with random, Fourier, PCA, and k-means filters and vary feature map counts, finding scale and map count matter more than learned filters.
- Metric proposal: use VGG-based Gram-matrix loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity instead of only qualitative inspection.
- Analysis: examine roles of ReLU nonlinearity and scale, and discuss a MaxEnt perspective on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary rejection reason: evidence is insufficient to support the strong claim that deep hierarchical representations are unnecessary for texture synthesis.
- Secondary rejection reason: lack of rigorous, fair evaluation against strong non-parametric baselines and indications that VGG-based (deep) models can outperform the proposed method in tasks like inpainting.
- Supporting argument: both single-layer and VGG samples are still much worse than non-parametric methods; no head-to-head controlled comparison with human evaluation.
- Supporting argument: main quantitative metric (VGG-loss) is derived from the deep baseline and may bias comparisons; it fails on some structured/man-made textures.
- Supporting argument: results are texture-dependent and mixed; authors must show shallow multi-scale models match/exceed deep models across diverse textures and independent evaluations.
- Supporting argument: computational costs of very large single-layer filters undercut the claim that depth is unnecessary, and no hierarchical alternative is empirically demonstrated.
- Supporting argument: insufficient quantitative treatment of variability vs. similarity; risk of overfitting to a narrow epsilon-ball around the reference texture.
- Feedback: run rigorous comparisons to non-parametric state-of-the-art with human studies or psychophysical tasks and report statistics/significance.
- Feedback: complement VGG-loss with additional independent perceptual metrics and human judgments to triangulate conclusions.
- Feedback: ensure fairness by controlling feature map/parameter counts, report runtime/FLOPs/memory, and include ablations for ReLU, filter sizes, and optimization scaling.
- Feedback: test sensitivity to random seeds and report aggregate statistics (mean/std) for perceptual metrics and losses.
- Feedback: propose and empirically validate hierarchical CNN architectures that reproduce multi-scale receptive fields with better efficiency.
- Feedback: analyze generalization across texture classes and provide a taxonomy (stochastic vs. structured/man-made).
- Questions: Which specific non-parametric methods were compared and can controlled quantitative (human) comparisons be included?
- Questions: How do you mitigate bias from using VGG-loss, and can you report additional metrics and human judgments?
- Questions: Can you show a hierarchical CNN with small kernels matching your single-layer multi-scale quality and efficiency without pretraining?
- Questions: How variable are samples across seeds and restarts; can you quantify diversity or human indistinguishability?
- Questions: Can you report comprehensive inpainting results across many textures and explain when/why the single-layer model underperforms?
- Questions: How sensitive are results to feature map count, scale choices, and ReLU, especially when fixing total parameter budgets?
- Questions: Under what conditions do deeper random CNNs exactly reconstruct references, and how does that affect the similarity–variability trade-off?
