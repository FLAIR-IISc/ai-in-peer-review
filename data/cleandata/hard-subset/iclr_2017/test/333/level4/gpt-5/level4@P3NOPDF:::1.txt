Summary
This paper proposes a simple yet strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer model with random, multi-scale filters. Empirically, the method sometimes rivals a VGG-based approach that relies on multi-layer, pre-trained representations. The authors argue that effective texture synthesis does not necessarily require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps.

Strengths
- Clear, thought-provoking contribution: a minimalist baseline that challenges the widely held assumption that deep, learned hierarchies are essential for high-quality texture synthesis.
- Empirical results show that random multi-scale features can match or approach the performance of VGG-based methods for certain textures, which is an insightful and practically relevant finding.
- The work invites the community to reconsider which statistics and architectural elements are truly necessary for parametric texture synthesis.

Concerns and limitations
- The main conclusion appears somewhat overstated given the mixed results. In particular, neither the VGG-based model nor the proposed single-layer baseline consistently produces perfect textures; both often lag behind strong non-parametric methods. This weakens the claim that deep hierarchies are unnecessary for texture synthesis in general.
- The inpainting results (e.g., Figure 7) suggest that the VGG-based model performs better in that setting, indicating that hierarchical features might still be important for tasks requiring long-range consistency or structural coherence.
- The paper does not convincingly separate the contributions of multi-scale processing from hierarchical depth. Using many filters at different scales is not the same as a hierarchical model; it remains unclear whether a hierarchical architecture could achieve similar or superior results more efficiently.
- Quantitative evaluation is limited. The paper relies primarily on qualitative comparisons, which makes it difficult to assess the strength and generality of the claims across texture categories.
- The scope of the claim (“does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations”) could be more carefully delimited to specific types of textures (e.g., stochastic vs. highly structured, near-regular textures).

Questions for the authors
- Can you characterize the classes of textures where the proposed method matches VGG and the classes where it fails? Are there systematic differences (e.g., stochastic vs. structured, presence of long-range regularities)?
- How sensitive are results to the number of filters, the distribution of filter sizes, and random initialization? What is the minimal configuration that still competes with VGG on representative textures?
- Would a shallow-but-hierarchical architecture (e.g., a few layers with dilations or pooling) achieve similar performance with fewer filters or better efficiency? Have you tried hierarchical variants holding the total parameter count constant?
- How do runtime and memory compare between your baseline and the VGG-based method during optimization? Please report wall-clock times and resource usage for typical resolutions.
- For inpainting, can you provide more extensive comparisons and appropriate perceptual evaluations to substantiate the observed gap with VGG-based features?

Suggestions for improvement
- Temper the central claim and make it conditional on texture type and task. Your results compellingly show that multi-scale random features are surprisingly strong for many textures, but they do not entirely obviate the value of hierarchical depth, especially for tasks like inpainting.
- Add quantitative evaluation. Options include:
  - Perceptual user studies comparing outputs from your method, VGG-based methods, and strong non-parametric baselines.
  - Statistical similarity measures tailored to textures (e.g., comparing key summary statistics across scales or using discriminability tests with a texture classifier).
- Include stronger baselines, especially non-parametric methods known to excel in texture synthesis, to substantiate the claim that both parametric methods still trail them on challenging cases.
- Provide comprehensive ablations:
  - Vary the number of filters per scale and the set of scales; analyze quality vs. compute trade-offs.
  - Compare random filters to learned filters under the same single-layer architecture to isolate the effect of learning.
  - Evaluate hierarchical variants that reuse parameters across scales (e.g., dilated convolutions, wavelet-like or scattering-inspired cascades) to address the efficiency question and clarify the role of depth vs. explicit multi-scale coverage.
- Expand the inpainting study with:
  - More examples spanning textures with different degrees of regularity.
  - Perceptual assessments and, if applicable, task-relevant metrics that reflect texture plausibility rather than purely pixelwise fidelity.

Clarity and presentation
- Overall, the paper is clearly written and the empirical comparisons are easy to follow. It would still help to more explicitly frame the claim as “for many textures, multi-scale random features suffice,” and to delineate where the approach struggles.
- Consider adding failure-case visualizations and a brief taxonomy of textures tested to improve the reader’s understanding of scope.

Minor issues
- Fix minor grammar/wording:
  - “sometime” → “sometimes”
  - “The conclusions are needed to be further testified” → “The conclusions need further validation”
  - “Besides VGG-based model seems to do better in inpainting task” → “Besides, the VGG-based model seems to perform better on the inpainting task”
  - “in inpainting” appears twice; streamline phrasing.

Overall assessment
This is an interesting and insightful paper that presents a valuable baseline and challenges prevailing assumptions in parametric texture synthesis. The qualitative evidence is compelling for a subset of textures, but the central claims would benefit from stronger quantitative evaluation, a more nuanced discussion of scope, and experiments that disentangle multi-scale coverage from hierarchical depth. With these additions and a moderated conclusion, the paper would make a solid and thought-provoking contribution.
