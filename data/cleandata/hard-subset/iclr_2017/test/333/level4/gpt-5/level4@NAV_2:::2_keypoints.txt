- Paper shows a minimal single-layer CNN with random filters and Gram-matrix statistics can synthesize high-quality textures.
- Benchmarks many single-layer variants (random, Fourier, hand-crafted, PCA, k-means; small vs large banks; single- vs multi-scale) and finds depth and supervised pretraining unnecessary.
- A multi-scale single-layer model with ReLU further improves capture of small-, mid-, and long-range structure.
- Uses a VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and discusses the fidelity–diversity trade-off via maximum-entropy principles.

- Decision: Accept — surprising, impactful central finding and careful ablation studies.

- Random filters perform as well or better than learned/hand-crafted banks, especially in multi-scale setups.
- Combining scales is crucial; single-scale small filters miss long-range structure and single large filters miss fine detail.
- ReLU nonlinearities are necessary to capture dependencies in texture synthesis.
- The VGG-based metric, while imperfect, is a reasonable quantitative proxy to complement visual inspection.
- The work challenges common assumptions in style-transfer/texture-synthesis pipelines and provides a strong, efficient baseline.

- Suggest adding a small human study to calibrate VGG loss, especially for regular man-made textures.
- Recommend reporting intra-model variability diagnostics (e.g., VGG distances across random seeds).
- Request full implementation details for the multi-scale model (scales, feature counts, filter-size distribution, initializations).
- Ask for documentation of sensitivity to optimization choices (gradient scaling, iterations, box constraints) and a brief ablation.
- Clarify preprocessing steps (mean subtraction, whitening) and their effects.
- Where VGG loss paradoxes occur, add analysis of causes (local minima, loss landscape, variability).
- Suggest reporting runtime and memory profiles to contextualize efficiency vs quality trade-offs.
- Recommend making the MaxEnt framing more actionable by specifying an epsilon-environment tied to the VGG metric.

- How sensitive are results to the number of random feature maps per scale and is there diminishing returns?
- How does image resolution affect synthesis quality and the need for filter sizes/scales?
- How sensitive is performance to choice of nonlinearity (ReLU vs leaky-ReLU/ELU)?
- How critical is the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling for L-BFGS-B convergence; would Adam or unscaled L-BFGS work similarly?
- Do different random initializations meaningfully affect sample diversity, and can early stopping control the similarity–variability trade-off?
- For textures where VGG loss mismatches perception (e.g., regular grids), can alternative descriptors be proposed?
- Can you report quantitative variability across samples (distribution of VGG distances) to support the similarity–variability discussion?
- Do conclusions extend beyond stationary textures to near-regular or weakly non-stationary textures?
- In deeper random CNNs where exact reconstructions occasionally occur, when does this happen and how can degeneracy be avoided while preserving quality?
