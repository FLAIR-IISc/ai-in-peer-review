- Paper proposes a simple single-layer parametric texture synthesis baseline using random multi-scale filters that sometimes rivals VGG-based multi-layer methods.
- Main claim: effective texture synthesis may not require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps.
- Strength: Clear, thought-provoking minimalist baseline challenging assumptions about deep hierarchies.
- Strength: Empirical results show random multi-scale features can match or approach VGG on certain textures.
- Strength: Encourages rethinking which statistics and architectures are necessary for parametric texture synthesis.
- Concern: Conclusion is overstated given mixed results; neither method consistently outperforms strong non-parametric baselines.
- Concern: VGG-based model appears better for inpainting, suggesting hierarchical features matter for long-range structure.
- Concern: The paper doesn’t disentangle multi-scale processing from hierarchical depth; many scales ≠ hierarchy.
- Concern: Quantitative evaluation is limited; reliance on qualitative comparisons weakens generality claims.
- Concern: Scope of the central claim should be limited to specific texture types (e.g., stochastic vs. structured).
- Question: Which texture classes work well or poorly for the proposed method—systematic differences like stochastic vs. structured?
- Question: How sensitive are results to number of filters, filter sizes, and random initialization; what’s the minimal competitive configuration?
- Question: Would shallow hierarchical architectures (dilations/pooling) match performance more efficiently; were hierarchical variants tested at equal parameter count?
- Question: How do runtime and memory compare between this baseline and VGG during optimization—report wall-clock and resources.
- Question: Can you expand inpainting comparisons with perceptual evaluations to substantiate the VGG gap?
- Suggestion: Temper claims and frame them conditionally for texture types and tasks.
- Suggestion: Add quantitative evaluations such as user studies, texture-specific statistical measures, or discriminability tests.
- Suggestion: Include stronger non-parametric baselines to benchmark challenging cases.
- Suggestion: Provide comprehensive ablations on filter counts/scales, random vs. learned filters, and hierarchical variants for efficiency comparisons.
- Suggestion: Expand inpainting study with more examples, perceptual assessments, and texture-plausibility metrics.
- Clarity: Paper is generally clear but should explicitly frame the claim as conditional and include failure-case visualizations and a texture taxonomy.
- Minor issues: Fix grammar/wording (e.g., “sometime”→“sometimes”; rephrase awkward sentences).
- Overall: Interesting and valuable baseline that challenges assumptions but needs stronger quantitative evaluation, nuanced scope, and experiments disentangling multi-scale vs. depth to solidify claims.
