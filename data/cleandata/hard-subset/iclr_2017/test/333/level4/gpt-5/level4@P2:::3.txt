Summary of contributions
This paper revisits the Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis framework of Gatys et al. and asks a focused question: which aspects of the feature representation are truly necessary to generate high-quality natural textures? Through careful ablations, the authors show that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining is indispensable. A single convolutional layer with random filters, especially in a multi-scale filter bank, can synthesize textures of striking perceptual quality—sometimes rivaling or even surpassing VGG-19-based synthesis. The paper further:
- Systematically compares single-layer architectures with random, hand-crafted (Fourier), PCA, and k-means filters.
- Demonstrates the importance of multi-scale filters and nonlinearities (ReLU).
- Proposes using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual distance.
- Discusses the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability and situates the findings within a maximum-entropy perspective.
- Provides a practical optimization insight: initializing VGG-based synthesis with shallow-model outputs improves results.

Decision: Accept
Reasoning: The work provides a strong, minimal baseline—single-layer random filter banks—for texture synthesis, supported by clear ablations and analysis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, supervised features are essential, offering both conceptual and practical value to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity and importance: The paper addresses a central question in neural texture synthesis—what properties of feature representations matter for generating natural textures? This is timely and relevant given the widespread use of deep features for generation.
- Motivation and positioning: The approach is well grounded in the literature (Julesz; Portilla & Simoncelli; Gatys et al.; subsequent improvements), and the ablations are designed to isolate the roles of depth, training, filter size, and scale.
- Empirical support:
  - Single-layer random filters with Gram statistics can produce textures that closely match the perceptual quality of deep VGG-based models across many exemplars.
  - Multi-scale random filter banks outperform single-scale versions, capturing both local and long-range structures; ReLU nonlinearity is shown to be crucial.
  - The VGG-based loss serves as a consistent quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and reveals cases where shallow optimization yields lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization; initializing VGG synthesis from shallow outputs mitigates optimization difficulties.
- Rigor and scope: The comparisons cover multiple filter types and capacities (363 vs. 3267 features, and a 1024-feature multi-scale model), with a clear synthesis protocol (L-BFGS-B) and a standardized evaluation surrogate. Qualitative results are broad and compelling.

Main limitations
- Evaluation remains anchored to the VGG-based loss and visual inspection; there is no quantitative assessment of sample diversity (entropy) or psychophysical validation. The authors acknowledge this and propose future directions, but a principled diversity measure is still missing.
- Some baselines (e.g., recent GAN- or autoregressive-based texture generators) are not included; while the focus is on Gram-statistics models, cross-paradigm context would strengthen the claims.
- The multi-scale single-layer approach can be computationally heavy due to large kernels; while efficiency is discussed, empirical runtime and memory reports would be helpful.
- The surprising cases where shallow models attain lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization highlight optimization brittleness in deep losses; more systematic analysis of optimization settings would be useful.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Include a small-scale psychophysical study or a proxy (e.g., two-alternative forced choice for inpainting detection) to jointly assess perceptual similarity and variability.
- Report variability across multiple seeds: per-texture distribution of VGG losses and perceptual diversity, possibly supplemented by nearest-neighbor analyses in feature space.
- Provide runtime, memory, and iteration counts for single-scale vs. multi-scale, and for shallow vs. VGG-based synthesis, to contextualize efficiency trade-offs.
- Offer more details on the multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, counts per scale, normalization) and sensitivity analyses (number of features, filter distributions, effect of removing ReLU).
- Clarify and standardize notation (e.g., normalization constants in losses), fix minor typos, and ensure all references are complete.
- Discuss failure cases and textures where the VGG-based loss poorly predicts perceptual quality (e.g., highly regular man-made patterns).

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the results to the distribution and scaling of random weights (e.g., Xavier vs. He initialization, Gaussian vs. uniform)? Does feature normalization (e.g., per-filter variance) matter?
- In the multi-scale model, how are feature counts allocated across scales, and how does this allocation affect quality and efficiency?
- What is the observed variability across different random seeds for the same texture (visually and in VGG-loss)? Can you quantify mode collapse tendencies, if any?
- Have you tried alternative nonlinearities (e.g., leaky ReLU, ELU) or per-feature normalization (instance/batch norm) and observed any systematic differences?
- Can you provide more insight into why VGG-loss can be lower for shallow-model samples than for direct VGG optimization? Is this due to better conditioning, smoother loss landscapes, or implicit regularization?
- Beyond textures, do your findings extend to style transfer or non-stationary patterns when using spatially varying Gram statistics or patchwise aggregation?

Overall, the paper offers a clear, well-executed study with impactful conclusions for texture synthesis. Despite limits in evaluation breadth, the minimal random-filter baseline and the ablation insights merit publication.
