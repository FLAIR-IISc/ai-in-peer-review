Summary of contributions
- The paper investigates which components are truly necessary to synthesize natural-looking textures using deep representations. Contrary to prevailing assumptions, it shows that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised training on natural images is indispensable. High-quality textures can be synthesized with a single convolutional layer, no pooling, and even random filters.
- The authors establish a strong minimal baseline: matching Gram matrices of feature activations from a single-layer CNN (typically with 11×11 filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and stride 1), optimized via L-BFGS-B from noise. They compare random filters to Fourier bases, PCA, and k-means filters (learned from ImageNet or the target texture) across small (363) and larger (3267) numbers of feature maps.
- A multi-scale random single-layer model (combining multiple filter sizes, up to 55×55) substantially improves results by simultaneously capturing small-, mid-, and long-range statistics. Removing ReLU harms synthesis quality.
- For evaluation, the paper uses a VGG-based texture loss (Gram matrices computed at multiple VGG layers) as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, supplementing qualitative inspection. In some cases, the multi-scale random model even achieves lower VGG loss than VGG-based synthesis.
- The discussion positions these findings within a maximum entropy framework, emphasizing that practical synthesis must allow small mismatches (“epsilon-environments”) to balance perceptual similarity and sample variability.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
1) Clear and surprising result with strong empirical support: single-layer random CNNs (especially multi-scale) can rival or approach VGG-based synthesis on many textures.
2) Well-motivated, carefully controlled analysis that isolates the roles of depth, supervision, and multi-scale structure; the multi-scale finding is particularly insightful and practical.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Understanding which components matter for texture synthesis is both scientifically interesting and practically important, given the widespread use of deep features for generation, style transfer, and texture modeling.
- Positioning and motivation: The work is well positioned with respect to prior texture models (e.g., Portilla-Simoncelli), VGG-based synthesis, and alternative generative models (GANs, PixelCNN). By going “backwards” and removing components, the paper complements prior efforts that add constraints or architecture depth.
- Empirical thoroughness: The comparisons span multiple filter types (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means), numbers of channels, and a multi-scale variant. The ablation showing that multi-scale helps beyond simply increasing the largest receptive field is convincing. The necessity of nonlinearities is also demonstrated.
- Evaluation: Using VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity is reasonable given the field’s current practice and limitations of SSIM/pixel distances. The paper is appropriately cautious about the metric’s imperfections and documents cases where it diverges from perceptual judgments.
- Rigor and claims: Claims are calibrated to the evidence. The paper does not overstate generality beyond texture synthesis. The discussion about variability vs. similarity and the MaxEnt perspective clarifies why optimization and constraint slack matter in practice.
- Reproducibility: Model descriptions, optimization settings (including the use of L-BFGS-B with box constraints and gradient scaling), and evaluation protocol are described with sufficient detail to reproduce results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not central to my decision)
- Human evaluation: While VGG-loss is a useful proxy, a small human study (e.g., two-alternative forced choice or inpainting discrimination) would strengthen claims about perceptual quality and variability. Even a modest number of participants could be informative.
- Variability quantification: The paper rightly emphasizes the trade-off between perceptual similarity and diversity. Including simple diagnostics (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss among multiple samples, texture-specific FID-like measures in feature space) would make the variability discussion more concrete.
- Sensitivity analysis: Briefly report sensitivity to the number of feature maps for each scale, relative weights across scales, and stopping criteria. These hyperparameters may materially affect the similarity–variability balance.
- Computational efficiency: Provide measured runtimes and memory footprints for the single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. hierarchical variants to substantiate the efficiency discussion.
- Scope of textures: Group results by texture class (stochastic vs. highly regular, man-made vs. natural) and summarize where the approach excels or struggles. This would help readers interpret the method’s applicability.
- Broader baselines: A quantitative comparison to scattering-based texture models (Bruna & Mallat) and a more systematic comparison to Portilla-Simoncelli would contextualize the “pre-DNN” state of the art.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: What filter sizes and counts were used per scale in the multi-scale model? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- Nonlinearities: Did you test alternatives to ReLU (e.g., leaky ReLU, absolute value) given their differing effects on Gram statistics?
- Color handling: How important is channel mixing? Do results change if filters operate on luminance only vs. RGB with cross-channel correlations?
- Optimization: How critical is the 10^7 scaling of the loss/gradients and the iteration budget? Do earlier stopping criteria improve variability without sacrificing too much perceptual quality?
- Initialization: How sensitive are results to initialization noise statistics (uniform vs. Gaussian) and dynamic range?
- Overfitting/finite-size effects: Can you provide additional evidence or experiments that distinguish exact reconstruction from genuine synthesis on small textures (e.g., cross-validation on disjoint patches)?
- Release: Will you release code and pretrained filter banks (for Fourier/PCA/k-means variants) to facilitate replication?
- Applications: Do these findings extend to style transfer (content+style) or only to pure texture synthesis? If tested, how does the multi-scale random layer behave when combined with a content loss?
