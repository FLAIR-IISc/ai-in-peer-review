This paper revisits the texture synthesis framework of Gatys et al., which matches empirical Gram matrices of deep feature activations, and investigates which architectures—deep or shallow—work best within this paradigm. A key and somewhat surprising finding is that very shallow networks, comprising a single convolutional filter bank with random weights, perform remarkably well. For simple and regular textures, these shallow models can even produce visually superior results compared to more complex, data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. The paper also provides a thoughtful discussion of the strengths and limitations of Gram-based methods for texture synthesis.

An especially interesting result, illustrated in Figure 4, is that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield synthesized textures that achieve lower VGG-19 Gram losses than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective. The authors reasonably attribute this to the optimization challenges posed by the highly non-linear VGG-19 loss landscape. The new supplementary material further strengthens this point by showing that initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with solutions from the shallow network leads to improved results. This is a useful and practical insight: it suggests that shallow models can serve as effective initializers or priors for more complex objectives.

The main limitation is the lack of a systematic, quantitative evaluation across methods. While it is straightforward to define statistics that reproduce an exact copy of a reference texture (which would be shallow and trivially achieve high visual fidelity), the true goal is to capture a distribution of textures. Measuring how well different methods meet this goal remains an open problem. As a result, although the empirical results suggest that simple statistics suffice for both quality and diversity—sometimes outperforming more complex alternatives—it is difficult to conclusively validate this claim without rigorous evaluation.

The authors propose measuring diversity via entropy, which is conceptually sound but practically challenging, as they acknowledge. Moreover, diversity alone does not address perceptual quality. The suggestion to conduct a psychophysical assessment seems well-motivated and may be the most practical path forward for evaluating both fidelity and variability in synthesized textures. However, this remains future work.

Despite these shortcomings, the paper makes a clear and impactful contribution: it challenges the assumption that deeper, data-adapted features are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis and highlights the importance of optimization in achieving good results. The evidence that shallow, random filter banks can be competitive—combined with the practical finding that they can bootstrap better optimization for deeper models—is valuable for the community.

Recommendation: accept. The paper raises an important question about the complexity required for effective texture synthesis and provides strong empirical evidence and insights into optimization behavior. To further strengthen the work, I encourage the authors to:
- Include at least a small-scale psychophysical study (e.g., pairwise preference tests) to complement the qualitative figures.
- Provide any feasible proxy measures of diversity (even if imperfect) and report their limitations transparently.
- Clarify the optimization setup and hyperparameters to facilitate reproducibility, especially for the VGG-19 vs. shallow objective comparison.

Overall, given the difficulty of evaluating generative image models, the paper’s conceptual clarity, empirical observations, and practical recommendations justify publication. A modest psychophysical assessment would substantially bolster the conclusions that are currently supported primarily by visual inspection of figures in the main paper and supplementary material.
