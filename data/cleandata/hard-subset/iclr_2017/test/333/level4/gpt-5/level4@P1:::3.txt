1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates which aspects of deep feature representations are truly necessary for parametric texture synthesis based on Gram (correlation) statistics. Building on the framework of Gatys et al., the authors show that surprisingly strong results can be obtained without depth, pooling, or supervised training: a single convolutional layer with random filters can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality. They systematically compare a variety of single-layer models (random, hand-crafted Fourier, and unsupervised filters such as k-means/PCA; different numbers of feature maps; single- vs multi-scale) and evaluate them both visually and via a VGG-based loss used as a proxy for perceptual quality. They also provide a valuable discussion of optimization effects (e.g., L-BFGS-B settings, loss scaling) and show that initializing VGG-based synthesis with outputs from the shallow model improves optimization. Finally, they connect their empirical findings to a broader discussion of maximum-entropy modeling, the balance between perceptual similarity and sample diversity, and the need for better quantitative measures (including potential psychophysical assessments).

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- The core empirical finding—that a minimal, single-layer random filter bank competes with (and sometimes surpasses) deep, supervised features for texture synthesis—is novel, carefully demonstrated, and practically impactful as a strong baseline.
- The paper offers clear analysis and insights into why this works (multi-scale coverage, role of nonlinearity, optimization considerations), and situates the work thoughtfully within the literature and theory.

3. Supporting arguments
- The experiments comprehensively cover different single-layer architectures and filter sets, demonstrating that:
  - Increasing the number of feature maps improves quality.
  - Multi-scale random filters capture small-, mid-, and long-range structures better than any single scale.
  - ReLUs are essential; removing nonlinearity degrades synthesis.
- Figure 4 shows that optimizing a shallow model can produce images with lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG objective, plausibly due to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-convex multi-layer loss. The new supplementary results showing that VGG-based optimization benefits from initialization with shallow-model outputs strengthen this interpretation and are a useful contribution.
- The paper is well positioned in the context of prior texture models (Portilla & Simoncelli), scattering-based approaches, and modern deep methods (Gatys et al., feedforward texture networks). It makes a compelling case that depth and supervised pretraining are not indispensable for this task—at least for textures—and that optimization and multi-scale coverage are crucial.
- The discussion of the MaxEnt framework and finite-size effects (e.g., possibility of exact reconstructions in over-constrained settings) is thoughtful and highlights the importance of allowing soft constraints and better distance metrics.

Limitations:
- The paper does not provide a systematic, quantitative evaluation of both perceptual fidelity and sample diversity. While the use of a VGG-based loss is reasonable as a proxy for perceptual similarity, it is known to be imperfect, especially for highly regular man-made textures. Diversity (entropy) is acknowledged as important but remains unmeasured, beyond qualitative examples.
- The known risk of trivial shallow statistics that simply copy the reference underscores the need for robust diversity assessment. Without such measures, it remains difficult to conclusively verify that simple statistics achieve a strong balance of quality and diversity across textures.

4. Suggestions to improve the paper (not required for acceptance)
- Evaluation:
  - Add a simple psychophysical task (e.g., patch inpainting discrimination or ABX tests) to jointly assess perceptual fidelity and diversity. Even a small-scale study would substantively strengthen claims.
  - Complement the VGG-based metric with additional analyses, such as human preference comparisons or a retrieval-style evaluation (e.g., distinguishing real vs synthesized patches).
  - Provide variability metrics, even approximate: e.g., pairwise VGG-loss variance across multiple samples per texture; coverage of local patch histograms; or a simple proxy for entropy via sample diversity in feature space.
- Fairness and ablations:
  - Control for the number of parameters and receptive field when comparing single-scale vs multi-scale to isolate the value of multi-scale aggregation.
  - Report sensitivity to filter distribution (e.g., Gaussian vs uniform random), normalization, and color handling (per-channel vs joint).
  - Include runtime and memory comparisons; discuss the trade-off between shallow large-kernel vs deep small-kernel hierarchies.
- Clarifications:
  - Detail the multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, counts per scale), exact normalization of Gram matrices, and boundary handling (padding).
  - Discuss more explicitly why random features suffice: e.g., coverage from many random filters, overlaps of convolutional patches, and parallels to random features or scattering moments.
  - Address failure cases, especially man-made regular structures where VGG-loss may be misleading, and analyze why the multi-scale random model sometimes outperforms VGG optimization even under the VGG metric.

Questions for the authors
- Diversity: Can you report variability across multiple samples per texture using the VGG-loss distribution (mean/variance) and perhaps a simple patch-level diversity measure? Are there cases where the method collapses to near-duplicates?
- Optimization: How sensitive are results to the optimizer (e.g., L-BFGS-B vs Adam) and to the gradient/loss scaling (10^7)? Do different stopping criteria yield better diversity-quality trade-offs?
- Architecture: What is the exact per-scale filter allocation in the multi-scale model? How do results change if you match parameter counts/receptive fields across models?
- Random filters: How robust are outcomes to the random seed and to the choice of distribution or normalization for the filters? Do learned filters (e.g., k-means) ever win decisively on particular texture classes?
- Evaluation metric: For highly regular textures where VGG-loss seems misaligned with perception, have you considered layer reweighting or alternative deep features? Does the conclusion (random single-layer competitiveness) hold under such variants?
- Generalization: How does the approach scale to higher-resolution textures or to non-stationary patterns? Could the same insight (random shallow features + Gram statistics) help in style transfer or other image generation tasks beyond textures?

Overall, despite the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, the paper presents a strong, clear result with practical value and sound analysis. A modest psychophysical evaluation and more systematic diversity assessment would further solidify the claims.
