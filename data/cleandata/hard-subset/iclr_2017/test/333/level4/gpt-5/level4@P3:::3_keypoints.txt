- Single-layer CNNs with random filters can synthesize high-quality textures, sometimes rivaling or surpassing deep pretrained models.  
- Authors systematically compare single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA) and introduce a multi-scale random model.  
- They keep Gatys-style Gram matching objective and vary representations to isolate architecture/training effects.  
- Propose using VGG-based Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity.  
- Discuss fidelity vs variability and relate synthesis to maximum-entropy modeling for finite images.  
- Decision: Accept — main finding is novel and clarifying: depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not necessary for Gram-based texture synthesis.  
- Strong empirical evidence and careful analysis provide a useful minimal baseline.  
- Single-layer random models with 11×11 filters capture small-to-mid scale structure; increasing feature maps improves quality.  
- Random filters often perform on par with or better than learned filters.  
- Multi-scale random model significantly improves long-range structure by combining receptive field sizes.  
- Ablations show small filters capture color marginals, medium capture mid-scale structure, large capture layout but may miss fine detail.  
- Nonlinearities (ReLU) are essential; removing them degrades synthesis quality.  
- VGG-based loss generally correlates with visual quality; sometimes shallow synthesis attains lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization.  
- Initializing VGG-based synthesis from shallow outputs can improve optimization results.  
- Exact constraint matching can collapse variability for finite images; soft constraints and ε-neighborhood metrics are preferable.  
- Limitation: Evaluation relies primarily on VGG-loss and lacks robust diversity/entropy metrics.  
- Limitation: Computational cost — large spatial filters in the multi-scale model are expensive; hierarchical implementations can be more efficient.  
- Suggestion: Report quantitative diversity (e.g., distributions of VGG distances, pairwise feature distances) across multiple samples.  
- Suggestion: Run small-scale psychophysical tests (e.g., 2AFC) to substantiate perceptual claims.  
- Suggestion: Provide optimization sensitivity analysis (L-BFGS-B scaling, iteration count, stopping criteria) and fairness of comparison details (compute budgets, multi-starts).  
- Suggestion: Detail multi-scale design (filter sizes, counts, normalization) and consider dilated/separable convolutions for efficiency.  
- Suggestion: Release code, random seeds, and texture lists to aid reproducibility.  
- Questions: How sensitive are results to number of feature maps vs filter size; is there a scaling law?  
- Questions: How many independent synthesis runs per texture were used and how variable are outputs visually and under VGG-loss?  
- Questions: Did authors test average vs max pooling or other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU), and their effects?  
- Questions: How does image resolution affect the advantage of multi-scale random models?  
- Questions: What is the impact of different input normalizations on synthesis quality?  
- Questions: Can authors quantify computational cost differences between single-layer multi-scale and hierarchical implementations?  
- Questions: When multi-scale random models outperform VGG under VGG-loss, is there over-smoothing or reduced diversity beyond the loss values?  
- Overall: Clear, well-motivated analysis with compelling empirical results that advances understanding and provides a valuable minimal baseline.
