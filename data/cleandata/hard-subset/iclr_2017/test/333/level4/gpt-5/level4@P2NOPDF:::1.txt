This paper proposes a simple yet strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis. In experiments, a single-layer model using multi-scale random filters sometimes rivals a VGG-based approach with pretrained multi-layer features. Based on these results, the authors argue that effective texture synthesis does not necessarily require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps.

The paper is interesting and insightful, but the central claims need stronger support, particularly regarding the necessity (or lack thereof) of deep hierarchical representations.

- First, outputs from both the VGG-based and the single-layer models are imperfect and substantially worse than those produced by non-parametric methods. This gap weakens the force of the conclusion that depth and learned features are unnecessary.

- Second, the VGG-based model appears to outperform the baseline on the inpainting task (Figure 7), suggesting that hierarchical, learned features may still confer advantages in certain settings.

- Finally, rather than using many filters of different sizes, would a hierarchical model handle multi-scale structure more efficiently? A discussion or comparison along these lines would help clarify whether the proposed baseline is truly competitive in capturing multi-scale statistics or if depth offers a more parsimonious mechanism.

Overall, the work provides a valuable and thought-provoking baseline, but additional evidence and analysis are needed to substantiate the broader conclusions about the role of depth and learned features in texture synthesis.
