1. Summary of contributions
- The paper investigates which ingredients of deep feature representations are actually necessary for natural texture synthesis. It presents a minimal, single-layer CNN with no pooling and randomly initialized filters that, using Gram-matrix statistics, can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality. 
- The authors benchmark a variety of single-layer settings (random, Fourier basis, hand-crafted, PCA, k-means on ImageNet and on the target texture; small vs large filter banks; single-scale vs multi-scale), and show that depth and supervised pretraining are not essential. 
- A multi-scale single-layer variant further improves results, capturing small-, mid-, and long-range structure when combined with ReLU nonlinearities. 
- For evaluation, the paper adopts a VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, and it discusses the trade-off between perceptual fidelity and sample diversity, relating practical synthesis to maximum-entropy principles.

2. Decision
Accept. 
Primary reasons: (i) the central empirical finding—that a simple, single-layer random-filter model can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis—is both surprising and impactful; (ii) the ablation studies are careful and illuminate what matters (multi-scale coverage and nonlinearity) versus what does not (depth, supervised training), thereby clarifying a widely used methodology.

3. Supporting arguments
- The comparisons span multiple filter constructions and cardinalities (363 vs 3267 vs 1024 multi-scale), showing the effect of feature count and filter scale on perceptual quality. Random filters perform on par with or better than learned or hand-crafted banks, especially in the multi-scale setup. 
- The analysis isolates key factors: combining scales is crucial (single large filters miss fine detail; single small filters miss long-range structure), and ReLU nonlinearities are necessary to capture dependencies. 
- The VGG-based quantitative metric, while imperfect, is a reasonable proxy to complement visual inspection and allows systematic reporting across many textures. 
- The work challenges prevailing assumptions used in style transfer/texture synthesis pipelines and offers a strong, efficient baseline for future research, even informing optimization practices (e.g., robust L-BFGS-B setup and gradient scaling).

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Evaluation breadth and balance:
  - Consider adding a small-scale human study (pairwise similarity judgments or an inpainting discrimination task) to calibrate the VGG loss as a perceptual proxy, especially for highly regular man-made textures where discrepancies were observed.
  - Report simple variability diagnostics (e.g., intra-model sample distances under the VGG metric across multiple random initializations) to complement the discussion on similarity vs diversity.
- Reproducibility and clarity:
  - Provide full implementation details for the multi-scale model: exact scales, number of feature maps per scale, filter-size distribution, and initialization distributions. 
  - Document sensitivity to optimization choices (gradient scaling factor, iteration budget, box constraints, and initialization). A brief ablation on these would help practitioners.
  - Clarify preprocessing (channel-wise mean subtraction, whitening choices) and its impact (e.g., Kmeans-NonWhite vs whitened).
- Comparisons and metrics:
  - Where VGG-synthesized results underperform the proposed multi-scale method under the VGG loss, add a short analysis to explain the “paradox” (e.g., local minima, loss-landscape issues, or reduced variability).
  - Consider reporting runtime and memory profiles for single-scale vs multi-scale vs deeper hierarchical variants to contextualize the “efficiency vs quality” trade-offs.
- Theory and framing:
  - The MaxEnt discussion is insightful; adding a concrete epsilon-environment definition tied to the VGG metric (and a recommended epsilon range) would make the framework more actionable.

Questions for the authors
- Robustness and sensitivity:
  - How sensitive are results to the number of random feature maps at each scale? Is there a diminishing returns regime?
  - How does image resolution affect synthesis quality and the need for larger filters or additional scales?
  - How sensitive is performance to the choice of nonlinearity (ReLU vs leaky-ReLU or ELU)?
- Optimization details:
  - How critical is the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling for L-BFGS-B convergence? Would Adam or LBFGS without scaling yield similar minima?
  - Do different random initializations meaningfully change the diversity of outcomes, and can an early stopping schedule trade off similarity for variability in a controlled way?
- Evaluation:
  - For textures where VGG loss does not match perceived quality (e.g., regular grids/walls), can you provide an analysis or propose alternative descriptors that better capture regularity?
  - Can you report quantitative variability across samples (e.g., distribution of VGG distances among synthesized images) to substantiate the similarity–variability discussion?
- Scope and generalization:
  - Do the conclusions extend beyond stationary textures (e.g., near-regular textures or stochastic textures with weak non-stationarities)?
  - In deeper random CNNs, you note occasional exact reconstructions. Can you characterize when this occurs and how to avoid degeneracy while retaining high perceptual quality?
