- Core contribution: A single convolutional layer with random filters can synthesize high-quality textures using Gram statistics, rivaling deep supervised features.
- Main empirical finding: Depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not necessary for strong parametric texture synthesis.
- Experimental scope: Systematic comparison of single-layer models (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA), varying feature counts and single- vs multi-scale designs.
- Key results: Increasing number of feature maps improves quality.
- Key results: Multi-scale random filters capture small-, mid-, and long-range structures better than single-scale filters.
- Key results: Nonlinearity (ReLU) is essential; removing it degrades synthesis.
- Optimization insight: Shallow-model optimization can yield lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG optimization, likely due to easier optimization of the shallow objective.
- Practical tip: Initializing VGG-based synthesis with outputs from the shallow model improves VGG optimization.
- Theoretical framing: Connects findings to maximum-entropy modeling, trade-offs between perceptual similarity and sample diversity, and finite-size effects.
- Positioning: Well situated relative to prior work (Portilla & Simoncelli, scattering, Gatys et al., feedforward texture nets) and offers a strong baseline.
- Limitation: No systematic quantitative evaluation of perceptual fidelity and sample diversity; VGG-loss is an imperfect proxy, especially for regular man-made textures.
- Limitation: Diversity (entropy) is acknowledged but remains unmeasured beyond qualitative examples, risking trivial copying of references.
- Suggested evaluation: Add psychophysical tests (e.g., ABX or patch discrimination) to jointly assess fidelity and diversity.
- Suggested metrics: Complement VGG-loss with human preference studies, retrieval-style evaluations, and variability/entropy proxies (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss variance).
- Suggested ablations: Control for parameter count and receptive field when comparing single- vs multi-scale; report sensitivity to filter distribution, normalization, and color handling.
- Suggested practical analysis: Include runtime and memory comparisons and discuss trade-offs between shallow large-kernel vs deep small-kernel designs.
- Clarifications requested: Detail multi-scale architecture, Gram normalization, and boundary handling.
- Explanation sought: More explicit reasoning for why random features suffice (coverage arguments, parallels to random features/scattering).
- Failure analysis: Address cases where VGG-loss misleads (highly regular textures) and why multi-scale random models sometimes outperform VGG optimization under the VGG metric.
- Questions for authors: Report VGG-loss mean/variance across samples, patch-level diversity, and instances of mode collapse or near-duplicates.
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to optimizer choice (L-BFGS-B vs Adam), loss/gradient scaling, and stopping criteria affecting diversity-quality trade-offs.
- Questions for authors: Exact per-scale filter allocations and results when matching parameter counts/receptive fields across models.
- Questions for authors: Robustness to random seed, filter distribution/normalization, and whether learned filters ever decisively outperform random ones.
- Questions for authors: Consider alternative deep-feature weightings or metrics for regular textures; does the main conclusion hold under such variants?
- Generalization question: How does the method scale to higher-resolution or non-stationary textures, and can it extend to style transfer or other generation tasks?
