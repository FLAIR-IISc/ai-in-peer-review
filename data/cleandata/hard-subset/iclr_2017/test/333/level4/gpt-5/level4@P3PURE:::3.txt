This paper builds on the framework of Gatys et al., which showed that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. It investigates in detail which kinds of deep or shallow networks work well within this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks—consisting of a single filter bank with random weights—perform surprisingly well and, for simple and regular textures, can produce results that are visually superior to those from complex, data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an interesting and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can produce textures with a lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the supplementary material, they further show that VGG-19-based optimization improves when initialized with the shallow network’s output, which usefully complements the original experiments.

The main limitation is the absence of a systematic, quantitative comparison against a well-defined objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that would allow one to exactly reproduce any reference texture—with very good visual quality—and such statistics could also be very shallow. The real aim is to capture a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Consequently, while the empirical results appear to support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (relative to more complex statistics), it is difficult to confirm this conclusively.

The authors suggest measuring diversity in terms of entropy. This is reasonable but, as they acknowledge in their responses, difficult to implement in practice. Moreover, it would not address the complementary issue of visual quality. They also propose a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical approach, but regard it as future work.

Overall, because evaluation of image generation is a hard problem, the paper’s contributions and insights are, in my view, sufficient to warrant publication at ICLR. Nevertheless, some form of psychophysical assessment would be valuable to substantiate the current intuitions that otherwise rely on inspecting the figures in the paper and supplementary material.
