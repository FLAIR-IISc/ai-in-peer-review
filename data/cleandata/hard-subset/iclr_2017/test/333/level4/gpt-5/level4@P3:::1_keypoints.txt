- Paper proposes a simple texture synthesis baseline: single-layer CNN with ReLU, no pooling, random (or non–task-trained) filters, and Gram-matrix statistics.
- Authors perform broad ablations: filter types (random, k-means, PCA, Fourier), single-scale vs. multi-scale, with/without ReLU, and varying channel counts.
- Optimization and evaluation use a consistent L-BFGS-B setup and a VGG-based texture loss to quantify quality.
- Key empirical claims: depth and supervised pretraining are not required for competitive texture synthesis.
- Key empirical claims: multi-scale random filters significantly improve quality over single-scale single-layer models.
- Key empirical claims: ReLU nonlinearity and sufficient channel count are important for quality.
- Conceptual contribution: connects texture synthesis to a softened maximum-entropy viewpoint and discusses perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-offs.
- Decision: paper rejected.
- Primary reason: evidence does not sufficiently support the strongest claims due to reliance on a VGG-derived loss and limited human/perceptual testing.
- Primary reason: efficiency/architecture claim is underdeveloped; multi-scale single layer uses very large kernels and may be computationally heavy.
- Supporting: problem motivation and relation to prior work are timely and well-situated.
- Supporting: baseline and breadth of single-layer variants are well specified and valuable.
- Concern: qualitative results are mixed; structured, highly regular textures often remain imperfect compared to other methods.
- Concern: evaluation circularity—VGG loss is used both for optimization/comparator and as the main metric, risking bias.
- Concern: variability vs. fidelity trade-off is identified but not quantitatively measured or validated with human studies.
- Concern: multi-scale model uses very large filters (up to 55×55) without detailed runtime/memory or controlled comparisons to hierarchical models.
- Presentation issues: reference formatting inconsistencies and missing bibliographic details.
- Presentation issues: some architectural and experimental details (multi-scale config, channels per scale, image resolutions) are insufficiently tabulated.
- Methodological issue: optimization choices (e.g., scaling gradients by 1e7) may affect convergence and fairness; ablations on optimizer settings are missing.
- Suggested improvement: add psychophysical studies (oddity or 2AFC) to validate VGG-loss correlation with human judgments.
- Suggested improvement: compare against modern non-parametric patch-based baselines on structured textures and report perceptual and diversity metrics.
- Suggested improvement: report synthesis diversity (pairwise VGG-loss or pixel-space stats across seeds) to quantify variability.
- Suggested improvement: provide controlled comparisons to hierarchical multi-layer random CNNs (small kernels) with runtime/memory benchmarks.
- Suggested improvement: detail sensitivity to initialization, iterations, optimizer hyperparameters, and justify gradient scaling.
- Suggested improvement: temper claims to state depth/training are not necessary in many cases unless stronger evidence is provided.
- Questions for authors: report consistency across random seeds (mean±std VGG-loss and qualitative failure rates).
- Questions for authors: provide exact multi-scale architecture, channels per scale, and total parameter count, and show effects of redistributing channels.
- Questions for authors: compare quality and speed to shallow hierarchical random CNNs with matched receptive fields.
- Questions for authors: show how results scale with input resolution and identify textures where single-layer fails vs. deeper models.
- Questions for authors: run a standardized perceptual test on a held-out texture set to validate VGG-loss correlation with humans.
- Questions for authors: clarify importance of 1e7 loss/gradient scaling for L-BFGS-B and whether it differentially affects convergence across models.
