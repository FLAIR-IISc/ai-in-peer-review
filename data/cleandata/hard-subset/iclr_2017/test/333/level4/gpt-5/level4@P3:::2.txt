1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper investigates which aspects of deep neural representations are actually necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis. Contrary to prevailing assumptions, the authors show that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining are indispensable. They demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with ReLU nonlinearity and random filters can synthesize textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality. A multi-scale variant (concatenating filters of several sizes) further improves long-range structure while retaining small- and mid-scale fidelity. The paper evaluates different single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA; 11×11 filters; 363 vs. 3267 feature maps), provides ablations on scale and nonlinearity, and uses a VGG-based Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity. The discussion connects these findings to maximum entropy modeling and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. Overall, it establishes a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis and clarifies which ingredients are truly essential.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear and surprising insight with strong empirical support: single-layer, random-filter CNNs can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis on many examples, challenging the perceived necessity of depth and pretraining.
- Thorough ablations (filter types, number of maps, scale, ReLU) and principled discussion provide a well-motivated contribution that will influence how the community evaluates and designs texture generators.

3. Supporting arguments
- Specific question/problem: What properties of deep feature representations are essential for natural texture synthesis? Is depth, pooling, or supervised training required?
- Motivation and placement: The work is well positioned relative to Gatys et al. (Gram statistics over deep features), classical parametric models (Portilla & Simoncelli), and contemporary generative models. It explicitly tests the necessity of elements commonly assumed to matter.
- Empirical support and rigor:
  - Architecture sweep: Multiple filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), two capacities (363 vs. 3267 maps), consistent preprocessing, and a multi-scale variant. The finding that random filters perform on par with learned/hand-crafted filters is compelling.
  - Ablations: Multi-scale vs. large single-scale filters; demonstration that ReLU is crucial; analysis that multi-scale combinations are key to capturing both small/mid- and long-range structure.
  - Evaluation: Uses VGG-based Gram losses across five layers as a quantitative proxy and complements it with qualitative comparisons across 20 textures. The observation that multi-scale random sometimes achieves even lower VGG loss than the VGG-optimized synthesis is noteworthy and prompts useful discussion about optimization and variability.
  - Optimization details: L-BFGS-B with constrained pixels, explicit mention of gradient scaling to avoid early stopping, and comparison showing that initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer outputs improves results.
- Limitations acknowledged and discussed: Dependence on a VGG-based metric (potential circularity), limited treatment of variability vs. similarity, and computational inefficiency of large single-layer filters. The authors’ discussion on MaxEnt and the need to model an ε-neighborhood rather than hard constraints is thoughtful and advances the discourse.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Evaluation robustness:
  - While the VGG-loss is a reasonable proxy, consider a small-scale human study (e.g., 2AFC on texture discrimination or inpainting tests) on a subset of textures to corroborate the metric.
  - Report variability across multiple random seeds for the random-filter models: distribution of VGG losses and qualitative diversity of samples relative to the reference (to quantify the similarity–variability trade-off).
  - Provide runtime and computational cost comparisons (wall-clock time, memory) for single-scale, multi-scale, and VGG-based models to substantiate the efficiency discussion.
- Fairness and sensitivity:
  - Analyze sensitivity to number of feature maps and filter-size allocation in the multi-scale model (scaling laws). How does quality improve with capacity?
  - Detail the filter normalization and color handling across Fourier/k-means/PCA filters to ensure comparisons are apples-to-apples (e.g., unit-norm filters, DC removal specifics).
  - Explore alternative layer weightings in the VGG evaluation loss (currently averaged) to test robustness of conclusions.
- Optimization choices:
  - Justify the 1e7 scaling of the loss/gradients for L-BFGS-B and show results without scaling. Does scaling affect convergence to different local minima?
  - Clarify whether pixel constraints are enforced by projection or bounds within the optimizer, and whether clamping introduces artifacts.
- Scope and generalization:
  - Discuss failure modes: textures with strong global geometry or non-stationarity, and how multi-scale random filters behave there.
  - Consider a brief extension to style transfer or non-texture images to delineate the limits of the random, single-layer approach.
- Reproducibility:
  - Provide code, pretrained filter banks (where applicable), and a script to reproduce the main figures and evaluation.

Questions for the authors
- How many random seeds were used for each model, and what is the variance in VGG-loss and perceptual quality across seeds? Can you quantify sample diversity for a fixed reference texture?
- What are the exact multi-scale settings (filter sizes used, number of maps per scale, normalization per scale)? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- Do results persist when using different VGG configurations (e.g., max vs. avg pooling, different layer weightings) for evaluation? Is there evidence of overfitting the synthesis to the evaluation metric?
- How do runtime and memory compare among single-scale, multi-scale, and VGG-based synthesis for the same target resolution and number of iterations?
- Could you report ablations on the gradient scaling factor and the maximum iterations to show stability of conclusions?
- For the k-means and PCA filters, are filters whitened/normalized post hoc? Are color-channel statistics matched across methods?
- Can you provide additional evidence on variability (e.g., an inpainting-based psychophysical test or quantitative proxy) to support the discussion on ε-environments and MaxEnt?
- Beyond ReLU, did you test other nonlinearities (e.g., leaky ReLU, absolute value) and do they affect performance or stability?

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated, and insightful paper that establishes a valuable minimal baseline and sharpens our understanding of what is necessary for texture synthesis. I recommend acceptance.
