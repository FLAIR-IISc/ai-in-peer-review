1. Summary of claims and contributions (positive)
- The paper interrogates which aspects of deep feature representations are actually required for texture synthesis within the Gram-matrix framework of Gatys et al. It shows that even extremely shallow architectures—a single convolutional layer with random filters and no pooling—can produce textures of high perceptual quality, and for simple/regular textures sometimes outperform synthesis based directly on VGG-19 features.
- The authors systematically explore single-layer architectures (random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned filters) and a multi-scale random variant, demonstrating that increasing the number of feature maps and combining multiple receptive field sizes improve results, and that ReLU non-linearities are essential.
- They provide an informative discussion on why shallow random features work (Gram statistics induce a stationary representation capturing rich correlations), on the optimization challenges of deep objectives, and on the broader issue of balancing perceptual similarity versus sample variability. They also propose using a VGG-based loss as a practical proxy for perceptual similarity and highlight the need for better quantitative measures (e.g., entropy, psychophysics) to assess diversity.
- Empirically, Figure 4 shows cases where optimizing images against shallow/random feature Grams can yield lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective, plausibly due to optimization difficulty in the deep, highly non-linear space. The supplementary experiments demonstrate that initializing VGG-based synthesis from shallow-network outputs improves the final result, supporting this explanation.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong and surprising empirical finding: a minimal single-layer, random-filter baseline can rival (and sometimes exceed) state-of-the-art deep, trained-feature methods in texture synthesis, with clear practical and conceptual implications.
- Insightful analysis connecting architecture choices, optimization behavior, and the similarity–variability trade-off, plus a useful pragmatic contribution (initialization strategy) that enhances deep-feature optimization.

3. Supporting arguments
- The study is carefully executed across multiple single-layer configurations and a multi-scale variant, revealing consistent trends: more feature maps improve synthesis; multi-scale random filters capture longer-range structure without sacrificing fine detail; non-linearities are necessary.
- The observation that shallow optimization can achieve lower VGG-based loss than direct VGG optimization is convincing and is corroborated by improved outcomes when seeding the VGG optimization with shallow results.
- The paper does not claim that shallow statistics universally dominate; instead, it demonstrates that for many textures—especially regular, man-made patterns—simple models suffice, reframing prior assumptions about the indispensability of depth and supervised pretraining for this task.
- Acknowledged limitation: the work does not deliver a comprehensive, quantitative assessment of diversity or distributional fidelity. While the qualitative evidence is strong and aligns with intuition, conclusively establishing that simple statistics capture both quality and diversity remains open. The authors appropriately discuss entropy-based measures and psychophysical evaluation as avenues forward, but these are not yet included.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the decision)
- Including a lightweight psychophysical evaluation (e.g., 2AFC discrimination or an inpainting-based Turing test) on a small set of textures would substantively support claims about perceptual quality and help calibrate the VGG-based proxy.
- Report simple diversity proxies across multiple independent syntheses per texture (e.g., dispersion of VGG-loss, within-texture LPIPS variance, or patch-level coverage analyses) to complement the similarity metrics and illustrate the similarity–variability trade-off.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol to ease reproduction: exact VGG configuration (layers, pooling type), loss normalization, optimization settings (L-BFGS-B stopping criteria, gradient scaling), and any preprocessing. Briefly discuss compute/runtime and memory costs for single-layer 55×55 convolutions versus hierarchical speedups.
- If space permits, add a quantitative comparison to a classical baseline (e.g., Portilla–Simoncelli) under the same VGG-based metric, to contextualize the gains from the proposed minimal model.
- Minor: ensure consistent reference formatting and fix any citation placeholders.

Questions for the authors
- Diversity: Beyond qualitative inspection, did you quantify variability across multiple runs (e.g., VGG-loss dispersion or LPIPS between independently synthesized samples)? If so, what trends did you observe relative to VGG-based synthesis?
- Optimization: Which alternatives to L-BFGS-B (Adam, LBFGS with different line search, multi-start strategies) did you try for the VGG objective, and do they narrow the gap with shallow optimization? How sensitive are your results to the loss/gradient scaling factor?
- Multi-scale design: How does performance vary with the number of feature maps per scale and the specific set of filter sizes? Is there a minimal multi-scale configuration that captures most of the gains?
- ReLU necessity: Did you test other non-linearities (e.g., leaky ReLU, absolute value, cosine) and do any offer advantages in stability, diversity, or fidelity?
- Evaluation scope: For textures where the VGG-based loss appears misaligned with perceptual judgments (e.g., highly regular grids), can you provide additional qualitative or task-based evidence to substantiate the observed advantage of the multi-scale shallow model?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, filters, and seeds to replicate all figures, including the initialization experiments for VGG-based synthesis?
