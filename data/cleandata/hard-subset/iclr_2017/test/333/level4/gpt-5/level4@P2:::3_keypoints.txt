- Paper shows single-layer random convolutional filter banks with Gram statistics can synthesize high-quality natural textures, sometimes rivaling VGG-19 results.
- Ablations systematically compare random, hand-crafted (Fourier), PCA, and k-means filters across single-layer architectures.
- Multi-scale filters and nonlinearities (ReLU) are crucial for capturing local and long-range texture structure.
- VGG-based synthesis loss is proposed and used as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity.
- The work frames findings within a maximum-entropy perspective and analyzes the perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-off.
- Initializing VGG-based optimization with shallow-model outputs improves synthesis results and mitigates optimization issues.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reasoning: Provides a strong, minimal baseline with clear ablations that challenge the need for deep supervised features and add conceptual and practical value.
- Problem is clearly stated and timely, grounded in prior literature (Julesz, Portilla & Simoncelli, Gatys et al.).
- Empirical support: single-layer random filters often match perceptual quality of deep models across many exemplars.
- Empirical support: multi-scale random banks outperform single-scale, and ReLU is shown essential.
- Empirical support: VGG-loss consistently correlates with perceptual quality and reveals cases where shallow models achieve lower VGG loss.
- Experimental rigor: multiple filter types/capacities tested, clear optimization protocol (L-BFGS-B), and broad qualitative results.
- Main limitation: Evaluation relies heavily on VGG-based loss and visual inspection; lacks quantitative diversity (entropy) or psychophysical validation.
- Main limitation: Missing comparisons to recent GAN- or autoregressive-based texture generators, limiting cross-paradigm context.
- Main limitation: Multi-scale approach can be computationally heavy; empirical runtime and memory metrics are missing.
- Main limitation: Cases where shallow models beat direct VGG optimization point to optimization brittleness needing deeper analysis.
- Suggestion: Include a small psychophysical study or proxy (e.g., 2AFC) to jointly assess perceptual similarity and variability.
- Suggestion: Report variability across seeds and per-texture distributions of VGG loss and perceptual diversity, with nearest-neighbor analyses.
- Suggestion: Provide runtime, memory, and iteration counts for single-scale vs. multi-scale and shallow vs. VGG synthesis for efficiency context.
- Suggestion: Give more architectural details for the multi-scale model (filter sizes, counts, normalization) and sensitivity analyses (features, ReLU).
- Suggestion: Clarify notation, normalization constants, fix typos, and ensure references are complete.
- Suggestion: Discuss failure cases and textures where VGG-loss poorly predicts perceptual quality (e.g., regular man-made patterns).
- Question: How sensitive are results to random weight distributions and scaling (Xavier vs. He, Gaussian vs. uniform) and per-filter normalization?
- Question: How are feature counts allocated across scales in the multi-scale model, and how does allocation affect quality and efficiency?
- Question: What is the variability across random seeds for the same texture, visually and in VGG-loss; any mode collapse tendencies?
- Question: Have alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, ELU) or per-feature normalization (instance/batch norm) been tried and how do they affect results?
- Question: Why can VGG-loss be lower for shallow-model samples than direct VGG optimization—better conditioning, smoother landscapes, or implicit regularization?
- Question: Do findings extend beyond stationary textures to style transfer or non-stationary patterns using spatially varying Gram statistics or patchwise aggregation?
