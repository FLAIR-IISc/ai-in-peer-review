The framework of Gatys et al. showed that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterisation of visual textures. This paper investigates in detail which kinds of deep or shallow networks perform well within this framework. One of the main findings is that very shallow networks—consisting of a single filter bank with random weights—work surprisingly well and, for simple and regular textures, can produce results that are visually superior to those obtained with complex, data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an interesting and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimising images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield texture images with a lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimising the VGG-19 objective. This is attributed to the difficulty of optimising the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the new supplementary material, the authors show that better optimisation results can be obtained by initialising the VGG-19-based optimisation with the shallow-network results, which usefully complements the original experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is the lack of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that allow the generation of an exact copy of any reference texture—hence achieving very high visual quality—and such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The real aim, however, is to capture a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. Thus, while the empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), it is difficult to confirm this conclusively.

The authors suggest measuring diversity in terms of entropy. This is reasonable but, as they acknowledge in their responses, difficult to do in practice. Moreover, it would still not account for the other key aspect of the problem: visual quality. They also suggest a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to address this issue, but they reserve this for future work.

Overall, since evaluating image generation is such a hard problem, I believe the paper has sufficient strengths to warrant publication at ICLR. Nevertheless, some form of psychophysical assessment would be valuable to substantiate the intuitions that, at present, can only be drawn from inspecting the figures in the paper and in the supplementary material.
