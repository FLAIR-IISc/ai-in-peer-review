Summary
The paper asks a clear and timely question: which aspects of deep representations are truly necessary to synthesize natural textures? It claims that high-quality textures can be generated without depth, pooling, or supervised training—using only a single convolutional layer, ReLUs, no pooling, and random filters—with Gram-matrix statistics as constraints. The authors provide a strong minimal baseline, extensive single-layer ablations (Fourier, k-means/PCA-learned, random; 363 vs. 3267 channels; with/without whitening; per-texture sampling), and a multi-scale random variant that markedly improves quality. They propose a quantitative evaluation using VGG-based Gram losses and discuss why multi-scale filters and nonlinearity are crucial, why optimization matters, and how perceptual similarity vs. variability interacts with MaxEnt perspectives. The work is carefully positioned relative to Gatys et al., Portilla–Simoncelli, and contemporaneous improvements.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated baseline overturns a widely held assumption (that depth and supervised pretraining are necessary), with thorough ablations and clear insights (multi-scale + ReLU are critical; training is not).
- The results are strong and broadly useful as a reference point for the community; the analysis is careful about limitations and connects to theory.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and positioning: The paper directly addresses a core question in neural texture synthesis/style transfer. It is well grounded in prior work and clarifies what is essential (Gram-based stationary statistics) versus incidental (depth, pooling, supervised features).
- Methodology: The synthesis pipeline is standard and clearly described (L-BFGS-B, constraints, scaling). The architectural ablations are comprehensive, spanning filter types, sizes, and counts. The multi-scale analysis convincingly demonstrates complementary roles of different receptive field sizes and the necessity of nonlinearity.
- Evidence: Qualitative comparisons across many textures are compelling, and the VGG-based loss provides a reasonable proxy for perceptual similarity, despite known imperfections. The observation that VGG synthesis improves when initialized with single-layer samples supports the optimization/landscape argument.
- Rigor and transparency: The paper openly discusses limitations of the evaluation metric, the trade-off between similarity and variability, and finite-size effects within MaxEnt frameworks. This strengthens credibility.

Caveats (not fatal)
- Evaluation leans heavily on VGG-based Gram losses, which can mis-rank certain highly regular textures and do not capture sample variability. A human study or a psychophysical task would strengthen claims.
- Computational efficiency: Large spatial filters in the multi-scale variant are costly; while the authors note hierarchical alternatives for speed, empirical runtime/memory comparisons are limited.
- Statistical robustness: Some claims (e.g., random vs. learned filters) would benefit from quantitative summaries over textures and seeds, not just illustrative figures.

Suggestions to improve (intended to help; not central to the accept decision)
- Evaluation:
  - Add a small-scale human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC on texture discrimination or inpainting-based tests) to complement the VGG loss.
  - Report variability: quantify diversity across multiple random initializations (e.g., feature-space dispersion) to document the similarity–variability trade-off.
  - Provide aggregate metrics across all textures and seeds (means, variances) and, where appropriate, simple significance tests.
- Method/reporting:
  - Include runtime and memory comparisons for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. hierarchical multi-layer variants; specify filter counts per scale.
  - Detail sensitivity to key hyperparameters (number of feature maps, filter sizes, loss scaling, optimizer choice).
  - Discuss failure cases and textures where VGG-based loss misaligns with perception.
  - Compare to fast feed-forward texture networks (e.g., Ulyanov et al.) in both quality and speed to situate practical trade-offs.
  - Consider other nonlinearities or mild pooling to see if conclusions hold.
  - Release code and evaluation scripts to establish this as a community baseline.

Questions for the authors
- Robustness:
  - How sensitive are results to the number of feature maps per scale and to the exact set of scales used? Is there a “sweet spot” balancing compute and quality?
  - How consistent are outcomes across optimization seeds and alternative optimizers (e.g., Adam)? Do you observe mode collapse (low variability) in some regimes?
- Evaluation:
  - Can you report aggregate VGG-loss statistics over all textures and seeds (with error bars) for random vs. learned filter banks to substantiate “on par or better” claims?
  - For textures where the VGG loss misaligns with perception, can you analyze which layers or statistics drive the discrepancy?
- Methodology:
  - Did you test other nonlinearities or light pooling/normalization (e.g., instance norm) and, if so, do the core conclusions persist?
  - Can the same single-layer random approach extend to style transfer, or does content preservation require depth?
- Practicality:
  - What are typical runtimes and memory footprints for the multi-scale model vs. VGG synthesis at common resolutions?
  - Will you release code and pretrained multi-scale configurations to facilitate adoption?

Overall, this paper delivers a compelling, well-argued minimal baseline that clarifies what it takes to generate natural textures. Despite evaluation limitations, the contributions are substantive and of broad interest. I recommend acceptance.
