Summary and contributions
The paper studies what aspects of convolutional feature representations are truly necessary for parametric texture synthesis. The authors propose a remarkably simple baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU, no pooling, and random (or otherwise non–task-trained) filters, using Gram matrices of feature maps as summary statistics. They conduct a broad set of ablations (random vs. k-means/PCA/Fourier filters; 11×11 single-scale vs. multi-scale; with/without ReLU; varying number of feature maps) and a consistent optimization setup (L-BFGS-B) to synthesize textures by matching Gram matrices. A VGG-based texture loss is used to quantify quality. Key empirical findings are: (1) depth and supervised pretraining are not required to produce competitive textures; (2) multi-scale random filters substantially improve quality over single-scale; and (3) ReLU nonlinearity and sufficient channel count are important. Conceptually, the paper connects practical texture synthesis to a softened maximum-entropy viewpoint and discusses the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Evidence does not yet sufficiently support the strongest claims. Evaluation is largely based on a VGG-derived loss closely related to the comparator, with limited human studies or rigorous perceptual testing; some qualitative examples remain imperfect, especially for structured textures.
- The efficiency/architecture claim is underdeveloped. The paper argues depth is not needed, yet the proposed multi-scale single layer relies on very large kernels and is computationally heavy; comparisons to truly hierarchical multi-layer random models (for efficiency and quality) are inconclusive or anecdotal.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The question—what components are indispensable for texture synthesis—is timely and well-motivated. Situating the work relative to Gatys et al., Portilla & Simoncelli, and scattering-based approaches is appropriate and clear.
- Methodology: The baseline is well specified, and the breadth of single-layer variants is a strength. The multi-scale random model is a compelling counterpoint to the narrative that depth and pretraining are crucial.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  - The core qualitative claim (“depth and training are not indispensable”) is plausible and interesting, but the empirical support is mixed. Some samples match or rival VGG-based results; others fall short, especially for highly regular, man-made patterns where non-parametric methods often excel.
  - The quantitative measure is the VGG-based loss. This introduces a circularity: it both defines the comparator’s objective and serves as the evaluation metric, potentially biasing comparisons. The paper acknowledges this and proposes inpainting-based evaluation in the appendix, where VGG appears stronger.
  - The variability vs. fidelity trade-off is identified but not measured. Without a perceptual variability metric or a systematic human study, it is hard to assess whether the proposed model overfits or collapses diversity for certain textures.
- Efficiency and architecture:
  - The single-layer multi-scale model requires very large filters (up to 55×55), which is expensive. The paper mentions testing hierarchical variants that are faster but “do not improve quality,” yet details, controlled comparisons, and runtime/memory measurements are missing. This weakens the conclusion that depth is unnecessary rather than merely less critical for quality given a fixed computational budget.
- Presentation and completeness:
  - The reference list contains formatting inconsistencies and missing details (e.g., years, venues). Some architectural details (exact multi-scale configuration, total channel counts per scale, and image resolutions used in each experiment) would benefit from clearer tabulation.
  - Optimization choices (e.g., scaling the loss/gradients by 1e7) could affect convergence and fairness across methods; an ablation on optimizer settings would increase confidence.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all tied to the acceptance decision)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Add a small but controlled psychophysical study (e.g., oddity or two-alternative forced-choice) across a representative texture set to validate the VGG-loss findings.
  - Compare against modern non-parametric patch-based baselines on structured textures and report both perceptual and diversity metrics.
  - Report synthesis diversity (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss or pixel-space statistics across multiple seeds) to quantify variability.
- Clarify architectural choices:
  - Precisely specify multi-scale settings (kernel sizes, number of filters per scale, total channels) and image sizes.
  - Provide controlled comparisons to hierarchical multi-layer random CNNs with small kernels achieving comparable receptive fields via stacking; include runtime and memory benchmarks.
- Fairness and robustness:
  - Examine sensitivity to initialization, number of iterations, and optimizer hyperparameters; justify the gradient scaling.
  - Consider adding simple regularizers (e.g., total variation) and report their effect on perceptual quality and artifacts.
- Scope of claims:
  - Temper the conclusion to “depth and supervised training are not necessary to obtain competitive quality in many cases,” rather than “none of these aspects are indispensable,” unless stronger evidence is provided.
- Writing and references:
  - Fix citation formatting, complete bibliographic entries, and ensure all datasets/code are accessible for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How consistent are the results across random seeds? Please report variability (mean ± std) of the VGG-based loss and qualitative failure rates over multiple initializations per texture.
- What is the exact multi-scale architecture (kernel sizes, channels per scale) and the total parameter count? How does the quality change when redistributing channels across scales?
- Can you provide systematic comparisons (quality and speed) to shallow hierarchical random CNNs with small kernels whose receptive fields match the large-kernel single-layer model?
- How do results scale with input resolution? Are there textures for which the single-layer model consistently fails (e.g., long-range global regularities) where deeper models succeed?
- Beyond the appendix inpainting example, can you include a standardized perceptual test on a held-out texture set to validate that VGG-loss correlates with human judgments for your setting?
- How critical is the 1e7 scaling of the loss/gradient for L-BFGS-B? Does this affect convergence differently across models, potentially confounding comparisons?
