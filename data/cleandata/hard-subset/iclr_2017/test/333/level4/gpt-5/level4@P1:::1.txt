Summary of contributions
- The paper asks what aspects of CNN representations are truly necessary for parametric texture synthesis. It claims that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining are indispensable: a single convolutional layer with random filters, especially in a multi-scale configuration, can synthesize textures of competitive perceptual quality.
- The authors systematically compare single-layer models using different filter sets (random, Fourier basis, PCA, k-means) and numbers of feature maps, and show that the number of feature maps and the inclusion of multiple spatial scales are key, while data-dependent training of filters is not.
- They propose using the VGG-based Gram-matrix loss as an objective quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, rather than relying solely on qualitative inspection.
- They analyze the roles of nonlinearity (ReLU) and scale, and discuss the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability within a MaxEnt perspective, noting practical implications for finite-size images and optimization.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evidence does not yet convincingly support the strong claim that deep hierarchical representations are unnecessary for texture synthesis; comparisons remain limited and at times contradictory to perceptual judgments.
2) The paper lacks a rigorous, fair evaluation against strong non-parametric baselines and shows that even the VGG-based model appears stronger under certain tests (e.g., the inpainting task in Figure 7).

Supporting arguments
- Both single-layer and VGG-based samples remain imperfect and, as indicated, are “much worse” than non-parametric methods. Without a head-to-head, controlled comparison to these non-parametric baselines (with human evaluation), it is difficult to assess the practical significance of the proposed minimal model.
- The main quantitative metric is the VGG-based loss, which is derived from the same feature representation used by the deep baseline. This creates potential bias and is not a definitive perceptual metric. The paper itself notes cases (regular, man-made structures) where lower VGG loss does not match human perception.
- The conclusion that depth is not necessary would benefit from stronger evidence that a shallow multi-scale model can match or exceed deep models across a broad, diverse set of textures under multiple, independent evaluation criteria (human psychophysics, inpainting, variability assessments). At present, the results are texture-dependent and mixed.
- The computational concerns behind the shallow multi-scale model are acknowledged (very large filters up to 55×55). The manuscript suggests hierarchical CNNs can recover efficiency, but does not show that this does not reintroduce the very properties (depth, multi-layer structure) it claims are unnecessary. This weakens the central narrative.
- The variability vs. similarity trade-off is central to texture synthesis. The manuscript leans toward maximizing similarity (easier optimization) and even reports anecdotal exact reconstructions in deeper random CNNs. Without a principled, quantitative treatment of variability, it is hard to conclude that the proposed model captures the texture distribution rather than overfitting to a reference’s statistics within a narrow epsilon-ball.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide a rigorous comparison to non-parametric state-of-the-art methods using human studies or well-designed psychophysical tasks. If inpainting is used (as in Figure 7), report systematic results across many textures with statistics and significance tests.
- Complement VGG-loss with additional, independent perceptual measures. Even if imperfect, a suite of metrics plus human studies can triangulate conclusions.
- Clarify fairness of comparisons: control for the number of feature maps/parameters across methods where possible; report runtime, FLOPs, and memory for all models; include ablations for ReLU, filter size distributions, and the optimization scaling factor (1e7) to show robustness.
- Investigate the sensitivity to random seeds and report variability across multiple runs. Provide aggregate statistics (mean/std) for perceptual metrics and loss.
- Expand the discussion of hierarchical efficiency: present concrete hierarchical architectures that match your single-layer multi-scale receptive fields, with empirical comparisons of quality and speed, to substantiate the efficiency claim.
- Discuss generalization across texture classes and provide a taxonomy analysis (e.g., stochastic vs. structured/man-made), since your own results suggest texture-dependent behavior.

Questions for the authors
- Non-parametric baselines: Which specific non-parametric methods were you comparing against when stating that both your method and VGG are “much worse”? Can you include controlled, quantitative comparisons (with human evaluations) to those methods?
- Metric choice: Given that VGG-loss is tied to the Gatys model, how do you mitigate bias in using it as the primary quantitative metric? Can you report additional metrics and human judgments to validate conclusions?
- Efficiency and hierarchy: Can you provide experimental evidence that a suitably designed hierarchical CNN with small kernels achieves similar quality to your multi-scale single-layer model while being more efficient, without relying on pretraining? How does this affect your claim that depth is not necessary?
- Variability: How variable are the samples from your single-layer models across different seeds and optimization restarts? Can you quantify variability (e.g., diversity metrics or human indistinguishability in an inpainting or oddity task)?
- Inpainting: In Figure 7, the VGG-based model seems to perform better. Can you report comprehensive inpainting results across many textures, and analyze when/why the single-layer model underperforms?
- Robustness to design choices: How sensitive are your results to the number of feature maps, the precise set of scales, and the presence of ReLU? Do your conclusions hold when the total parameter budget is fixed across models?
- Exact reconstructions: You mention instances where deeper random CNNs exactly reconstruct the reference. Under what conditions does this occur, and how does it affect the similarity–variability trade-off central to texture modeling?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful minimal baseline and raises thoughtful questions about what is essential for texture synthesis. Strengthening the empirical evaluation and clarifying the role of depth and efficiency would substantially improve its contribution.
