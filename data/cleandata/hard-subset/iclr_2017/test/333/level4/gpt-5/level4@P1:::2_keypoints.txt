- Main finding: High-quality textures can be synthesized without depth, pooling, or supervised training — even single-layer random CNNs suffice.
- Strong minimal baseline: Match Gram matrices of activations from a single-layer CNN (typically 11×11 filters, ReLU, stride 1) optimized from noise.
- Baseline comparisons: Random filters compared to Fourier, PCA, and k-means filters across small and large channel counts.
- Multi-scale model: Combining multiple filter sizes (up to 55×55) in a random single-layer significantly improves capture of short- to long-range statistics.
- Nonlinearity matters: Removing ReLU degrades synthesis quality.
- Evaluation metric: Use VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity; multi-scale random model sometimes outperforms VGG-based synthesis by this metric.
- Theoretical framing: Results interpreted in a maximum-entropy perspective emphasizing practical need for allowed mismatch ("epsilon-environments") to balance similarity and variability.
- Decision: Accept.
- Primary reason 1: Clear, surprising empirical result that single-layer random CNNs can rival VGG-based synthesis on many textures.
- Primary reason 2: Careful, controlled analysis isolating roles of depth, supervision, and multi-scale structure; multi-scale finding is practical and insightful.
- Significance: Addresses an important scientific and practical question about which components matter for texture synthesis and related generation tasks.
- Positioning: Well-situated relative to prior texture models (Portilla–Simoncelli), VGG-based synthesis, and generative models (GANs, PixelCNN); complements depth-added approaches by removing components.
- Empirical thoroughness: Extensive comparisons across filter types, channel counts, and a convincing ablation that multi-scale helps beyond larger receptive fields alone.
- Evaluation caution: VGG-loss is reasonable but imperfect; authors acknowledge and document divergences from perception.
- Claims calibrated: Conclusions are appropriately limited to texture synthesis and framed with discussion of variability vs. similarity.
- Reproducibility: Model details, optimization settings (L-BFGS-B, gradient scaling), and evaluation protocol are described sufficiently for replication.
- Suggestion — human evaluation: Add a small perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC or inpainting discrimination) to strengthen perceptual claims.
- Suggestion — variability metrics: Quantify sample diversity (pairwise VGG-loss, FID-like measures) to make variability discussion concrete.
- Suggestion — sensitivity analysis: Report sensitivity to number of feature maps per scale, scale weights, and stopping criteria.
- Suggestion — compute costs: Provide runtimes and memory footprints for single-scale, multi-scale, and hierarchical variants.
- Suggestion — texture scope: Group results by texture class (stochastic vs. regular, man-made vs. natural) to clarify applicability.
- Suggestion — broader baselines: Compare quantitatively to scattering-based models (Bruna & Mallat) and Portilla–Simoncelli.
- Question — multi-scale design: What filter sizes and counts per scale were used and how sensitive are results to this allocation?
- Question — nonlinearities: Were alternatives to ReLU (leaky ReLU, absolute value) tested and how do they affect Gram statistics?
- Question — color handling: How important is cross-channel mixing (luminance-only vs. RGB filters)?
- Question — optimization: How critical is the 1e7 scaling of loss/gradients and the chosen iteration budget; can earlier stopping improve variability?
- Question — initialization: Sensitivity to initialization noise distribution (uniform vs. Gaussian) and dynamic range?
- Question — overfitting: Additional evidence to distinguish exact reconstruction from genuine synthesis on small textures (e.g., cross-validation on disjoint patches)?
- Question — release: Will code and pretrained filter banks (Fourier/PCA/k-means) be released for replication?
- Question — applications: Do findings extend to style transfer (content+style) or only pure texture synthesis; behavior when combined with a content loss?
