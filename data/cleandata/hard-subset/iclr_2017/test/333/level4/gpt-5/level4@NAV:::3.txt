Gatys et al.’s framework showed that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature activations provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper examines in detail which types of deep or shallow networks work well within this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, perform surprisingly well and, for simple and regular textures, can produce results that are visually superior to those obtained with complex data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an interesting and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 demonstrates that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield texture images that achieve a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. This is attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the new supplementary material, the authors show that improved optimization results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the shallow network’s solution, which usefully complements the original experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is the lack of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that allow one to generate an exact copy of any reference texture, thereby achieving very high visual quality; such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The goal, however, is to capture a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Thus, while the empirical results seem to support the intuition that simple statistics suffice for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (relative to more complex statistics), it is difficult to confirm this conclusively.

The authors indicate that diversity could be measured in terms of entropy. This is reasonable but, as they acknowledge in their responses to questions, challenging to implement in practice. Furthermore, this would still not address the other aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also suggest conducting a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to address this issue, but regard it as future work.

Overall, since evaluating image generation is such a hard problem, I believe the paper has sufficient strengths to warrant publication at ICLR. Still, some form of psychophysical assessment would be useful to substantiate the intuitions that currently can only be drawn from inspecting the figures in the paper and in the supplementary material.
