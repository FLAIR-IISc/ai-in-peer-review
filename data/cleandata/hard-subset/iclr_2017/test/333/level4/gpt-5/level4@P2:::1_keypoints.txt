- Paper studies which properties of CNN features are necessary for Gram-matrix-based parametric texture synthesis; claims depth and supervised pretraining are not essential and a single-layer random CNN can rival VGG-based models on some textures.

- Authors propose a minimal baseline (single-layer random filters), explore multi-scale variants, and compare random, Fourier, k-means, and PCA filters, highlighting multi-scale filters and ReLU importance.

- Introduce quantitative evaluation using an averaged VGG-based texture loss and present a MaxEnt perspective allowing small mismatches in summary statistics for finite images.

- Decision: Reject.

- Key reason 1: Central claim (“depth and pretraining are not indispensable”) is not convincingly supported due to incomplete and potentially biased evaluation; critical baselines and human studies are missing.

- Key reason 2: Evaluation over-relies on the VGG loss (potentially biased) and qualitative inspection; strong non-parametric baselines and perceptual human judgments are absent, and inpainting results favor VGG-based models.

- Scope limitation: Showing some textures work well with a single-layer random model does not establish the generality of the claim; many textures remain imperfect with missed multi-scale structure.

- Evaluation bias: Using VGG-based loss can favor particular representations; paradoxical cases where random multi-scale achieves lower VGG loss than VGG-optimized samples indicate metric-model coupling.

- Missing baselines: No systematic comparison to strong non-parametric/patch-based synthesis methods that are often superior for structured textures.

- Inpainting evidence: The inpainting experiment suggests the single-layer model is less robust at capturing long-range coherent structure compared to VGG-based models.

- Efficiency concern: The multi-scale single-layer model uses very large kernels (up to 55×55), making it computationally inefficient; no thorough efficiency-quality tradeoff is reported.

- Variability vs similarity: The random model may prioritize perceptual similarity over variability (near-exact reconstructions reported); no principled diversity analysis (entropy, psychophysics) is provided.

- Optimization sensitivity: Improvements are attributed to tuning optimization, but sensitivity to optimizer choice, scaling (e.g., 1e7), and initialization is not systematically explored, hurting reproducibility and generality.

- Suggestion: Add strong non-parametric baselines (patch-based, quilting, modern patch neural methods) with visual and quantitative comparisons and include a human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC).

- Suggestion: Provide a thorough efficiency-quality analysis (runtime, memory, FLOPs) comparing single-layer multi-scale to hierarchical/multi-layer and explore dilated convolutions as alternatives.

- Suggestion: Quantify sample diversity across multiple draws per texture; explore mechanisms (early stopping, temperature) to trade similarity for diversity.

- Suggestion: Provide broader ablations: number of filters, filter sizes, random weight distributions, whitening/data statistics, nonlinearity variants, and optimizer/hyperparameter sensitivity.

- Suggestion: Investigate and clarify surprising VGG-loss results where random multi-scale beats VGG-optimized images; analyze which textures and why.

- Suggestion: Tone down generalized claims or substantiate them with more textures, human studies, extra tasks, and release code and seeds for reproducibility.

- Question: How do results compare visually and quantitatively to strong non-parametric texture synthesis methods on the same texture set?

- Question: How sensitive are results to optimizer, step size schedule, iterations, and the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling?

- Question: How robust are results across random seeds for filters and initializations—report variance and failure cases.

- Question: What are the computational costs (runtime, memory) of single-layer multi-scale vs hierarchical multi-layer models of comparable quality, and can dilation reduce cost without loss?

- Question: How does sample diversity vary with number of constraints (filters, scales) and early stopping; can diversity be quantified (patch-wise metrics, FID-like, psychophysics)?

- Question: Why does the VGG-based loss sometimes favor random multi-scale outputs over VGG-optimized ones—optimization traps or metric-model mismatch?

- Question: Beyond ReLU, were alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, ELU, tanh) and normalization (batch/instance) tested and do they affect quality/diversity?

- Question: How do different random filter distributions (Gaussian vs uniform, orthogonal vs i.i.d.) and energy normalization across scales affect performance?

- Question: Can a hierarchical random CNN with small kernels and dilation approximate the single-layer multi-scale receptive fields while maintaining quality?

- Question: Does the approach generalize to non-texture imagery or spatially non-stationary textures using localized Gram matrices or windowed statistics?
