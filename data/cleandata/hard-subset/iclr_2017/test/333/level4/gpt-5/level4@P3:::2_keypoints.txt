- Core claim: A single convolutional layer with ReLU and random filters can synthesize high-quality textures; a multi-scale variant improves long-range structure.  
- Main implication: Depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not essential for strong texture synthesis — establishes a minimal baseline.  
- Evaluated architectures: Compared random, Fourier, k-means, PCA filters, two capacities (363 vs. 3267 maps), and multi-scale concatenation.  
- Ablations: Showed ReLU is crucial; multi-scale combinations capture small/mid- and long-range structure better than single large-scale filters.  
- Quantitative evaluation: Used VGG-based Gram loss across five layers as a proxy for perceptual similarity and complemented with qualitative comparisons on 20 textures.  
- Notable finding: Multi-scale random sometimes attains lower VGG loss than VGG-optimized synthesis, prompting discussion about metric and variability.  
- Optimization details: Used L-BFGS-B with pixel constraints and gradient scaling to avoid early stopping; initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer outputs helps.  
- Theoretical framing: Connected results to maximum entropy modeling and the perceptual-similarity versus variability trade-off (ε-neighborhoods vs hard constraints).  
- Decision: Accept — clear, surprising insight with strong empirical support and thorough ablations expected to influence the field.  
- Acknowledged limitations: Dependence on VGG metric (possible circularity), limited treatment of sample variability, and computational cost of large single-layer filters.  
- Eval robustness suggestion: Run a small human study (e.g., 2AFC) to validate VGG-loss as a perceptual proxy.  
- Variability suggestion: Report results across multiple random seeds and distribution of VGG losses to quantify diversity and similarity trade-offs.  
- Efficiency suggestion: Provide wall-clock time and memory comparisons for single-scale, multi-scale, and VGG-based syntheses.  
- Sensitivity suggestion: Analyze sensitivity to number of feature maps and allocation across scales (scaling laws).  
- Comparison fairness: Detail filter normalization, DC removal, and color handling to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons across filter types.  
- Evaluation robustness: Test alternative VGG layer weightings (not just averaged) to confirm conclusions aren’t metric-dependent.  
- Optimization scrutiny: Justify the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling, show results without scaling, and clarify how pixel constraints are enforced (projection vs bounds).  
- Scope and failure modes: Discuss limits on non-stationary or globally structured textures and potential extension to style transfer or non-texture images.  
- Reproducibility: Release code, filter banks, and scripts to reproduce main figures and evaluations.  
- Key question: How many random seeds were used and what is the variance in VGG-loss and perceptual quality across seeds?  
- Key question: What are the exact multi-scale settings (filter sizes, maps per scale, per-scale normalization) and sensitivity to them?  
- Key question: Do results hold under different VGG configurations (pooling, layer weightings), or is there overfitting to the evaluation metric?  
- Key question: How do runtime and memory compare among single-scale, multi-scale, and VGG-based methods for the same resolution and iterations?  
- Key question: Provide ablations on gradient scaling factor and max iterations to show optimization stability.  
- Key question: For k-means/PCA filters, were filters whitened/normalized and were color-channel stats matched across methods?  
- Key question: Can you provide additional evidence of sample variability (e.g., inpainting psychophysics or quantitative proxies) to support the MaxEnt discussion?  
- Key question: Were other nonlinearities tested (leaky ReLU, absolute value) and how do they affect performance/stability?
