- Gatys et al.’s Gram-matrix framework effectively characterizes visual textures via correlation statistics of deep feature activations.  
- This paper systematically studies which deep or shallow networks work well within that framework.  
- Key finding: very shallow networks (single random filter bank) perform surprisingly well for texture synthesis.  
- For simple/regular textures, shallow random filters can produce results visually superior to complex, data-adapted filters like VGG-19.  
- Figure 4 shows optimizing with shallow filter banks can yield images with lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective.  
- The authors attribute this to optimization difficulty of the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost; initializing VGG-19 optimization from the shallow solution improves results (supplementary).  
- Main limitation: lack of a systematic, quantifiable comparison across methods and no conclusive measure of capturing the texture distribution.  
- Trivial shallow statistics can reproduce reference textures exactly, so visual quality alone doesn’t prove capturing the texture distribution.  
- Diversity could be measured by entropy but is practically challenging; psychophysical evaluation is suggested as the practical way to assess visual quality and diversity.  
- Overall assessment: paper has sufficient strengths for ICLR publication, though a psychophysical assessment would strengthen the claims.
