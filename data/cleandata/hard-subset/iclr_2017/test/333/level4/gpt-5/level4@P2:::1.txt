Summary and contributions
- The paper studies what aspects of deep CNN features are necessary for parametric texture synthesis based on Gram-matrix statistics. It claims that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is essential: a single-layer CNN with random filters and no pooling can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality, sometimes rivaling a VGG-based multi-layer model. 
- The authors propose a strong minimal baseline (single layer, random filters), explore multi-scale variants, and compare random, hand-crafted (Fourier), and unsupervised filters (k-means, PCA). They emphasize the importance of multi-scale filters and nonlinearities (ReLU). 
- They introduce a quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based texture loss (averaged across layers) and discuss a MaxEnt perspective that formalizes allowing small mismatches in summary statistics for finite images.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The central claim (“depth and pretraining are not indispensable”) is not sufficiently supported by rigorous, unbiased evaluation; critical baselines and perceptual/user studies are missing.
2) The evaluation relies heavily on the VGG loss (a potentially biased metric) and qualitative inspection, while strong non-parametric synthesis baselines and human judgments are absent. The inpainting experiment suggests the VGG-based model may better capture perceptual structure.

Supporting arguments
- Scope of the claim: Demonstrating that some textures can be synthesized competitively by a single-layer random multi-scale model is interesting, but does not establish that depth and learned features are unnecessary in general. The paper itself notes many textures remain imperfect, with structure missed at different scales.
- Evaluation bias: Using the VGG-based loss to assess quality can favor certain representations, and paradoxical cases (multi-scale random achieving lower VGG-loss than VGG-optimized samples) highlight metric-model coupling issues. Without human perceptual studies, it is hard to validate the main conclusion.
- Missing baselines: The paper asserts that generated samples are competitive, yet there is no systematic comparison with strong non-parametric/patch-based methods that often set the bar for visual fidelity (especially for highly structured textures). This weakens the claim of “rivaling state-of-the-art.”
- Inpainting evidence: In the presented inpainting task (Fig. 7), the VGG-based model appears to outperform the single-layer model, suggesting the latter may be less robust for tasks that require coherent long-range structure.
- Efficiency: The proposed multi-scale random single-layer model uses very large kernels (up to 55×55), which is computationally inefficient. The paper acknowledges that hierarchical models are faster, but does not provide a thorough efficiency-quality tradeoff or show that hierarchical (possibly random) CNNs can match the single-layer results while improving speed.
- Variability vs similarity: The discussion notes that the random model may lean toward perceptual similarity at the expense of variability, with anecdotal near-exact reconstructions in deeper random CNNs. A principled analysis of diversity (e.g., sample entropy or psychophysics) is missing.
- Optimization sensitivity: The improvement over earlier negative results with random weights is attributed to “tuning the optimization,” but the sensitivity to optimizer choice, scaling factors (e.g., 1e7), and initialization is not systematically explored. This reduces confidence in reproducibility and generality.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Add strong non-parametric baselines (patch-based synthesis, quilting, modern patch neural synthesis) with visual comparisons and quantitative metrics; include a small-scale human perceptual study (e.g., two-alternative forced-choice on discrimination or inpainting realism).
- Report a thorough efficiency-quality analysis: runtime, memory, and FLOPs for single-layer multi-scale vs multi-layer hierarchical variants (including dilated convolutions). Demonstrate a hierarchical alternative that matches quality while improving efficiency, or clarify why it cannot.
- Quantify variability: measure diversity across multiple samples per texture; explore controlled early stopping or temperature-like mechanisms to trade similarity for diversity; consider the psychophysical inpainting protocol outlined in the discussion.
- Provide broader ablations: number of filters, filter sizes, distribution of random weights, effect of whitening and data statistics, role of nonlinearity (beyond a single ReLU/no-ReLU comparison), and sensitivity to optimizer and hyperparameters.
- Clarify surprising VGG-loss results (random multi-scale outperforming VGG-optimized images in that metric for some textures). Analyze which textures benefit and why.
- Tone down the generality of claims or strengthen them with broader evidence (more textures, human studies, additional tasks beyond synthesis and inpainting).
- Release code and seeds to ensure reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) How do your results compare, visually and quantitatively, to strong non-parametric texture synthesis methods across the same texture set?
2) How sensitive are your conclusions to the choice of optimizer, step size schedule, number of iterations, and the 1e7 scaling of loss/gradients?
3) How robust are results across random seeds (filters and initialization)? Please report variance and failure cases.
4) What is the computational cost (runtime, memory) of single-layer multi-scale vs hierarchical multi-layer models achieving comparable quality? Can dilated convolutions reduce cost without quality loss?
5) How does the diversity of samples vary with the number of constraints (filters, scales) and with early stopping? Can you quantify variability (e.g., patch-wise diversity, FID-like surrogate, or psychophysics)?
6) Why does the VGG-based loss sometimes favor the random multi-scale outputs over the VGG-optimized ones? Is this due to optimization traps in VGG synthesis or a metric-model mismatch?
7) Beyond ReLU, did you test alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, ELU, tanh) and normalization (batch/instance normalization)? Do these change quality or diversity?
8) How do different random filter distributions (Gaussian vs uniform, orthogonalized vs i.i.d.) and energy normalization across scales affect performance?
9) Can a hierarchical random CNN with small kernels and dilation approximate your single-layer multi-scale model’s receptive fields while maintaining quality?
10) Does the approach generalize to non-texture imagery (e.g., object-like patterns) or to spatially non-stationary textures when using localized Gram matrices or windowed statistics?
