Gatys et al. showed that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide a powerful characterization of visual textures. This paper examines in detail which network architectures—deep or shallow—work well within that framework. A key finding is that very shallow models, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, perform surprisingly well. For simple, regular textures, they can even produce results that look better than those obtained with complex, data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers a thoughtful and informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 demonstrates that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield textures with lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear, nonconvex VGG-19 loss, which is a plausible explanation. The new supplementary material further shows that initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the result from the shallow model improves performance, a useful complement to the original experiments.

The main limitation is the lack of a systematic, quantitative comparison across methods. It is trivial to define image statistics that would reproduce an exact copy of a reference texture—achieving excellent visual quality with very shallow models—but such statistics do not capture a distribution. The real aim is to model a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Thus, while the empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics may suffice for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (relative to more complex statistics), this cannot be conclusively established from the current evaluation.

The authors suggest measuring diversity via entropy. This is reasonable, but, as they acknowledge, difficult to implement in practice, and it still does not address perceptual quality. They also propose psychophysical evaluation, which may be the most practical way to assess both diversity and visual fidelity, but leave it for future work.

Overall, given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, the paper’s insights and empirical findings are, in my view, strong enough to merit publication at ICLR. Nevertheless, incorporating some form of psychophysical assessment would substantively strengthen the claims that are currently supported primarily by visual inspection of the figures in the paper and supplementary material.
