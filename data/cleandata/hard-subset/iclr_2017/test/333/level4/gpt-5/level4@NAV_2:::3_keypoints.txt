- Main claim: Single-layer, random-filter Gram-matrix models can synthesize high-quality textures, sometimes outperforming VGG-19-based synthesis for simple/regular patterns.  
- Experimental scope: Systematic exploration of single-layer variants (random, hand-crafted, unsupervised) and a multi-scale random model.  
- Architectural findings: More feature maps and combining multiple receptive field sizes improve results; ReLU non-linearities are essential.  
- Explanation offered: Gram statistics induce a stationary representation capturing rich correlations; optimization of deep objectives is harder and may explain shallow wins.  
- Proxy & metrics: Propose using VGG-based loss as a practical perceptual proxy and call for better quantitative diversity measures (entropy, psychophysics).  
- Empirical observation: Shallow/random-feature Grams sometimes achieve lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG optimization; seeding VGG synthesis with shallow outputs improves final results.  
- Decision: Accept — motivated by a strong, surprising empirical finding and insightful analysis plus a useful initialization strategy.  
- Supporting evidence: Careful experiments show consistent trends (more maps → better synthesis; multi-scale captures long-range structure; nonlinearity required).  
- Caveat: Authors do not claim universal superiority of shallow models and acknowledge lack of comprehensive quantitative assessment of diversity/distributional fidelity.  
- Suggestion: Add a lightweight psychophysical evaluation (e.g., 2AFC or inpainting Turing test) to support perceptual-quality claims.  
- Suggestion: Report simple diversity proxies across independent syntheses (VGG-loss dispersion, LPIPS variance, patch coverage) to illustrate similarity–variability trade-off.  
- Reproducibility request: Clarify evaluation protocol (VGG layers, pooling, loss normalization, L-BFGS-B settings, preprocessing) and report compute/runtime/memory costs.  
- Baseline comparison: If possible, include quantitative comparison to classical methods (e.g., Portilla–Simoncelli) under the same VGG-based metric.  
- Minor fixes: Ensure consistent reference formatting and remove citation placeholders.  
- Questions for authors: Quantify variability across runs; which optimizers/strategies were tried for VGG objective and sensitivity to scaling; how performance varies with maps per scale and filter sizes; tests of other non-linearities; more evidence where VGG-loss misaligns with perception; and plans to release code, filters, and seeds.
