The framework by Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide excellent representations of visual textures. This paper examines in detail what types of deep or shallow networks can work well in this framework. One of the main findings is that very shallow nets, consisting of single filter banks with random weights, work surprisingly well, and can produce results for simple and regular textures that are visually superior to the complex data-optimized filters present in networks like VGG-19. Broadly, the paper includes an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield texture images with lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing for the VGG-19 objective. This is attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the new supplementary material, the authors show that VGG-19-based optimization can achieve better optimization results by starting with shallow network optimization results, which is a useful supplement to the original experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantitative objective. Defining image statistics that would allow for the exact replication of any reference texture, therefore with very good visual quality, would be trivial. Such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. Instead, the objective is to capture a texture distribution, and how well a method copes with this challenge remains an open problem. Therefore, while the empirical results seem to confirm the intuition that simple statistics are good enough for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (compared to more complex statistics), it is difficult to conclusively confirm that this is the case.

The authors say that diversity can be measured in terms of entropy. This is reasonable, but, as they acknowledge in their answers to the questions, it is difficult to do in practice. Furthermore, it would still not account for the other aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also suggest conducting a psychophysical evaluation, which may be the only practical way to address this problem, but consider it material for future work.

Overall, since evaluating image construction is such a difficult problem, I think the paper still has enough strength to justify publication in ICLR. Nevertheless, some form of psychophysical evaluation would be useful to confirm the intuition that can currently only be obtained by looking at the image in the paper and supplementary material.
