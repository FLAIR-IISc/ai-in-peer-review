This work builds upon the foundation laid by Gatys et al., which demonstrated the effectiveness of correlation statistics – specifically, empirical Gram matrices of deep features – for characterizing visual textures. This paper provides a detailed investigation into the performance of various network architectures, both deep and shallow, within this framework. A key finding is the surprising efficacy of very shallow networks, comprised of a single filter bank with random weights. For simple, regular textures, these shallow networks can even yield visually superior results compared to those generated using complex, data-adapted filters found in networks like VGG-19. More generally, the paper offers a valuable and insightful discussion regarding the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

The results presented in Figure 4 demonstrate that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can achieve lower VGG-19 loss values than direct optimization of the VGG-19 objective. The authors attribute this to the challenges associated with optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, a plausible explanation. The inclusion of new supplementary material showcasing improved VGG-19 optimization results when initialized with the output of the shallow network optimization is a beneficial addition to the original experimental findings.

A primary limitation of this work is the absence of a systematic comparison against quantifiable objectives. While it is straightforward to define image statistics that would allow for the exact replication of a reference texture – and thus achieve high visual quality – such approaches are inherently shallow. The core challenge lies in capturing a broader texture *distribution*, and objectively measuring performance in this regard remains an open research question. Consequently, while the empirical results support the idea that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), definitive confirmation is difficult to achieve.

The authors propose entropy as a metric for assessing diversity, acknowledging the practical difficulties associated with its implementation. Moreover, they recognize that entropy alone does not address the crucial aspect of visual quality. They also suggest a psychophysical evaluation as a potential solution, but rightly identify this as a direction for future work.

In conclusion, given the inherent difficulties in evaluating image generation techniques, I believe this paper possesses sufficient merit for publication in ICLR. However, incorporating some form of psychophysical assessment would strengthen the conclusions and provide further validation for the intuitions currently supported only by visual inspection of the figures presented in the paper and supplementary materials.
