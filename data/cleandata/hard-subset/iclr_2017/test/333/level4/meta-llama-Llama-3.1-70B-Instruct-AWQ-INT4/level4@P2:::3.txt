This paper explores the requirements for generating natural textures using deep learning approaches. The authors investigate which aspects of hierarchical representations in deep neural networks are crucial for natural image generation, specifically for texture synthesis. They demonstrate that neither the depth of the network nor the supervised training of the feature spaces is indispensable for natural texture synthesis. Instead, they show that a single convolutional layer with random filters can generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper's main contributions include presenting a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer network and random filters, and demonstrating that textures synthesized from this baseline are of high quality. The authors also test and compare various single-layer architectures with different filter sizes and types against the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al. They introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model to replace qualitative human inspection.

The authors' findings suggest that natural image generation does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations or the training of feature maps. Instead, for texture synthesis, these aspects seem to serve as fine-tuning of the image representation. The paper also discusses the importance of finding a balance between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis models.

The paper has several strengths, including its thorough investigation of the requirements for natural texture synthesis and its introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure. The authors' discussion on the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability is also insightful.

However, the paper has some limitations. The evaluation of image generation is a challenging problem, and the authors' reliance on qualitative human inspection and quantitative measures based on the VGG-based model may not be sufficient to conclusively confirm their findings. The authors acknowledge the difficulty of measuring diversity and visual quality, and suggest that psychophysical assessment may be necessary to address these challenges.

Overall, I recommend accepting this paper for publication in ICLR. While it has some limitations, its strengths and contributions to the field of natural image generation warrant publication. To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors consider including some form of psychophysical assessment to confirm their findings and provide a more comprehensive evaluation of their approach.
