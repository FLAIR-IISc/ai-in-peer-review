The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics, specifically empirical Gram matrices of deep feature responses, provide an excellent characterisation of visual textures. This paper investigates which type of deep or shallow networks may work well in this framework. One of the main findings is that very shallow nets, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, work surprisingly well, and for simple and regular textures may produce results that are visually superior to complex data-adapted filters such as the ones in networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strength and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimising images with respect to shallow filter banks may result in texture images that have a lower VGG-19 loss than optimising the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimising the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. The new supplementary material provides a useful complement to the original experiments, demonstrating that better optimisation results can be obtained by initialising the VGG-19-based optimisation with the shallow network optimisation results.

The main limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable objective. While it is trivial to define image statistics that would allow for the generation of an exact copy of any reference texture, thereby achieving good visual quality, the aim is instead to capture a texture distribution. However, measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. Although the empirical results seem to confirm the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of quality and diversity when compared to more complex statistics, it is difficult to conclusively confirm this.

The authors suggest measuring diversity in terms of entropy, which is a reasonable approach, but acknowledge the difficulty of implementing this in practice. Furthermore, this would not account for the other aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also propose performing a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to address this problem, but consider this to be material for future work.

Overall, given the inherent challenges in evaluating image generation, I believe the paper still has sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, some form of psychophysical assessment would be useful to confirm the intuitions that, at present, can only be obtained by inspecting the figures in the paper and the supplementary material.
