This study builds upon Gatys et al.'s framework, which successfully leveraged correlation statistics from deep feature responses to characterise visual textures. The authors delve into the specifics of deep and shallow networks that excel within this framework, yielding a key finding that extremely shallow networks, comprising a single filter bank with random weights, exhibit surprisingly impressive performance. Notably, these simple networks can produce visually superior results for regular and simple textures, even outperforming complex data-adapted filters like VGG-19.

The paper offers an engaging discussion on the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis, providing valuable insights into their applications. Figure 4 demonstrates that optimising images using shallow filter banks can result in texture images with lower VGG-19 loss compared to direct optimisation of the VGG-19 objective. The authors attribute this to the challenges of optimising the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, a reasonable explanation. The supplementary material presents a useful complement to the original experiments, showcasing improved optimisation results when initialising VGG-19-based optimisation with shallow network optimisation results.

The primary limitation of this study lies in its lack of systematic comparison between different methods using a quantifiable objective. While it is straightforward to define image statistics that can generate an exact replica of a reference texture with excellent visual quality, the goal is to capture a texture distribution. Quantifying the performance of methods in achieving this goal remains an open challenge. Although the empirical results suggest that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of quality and diversity, this conclusion is difficult to confirm conclusively.

The authors propose measuring diversity in terms of entropy, a reasonable approach, but acknowledge the practical difficulties in doing so. Moreover, this metric would not account for visual quality. They suggest a psychophysical assessment as a potential solution, but consider it a topic for future work.

Despite the challenges in evaluating image generation, this paper possesses sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, incorporating a psychophysical assessment would be beneficial in confirming the intuitions currently derived from inspecting the figures in the paper and supplementary material.
