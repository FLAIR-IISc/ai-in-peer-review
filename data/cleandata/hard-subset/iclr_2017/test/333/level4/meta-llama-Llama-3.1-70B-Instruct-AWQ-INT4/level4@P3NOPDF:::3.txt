The framework of Gatys et al. has demonstrated that correlation statistics, specifically empirical Gram matrices of deep feature responses, provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper delves into the details of which deep or shallow networks may work well within this framework. One of the primary findings is that very shallow nets, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, work surprisingly well, and for simple and regular textures, may produce results that are visually superior to complex data-adapted filters, such as those in networks like VGG-19. Furthermore, the paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 illustrates that the optimization of images with respect to shallow filter banks may result in texture images with a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. The supplementary material provides additional insight, showing that better optimization results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the shallow network optimization results, which serves as a useful complement to the original experiments.

The primary limitation of the paper lies in its lack of systematic comparison between different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that would allow for the generation of an exact copy of any reference texture, thereby achieving very good visual quality. However, such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The goal, instead, is to capture a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. While the empirical results appear to confirm the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), it is challenging to conclusively confirm this.

The authors suggest that diversity could be measured in terms of entropy, which is reasonable, but, as they acknowledge, difficult to implement in practice. Moreover, this approach would still not account for the other aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also propose performing a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to address this issue, but consider it material for future work.

Despite the challenges in evaluating image generation, I believe the paper has sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, incorporating some form of psychophysical assessment would be beneficial in confirming the intuitions that, at present, can only be obtained by inspecting the figures in the paper and the supplementary material.
