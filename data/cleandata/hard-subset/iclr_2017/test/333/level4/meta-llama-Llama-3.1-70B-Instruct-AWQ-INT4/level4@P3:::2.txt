This paper provides an interesting analysis of the conditions that enable the generation of natural-looking textures. The results are quite surprising, and the analysis is thorough. The authors tackle a specific question in the field of natural image generation, namely, what aspects of feature representation are crucial for natural texture synthesis. They demonstrate that a single-layer network with random filters can generate high-quality textures, challenging the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and supervised training of feature spaces are necessary.

I recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons: first, the paper addresses a well-defined problem and provides a clear answer, and second, the approach is well-motivated and grounded in the literature.

The paper supports its claims through a series of experiments that compare the performance of different single-layer networks with random filters to the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al. The results show that the single-layer networks can generate high-quality textures that often rival those produced by the state-of-the-art model. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the importance of different components of the texture synthesis algorithm, including the number of feature maps, filter sizes, and non-linearities.

One potential weakness of the paper is the evaluation method, which relies on visual inspection and a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of their evaluation method, they argue that it is sufficient for the purposes of this paper. I agree with this assessment, as the evaluation method is adequate for demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

To further improve the paper, I suggest that the authors consider addressing the following points:

* Provide more details about the optimization procedure used to tune the single-layer networks. How did the authors determine the optimal hyperparameters for each network?
* Discuss the potential applications of the proposed approach beyond texture synthesis. Could the same principles be applied to other tasks, such as image generation or image-to-image translation?
* Consider using additional evaluation metrics to assess the quality of the generated textures. For example, the authors could use metrics such as the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) or the Inception Score (IS) to evaluate the realism and diversity of the generated textures.

Overall, I think this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural image generation and provides a new perspective on the conditions necessary for generating natural-looking textures. I recommend accepting the paper and look forward to seeing the authors' future work on this topic.
