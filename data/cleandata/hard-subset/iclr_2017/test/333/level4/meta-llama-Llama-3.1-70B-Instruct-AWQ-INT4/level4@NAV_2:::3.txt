This paper explores the question of what aspects of deep neural network representations are crucial for natural image generation, specifically in the context of natural texture synthesis. The authors investigate whether the depth, pooling, or training of features on natural images are indispensable for generating high-quality textures. They demonstrate that a single-layer network with random filters can produce textures that often rival the perceptual quality of state-of-the-art approaches, suggesting that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as crucial as previously thought.

I recommend accepting this paper with one key reason being the novelty of the approach and the thorough investigation of the problem. The paper provides a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer network and random filters, and the results are impressive, especially considering the simplicity of the model.

The authors provide supporting arguments for their claims by comparing their approach to the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al. (2015a) and demonstrating that their single-layer model can produce high-quality textures that are often comparable to, or even surpass, those generated by the more complex model. They also investigate the importance of different aspects of their model, such as the number of feature maps and the type of filters used, and provide a thorough analysis of the results.

One potential limitation of the paper is that it does not provide a quantitative evaluation of the texture quality, relying instead on visual inspection. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and discuss potential ways to address it in future work.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors consider the following points:

* Provide more details on the optimization procedure used to generate the textures, as this seems to be a crucial aspect of the approach.
* Investigate the use of different types of filters, such as learned filters or filters with different sizes, to see if they can improve the quality of the generated textures.
* Consider using a more objective evaluation metric, such as the one proposed in the paper, to quantify the quality of the generated textures.
* Provide more discussion on the implications of the results, particularly in terms of the potential applications of the approach and the limitations of the current state-of-the-art models.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the optimization procedure used to generate the textures, and how it was tuned to achieve the best results?
* Have you considered using different types of filters, such as learned filters or filters with different sizes, to see if they can improve the quality of the generated textures?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of not having a quantitative evaluation metric for texture quality, and what potential solutions do you propose?
* Can you provide more discussion on the implications of the results, particularly in terms of the potential applications of the approach and the limitations of the current state-of-the-art models?
