The framework of Gatys et al. has demonstrated that correlation statistics, specifically empirical Gram matrices, of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterisation of visual textures. This paper delves into the details of which types of deep or shallow networks may work well within this framework. One of the primary findings is that very shallow nets, comprising a single filter bank with random weights, work surprisingly well, and for simple and regular textures, may produce results that are visually superior to complex data-adapted filters, such as those in networks like VGG-19.

More broadly, the paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis. Figure 4 shows that the optimisation of images with respect to shallow filter banks may result in texture images that have a lower VGG-19 loss than optimising the VGG-19 objective directly. This is attributed to the difficulty of optimising the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. The new supplementary material provides a useful complement to the original experiments by demonstrating that better optimisation results can be obtained by initialising the VGG-19-based optimisation with the shallow network optimisation results.

The main limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that would allow for the simple generation of an exact copy of any reference texture, thus achieving very good visual quality. Such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The aim, however, is to capture a texture distribution, and measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. While the empirical results seem to confirm the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), it is difficult to conclusively confirm that this is the case.

The authors suggest that diversity could be measured in terms of entropy, which is a reasonable approach, but as they acknowledge, it is difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, this would still not account for the other aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also propose performing a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to address this problem, but deem it to be material for future work.

Overall, given that the evaluation of image generation is a challenging problem, I believe the paper still has sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR. However, some form of psychophysical assessment would be useful to confirm the intuitions that, at present, can only be obtained by inspecting the figures in the paper and the supplementary material.
