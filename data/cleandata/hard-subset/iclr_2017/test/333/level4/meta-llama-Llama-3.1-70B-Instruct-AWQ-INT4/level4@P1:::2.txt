This paper provides an interesting analysis of the conditions that enable the generation of natural-looking textures. The results are quite surprising, and the analysis is thorough. The authors' main contribution is to demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated from networks with only a single layer, no pooling, and random filters, challenging the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and supervised training of feature spaces are essential for natural image generation.

I decide to accept this paper, with the primary reason being the novelty and significance of the findings. The authors' experiments and analysis provide strong evidence that single-layer networks with random filters can generate high-quality textures, often rivaling state-of-the-art approaches. This is a valuable contribution to the field of texture synthesis and natural image generation.

One potential concern is the evaluation method used in the paper. While the authors provide a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model, this measure may not perfectly capture the perceptual differences between the reference and synthesized textures. However, as other reviewers have noted, this could be an interesting line of work moving forward, and it does not detract from the current paper's contributions.

To further improve the paper, I suggest that the authors consider the following:

* Provide more details on the optimization procedure used in the experiments, as the authors mention that a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure is crucial for the success of the single-layer network with random filters.
* Discuss the potential limitations of using a single-layer network with random filters, such as the increased computational cost and the potential lack of generalizability to other image generation tasks.
* Consider providing more comparisons with other state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods, such as those using generative adversarial networks (GANs) or variational autoencoders (VAEs).

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of texture synthesis and natural image generation, and I recommend its acceptance.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the optimization procedure used in the experiments, and how it was tuned for the single-layer network with random filters?
* How do you think the results would change if you used a different type of non-linearity, such as sigmoid or tanh, instead of ReLU?
* Have you considered applying the proposed method to other image generation tasks, such as object generation or scene generation? If so, what are the potential challenges and limitations?
