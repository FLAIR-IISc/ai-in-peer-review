This paper explores the question of what aspects of deep neural network feature representations are crucial for natural image generation, specifically in the context of texture synthesis. The authors investigate whether the hierarchical multi-layer representation, the pooling, or the training of the features on natural images are indispensable for generating high-quality textures. They demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random filters can synthesize textures that often rival the perceptual quality of state-of-the-art approaches, such as Gatys et al. (2015a).

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason for this choice being that the authors provide a thorough and well-motivated analysis of the problem, and their approach is well-placed in the literature. They present a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis that solely relies on a single-layer network and random, data-independent filters.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) presenting a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, (2) showing that textures synthesized from this baseline are of high quality and often rival state-of-the-art approaches, (3) testing and comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures, and (4) utilizing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model.

The authors' results show that for natural texture synthesis, one neither needs a hierarchical deep network architecture with spatial pooling nor filters that are adapted to the statistics of natural images. They also demonstrate that the combination of different scales in the multi-scale network is important for good texture synthesis, as it allows for simultaneously modeling small-, mid-, and long-range correlations of the textures.

The paper's discussion on the importance of finding a balance between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis is also noteworthy. The authors suggest that measuring variability is challenging, but propose a psychophysical assessment as a potential solution.

One potential limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable objective. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation and suggest that measuring diversity and visual quality remains an open problem.

To improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors consider including a more systematic comparison of different methods, as well as a more detailed analysis of the limitations of their approach. Additionally, a psychophysical assessment of the generated textures could provide further evidence for the effectiveness of their method.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper are:

* Can the authors provide more details on how they selected the specific architectures and hyperparameters for their single-layer networks?
* How do the authors plan to address the challenge of measuring diversity and visual quality in texture synthesis?
* Can the authors provide more insights into why they believe that the combination of different scales in the multi-scale network is important for good texture synthesis?

Overall, I think the paper has sufficient strengths to warrant publication in ICLR, and I look forward to seeing the authors' responses to these questions.
