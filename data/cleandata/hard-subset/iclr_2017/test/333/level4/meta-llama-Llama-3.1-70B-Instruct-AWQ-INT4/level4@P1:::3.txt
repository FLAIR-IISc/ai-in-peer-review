**Summary of the Paper's Contributions**

The paper investigates the requirements for generating natural textures using deep neural networks. Specifically, it examines the importance of hierarchical representations, pooling, and training on natural images in achieving high-quality texture synthesis. The authors propose a minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer network with random, data-independent filters. They demonstrate that this approach can generate high-quality textures that rival state-of-the-art methods, suggesting that depth and pre-training are not as crucial for natural image generation as previously thought.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the review, I recommend accepting the paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, which challenges the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and training on natural images are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.
2. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model, which allows for a more objective assessment of texture quality.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the demonstration that a single-layer network with random filters can generate high-quality textures that rival state-of-the-art methods. This finding has significant implications for the field of texture synthesis, as it suggests that simpler models can be effective for this task. The authors also provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, including a comparison with the VGG-based model, which allows for a more objective assessment of texture quality.

The paper's discussion on the importance of non-linearities and the combination of different scales in the multi-scale network is also insightful. The authors demonstrate that the combination of different scales is crucial for good texture synthesis, as it allows for the simultaneous modeling of small-, mid-, and long-range correlations of the textures.

**Additional Feedback**

While the paper presents a strong contribution, there are some areas that could be improved. One limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable objective. The authors acknowledge that measuring diversity and visual quality is an open problem, but it would be useful to provide more concrete suggestions for how to address this challenge.

Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the implications of the findings for the broader field of natural image generation. The authors mention that their results suggest that natural image generation does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations or training on natural images, but it would be useful to elaborate on this point and discuss the potential applications of this finding.
