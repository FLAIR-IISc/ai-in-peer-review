

This paper presents a compelling exploration of the essential components for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks. The authors propose a minimalist baseline that employs a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be synthesized without hierarchical representations, pooling operations, or supervised training of features. Their key insight—that natural texture synthesis does not require deep architectures or pre-trained filters—challenges prevailing assumptions in the field and is supported by extensive empirical comparisons across multiple single-layer architectures (including Fourier, K-means, and random filters) against the state-of-the-art VGG-based model (Gatys et al., 2015a).

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant contribution by identifying the minimal requirements for effective texture synthesis, thereby clarifying what aspects of deep networks are truly essential for this task. The paper's most compelling evidence is the multi-scale random filter model, which often rivals the VGG-based approach in perceptual quality while using a dramatically simpler architecture. This finding fundamentally challenges the assumption that deep hierarchical representations are indispensable for natural image generation.

The paper's strength lies in its rigorous empirical evaluation. The authors systematically test various filter types (random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned), filter sizes, and feature map counts, demonstrating that a larger number of feature maps generally improves perceptual quality. Crucially, they show that random filters perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural image statistics—a counterintuitive finding that contradicts earlier reports (Gatys et al., 2015a). The multi-scale model's superior performance further demonstrates the importance of receptive field size and scale diversity, rather than network depth or pre-training.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing three key points:
1. The authors should explicitly compare their single-layer model against non-parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000), which were state-of-the-art before deep learning approaches. While the paper mentions these methods, a direct comparison would better contextualize their performance gains.

2. The paper claims that single-layer models "often rival" the VGG-based model, but the evaluation primarily relies on qualitative human assessment. I suggest incorporating the quantitative VGG-based loss metric (E(y) in Eq. 7) as a primary evaluation criterion, which would provide more objective evidence for the claimed performance parity. The current comparison in Figure 4 shows some textures where the VGG loss is actually lower for the single-layer model than for the VGG-based synthesis, which is counterintuitive and warrants further explanation.

3. The paper should clarify why the single-layer model with random filters achieves better results than previous attempts with random weights. The authors attribute this to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," but this needs more concrete details. What specific optimization adjustments were made, and how do they differ from Gatys et al.'s approach?

To further validate the conclusions about the necessity of deep architectures, I have two specific questions for the authors:
1. Would a hierarchical model with random weights (rather than using multiple filter scales in a single layer) achieve comparable performance to the multi-scale single-layer model? This would help determine whether the key factor is the receptive field size (which could be achieved through depth) or the specific multi-scale filter combination.

2. The paper notes that the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantages for certain textures (e.g., brick walls). How might this limitation affect the broader applicability of the VGG-based metric for texture synthesis evaluation, and what alternative metrics would better capture perceptual quality across diverse texture types?

The paper's theoretical contribution regarding the MaxEnt framework and the importance of defining appropriate distance metrics (d(x,y)) and perceptual equivalence partitions (Px) is particularly valuable. The discussion of how finite-size images complicate the MaxEnt framework provides important insights for future texture synthesis work. This theoretical foundation strengthens the paper's practical findings and makes the work relevant beyond just texture synthesis.

The authors' demonstration that simple single-layer networks can rival deep architectures for texture synthesis is a significant contribution that will likely influence future work in generative modeling. By clearly identifying what is—and isn't—necessary for effective texture synthesis, the paper helps focus research efforts on the truly critical aspects of network design. The computational efficiency of the single-layer approach (despite its large filters) also offers practical advantages for real-world applications.

In summary, this paper makes a strong contribution by challenging assumptions about deep networks' necessity for texture synthesis, providing compelling empirical evidence through thorough experimentation, and offering theoretical insights into the underlying principles. The work is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and has significant implications for the field.
