- Single-layer CNN with random filters can synthesize high-quality textures, often matching or exceeding deep pre-trained models.  
- Rigorous ablation studies across filter types (Fourier, K-means, random) and architectures (single-scale vs. multi-scale).  
- The number of feature maps and multi-scale filtering are critical for perceptual quality.  
- Depth, pooling, and supervised pre-training are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.  
- Multi-scale random model achieves perceptual quality comparable to SOTA on 18/20 textures and outperforms on structured textures (e.g., brick walls) (Fig. 4).  
- Multi-scale filtering (not merely larger receptive fields) is essential to capture short-, mid-, and long-range correlations (Fig. 2).  
- Random filters outperform trained ones due to optimization/tuning considerations, supported by Appendix A.  
- VGG-based loss metric provides a reproducible evaluation that aligns with human perception for most textures but has limitations for highly regular structures (e.g., grids).  
- Suggestion: explicitly quantify the human evaluation gap (e.g., run a small-scale human study for the remaining textures).  
- Suggestion: clarify practical computational costs of the multi-scale large-filter model versus hierarchical alternatives.  
- Key question 1: For textures where VGG loss fails (e.g., grids), how would the loss function or optimization be modified to better align with perception?  
- Key question 2: When the multi-scale model outperforms VGG synthesis despite optimizing a different loss, is the VGG loss suboptimal for certain textures or is this an optimization artifact?
