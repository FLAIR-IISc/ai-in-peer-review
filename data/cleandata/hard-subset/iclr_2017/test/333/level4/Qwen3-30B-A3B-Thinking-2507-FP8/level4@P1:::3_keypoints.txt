- Main contribution: shows single-layer convnets with random filters (no pooling, no pre-training) can synthesize high-quality natural textures, challenging need for deep or pre-trained features.  
- Strong experimental support: extensive tests across filter types and sizes demonstrate random multi-scale filters often match or exceed VGG-based synthesis.  
- Theoretical insight: success attributed partly to simpler optimization landscapes, with implications for maximum-entropy texture models.  
- Multi-scale model: captures multiple spatial scales and outperforms single-scale variants.  
- Supplementary finding: initializing VGG synthesis with random-filter results yields improved samples.  
- Evaluation critique: VGG loss generally correlates with perceptual quality but fails for highly regular textures (e.g., grids, brick walls).  
- Proposed evaluation framework: defines perceptual similarity and variability as a foundation for future assessments.  
- Recommendation: Accept — paper challenges a key field assumption and systematically explores filter/optimization design.  
- Suggestion: add quantitative diversity/entropy metrics or discuss practical difficulties of measuring variability in high-dimensional spaces.  
- Suggestion: include direct quantitative comparisons (e.g., SSIM/LPIPS) to non-neural methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000).  
- Suggestion: clarify optimization differences from Gatys et al. (2015a), including loss choice and optimization parameters.  
- Fix: complete the incomplete caption for Figure 3.  
- Question 1: How was the acceptability threshold for VGG-based loss as a perceptual proxy determined, and where does it fail?  
- Question 2: Why does VGG loss sometimes favor multi-scale samples over VGG-based ones for grid-like textures, and does this expose a VGG metric limitation?  
- Question 3: How texture-dependent is the multi-scale model’s superiority versus VGG-based synthesis; which texture categories still favor VGG?
