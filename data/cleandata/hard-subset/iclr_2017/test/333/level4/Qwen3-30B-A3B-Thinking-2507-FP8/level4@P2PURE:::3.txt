

The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper investigates which deep or shallow networks perform effectively within this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks—comprising a single filter bank with random weights—surprisingly perform well, and for simple, regular textures can produce visually superior results compared to complex, data-adapted filters like those in VGG-19. The paper offers an informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such texture synthesis methods.  

Figure 4 demonstrates that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can yield texture images with lower VGG-19 loss than direct optimization of the VGG-19 objective. This is reasonably attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. The supplementary material further shows that improved optimization results can be achieved by initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from shallow network optimization, providing a valuable complement to the original experiments.  

The primary limitation is the lack of systematic comparison against a quantifiable objective. While trivial image statistics could permit exact replication of reference textures (with high visual quality but no generative capability), the goal is to capture texture distributions. Measuring how well methods achieve this remains an open problem. Consequently, although empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics suffice for texture synthesis in terms of quality and diversity (relative to more complex statistics), it remains difficult to conclusively validate this claim.  

The authors propose measuring diversity via entropy, which is reasonable but, as acknowledged in their responses, challenging to implement practically. Moreover, entropy alone would not address visual quality. They suggest psychophysical assessment as the most viable solution, though they deem it suitable for future work.  

Given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, the paper’s merits are sufficient to merit publication at ICLR. However, a psychophysical assessment would be beneficial to substantiate the intuitions currently supported only by visual inspection of figures in the paper and supplementary material.
