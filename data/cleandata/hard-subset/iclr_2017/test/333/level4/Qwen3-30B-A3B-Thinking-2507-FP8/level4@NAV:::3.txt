

The framework introduced by Gatys et al. established that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses offer an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper provides a detailed investigation into which deep or shallow network architectures perform effectively within this framework. A key finding demonstrates that extremely shallow networks—comprising a single random-weight filter bank—perform surprisingly well, often producing visually superior results for simple, regular textures compared to complex, data-adapted filters such as those in VGG-19. The paper further delivers an insightful discussion on the strengths and limitations of such texture synthesis approaches.

Figure 4 reveals that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can yield texture images with lower VGG-19 loss than direct optimization of the VGG-19 objective, attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function—a reasonable explanation. The supplementary material addresses this by showing that initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from shallow network optimization significantly improves outcomes, providing a valuable extension to the original findings.

The primary limitation is the absence of systematic comparison against a quantifiable evaluation metric. While trivial image statistics could generate exact copies of reference textures (achieving high visual quality), the true challenge lies in capturing the underlying texture distribution. Defining a robust metric for this purpose remains an open problem. Consequently, although empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics suffice for high-quality and diverse texture synthesis (relative to more complex statistics), conclusive validation is challenging without a standardized measure.

The authors propose measuring diversity via entropy, which is reasonable but acknowledges practical difficulties in implementation. This approach also fails to address visual quality, a separate critical aspect. They suggest future psychophysical assessment as the most viable solution, though this is deferred to subsequent work.

Given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, the paper's strengths are sufficient for publication at ICLR. However, incorporating a psychophysical assessment would strengthen the validation of the current empirical observations, which are currently limited to visual inspection of figures in the paper and supplementary material.
