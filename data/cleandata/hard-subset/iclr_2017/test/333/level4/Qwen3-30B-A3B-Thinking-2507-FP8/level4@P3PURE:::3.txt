

The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper investigates which deep or shallow networks are effective within this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks—consisting of a single filter bank with random weights—work surprisingly well, and for simple, regular textures can produce visually superior results compared to complex data-adapted filters (e.g., VGG-19). The paper also offers an informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of such texture synthesis methods.  

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield texture images with lower VGG-19 loss than direct optimization of the VGG-19 objective. This is reasonably attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. The supplementary material further demonstrates that better optimization results can be achieved by initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from shallow network optimization, providing a useful complement to the original experiments.  

The main limitation is the lack of systematic comparison against a quantifiable objective. While it is trivial to define image statistics that permit exact replication of a reference texture (yielding high visual quality), the goal is to capture a texture distribution. Measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Although empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics suffice for both quality and diversity in texture synthesis (compared to more complex statistics), conclusive validation is difficult without a formal metric.  

The authors propose measuring diversity via entropy, which is reasonable but, as acknowledged, challenging to implement practically. Moreover, entropy alone would not address visual quality. They suggest psychophysical assessment as the most practical solution, though deem it suitable for future work.  

Given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, the paper’s strengths justify publication in ICLR. However, a psychophysical assessment would be valuable to confirm the intuitions currently supported only by visual inspection of figures in the paper and supplementary material.
