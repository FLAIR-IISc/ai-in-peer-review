

**Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**  
This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of texture synthesis by rigorously investigating the minimal requirements for effective texture generation using neural network representations. The authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be synthesized using remarkably simple architectures—specifically, single-layer convolutional networks with random filters, no pooling, and no pre-training on natural images. This challenges the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical representations or data-adapted features are essential for natural texture synthesis. The work is well-structured, thoroughly explores a wide range of architectural variations, and provides a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical implications for maximum entropy models of texture generation. The authors convincingly show that their single-layer random filter approach often produces textures visually comparable to, and sometimes superior to, state-of-the-art methods based on deep networks like VGG-19.

**2. Decision**  
I recommend *Accept* for this paper. The primary reasons are:  
(1) The paper provides a strong, well-supported demonstration that depth and pre-training are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis, fundamentally challenging a key assumption in the field.  
(2) The systematic exploration of filter banks and optimization procedures offers valuable insights for future work in texture synthesis and generative modeling.

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's core claim—that single-layer networks with random filters can generate textures rivaling VGG-based methods—is well-supported by extensive experiments. Figure 1 demonstrates that random filter banks (particularly the multi-scale variant) capture significant texture structure, while Figure 4 provides compelling visual evidence that the random filter approach often matches or exceeds VGG-based synthesis. The authors' explanation for their success—improved optimization due to a simpler objective landscape—adds significant theoretical weight to their findings. The supplementary material showing that initializing VGG synthesis with random filter results yields superior samples further strengthens their argument.

The paper's most significant contribution is its methodical deconstruction of what aspects of deep networks are truly essential for texture synthesis. By testing filters from multiple categories (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and varying filter sizes, the authors provide a comprehensive understanding of the minimal requirements. Their observation that the multi-scale model outperforms single-scale variants by capturing multiple spatial scales simultaneously is particularly insightful.

The discussion on the limitations of current evaluation methods (Section 7) is thoughtful and well-argued. The authors acknowledge that while VGG loss correlates with perceptual quality, it fails for highly regular textures like brick walls and grids—a limitation they properly contextualize. Their proposed framework for evaluating texture synthesis (defining both perceptual similarity and variability) provides a valuable theoretical foundation for future work.

**4. Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

- **Quantitative diversity metrics**: While the paper acknowledges the challenge of measuring texture variability, the authors could briefly discuss how their current framework might be extended to incorporate diversity metrics. Even a short discussion on the practical difficulties of measuring entropy in high-dimensional spaces would be helpful.

- **Comparison to non-neural texture synthesis**: The paper rightly notes that their method surpasses older parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000), but a more direct quantitative comparison would strengthen this claim. For instance, reporting SSIM or LPIPS scores against these baselines would provide additional context.

- **Clarification on optimization**: The paper mentions that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture." It would be helpful to elaborate on how the optimization procedure differs from Gatys et al. (2015a), particularly regarding the choice of loss function and optimization parameters.

- **Figure 3**: The caption for Figure 3 is incomplete (appears to be cut off). Please complete the caption to clarify the comparison being made.

**Key Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences." Could you elaborate on how you determined this threshold of acceptability, and whether there are specific texture types where this approximation fails?

2. In Section 6, you mention that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Could you explain why this occurs, and whether this suggests a limitation of the VGG loss as a perceptual metric?

3. The paper notes that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a)." To what extent does this superiority depend on the specific texture type, and are there texture categories where VGG-based synthesis still clearly outperforms your approach?

This paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of texture synthesis and generative modeling, with implications extending beyond texture synthesis to fundamental questions about what aspects of deep learning representations are truly necessary for natural image generation. The work is well-executed, thoughtfully analyzed, and represents a valuable step forward in the field.
