

This paper introduces a simple yet effective parametric baseline for texture synthesis, leveraging multi-scale random filters. Empirical results demonstrate that samples generated by this baseline sometimes rival those produced by VGG-based models, which employ multi-layer, pre-trained filters. The authors conclude that texture synthesis may not inherently require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps. While the work presents an interesting and valuable insight, the conclusions require further validation, particularly concerning the role of hierarchical representations.  

First, the generated samples from both the VGG-based model and the single-layer baseline remain imperfect and consistently inferior to state-of-the-art non-parametric methods (e.g., exemplar-based synthesis). This gap suggests that the proposed baseline, while competitive with certain parametric approaches, does not yet achieve the quality of the most effective texture synthesis techniques. Second, Figure 7 reveals that the VGG-based model outperforms the baseline in the inpainting task, contradicting the claim that hierarchical features are unnecessary for complex synthesis tasks. This discrepancy warrants deeper analysis, as inpainting relies heavily on structural coherence—a strength typically attributed to deep representations.  

A critical unresolved question pertains to the efficiency of multi-scale processing. The authors use numerous filters of varying sizes to handle scale diversity, but it remains unclear whether a hierarchical architecture (e.g., a CNN with stacked layers) could achieve equivalent or superior multi-scale modeling with fewer parameters and greater computational efficiency. Such a comparison would strengthen the argument regarding the necessity of hierarchical representations. The current work does not address whether the observed performance of the random-filter baseline stems from its multi-scale design or its avoidance of deep learning, leaving open the possibility that hierarchical models might still offer advantages in scalability or quality.  

The paper’s core contribution—demonstrating that simple, non-hierarchical parametric methods can match VGG-based baselines in some scenarios—is noteworthy. However, the conclusion that "texture synthesis does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations" overreaches given the limited scope of evaluation. The experiments primarily compare two parametric approaches without sufficient context against non-parametric baselines or rigorous ablation studies on hierarchical components. To support the broader claim, future work should: (1) systematically evaluate the baseline against non-parametric methods across diverse textures, (2) investigate why VGG excels in inpainting despite the authors’ conclusion, and (3) directly compare the multi-filter approach with hierarchical architectures for multi-scale processing.  

In summary, this work offers a useful baseline and raises important questions about the role of deep features in texture synthesis. Its insights are compelling but currently lack the empirical depth needed to dismiss hierarchical representations as unnecessary. With additional validation and clearer experimental scope, the paper could make a stronger contribution to the field. The authors are encouraged to address these points in revision.
