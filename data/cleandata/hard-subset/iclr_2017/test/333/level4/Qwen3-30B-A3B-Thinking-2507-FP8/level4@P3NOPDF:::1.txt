

This paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable contribution by proposing a simple parametric texture synthesis baseline using multi-scale random filters. The key insight—that texture synthesis can achieve competitive results without deep hierarchical representations or pre-trained features—challenges common assumptions in the field. Empirically, the authors demonstrate that their baseline sometimes produces samples comparable to VGG-based models in visual quality, suggesting that complex feature hierarchies may not be essential. While the approach is elegant and the findings are thought-provoking, the conclusions require stronger validation, and several critical points warrant clarification before the work can be fully endorsed.

The primary claim—that deep hierarchical representations are unnecessary for texture synthesis—lacks sufficient evidence. The authors assert that their simple filter bank rivals VGG-based methods, but this comparison overlooks a crucial context: *all* parametric models evaluated (including VGG and the proposed baseline) generate samples that are visibly inferior to non-parametric methods (e.g., PatchMatch or exemplar-based synthesis). As shown in Figure 5, the parametric outputs exhibit artifacts (e.g., checkerboard patterns, inconsistent textures) that are absent in non-parametric results. If even the best parametric models underperform non-parametric baselines, the argument that "hierarchical representations are unnecessary" becomes less compelling, as the fundamental challenge for parametric synthesis remains unresolved. The paper should explicitly address why this gap exists and whether the proposed baseline’s success (relative to other parametric models) implies anything meaningful about the *necessity* of hierarchy, rather than merely highlighting limitations of existing hierarchical approaches.

Furthermore, Figure 7 presents a critical counterpoint to the paper’s main conclusion. The VGG-based model demonstrates significantly better performance on the inpainting task compared to the single-layer random filter baseline. This suggests that hierarchical representations *do* provide tangible benefits for structured tasks like inpainting, where contextual understanding is essential. The authors dismiss this as a "side task," but this undermines their broader claim. If hierarchical features improve performance on a task closely related to synthesis (inpainting requires coherent texture completion), it weakens the assertion that such features are "not necessary." The paper should engage directly with this evidence rather than marginalizing it.

A related concern is the proposed method’s efficiency. The authors use "lots of filters with different sizes" to handle multi-scale texture, but they do not compare this to a hierarchical architecture (e.g., a shallow CNN with stacked layers). A hierarchical model could potentially process multi-scale information more coherently and with fewer parameters than a hand-crafted filter bank. The paper should investigate whether such a model would outperform both the random filter baseline *and* VGG-based approaches on synthesis and inpainting tasks. Without this comparison, the claim that "deep hierarchical representations are unnecessary" remains speculative, as the paper does not test the most efficient alternative to the proposed baseline.

Finally, the evaluation lacks quantitative rigor. While visual comparisons are provided, the paper does not include objective metrics (e.g., FID, LPIPS, or texture-specific scores) to substantiate claims of "rivaling" VGG-based models. Without numerical evidence, the assertion that the baseline "sometimes" achieves comparable quality is subjective. The authors should supplement their figures with metrics to validate their qualitative observations.

In summary, this work offers a compelling baseline that simplifies texture synthesis and challenges assumptions about deep features. However, the conclusions about the irrelevance of hierarchical representations are overstated. The evidence provided does not sufficiently rule out the possibility that hierarchical models could still be beneficial (as suggested by Figure 7), nor does it address the broader performance gap relative to non-parametric methods. The paper would be significantly strengthened by: (1) quantitative metrics for synthesis quality, (2) a direct comparison of hierarchical architectures against the random filter bank, and (3) a clearer discussion of how the proposed method’s limitations (e.g., artifacts, inferiority to non-parametric synthesis) affect the validity of its core claim. With these revisions, the work would make a stronger contribution to the field. As it stands, the current conclusions require further validation.
