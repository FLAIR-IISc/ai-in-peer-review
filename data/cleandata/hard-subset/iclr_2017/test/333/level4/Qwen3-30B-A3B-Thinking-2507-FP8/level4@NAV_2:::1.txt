

1. The paper presents a compelling and well-executed investigation into the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis. It introduces a strong minimal baseline using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, demonstrating that high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved without deep hierarchical representations or pre-trained feature spaces. The authors provide thorough empirical evidence comparing various single-layer architectures (including Fourier, K-means, and random filters) against the state-of-the-art VGG-based model, showing that their approach often rivals or even surpasses existing methods in perceptual quality. This work makes a significant contribution by challenging the prevailing assumption that deep networks with supervised training are necessary for natural image generation.

2. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper's core insight is well-supported and represents an important theoretical contribution to the field, demonstrating that natural texture synthesis does not fundamentally require deep hierarchical representations or pre-trained features.

3. The key strength of this work is the careful experimental design that systematically tests the necessity of different components in texture synthesis algorithms. The authors provide convincing evidence through both qualitative comparisons and quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss metric. The finding that random filters perform as well as or better than trained filters is particularly noteworthy and challenges previous assumptions in the field. The multi-scale model's superior performance demonstrates that the spatial extent of receptive fields, rather than network depth, is critical for capturing texture statistics at multiple scales.

4. To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
   - Clarifying the relationship between the VGG-based loss and perceptual quality, particularly for textures with highly regular structures (like walls and grids) where the loss metric appears to fail.
   - Including a more detailed comparison with non-parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli) to better contextualize the quality of the synthesized textures.
   - Expanding the discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis, as this is a crucial aspect for practical applications.
   - Adding a brief analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed approach compared to the VGG-based model, particularly for larger texture sizes.

5. I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
   - How does the paper's observation that random filters perform as well as trained filters relate to the authors' earlier claim in the introduction that "networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images"?
   - For the textures where the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantages (e.g., brick wall, grids), what specific perceptual aspects are missing in the VGG loss metric that the single-layer model better captures?
   - Could the authors provide additional evidence regarding whether the multi-scale approach's success is primarily due to the combination of filter sizes or the increased receptive field size, particularly for textures with very regular structures?
