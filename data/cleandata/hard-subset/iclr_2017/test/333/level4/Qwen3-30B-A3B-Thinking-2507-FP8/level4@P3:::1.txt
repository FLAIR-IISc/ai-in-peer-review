

**Summary:**  
This paper makes a compelling contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in natural texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are essential. Through rigorous empirical evaluation of single-layer convolutional networks with various filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), the authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated without depth, pooling, or supervised training. Their key insight—that random filters can match or exceed learned filters in performance—represents a significant conceptual advance. The multi-scale model, in particular, often rivals the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach (Gatys et al., 2015a), suggesting that hierarchical depth primarily serves efficiency rather than quality. The paper's thorough ablation studies and introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric (VGG loss) significantly strengthen the field's methodological toolkit.

**Decision:**  
Accept with minor revisions.

**Key Reasons:**  
1. The paper rigorously tests a critical, previously unexamined question about the necessity of deep representations for texture synthesis.  
2. The empirical evidence is robust, with extensive comparisons across multiple architectures and datasets, and the multi-scale model's performance consistently challenges the field's assumptions.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
The paper's strength lies in its clear experimental design and thoughtful ablation studies. The authors systematically isolate variables (depth, filter type, scale) to show that neither hierarchical structure nor pre-training is indispensable. For instance, their comparison of single-scale models with varying filter sizes (Fig. 2) reveals that multi-scale combination—not just receptive field size—enables modeling of both local and global texture statistics. The finding that random filters outperform learned ones (Fig. 1, Table 1) is particularly surprising and well-supported, with the authors credibly attributing this to a refined optimization procedure rather than a methodological flaw. The quantitative evaluation via VGG loss (Section 5) also provides a valuable alternative to subjective human assessment, aligning with the paper's goal of scientific rigor.

While the draft review rightly notes that the synthesized textures are not perfect (e.g., occasional artifacts in highly structured textures like grids), this is a limitation of texture synthesis *in general*, not a flaw specific to the proposed method. The paper's results still significantly outperform earlier parametric approaches (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) and often rival the VGG baseline. The apparent superiority of VGG in the inpainting task (Fig. 7) reflects a different evaluation metric—perceptual similarity versus structural consistency—and does not undermine the core claim about the necessity of deep representations for *synthesis*.

**Additional Feedback:**  
To further strengthen the paper, consider these suggestions:  
1. **Contextualize limitations:** Briefly discuss why the method might struggle with highly regular textures (e.g., grids) compared to VGG. Is this due to the optimization procedure or inherent limitations of single-layer statistics?  
2. **Expand the "why" of random filters:** The paper notes that random filters work well but doesn't deeply explore why. A short discussion on how random filters might implicitly capture statistical regularities (e.g., via the central limit theorem) would add theoretical depth.  
3. **Clarify the variability trade-off:** The paper touches on the perceptual similarity vs. variability balance (Section 7) but could more explicitly link this to the observed artifacts in highly structured textures.  

**Questions for Clarification:**  
1. The paper states that "random filters perform on par or better than filters trained on natural images." However, the K-means-363 model (trained on ImageNet) slightly outperforms random filters in some cases (Fig. 1). Could you clarify whether this discrepancy is systematic or dataset-dependent?  
2. The multi-scale model uses 1024 filters across scales but is computationally expensive. Would a hierarchical model (e.g., stacked layers with small filters) achieve similar multi-scale performance more efficiently? If so, why does the paper find hierarchical architectures inferior in quality?  
3. The paper notes that VGG loss fails for "very regular man-made structures" (e.g., grids). How might this limitation inform future work on perceptual metrics for texture synthesis?  

In summary, this paper makes a strong case that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for natural texture synthesis, a finding with broad implications for generative modeling. The experiments are thorough, the claims are well-supported, and the proposed minimal baseline offers a valuable new direction for the field. With minor revisions addressing the above points, this work would make an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
