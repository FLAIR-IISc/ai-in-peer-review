

This paper makes a compelling contribution by rigorously investigating the essential components required for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that hierarchical feature representations (depth) and pretraining on natural images are indispensable, demonstrating instead that high-quality textures can be generated from a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. Their analysis systematically evaluates multiple filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and scales across diverse textures, revealing that random filters often match or exceed the perceptual quality of state-of-the-art multi-layer models. The work is well-motivated, positioning itself clearly against Gatys et al. (2015) and prior texture synthesis literature, and provides a thorough empirical validation through both qualitative comparisons and a novel quantitative metric based on VGG loss.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper should be accepted for its **novel conceptual insight** (depth/pretraining are not necessary for texture synthesis) and **rigorous empirical validation** (extensive ablation across filter types/scales, including multi-scale architectures that rival SOTA). These contributions significantly advance the understanding of deep network representations in generative tasks.

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Conceptual novelty and rigor**: The core claim—that random filters suffice—directly contradicts prior work (e.g., Gatys et al. 2015a’s assertion that random weights fail) and is convincingly supported by systematic experiments. The authors test 8 distinct single-layer architectures (e.g., Fourier-363, Random-3267) and a multi-scale variant, demonstrating that perceptual quality correlates with filter count (not training) and that multi-scale combinations capture long-range statistics missed by single-scale models. Crucially, they quantify results using VGG-based loss (Eq. 7), moving beyond subjective human evaluation. This methodological shift strengthens the paper’s scientific rigor.

2. **Thorough contextualization**: The paper adeptly places its work within the texture synthesis literature (Section 1), clarifying how it diverges from prior efforts (e.g., "we go the opposite way" from adding constraints). The discussion of limitations (e.g., computational inefficiency of large filters) and connections to maximum entropy theory (Section 7) shows deep engagement with the field. The multi-scale model’s superiority (Fig. 4) and the observation that random filters outperform trained ones (e.g., Random-3267 vs. K-means-3267) are particularly compelling.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
To further strengthen the paper, consider these points:  
- **Quantify human evaluation**: While VGG loss is a useful proxy, explicitly validating it against human ratings (e.g., for 5–10 textures) would solidify the metric’s reliability. The paper notes VGG loss fails for "regular man-made structures" (e.g., grids)—a limitation worth addressing with a small-scale human study.  
- **Clarify variability trade-offs**: Section 7 rightly identifies the perceptual similarity/variability tension but lacks concrete examples. Adding a brief analysis (e.g., "For texture X, samples from random filters show Y% higher entropy than VGG-based synthesis") would make this insight more actionable.  
- **Streamline the filter descriptions**: The section on network architectures (Section 2) is dense. A concise table summarizing filter types, counts, and key properties (e.g., "Random: 363 filters, uniform, no training") would improve readability without adding length.  

**Clarifying Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states random filters "often rival" VGG-based synthesis (Fig. 4), yet the VGG loss (Eq. 7) is paradoxically *lower* for multi-scale samples than for VGG-based samples in some cases (e.g., grids). Could you clarify whether this indicates the VGG loss *over*-penalizes texture regularity, or if the multi-scale model’s optimization implicitly captures higher-order statistics the VGG loss doesn’t fully measure?  
2. The multi-scale model uses 1024 filters across scales (3×3, 11×11, 55×55), while single-scale models use 1024 filters *at one scale*. How does the receptive field size (55×55 vs. 11×11) specifically contribute to capturing long-range structures, versus the number of filters? Could a single-scale model with 55×55 filters (but fewer total filters) achieve similar results?  

This work offers a paradigm-shifting perspective on deep networks in generative tasks, with clear implications for efficiency and model design. The thorough analysis, rigorous evaluation, and thoughtful discussion of limitations make it a strong candidate for ICLR 2017. The minor suggestions above would further elevate its impact, but are not required for acceptance.
