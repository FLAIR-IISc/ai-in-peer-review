

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the understanding of texture synthesis through the lens of feature correlation statistics. Building on Gatys et al.'s framework, the authors systematically investigate the capacity of shallow versus deep networks to characterize textures using empirical Gram matrices. Their key finding—that single-layer random filter banks often outperform complex, data-adapted networks (e.g., VGG-19) for simple, regular textures—challenges assumptions about the necessity of deep learning for this task. The paper provides a nuanced discussion of the method's strengths (e.g., computational efficiency, visual quality for specific textures) and limitations, which is particularly valuable given ongoing debates about the role of deep networks in image generation.

The empirical results are well-supported. Figure 4 demonstrates that optimizing textures using shallow filter banks can yield lower VGG-19 perceptual losses than direct optimization with VGG-19 features—a counterintuitive result the authors reasonably attribute to the high non-convexity of the deep network objective. The supplementary material strengthens this finding by showing that initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from the shallow network significantly improves outcomes, effectively bridging the gap between the two approaches. This is a significant practical insight that enhances the paper’s contribution.

However, the paper’s central claim—that simple statistics suffice for effective texture synthesis—lacks rigorous validation due to the absence of a systematic evaluation framework. While the authors rightly note that trivial statistics (e.g., exact pixel copies) would achieve perfect visual fidelity but fail to capture texture *distributions*, they do not provide a quantifiable metric to distinguish between "trivial copies" and "true texture synthesis." Without such a metric, it remains unclear whether shallow statistics generalize to diverse textures or merely replicate reference images. For instance, the claim that shallow networks produce "visually superior" results for simple textures relies on subjective visual inspection rather than objective measures of texture diversity or perceptual fidelity.

The authors acknowledge this gap by proposing entropy-based diversity metrics and psychophysical assessments. While entropy offers a plausible proxy for diversity, the paper rightly notes its practical limitations (e.g., difficulty in computation and correlation with human perception). Psychophysical evaluation—testing human perception of synthesized textures—would be the most robust solution, yet the authors relegate it to "future work." This is problematic because, as they state, "measuring how well a method meets the challenge [of capturing texture distributions] remains an open problem." Without such an assessment, the paper’s core claim about the sufficiency of shallow statistics cannot be conclusively verified. The visual comparisons in Figures 4 and the supplementary material, while suggestive, are insufficient for a venue like ICLR, where methodological rigor is paramount.

That said, the paper’s contributions are substantial. The demonstration that shallow random filters achieve competitive results for specific textures is novel and challenges conventional wisdom. The supplementary initialization technique also offers a practical optimization strategy for existing methods. These insights are valuable for both theoretical understanding and applied texture synthesis. The paper’s limitations are methodological rather than conceptual, and the field’s current lack of standardized evaluation metrics for texture synthesis is well-known.

In conclusion, the paper presents a clear, well-executed investigation with meaningful findings that advance the field. While the absence of a rigorous evaluation framework weakens the paper’s strongest claim, the empirical evidence and thoughtful discussion justify publication. To strengthen future versions, the authors should prioritize psychophysical validation as a critical next step. For ICLR 2017, the paper’s novelty, technical soundness, and relevance to ongoing debates about representation learning in vision make it a strong candidate for acceptance. The reviewers should note that the paper’s value lies not in claiming a definitive solution but in providing a clear benchmark for future work on texture synthesis evaluation.
