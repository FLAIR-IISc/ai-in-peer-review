

This paper makes a valuable contribution by rigorously analyzing the conditions enabling the generation of natural-looking textures. The authors establish a novel theoretical framework that identifies critical factors—such as spectral properties and spatial coherence—underpinning perceptually valid texture synthesis. Their analysis is notably thorough, combining mathematical derivations with empirical validation across diverse datasets, yielding insights that significantly advance our understanding of texture generation mechanisms. The findings are both compelling and unexpected, particularly regarding the role of higher-order statistical constraints in avoiding artifacts common in existing methods.

The primary strength lies in the paper’s conceptual clarity and depth of analysis. The authors effectively bridge theoretical insights with practical implications, demonstrating how their framework explains limitations in prior work (e.g., GAN-based texture synthesis) and offers actionable pathways for improvement. Figures 3 and 4 provide particularly convincing visual evidence of the proposed conditions’ impact, making the core argument accessible and persuasive. This work represents a meaningful step toward principled texture generation, moving beyond ad hoc empirical approaches.

That said, the evaluation methodology requires refinement to fully substantiate the paper’s claims. While the ablation studies (Section 4.2) effectively isolate key variables, the comparative baselines lack depth. Specifically, the paper omits comparisons against recent state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods (e.g., those leveraging diffusion models or implicit neural representations), which would strengthen the argument for the proposed conditions’ generality. Additionally, quantitative metrics (e.g., FID, perceptual scores) are underutilized; their inclusion would provide objective support for the qualitative observations. However, these limitations do not undermine the paper’s core theoretical contribution, as the analysis of *why* certain conditions succeed remains robust regardless of specific implementation details. The suggested improvements are largely methodological and would be natural extensions for future work, not fundamental flaws in the current study.

The writing is generally clear, though some sections (e.g., Section 2.1) could benefit from tighter phrasing to reduce redundancy. The technical exposition is sound, and the paper’s reproducibility is enhanced by the public code release. No major methodological or ethical concerns were identified.

In summary, this paper delivers a significant theoretical advance with strong empirical backing. The analysis of texture generation conditions is original, rigorous, and well-motivated, offering clear value to the computer vision and graphics communities. While the evaluation could be expanded, this does not detract from the paper’s core contribution, which is substantial and well-executed. I recommend acceptance as a strong contribution to ICLR 2017.
