- Main contribution: Shows high-quality natural textures can be synthesized from single-layer networks with random filters, challenging the need for deep supervised representations.
- Decision: Recommend acceptance.
- Novelty: Provides a strong minimal baseline revealing depth and supervised filter training are not indispensable for texture synthesis.
- Experimental rigor: Thorough comparisons across single-layer architectures and filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned).
- Key result: Random-filter single-layer models often rival or exceed VGG-based (Gatys et al.) state-of-the-art in perceptual texture quality.
- Multi-scale finding: Combining multiple filter scales is crucial to capture both small- and long-range texture structures, explaining multi-scale superiority.
- Quantitative evaluation: Introduces a VGG-based texture quality metric for more objective assessment than human inspection alone.
- Explanation of discrepancy: Attributes earlier negative findings on random-weight networks to differences in optimization—more elaborate optimization here yields better results.
- Surprising insight: Random filters can perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural image statistics.
- Practical caveat: Multi-scale model can be computationally inefficient due to very large filters (up to 55×55).
- Generalization concern: Unclear whether findings extend to more complex tasks like artistic style transfer or general image generation.
- Limitations of VGG loss: VGG-based loss sometimes fails on very regular textures (e.g., bricks/grids); needs further analysis of which texture characteristics cause failures.
- Optimization question: Authors claim single-layer optimization is less prone to local minima than deep models—request more insight and implications for broader deep learning optimization.
