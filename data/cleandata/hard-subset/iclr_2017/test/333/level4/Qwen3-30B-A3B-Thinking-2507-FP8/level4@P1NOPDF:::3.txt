

The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper investigates which deep or shallow networks perform well within this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks—consisting of a single filter bank with random weights—work surprisingly well, often producing visually superior results for simple, regular textures compared to complex, data-adapted filters like those in VGG-19. The paper also offers a thoughtful discussion of the strengths and limitations of such texture synthesis methods.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can yield texture images with lower VGG-19-based loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-convex VGG-19 cost function, a reasonable explanation. The supplementary material further demonstrates that initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from shallow networks improves performance, providing a useful complement to the original experiments.

The main limitation is the lack of systematic comparison against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that could perfectly reproduce any reference texture (e.g., by directly copying pixel values), yielding high visual quality but failing to capture the underlying texture distribution. The goal, instead, is to model the distribution, and no robust metric exists to evaluate how well methods achieve this. While empirical results align with the intuition that simple statistics suffice for quality and diversity (compared to complex alternatives), conclusive validation remains elusive.

The authors propose measuring diversity via entropy, which is reasonable but, as they acknowledge, difficult to implement practically. Crucially, entropy alone does not address visual quality. They also suggest psychophysical assessment as the most viable path forward, though they deem it suitable for future work. This is a valid acknowledgment of the evaluation challenge.

Overall, given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation, the paper’s strengths—including the insightful analysis of shallow networks and the supplementary optimization strategy—justify publication. However, a psychophysical assessment would significantly strengthen the claims by objectively verifying the visual intuitions currently supported only by qualitative inspection of figures in the paper and supplementary material. Without it, the conclusion that simple statistics are sufficient remains largely empirical. That said, the core contributions are compelling, and the paper makes a meaningful step toward understanding texture synthesis.
