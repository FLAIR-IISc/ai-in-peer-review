- Proposes a minimalist single-layer convolutional model with random filters that synthesizes high-quality natural textures without depth, pooling, or supervised feature training.

- Key insight: deep architectures and pre-trained filters are not necessary for effective natural texture synthesis.

- Empirical claim: multi-scale random-filter single-layer model often rivals the VGG-based (Gatys et al., 2015a) approach in perceptual quality.

- Systematic experiments compare filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervised), filter sizes, and feature map counts, showing more feature maps typically improve perceptual quality.

- Counterintuitive finding: random filters perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural image statistics.

- Multi-scale filters and receptive field size/scale diversity are more important than network depth or pre-training for texture quality.

- Recommendation: accept — the paper clarifies minimal requirements for texture synthesis and challenges prevailing assumptions.

- Suggested improvement 1: include direct comparisons with classical non-parametric methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) to contextualize gains.

- Suggested improvement 2: add quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss (E(y), Eq.7) to complement qualitative human assessments and explain counterintuitive loss results.

- Suggested improvement 3: provide concrete details on optimization adjustments that enabled better results with random filters compared to prior random-weight attempts.

- Open question 1: would a hierarchical model with random weights (depth) match the multi-scale single-layer model, isolating the role of receptive field size versus multi-scale combination?

- Open question 2: how well does the VGG-based loss capture perceptual quality across textures (e.g., brick walls), and what alternative metrics better reflect perceptual differences?

- Theoretical contribution: frames texture synthesis in a MaxEnt perspective, emphasizing the importance of distance metrics and perceptual equivalence partitions for finite-size images.

- Practical benefit: single-layer approach offers computational efficiency advantages despite requiring large filters.
