

This paper provides a compelling and well-executed investigation into the essential components for natural texture synthesis. It rigorously challenges the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are indispensable, demonstrating instead that high-quality textures can be generated using a single-layer CNN with random filters—often matching or exceeding state-of-the-art results. The analysis is thorough, with extensive ablation studies across filter types (Fourier, K-means, random) and architectures (single-scale vs. multi-scale), revealing that the number of feature maps and multi-scale filtering are critical for perceptual quality, while depth, pooling, and supervised training are not.  

Accept. The paper successfully identifies and validates that hierarchical depth and pre-training are unnecessary for high-quality texture synthesis, and its multi-scale random-filter approach consistently rivals the Gatys et al. (2015a) benchmark.  

The core contribution is robustly supported: (1) Fig. 4 demonstrates that the multi-scale random model achieves perceptual quality comparable to SOTA on 18/20 textures, with superior performance on structured textures (e.g., brick walls); (2) Fig. 2 establishes that multi-scale filtering (not just large receptive fields) is essential for capturing short-, mid-, and long-range correlations; and (3) the authors address the counterintuitive finding that random filters outperform trained ones by attributing it to optimization tuning, supported by Appendix A. The VGG-based loss metric (Section 5) provides a quantifiable, reproducible evaluation framework that aligns with human perception for most textures, though its limitations for highly regular structures (e.g., grids) are appropriately acknowledged in Section 7.  

To further strengthen the work: (1) Explicitly quantify the human evaluation gap (e.g., "While VGG loss correlates with human judgment in 18/20 cases, could a small-scale human study confirm this for the remaining textures?"); (2) Clarify how the multi-scale model’s computational inefficiency (large filters) compares to hierarchical alternatives in practice (Section 7).  

Key questions for authors:  
1. For textures where VGG loss fails (e.g., grids), how would you modify the loss function or optimization to better align with perceptual quality?  
2. In Section 6, the multi-scale model outperforms VGG synthesis on some samples despite optimizing a different loss—does this indicate the VGG loss is suboptimal for certain textures, or is it an artifact of the optimization process?
