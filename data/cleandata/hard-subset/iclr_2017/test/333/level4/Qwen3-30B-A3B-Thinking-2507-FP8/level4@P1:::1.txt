

This work proposes a compelling and well-executed baseline for parametric texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters—challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are essential. Their empirical results show that the multi-scale random filter model often rivals the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach (Gatys et al., 2015a), suggesting that depth and feature pre-training may be less critical than previously believed. The paper’s systematic comparison of filter types (Fourier, k-means, random) and scales provides strong evidence for this claim, and the introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss is a valuable contribution.  

**Decision: Reject**  
The paper’s core claim—that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are unnecessary for texture synthesis—lacks sufficient support. Two key issues prevent acceptance: (1) the evaluation incompletely addresses the role of *depth* in hierarchical representations, and (2) the conclusion overgeneralizes beyond the specific context of texture synthesis.  

**Supporting arguments**  
First, the paper focuses exclusively on *single-layer* networks but fails to rigorously test *shallow hierarchical architectures* as a baseline for depth. For instance, a two-layer network with random filters (e.g., 11×11 filters followed by 5×5 filters) could achieve similar multi-scale capabilities without the computational inefficiency of the single-layer multi-scale model. The authors acknowledge that hierarchical models improve synthesis *efficiency* (Section 7), yet they do not demonstrate whether such models also match the *quality* of their single-layer baseline. Without this comparison, the claim that "depth is not indispensable" remains unsubstantiated.  

Second, the conclusion overgeneralizes to "natural image generation" broadly, while the paper only studies *texture synthesis*. The authors note that their findings do not extend to tasks like style transfer (Section 7), yet the abstract and discussion frame the result as a general principle for natural image generation. This overreach weakens the contribution: the work is a significant advance for texture synthesis but does not overturn assumptions about deep networks in other generation tasks.  

**Additional feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, address these points:  
1. **Clarify scope**: Explicitly limit claims to texture synthesis (not "natural image generation"). The discussion of style transfer (Section 7) should be retracted or qualified.  
2. **Expand depth comparison**: Test a shallow hierarchical architecture (e.g., two layers with random filters) to isolate the impact of depth versus multi-scale filtering.  
3. **Quantify non-parametric gaps**: The paper notes that results "do not match the quality of non-parametric methods" (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli), but this is not contextualized. A brief comparison to recent non-parametric baselines (e.g., Aittala et al., 2016) would clarify the baseline’s significance.  

**Key questions for authors**  
1. How would a two-layer hierarchical network with random filters (e.g., 11×11 followed by 5×5) perform on the same texture synthesis task? Does it match the perceptual quality of your single-layer multi-scale model *without* the 55×55 filter inefficiency?  
2. The paper states that "VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantage for regular structures" (e.g., brick walls). Yet Figure 4 shows VGG samples for these textures are often inferior to your model. Could this indicate that the VGG loss *does* correlate with perceptual quality for these cases, and the failure is instead due to optimization limitations?  
3. In Appendix A, Figure 5 shows your single-layer model improves VGG synthesis. Does this suggest that *optimization* (not depth) is the key bottleneck for VGG-based synthesis? If so, how does this reconcile with your claim that depth is unnecessary?
