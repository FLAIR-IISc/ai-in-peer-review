

This paper makes a compelling contribution by challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks are essential for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters—requiring neither depth, pooling, nor pre-training on natural images—can generate textures of perceptual quality rivaling state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Gatys et al., 2015a). Through systematic experimentation across diverse filter banks (Fourier, K-means, random), they show that texture synthesis success hinges on the *number* of feature maps and *multi-scale filtering*, not network depth or trained filters. Crucially, they introduce a quantitative metric (VGG-based reconstruction loss) to complement human evaluation, revealing that their shallow model often achieves lower loss than the VGG-based synthesis itself—a finding attributed to more robust optimization.

**Decision: Accept**  
*Key reasons:* (1) The work fundamentally challenges a core assumption in the field with rigorous empirical evidence; (2) It provides a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis that outperforms hand-crafted methods and rivals deep network approaches, advancing both theoretical understanding and practical efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
The paper’s core strength lies in its clear, targeted investigation of *what is truly indispensable* for texture synthesis. By isolating variables (depth, training, pooling), the authors decisively show these are not required. Their multi-scale random filter model consistently produces high-quality textures (Fig. 4), with perceptual quality often exceeding Gatys et al.’s method—particularly for structured textures (e.g., brick walls, grids). The quantitative analysis using VGG loss (Section 5) provides a valuable objective benchmark, revealing that the shallow model’s optimization is less prone to local minima (Appendix A, Fig. 5), explaining its superior performance. The discussion of the *trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability* (Section 7) is particularly insightful, acknowledging that current metrics (like VGG loss) imperfectly capture human perception—a nuanced perspective that elevates the work beyond mere empirical claims.

The paper’s limitations are acknowledged but do not undermine its contribution. While texture diversity remains challenging to quantify (as the authors note), the work provides a concrete framework for future progress. The proposed psychophysical assessment (Appendix A, Fig. 7) is a practical, actionable step toward better evaluation. The authors correctly position the VGG loss as a *proxy* for perceptual quality, not a perfect metric—this self-awareness strengthens their conclusions. Critically, the paper does not claim to solve texture diversity *alone* but demonstrates that shallow networks can achieve high similarity *without* depth, a significant shift in perspective.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**  
1. **Clarify the "minimal baseline" scope:** Explicitly state that the work focuses *only* on texture synthesis (not general image generation), as the title’s phrasing ("natural textures") could be misinterpreted.  
2. **Strengthen the diversity discussion:** Briefly elaborate how the shallow model’s optimization bias toward perceptual similarity *could* be adjusted (e.g., via loss scaling or stopping criteria) to improve variability, as hinted in Section 7.  
3. **Quantify the VGG loss discrepancy:** In Section 6, add a sentence noting the *magnitude* of the VGG loss advantage (e.g., "multi-scale model achieves 15% lower loss on average") to emphasize the significance beyond qualitative observation.  
4. **Address potential overfitting:** Acknowledge that exact reconstruction of reference textures (mentioned in Section 7) is a finite-size effect, reinforcing why Gram matrices are treated as soft constraints.

**Key Questions for Authors:**  
1. The paper notes that the multi-scale model’s receptive fields (up to 55×55) are large. How would the results change if these were replaced by a *deep* network with equivalent receptive fields (e.g., 5 layers of 5×5 filters)? This would directly test if *depth* is unnecessary, not just *pre-training*.  
2. The VGG loss is lower for the shallow model on some textures (e.g., grids), yet human perception may still prefer Gatys et al.’s results. Can you provide a specific example where the VGG loss metric *misaligns* with human preference, and how the model could be adjusted to address this?  

This work makes a clear, well-executed contribution to understanding the core requirements for natural texture synthesis. By demonstrating that shallow networks with random filters suffice—and even outperform deep models in key scenarios—it reframes a central question in the field. The paper’s rigor, clarity, and thoughtful discussion of limitations (especially regarding evaluation) make it a strong candidate for ICLR. The proposed psychophysical assessment is a valuable next step, but the current work’s theoretical and empirical insights are sufficiently robust for acceptance.
