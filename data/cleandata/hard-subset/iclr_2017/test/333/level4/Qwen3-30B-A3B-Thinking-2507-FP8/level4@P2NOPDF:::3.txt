

The framework of Gatys et al. established that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an effective characterization of visual textures. This paper systematically investigates which network architectures—deep or shallow—best suit this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks, comprising a single random-weight filter bank, perform remarkably well for simple, regular textures, often producing visually superior results compared to complex, data-adapted filters like those in VGG-19. The authors offer a thoughtful discussion of the strengths and limitations of such texture synthesis approaches.

Figure 4 demonstrates that optimizing textures using shallow filter banks can yield lower VGG-19 loss than direct optimization of the VGG-19 objective, attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. The supplementary material further supports this by showing that initializing VGG-19-based optimization with results from shallow networks improves performance, providing a practical refinement to the core methodology.

The primary limitation is the absence of systematic comparison against a quantifiable objective. While trivial statistics (e.g., those producing exact copies of reference textures) could achieve high visual quality, the goal is to capture the underlying texture distribution. Measuring how well methods approximate this distribution remains an open challenge. Empirical results suggest shallow statistics suffice for quality and diversity relative to complex alternatives, but without a standardized metric, this claim cannot be conclusively validated.

The authors propose diversity metrics based on entropy, acknowledging practical difficulties in implementation. They also suggest psychophysical assessments as the most viable evaluation method, though they defer this to future work. However, as noted, entropy alone does not address visual quality, and psychophysical studies would be essential to resolve these evaluation gaps.

Despite these challenges, the paper makes a significant contribution. The empirical evidence is robust, and the insights into shallow networks' efficacy for texture synthesis are compelling. Given the inherent difficulty of evaluating image generation tasks, the paper’s strengths outweigh its limitations. The findings advance understanding of texture synthesis mechanisms, and the supplementary material strengthens the core arguments. Publication is justified, though future work incorporating psychophysical assessments would provide definitive validation of the observed visual improvements. The authors are encouraged to pursue this direction to solidify their intuitive findings, which currently rely on qualitative inspection of results.
