- Core contribution: Shows high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved with a single convolutional layer of random, data-independent filters, challenging the need for depth and pretraining.  
- Main claim: Depth and pretraining are not necessary for natural texture synthesis; filter count and scale matter more.  
- Filter comparison: Systematically evaluates Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters across textures and scales.  
- Key result: Random filters often match or exceed perceptual quality of multi-layer, pretrained (VGG) models.  
- Multi-scale finding: A multi-scale architecture captures long-range structure and rivals state-of-the-art multi-layer models.  
- Quantitative metric: Introduces a VGG-based loss (Eq. 7) for objective evaluation beyond subjective comparisons.  
- Empirical rigor: Tests eight single-layer architectures and ablations, showing perceptual quality correlates with filter count, not training.  
- Contextualization: Positions work against Gatys et al. (2015) and connects to maximum entropy theory and texture literature.  
- Limitations noted: Large filters can be computationally inefficient; VGG loss may fail on regular man-made structures.  
- Recommendation: Accept — novel conceptual insight plus rigorous empirical validation merits publication.  
- Suggestion 1: Validate VGG loss against human ratings for a subset of textures to confirm it correlates with perception.  
- Suggestion 2: Provide concrete analysis/examples quantifying the perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-off.  
- Suggestion 3: Streamline network/filter descriptions with a concise table of filter types, counts, and properties.  
- Clarifying questions: Does VGG loss over-penalize regularity or miss higher-order stats where multi-scale models score better?  
- Clarifying questions: How much does receptive field size versus total filter count contribute to capturing long-range structures (e.g., single-scale 55×55 vs. multi-scale)?
