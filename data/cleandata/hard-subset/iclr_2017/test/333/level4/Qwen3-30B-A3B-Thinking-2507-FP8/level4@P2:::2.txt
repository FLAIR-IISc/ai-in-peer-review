

This paper presents a compelling challenge to a core assumption in deep learning-based texture synthesis: that hierarchical deep networks with supervisedly trained features are essential for generating natural textures. The authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer CNN with random filters, no pooling, and no pre-training—contradicting earlier work by Gatys et al. (2015a) that claimed random weights fail for this task. The key insight is that a well-tuned optimization procedure (using L-BFGS-B with scaled gradients) enables these minimal models to rival state-of-the-art results. The paper’s thorough ablation study compares eight single-layer architectures (including Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters with 363 or 3267 feature maps), revealing that: (1) a large number of feature maps is crucial for perceptual quality, (2) random filters often outperform trained ones, and (3) multi-scale filter combinations (e.g., 3×3, 11×11, 55×55) capture long-range correlations better than single-scale models.  

**Decision: Accept.**  
Two key reasons support this:  
1. The paper rigorously addresses the question of *what aspects of deep networks are indispensable* for texture synthesis, demonstrating that depth, pooling, and pre-training are not required—directly contradicting prior work.  
2. The experimental design is exceptionally thorough, with quantitative evaluation via a VGG-based loss (replacing subjective human inspection) and extensive comparisons across filter types, scales, and architectures.  

**Supporting Arguments:**  
The paper’s contribution is both theoretically significant and empirically robust. First, it reframes a central problem in the field: instead of asking *how to improve* deep-network-based synthesis (as most prior work does), it asks *what is absolutely necessary*, yielding a surprising minimal baseline. The finding that random filters outperform learned ones (e.g., K-means) is particularly striking and well-supported by results (Fig. 4), suggesting that the statistical properties of natural textures may be captured more effectively by generic filter banks than by task-specific feature learning. Second, the ablation study systematically isolates critical factors: the multi-scale model’s superiority (Fig. 2) proves that combining filter sizes—*not* just larger receptive fields—is key, while the failure of ReLU-less models (Fig. 2, right) underscores the role of non-linearities. The quantitative VGG loss metric (Eq. 7) also provides a valuable, objective benchmark, though the authors rightly acknowledge its limitations for highly regular textures (e.g., grids). Critically, the paper’s claims are fully supported: the synthesis procedure is well-motivated (Sec. 4), the results are reproducible (details in Sec. 2), and the comparison to Gatys et al. (2015a) is fair and comprehensive (Fig. 4).  

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**  
- Clarify the *why* behind random filters’ success. The paper notes they "often rival" trained filters but does not deeply analyze why (e.g., do random filters better approximate the statistical invariants of textures?). A brief discussion of this could strengthen the theoretical contribution.  
- Address the variability vs. similarity trade-off more explicitly. The paper acknowledges that high perceptual similarity may reduce sample diversity (Sec. 7), but a sentence on how to tune optimization for better variability (e.g., early stopping) would make the method more practical.  
- In Fig. 4, include a qualitative comparison of VGG loss vs. human judgment for the "wall" texture (where VGG loss fails) to better illustrate this limitation.  

**Questions for Authors:**  
1. The multi-scale model uses 1024 filters (3×3, 11×11, 55×55), but the single-scale models use 1024 filters *of one size*. Did you test a single-scale model with 1024 filters *all of size 55×55* (to isolate the effect of scale diversity vs. receptive field size)?  
2. The paper states random filters "outperform" trained filters (e.g., K-means-363), but Gatys et al. (2015a) used a 19-layer network. Could the performance gap be partly due to the *number* of filters (363 vs. 19-layer features)? If so, how would a 19-layer network with 363 filters (not 19×363) compare?  
3. The VGG loss (Eq. 7) is used for evaluation, but the synthesis itself uses a single-layer model. How would the VGG loss perform *if directly optimized* on the single-layer model (e.g., minimizing Eq. 7 for the single-layer Gram matrix)? This would clarify whether the loss metric is inherently better for single-layer synthesis.  

This work is a significant step toward understanding *why* deep networks succeed in generative tasks. By showing that shallow random networks suffice for texture synthesis, it challenges the field to rethink the role of depth and pre-training—not as necessities, but as potential refinements. The paper’s clarity, rigor, and novel perspective make it a strong fit for ICLR.
