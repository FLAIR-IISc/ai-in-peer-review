- Challenges the necessity of hierarchical deep networks and pre-trained features for natural texture synthesis.  
- Shows single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can synthesize textures matching state-of-the-art.  
- Systematically evaluates single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, random) across designs.  
- Finds perceptual quality depends on number of feature maps and filter scale combination, not network depth or supervised training.  
- Reports random filters often outperform data-adapted (trained) filters.  
- Proposes a multi-scale model that matches or exceeds VGG-based synthesis and achieves lower VGG loss.  
- Frames texture synthesis as a maximum entropy problem and examines the role of perceptual distance metrics.  
- Decision: Accept — significant, counterintuitive contribution with strong empirical support.  
- Precisely targets which elements of hierarchical networks (depth, pooling, pre-training) are indispensable.  
- Well-motivated approach grounded in Gatys et al.; systematic ablation isolates key factors (filter count, scale, nonlinearity).  
- Empirical claims supported by robust results and objective VGG-loss evaluations, with supplementary validation.  
- Acknowledges evaluation limitations (VGG-loss is an imperfect proxy for perceptual quality).  
- Suggests clarifying VGG-loss as a proxy and discussing its failure cases (e.g., regular/grid textures).  
- Recommends concrete diversity evaluation (e.g., psychophysical test or inpainting task) instead of deferring to future work.  
- Advises quantifying multi-scale justification (explicit receptive field sizes and correlation with structural capture).  
- Requests clarification on the VGG-loss paradox: does lower loss reflect true perceptual quality or optimization advantage?  
- Asks for statistics on how often random filters outperform trained ones (e.g., percentage across textures).  
- Asks to disentangle improvements from filter scale diversity versus increased receptive field size with quantitative comparisons.
