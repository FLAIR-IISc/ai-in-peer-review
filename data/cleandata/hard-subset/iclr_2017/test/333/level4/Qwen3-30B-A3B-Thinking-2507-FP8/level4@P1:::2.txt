

This paper presents a compelling investigation into the essential components required for generating natural-looking textures using deep neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical representations with supervised training are necessary for natural texture synthesis, demonstrating instead that high-quality textures can be generated from single-layer networks with random filters. Their work is particularly significant because it provides a strong minimal baseline that reveals surprising insights about the role of network depth and filter training in texture generation. The authors conduct a thorough experimental analysis comparing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned), providing convincing evidence that neither network depth nor supervised filter training is indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis.

Decision: Accept

Key reasons: The paper makes a novel contribution by demonstrating that natural textures can be synthesized from single-layer networks with random filters, challenging the established belief that deep hierarchical representations are essential. The experimental design is rigorous, with comprehensive comparisons across multiple filter types and architectures, and the results consistently show that random filters can rival or even surpass state-of-the-art methods in perceptual quality.

Supporting arguments: The authors' claim is well-supported by their extensive experiments. They show that textures synthesized from single-layer networks with random filters often rival those generated by the VGG-based model (Gatys et al., 2015a), which is the current state-of-the-art. Their analysis of the multi-scale model reveals that combining different filter scales is crucial for capturing both small- and long-range texture structures, explaining why it outperforms single-scale models. The paper also makes an important contribution by introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model, which provides a more objective evaluation method than traditional human inspection.

The paper's most surprising and valuable insight is that random filters can perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural image statistics. This contradicts earlier findings by Gatys et al. (2015a) that random-weight networks fail to generate perceptually interesting images, and the authors provide a plausible explanation for this discrepancy: their more elaborate optimization procedure.

Additional feedback: While the paper's main contribution is strong, the authors might consider addressing two points to further strengthen their work. First, the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model (with large filters up to 55×55) could be discussed more thoroughly, as this is a practical limitation for real-world applications. Second, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their findings might generalize to other image generation tasks beyond texture synthesis, such as style transfer or image generation.

Questions for authors: 
1. The paper demonstrates that random filters can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis, but it's unclear whether this finding extends to more complex image generation tasks. Could you comment on how your findings might apply to tasks like artistic style transfer, where the hierarchical representation might be more crucial?
2. The authors note that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages for textures with very regular structures (e.g., brick walls or grids). Could you elaborate on why this happens and whether there are specific characteristics of these textures that make them particularly challenging for the VGG-based loss function?
3. The paper mentions that the single-layer model's optimization is less prone to local minima than deeper models. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and whether this has implications for other optimization problems in deep learning?

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of the essential components for natural image generation. By showing that deep hierarchical representations and supervised filter training are not indispensable for texture synthesis, the authors open up new avenues for simpler and potentially more efficient texture generation models. The work is well-executed, the experiments are thorough, and the findings are both surprising and important for the field. I recommend acceptance.
