

1. Summary: The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that natural texture synthesis can be effectively achieved using extremely simple architectures—specifically, a single convolutional layer with random filters, without requiring hierarchical depth, pooling, or pre-training on natural images. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence showing that such minimal models often rival or exceed the perceptual quality of state-of-the-art approaches based on deep networks like VGG-19. The work thoroughly investigates the role of filter design (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), feature map count, and multi-scale architectures, offering valuable insights into the fundamental requirements for texture synthesis.

2. Decision: Accept. Two key reasons support this: (1) The paper conclusively shows that neither network depth nor pre-training on natural images is indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis, overturning a key assumption in prior work; (2) The authors provide robust empirical validation through extensive experiments and a novel quantitative evaluation using VGG-based loss, demonstrating that shallow random-filter models achieve perceptual quality comparable to or exceeding deeper models.

3. Supporting arguments: The core findings are rigorously validated. The authors systematically test diverse single-layer architectures (e.g., Random-363, Fourier-3267, Kmeans-Sample-3267) and demonstrate that random filters outperform trained filters for synthesis quality—a counterintuitive result they carefully contextualize by attributing prior failures to suboptimal optimization. Crucially, Figure 4 shows their multi-scale random model achieves lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization (Gatys et al.), with supplementary evidence (Appendix A) confirming that initializing VGG synthesis with shallow-network results further improves quality. The analysis of filter scale effects (Fig. 2) and the role of non-linearities (ReLU) provides mechanistic insight into why simple models succeed. The paper’s theoretical discussion of MaxEnt framework limitations and the necessity of soft constraints for finite-size textures is also insightful.

4. Additional feedback: These suggestions aim to strengthen the paper’s impact, not affect the acceptance decision. First, explicitly quantify the "perceptual similarity vs. variability" trade-off observed in Fig. 3 and Appendix A. For instance, compute entropy-based diversity metrics for synthesized samples across models (even if approximate) to complement the qualitative observations. Second, clarify the VGG loss's limitations in Fig. 4 (e.g., why it fails for regular textures like walls/grids) by discussing how the loss's sensitivity to global structure might interact with texture regularity. Third, briefly compare the computational efficiency of the multi-scale model (large filters) versus hierarchical alternatives in the main text—this is noted in Section 7 but merits emphasis for practical relevance.

5. Questions for authors: (1) How would you formally quantify the "variability" of synthesized textures for the random-filter models versus VGG-based synthesis? Specifically, what metrics (beyond visual inspection) would you recommend to rigorously measure the diversity of samples from the same reference texture? (2) Given that the VGG loss occasionally underestimates perceptual quality for highly regular textures (e.g., grids), could you elaborate on how the MaxEnt framework’s "soft constraints" (Section 7) might be adjusted to better capture such cases, and whether this would require new summary statistics beyond Gram matrices?
