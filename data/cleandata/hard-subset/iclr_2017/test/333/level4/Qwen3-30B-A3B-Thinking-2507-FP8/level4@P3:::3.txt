

The paper investigates whether hierarchical deep networks or pre-trained features are essential for natural texture synthesis, challenging the foundational assumptions of Gatys et al. (2015a). It demonstrates that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters—requiring neither depth, pooling, nor supervised training—can synthesize textures rivaling state-of-the-art results. The authors systematically evaluate diverse single-layer architectures (e.g., Fourier, K-means, random filters), showing that perceptual quality depends critically on the *number* of feature maps and *filter scale combination*, not network depth or filter training. Crucially, they reveal that random filters outperform data-adapted ones in many cases and propose a multi-scale model that matches or exceeds VGG-based synthesis (Figure 4), with lower VGG loss despite using a simpler optimization. The work also offers valuable insights into texture synthesis as a maximum entropy problem and the role of perceptual distance metrics.

**Decision: Accept**  
This paper makes a significant contribution by rigorously testing the necessity of deep hierarchical representations for texture synthesis. The core finding—that random shallow networks suffice—directly contradicts prior assumptions and is compellingly supported by extensive empirical results. The proposed multi-scale model achieves state-of-the-art perceptual quality while being conceptually minimal, addressing a key open question in the field.

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Specific question tackled**: The paper precisely identifies the gap in prior work: *Which elements of hierarchical deep networks (depth, pooling, pre-training) are indispensable for natural texture synthesis?* It moves beyond incremental improvements to fundamentally question the role of network architecture.  
2. **Well-motivated approach**: The motivation is clear and grounded in Gatys et al.’s framework, directly testing their assumptions. The systematic ablation (Section 6) isolates key factors (filter count, scale, nonlinearity), and the multi-scale model’s success (Figure 2) provides a strong, falsifiable counterpoint to the necessity of depth.  
3. **Claims supported**: Empirical results are robust and well-justified. The VGG-loss metric (Equation 7) objectively quantifies perceptual quality, and the multi-scale model’s consistent performance (Figure 4) and lower loss than VGG-based synthesis (Figure 4, third row) are compelling. The supplementary evidence (Appendix A, Figure 5) further validates that shallow networks yield superior initialization for deeper models. While the paper acknowledges limitations in evaluation (Section 7), the empirical evidence for the *core claim* is strong.  

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
To strengthen the paper’s impact, consider these points:  
- **Clarify the evaluation framework**: The paper rightly notes that perceptual quality lacks a perfect metric, but the current reliance on VGG-loss (which correlates with human judgment but has known limitations) could be more transparently framed. Explicitly state that VGG-loss serves as a *proxy* for perceptual quality, and discuss its failure cases (e.g., regular textures like grids in Figure 4) to contextualize results.  
- **Expand on diversity**: The paper’s discussion of variability (Section 7) is insightful but needs concrete steps. Instead of deferring to "future work," propose a simple psychophysical test (e.g., a texture inpainting task where humans distinguish reference vs. synthesized patches) as a baseline for assessing diversity, as suggested in the paper. This would directly address the "perceptual similarity vs. variability" trade-off.  
- **Refine the multi-scale justification**: While Figure 2 shows the importance of multiple filter scales, explicitly quantify the receptive field size (e.g., 55×55 vs. 11×11) and its correlation with structural capture (e.g., "55×55 filters model >90% of long-range correlations in pebble textures"). This would make the argument more rigorous.  

**Key Questions for Authors**  
To ensure confidence in the assessment, please clarify:  
1. The VGG-loss paradox (lower loss for shallow-synthesis than VGG-based synthesis) is attributed to better optimization. However, does the *perceptual quality* of shallow-synthesis samples (Figure 4) correlate with this lower loss? If so, could this indicate that VGG-loss *underestimates* perceptual quality for shallow models, or is it a genuine optimization advantage?  
2. The paper states that random filters outperform trained filters (e.g., K-means) but does not quantify *how often* this occurs across textures. Could you provide a statistical summary (e.g., "random filters win in 70% of textures") to strengthen the claim?  
3. For the multi-scale model, how much does the improvement stem from *filter scale diversity* versus simply *increased receptive field size*? The ablation in Figure 2 (single-scale variants) suggests scale diversity is key, but a quantitative comparison (e.g., "11×11 vs. 55×55: 15% higher VGG loss for 55×55 alone") would further solidify this.  

This work is a valuable contribution that rethinks core assumptions in texture synthesis. The empirical evidence is robust, the findings are counterintuitive yet well-supported, and the discussion of evaluation challenges is thoughtful. With minor clarifications on evaluation and the multi-scale rationale, it will be a strong ICLR paper.
