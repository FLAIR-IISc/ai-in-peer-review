- Proposes a single-layer convolutional network with random filters as a strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis, often rivaling VGG-based methods.  
- Shows that multi-scale random-filter models can generate high perceptual-quality natural textures, challenging the necessity of deep pre-trained features.  
- Provides a systematic comparison of filter types (Fourier, k-means, random) and scales.  
- Introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main critique 1: Paper fails to rigorously test the role of depth—no comparison to shallow hierarchical (e.g., two-layer) random-filter architectures.  
- Main critique 2: Conclusions overgeneralize beyond texture synthesis to "natural image generation," despite not evaluating other generation tasks.  
- Authors note hierarchical models improve efficiency but do not demonstrate whether shallow hierarchies match single-layer quality.  
- Recommendation: Clarify scope and limit claims to texture synthesis; retract or qualify style-transfer/generalization statements.  
- Recommendation: Run experiments on shallow hierarchical models (e.g., 11×11 → 5×5 random filters) to isolate depth vs multi-scale effects.  
- Recommendation: Quantify gaps relative to non-parametric baselines (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, Aittala et al.) to contextualize quality differences.  
- Key question 1: Can a two-layer random-filter network match perceptual quality without the single-layer’s large (55×55) filter inefficiency?  
- Key question 2: Could VGG loss actually correlate with perceptual quality for regular textures, and are observed failures due to optimization rather than the loss?  
- Key question 3: Does the improvement of VGG synthesis by the single-layer model (Appendix A) imply optimization—not depth—is the key bottleneck, and how does this affect the central claim?
