- Paper shows high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved with a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, challenging the need for deep pre-trained features.
- Authors introduce a strong minimal baseline and provide thorough empirical comparisons to VGG-based state-of-the-art methods.
- Single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, random filters) often rival or surpass VGG-based models in perceptual quality.
- Key theoretical contribution: deep hierarchical representations and supervised pre-training are not fundamentally required for natural texture synthesis.
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.
- Strength: careful experimental design with both qualitative comparisons and quantitative evaluation using a VGG-based loss metric.
- Notable finding: random filters perform as well as or better than trained filters; multi-scale models show spatial extent/receptive field size matters more than depth.
- Revision request: clarify relationship between VGG-based loss and perceptual quality, especially for highly regular textures where the metric fails.
- Revision request: include detailed comparisons with non-parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli).
- Revision request: expand discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in synthesized textures.
- Revision request: add a brief analysis of computational efficiency versus the VGG-based model for larger texture sizes.
- Clarification sought: reconcile the claim that random networks fail to generate interesting images with the new observation that random filters work well.
- Clarification sought: specify which perceptual aspects VGG loss misses for textures like brick walls/grids that the single-layer model captures.
- Clarification sought: provide evidence whether multi-scale success stems from combining filter sizes or simply from increased receptive field size.
