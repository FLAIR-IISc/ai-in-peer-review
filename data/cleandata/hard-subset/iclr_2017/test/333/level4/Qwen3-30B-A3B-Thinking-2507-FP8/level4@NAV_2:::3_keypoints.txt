- Demonstrates natural texture synthesis can succeed with a single convolutional layer of random filters, without depth, pooling, or pre-training.  
- Empirical results show these minimal models often match or exceed perceptual quality of deep VGG-19–based methods.  
- Systematic study of filter designs (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), feature map counts, and multi-scale architectures provides insight into synthesis requirements.  
- Decision: Accept — evidence overturns the assumed necessity of network depth and pre-training for high-quality texture synthesis.  
- Robust experimental validation includes VGG-based quantitative evaluation showing shallow random-filter models achieve comparable or better perceptual quality.  
- Counterintuitive finding: random filters can outperform trained filters; multi-scale random models can yield lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization.  
- Analysis explores filter scale effects and role of non-linearities (ReLU), offering mechanistic explanations for simple-model success.  
- Theoretical discussion highlights MaxEnt framework limitations and the need for soft constraints for finite-size textures.  
- Suggest quantifying the perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-off (e.g., entropy-based diversity metrics) to complement qualitative observations.  
- Recommend clarifying VGG loss limitations for regular textures (e.g., walls/grids) and how loss sensitivity to global structure affects evaluation.  
- Advise comparing computational efficiency of the multi-scale large-filter model versus hierarchical alternatives in the main text.  
- Questions for authors: how to formally quantify variability/diversity of synthesized samples (which metrics)?  
- Questions for authors: how might MaxEnt soft constraints or new summary statistics (beyond Gram matrices) be adapted to better capture highly regular textures?
