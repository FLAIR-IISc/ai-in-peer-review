### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the essential components for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical representations and features pre-trained on large datasets are indispensable for this task. The authors make a compelling case by demonstrating that a remarkably simple model—a single-layer convolutional network with random, multi-scale filters and no pooling—can generate textures of high perceptual quality. In several cases, the results from this minimal baseline are shown to rival or even surpass those from the state-of-the-art model by Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a deep, pre-trained VGG network. A further contribution is the proposal to use the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model as a quantitative, perceptual metric for evaluating texture quality.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents an insightful and surprising result by establishing a very strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis. This finding forces a re-evaluation of the necessity of depth and pre-training, which is a valuable contribution to the field of generative modeling.
2.  **Overstated Claims Regarding Hierarchy:** While the results are compelling, the conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for texture synthesis may be too strong. The evidence provided is not conclusive, and the paper itself acknowledges that hierarchies offer significant efficiency benefits. The comparison is primarily between a shallow-random model and a deep-trained model, leaving the role of a deep-random model underexplored.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its "less is more" approach. The authors systematically dismantle a complex, state-of-the-art model to identify its core functional components. The demonstration that random filters in a shallow network can capture complex, long-range correlations (as seen in the pebble and wall textures) is a powerful result. This challenges the community's intuition and provides a crucial sanity check on the role of learned features versus architectural priors. The results, particularly for regular and semi-regular textures, are impressive and effectively support the claim that pre-training is not a strict requirement.

However, the claims about the dispensability of deep hierarchies are not as well-supported.
1.  **Efficiency vs. Quality:** In Section 7, the authors mention that hierarchical architectures are more efficient for achieving large receptive fields but do not improve quality. This is a critical point that is understated in the main results. An efficient model that produces equivalent-quality results is often superior. A more thorough comparison would involve a deep network with random filters to isolate the effect of depth from pre-training. Without this, it is difficult to conclude that hierarchies are unnecessary, rather than just an alternative, more efficient implementation.
2.  **Inconsistent Visual Superiority:** While the proposed model performs very well on some textures, the VGG-based model appears to produce qualitatively better results on others. For instance, the inpainting example in the appendix (Figure 7) suggests that the VGG model captures more coherent statistics for conditional generation, which may point to a limitation of the random-filter approach. The claim of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art is fair, but the evidence does not support consistent superiority.
3.  **Context of Parametric Models:** The visual quality of all presented parametric methods, including the baseline and the VGG model, still falls short of what can be achieved with leading non-parametric texture synthesis methods. While this does not diminish the paper's core contribution within its subfield, framing the claims with this broader context would provide a more balanced perspective.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   **Elaboration on Hierarchical Experiments:** In the discussion, you state that you conducted "extensive experiments with various hierarchical architectures" and found no quality improvement. This is a very important finding that supports your thesis. Could you provide more details on these experiments, even if briefly in the appendix? Specifically, what architectures were tested, and were their filters also random? This would significantly strengthen your claims about the role of hierarchy.
*   **Inpainting Task Performance:** The inpainting result in Figure 7 is fascinating. The VGG-based model appears to produce a more plausible completion. Do you have a hypothesis for why this might be the case? Does this suggest that while pre-trained features are not essential for matching global statistics (unconditional synthesis), they might be better at capturing the local, semantically meaningful distributions required for conditional tasks like inpainting? A short comment on this could add valuable nuance to your discussion.
*   **Nuancing the Conclusion:** I would suggest slightly softening the main claim. For instance, instead of stating that deep representations are "not necessary," you could frame the conclusion to highlight that "surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved without deep hierarchies or pre-trained features, suggesting their primary role may relate more to computational efficiency and fine-tuning than to being a fundamental requirement for the task." This would more accurately reflect the presented evidence.
