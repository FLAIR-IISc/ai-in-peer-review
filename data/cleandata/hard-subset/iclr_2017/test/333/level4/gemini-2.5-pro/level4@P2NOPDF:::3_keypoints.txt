- Builds on Gatys et al.'s Gram-matrix texture framework and investigates effectiveness of different network architectures for texture synthesis.  
- Explores a spectrum of models from deep pre-trained networks to very shallow, even single-layer, randomly-weighted filter banks.  
- Key finding: surprisingly shallow networks (including random single-filter banks) can be highly effective for texture synthesis.  
- For simple and regular textures, shallow models can produce visually superior results compared to deep, data-adapted filters like VGG-19.  
- Figure 4 shows optimizing with a shallow filter bank can yield a lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective.  
- Authors attribute this to optimization difficulty of the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost; supplement shows initializing VGG-19 synthesis from shallow output improves results.  
- Primary limitation: lack of systematic, quantitative comparisons—evaluation relies heavily on visual inspection.  
- Important conceptual point: reconstructing a single instance with trivial statistics is not the goal; texture synthesis should model a distribution of textures.  
- Measuring how well methods capture the distribution is an open problem; entropy is suggested but difficult and insufficient for visual quality.  
- A psychophysical study is proposed as the most practical and promising direction for rigorous evaluation.  
- Conclusion: despite evaluation limitations, the paper's insights on shallow/random-filter networks are a valuable contribution and merit publication at ICLR.
