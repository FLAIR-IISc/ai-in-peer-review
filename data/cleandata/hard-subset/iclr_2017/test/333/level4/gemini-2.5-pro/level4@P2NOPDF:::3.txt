This paper builds on the framework of Gatys et al., which uses deep feature correlations (Gram matrices) to characterize visual textures, by investigating the efficacy of various network architectures for this task. The authors explore a range of models, from deep, pre-trained networks to very shallow ones.

A key finding is that surprisingly shallow networks, even those with a single, randomly-weighted filter bank, can be highly effective. For simple and regular textures, these shallow models can produce results that are visually superior to those generated using complex, data-adapted filters from deep networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an insightful discussion on the strengths and limitations of these correlation-based methods for texture synthesis.

The authors present a compelling result in Figure 4: optimizing an image using a shallow filter bank can yield a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing with the VGG-19 objective directly. They reasonably attribute this phenomenon to the challenges of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. The supplementary material further strengthens this point by demonstrating that VGG-19-based synthesis can be improved by initializing it with the output from the shallow network optimization. This is a valuable addition that complements the main experiments.

The primary limitation of this work is the absence of a systematic, quantitative comparison between different methods. The evaluation relies heavily on visual inspection. While one could define trivial, shallow image statistics that perfectly reconstruct a reference texture (thus achieving high visual quality), such an approach would fail to capture the true goal of texture synthesis, which is to model a *distribution* of textures, not just replicate a single instance.

Measuring how effectively a method captures this distribution remains a significant open problem. Consequently, while the paper's empirical results support the intuition that simpler statistics can achieve excellent quality and diversity compared to more complex ones, this conclusion cannot be definitively confirmed without a more rigorous evaluation framework.

The authors suggest that diversity could be measured via entropy—a reasonable proposal that, as they acknowledge, is difficult to implement. Moreover, an entropy-based metric would not address the equally important dimension of visual quality. A psychophysical study is also proposed as a potential solution and is perhaps the most practical path forward for evaluation. The authors have rightly identified this as a promising direction for future work.

In conclusion, despite the noted limitations in evaluation—an acknowledged and difficult challenge in generative modeling—the paper's strengths are sufficient to warrant publication at ICLR. The exploration of shallow, random-filter networks for texture synthesis is a valuable contribution. While the current conclusions rely on visual inspection of the figures, the inclusion of a psychophysical study in future work would provide stronger, quantitative validation for the compelling intuitions presented here.
