- Paper investigates which components are necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, building on Gatys et al.'s Gram-matrix framework.
- Authors question the necessity of deep, hierarchical representations and supervised pre-training for texture synthesis.
- Main finding: a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters can produce textures perceptually on par with, and sometimes better than, VGG-19.
- Multi-scale random-filter variant captures complex, long-range texture correlations comparable to deep pre-trained models.
- Empirical evidence shows the shallow model can yield lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG-based optimization, suggesting a simpler optimization landscape.
- Initializing VGG-based optimization with the shallow model’s output improves VGG results, supporting the optimization-landscape hypothesis.
- Paper offers an insightful discussion on trade-offs between perceptual similarity and sample diversity in texture synthesis.
- Decision: Accept.
- Primary reasons for acceptance: the surprising baseline result challenging prevailing assumptions, and the thoughtful discussion of limitations and evaluation challenges.
- Major limitation: lack of a systematic, quantifiable evaluation of how well the model captures a distribution over textures (quality vs variability).
- Authors acknowledge the difficulty of formal evaluation and suggest avenues like entropy measures or psychophysical studies.
- Suggested improvement: a small-scale psychophysical (A/B) study would strengthen claims about perceptual quality, especially for regular/man-made textures.
- Suggested experiments: investigate explicit control of the perceptual-quality vs diversity trade-off (e.g., early stopping or diversity-encouraging regularization).
