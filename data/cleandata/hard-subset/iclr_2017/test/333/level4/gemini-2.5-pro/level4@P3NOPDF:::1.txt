This paper proposes a simple yet effective baseline for parametric texture synthesis. The proposed method utilizes a single layer of random, multi-scale filters to compute feature statistics for the synthesis process. The authors demonstrate through empirical experiments that the textures generated by this model are often qualitatively competitive with those produced by a VGG-based model, which relies on multi-layer, pre-trained filters. Based on these results, the authors conclude that the success of texture synthesis does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps, challenging a common assumption in the field.

This is an interesting and insightful paper that provides a valuable contribution to our understanding of texture synthesis. By constructing a simple and carefully controlled baseline, the authors elegantly probe the question of what components are truly essential for this task. The finding that random, multi-scale filters can perform comparably to a pre-trained deep network is surprising and thought-provoking, and it serves as an important sanity check for the community.

However, while the work is insightful, the primary conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary may be overstated and requires further investigation. I have the following concerns:

1.  The qualitative results for both the proposed baseline and the VGG-based model show visible artifacts and are not on par with the state-of-the-art results from non-parametric methods. While the comparison between the two parametric models is the focus, the overall lower quality raises a question: if neither model perfectly captures the target texture, can we confidently conclude that the components of the VGG-based model (like its hierarchy) are unnecessary? It is possible that both models are missing a key ingredient for high-fidelity synthesis, which might indeed be related to hierarchical feature processing that is not fully exploited even by the VGG-based approach in its current formulation.

2.  The authors' own results appear to provide some counter-evidence to their main claim. In the inpainting task presented in Figure 7, the VGG-based model seems to produce qualitatively superior results, particularly in synthesizing more structured or large-scale patterns. This suggests that the learned, hierarchical features of the VGG network may indeed be beneficial for tasks requiring a larger receptive field or a more semantic understanding of the image content. This point should be acknowledged and discussed more thoroughly, as it seems to challenge the central conclusion of the paper.

3.  The proposed baseline achieves multi-scale analysis by using a large bank of filters with varying sizes. An alternative perspective is that a key advantage of hierarchical models (like CNNs) is their ability to capture multi-scale information efficiently through successive pooling and striding operations, which gradually build a hierarchy of receptive fields. It would strengthen the paper to include a discussion on the efficiency and scalability of the proposed approach versus a hierarchical one. Is the proposed model's performance simply a result of a brute-force sampling of scales, and could a hierarchical architecture provide a more principled and efficient solution to the same problem?

In summary, this is a well-motivated paper with a clever experimental design. The results are valuable in demonstrating the power of a surprisingly simple model. However, the conclusions drawn from these results may be too strong and are not fully supported by the evidence presented. I would encourage the authors to moderate their claims and provide a more nuanced discussion of the role of hierarchical representations, especially in light of the inpainting results and the question of architectural efficiency.
