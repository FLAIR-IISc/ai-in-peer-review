- Contribution: Demonstrates that a single untrained convolutional layer with random multi-scale filters (no pooling) can synthesize high-quality natural textures rivaling VGG-based methods.
- Main claim challenged: Questions the necessity of depth and supervisedly learned hierarchical features for parametric texture synthesis.
- Core result significance: Surprising finding that matching second-order statistics (Gram matrix) of a shallow random filter bank captures much textural structure.
- Empirical support: Extensive, systematic experiments and ablations across shallow architectures, filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), and feature counts.
- Multi-scale importance: Shows combining multiple filter scales is crucial to the final model design.
- Baseline value: The proposed minimal model provides a strong, important baseline for future generative texture research.
- Quantitative metric: Introduces a VGG-based loss as a quantitative texture-quality metric, moving beyond purely qualitative evaluation.
- VGG-loss paradox: Observes cases where the random model attains lower VGG-loss than the VGG-optimized model, indicating limitations of the metric or optimization issues.
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends Accept based on novelty and strong empirical evidence.
- Suggested evaluation improvement: Add a small-scale human perceptual study to more robustly validate perceptual quality claims.
- Variability critique: Recommend visually demonstrating sample variability (multiple syntheses per texture) to clarify trade-offs between perceptual similarity and diversity.
- Theoretical framing: Suggest making the Maximum Entropy connection more explicit early (e.g., in the introduction) to frame the work as testing constraints for MaxEnt texture models.
- Clarification requested (1): How sensitive is the model to optimization tuning/scaling of loss and gradients—robust or narrow range?
- Clarification requested (2): Provide details on the hierarchical random network architectures tested, since negative results on depth are important.
- Clarification requested (3): What explains instances where the random model achieves superior VGG-loss—optimization local minima in VGG-based methods or other factors?
