### **Summary**

This paper introduces a simple yet surprisingly powerful baseline for parametric texture synthesis. The proposed model uses a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters, forgoing both the deep, hierarchical architecture and the pre-trained features common to state-of-the-art methods like the VGG-based model (Gatys et al., 2015a). Through empirical experiments, the authors demonstrate that samples generated by their baseline can often rival the quality of those produced by the multi-layer, pre-trained VGG model. Based on these results, they conclude that neither deep hierarchical representations nor learned features are indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis. The work is insightful and presents an interesting challenge to common assumptions in generative modeling.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend rejecting this paper in its current form. While the findings are intriguing, the central claims are not yet sufficiently supported by the evidence provided.

1.  **The main claim—that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for texture synthesis—is a strong one, but I am not fully convinced by the supporting evidence.** The comparison between a single-layer random model and a deep pre-trained model does not isolate the contribution of the hierarchical structure itself.
2.  **The qualitative results, while impressive for such a simple model, do not consistently demonstrate superiority over the VGG-based baseline.** In fact, the VGG model appears to perform better on the inpainting task shown in the appendix, suggesting it captures more robust representations.

### **Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on the following points:

The paper's primary argument rests on comparing a single-layer, multi-scale random model to a deep, pre-trained VGG model. While the random model performs surprisingly well, this does not definitively prove that hierarchical structures are unnecessary. The two models differ in at least two key aspects: depth (hierarchy) and filter type (random vs. pre-trained). To truly isolate the importance of hierarchy, a more controlled comparison would be necessary, for example, against a deep model with random filters. The authors briefly mention in the discussion that they experimented with hierarchical random models and found no improvement in quality, but this is a critical point that requires thorough experimental support and analysis within the main body of the paper to be convincing.

Furthermore, the qualitative evidence presented is not strong enough to fully support the paper's claims. The generated samples from both the single-layer and VGG models often contain noticeable artifacts and, in my view, do not yet match the quality of leading non-parametric texture synthesis methods. This general level of quality makes it difficult to draw firm conclusions about the superiority of one parametric approach over another. More importantly, as shown in the appendix (Figure 7), the VGG-based model seems to produce more coherent results on the inpainting task. This suggests it may capture more meaningful long-range dependencies, a feature often attributed to hierarchical processing, which contradicts the conclusion that such structures are not critical.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I would like to ask the authors some questions to help clarify my understanding.

1.  The discussion mentions that hierarchical architectures were tested but did not improve quality. Could you please elaborate on this? Specifically, what architectures were tried? It seems counter-intuitive that a deep architecture—which is a more parameter-efficient way to create large receptive fields than using very large filters—would not offer any advantage. Is it possible that the optimization for these deeper random models was more difficult, leading to poorer results?

2.  The paper proposes using the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality. However, the results show cases where the single-layer model achieves a lower VGG loss than the VGG model itself, which is paradoxical since the latter directly optimizes this loss. Could you comment on the reliability of this metric and its limitations? Does this paradox suggest that the optimization for the VGG model is getting stuck in poor local minima, as you hypothesized? A deeper analysis of this proposed metric would strengthen the paper.
