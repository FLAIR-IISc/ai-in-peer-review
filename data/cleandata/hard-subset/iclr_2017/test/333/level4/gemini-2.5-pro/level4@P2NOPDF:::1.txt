This paper proposes a simple yet strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis. The proposed model utilizes a single layer of random, multi-scale filters. Through empirical experiments, the authors demonstrate that samples generated by this baseline can be competitive with those from a VGG-based model, which relies on deep, pre-trained features. Based on these results, the authors conclude that deep hierarchical representations and learned features are not necessarily required for high-quality texture synthesis.

The work is interesting and insightful, providing a valuable counterpoint to the prevailing trend of using complex, pre-trained deep networks for synthesis tasks. The strength of this work lies in its simplicity and its ability to challenge common assumptions about the necessary components for texture modeling.

However, the main conclusion—that deep hierarchical structures are not essential—may be overstated and requires further justification. I have the following concerns:

1.  The qualitative results for both the proposed baseline and the VGG-based model show visible artifacts and are arguably not on par with state-of-the-art non-parametric methods. Since neither model produces perfect results, it is difficult to conclude that the simpler model's capabilities are fully equivalent to the VGG model's. Both models have clear limitations, which complicates a direct comparison of their representational power.

2.  The VGG-based model appears to perform noticeably better in the inpainting task shown in Figure 7. This suggests that the hierarchical features learned by VGG may capture longer-range spatial statistics or semantic information that is beneficial for this related task. This hints at an advantage of deep representations that is not fully captured by the texture synthesis comparison alone.

3.  A key question regarding efficiency and scalability arises. The baseline model relies on a large bank of filters with varying sizes to capture multi-scale statistics. An alternative perspective is that a hierarchical model is precisely an *efficient* architecture for capturing such multi-scale information. Could the authors comment on whether a deep, hierarchical structure might offer a more parameter-efficient or computationally efficient alternative to their proposed flat, multi-filter approach for modeling features at different scales?
