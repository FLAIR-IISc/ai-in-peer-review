### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for synthesizing natural textures, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical, and pre-trained convolutional networks are necessary for this task. The authors systematically dismantle the state-of-the-art texture synthesis model (Gatys et al., 2015a) to identify its indispensable components. Their central and surprising finding is that a remarkably simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters and no pooling—is sufficient to generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling those produced by deep, pre-trained VGG networks. The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis, comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA from natural images) and sizes. The work makes several key contributions: it establishes a strong and minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, demonstrates that learned hierarchical features are not essential for this task, and introduces the use of a VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality, while also thoughtfully discussing its limitations and the broader theoretical challenges in texture modeling.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with a surprising and impactful result that provides a valuable new perspective on generative image modeling. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant and Counter-intuitive Main Finding:** The paper compellingly demonstrates that a simple, shallow architecture with random weights can be highly effective for a complex generative task. This finding challenges the community's conventional wisdom about the necessity of deep, learned hierarchies for capturing natural image statistics and provides a crucial new baseline for future work in texture synthesis and beyond.
2.  **Thorough and Methodical Investigation:** The authors support their claims with a rigorous and well-structured empirical study. They systematically explore a variety of model configurations, including different filter types and scales, and provide clear qualitative and quantitative evidence. The analysis is transparent and convincingly isolates the factors that are—and are not—critical for success.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The core strength of this paper lies in its clear and falsifiable hypothesis and the simple yet elegant experiments designed to test it. The discovery that a single layer of random filters can produce results competitive with a 19-layer, ImageNet-trained VGG network is a significant contribution. It forces a re-evaluation of why deep models succeed in this domain, suggesting that the Gram matrix matching procedure, combined with a sufficiently overcomplete and multi-scale set of filters, may be more critical than the learned nature of the features themselves.

The experimental methodology is sound. The ablation study in Figure 2, which teases apart the contributions of filter size and the combination of multiple scales, is particularly effective. It clearly illustrates that while small filters capture fine detail and large filters capture long-range structure, only their combination can successfully model the full texture. This provides strong support for their multi-scale random model.

Furthermore, the paper makes a valuable attempt to move beyond purely subjective evaluation by proposing the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric for perceptual similarity. While the authors are commendably candid about its shortcomings (e.g., for regular man-made textures), introducing and analyzing such a metric is a positive step towards more reproducible and objective evaluation in this field.

Finally, the discussion section is insightful, connecting their empirical findings to the broader theoretical context of the Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt) framework. The authors raise important and nuanced points about the practical challenges of texture synthesis, such as the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability and the role of the perceptual distance metric in defining a "texture." This thoughtful analysis elevates the paper from a simple presentation of results to a more profound commentary on the problem itself.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Human Evaluation:** The central claim is that the generated textures are of "high perceptual quality" and often "rival" the state-of-the-art. While the visual evidence in the figures is strong, this claim could be more rigorously substantiated with a controlled psychophysical study. For example, a simple two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) experiment where participants judge which of two synthesized images (one from your model, one from VGG) is a better match to the original texture would provide powerful, quantitative support for your claims of perceptual parity or superiority.
*   **Substantiating the "More Robust Optimization" Claim:** You suggest that the single-layer model is "less prone to local minima" than the deeper VGG model. This is a plausible and interesting explanation for its success on some textures. This claim could be strengthened by providing more direct evidence, such as showing loss curves for both models or analyzing the variance in synthesis quality across multiple random initializations.
*   **Connecting Theory and Practice:** The discussion of the MaxEnt framework and the need for an ε-environment around the reference statistics is excellent. To make this point more concrete, it would be interesting to see how changing the optimization's stopping criterion (effectively tuning ε) impacts the trade-off between similarity (VGG-loss) and variability (e.g., diversity among multiple generated samples) for your model.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if you could clarify the following points in your author response:

1.  Could you elaborate on the "more robust optimization" of the single-layer model? Do you hypothesize this is due to a smoother or more convex loss landscape compared to the deep VGG model? Have you performed any analysis to confirm this?
2.  You show examples where the VGG-loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of your model, particularly for textures with regular, geometric structures. What is your intuition for why a feature space trained for object recognition might fail in these specific cases? Does this point to a fundamental limitation of using such features for evaluating texture perception?
3.  You note that your model leans towards perceptual similarity over variability. Have you explored simple modifications to encourage more variability in the generated samples, such as early stopping of the optimization or adding a diversity-promoting regularization term to the loss?
