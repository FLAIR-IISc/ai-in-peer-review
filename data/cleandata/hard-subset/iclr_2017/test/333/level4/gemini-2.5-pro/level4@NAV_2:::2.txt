This paper investigates the minimal architectural components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical representations from supervisedly trained networks are essential for this task. The core contribution is a compelling demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained, multi-scale filters and no pooling—can generate natural textures of high perceptual quality. These results often rival those produced by the current state-of-the-art, which relies on a 19-layer pre-trained VGG network. The paper supports this central claim through a thorough empirical study of various shallow architectures and introduces a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the VGG model's loss.

I recommend that this paper be **Accepted**. My decision is based on two key strengths:
1.  The paper presents a significant and surprising finding that questions established beliefs about the necessity of depth and learned features for generative texture modeling.
2.  The claims are substantiated by extensive and systematic experiments, providing strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed minimal model.

The main strength of this work lies in its impactful core result. The field has largely moved towards increasingly deep and complex models for image generation, often assuming that hierarchical features learned on large-scale datasets like ImageNet are fundamental to capturing the statistics of natural images. This paper provides a powerful counter-narrative, demonstrating that a significant amount of textural structure can be captured by merely matching the second-order statistics (the Gram matrix) of responses from a random, shallow filter bank. This finding is not only surprising but also important, as it encourages a re-evaluation of which components of deep networks are truly critical for such tasks. The presented model serves as a strong and crucial baseline for future research in parametric image generation.

Furthermore, the authors' empirical methodology is rigorous and convincing. They systematically ablate the key components of the state-of-the-art model, comparing various single-layer networks with filters derived from different principles (random, Fourier basis, k-means, PCA) and varying numbers of feature maps. The visual results presented in Figure 1 and Figure 4 are compelling, clearly showing that the random multi-scale filter model produces high-quality textures. The investigation into the importance of combining multiple filter scales (Figure 2) provides a clear rationale for their final model design. The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative evaluation metric, while not perfect, is a commendable step towards a more objective assessment than the purely qualitative inspection common in this area.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   **Evaluation Metric:** The use of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric is a valuable contribution. However, the paper rightly notes the paradoxical result where the proposed model sometimes achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-based model that directly optimizes this objective. This suggests that the VGG-loss is an imperfect proxy for perceptual quality or that the optimization landscape is a major confounding factor. The claims of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art could be made even more robust by supplementing this metric with a small-scale human perceptual study, asking participants to rate the quality of synthesized textures or distinguish them from the original.
*   **Discussion on Variability:** The paper includes an insightful discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability. While a full quantitative analysis of variability is correctly identified as beyond the scope of this work, the paper could be strengthened by visually demonstrating this trade-off. For example, showing several different samples generated for the same texture would help readers appreciate the variability of the proposed model versus the baseline.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** The discussion in Section 7 connecting the empirical findings to the Maximum Entropy framework is very interesting. However, this connection could be made more explicit throughout the paper. For instance, the introduction could frame the work as an empirical test of the constraints needed for a MaxEnt model of textures, making the final discussion a more direct conclusion of the paper's investigation.

To further clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1.  In the introduction, you suggest that the discrepancy between your findings and earlier reports (Gatys et al., 2015a) on the failure of random networks stems from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," later mentioning scaling the loss and gradients. How sensitive is your model's success to this specific tuning? Is there a narrow range of scaling factors that works, or is the approach generally robust?
2.  You mention conducting extensive experiments with hierarchical random networks that did not improve texture quality. Could you provide a few more details on the architectures tested? This is a significant negative result that strongly supports your claim that depth is not indispensable for quality and would be a valuable addition to the paper.
3.  Regarding the instances where your model achieves a superior VGG-loss compared to the VGG-based model, what is your primary hypothesis for this phenomenon? Do you believe it is primarily due to the VGG model getting stuck in poor local minima in a more complex optimization landscape, or are there other factors at play?
