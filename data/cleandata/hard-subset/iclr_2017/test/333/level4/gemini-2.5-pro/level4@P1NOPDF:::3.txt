The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (i.e., Gram matrices) of deep feature responses serve as excellent descriptors of visual texture. This paper investigates which types of deep or shallow networks are most effective within this framework. A key finding is that very shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, perform surprisingly well. For simple and regular textures, these shallow models can even produce results that are visually superior to those generated using complex, data-adapted filters from networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper provides an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of these methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 demonstrates that optimizing images using shallow filter banks can result in textures that have a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing for the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the supplementary material, the authors further show that superior optimization results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the output from the shallow network, a useful addition that complements the original experiments.

The paper's main limitation is its lack of systematic comparison against a quantifiable objective. For instance, it is trivial to define image statistics that would allow for the generation of an exact copy of any reference texture, thereby achieving perfect visual quality by definition. Such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The true aim, however, is to capture a *distribution* of textures, and measuring how well a method achieves this remains an open problem. Therefore, while the empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity (when compared to more complex statistics), it is difficult to confirm this conclusively without a proper quantitative framework.

The authors suggest that diversity could be measured using entropy. While this is a reasonable proposal, they acknowledge in their response that it is difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, an entropy-based metric would still fail to account for the crucial aspect of visual quality. They also propose a psychophysical assessment as a potential solution, which may indeed be the most practical way forward, but they leave this for future work.

Overall, given that the evaluation of generative models is a notoriously difficult problem, the paper's empirical findings and analysis are strong enough to warrant publication at ICLR. Nevertheless, the central claims of the paper would be significantly strengthened by some form of psychophysical assessment. Such an evaluation would be invaluable for confirming the intuitions that, at present, are supported only by visual inspection of the figures in the paper and its supplement.
