### **Summary**

This paper investigates the minimal architectural components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical networks with features pre-trained on large-scale object recognition tasks are necessary for this problem. The central and surprising claim is that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised filter training are indispensable. The paper demonstrates that a simple, single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters can generate textures of high perceptual quality. Their proposed multi-scale random filter model often produces results that are competitive with, and occasionally superior to, the state-of-the-art VGG-based method by Gatys et al. (2015a). As a secondary contribution, the authors propose using the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality, offering a more objective evaluation method than purely qualitative human inspection.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that should be accepted. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper's core result—that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained one for texture synthesis—is counter-intuitive and impactful. It establishes a powerful and remarkably simple baseline, forcing a re-evaluation of what aspects of deep representations are truly critical for generative texture modeling.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Analysis:** The authors support their claims with a comprehensive set of experiments. They systematically ablate key architectural components (depth, pooling) and test a wide variety of filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and scales, providing convincing evidence for their conclusions.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The prevailing success of methods like Gatys et al. (2015a) led to a strong prior in the community that the hierarchical features learned by deep networks like VGG were capturing fundamental, complex statistics of natural images essential for synthesis. This paper compellingly refutes that notion for the task of texture synthesis. By showing that the Gram matrix statistics from a single layer of random filters are sufficient, the authors demonstrate that a powerful texture descriptor can be achieved with a much simpler and more generic mechanism. This finding is not only interesting in its own right but also provides a crucial data point for understanding the properties of generative models based on feature statistics.

The strength of the paper lies in its methodical investigation. Rather than just presenting a single successful random architecture, the authors conduct a thorough comparison across multiple single-layer models. They explore filters derived from first principles (Fourier basis), unsupervised learning (K-means, PCA), and pure randomness. The clear result that random filters perform on par with or better than learned ones is a very strong piece of evidence. Furthermore, the analysis in Figure 2, which teases apart the effect of filter scale, convincingly argues that the success of their best model stems from the combination of multi-scale information rather than just a large receptive field, adding depth to their analysis.

Finally, while the proposed VGG-based loss is not a perfect perceptual metric (as the authors acknowledge), its introduction is a valuable contribution. It moves the field towards more reproducible and quantitative evaluation, which has been a persistent challenge for generative modeling. The paper is honest about the metric's limitations, noting its failure on certain regular, man-made textures, which in itself is an interesting finding.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of Failure Cases for VGG-Loss:** The observation that the VGG-loss is lower for the multi-scale model's output than for the VGG-model's own output on certain textures (e.g., grids) is fascinating. The paper could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of *why* this might be the case. Is it because VGG, being trained for object recognition, is biased against perfect, periodic structures and thus assigns a higher loss to them? A deeper analysis here could provide valuable insights into the properties of these feature spaces.
*   **Visualizing Variability:** The paper discusses the important trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability. While Figure 3 shows variability across different textures, it would be highly beneficial to include a figure in the main paper or appendix that shows multiple different samples generated from the *same* source texture for both the proposed random multi-scale model and the VGG-based model. This would provide a direct visual comparison of the models' variability.
*   **Optimization Details:** The authors suggest that a discrepancy with prior work (which found random networks ineffective) may be due to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically mentioning the scaling of the loss and gradients. This is a critical detail. I would recommend emphasizing this in the main text and perhaps providing a small ablation study or more detailed explanation in the appendix about the sensitivity of the synthesis quality to this scaling factor.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you elaborate on which specific aspects of the optimization were most critical to achieving high-quality results with the random filter models? How sensitive is the final quality to the choice of optimizer (L-BFGS vs. Adam, etc.) and the loss scaling factor you mention?
2.  The paper notes that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters. Could you provide a rough quantitative comparison of the synthesis time (e.g., time per 2000 iterations for a fixed image size) between your single-layer multi-scale model and the 19-layer VGG model?
3.  Given your findings, what is your intuition on why the deep, pre-trained VGG model works so well for artistic style transfer, a related task also pioneered by Gatys et al.? Do you believe a shallow, random network could also succeed at that task, or is there something fundamentally different about style transfer that requires a deep, trained feature hierarchy?
