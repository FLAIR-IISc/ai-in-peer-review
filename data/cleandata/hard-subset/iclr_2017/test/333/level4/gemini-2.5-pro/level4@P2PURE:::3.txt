This paper investigates which types of deep or shallow networks are effective within the texture synthesis framework of Gatys et al., which uses correlation statistics (Gram matrices) of feature responses to characterize visual textures. One of the main findings is that very shallow networks, consisting of just a single, random-weight filter bank, can perform surprisingly well. For simple and regular textures, these shallow networks may even produce results that are visually superior to those from complex, data-adapted networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such methods for texture synthesis.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to a shallow filter bank can yield textures that achieve a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. This result is attributed to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the supplementary material, the authors usefully complement their original experiments by showing that better results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19 optimization with the output of the shallow network optimization.

The main limitation of the paper is its lack of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define shallow image statistics that could generate an exact copy of a reference texture, thus achieving high visual quality. The goal, however, is to capture a texture *distribution*, and measuring how well a method accomplishes this remains an open problem. Consequently, while the empirical results support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis in terms of both quality and diversity, it is difficult to confirm this conclusively.

The authors suggest that diversity could be measured by entropy. This is a reasonable idea, but as they acknowledge, it is difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, an entropy-based metric would not account for visual quality. They also propose conducting a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way to evaluate their claims, but they consider this to be future work.

Overall, given that the evaluation of image generation models is such a hard problem, the paper's strengths are sufficient to warrant publication at ICLR. Nevertheless, some form of psychophysical assessment would have been valuable to substantiate the claims, which at present rely on visual inspection of the figures in the paper and supplementary material.
