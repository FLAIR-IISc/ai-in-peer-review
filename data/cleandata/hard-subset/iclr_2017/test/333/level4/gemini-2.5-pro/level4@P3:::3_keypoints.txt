- Demonstrates that a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters (no pooling) can synthesize high-quality textures, often rivaling or surpassing VGG-19.
- Challenges the assumption that depth and learned features are necessary, providing a strong, surprising baseline for texture synthesis.
- Systematically compares many single-layer architectures and provides a thoughtful empirical analysis.
- Finds the shallow model can achieve lower VGG-based synthesis loss than models directly optimizing that loss, highlighting non-trivial optimization properties.
- Attributes superior results on some regular textures to a simpler optimization landscape in the shallow model versus deep VGG.
- Shows initializing VGG-based optimization with the shallow model’s output yields improved results, supporting the optimization hypothesis.
- Provides theoretical discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture modeling.
- Main limitation: lacks rigorous evaluation of generated texture distributions—diversity vs. perceptual similarity is not systematically measured.
- Evaluation relies mainly on visual inspection and a VGG-based loss, which may favor reconstruction over sampling diverse textures.
- Recommends adding psychophysical/user studies (e.g., 2AFC or side-by-side comparisons) to objectively support claims of outperforming state of the art.
- Suggests more explicitly connecting the paper’s perceptual metric framework to the practical VGG-loss implementation and stopping criteria.
- Encourages further investigation of “overfitting” in deeper random CNNs and reporting architectural factors that lead to exact reconstructions.
