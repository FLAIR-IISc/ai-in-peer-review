### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical neural networks with features learned from large natural image datasets (like VGG) are essential for this task. The authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art model by Gatys et al. (2015a) and demonstrate, through extensive experiments, that surprisingly high-quality textures can be generated using a much simpler model: a single convolutional layer with no pooling and random, data-independent filters.

The core contribution is a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis that relies on matching the Gram matrix of feature responses from a random, multi-scale filter bank. The results show that this simple model often generates textures that are perceptually competitive with, and in some cases for highly regular patterns, superior to those from the 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network. The paper also contributes a thorough empirical comparison of various single-layer architectures (using filters derived from Fourier bases, K-means, PCA, and random initializations) and proposes using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a compelling and counter-intuitive result that challenges the community's understanding of what is necessary for generative texture modeling. By showing that depth and learned features are not indispensable, it shifts the focus towards the power of matching second-order statistics in a sufficiently rich, even random, feature space. This is an important insight for the field of generative models.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support and Valuable Baseline:** The claims are well-supported by a systematic series of experiments that convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed simple model. The random multi-scale model serves as an excellent and hard-to-beat baseline that future work in parametric texture synthesis should be compared against.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous empirical validation. The authors effectively isolate and test the core components of the modern texture synthesis pipeline: network depth, pooling, and learned features. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are striking; the single-layer random multi-scale model produces textures that are often visually indistinguishable from or even better than those from the much more complex VGG-based model. This finding forces a re-evaluation of why deep models work for this task—perhaps their success is less about learned hierarchical concepts and more about providing a rich, multi-scale feature space, a property that can be replicated with a well-designed random filter bank.

However, while the central claim is strong, the conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for texture synthesis may be slightly overstated. The paper's own evidence suggests a more nuanced picture. For instance, the inpainting experiment in the Appendix (Figure 7) shows the VGG-based model producing a more coherent completion than the single-layer model. This suggests that while a shallow model can capture stationary statistics effectively, a deep hierarchy may be better at learning non-local structural priors that are crucial for tasks beyond pure texture synthesis.

Furthermore, the authors note in the discussion that while they explored deeper random models that were more computationally efficient, they did not see a quality improvement. This is a crucial point, but without more detail on these experiments, it is difficult to conclude definitively that depth offers no potential quality benefit. It is possible that a different deep random architecture or optimization scheme could have yielded superior results.

Despite these points, the paper's main contribution remains intact and highly valuable. It successfully demonstrates that an unexpectedly simple model can achieve state-of-the-art performance, providing both a crucial insight and a powerful new baseline for the field.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Nuanced Phrasing of Claims:** The central message is very strong, but the authors could consider slightly nuancing their claims regarding the dispensability of depth. For example, framing it as "a surprisingly large degree of texture synthesis quality can be achieved without learned features or depth" might more accurately reflect the evidence, especially in light of the inpainting results.
*   **Discussion of Optimization:** The paper notes that the optimization for the single-layer model may be less prone to local minima, which could explain some of its superior results on regular textures. This is an interesting point that could be explored further. A brief analysis or visualization of the loss landscapes for the shallow versus deep models could provide compelling support for this hypothesis.
*   **The Role of the Gram Matrix:** The paper clearly shows that the choice of filters is less critical than previously thought. This elevates the importance of the summary statistic itself—the Gram matrix. A slightly deeper discussion on *why* matching second-order correlations in a high-dimensional random projection is so effective would be a welcome addition.
*   **VGG-Loss Limitations:** The authors rightly point out that their proposed VGG-loss metric is not perfect and sometimes fails to align with human perception. Expanding this point with a brief discussion on the challenges of creating automated perceptual metrics and potential future directions would enhance the paper's contribution.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the inpainting result in Figure 7, do you interpret this as evidence that hierarchical representations, while not essential for recreating stationary textures, are critical for capturing more complex, long-range structural dependencies required for conditional generation tasks?
2.  You mention testing deeper, hierarchical random models which did not improve quality. Could you provide more details on the architectures of these models (e.g., number of layers, use of pooling, filter sizes)? Is it possible that with a different configuration or optimization strategy, a deep random model could outperform the single-layer multi-scale baseline?
3.  The paper states that scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7 was important to avoid early stopping. This seems like a critical implementation detail. Could you elaborate on why this is necessary and how sensitive the synthesis quality is to this hyperparameter? Does the deep VGG-based model require similar scaling?
