The framework of Gatys et al. demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. This paper investigates in detail which types of deep or shallow networks are effective within this framework. One of the main findings is that very shallow networks, consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, perform surprisingly well. For simple and regular textures, they may even produce results that are visually superior to those from complex, data-adapted filters, such as the ones in VGG-19. More broadly, the paper contains an interesting and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such texture synthesis methods.

Figure 4 shows that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can result in textures that have a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. In the supplementary material, the authors further show that better optimization results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19-based optimization with the results from the shallow network optimization. This is a useful complement to the original experiments.

The main limitation of the paper is that it does not systematically compare different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define image statistics that would allow for the generation of an exact copy of any reference texture, thus achieving very high visual quality. Such trivial statistics would also be very shallow. The aim, however, is to capture a texture *distribution*, and measuring how well a method meets this challenge remains an open problem. Hence, while the empirical results seem to confirm the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for texture synthesis—both in terms of quality and diversity when compared to more complex statistics—it is difficult to conclusively confirm that this is the case.

The authors indicate that diversity could be measured in terms of entropy. This is a reasonable suggestion but, as they acknowledge, it is difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, this measure would still not account for the other critical aspect of the problem, namely visual quality. They also propose a psychophysical assessment, which may be the only practical way of addressing this problem, but they defer this to future work.

Overall, given that the evaluation of image generation is an inherently difficult problem, the paper has sufficient strengths to warrant publication at ICLR. Nevertheless, some form of psychophysical assessment would be useful to substantiate the paper's claims, which at present rely on visual inspection of the figures in the paper and supplementary material.
