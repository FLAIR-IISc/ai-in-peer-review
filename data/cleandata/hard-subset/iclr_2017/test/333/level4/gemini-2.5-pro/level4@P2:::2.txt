### Summary

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for generating high-quality natural textures using parametric models based on convolutional neural network (CNN) statistics. Challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical representations from supervisedly trained networks (like VGG) are essential, the authors demonstrate a surprising and powerful result: a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters and no pooling can synthesize textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art. The work systematically explores various single-layer architectures, identifying the number of feature maps, the use of multi-scale filters, and the presence of non-linearities as the crucial components. Furthermore, the paper proposes using the loss from a VGG-based model as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality and provides a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical trade-offs between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture synthesis.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The core contribution—that a simple, single-layer network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained model for texture synthesis—is a strong, counter-intuitive, and impactful result. It provides a powerful minimal baseline and forces a re-evaluation of what components are truly necessary for this generative task.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Analysis:** The authors support their claims with a comprehensive set of experiments. They systematically ablate different aspects of the model, including filter types (random, k-means, PCA, Fourier), the number of features, and the impact of filter scale, providing convincing evidence for their conclusions.

### Supporting Arguments

The strength of this paper lies in its clear hypothesis and the systematic way it is tested. The prevailing method by Gatys et al. (2015a) relied on the rich, hierarchical features of a 19-layer VGG network trained on ImageNet. It was widely assumed that both the network's depth and its learned features were critical for capturing the complex statistics of natural textures. This paper compellingly argues otherwise.

The authors' primary claim is validated through extensive visual and quantitative results. Figure 1 shows that even single-scale random filters produce reasonable results, and Figure 4 demonstrates that the proposed multi-scale random model generates textures that are often visually on par with, and sometimes superior to, those from the much more complex VGG-based model, especially for regular structures like brick walls and grids.

The ablation studies are crucial for deconstructing the problem. The comparison between single-scale models of varying filter sizes and the multi-scale model in Figure 2 clearly illustrates that a combination of scales is necessary to capture both fine-grained detail and long-range structure. The demonstration that removing the ReLU non-linearity results in a complete failure to capture texture statistics further isolates the essential components of their model.

Finally, the paper's discussion on evaluation and the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis is a valuable contribution. The proposal to use the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric, while acknowledged as imperfect, is a step towards more objective evaluation in a field that often relies on subjective visual inspection. The authors' nuanced discussion of the tension between perceptual similarity and variability, and how optimization and finite-image effects complicate the classical MaxEnt framework, adds significant depth to the paper.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **On the Role of Optimization:** You suggest that the discrepancy between your success with random filters and prior failures reported by Gatys et al. (2015a) may stem from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically the scaling of the loss and gradients. This is a critical detail. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of your results to this scaling factor (10^7)? Is there a principled reason for this value, or was it found empirically? A deeper insight here could be very valuable for others trying to reproduce or build upon your work.
*   **The VGG Loss Paradox:** It is fascinating that for some textures (e.g., the grids), your multi-scale model achieves a *lower* VGG-based loss than the model that directly optimizes this loss. You suggest this might be due to the simpler optimization landscape of your model. Could you expand on this hypothesis? Does this imply that the VGG-based synthesis is prone to getting stuck in poor local minima that are nonetheless "close" to the target statistics, while your model finds a better, perceptually superior solution?
*   **Clarification on Over-Constraining:** You provide "anecdotal evidence" that deeper multi-layer random CNNs can perfectly reconstruct the reference texture. This is a very interesting observation that directly supports your discussion on over-constrained models and finite-size effects. While I understand not including full experiments, could you briefly characterize the architectures (e.g., number of layers/filters) where this reconstruction occurred? It would strengthen the connection between your empirical results and your theoretical discussion.
*   **Variability vs. Similarity:** Your discussion on balancing perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. While measuring variability is difficult, have you qualitatively observed how the variability of samples from your model compares to that of the VGG model? For instance, do different random initializations produce a wider or narrower range of outputs compared to the baseline? A brief comment on this could provide further context.
