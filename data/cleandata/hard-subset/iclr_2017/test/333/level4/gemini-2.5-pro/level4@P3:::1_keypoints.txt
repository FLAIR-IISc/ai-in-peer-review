- Paper shows high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved with a single convolutional layer of random, multi-scale filters matching Gram matrices, challenging need for deep learned features.  
- Core contribution: a strong, minimal baseline (random single-layer multi-scale model) that is often perceptually competitive with or superior to VGG-based synthesis.  
- Thorough empirical comparison across single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and proposal to use VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Significant and surprising finding that depth and learned features are not indispensable, shifting focus to matching second-order statistics in rich feature spaces.  
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical support; the proposed random multi-scale model is a valuable, hard-to-beat baseline.  
- Strength: Clear hypothesis and rigorous experiments isolating depth, pooling, and learned features, with striking visual results supporting claims.  
- Caveat: Claim that deep hierarchical representations are unnecessary may be overstated—VGG outperforms the single-layer model in inpainting, suggesting depth helps with non-local structural priors.  
- Unclear point: Deeper random models tested showed no quality improvement, but lacking architectural/experimental details prevents definitive conclusions about depth’s potential.  
- Suggestion: Slightly nuance claims to reflect that much texture quality can be achieved without learned depth, rather than depth being entirely dispensable.  
- Suggestion: Analyze optimization differences (e.g., loss landscapes) as optimization behavior may explain superior results on regular textures.  
- Suggestion: Expand discussion on why matching Gram matrices of random projections works so effectively (role of second-order correlations).  
- Suggestion: Acknowledge limitations of the VGG-loss metric and discuss challenges/future directions for automated perceptual metrics.  
- Question 1: Do authors interpret Figure 7’s inpainting result as evidence that hierarchical representations are critical for capturing long-range structural dependencies in conditional tasks?  
- Question 2: Please provide details on the deeper random architectures tested (layers, pooling, filter sizes) and whether different configurations/optimizers might improve deep random models.  
- Question 3: Why was scaling loss/gradients by 10^7 necessary to avoid early stopping, how sensitive is synthesis quality to this hyperparameter, and does VGG-based synthesis require similar scaling?
