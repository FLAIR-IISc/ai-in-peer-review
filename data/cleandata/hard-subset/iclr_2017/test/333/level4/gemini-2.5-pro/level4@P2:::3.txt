### **Summary**

This paper investigates the essential components required for parametric texture synthesis, a problem for which deep neural networks have recently defined the state of the art. Building upon the framework of Gatys et al. (2015a), which uses the correlation statistics of deep features to characterize textures, the authors systematically deconstruct this approach to determine which elements—network depth, pooling, or supervised pre-training—are truly indispensable. The central and surprising finding is that none of these are strictly necessary. The authors demonstrate that a remarkably simple model, consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters, can generate textures of high perceptual quality. For certain regular textures, the results from this minimal model are visually superior to those from the deep, pre-trained VGG-19 network. The paper supports this claim with an extensive empirical analysis of various shallow architectures and offers an insightful discussion on the broader challenges in generative modeling, such as the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample diversity.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a significant and counter-intuitive finding that challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical, and learned features are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. This "less is more" result is an important contribution to our understanding of generative models for images.
2.  The work establishes a strong and surprisingly effective minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis. The claims are well-supported by compelling visual results and a thorough empirical investigation across a wide range of model configurations.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this paper is its clear and impactful core message. By demonstrating that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained network, the authors force a re-evaluation of what aspects of CNNs are responsible for their success in texture modeling. This is a valuable scientific contribution that pares down a complex model to its essential components.

The empirical support for this claim is convincing. The authors methodically compare numerous single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and sizes. The visual evidence presented in Figures 1 and 4 clearly shows that the random multi-scale model produces high-quality textures that are often competitive with, and occasionally superior to, the state-of-the-art VGG-based model.

Furthermore, the paper provides a compelling explanation for some of these results. The observation that the shallow models can sometimes achieve a lower VGG-based synthesis loss than the VGG model itself (which directly optimizes this loss) is a powerful piece of evidence. The authors' hypothesis—that this is due to a simpler and better-behaved optimization landscape for the shallow model—is reasonable and is further supported by the experiment showing that VGG-based synthesis can be improved by initializing it with the output of the shallow model.

The main weakness of the paper is the reliance on qualitative visual assessment and a proxy metric (VGG-loss) which the authors themselves show can be flawed. The evaluation of generative models, particularly for quality and diversity, is an acknowledged open problem. It is trivial to define statistics that would perfectly reconstruct a reference texture (achieving high visual quality but zero diversity), and the paper rightly discusses the need to balance perceptual similarity with variability. While the empirical results suggest that simple statistics are sufficient for this task, it is difficult to draw a definitive conclusion without a more rigorous, quantifiable evaluation framework. However, given the inherent difficulty of this evaluation problem, the paper's extensive qualitative results and insightful discussion are sufficient to support its primary claims.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

1.  **Psychophysical Evaluation:** The paper's claims would be significantly strengthened by some form of quantitative human evaluation. While I understand a large-scale study may be beyond the scope of this work, even a small-scale, controlled experiment (e.g., a two-alternative forced-choice task asking participants which synthesis looks more like the original texture) could provide more objective evidence to complement the visual inspection of the figures. The inpainting task suggested in the discussion is also an excellent idea for future work to probe sample variability.

2.  **Quantifying Diversity:** The authors correctly identify the measurement of sample diversity (variability) as a major challenge, mentioning entropy as a theoretical but impractical measure. Could you elaborate on any other potential avenues for approximating this? For instance, would it be feasible to measure the average distance (in some feature space, perhaps the VGG space itself) between multiple unique samples generated from the same source texture as a proxy for diversity?

3.  **Conceptual Contribution:** The discussion in Section 7 regarding the limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-size images and the proposal of a new definition for a texture model based on a metric `d(x,y)` and an `epsilon`-environment is very insightful. This is a valuable conceptual contribution that could be highlighted more prominently, as it provides a path forward for thinking more formally about the trade-off between fidelity and variation in texture synthesis.
