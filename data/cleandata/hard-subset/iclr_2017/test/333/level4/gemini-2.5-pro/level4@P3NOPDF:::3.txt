This paper presents a detailed investigation into the network architectures best suited for the texture synthesis framework introduced by Gatys et al. This framework established that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses provide an excellent characterization of visual textures. A key finding of this work is that very shallow networks, even those consisting of a single filter bank with random weights, can achieve surprisingly effective results. For simple and regular textures, these shallow models can produce results that are visually superior to those generated using complex, data-adapted filters from deep networks like VGG-19. More broadly, the paper offers a valuable and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of such statistical methods for texture synthesis.

One particularly noteworthy finding, illustrated in Figure 4, is that optimizing an image with respect to a shallow filter bank can result in a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. This counter-intuitive result is attributed to the difficulty of navigating the highly non-linear optimization landscape of the VGG-19 cost function, which is a reasonable explanation. The authors strengthen this point in the supplementary material by demonstrating that initializing the VGG-19 optimization with the result from the shallow network optimization leads to improved outcomes. This is a useful addition that complements the original experiments.

The primary limitation of the paper is the absence of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. The goal of texture synthesis is to capture a *texture distribution*, not merely to replicate a single reference image. It is trivial to define shallow image statistics that could generate an exact copy of any reference texture, thus achieving perfect visual quality but failing entirely to model the underlying distribution. Since the paper's claims rest on visual inspection, it is difficult to conclusively determine whether the proposed methods are successfully capturing these distributions or simply performing high-fidelity reconstruction. Therefore, while the empirical results strongly suggest that simple statistics are sufficient for synthesizing high-quality and diverse textures (when compared to more complex statistics), the lack of quantitative evaluation makes it difficult to draw a definitive conclusion.

The authors acknowledge this evaluation challenge. They suggest that diversity could be measured via entropy, which is a sound approach in theory. However, as they concede, this is difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, an entropy-based metric would only address sample diversity and would not capture the equally crucial aspect of visual quality. The authors also propose conducting a psychophysical assessment, which may be the most practical way to address both quality and diversity. However, they deem this to be material for future work.

Overall, given that the robust evaluation of image generation models remains a significant open problem, I believe the paper's strengths are sufficient to warrant publication at ICLR. Its insightful analysis and compelling empirical results make a solid contribution. Nonetheless, the inclusion of even a preliminary psychophysical assessment would have substantially strengthened the paper's claims, which, at present, rely on the reader's visual inspection of the figures in the paper and its supplement.
