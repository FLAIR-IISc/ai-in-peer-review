This paper investigates the necessary components for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, building on the framework of Gatys et al. which uses correlation statistics (Gram matrices) of deep feature responses. The authors systematically question the assumptions that deep, hierarchical representations and supervised pre-training are essential for this task. Their main finding is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually on par with, and sometimes superior to, those produced by a deep, pre-trained VGG-19 network. More broadly, the paper provides an insightful discussion on the fundamental challenges and trade-offs in texture synthesis, particularly the balance between perceptual similarity and sample diversity.

**Decision: Accept**

My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper presents a strong and surprising finding: that deep, pre-trained features are not strictly necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The proposed single-layer, random-filter model serves as a powerful and important baseline for the field.
2.  The work includes a thoughtful and valuable discussion on the limitations of current texture synthesis methods and evaluation, particularly concerning the difficulty of optimizing deep models and the challenge of balancing perceptual quality with sample variability.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core strength of this paper lies in its clear and effective challenge to prevailing assumptions in the field of neural texture generation. The authors convincingly demonstrate that a shallow network with random filters, particularly the multi-scale variant, can capture complex, long-range correlations in textures, rivaling the state-of-the-art model from Gatys et al. (2015a). This result is not only surprising but also forces a re-evaluation of what aspects of deep neural networks are truly critical for modeling natural image statistics.

Furthermore, the paper uncovers a fascinating insight into the optimization process. As shown in Figure 4, optimizing an image using the shallow random model can result in a sample that achieves a *lower* VGG-19 loss than a sample generated by optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors reasonably attribute this to the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow model compared to the highly non-linear cost function of the deep VGG network. The supplementary experiment, where the output of the shallow model is used to initialize the VGG-based optimization to achieve better results, provides strong support for this hypothesis and is a valuable addition.

The primary limitation of the work is the lack of a systematic comparison against a quantifiable objective that measures how well the model captures a *distribution* over textures, rather than just generating a single high-quality sample. As the authors themselves discuss, the goal of texture synthesis is not merely to reproduce the reference image. Indeed, one could trivially define shallow image statistics that allow for perfect reconstruction, achieving excellent visual quality but zero variability. While the paper's empirical results suggest that simple statistics are sufficient for generating perceptually convincing and diverse samples, it is difficult to confirm this conclusively without a formal evaluation framework.

However, this limitation is not a fatal flaw. Evaluating the quality and diversity of generative models is a notoriously difficult and largely unsolved problem. The authors acknowledge this challenge explicitly, discussing potential avenues like measuring entropy or conducting psychophysical studies. Given the difficulty of the evaluation problem, the paper's empirical evidence and insightful discussion are substantial enough to be a valuable contribution.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   A small-scale psychophysical study would have significantly bolstered the claims about perceptual quality. The paper notes that the quantitative VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture the perceptual advantages of the multi-scale model, particularly for regular, man-made textures. A human study, even a simple one (e.g., A/B testing), could provide definitive evidence in these cases and strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between "perceptual similarity" and "variability" is excellent. I would be interested to know if the authors experimented with methods to explicitly control this balance. For example, did they investigate the effect of early stopping during optimization, or consider adding a regularization term to encourage diversity among synthesized samples?
