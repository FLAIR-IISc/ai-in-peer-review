- Demonstrates a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters can synthesize high-quality textures, sometimes outperforming deep pre-trained VGG-19 on regular textures.  
- Shows depth, pooling, and supervised pre-training are not strictly necessary for state-of-the-art parametric texture synthesis.  
- Establishes a strong minimal baseline with systematic comparisons across single-layer architectures (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and filter sizes.  
- Empirical visual results (Figures 1 and 4) convincingly support the claim that random multi-scale models produce competitive textures.  
- Finds shallow models can achieve lower VGG-based synthesis loss than VGG itself, suggesting a simpler, better-behaved optimization landscape.  
- Demonstrates that initializing VGG-based synthesis with the shallow model’s output can improve VGG synthesis results.  
- Main weakness: heavy reliance on qualitative visual assessment and the flawed VGG-loss proxy, with limited rigorous, quantitative evaluation of perceptual quality and diversity.  
- Notes the inherent difficulty of evaluating generative models and the risk of statistics that trivially reconstruct textures (high quality but zero diversity).  
- Recommends adding psychophysical evaluation (e.g., small-scale 2AFC or inpainting tasks) to provide objective perceptual evidence.  
- Suggests quantifying diversity via proxies such as average feature-space distance between multiple generated samples (e.g., in VGG feature space).  
- Highlights the paper’s conceptual contribution: critiquing the Maximum Entropy framework for finite images and proposing a new texture-model definition using a metric d(x,y) and an ε-environment, which should be emphasized.
