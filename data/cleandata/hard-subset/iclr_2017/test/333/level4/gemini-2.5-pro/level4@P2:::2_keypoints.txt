- Paper shows single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters and no pooling can synthesize textures often competitive with deep, pre-trained VGG-based models.
- Systematic exploration finds number of feature maps, multi-scale filters, and presence of non-linearities (ReLU) are crucial for quality.
- Ablation studies compare filter types (random, k-means, PCA, Fourier), feature counts, and filter scales, providing strong empirical support.
- Multi-scale random model often matches or outperforms VGG-based synthesis on regular structures (e.g., brick walls, grids).
- Removing ReLU non-linearity causes failure to capture texture statistics, isolating essential model components.
- Authors propose using VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality, acknowledging limitations.
- Paper provides a thoughtful theoretical discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability and on finite-image/MaxEnt issues.
- Decision: Accept.
- Major contribution is surprising and impactful, offering a strong minimal baseline that challenges assumptions about depth and learned features.
- Request for clarification on optimization: sensitivity to loss/gradient scaling (10^7) and whether that choice is principled or empirical.
- VGG-loss paradox: multi-scale random model sometimes achieves lower VGG loss than the model optimizing that loss—ask for expansion on optimization landscape/local minima hypothesis.
- Ask for brief characterization of architectures where deeper random CNNs perfectly reconstruct textures to support over-constraining claim.
- Request qualitative comparison of sample variability from the random model versus the VGG model (e.g., variability across random initializations).
