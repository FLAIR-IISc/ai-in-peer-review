Building upon the framework of Gatys et al., which demonstrated that correlation statistics (empirical Gram matrices) of deep feature responses can effectively characterize visual textures, this paper investigates the performance of different deep and shallow networks within this context. A key finding is that very shallow networks, even those with a single, randomly weighted filter bank, perform remarkably well. For simple, regular textures, these models can produce results that are visually superior to those generated by complex, data-adapted filters from networks like VGG-19. More generally, the paper provides an insightful and informative discussion on the strengths and limitations of these texture synthesis methods.

Figure 4 illustrates that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can result in textures that achieve a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing the VGG-19 objective directly. The authors plausibly attribute this to the difficulty of optimizing the highly non-linear VGG-19 cost function. As a useful complement to the original experiments, the new supplementary material shows that superior optimization results can be obtained by initializing the VGG-19-based synthesis with the output from the shallow network optimization.

The primary limitation of the paper is the lack of a systematic comparison of different methods against a quantifiable objective. It is trivial to define shallow image statistics that could generate a perfect copy of a reference texture, thus achieving high visual quality. The true goal, however, is to capture the underlying distribution of a texture, and measuring how well a method accomplishes this remains an open problem. Consequently, while the empirical results seem to support the intuition that simple statistics are sufficient for high-quality and diverse texture synthesis compared to more complex statistics, it is difficult to conclusively confirm this is the case.

The authors propose that diversity could be quantified via entropy. While this is a reasonable approach, they acknowledge it is difficult to implement in practice. Furthermore, such a metric would not address the other critical aspect of the problem: visual quality. They also suggest conducting a psychophysical assessment, which may be the most practical way to address this evaluation challenge, but they have deferred this as material for future work.

Overall, given that evaluating image generation is an inherently difficult problem, the paper's strengths are sufficient to warrant publication. Nevertheless, some form of psychophysical assessment would be valuable to formally confirm the intuitions that, at present, can only be gained by visually inspecting the figures in the paper and the supplementary material.
