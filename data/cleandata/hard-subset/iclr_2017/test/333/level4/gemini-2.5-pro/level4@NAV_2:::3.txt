This paper investigates the essential components for parametric texture synthesis, building upon the framework that uses feature statistics from deep neural networks. The authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art model (Gatys et al., 2015a) to question the necessity of network depth, pooling, and supervised pre-training. The paper's main contribution is the compelling demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate high-quality textures. The results show that this minimal baseline often rivals, and in the case of regular textures, sometimes visually surpasses samples generated from a deep, pre-trained VGG-19 network. Furthermore, the paper provides a valuable and insightful discussion on the broader challenges in texture synthesis, including the trade-off between perceptual quality and sample variability.

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. My decision is based on two key factors:
1.  The paper presents a strong, clear, and surprising primary finding: that network depth and learned features are not indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis. This provides an important minimal baseline for the field and challenges prevailing assumptions.
2.  While the paper's evaluation is primarily qualitative, the visual results are compelling, and the authors provide an excellent discussion of the inherent difficulties in quantitatively evaluating generative models. The strengths of the empirical investigation and the conceptual insights outweigh the limitations of the evaluation.

The core strength of this paper is its effective demonstration that a shallow network with random filters can be remarkably successful. This finding is counter-intuitive and forces a re-evaluation of what aspects of deep representations are critical for generative tasks. The analysis is well-supported by a broad range of experiments comparing different single-layer architectures (random, k-means, Fourier, etc.). A particularly interesting result, shown in Figure 4, is that the shallow models can sometimes produce textures with a lower VGG-19 synthesis loss than the VGG-based model that directly optimizes this objective. The authors plausibly attribute this to the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow model, a point further supported by their experiment showing that VGG-based synthesis can be improved by initializing it with the output from the shallow model.

The main limitation of the work is the lack of a systematic, quantitative comparison between methods. The evaluation of texture quality relies heavily on visual inspection by the reader and the proposed VGG-based loss, which, as the authors note, is itself imperfect. The ultimate goal of texture synthesis is to capture a *distribution* of images that are perceptually similar to a reference, which involves balancing perceptual quality with sample diversity. Without robust metrics for these two aspects, it is difficult to move beyond intuition and conclusively state that one model is better than another. For example, a trivial set of statistics could force the model to simply reconstruct the original texture, achieving perfect visual quality but zero diversity. While the authors' results appear to strike a good balance, the claims cannot be fully validated without a more rigorous evaluation protocol.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent but could be made more prominent. This is a critical challenge in all generative modeling, and the paper’s framing of it in the context of texture synthesis is very clear.
*   Given the acknowledged limitations of quantitative metrics, a small-scale psychophysical study could significantly strengthen the paper's claims about perceptual quality. Even a simple two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task asking human observers to select the more realistic synthesis between the proposed model and the VGG baseline for a few key textures would provide powerful, complementary evidence.
*   The inpainting task suggested in the discussion as a way to assess variability is a great idea and would be a strong direction for future work. Highlighting this more explicitly as a potential evaluation protocol could be valuable for the community.

To help clarify my understanding, I would be interested in the authors' answers to the following questions:
*   The paper suggests that the superior performance of shallow models on regular textures is due to a simpler optimization landscape. Could you elaborate on this? Is the VGG model's feature space perhaps biased against perfect, grid-like regularity due to its training on natural scenes, leading to an objective function whose minimum does not correspond to the perceptually ideal texture?
*   The discussion of defining a texture model via a distance metric `d(x,y)` and an `ε`-environment is very insightful. How do you believe the choice of filters (e.g., random vs. Fourier vs. k-means) implicitly shapes this `ε`-environment and influences the kinds of variations the model produces, even when the optimization procedure is held constant?
*   Your multi-scale random model is highly effective. Have you investigated whether there is an optimal distribution of filter sizes? Or is the primary benefit simply having a wide range of receptive fields available to capture correlations across different spatial scales?
