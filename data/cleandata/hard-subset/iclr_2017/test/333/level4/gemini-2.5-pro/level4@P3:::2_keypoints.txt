- Core claim: a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters can produce high-quality texture synthesis, challenging the need for depth, pooling, or supervised training.
- The proposed multi-scale random-filter model often matches or outperforms the VGG-based Gatys et al. (2015a) method.
- Secondary contribution: using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason 1: The finding is surprising and significant, forcing reevaluation of which aspects of deep representations are critical for texture modeling.
- Reason 2: The paper provides a thorough, systematic empirical analysis, including ablations on depth and pooling and tests across many filter types.
- Key technical insight: Gram matrix statistics from a single layer of random filters suffice as a powerful texture descriptor.
- Comparative experiments: filters from Fourier basis, K-means, PCA, and pure randomness were evaluated; random filters performed on par or better.
- Analysis (Fig.2): multi-scale information, not merely large receptive field, explains the success of the best model.
- VGG-loss caveat: while a useful quantitative metric, it has limitations and can fail on certain regular, man-made textures.
- Suggested improvement: analyze VGG-loss failure cases to understand biases (e.g., against perfect periodic structures) in VGG feature space.
- Suggested improvement: include visualizations comparing multiple samples from the same source texture for both models to show variability trade-offs.
- Suggested improvement: emphasize and ablate optimization details (loss/gradient scaling) that enabled success with random networks.
- Questions for authors: clarify which optimization aspects (optimizer choice, loss scaling) were critical and sensitivity; provide runtime comparison between multi-scale single-layer and 19-layer VGG; discuss implications for style transfer and whether shallow random networks could work there.
