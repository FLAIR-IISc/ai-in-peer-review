- Paper studies which components (depth, pooling, supervised pre-training) are essential for parametric texture synthesis using deep feature statistics.
- Authors deconstruct Gatys et al. (2015a) to test necessity of network depth, pooling, and learned features.
- Main contribution: a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters can generate high-quality textures.
- The minimal, shallow baseline often rivals—and for regular textures sometimes surpasses—VGG-19-based synthesis.
- Paper discusses the important trade-off between perceptual quality and sample variability in texture synthesis.
- Recommendation: Accept, based on the surprising primary finding and compelling qualitative results plus strong conceptual discussion.
- Core strength: clear demonstration that shallow random-filter networks are highly effective, challenging assumptions about deep representations.
- Empirical support includes comparisons across single-layer variants (random, k-means, Fourier, etc.).
- Notable result: shallow models sometimes attain lower VGG-19 synthesis loss than the VGG-based model; initializing VGG with shallow output improves optimization.
- Main limitation: evaluation is mainly qualitative with no systematic, quantitative comparison between methods.
- Reliance on visual inspection and imperfect VGG-based loss makes it hard to conclusively compare perceptual quality and diversity.
- Risk noted: trivial statistics could reconstruct the original image, achieving high quality but zero diversity.
- Suggestion: make the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability more prominent in the paper.
- Suggestion: run a small psychophysical study (e.g., 2AFC) to provide human-evaluation evidence of perceptual quality.
- Suggestion: use inpainting as an evaluation protocol to better assess sample variability; highlight it as future work.
- Question: please elaborate why shallow models perform better on regular textures—does VGG’s feature space bias against grid-like regularity?
- Question: how do different filter choices (random vs. Fourier vs. k-means) shape the implicit ε-environment and the types of variations produced?
- Question: for the multi-scale random model, is there an optimal filter-size distribution or is having a wide range of receptive fields the main benefit?
