This paper presents a compelling and insightful investigation into the fundamental components required for parametric texture synthesis. The authors make a surprising claim: that high-quality textures can be generated using a very simple model consisting of just a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. By systematically removing elements like network depth, pooling, and supervised pre-training—all previously considered crucial—the work establishes a strong minimal baseline. The core contribution is the demonstration that this simple model can produce textures that often rival those from the state-of-the-art VGG-based method, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, learned hierarchical representations are indispensable for this task.

My recommendation is to **Reject** this paper in its current form. While the work is provocative and the core idea is valuable, my decision is based on two key reasons: 1) The main conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not essential is not fully substantiated and appears to be contradicted by some of the paper's own evidence. 2) The argument is based on a comparison where all models, including the state-of-the-art baseline, produce samples of limited quality, which restricts the generality of the conclusions about what is truly required to generate natural textures.

The primary claim that network depth is not a necessary component for high-quality texture synthesis is a strong one, but the provided evidence is not entirely convincing. While the multi-scale random model performs remarkably well on certain textures, the VGG-based model appears superior in tasks that require understanding broader spatial context. For example, in the inpainting results presented in Appendix Figure 7, the VGG model seems to generate a more structurally coherent fill, suggesting its hierarchical nature is beneficial for modeling non-local dependencies. This piece of evidence undermines the central thesis. Furthermore, even if the quality is often comparable, claiming an architectural feature is "not indispensable" requires showing that it provides little to no advantage across a wide range of challenging cases, which is not fully established here.

Secondly, the conclusions are drawn from experiments where the quality of generated samples from all methods is still far from perfect. Both the proposed single-layer models and the VGG-based model produce textures that contain visible artifacts and often fail to capture the full richness of the original image, especially when compared to results from leading non-parametric approaches. When the performance ceiling for all tested models is relatively low, it is difficult to make definitive claims about the dispensability of architectural components for achieving true high-fidelity synthesis. The observed parity in performance might simply indicate that both deep and shallow parametric models struggle with similar fundamental challenges, rather than proving that depth itself is superfluous.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the manuscript for a future submission and are not the primary basis for my decision.

The discussion section makes a crucial claim that you "conducted extensive experiments with various hierarchical architectures" and found that the quality did not improve compared to the single-layer model. This is a very important result that directly supports your thesis, but it is presented without any supporting evidence. Providing details of these experiments would significantly strengthen your argument. What specific hierarchical, random-filter architectures were tested? Could you include a figure in the appendix showing a qualitative comparison of their outputs against the single-layer multi-scale model?

Your paper concludes that while depth may not be necessary for quality, it can improve efficiency. This is an interesting trade-off. Could you provide a more concrete analysis of this? For instance, a comparison of the synthesis time or computational cost for the single-layer multi-scale model (which uses large, inefficient filters) versus a deeper, hierarchical model with a similar effective receptive field would provide valuable context for practitioners.

I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on whether the challenges faced by the VGG-based model could be more related to optimization difficulties in a very deep landscape, rather than a fundamental limitation of the representation itself. The paper notes that initializing VGG synthesis with the single-layer result improves quality, which points towards this. Could a more advanced optimization scheme for the VGG model potentially unlock the power of its deep representation and create a larger quality gap with your baseline model?
