

The paper investigates the fundamental requirements for generating natural-looking textures using deep learning, challenging the prevailing assumption that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of features are necessary. It demonstrates that high-quality natural textures can be synthesized from a single convolutional layer with random filters, no pooling, and no training—contradicting earlier work that suggested such elements were indispensable. This is a significant conceptual advance with clear implications for understanding the core mechanisms of texture representation.

I recommend acceptance with two key reasons: (1) the paper's central claim is rigorously supported by thorough empirical analysis across multiple architectures and filter types, and (2) the findings are genuinely surprising and challenge established assumptions in the field.

The paper's strength lies in its comprehensive experimental design, systematically comparing Fourier-based, K-means, PCA, and random filters across varying numbers of feature maps (363 and 3267) and filter sizes. The multi-scale model with random filters consistently produces textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods, and the analysis thoroughly explains why this occurs—particularly the importance of combining multiple filter scales rather than relying on depth or training. The authors provide compelling evidence that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer representations are not as critical as previously believed.

While the results are impressive, the evaluation methods require strengthening. The reliance on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is limited, particularly for highly regular textures where the loss fails to capture perceptual advantages. I recommend supplementing with additional quantitative metrics (e.g., LPIPS) and more extensive human evaluation across diverse textures.

To further improve the paper, consider clarifying: (1) why random filters outperform trained filters in this specific context, and (2) how the authors reconcile their findings with the common observation that random weights fail for image generation tasks. These insights would strengthen the theoretical contribution and help readers understand the precise conditions under which random filters prove effective. The paper's promising direction for future work on texture synthesis is well-articulated and aligns with other reviewers' perspectives.
