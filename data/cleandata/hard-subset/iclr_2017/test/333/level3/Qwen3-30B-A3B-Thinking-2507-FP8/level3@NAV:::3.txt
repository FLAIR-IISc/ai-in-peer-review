

**Review**

The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging the widely held assumption that deep, pre-trained networks are essential for high-quality texture synthesis. It demonstrates that surprisingly simple, shallow networks with random filters can produce textures of perceptual quality rivaling state-of-the-art methods based on deep networks like VGG-19. Through systematic experiments comparing various single-layer architectures (including random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned filters), the authors show that neither depth nor pre-training is necessary for effective texture modeling. Their key finding that shallow random networks sometimes outperform VGG-19 on regular textures, coupled with evidence that initializing VGG optimization with shallow network results improves outcomes, represents a compelling theoretical shift in understanding texture representation.

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution with well-supported claims that fundamentally challenge conventional wisdom about the necessity of deep hierarchical representations for texture synthesis. The work is well-motivated, clearly situated within the literature, and demonstrates scientific rigor through extensive empirical validation.

The main claim is robustly supported by thorough experiments across multiple texture types and architectures. The authors provide compelling evidence through Figure 4 showing that shallow networks can achieve lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG optimization, and their supplementary results on improved VGG initialization further strengthen their argument. The paper's thoughtful discussion of limitations, particularly regarding diversity measurement and the need for psychophysical evaluation, demonstrates critical awareness of the field's challenges.

Additional feedback: While the VGG loss metric provides useful quantitative comparison, including a psychophysical assessment would significantly strengthen claims about perceptual quality. The authors might also consider discussing how their findings might extend to other texture synthesis applications beyond those tested.

Questions for authors: How do you interpret cases where VGG loss is lower for shallow-network-generated textures than for VGG-generated textures? Could you elaborate on whether the multi-scale shallow network's success stems from receptive field size or filter diversity, and how these factors might generalize to other synthesis tasks?
