- Single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can generate natural textures that rival or surpass state-of-the-art deep hierarchical methods.  
- The paper challenges the assumed necessity of depth, pooling, and pre-trained features for texture synthesis.  
- Primary factors for success are the number of feature maps and use of non-linearities, not network depth or learned weights.  
- Thorough experimental analysis compares multiple single-layer architectures and filter types (random, Fourier, k-means).  
- Random filters perform as well as or better than learned/hand-crafted filters when optimization is carefully tuned.  
- Introduces a quantitative texture quality metric based on VGG synthesis loss for more objective evaluation.  
- Decision: Accept — significant, surprising, and well-supported contribution with promising future directions.  
- Recommendation to include more rigorous human evaluations to validate the VGG-based perceptual proxy.  
- Notes a paradox where multi-scale models can look perceptually better despite higher VGG loss; needs deeper discussion.  
- Requests a systematic analysis of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in synthesized textures.  
- Asks for clarification of the specific perceptual differences observed in single-layer model samples and texture-specific strengths/weaknesses.  
- Seeks details on the optimization adjustments that enabled random filters to succeed compared to prior work.  
- Requests specific examples and explanations for cases where VGG loss fails to capture perceptual advantages of multi-scale synthesis.  
- Asks how optimization could be adjusted to better control the balance between perceptual similarity and variability and the practical implications.  
- Overall appraisal: strong, well-supported challenge to established assumptions that opens valuable new research directions.
