- Investigates which components of deep nets are necessary for natural texture synthesis and shows surprising minimal requirements.
- Builds on Gatys et al. (2015) and challenges the necessity of depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining.
- Demonstrates single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can produce high perceptual-quality textures.
- Single-layer random-filter models often rival a 19-layer VGG network, especially for regular textures.
- Systematically evaluates filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and finds random filters can match or outperform learned filters.
- Introduces a quantitative VGG-based evaluation metric to supplement human visual inspection.
- Argues the crucial factor is having enough feature maps to capture statistical structure, not network depth or training.
- Shows multi-scale filters outperform single-scale by capturing both local and global texture structure via varied receptive fields.
- Observes single-layer optimization can yield lower VGG loss than VGG-based synthesis and can improve VGG results when used as initialization.
- Recommends acceptance to ICLR 2017 based on strong empirical evidence and clear experimental design.
- Major limitation: lacks a systematic quantitative comparison of texture diversity across methods.
- Notes the absence of diversity metrics undermines the claim that shallow methods fully rival state-of-the-art approaches.
- Requests a quantitative diversity measure (e.g., average pairwise distance between synthesized samples).
- Asks for clarification on interpreting VGG loss when a multi-scale model gets lower loss than the VGG model itself.
- Asks for explanation why random filters outperform learned filters contrary to prior reports.
- Suggests: relate to the MaxEnt framework, compare to non-deep texture methods, temper broad claims about depth/pretraining, and strengthen diversity discussion with examples or metrics.
