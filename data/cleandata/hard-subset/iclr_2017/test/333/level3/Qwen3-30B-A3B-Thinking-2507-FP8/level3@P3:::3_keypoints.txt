- Main contribution: shows hierarchical structure and supervised pretraining are not necessary—single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can synthesize high-quality textures.  
- Empirical finding: shallow random-filter networks (with many feature maps) often rival or outperform VGG-19-based methods in perceptual quality.  
- Methodology: thoroughly evaluates multiple shallow architectures (Fourier, k-means, random) and analyzes optimization dynamics.  
- Key result: a multi-scale random network sometimes attains lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG optimization, indicating a more complex VGG optimization landscape.  
- Validation: initializing VGG optimization with shallow-network results improves outcomes, supporting the main claim.  
- Novelty: contradicts prior reports that random networks fail to produce interesting textures, attributed here to better optimization tuning and filter quantity.  
- Insight on filters: random filters with large channel counts (e.g., 3267 maps) outperform hand-crafted and unsupervised filters for texture synthesis.  
- Limitation highlighted: Gram-matrix-based matching of a single reference image is insufficient to model texture distributions.  
- Evaluation critique: current metrics poorly capture diversity and perceptual quality; psychophysical evaluation is recommended.  
- Suggested improvement 1: add systematic quantitative comparisons using metrics beyond VGG loss (e.g., SSIM, LPIPS).  
- Suggested improvement 2: report computational efficiency (training time, memory) of shallow vs VGG-based synthesis.  
- Suggested improvement 3: expand discussion/examples of "trivial reproduction" where shallow nets perfectly reproduce reference textures and implications for distribution modeling.  
- Suggested improvement 4: discuss potential generalization of findings to other image-generation tasks beyond texture synthesis.  
- Question 1 for authors: provide concrete experiment examples where only the reference image exactly reproduced summary statistics and how that affected evaluation.  
- Question 2 for authors: specify which psychophysical tasks would be most informative for assessing visual quality and diversity.  
- Question 3 for authors: explain why multi-scale random networks sometimes reach lower VGG loss than VGG-based synthesis and how this relates to optimization dynamics.  
- Question 4 for authors: clarify how the "softness" of Gram-matrix constraints was chosen and whether it depended on texture type or network architecture.
