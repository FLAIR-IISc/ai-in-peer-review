

# Review for "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of natural texture synthesis by rigorously investigating the essential components required for generating high-quality natural textures. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly simple single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can produce textures that rival or even surpass the quality of state-of-the-art approaches based on deep hierarchical representations (Gatys et al., 2015a). The paper challenges the prevailing assumption that depth, pooling, and pre-trained features are indispensable for natural texture generation, instead showing that these aspects serve more as fine-tuning mechanisms rather than fundamental requirements. The authors present a thorough experimental analysis across multiple single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier basis, k-means learned, etc.) and demonstrate that the key factors for success are the number of feature maps and the use of non-linearities, rather than the depth or training of the network.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by challenging established assumptions with rigorous experiments. The findings are surprising yet well-supported, and the work opens up promising new directions for future research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution lies in its thorough experimental analysis that systematically tests the necessity of various architectural components. The authors demonstrate that random filters can perform as well as or better than hand-crafted or learned filters, which contradicts previous claims (Gatys et al., 2015a) that random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images. This discrepancy is explained by the authors' more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence through multiple experiments showing that single-layer networks with random filters can generate textures that often rival or exceed the quality of state-of-the-art methods. The authors also make a valuable contribution by introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss, which provides a more objective evaluation than the common qualitative human inspection.

The most compelling aspect of the paper is its clear demonstration that neither depth nor pre-training is essential for natural texture synthesis. This finding has significant implications for the field, suggesting that the core mechanisms for texture generation may be more fundamental than previously thought.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong overall, there are a few areas where additional work could further strengthen the contribution:

1. The evaluation methodology could be enhanced by including more rigorous human evaluation metrics. The paper relies primarily on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, but it would be valuable to include a more systematic human evaluation to confirm that the quantitative metric aligns with human perception.

2. The paper mentions that the multi-scale model sometimes outperforms the VGG-based model in terms of perceptual quality despite having a higher VGG loss for some textures. This apparent paradox merits more detailed discussion. How can the VGG loss fail to capture perceptual advantages, and what does this imply about the limitations of current perceptual metrics?

3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in the synthesized textures. The authors mention this as an important consideration but don't provide a systematic investigation.

## 5. Questions for the Authors
1. The paper states that "samples from the single-layer model still exhibit large perceptual differences" (Section 6). Could you elaborate on what these perceptual differences are, and how they relate to the VGG loss metric? Are there specific texture types where the single-layer model performs particularly well or poorly?

2. The paper claims that the success of random filters "originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" (Section 1). Could you provide more details about these optimization adjustments, and how they differ from the standard procedure used in previous work?

3. The authors mention that "the VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for certain textures (Section 6). Could you provide specific examples of these textures and explain why the VGG loss fails to detect the perceptual improvement?

4. The paper discusses the balance between perceptual similarity and variability as a key challenge (Section 7). How might the optimization procedure be adjusted to better control this balance, and what would be the practical implications for texture synthesis applications?

This paper makes a strong contribution to the field by challenging assumptions about the necessity of deep hierarchical representations for texture synthesis. The findings are well-supported, the experimental analysis is thorough, and the work opens up promising new directions for future research. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR 2017 program.
