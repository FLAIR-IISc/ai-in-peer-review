- Main claim: Single-layer convolutional networks with random multi-scale filters can synthesize natural textures rivaling deep pre-trained hierarchical models.  
- Key insight: Depth and pre-training are not necessary for effective texture synthesis; random filters often match or exceed VGG-based methods.  
- Experimental strength: Systematic empirical comparison across single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) varying filter sizes and counts.  
- Multi-scale benefit: Using multiple filter sizes (e.g., 3×3, 11×11, 55×55) consistently outperforms single-scale models.  
- Surprising result: Random filters outperform hand-crafted and unsupervisedly trained filters, contradicting prior claims that random filters fail.  
- Explanation offered: Improved optimization procedures likely explain why random filters succeed where earlier work reported failure.  
- Metric contribution: Introduces a VGG-based quantitative texture quality measure as an objective evaluation beyond human inspection.  
- Decision: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Revision 1: Provide a more direct and detailed comparison with non-parametric/parametric methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli) to support the claim of surpassing them.  
- Revision 2: Clarify the relevance of the VGG model’s superior inpainting performance to the paper’s core claim about texture synthesis.  
- Revision 3: Expand the analysis explaining why random filters capture texture statistics so effectively beyond optimization differences.  
- Revision 4: Discuss computational efficiency and practical costs of the multi-scale approach, especially large filters.  
- Question 1: Clarify the apparent discrepancy between claiming to surpass Portilla & Simoncelli and Figure 4’s textures that look worse than references.  
- Question 2: Reconcile VGG’s better inpainting results with the conclusion that single-layer random models match perceptual quality of VGG textures.  
- Question 3: Could a hierarchical multi-layer network with pooling achieve similar multi-scale behavior more efficiently, and would that challenge the claim that depth is unnecessary?  
- Question 4: Specify which optimization improvements enabled random filters to succeed relative to Gatys et al. (2015a) and whether these improvements generalize to other generative tasks.
