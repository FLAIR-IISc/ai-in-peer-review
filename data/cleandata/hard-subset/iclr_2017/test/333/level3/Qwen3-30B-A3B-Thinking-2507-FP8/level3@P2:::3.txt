

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of texture synthesis by investigating the fundamental requirements for high-quality natural texture generation using deep neural network statistics. The authors systematically examine whether the depth, hierarchical structure, or supervised training of feature representations are truly necessary for effective texture synthesis. Their key finding—that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters can generate textures of perceptual quality rivaling state-of-the-art multi-layer approaches—challenges previous assumptions in the field. The paper provides a thorough experimental comparison across various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters), with insightful analysis of filter size and number of feature maps. The authors also make a valuable theoretical contribution by discussing the limitations of the maximum entropy framework for texture synthesis and proposing a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between perceptual similarity and texture variability.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance: (1) The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by challenging the assumption that deep, pre-trained networks are necessary for texture synthesis, demonstrating that random shallow networks can produce comparable results. (2) The experimental design is rigorous and well-executed, with careful comparison across multiple single-layer architectures and thorough analysis of the factors influencing texture quality.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a fundamental question in texture synthesis that has been implicitly assumed but not systematically tested. The authors' experimental approach is methodical and well-justified, systematically varying key components (filter type, number of feature maps, filter size) to isolate their effects on texture quality. The visual results in Figure 4 are particularly compelling, showing that their single-layer random filter approach often produces textures that match or exceed the quality of the VGG-based approach, especially for regular textures like brick walls and grids.

The paper's quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss (Equation 7) provides a valuable objective measure that complements the traditional qualitative human inspection. The finding that optimizing with the shallow network often yields lower VGG loss than direct optimization with the VGG network (Fig. 4) is particularly significant—it suggests that the shallow network's optimization landscape is less prone to local minima, which is a practical insight for future work.

The theoretical discussion in Section 7 is insightful and well-motivated, particularly the distinction between "perceptual similarity" and "variability" as key desiderata for texture synthesis. The authors correctly identify that their method leans toward perceptual similarity at the expense of variability, which explains why it sometimes outperforms the VGG approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. While the paper provides valuable visual comparisons and VGG loss metrics, a more systematic quantitative comparison of texture diversity would strengthen the claims. The authors rightly acknowledge this limitation, but a simple metric (e.g., average pairwise distance between multiple samples) would provide concrete evidence that their method captures the texture distribution rather than just reproducing the reference image.

2. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the computational efficiency of the proposed method. While the authors note that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient, a quantitative comparison of runtime versus quality would help readers understand the trade-offs.

3. The supplementary material shows that initializing VGG-based synthesis with shallow-network results improves VGG performance. This finding should be integrated into the main text as it provides a practical insight for future work.

4. The discussion of the MaxEnt framework in Section 7 is valuable but could be more precise in explaining how the paper's approach differs from the theoretical framework. A brief comparison of the two approaches (with and without the ε-environment) would clarify this contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences between the reference and the synthesized texture." Can the authors provide more specific examples where this approximation fails (beyond the regular textures mentioned in Section 6), and what these failures might indicate about the limitations of current deep feature statistics for texture synthesis?

2. The authors note that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Can the authors elaborate on why this occurs, and whether this suggests the VGG-based loss might be capturing different aspects of texture quality than human perception?

3. Given that the paper demonstrates that random shallow networks can match or exceed VGG performance, what do the authors believe are the true benefits of deep networks for texture synthesis beyond computational efficiency (which the authors note is not improved by deeper architectures)?
