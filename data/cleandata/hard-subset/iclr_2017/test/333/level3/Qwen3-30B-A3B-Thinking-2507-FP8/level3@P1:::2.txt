

The paper presents a compelling investigation into the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks with supervised training are essential. The authors demonstrate that high-quality natural textures can be synthesized using a single convolutional layer with random filters, no pooling, and no pre-training on natural images. This finding directly contradicts prior work (Gatys et al., 2015a) which claimed random filters fail to generate perceptually valid textures. The thorough experimental analysis systematically tests diverse single-layer architectures—including Fourier bases, k-means clusters, PCA, and random filters—across filter sizes, numbers of feature maps, and multi-scale configurations. Crucially, the multi-scale random filter model often matches or exceeds the perceptual quality of the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach, particularly for textures with regular structures (e.g., brick walls, grids).

I recommend **acceptance** for two key reasons: (1) The core claim—that depth, pooling, and pre-training are not indispensable for natural texture synthesis—is rigorously validated through extensive ablation studies, and (2) The work fundamentally reshapes understanding of deep features' role in generative tasks, revealing that statistical properties of random filters suffice for high-quality synthesis.

The supporting evidence is exceptionally strong. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of 9+ single-layer models against VGG synthesis across 20 textures (Fig. 4), showing random filters consistently rival VGG. They further demonstrate that (a) increasing feature map count (363 vs. 3267) improves quality, (b) multi-scale filters (combining 3×3, 11×11, 55×55) capture long-range correlations missed by single-scale filters, and (c) non-linearities (ReLUs) are essential. The analysis of optimization dynamics explains why random-filter models outperform VGG in some cases: simpler optimization avoids local minima, and VGG-loss itself can paradoxically favor less variable samples (Fig. 4, Appendix A). Critically, the authors acknowledge limitations—VGG-loss fails for highly structured textures like grids—and contextualize their work within the MaxEnt framework, arguing that perceptual quality depends on the *distance metric* used, not just summary statistics.

The primary limitation is the reliance on VGG-based loss for evaluation, which the authors admit is imperfect (e.g., fails for wall/grids). While human evaluation is standard, the paper uses VGG-loss as a proxy due to practical constraints. This is a reasonable trade-off for a methodological paper, but the evaluation could be strengthened by including human assessment for the top-performing samples (e.g., multi-scale random vs. VGG for brick wall).

To further improve the paper, consider:  
1. **Adding a brief human evaluation study** for 5-10 key samples (e.g., multi-scale random vs. VGG for wall/grids) to directly validate VGG-loss limitations.  
2. **Clarifying the "variability" trade-off** mentioned in Section 7. Explicitly discuss how the optimization stopping criterion affects sample diversity (e.g., early vs. late stopping) and its impact on perceptual quality.  
3. **Addressing the computational inefficiency** of large filters (55×55) in multi-scale models. While the authors note hierarchical nets are faster but lower quality, a sentence on practical deployment constraints would strengthen the conclusion.

Key questions for the authors:  
1. For the grid texture (Fig. 4, row 3), VGG-loss is *lower* for your multi-scale random samples than for VGG-based samples. Does this indicate a fundamental flaw in VGG-loss as a perceptual metric, or is it a quirk of the grid's regularity?  
2. In Section 7, you note that "finite-size images" allow exact reconstruction of the reference texture during synthesis. How would you modify the optimization to *guarantee* sample variability while maintaining perceptual similarity?  
3. The multi-scale model uses 1024 filters (not 3267). Was this chosen for computational reasons, or does it represent an optimal balance between quality and efficiency?  

This work is a significant methodological contribution that redefines assumptions about deep networks in generative modeling. It provides a minimal, interpretable baseline for texture synthesis and opens pathways to more efficient generative models. The thorough analysis, surprising results, and clear alignment with the field's foundational questions make it a strong candidate for ICLR 2017.
