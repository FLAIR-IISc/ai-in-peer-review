

# Review for "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of texture synthesis by critically examining the essential components of deep learning-based texture synthesis approaches. The authors build upon Gatys et al.'s influential work by demonstrating that the hierarchical structure and supervised training of deep networks are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. They present a compelling minimal baseline using single-layer convolutional networks with random filters that often produces textures rivaling those generated by the state-of-the-art VGG-19-based method. The paper thoroughly investigates various shallow network architectures (including Fourier bases, k-means clusters, and random filters), provides insightful analysis of optimization dynamics, and offers a thoughtful discussion of the limitations of current evaluation methodologies. The main finding that very shallow networks with random filters can generate perceptually compelling textures challenges previous assumptions about the necessity of deep, pretrained feature representations.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with well-executed experiments and insightful analysis. The main limitation regarding evaluation methodology is acknowledged and discussed, and the core findings are robustly supported.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a fundamental question in deep learning-based image generation: what aspects of deep neural networks are truly essential for texture synthesis. The experimental evidence is compelling, particularly in Figure 4 which demonstrates that shallow networks can achieve lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG-optimization, suggesting VGG's optimization landscape is more challenging. The supplementary results showing that initializing VGG with shallow-network results improves outcomes provide additional validation of the main findings.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly building on Gatys et al.'s work while challenging some of their assumptions. The authors demonstrate that random filters, when combined with sufficient quantity (3267 feature maps), can outperform hand-crafted and unsupervisedly trained filters for texture synthesis. This finding is particularly noteworthy as it contradicts earlier reports that random networks fail to produce perceptually interesting textures, with the authors attributing this to better optimization tuning.

The paper's discussion of the limitations of Gram-matrix-based methods is particularly valuable. The authors correctly identify that matching a single reference texture is insufficient for modeling texture distributions, and they thoughtfully discuss the challenges of measuring diversity and perceptual quality. Their suggestion of psychophysical evaluation as a practical assessment method is well-founded.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper further, consider the following improvements:

1. Provide a more systematic comparison between the shallow networks and state-of-the-art methods using multiple quantitative metrics beyond VGG loss, such as SSIM, LPIPS, or perceptual metrics that better capture visual quality.

2. Include a brief analysis of the computational efficiency of the shallow network approach compared to VGG-based synthesis, including training time and memory requirements.

3. Expand the discussion on the "trivial reproduction" limitation by providing specific examples where shallow networks perfectly reproduce reference textures, and how this relates to the concept of texture distribution modeling.

4. Consider adding a short section on how the findings might generalize to other image generation tasks beyond texture synthesis.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper notes that "for finite size images it is well possible that only the reference image can exactly reproduce all the summary statistics." Can you provide more concrete examples from your experiments where this occurred, and how it affected your evaluation?

2. The authors suggest psychophysical evaluation as the practical way to assess visual quality and diversity. Could you elaborate on what specific psychophysical tasks you envision would be most informative for texture synthesis evaluation?

3. In Figure 4, the multi-scale random network occasionally achieves lower VGG loss than the VGG-based synthesis. What do you hypothesize is the mechanism behind this paradoxical result, and how does it relate to the optimization dynamics of the different methods?

4. The paper mentions that "the Gram matrices are not treated as hard constraints but as soft constraints only." How did you determine the appropriate softness level for the constraints in your optimization? Was this sensitive to the texture type or network architecture?
