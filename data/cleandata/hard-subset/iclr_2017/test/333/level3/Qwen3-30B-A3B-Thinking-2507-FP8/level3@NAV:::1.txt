

The paper presents a compelling exploration of the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, challenging the prevailing belief that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are essential. The authors provide a strong baseline that often rivals state-of-the-art VGG-based models, showing that neither deep architecture nor learned feature maps are indispensable for texture synthesis. Their multi-scale random filter approach achieves impressive perceptual quality while being computationally simpler than hierarchical models.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The work is conceptually important, empirically rigorous, and makes a valuable contribution to understanding the minimal requirements for texture synthesis. The key strength is the demonstration that simple, random filters can achieve results comparable to complex, pre-trained networks, which challenges assumptions in the field. The thorough empirical evaluation across multiple filter types and scales provides strong evidence for their claims.

The main limitation is the lack of thorough validation regarding the claim that hierarchical representations aren't necessary. While the results are impressive, the comparison to hierarchical models is limited to specific architectures, and it would be valuable to see more systematic comparisons with various hierarchical models. Additionally, the observation that both VGG and single-layer models produce imperfect samples (substantially worse than non-parametric methods) should be more thoroughly discussed in relation to the paper's conclusions.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify: (1) How do they account for the fact that their multi-scale model still uses a form of hierarchy (multiple filter sizes), and whether this could be seen as a different form of hierarchy than traditional deep networks? (2) Why does the VGG-based model perform better on inpainting (Figure 7), which seems to contradict their main conclusion about the necessity of hierarchical representations? (3) Could a hierarchical model with fewer parameters than their multi-scale model potentially achieve better results while maintaining computational efficiency?
