

This paper challenges fundamental assumptions about deep neural networks (DNNs) in natural texture synthesis. It proposes a minimal baseline using a single-layer CNN with random filters (no pre-training, no pooling, no depth) and demonstrates that such a model can generate textures rivaling the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach (Gatys et al., 2015a). The authors systematically test diverse single-layer architectures—using Fourier bases, K-means clusters, PCA, and random filters—and show that high perceptual quality emerges with sufficient feature maps (e.g., 3267) and multi-scale filter configurations. Crucially, they quantify results via the VGG-based loss (Gatys et al.), revealing that random filters often match or exceed VGG in synthesis quality, while the multi-scale variant outperforms single-scale models by capturing long-range structures. The work concludes that hierarchical depth and pre-trained features are not indispensable for texture synthesis, suggesting these aspects primarily serve as fine-tuning rather than foundational requirements.

Accept. The core claim—that random single-layer networks suffice for high-quality texture synthesis—is rigorously validated through extensive empirical comparisons across 10+ filter types and 20 textures. The authors effectively dismantle the assumption that deep, pre-trained representations are necessary, using both qualitative (Fig. 4) and quantitative (VGG-loss) evidence. The multi-scale model’s superiority (Fig. 2) and the demonstration that random filters outperform learned ones (e.g., K-means) are particularly compelling, directly addressing the paper’s central question.

The review supports this decision by emphasizing three key strengths: First, the experimental design comprehensively isolates variables (depth, training, pooling) by testing single-layer variants against VGG. Second, the use of VGG-loss for objective evaluation (Sec. 5) provides a robust, reproducible metric beyond subjective human inspection. Third, the analysis of filter scales (Fig. 2) conclusively shows that multi-scale convolution (not hierarchical depth) enables long-range structure capture, resolving a key ambiguity. The paper’s contribution is significant: it reframes texture synthesis as a problem solvable with minimal DNN complexity, potentially guiding future work toward more efficient architectures.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:  
1. Clarify why random filters outperform learned ones (e.g., K-means). The authors attribute this to "optimization tuning" (Sec. 4), but a deeper analysis of the loss landscape (e.g., gradient norms, convergence speed) would strengthen this claim.  
2. Address the perceptual gap between synthesized textures and ground truth. Both VGG and single-layer models produce samples inferior to non-parametric methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000), as seen in Fig. 3. Explicitly discuss whether the VGG-loss metric adequately captures this limitation.  
3. Compare the multi-scale model’s efficiency against hierarchical alternatives. The paper notes that hierarchical models synthesize faster but don’t improve quality (Sec. 7), yet it’s unclear if a *single* hierarchical network (e.g., VGG) could match the multi-scale model’s structure capture without the 55×55 filters. A brief experiment on this would clarify if multi-scale is merely a proxy for hierarchical processing.

Key questions for authors:  
1. In Sec. 4, you state random filters "rival" VGG, yet Gatys et al. (2015a) claimed random weights fail. How does your optimization procedure (e.g., scaling factor 10⁷, max iterations) specifically resolve this discrepancy?  
2. Your multi-scale model uses 1024 filters across scales (3×3, 11×11, 55×55). Could a hierarchical network (e.g., VGG with 19 layers) achieve similar multi-scale structure capture *without* large filters, thereby improving computational efficiency while maintaining quality?
