

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of texture synthesis by challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical representations are essential for generating natural textures. The authors propose a simple but powerful baseline using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters (no training, no pooling, no depth), demonstrating that this minimal approach can produce textures rivaling state-of-the-art VGG-based models. Their key insight—that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features may not be indispensable for texture synthesis—has significant theoretical and practical implications for the field. The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple texture types, filter configurations, and evaluation metrics, making a strong case for re-evaluating the necessity of deep networks in texture generation.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in the field with well-executed experiments, and the results are scientifically rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a crucial question: What aspects of deep networks are truly necessary for natural texture synthesis? The authors systematically test four key assumptions (hierarchy, pooling, pre-training, and depth) and demonstrate that none are indispensable. Their empirical results are particularly compelling: the multi-scale random filter approach often matches or exceeds VGG-based synthesis quality (Figure 4), and the single-layer models consistently outperform hand-crafted parametric methods from two years prior.

The paper is well-motivated within the literature, building directly on Gatys et al.'s seminal work while taking a contrarian approach to explore minimal requirements. The authors carefully address previous work suggesting random networks fail for image generation (Gatys et al., 2015a) by demonstrating that their more elaborate optimization procedure resolves this discrepancy.

The scientific rigor is evident in the thorough ablation studies: they test different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), filter sizes (3×3 to 55×55), and configurations (single-scale vs. multi-scale), providing clear evidence that the multi-scale combination of filters is crucial for capturing both small- and large-scale texture structures. The authors also introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss, which provides an objective complement to subjective human evaluation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper makes a strong case for the sufficiency of single-layer random filters, it would benefit from:
- A more detailed comparison with non-parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli), as the generated samples appear substantially worse than these approaches despite the authors' claims of high perceptual quality.
- A deeper exploration of why the single-layer random filter approach works so well, particularly given previous negative results with random networks.
- More concrete discussion of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in the generated textures, including potential metrics for quantifying variability.
- A clearer explanation of the computational efficiency claim, with runtime comparisons between single-layer and multi-layer approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper notes that "a larger number of feature maps generally increases the perceptual quality," but Figure 4 shows that Random-3267 often performs worse than Random-363. Could you clarify this apparent contradiction and explain the optimal number of feature maps for texture synthesis?

2. The VGG-based model performs better on inpainting (Figure 7), suggesting potential limitations of single-layer models for certain tasks. Could you discuss why this might be the case, and whether the single-layer model's limitations are related to its lack of hierarchical representation?

3. The authors conclude that "deep hierarchical representations are not necessary but can improve efficiency." Could you elaborate on whether a hierarchical model (with random filters) might handle multi-scale structure more efficiently than the multi-scale single-layer approach used in the paper?
