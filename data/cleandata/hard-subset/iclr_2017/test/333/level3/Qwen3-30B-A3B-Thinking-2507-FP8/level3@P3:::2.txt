

# Review of "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling and counterintuitive contribution to the field of deep learning-based texture synthesis. The authors systematically investigate which aspects of deep neural networks are truly necessary for generating natural-looking textures, challenging the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are essential. They demonstrate that a simple single-layer convolutional network with random filters can produce textures of perceptual quality that rival state-of-the-art methods using deep networks. The paper presents a thorough empirical analysis across multiple architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), filter sizes, and single- vs. multi-scale configurations. The authors' findings fundamentally challenge our understanding of what makes deep networks effective for natural image generation, suggesting that the key elements are the number of feature maps and the combination of multiple spatial scales rather than network depth or supervised training.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution that challenges the field's assumptions, and the experimental evidence supporting this claim is robust.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's most compelling contribution is its thorough analysis of the minimal requirements for texture synthesis. The authors systematically test numerous architectural variations and demonstrate that random filters perform as well as or better than trained filters, which is a surprising and important finding. This directly challenges Gatys et al.'s (2015a) earlier claim that random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting textures, suggesting the discrepancy arises from differences in optimization procedures rather than the nature of the filters themselves.

The paper's analysis is exceptionally thorough. The authors not only test different filter types but also conduct careful ablation studies on filter size, multi-scale architectures, and the role of non-linearities. Their demonstration that the multi-scale model outperforms single-scale models (Fig. 2) and that the combination of different filter scales is crucial for capturing both small- and large-scale structures provides valuable theoretical insights. The paper also makes an important contribution by introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based loss, which provides a more objective evaluation than the traditional reliance on human inspection.

The paper's findings align with and extend the recent literature on texture synthesis. The authors correctly position their work within the MaxEnt framework for texture modeling and provide a thoughtful discussion of how their results relate to theoretical expectations. Their suggestion that the key to good texture synthesis lies in finding appropriate distance metrics rather than using more complex networks points to a promising direction for future research.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper makes a strong contribution, there are a few areas where additional work would strengthen it. The evaluation methods, while more objective than traditional human inspection, still rely on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality. The authors acknowledge this limitation (Section 7), and additional quantitative metrics (e.g., comparing against SSIM, LPIPS, or other perceptual metrics) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation. The paper could also benefit from a more detailed discussion of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in synthesized textures, which is crucial for practical applications.

The computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model with large filters (up to 55×55) is noted in Section 7, but the authors could explore potential optimizations or alternatives to achieve similar receptive fields more efficiently. While the paper demonstrates that deeper networks don't improve quality, they might consider briefly discussing how their findings might inform the design of more efficient texture synthesis architectures.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The authors note a discrepancy between their results with random filters and Gatys et al.'s (2015a) claim that random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images. Can you provide more insight into what specific aspects of your optimization procedure (beyond the loss scaling mentioned in Section 4) account for this difference?

2. In Figure 2, the multi-scale model with 1024 random filters outperforms single-scale models with the same number of filters. How does the receptive field size (55×55) contribute to this performance compared to the combination of multiple scales? Could the same effect be achieved with a single large filter, or is the multi-scale aspect critical?

3. The paper suggests that the Gram matrix approach works well for texture synthesis despite the MaxEnt framework's theoretical limitations for finite-size images. How might the authors' findings inform future work on improving texture synthesis models to better balance perceptual similarity and variability, especially for applications where texture diversity is important?
