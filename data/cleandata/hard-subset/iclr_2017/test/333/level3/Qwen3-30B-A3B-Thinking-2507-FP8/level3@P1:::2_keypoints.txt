- Main claim: A single convolutional layer with random filters, no pooling, and no pre-training can synthesize high-quality natural textures, contradicting prior assumptions.  
- Key result: Multi-scale random-filter models often match or exceed perceptual quality of VGG-based synthesis, especially for regular textures (e.g., brick walls, grids).  
- Experimental scope: Systematic evaluation of diverse single-layer architectures (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random) across filter sizes, feature counts, and multi-scale setups.  
- Ablations: Increasing feature maps improves quality; combining multiple filter scales captures long-range correlations; ReLU non-linearities are essential.  
- Optimization insight: Simpler optimization with random filters can avoid VGG-related local minima and produce more variable/better samples.  
- Metric critique: VGG-loss can favor less variable samples and can fail as a perceptual metric for highly structured textures.  
- Theoretical framing: Results are contextualized within the MaxEnt framework—perceptual quality depends on the distance metric, not merely summary statistics.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance because the claim is rigorously validated and it reshapes understanding of deep features in generative tasks.  
- Evidence strength: Comprehensive comparison of 9+ single-layer models vs VGG on 20 textures supports the claims.  
- Limitation: Heavy reliance on VGG-based loss for evaluation; acknowledged as imperfect for certain textures.  
- Suggested improvement 1: Add a brief human evaluation for 5–10 key samples to validate VGG-loss limitations.  
- Suggested improvement 2: Clarify the "variability" trade-off and how optimization stopping criteria affect sample diversity and perceptual quality.  
- Suggested improvement 3: Discuss computational inefficiency of large filters (e.g., 55×55) and practical deployment constraints.  
- Open question 1: Does VGG-loss’s lower score for multi-scale random samples on grid textures indicate a fundamental flaw in VGG-loss or a quirk of regular textures?  
- Open question 2: How can optimization be modified to guarantee sample variability while maintaining perceptual similarity?  
- Open question 3: Was the choice of 1024 filters in the multi-scale model driven by computational limits or an optimal quality/efficiency trade-off?  
- Overall judgment: Significant methodological contribution providing a minimal, interpretable baseline for texture synthesis and opening paths to more efficient generative models.
