- Main contribution: Shows that single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can generate natural textures of perceptual quality comparable to state-of-the-art multi-layer (VGG) approaches.  
- Research question: Systematically tests whether depth, hierarchical structure, or supervised training are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.  
- Experimental breadth: Compares multiple single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and analyzes filter size and number of feature maps.  
- Key empirical finding: Random shallow networks often match or exceed VGG-based synthesis, especially for regular textures like brick walls and grids.  
- Optimization insight: Optimizing with the shallow network frequently yields lower VGG loss than direct optimization with VGG, suggesting a smoother/less problematic optimization landscape.  
- Theoretical contribution: Argues limitations of the maximum entropy framework and distinguishes between perceptual similarity and texture variability as separate desiderata.  
- Acceptance decision: Recommend accept due to challenging an important assumption and rigorous experimental design.  
- Supporting argument: Addresses a fundamental, previously untested assumption in texture synthesis with methodical, well-justified experiments.  
- Visual evidence: Figure 4 shows compelling qualitative results where shallow random filters produce high-quality textures.  
- Quantitative evaluation: Uses VGG-based loss (Equation 7) as an objective complement to qualitative inspection.  
- Feedback 1: Recommend adding a systematic quantitative metric for texture diversity (e.g., average pairwise distance between samples).  
- Feedback 2: Request clearer, quantitative discussion of computational efficiency and runtime versus quality trade-offs.  
- Feedback 3: Suggest integrating the supplementary result that initializing VGG-based synthesis with shallow-network results improves performance into the main text.  
- Feedback 4: Ask for a more precise comparison of the paper’s approach to the MaxEnt framework (including the ε-environment distinction).  
- Question 1: Ask for concrete examples where VGG-based loss fails to approximate perceptual differences and what those failures imply.  
- Question 2: Ask why multi-scale model samples can have lower VGG loss than VGG-based model for some textures and whether VGG loss captures different aspects than human perception.  
- Question 3: Ask what genuine benefits deep networks provide for texture synthesis beyond computational efficiency, given the shallow-network results.
