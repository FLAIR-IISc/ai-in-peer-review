- Main claim: A single-layer convolutional network with random filters can generate natural-looking textures rivaling deep, pretrained models.  
- Approach: Systematic empirical study across filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), sizes, and single- vs multi-scale architectures.  
- Key insight: Number of feature maps and multi-scale receptive fields matter more than network depth or supervised pretraining.  
- Decision: Accept — contribution is significant and experimentally robust.  
- Surprising finding: Random filters perform as well or better than trained filters, contradicting earlier claims (Gatys et al.).  
- Thoroughness: Extensive ablation studies on filter size, non-linearities, and multi-scale design support conclusions.  
- Multi-scale benefit: Multi-scale models outperform single-scale models by capturing both small- and large-scale structures.  
- Evaluation: Introduces a VGG-based quantitative texture quality measure to complement human inspection.  
- Theoretical framing: Results positioned within the MaxEnt framework and suggest focusing on distance metrics rather than deeper networks.  
- Limitation: VGG-based loss is only a proxy for perceptual quality—additional metrics (SSIM, LPIPS) would strengthen evaluation.  
- Trade-offs: Need more discussion on balancing perceptual similarity versus variability in synthesized textures.  
- Efficiency concern: Multi-scale models with large filters (up to 55×55) are computationally inefficient; optimizations or alternatives should be explored.  
- Practical implication: Findings could inform design of more efficient texture synthesis architectures, but authors should discuss this more.  
- Question 1: Which specific optimization choices (beyond loss scaling) explain why random filters work here but failed in Gatys et al.?  
- Question 2: How much of the multi-scale benefit comes from large receptive fields (55×55) versus combining multiple scales—could a single large filter suffice?  
- Question 3: How can these findings guide future work to better balance perceptual similarity and variability for applications needing texture diversity?
