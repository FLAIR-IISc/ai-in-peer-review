

# Review for "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## Summary
This paper makes a compelling contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in deep learning-based texture synthesis: that hierarchical deep networks with pre-trained features are essential for generating natural textures. The authors present a surprisingly simple yet powerful baseline that relies on a single-layer convolutional network with random filters (and multi-scale filtering) to synthesize textures of high perceptual quality. Their key insight is that neither depth nor pre-training of features is necessary for effective texture synthesis, as random filters can generate textures that often rival state-of-the-art methods based on multi-layer VGG networks. The work is well-executed, with thorough empirical comparisons across various single-layer architectures and comprehensive analysis of the role of filter size, number, and type.

## Decision
I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses a fundamental question in texture synthesis with clear empirical evidence. The key contribution lies in demonstrating that random filters can match or exceed the performance of sophisticated deep networks, which directly challenges the prevailing view that hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are indispensable. This is a significant conceptual contribution that could reshape how we think about deep feature representations for generative tasks.

The empirical results are well-designed and convincing. The authors systematically test various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random filters) with different numbers of feature maps and filter sizes, and their multi-scale approach consistently outperforms single-scale models. The observation that random filters work better than hand-crafted or unsupervisedly trained filters is particularly striking and challenges prior work (Gatys et al., 2015a) that claimed random filters fail. The authors provide a plausible explanation for this discrepancy: improved optimization procedures rather than feature quality.

The paper's methodological contribution is also strong, introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based loss that provides an objective metric beyond human inspection. This is valuable for future work in the field.

## Additional Feedback
While the paper makes a strong case, there are a few areas where the presentation could be strengthened:

1. The comparison with non-parametric texture synthesis methods (like Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) is mentioned but not fully elaborated. Given that the authors claim their method "surpasses parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli," a more direct comparison would strengthen their position.

2. The paper states that "the VGG-based model seems to perform better on inpainting (Figure 7)" but doesn't explain why this matters for their main claim about texture synthesis. Clarifying how this relates to the core thesis would be valuable.

3. The explanation for why random filters work so well could be expanded. The paper mentions the optimization procedure as a key factor, but a more detailed analysis of why random filters capture texture statistics effectively would add depth.

4. The computational efficiency of the multi-scale approach (with large filters) should be discussed more explicitly, as this is a practical concern for real-world applications.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper notes that their random filter approach "surpasses parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli," yet Figure 4 shows textures that appear substantially worse than the reference textures. Could you clarify this discrepancy and provide a more direct quantitative comparison?

2. You mention that the VGG-based model performs better on inpainting (Figure 7), which seems to contradict your main thesis about the superiority of single-layer random filter models. How do you reconcile this finding with your overall conclusions about the perceptual quality of synthesized textures?

3. Your multi-scale model achieves good results by using multiple filter sizes (3×3, 11×11, 55×55). Could a hierarchical multi-layer network (with standard pooling) achieve similar multi-scale capabilities more efficiently, and if so, how would this affect your conclusion that depth is unnecessary?

4. You state that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Given that previous work (Gatys et al., 2015a) suggested random filters fail, what specific optimization improvements enabled this success, and could these be generalized to other generative tasks?

This paper makes an important conceptual contribution that challenges assumptions in the field while providing a simple yet effective texture synthesis baseline. The work is well-executed, the results are convincing, and the implications extend beyond texture synthesis to broader questions about the necessity of deep hierarchical representations in generative modeling. With minor clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
