- Proposes a minimal single-layer CNN with random filters (no pre-training, pooling, or depth) that can synthesize textures rivaling VGG-based methods.  
- Systematically evaluates many single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and shows high perceptual quality with enough feature maps (e.g., ~3267) and multi-scale filters.  
- Uses VGG-based loss for quantitative evaluation, finding random filters often match or exceed VGG, and multi-scale models capture long-range structure better than single-scale.  
- Concludes that hierarchical depth and pre-trained features are not essential for texture synthesis and mainly serve as fine-tuning.  
- Recommendation: Accept — claims are rigorously validated with extensive experiments across 10+ filter types and 20 textures.  
- Strength 1: Experimental design cleanly isolates variables (depth, training, pooling) by comparing single-layer variants to VGG.  
- Strength 2: VGG-loss provides a robust, reproducible metric beyond subjective inspection.  
- Strength 3: Filter-scale analysis demonstrates multi-scale convolution (not hierarchical depth) enables long-range structure capture.  
- Contribution: Reframes texture synthesis as solvable with minimal DNN complexity, pointing toward more efficient architectures.  
- Improvement 1: Clarify why random filters outperform learned ones; analyze the loss landscape (e.g., gradient norms, convergence speed) to support the "optimization tuning" claim.  
- Improvement 2: Address the perceptual gap to ground truth and non-parametric methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli); discuss VGG-loss limitations in capturing this.  
- Improvement 3: Compare multi-scale model efficiency to hierarchical alternatives; test whether a hierarchical network could match multi-scale performance without large filters.  
- Key question 1: How does your optimization procedure (e.g., scaling factor 10^7, max iterations) resolve the discrepancy with Gatys et al. who reported random weights fail?  
- Key question 2: Could a hierarchical network (e.g., VGG-19) capture multi-scale structure without large filters and thus be more computationally efficient while maintaining quality?
