- Proposes a single-layer convolutional network with random filters (no training/pooling/depth) that can produce textures rivaling VGG-based models.  
- Challenges the assumption that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are necessary for natural texture synthesis.  
- Provides thorough empirical validation across multiple texture types, filter configurations, and evaluation metrics.  
- Decision: Accept — paper makes a significant, well-executed contribution with rigorous experiments.  
- Systematically tests four assumptions (hierarchy, pooling, pre-training, depth) and finds none are indispensable.  
- Multi-scale random-filter approach often matches or exceeds VGG-based synthesis quality and outperforms older parametric methods.  
- Resolves prior claims that random networks fail for image generation by using a more elaborate optimization procedure.  
- Ablation studies explore filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), sizes (3×3 to 55×55), and single- vs multi-scale configurations; multi-scale combination is crucial.  
- Introduces a quantitative VGG-loss evaluation metric to complement human perceptual evaluation.  
- Suggests need for a more detailed comparison with non-parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli).  
- Requests deeper analysis of why single-layer random filters work well despite earlier negative results.  
- Recommends investigation of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and metrics to quantify variability.  
- Asks for clearer explanation and runtime comparisons supporting the computational efficiency claim.  
- Question: clarify why increasing feature maps sometimes decreases quality (Random-3267 vs. Random-363) and what the optimal feature count is.  
- Question: explain why VGG-based models perform better on inpainting and whether single-layer limitations stem from lack of hierarchy.  
- Question: discuss whether hierarchical models with random filters could handle multi-scale structure more efficiently than the paper’s multi-scale single-layer approach.
