

The paper "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?" investigates the essential components of deep neural networks for natural texture synthesis. Building on Gatys et al. (2015), the authors explore which aspects of network architecture and feature representations are truly necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. They demonstrate that neither the depth of the network, the use of pooling layers, nor the supervised training of features are indispensable. Instead, a single-layer convolutional network with random filters can generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling the state-of-the-art approach using a 19-layer VGG network. The authors systematically test various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier-based, K-means learned, PCA-based) and show that random filters can perform as well as or better than learned filters. They also introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model to complement traditional human inspection.

I recommend acceptance for ICLR 2017. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that deep hierarchical representations are not essential for natural texture synthesis, challenging previous assumptions in the field. This finding is supported by thorough empirical evidence showing that simple single-layer networks with random filters can produce textures that visually match or exceed those generated by more complex models, particularly for regular textures. The key strength of the paper lies in its clear experimental design and comprehensive comparison of different network architectures.

The paper's main contribution is showing that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable as previously thought. The authors provide convincing evidence that the key requirement is having a sufficient number of feature maps (to capture necessary statistical structure), rather than network depth or training. Their experiments with different filter types and network configurations, including the surprising finding that random filters outperform learned filters in some cases, are particularly compelling. The paper also provides valuable insights into why multi-scale filters outperform single-scale ones, showing that the combination of different receptive field sizes is crucial for capturing both local and global texture structures.

The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss function is a significant contribution, providing a more objective measure of texture quality than human inspection alone. The authors demonstrate that optimizing with the single-layer model can sometimes yield lower VGG loss than optimizing directly with the VGG model itself, suggesting that the single-layer model's optimization landscape is less prone to local minima. This is further supported by the observation that initializing VGG-based synthesis with results from the single-layer model improves performance.

However, the paper has a notable limitation: it lacks a systematic quantitative comparison of texture diversity. While the paper acknowledges that texture synthesis should balance perceptual similarity and diversity, it doesn't provide a rigorous way to measure diversity. The authors suggest psychophysical evaluation as the practical way to assess visual quality and diversity but defer this to future work. This is a significant gap, as the ability to generate diverse samples from the same reference texture is crucial for a good texture synthesis model. The paper's claim that shallow networks can "rival" state-of-the-art methods is somewhat undermined by this lack of diversity metrics.

I have three specific questions for the authors:
1. Could you provide a more systematic quantitative comparison of texture diversity across methods, perhaps using a simple measure like the average pairwise distance between synthesized textures from the same reference?
2. The paper mentions that "for finite size images it is well possible that only the reference image can exactly reproduce all the summary statistics." How does this affect the interpretation of the VGG loss results, particularly when the VGG loss for the multi-scale model is lower than for the VGG-based model itself?
3. Could you elaborate on why random filters perform better than learned filters, especially given that the paper notes "this is in contrast to earlier reports (Gatys et al., 2015a) that suggested that networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images"?

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
- The paper would benefit from a more explicit discussion of how the proposed method relates to the MaxEnt framework for texture modeling, which is mentioned but not deeply explored.
- Consider adding a brief comparison to other texture synthesis methods that don't use deep networks, to better contextualize the performance gains.
- The paper's claim that "the depth and the pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable" might be overstated; it's possible these aspects are still useful for certain texture types, and the paper should acknowledge this nuance.
- The discussion of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and diversity is insightful but could be strengthened with more concrete examples or a simple metric for diversity.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes an important contribution to understanding the minimal requirements for deep learning-based texture synthesis. The findings challenge previous assumptions in the field and open up new avenues for research. The current evidence is sufficient for acceptance at ICLR 2017, though the addition of a more rigorous diversity assessment would significantly strengthen the paper.
