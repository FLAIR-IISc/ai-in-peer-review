## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the surprisingly minimal requirements for generating high-quality natural textures. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential for this task, the authors demonstrate that textures of comparable perceptual quality can be synthesized using a single-layer network with *random* filters. The work is a thorough exploration of various single-layer architectures and a compelling argument that the depth and supervised training previously considered crucial are not indispensable. The authors also introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG feature distances and discuss the theoretical underpinnings of their findings within the context of maximum entropy models. This is a significant result with implications for understanding the core mechanisms of natural image generation.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** This paper presents a counter-intuitive and well-supported finding that challenges existing assumptions in the field of texture synthesis. The novelty of the results, combined with the thoroughness of the experimental analysis, warrants acceptance despite some limitations in the evaluation methodology.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles a fundamental question in natural image generation: what features of deep neural networks are truly necessary for generating realistic textures? Previous work largely assumed depth and pre-training were critical. This work directly challenges those assumptions, offering a simpler and surprisingly effective alternative. The approach is well-motivated by the recent advances in texture synthesis using deep features (e.g., Gatys et al., 2015) and a desire to understand the core principles underlying these methods.

The authors rigorously test their hypothesis by comparing numerous single-layer architectures (Fourier filters, K-means filters, PCA filters, random filters) against the state-of-the-art VGG-based method. The results are striking: a single layer with random filters, particularly in a multi-scale configuration, can generate textures rivaling those produced by much more complex networks. This is not merely a demonstration of qualitative similarity; the authors introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG feature space, providing some objective support for their perceptual claims. The exploration of filter size and the impact of non-linearities (ReLUs) further strengthens the analysis.

The discussion section is particularly insightful, linking the empirical findings to the theoretical framework of maximum entropy models and highlighting the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis. The acknowledgement of limitations and the suggestions for future work demonstrate a thoughtful and nuanced understanding of the research landscape.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong overall, several aspects could be improved.

*   **Evaluation Robustness:** The reliance on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is a potential weakness. While it offers a more objective measure than solely relying on human inspection, it is still a learned metric and may not fully capture human perception. Exploring additional quantitative metrics or conducting a formal user study would further strengthen the evaluation.
*   **Optimization Details:** The paper mentions scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10<sup>7</sup> to avoid early stopping. The justification for this particular scaling factor could be elaborated upon. Is this a standard practice, or was it determined empirically? Providing more insight into the optimization process would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Cost:** The multi-scale random filter approach, while effective, is computationally expensive due to the large filter sizes. A more detailed discussion of the computational cost compared to the VGG-based method would be useful.
*   **Generalizability:** The textures used in the experiments are visually appealing and demonstrate the method's effectiveness, but are they representative of a broad range of textures? Testing the method on a more diverse dataset could reveal potential limitations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that textures synthesized from the VGG-based model sometimes exhibit *lower* VGG-based loss but are perceptually worse than textures from the multi-scale random model. Could you provide examples of these textures and discuss potential reasons for this discrepancy in more detail?
2.  You mention a potential finite-size effect in the maximum entropy framework. How do you believe this effect influences the choice of summary statistics and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability?
3.  Regarding the optimization procedure, what criteria were used to determine the scaling factor of 10<sup>7</sup> for the loss and gradients? Was any sensitivity analysis performed with different scaling factors?
4.  Have you explored techniques to reduce the computational cost of the multi-scale model, such as filter pruning or dimensionality reduction?
