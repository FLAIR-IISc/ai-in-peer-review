## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper investigates the requirements for generating natural textures using the deep-feature based approach popularized by Gatys et al. (2015). The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained networks are essential for high-quality texture synthesis, demonstrating that surprisingly good results can be achieved with a single-layer network employing random filters. The work offers valuable insights into the core mechanisms driving this texture synthesis technique and raises important questions about the role of depth and pre-training in capturing the essential statistics of natural images.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a compelling and counterintuitive finding – that surprisingly simple networks can generate textures competitive with state-of-the-art methods. This challenges a widely held belief in the field and provides a strong minimal baseline for future work. The thorough exploration of different single-layer architectures and the detailed discussion of the strengths and limitations of the approach significantly contribute to our understanding of texture synthesis.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper convincingly demonstrates its core claim through a series of well-designed experiments. The comparison of various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) against the established Gatys et al. (2015) method is thorough and insightful. The results clearly show that a single layer with random filters can produce textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling those generated by much more complex networks. The observation that the multi-scale model performs particularly well, and the analysis of why (combining different spatial scales), is a key contribution.

Furthermore, the authors go beyond simply presenting results; they critically analyze the underlying mechanisms. The discussion regarding the optimization landscape, illustrated by the observation that textures synthesized from shallow networks can lead to lower VGG-19 loss compared to directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective, is particularly insightful. This suggests that the optimization process itself plays a crucial role and that the shallower models may be easier to optimize.  The finding that initializing VGG-19 synthesis with results from the single-layer model improves performance reinforces this point.

The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their evaluation methodology. They correctly point out the challenges of quantifying texture quality and diversity and advocate for psychophysical evaluation as a more reliable measure. This honest assessment strengthens the credibility of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few refinements could further improve its impact.

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The reliance on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is reasonable, but the authors acknowledge its shortcomings. Exploring additional quantitative metrics, even if imperfect, could provide a more comprehensive comparison.
*   **Diversity Metrics:** Although the authors acknowledge the difficulty in quantifying diversity, exploring some preliminary measures (e.g., entropy of feature activations) could be valuable, even if just to demonstrate the challenges involved.
*   **Ablation Studies on Optimization:** A more detailed ablation study of the optimization procedure (e.g., learning rate, regularization) might further illuminate the factors contributing to the success of the simpler models.
*   **Visual Examples:** While Figure 4 presents a good comparison, including a larger and more diverse set of texture examples would further strengthen the visual impact of the results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the optimization landscape for deeper models is more difficult. Can you elaborate on this? Were there specific difficulties encountered during optimization (e.g., vanishing gradients, getting stuck in local minima) that led to this conclusion?
2.  The paper discusses the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability. Considering the limitations of current metrics, what specific experimental design would you recommend for a psychophysical evaluation of texture quality and diversity? What would be the critical parameters of such a study?
3.  Could you clarify the role of the scaling factor (10<sup>7</sup>) applied to the loss and gradients during optimization? What is the intuition behind this choice, and how sensitive are the results to its value?
4.  Given the success of single layer networks, do you anticipate similar results with other image generation tasks, such as image completion or style transfer? What adaptations, if any, would be necessary?
