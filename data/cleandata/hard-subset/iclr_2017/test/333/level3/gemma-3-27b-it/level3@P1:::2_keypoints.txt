- Paper shows high-quality natural textures can be synthesized with a single-layer network using random filters, challenging the need for deep, pre-trained networks.
- Experimental results are comprehensive and counterintuitive, supporting acceptance.
- Systematic comparison across filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and varying feature map counts demonstrates random filters can rival VGG-based methods.
- Authors argue prior negative shallow-network results were due to inadequate optimization and show improved tuning resolves this.
- Introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on synthesis loss in a VGG model to supplement subjective evaluation.
- Discussion links findings to maximum-entropy theory and highlights trade-offs between perceptual similarity and variability.
- Multi-scale model consistently outperforms single-scale models, indicating scale diversity is important.
- Reviewer requests more detail on optimization scaling: how scaling factors were chosen and their impact.
- Whitening of patches improves K-means filter performance; reviewer asks for explanation of why whitening matters.
- Reviewer asks for sensitivity analysis of multi-scale performance to the combination of filter sizes and whether an optimal scale distribution exists.
- Reviewer requests quantitative comparisons of computational cost (training/synthesis time) between single-layer models and VGG-based approaches.
- Questions for authors: elaborate on loss/gradient scaling rationale and selection method.
- Questions for authors: provide theoretical or empirical explanation for whitening’s effect on K-means filters.
- Questions for authors: compare effective receptive field sizes of single-layer/multi-scale models to deeper architectures.
- Questions for authors: suggest alternative or complementary metrics to VGG-based loss for evaluating texture quality and diversity.
