- Decision: Accept — paper presents a compelling, well-supported, counterintuitive result.
- Core claim: Depth and pre-training are not necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis.
- Main evidence: Single-layer networks with random filters can rival or exceed VGG-based methods in many cases.
- Experimental scope: Systematic comparison across random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA single-layer filters versus VGG.
- Key insight: Multi-scale filtering (combining different filter sizes) is crucial for success, outperforming single large filters.
- Quantitative evaluation: Introduces a VGG-based synthesis loss as an objective texture quality measure.
- Metric limitation: The VGG-based metric can miss perceptual advantages and struggles with highly regular textures.
- Theoretical contribution: Links findings to maximum-entropy models and discusses complexities in defining texture similarity.
- Ablation feedback: Ablation studies are thorough but need more systematic hyperparameter exploration, especially optimization settings.
- Optimization concern: Sensitivity to L-BFGS-B hyperparameters is unclear and should be analyzed further.
- Computational efficiency: Discussion is limited; a direct cost comparison with VGG-based models is missing.
- Suggested evaluations: Add other quantitative metrics (e.g., feature-statistics-based) and consider a controlled human study.
- Request for examples: Provide specific cases where VGG loss is misleading and propose alternative metrics.
- Generalization question: Clarify whether findings extend to other tasks (style transfer, inpainting) and why texture may be special.
- Loss landscape question: Explain why single-layer models appear less prone to local minima — simpler loss landscape or other factors.
- MaxEnt extension: Offer a concrete outline for incorporating variability in summary statistics within the MaxEnt framework.
