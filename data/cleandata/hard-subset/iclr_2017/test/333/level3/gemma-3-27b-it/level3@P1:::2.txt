## Review for ICLR 2017 - WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for generating natural-looking textures using deep convolutional neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchically trained networks are essential for this task. They demonstrate that surprisingly high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer network with random filters, challenging previous findings and offering a simpler, more efficient approach to texture generation. This is a significant and potentially impactful contribution to the field of image generation.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The findings are counterintuitive and well-supported by comprehensive experimentation. The work effectively demonstrates that the depth and pre-training commonly associated with successful texture synthesis are not indispensable, opening up new avenues for research and potentially more efficient implementations. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core strength of this paper lies in its thorough exploration of the design space for single-layer texture synthesis models. The authors systematically compare numerous architectures – Fourier basis filters, K-means learned filters, PCA filters, and random filters – with varying numbers of feature maps. This comparative analysis rigorously demonstrates that even random filters, when used in sufficient quantity, can produce textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods based on deep, pre-trained networks (Gatys et al., 2015). 

The authors convincingly argue that prior negative results with shallow networks (as reported in Gatys et al., 2015) were likely due to inadequate optimization procedures. Their more elaborate tuning addresses this limitation, enabling successful synthesis with simpler models. Furthermore, the introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure – utilizing the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model – represents a valuable step towards more objective evaluation in this field, which traditionally relies on subjective human inspection. This quantitative approach lends further credibility to their claims.

The discussion section is insightful, connecting the findings to the broader theoretical framework of maximum entropy models and identifying areas for future research, specifically concerning the balance between perceptual similarity and variability in generated textures. This demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying principles and a forward-looking perspective.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor points could enhance clarity and impact. 

*   **Optimization Details:** The authors mention scaling the loss and gradients during optimization, stating it avoids early stopping. Providing more detail on the scaling factor’s selection and impact would be beneficial. Was this scaling factor determined empirically?
*   **Filter Whitening:** The results show that whitening patches used for K-means filters impacts results. It would be useful to discuss *why* whitening seems to matter in this context. Is it related to the distribution of patch statistics or something else?
*   **Multi-Scale Model Robustness:**  The multi-scale model consistently outperforms the single-scale models. It would be valuable to analyze the sensitivity of this performance to the specific combination of filter sizes used. Is there an optimal scale distribution, or does performance remain robust across different scale combinations?
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions computational inefficiency due to large filters. Providing a quantitative comparison of training/synthesis time between the single-layer models and the VGG-based approach would further strengthen the argument for the efficiency benefits of the proposed method, even if deeper networks are faster.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors state the scaling of loss and gradients was to avoid early stopping. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind this scaling factor and provide details on how it was determined?
2.  Why does whitening the patches seem to matter for the performance of K-means-based filters? Is there a theoretical explanation or empirical evidence to support this observation?
3.  Were any efforts made to directly compare the receptive field sizes achieved in the single-layer models to those in deeper architectures? How does the effective receptive field of the multi-scale model compare to that of a comparable (in terms of parameters) deeper network?
4. The paper mentions the limitations of the VGG-based loss as a metric for perceptual quality. Could you expand on possible alternative or complementary metrics for evaluating the quality and diversity of generated textures?
