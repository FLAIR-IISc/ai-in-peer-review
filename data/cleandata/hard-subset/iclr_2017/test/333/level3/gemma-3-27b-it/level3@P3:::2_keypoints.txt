- Paper shows high-quality natural textures can be synthesized with a single-layer network using random filters, challenging the need for deep, pre-trained convnets.  
- Decision: Accept — results are novel, counter-intuitive, and well-supported despite some evaluation limitations.  
- Thorough empirical comparison of single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) against VGG-based state-of-the-art.  
- Key result: multi-scale single-layer random filters produce textures perceptually comparable to deep VGG-based methods.  
- Introduces a quantitative texture-quality metric based on VGG feature distances to complement qualitative comparisons.  
- Explores effects of filter size and non-linearities (ReLUs), strengthening the empirical analysis.  
- Links empirical findings to maximum entropy theory, discussing trade-offs between perceptual similarity and variability.  
- Strength: rigorous, insightful discussion and acknowledgment of limitations with suggestions for future work.  
- Evaluation concern: reliance on learned VGG-based loss may not fully capture human perception; additional metrics or user studies suggested.  
- Optimization concern: unexplained scaling of loss/gradients by 1e7 to avoid early stopping; needs justification and sensitivity analysis.  
- Computational concern: multi-scale random filter approach is computationally expensive due to large filters; compare costs to VGG-based methods.  
- Generalizability concern: experiments may not cover a broad enough range of textures; recommend testing on more diverse datasets.  
- Questions for authors: provide examples where VGG loss is lower but perceptual quality worse, clarify finite-size effects in max-entropy framing, justify the 1e7 scaling choice and report sensitivity, and discuss methods to reduce computational cost (pruning, dimensionality reduction).
