This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis using deep convolutional neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing notion that depth and pre-training are essential, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated from a surprisingly simple model: a single-layer network with random filters. They present a multi-scale version of this model that often rivals the performance of state-of-the-art, VGG-based approaches, suggesting that the complexities of deep, pre-trained networks may not be as crucial for this task as previously believed. The work is accompanied by a quantitative evaluation metric based on the synthesis loss in the VGG model, offering a more objective assessment than traditional human inspection.

**Reject.** While the paper presents interesting findings and a compelling argument, I believe the claims regarding the *necessity* of deep hierarchical representations and pre-training are overstated given the limitations in comparative performance and the qualitative evidence presented. The presented single-layer models, including the multi-scale version, still fall short of truly state-of-the-art results and existing non-parametric methods, as acknowledged by the authors. Therefore, while insightful, the paper doesn't definitively demonstrate that depth and pre-training are *not* important, but rather that they are *not indispensable* – a much weaker claim.

My reasoning stems from several points. Firstly, the quantitative evaluation using VGG loss, while helpful, doesn't fully capture perceptual quality. The authors themselves acknowledge its limitations, particularly for textures with regular structures. While they demonstrate comparable VGG loss scores, visual inspection (Figures 1 & 4) reveals noticeable differences in the aesthetic quality and realism of the synthesized textures compared to those generated by the VGG-based model. This discrepancy suggests the VGG loss might not be a reliable proxy for human perception in all cases. Secondly, the claim that the multi-scale random filter model "rivals" state-of-the-art is debatable. While it performs competitively, it doesn't consistently outperform the VGG model, and the difference isn’t substantial enough to overturn the current paradigm. Crucially, the paper doesn't compare against alternative *non-parametric* texture synthesis techniques, making it difficult to assess the absolute quality of the generated textures. The acknowledgement that samples are imperfect and substantially worse than these other methods is significant. Finally, the argument relies heavily on optimizing the synthesis procedure (L-BFGS-B), and it remains unclear how sensitive the results are to this specific optimization algorithm and its parameters.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

The authors should consider including a comparison against established non-parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) to provide a more complete benchmark. A more thorough perceptual evaluation, potentially involving a user study, would strengthen the claims regarding texture quality. Exploring the sensitivity of the results to the optimization algorithm and parameter tuning is also recommended. The discussion on the interplay between perceptual similarity, variability, and the choice of distance metrics is insightful, but could be expanded. It would be beneficial to address whether a deep hierarchical model, rather than simply multiple filter sizes, might offer a more efficient solution for capturing multi-scale structure. Expanding on the failure cases of the single-layer models would also provide valuable insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of optimization algorithm (L-BFGS-B) and its parameters (maximum iterations, box constraints, scaling factor)? Were other optimization methods considered, and if so, how did they compare?
2.  The paper states the single-layer models sometimes rival the VGG-based approach. Could you quantify this rivalry with statistical significance tests?
3.  The authors note that the VGG-based loss isn't a perfect proxy for human perception. Could you elaborate on the specific types of textures where the VGG loss appears to be most unreliable, and why?
4.  While the multi-scale approach improves performance, is there a trade-off in computational efficiency compared to a deep, hierarchical model with a similar receptive field size?
5.  You mention inpainting tasks (Appendix A, Figure 7) suggest the VGG-based model may perform better. Could you provide a more quantitative comparison of inpainting performance between the single-layer and VGG-based models?
