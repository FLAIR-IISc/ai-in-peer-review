## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper tackles the fundamental question of what is truly necessary for generating natural-looking textures using deep learning approaches. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training in convolutional networks are indispensable, demonstrating that surprisingly high-quality textures can be synthesized from networks with only a single layer, no pooling, and even *random* filters. This is a compelling and counterintuitive finding.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The results are genuinely surprising, and the thorough analysis provides strong support for the authors’ claims. The work is well-motivated, clearly articulating the gap in understanding regarding the core requirements for texture generation and positioning itself well within the existing literature on parametric texture modeling and deep learning approaches like those of Gatys et al.

The paper’s strength lies in its comprehensive experimental approach. The authors systematically explore a wide range of single-layer architectures, comparing them to the established state-of-the-art. The introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure—using the VGG-based synthesis loss—is a valuable contribution, offering a more objective evaluation method than solely relying on human inspection. The discussion on balancing perceptual similarity and variability is insightful and highlights important future research directions. The thorough ablation studies and comparisons (Figures 1-4) convincingly demonstrate the capabilities of their proposed method.

However, the evaluation, while improved with the VGG-based loss, could be further strengthened. While the authors acknowledge its imperfections, a more robust quantitative evaluation, perhaps incorporating additional perceptual metrics or user studies, would be beneficial. Additionally, the optimization procedure is mentioned as being more elaborate, but the details of this "tuning" remain somewhat opaque. 

Specifically, I would like to understand: 
1. Could the authors elaborate on the “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure”? What specific parameters were adjusted and how did these adjustments impact performance?
2. What is the computational cost of the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based model during synthesis? A quantitative comparison would be useful to assess the practicality of the proposed approach.
3. The paper mentions a potential finite size effect in the MaxEnt framework. Could the authors discuss how this might influence the interpretation of their results, particularly concerning the observed variability in the synthesized textures?
