## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper investigates the requirements for generating natural textures using deep learning approaches, specifically focusing on the importance of network depth and pre-training. The authors demonstrate surprisingly strong results with extremely shallow networks – even single-layer networks with random filters – for texture synthesis, often rivaling the performance of deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG-19 on simpler textures. They provide a thorough analysis of various single-layer architectures, a quantitative comparison using VGG-based loss, and a compelling argument that optimization landscape complexity may hinder deeper networks. The paper also offers insightful discussion regarding the limitations of current evaluation metrics and the challenge of balancing perceptual similarity and diversity in texture generation.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a significant and counterintuitive finding – the surprising efficacy of very shallow networks for texture synthesis – and supports this finding with comprehensive experimentation. The analysis is well-motivated and offers valuable insights into the underlying mechanisms of texture generation. While a formal psychophysical evaluation is acknowledged as desirable, the evidence presented is sufficient to justify publication.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively addresses a relevant and important question within the field of generative modeling: what architectural components are *truly* necessary for generating realistic textures? By systematically ablating components of the established Gatys et al. approach, the authors convincingly demonstrate that depth and pre-training are not as crucial as previously believed. The extensive experimentation with different single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) provides a strong basis for comparison and highlights the surprising performance of random filters, particularly in the multi-scale configuration. 

The use of VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a clever way to circumvent the reliance on subjective human evaluation. The observation that optimizing images with respect to shallow filter banks can yield lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective is particularly intriguing and supports the authors' argument about optimization landscape difficulty. The supplementary result showing that initializing VGG-19 optimization with shallow-network results improves outcomes further strengthens this claim.

Furthermore, the paper doesn't shy away from discussing limitations. The authors acknowledge the lack of a systematic, quantifiable comparison and the potential for trivial reproduction of training examples. They also thoughtfully address the challenges of measuring diversity and propose psychophysical evaluation as the ideal, albeit deferred, solution. This honesty and self-awareness enhance the credibility of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify "scales" in multi-scale model:** The paper mentions different scales in the multi-scale model (3x3, 11x11, 55x55). It would be helpful to explicitly describe how these different scales are combined during the synthesis process. Is it a parallel application of filters, or a sequential one?
* **Elaborate on optimization details:** While the paper mentions using L-BFGS-B, further details about the learning rate schedule, convergence criteria, or any other crucial optimization parameters could be beneficial for reproducibility.
* **Visual comparison of optimization trajectories:** Showing the optimization trajectories (VGG loss vs. iteration) for both VGG-19 and the shallow networks could visually demonstrate the differences in optimization landscape difficulty.
* **Diversity metrics beyond entropy:** While the authors rightly dismiss entropy as impractical, exploring other (even approximate) diversity metrics, such as the average pairwise distance between generated samples, might provide some quantitative insight.
* **Impact of image size:** How does the performance of the shallow networks vary with the size of the synthesized texture? Is there a point where the limited receptive field becomes a significant bottleneck?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the multi-scale model’s success stems from modeling correlations at different ranges. Did you experiment with different weighting schemes for the different scales, and if so, did any particular weighting significantly improve performance?
2.  The paper observes lower VGG-19 loss for samples from the multi-scale model when optimizing VGG directly. Could you provide some example images illustrating this phenomenon, highlighting the differences between the VGG-optimized and multi-scale-initialized VGG-optimized samples?
3.  You acknowledge the limitation of potentially reproducing training examples. Could you quantify this to some extent? For example, what is the average similarity (using VGG-based distance) between generated textures and the textures used during the training of the VGG network?
4.  The paper mentions “finite size effects” impacting the constraints in the MaxEnt framework. Is there a theoretical or empirical justification for choosing a particular value for the 'epsilon' parameter (tolerance for mismatch in summary statistics) that balances perceptual similarity and variability?
