This paper investigates the question of what is essential for generating natural textures using deep neural networks. Specifically, it challenges the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are critical, demonstrating that surprisingly good texture synthesis can be achieved with a single-layer convolutional network using random filters. This is a compelling and potentially impactful finding, as it suggests that the power of deep features for texture generation might be overstated.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated study with strong empirical results demonstrating that surprisingly simple networks can generate high-quality textures, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods. While a formal, psychophysical evaluation would further strengthen the claims, the current evidence is convincing enough for acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core contribution of the paper is the successful demonstration of high-quality texture synthesis using a single-layer CNN with random filters. This result directly challenges previous work suggesting the necessity of deep, pre-trained networks for this task. The authors rigorously compare their approach against a wide range of single-layer architectures, as well as the established Gatys et al. (2015a) method, providing a comprehensive evaluation of different filter types and network configurations. 

The observation that random filters can perform on par with, or even better than, pre-trained filters is particularly noteworthy. The authors attribute this to a more elaborate optimization procedure, which is a plausible explanation. Furthermore, the authors intelligently utilize the VGG-based texture model's loss function as a quantitative metric for comparison, addressing the common issue of subjective visual evaluation in texture synthesis.

The discussion section is strong, acknowledging limitations and proposing avenues for future work. The authors correctly identify the need to balance perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis and highlight the challenges of quantifying these aspects. Their discussion of the relationship between the MaxEnt framework and practical implementations of texture synthesis is insightful. The finding that optimizing with shallow filter banks can lead to lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective is fascinating and points to complexities in the optimization landscape. The supplementary material detailing the benefit of initializing VGG-19 optimization with textures from the single-layer model further supports the argument.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, several points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact.

* **Quantitative Evaluation:** While the VGG-based loss is a useful metric, it is still an indirect measure of perceptual quality. A formal, psychophysical evaluation (e.g., a human subject study asking participants to distinguish between real and synthesized textures) would significantly strengthen the claims. The authors acknowledge this as a direction for future work, but even a small-scale pilot study would be valuable.
* **Diversity Measurement:** The paper correctly identifies the importance of texture diversity. While acknowledging the difficulty of quantifying this, exploring alternative metrics (beyond entropy) or providing more detailed visual examples showcasing the variety of generated textures would be beneficial.
* **Filter Size and Receptive Field:** The investigation of multi-scale filters is insightful. A more detailed analysis of the receptive field size of different filter configurations and its relationship to the quality of the generated textures could provide further understanding.
* **Optimization Details:** The authors mention scaling the loss and gradients during optimization. A more detailed explanation of this scaling factor and its impact on the optimization process would be helpful.
* **Limitations in Generalizability:** The observation about "perfect reproduction" with sufficiently many constraints should be emphasized more strongly. A discussion of the limitations of Gram matrices in truly capturing the underlying distribution of natural textures is warranted.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you elaborate on the specific tuning of the optimization procedure that you believe accounts for the improved performance of random filters compared to previous reports (e.g., Gatys et al., 2015a)?
2. While you acknowledge the limitations of the VGG-based loss, were there any specific cases or texture types where it appeared to be particularly unreliable in reflecting perceptual quality?
3. In the discussion of the MaxEnt framework, you mention allowing a small mismatch in the constraints. Could you provide a concrete example of how this mismatch might be implemented or quantified in practice?
4. Were any attempts made to compare the computational efficiency (e.g., training and synthesis time) of the single-layer networks with the deeper VGG-based model?
5. The paper focuses on natural textures. Do you anticipate that the findings regarding the importance of depth and pre-training would generalize to other image generation tasks, such as style transfer or image inpainting?
