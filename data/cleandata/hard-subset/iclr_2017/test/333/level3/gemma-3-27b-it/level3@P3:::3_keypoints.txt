- Main finding: extremely shallow networks (even single-layer random filters) can synthesize natural textures, often rivaling deeper pre-trained networks on simpler textures.
- Experimental breadth: systematic ablation and comparison of single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) with multi-scale configurations.
- Multi-scale result: multi-scale random filters perform particularly well by modeling correlations at different spatial ranges.
- Quantitative metric: uses VGG-based loss to compare methods and shows shallow-filter outputs can yield lower VGG-19 loss than direct VGG optimization.
- Optimization insight: argues that optimization landscape complexity limits deeper networks; initializing VGG optimization with shallow results improves performance.
- Limitations acknowledged: absence of formal psychophysical evaluation, potential for reproducing training examples, and difficulty balancing perceptual similarity vs. diversity.
- Decision: accept — findings are significant, counterintuitive, and well-supported by experiments.
- Supporting claim: depth and pre-training are not strictly necessary for realistic texture generation, shown via thorough experiments.
- Reproducibility feedback: request clarification on how multi-scale filter sizes are combined and more details on L-BFGS-B parameters, learning rates, and convergence criteria.
- Visualization suggestion: provide optimization trajectories (VGG loss vs iterations) for VGG-19 and shallow networks to illustrate landscape differences.
- Diversity metrics: recommend exploring practical alternatives to entropy, e.g., average pairwise distance between generated samples.
- Image-size question: ask how synthesis performance varies with image size and when limited receptive fields become a bottleneck.
- Specific author questions: experiments with scale weighting, example images comparing VGG-optimized vs multi-scale-initialized VGG-optimized outputs, quantification of similarity to textures used to train VGG, and justification/choice of 'epsilon' in the MaxEnt tolerance.
