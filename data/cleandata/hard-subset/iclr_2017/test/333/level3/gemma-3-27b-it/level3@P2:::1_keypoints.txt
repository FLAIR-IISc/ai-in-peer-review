- Decision: Accept.  
- Core contribution: Shows single-layer CNNs with random filters can synthesize high-quality textures, questioning the necessity of depth and pre-training.  
- Significance: A simple model achieves competitive results, suggesting more efficient and interpretable texture generation is possible.  
- Clarity and positioning: Paper is well-structured and situates itself clearly within prior texture synthesis work.  
- Experimental breadth: Extensive comparisons across single-layer variants (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) against VGG-based methods.  
- Quantitative evaluation: Uses VGG-based loss as an objective measure in addition to visual inspection.  
- Performance: Multi-scale random filter model often rivals VGG, particularly on regular-structure textures.  
- Ablation: Fig. 2 shows combining filters at multiple scales is crucial to performance.  
- Discussion: Raises important points about the perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-off and connects to maximum entropy (MaxEnt) theory.  
- Limitation: Authors acknowledge generated samples are still substantially worse than non-parametric (patch-based) methods — recommend direct quantitative comparisons.  
- Inpainting: VGG model outperforms single-layer for inpainting (Fig. 7); explanation (e.g., contextual/long-range representation) should be elaborated.  
- Computational cost: Multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters; request detailed runtime and memory comparisons with VGG.  
- Generalizability: Recommend testing on broader texture datasets and analyzing sensitivity to different texture characteristics.  
- Theory: Ask for a clearer, more concrete connection between the single-layer Gram-matrix approach and the MaxEnt framework, including how Gram constraints behave for finite images.  
- Specific author questions requested: quantitative non-parametric comparison, why VGG helps inpainting, detailed computational breakdown, why deeper models don't improve textures, and how Gram matrix constraints map to MaxEnt with finite-sample mismatches.
