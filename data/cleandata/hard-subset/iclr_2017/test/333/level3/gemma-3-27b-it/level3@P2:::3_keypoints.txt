- Paper challenges the assumption that depth and pre-training are necessary for natural texture synthesis, showing strong results with a single-layer CNN using random filters.  
- Decision: Accept — well-motivated study with convincing empirical evidence.  
- Core contribution: high-quality texture synthesis using a single-layer random-filter CNN that often rivals state-of-the-art methods.  
- Comprehensive empirical comparison across single-layer architectures and against Gatys et al. (2015a).  
- Key finding: random filters can match or outperform pre-trained filters, plausibly due to improved optimization.  
- Uses VGG-based texture-model loss as a quantitative metric to mitigate purely subjective evaluation.  
- Discussion highlights limitations, future directions, and the tradeoff between perceptual similarity and variability.  
- Insight: optimizing shallow filter banks can yield lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective; initializing VGG-19 with these textures is helpful.  
- Suggestion: add formal psychophysical (human) evaluation because VGG loss is an indirect perceptual measure.  
- Suggestion: improve diversity measurement—explore metrics beyond entropy and show more varied visual examples.  
- Suggestion: provide a more detailed analysis of filter sizes/receptive fields and their relation to texture quality.  
- Suggestion: clarify optimization details, specifically the loss/gradient scaling and tuning that improved random-filter performance.  
- Limitation: emphasize that many constraints can lead to near-perfect reproduction and discuss Gram-matrix limitations in capturing true texture distributions.  
- Questions for authors: detail optimization tuning, cases where VGG loss fails perceptually, concrete examples of MaxEnt constraint mismatch, comparisons of computational efficiency, and applicability to other image-generation tasks.
