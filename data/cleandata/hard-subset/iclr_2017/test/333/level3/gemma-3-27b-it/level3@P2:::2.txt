This paper investigates the surprisingly minimal requirements for generating natural-looking textures. Challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer network with random filters. This is a significant and counterintuitive finding with implications for our understanding of what features are crucial for image generation.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported argument that contradicts established wisdom in the field. The experimental results are convincing, and the analysis is thorough, despite some limitations in the evaluation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core contribution of this work is the demonstration that depth and pre-training are *not* indispensable for natural texture synthesis. This is a strong claim, given the dominance of deep learning approaches in this area, and the authors provide substantial evidence to support it. The paper systematically compares a wide range of single-layer architectures – employing random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters – with the state-of-the-art VGG-based method. The consistent performance of the random filter models, and in some cases their ability to rival or surpass the VGG model, is striking. 

The authors’ identification of multi-scale filtering as a key component of success is also insightful. Their analysis shows that combining filters of different sizes is more effective than using a single large filter, providing a nuanced understanding of the necessary receptive field characteristics. The inclusion of a quantitative texture quality measure, based on the VGG-based model's synthesis loss, is a step toward more objective evaluation, moving beyond purely qualitative human inspection. While the authors acknowledge limitations in this metric (discussed below), it provides a useful comparative tool. 

The theoretical discussion relating the findings to maximum entropy models and the challenges of defining texture similarity is valuable. The authors thoughtfully address the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, acknowledging the complexities of defining an appropriate distance metric for texture comparison.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few areas could be improved. The evaluation, while better than purely subjective assessment, relies heavily on the VGG-based loss function. As the authors themselves admit, this metric isn't perfect and can fail to capture perceptual advantages, particularly for textures with highly regular structures. A more comprehensive evaluation would benefit from including additional quantitative measures (e.g., those based on feature statistics) and potentially a controlled human study, even if limited in scope.

The ablation studies, while thorough, could benefit from a more systematic exploration of hyperparameter space, particularly for the optimization procedure. It's not entirely clear how sensitive the results are to the specific parameters used in the L-BFGS-B algorithm, and a more detailed investigation could strengthen the claims.

The discussion of computational efficiency, while acknowledging the potential drawback of large filters, could be expanded. A comparison of the computational cost of the single-layer multi-scale model with the VGG-based model would be useful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of your results to the hyperparameters of the L-BFGS-B optimization algorithm? Were different settings explored, and how did they affect the generated textures?
2.  You mention that the VGG-based loss function sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages. Could you provide specific examples of textures where the VGG loss is misleading, and discuss potential alternative metrics that might address this limitation?
3.  The paper focuses primarily on natural textures. Do you anticipate that the findings generalize to other image generation tasks, such as style transfer or image inpainting? If not, what aspects of texture synthesis make it particularly amenable to this minimal approach?
4.  You state that textures synthesized from single-layer networks are less prone to local minima during optimization. Can you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it related to the simpler loss landscape, or other factors?
5.  In the discussion of the MaxEnt framework, you mention the possibility of extending it to allow for variability in the summary statistics. Could you provide a more concrete outline of how this might be achieved?
