## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper presents a compelling argument that surprisingly simple models – single-layer convolutional networks with random filters – can achieve texture synthesis quality competitive with more complex, deeply-trained VGG-based approaches. The authors convincingly demonstrate this through extensive experimentation, questioning the necessity of depth and pre-training for this specific task. The work is well-motivated, addressing a fundamental question regarding the core requirements for natural image generation, specifically in the context of texture synthesis. The novelty lies in successfully employing random filters and highlighting the importance of multi-scale analysis, even within a single-layer framework.

I recommend **Accept** this paper. The central claim – that strong texture synthesis doesn’t *require* deep hierarchical representations – is provocative and supported by robust empirical evidence. The authors provide a strong minimal baseline and thoughtfully compare it to state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, the introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure utilizing the VGG-based model’s synthesis loss is a valuable contribution. 

The paper’s strength lies in its insightful experimental results.  The comparison between single-scale and multi-scale random networks clearly demonstrates the benefit of capturing structures at different resolutions. While the generated samples aren’t flawless (as acknowledged and evident in Figure 3, and consistent with existing limitations of texture synthesis), they are demonstrably competitive, especially considering the simplicity of the model. The observation that VGG-based models perform better on inpainting (Figure 7) is also a valuable nuance. 

However, further validation of the core conclusion – regarding the necessity of deep representations – would be beneficial. It would be insightful to see if carefully designed *hierarchical* models (rather than simply multiple filter sizes in a single layer) could outperform the multi-scale random network more consistently.  Could a hierarchical structure more efficiently handle the multi-scale structure observed in natural textures?

Additionally, could the authors elaborate on the optimization procedure? What specific tuning was applied, and how might this contribute to the difference in results compared to earlier reports?  It would also be helpful to understand the limitations of the VGG-based loss function as a perceptual metric more deeply. Does it consistently underestimate the quality of diverse samples? Finally, investigating the relationship between the number of feature maps and computational cost would strengthen the practical implications of the proposed approach.
