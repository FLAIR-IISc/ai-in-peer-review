## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper investigates the core components necessary for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks, specifically challenging the assumption that depth and pre-training are indispensable. The authors demonstrate surprisingly strong performance using a single-layer convolutional network with *random* filters, often rivaling results from deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG-19, particularly for simpler, regular textures. A key finding is that optimizing images with respect to these shallow filter banks can even yield lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective, hinting at a more tractable optimization landscape. The work also provides a valuable discussion of the limitations of Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis, acknowledging the difficulties in truly measuring texture distribution beyond simply reproducing a sample.

I recommend **Accept** this paper for ICLR publication. The core finding – that surprisingly simple networks can achieve compelling texture synthesis results – is a significant and counterintuitive contribution. The analysis regarding the optimization landscape and the limitations of current evaluation methods are also valuable.

My decision rests primarily on the novelty of the results and the clear presentation of the findings. The authors convincingly demonstrate that depth isn’t a prerequisite for generating visually plausible textures, prompting a re-evaluation of what features are truly critical for this task. The exploration of the optimization landscape (Figure 4 and the supplementary material) is particularly insightful, offering a potential explanation for the success of shallow networks. Further, the paper doesn't shy away from acknowledging the challenges in objectively evaluating texture synthesis, a common issue in the field. 

However, as the authors themselves note, the lack of a systematic, quantifiable comparison across methods is a limitation. While visual examples are compelling, a more rigorous evaluation is needed to definitively claim that the shallow networks capture the *true* texture distribution. I also wonder about the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter tuning; a brief discussion of this would be helpful. 

Specifically, I’d like to understand:

*   What is the sensitivity of the results to different initialization schemes for the optimization process (beyond the reported initialization with shallow network results)?
*   Could the authors elaborate on the specific textures where the shallow networks demonstrably *fail* to capture the complexity of the VGG-19 results, and what characteristics differentiate those textures?
*   Given the acknowledged difficulty in measuring diversity, could the authors provide more detail on the selection of the example textures presented in the paper, ensuring a reasonably diverse set?
