## Review of “What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?”

This paper presents a surprisingly strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis, achieving competitive results with significantly simpler models than previously employed. The authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be generated using single-layer convolutional networks with random filters, challenging the assumption that depth and pre-training are indispensable for this task. This is a valuable and insightful contribution to the field of texture synthesis and generative modeling.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles an important question with a novel and effective approach, and the results are compelling. The core finding – that complex hierarchical representations may not be *necessary* for texture synthesis – is a significant observation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated. The authors clearly articulate the increasing reliance on deep, pre-trained networks for image generation tasks and question the necessity of their complexity. The work directly addresses the specific components (depth, pooling, pre-training) of these models, providing a clear and focused investigation. Their approach of systematically simplifying the standard VGG-based texture synthesis pipeline is a logical and effective strategy. 

The empirical results strongly support the claims. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison between their single-layer models (including variations in filter types and sizes) and the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach. The qualitative results (Figure 1 and 4) convincingly demonstrate that the single-layer, particularly the multi-scale, models can generate textures of comparable quality. The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss provides further evidence supporting this observation. The ablation study exploring the impact of filter scale (Figure 2) is particularly insightful, demonstrating the importance of multi-scale representations even within a single-layer framework. The authors appropriately acknowledge limitations, such as the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model. 

The paper is rigorous in its methodology. The authors detail their implementation choices, optimization procedure, and evaluation metrics. The use of L-BFGS-B for optimization and the scaling of the loss function are crucial details that explain the discrepancy with earlier findings (mentioned in the introduction) regarding random filter performance. The inclusion of a comparison to other texture synthesis methods (Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) places the results in context.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact:

* **Discussion of Optimization:** The optimization process seems critical to the success of the single-layer models. Expanding the discussion on *why* this optimization works better for shallower models would be valuable. Is it simply a matter of avoiding local minima, or are there other factors at play?
* **Inpainting Task Expansion:** The mention of an inpainting task in the discussion (Section 7) is interesting. Expanding on the results of this task (even informally) would provide stronger evidence for the claim regarding variability and the limitations of the VGG-based loss. The authors note VGG performs better on inpainting (Figure 7) and could elaborate on why.
* **Hierarchical vs. Multi-scale:** The authors briefly touch on the possibility of using a hierarchical network instead of a multi-scale single-layer network. Could they elaborate on *why* the hierarchical architectures they experimented with didn’t improve performance? Was it a matter of architecture choice, optimization, or something else?
* **Theoretical Connection to MaxEnt:** The discussion of the Maximum Entropy framework is intriguing, but somewhat dense. Connecting it more explicitly to the practical implications of their findings would be helpful. Specifically, how does the observed need for a "small mismatch" in constraints relate to the limitations of the MaxEnt framework for finite-size images?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. You mention that the simpler optimization procedure is less prone to local minima in shallower models. Could you provide more detail on the characteristics of the loss landscape for the single-layer models compared to the VGG-based model? Were there noticeable differences in the optimization trajectory or the types of local minima encountered?
2. You experimented with hierarchical architectures but found they didn’t outperform the multi-scale single-layer model. Could you describe the specific hierarchical architectures you tested and the reasons you believe they were less effective?
3. The inpainting task is mentioned as a potential method for assessing variability. Could you share any initial results from this experiment, even if they are not conclusive? Specifically, how did human observers perform when distinguishing between real and inpainted texture patches generated from different models?
4. In the discussion of the MaxEnt framework, you mention the need for a "small mismatch" in constraints. How does this observation influence your understanding of the optimal design of texture synthesis algorithms, and what are the implications for future research in this area?
