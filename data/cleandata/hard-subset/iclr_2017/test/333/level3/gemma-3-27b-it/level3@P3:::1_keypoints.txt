- Core claim: single-layer convolutional networks with random filters form a surprisingly strong baseline for texture synthesis, challenging the need for depth and pre-training.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Motivation: paper questions reliance on deep, pre-trained networks and isolates components (depth, pooling, pre-training).  
- Method: systematic simplification of the standard VGG-based texture synthesis pipeline.  
- Empirical strength: qualitative and quantitative comparisons show multi-scale single-layer models can match VGG-based results.  
- Ablation insight: filter scale and multi-scale representations within a single layer are crucial for quality.  
- Limitation noted: multi-scale model is computationally inefficient.  
- Methodological rigor: clear implementation details, optimization (L-BFGS-B), and loss scaling explain discrepancies with prior work.  
- Contextualization: comparison to classical methods (Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) included.  
- Feedback — optimization: ask for expanded discussion on why the optimization succeeds for shallower models and loss-landscape behavior.  
- Feedback — inpainting: request more results and explanation on inpainting experiments and VGG’s apparent advantage.  
- Feedback — hierarchical vs. multi-scale: ask for elaboration on why tested hierarchical architectures didn’t outperform the multi-scale single-layer model.  
- Feedback — theory: suggest clarifying the MaxEnt connection and practical implications of the required “small mismatch” in constraints.  
- Questions to authors: (1) characterize loss landscapes and optimization trajectories for single-layer vs VGG models; (2) list and analyze hierarchical architectures tested and failure modes; (3) share any inpainting experiment outcomes and human discrimination results; (4) explain how the “small mismatch” observation guides texture-synthesis design and future work.
