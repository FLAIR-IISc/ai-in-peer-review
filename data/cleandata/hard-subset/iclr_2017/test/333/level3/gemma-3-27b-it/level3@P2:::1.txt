This paper presents a surprisingly effective baseline for natural texture synthesis using single-layer convolutional neural networks with random filters. The authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be generated without relying on the depth or pre-training typically associated with state-of-the-art approaches like those based on VGG networks. This challenges the conventional wisdom in the field and offers a fresh perspective on what is truly essential for natural image generation.

**Decision: Accept**

The core contribution – demonstrating competitive texture synthesis with a remarkably simple model – is significant and well-supported by the presented results. The paper successfully casts doubt on the necessity of deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features for this specific task, opening avenues for more efficient and potentially interpretable texture generation methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-structured and clearly articulates its motivation and contributions. The authors effectively position their work within the existing literature on texture synthesis, highlighting the prevailing reliance on deep networks and questioning the necessity of this complexity. The extensive experimental evaluation, comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) against the VGG-based model, provides strong empirical evidence for their claims. The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss function is a valuable addition, providing a more objective measure of texture quality than solely relying on human inspection. The results consistently show that the multi-scale random filter model performs remarkably well, often rivaling the VGG-based approach, particularly for textures with regular structures. The ablation study (Fig. 2) clearly demonstrates the importance of combining filters at different scales, further strengthening the authors’ arguments. Finally, the insightful discussion section raises important considerations regarding the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and connects the work to the broader theoretical framework of maximum entropy models.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be addressed to further enhance its clarity and impact.

*   **Comparison to Non-Parametric Methods:** The paper mentions that generated samples are imperfect and “substantially worse than non-parametric methods.” Expanding on this point with a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art non-parametric methods (e.g., patch-based synthesis) would provide valuable context and further clarify the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.
*   **Inpainting Performance:** The observation that the VGG-based model performs better on inpainting (Figure 7) is intriguing. Elaborating on why this might be the case – perhaps due to the VGG model’s better representation of contextual information – would be beneficial.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** While the authors acknowledge the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model due to the large filter sizes, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost compared to the VGG-based model would be informative.
*   **Generalizability:** It would be interesting to explore the performance of the proposed method on a wider range of texture datasets and investigate its sensitivity to different texture characteristics.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** The discussion on the MaxEnt framework is thought-provoking. Could the authors provide a more concrete link between their single-layer model and this theoretical foundation? Specifically, how does the choice of the Gram matrix as a summary statistic relate to the principle of maximizing entropy subject to constraints?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that samples generated from both VGG and single-layer models are substantially worse than non-parametric methods. Could you provide a quantitative comparison to a representative non-parametric method (e.g., patch-based synthesis) on a benchmark dataset?
2.  Why do you believe the VGG-based model performs better on the inpainting task shown in Figure 7? Is this related to its ability to capture more long-range dependencies in the image?
3.  While you address the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (e.g., runtime, memory usage) compared to the VGG-based model for generating textures of a given size?
4.  You state that deeper hierarchical models don't improve texture quality. Given the known benefits of hierarchical representations in other vision tasks, could you speculate on why this might be the case for texture synthesis and what factors might limit the effectiveness of deeper models in this context? Specifically, does the Gram matrix effectively capture the relevant information, rendering deeper layers redundant?
5.   Could you elaborate on how the Gram matrix used in your approach relates to the constraints in the MaxEnt framework, and how a small mismatch is allowed in the constraints for finite image sizes?
