## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper presents a compelling investigation into the requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchically trained networks are essential. The authors propose a surprisingly strong baseline using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, demonstrating that it can often rival the performance of more complex VGG-based models. This is a significant finding, suggesting that the depth and pre-training commonly employed in texture synthesis may not be as crucial as previously believed. The work is well-motivated, building upon the established literature in texture synthesis and deep learning-based image generation. The experimental setup appears thorough, with comparisons to various single-layer architectures and the standard VGG-based approach.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper. The core contribution – demonstrating competitive texture synthesis with a remarkably simple model – is both insightful and potentially impactful. The results genuinely question established practices and open up new avenues for research in this area. Furthermore, the introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative evaluation metric is a valuable addition.

The paper’s strength lies in its counter-intuitive findings. However, further validation of the conclusions regarding the necessity of deep hierarchical representations would strengthen the work. While the authors show a single layer *can* work well, it doesn’t definitively prove that deeper networks offer no advantage. The observation that VGG performs better on inpainting (Figure 7) hints at potential benefits of deeper features for tasks requiring more complex understanding of image structure. 

Additional feedback: The discussion section touches on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, but this could be expanded. It would be beneficial to see a more detailed analysis of *why* the single-layer model performs well, perhaps exploring the role of the multi-scale approach in capturing different frequency components of the texture. Finally, could the authors comment on the computational cost of training and synthesizing textures with the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based approach?

Specifically, I’d like to understand: 
1. Were ablation studies performed to determine the optimal number of feature maps for the single-layer models? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
2. The paper mentions the optimization procedure being more elaborate. Could the authors elaborate on the specific tuning parameters that led to the improved results with random filters, compared to previous work?
3. How sensitive are the results to the choice of initialization for the random filters? Were different initialization schemes explored?
