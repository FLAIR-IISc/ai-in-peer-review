## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper tackles the fundamental question of what components are truly necessary for generating natural-looking textures using deep learning approaches. Specifically, the authors investigate whether the depth of networks, pooling layers, or pre-training on natural images are indispensable for successful texture synthesis. The core contribution lies in demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated even from single-layer networks with random filters, challenging previously held assumptions in the field. This is a surprising and potentially impactful finding.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The results are genuinely surprising and counterintuitive, and the analysis presented is remarkably thorough. The authors systematically explore a wide range of single-layer architectures and convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of a surprisingly simple baseline. 

The strength of this work lies in its rigorous experimental evaluation. The authors not only present visual comparisons but also introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model, offering a more objective assessment than purely subjective human evaluation. The ablation studies, comparing different filter types and network configurations, are well-executed and provide valuable insights. The discussion of the limitations of current evaluation metrics and the connection to maximum entropy models further strengthens the paper. 

However, the evaluation methods could be improved. While the VGG-based loss is a step towards objectivity, it doesn't fully capture perceptual quality, as acknowledged by the authors. It would be beneficial to include a small-scale user study to validate the perceptual quality of the generated textures, even if only for a subset of the results. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the optimization procedure used for texture synthesis. What specific tuning was done, and how might this contribute to the observed results? 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors: 

*   Could you elaborate on the specific parameters of the L-BFGS-B optimization algorithm used? Were any adjustments made beyond the default settings and the scaling factor mentioned in the paper?
*   The paper mentions that the multi-scale model often rivals the state-of-the-art. Could you provide a more quantitative comparison, perhaps using metrics beyond the VGG-based loss, to support this claim?
*   You discuss the limitations of the VGG-based loss. Are there any other quantitative metrics you considered but ultimately rejected, and if so, why?
