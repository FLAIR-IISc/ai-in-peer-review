## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper investigates the core components necessary for effective texture synthesis using deep learning, specifically challenging the assumptions that depth and pre-training are indispensable. The authors demonstrate surprisingly strong results using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, often matching or exceeding the performance of deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG-19 on simpler textures. They provide a thorough analysis of different single-layer architectures and offer insightful discussion on the limitations of Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis, including the potential for trivial reproduction of reference textures and the difficulty of quantifying diversity. The finding that optimizing with shallow filter banks can lead to lower VGG-19 loss is particularly interesting, and the supplementary material showing improved VGG-19 optimization when initialized with shallow network results is a valuable contribution.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for publication at ICLR. The work presents a counterintuitive yet compelling result – that surprisingly simple networks can achieve high-quality texture synthesis – and offers a valuable re-evaluation of the necessity of depth and pre-training in this domain. The paper is well-written and clearly articulates its contributions and limitations.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the question of what truly drives natural texture generation. The approach is well-motivated, building directly on the established work of Gatys et al. and offering a necessary exploration of the underlying principles. The empirical results are convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed method and providing a strong baseline for future research. The discussion of the limitations, particularly regarding the difficulty of objectively measuring texture diversity and the potential for trivial reproduction, is honest and insightful. The connection to the MaxEnt framework and the discussion of perceptual distance metrics further strengthen the theoretical grounding of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper acknowledges the lack of a systematic, quantifiable comparison, a more rigorous evaluation of diversity would be beneficial. The authors suggest psychophysical evaluation as a future direction, and I strongly concur. Beyond that, it would be helpful to understand *why* random filters work so well. Is it simply a matter of sufficient capacity (number of filters), or is there something inherent in the random initialization that facilitates texture capture? 

Specifically, I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the optimization process? What specific hyperparameters (learning rate, regularization, etc.) were used, and how sensitive were the results to these settings?
*   You mention the potential for trivial reproduction. Could you provide examples of textures where this occurs, and discuss how this might be mitigated?
*   Regarding the VGG-loss, could you provide a more detailed analysis of *which* textures exhibit the lower VGG-loss when synthesized with the shallow network, and what characteristics those textures share?
