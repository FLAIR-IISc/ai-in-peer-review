- Paper asks which properties of feature extractors are necessary for Gatys-style Gram-matrix texture synthesis.
- Main finding: depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not indispensable—single-layer random convs with ReLU can produce high-quality textures.
- Multi-scale aggregation of shallow filters is crucial and often rivals VGG-19 results.
- Systematic exploration: tested random, Fourier, PCA, k-means (ImageNet and target), and multi-scale variants.
- Identified key factors: sufficient channel count, ReLU nonlinearities, and multi-scale filters.
- Argument: Gram-matrix statistics themselves are the primary driver of synthesis quality.
- Empirical insight: shallow-filter optimization can achieve lower VGG-19 Gram loss than direct VGG optimization (Figure 4).
- Initializing VGG optimization with shallow-network outputs improves VGG synthesis, implicating optimization hardness not just representation.
- Authors candidly discuss limitations of VGG loss as an evaluation metric and the need for metrics beyond VGG loss.
- Noted trade-offs: perceptual similarity vs. variability, finite-size effects, and optimization challenges.
- Reviewer decision: Accept—motivated by a strong, surprising empirical result and a useful baseline for the community.
- Paper is well-motivated and well-placed in literature (builds on Gatys et al., Heeger & Bergen, Portilla & Simoncelli, Zhu et al.).
- Results largely support claims via qualitative comparisons, ablations, and VGG Gram-loss proxies.
- Strength: reframes prior assumptions that depth/training are necessary for Gram-based texture synthesis.
- Strength: extensive empirical evaluation clarifying roles of filter bank design and channel count.
- Strength: provides practical optimization insights showing landscape differences between shallow and deep models.
- Limitation: no rigorous, systematic assessment of sample diversity or perceptual quality beyond VGG loss and visuals.
- Limitation: proposed entropy-based diversity measure deemed impractical and psychophysics deferred.
- Limitation: single-layer multi-scale approach can be computationally slower than hierarchical/deeper alternatives.
- Suggested improvement: quantify diversity via multiple samples per texture, dispersion in VGG Gram and pixel spaces, and patch-level nearest-neighbor checks.
- Suggested improvement: run small psychophysics pilots (e.g., 2AFC) to bolster perceptual claims.
- Suggested improvement: ablate optimization settings (L-BFGS-B, iterations, scaling, box constraints), test other optimizers, and report multistart variance.
- Suggested improvement: provide runtime/memory comparisons and note engineering tricks (FFT convs) for large filters.
- Suggested improvement: clarify which texture categories favor shallow models vs. VGG and quantify across a categorized texture set.
- Suggested improvement: show analyses to mitigate trivial reproduction (cross-correlation peaks, tiling/artifact checks).
- Reviewer questions: report total number of textures tested and aggregate VGG-loss statistics (median/quantiles).
- Reviewer questions: sensitivity to number of feature maps and performance–channels saturation curves.
- Reviewer questions: quantify VGG optimization difficulty (curvature, gradient norms, loss/iteration traces) and effect of shallow initialization.
- Reviewer questions: do shallow models ever exactly reconstruct the reference and under what conditions?
- Reviewer questions: impact of color handling and whether grayscale textures behave differently; request code and seeds; ask if other proxy metrics were tried (e.g., spectrum constraints).
