- Proposes a minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis: single-layer CNN with multi-scale random filters optimized to match Gram statistics.
- Empirically, the multi-scale random baseline often rivals the popular VGG-based multi-layer pre-trained model on diverse textures.
- Main insight: multi-scale coverage and nonlinearity (ReLU) matter more than depth or supervised pretraining for perceptually plausible texture generation.
- Paper is well-executed with extensive variants (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA; single vs. multi-scale; role of ReLU) and careful ablations.
- Decision: Accept (weak) because the baseline is compelling and will recalibrate assumptions in the field.
- Caveat: Evaluation leans heavily on a VGG-based loss and visual inspection without a human perceptual study to validate “rivaling” claims.
- Caveat: Both the VGG and single-layer methods still lag high-quality non-parametric patch-based synthesis in many settings.
- Suggestion: Add human perceptual evaluations (e.g., 2AFC, ABX) to quantify perceptual equivalence and variability.
- Suggestion: Include comparisons to modern non-parametric/patch-based baselines to calibrate absolute quality.
- Suggestion: Report computational cost vs. quality (FLOPs, memory, wall-clock) comparing single-layer multi-scale and hierarchical/dilated alternatives.
- Concern: Superiority on some textures might be due to easier optimization (fewer local minima) rather than superior representation—need controls with matched stopping criteria.
- Suggestion: Use additional metrics beyond VGG-loss (retrieval-based, LPIPS, psychophysics) for evaluation.
- Questions for authors: Can you run a user study and quantify results across random seeds, and test man-made vs. organic texture sets?
- Questions for authors: How do results change when controlling compute (same FLOPs/params) between single-layer multi-scale and shallow hierarchical/dilated models, and why does VGG appear stronger for inpainting/global consistency?
- Question for authors: How sensitive is performance to the number of feature maps versus the range of scales?
