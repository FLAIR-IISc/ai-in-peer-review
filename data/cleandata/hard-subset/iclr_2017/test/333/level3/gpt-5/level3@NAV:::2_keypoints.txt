- Paper asks which ingredients are truly necessary for natural-looking texture synthesis and shows depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not required.  
- Main finding: a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU, and multi-scale receptive fields can produce high-quality textures.  
- Thorough empirical analysis: ablations over filter count, scale, filter type (random/Fourier/k-means/PCA), and nonlinearity.  
- The work is framed within Gram-matrix–based texture modeling and the MaxEnt perspective, offering a strong minimal baseline.  
- Careful optimization protocol (L-BFGS-B, scaling) and discussion of the similarity-versus-variability trade-off.  
- Decision: Accept — results are surprising, impactful, and overturn the need for deep pretrained features in texture synthesis.  
- Key causal factors identified: multi-scale filters + ReLU + sufficient channel count; empirical evidence is systematic and convincing.  
- Well grounded in prior literature and practically useful as a minimal model that rivals VGG-based synthesis.  
- Evidence includes extensive qualitative comparisons and VGG-loss as a proxy metric; scale ablation and ReLU importance are particularly compelling.  
- Suggested evaluation improvements: add psychophysical tests (2AFC, inpainting plausibility), report inter-sample variability, and quantify diversity (pairwise VGG/LPIPS, MMD); consider standard benchmarks (DTD, Brodatz).  
- Request reporting of compute: wall-clock time, memory, scaling with filter size and channels, and exploration of hierarchical variants for efficiency.  
- Ask for sensitivity analyses over optimization hyperparameters, initialization, and random seeds with confidence intervals.  
- Reproducibility request: release code and pretrained configurations and document exact Gram normalization choices.  
- Specific questions: sensitivity to Gram construction (centering, normalization, weighting); performance distribution across seeds and channel counts; diminishing returns past ~1k channels; quantify similarity–variability trade-off; effects of alternative nonlinearities/normalization; compute/memory comparison vs VGG.  
- Overall assessment: aligns with other reviewers — a promising direction that clarifies what matters for texture generation.
