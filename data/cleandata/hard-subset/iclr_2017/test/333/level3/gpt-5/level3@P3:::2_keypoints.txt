- Paper asks which CNN representation aspects are necessary for natural-looking textures and shows a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU, and Gram statistics can produce high-quality textures; a random multi-scale variant often rivals VGG-based methods.

- Presents a strong minimal baseline using single-layer filter banks (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and a multi-scale random model.

- Provides careful ablations across number of feature maps, filter sizes, and single- vs multi-scale designs, highlighting multi-scale receptive fields and nonlinearity as critical.

- Analyzes optimization choices (L-BFGS-B, rescaling) and resolves prior negative results with random weights.

- Introduces a quantitative evaluation proxy using VGG-based Gram losses to complement qualitative inspection.

- Discusses connections to maximum-entropy texture models and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

- Decision: Accept.

- Primary reason: surprising and significant finding that depth and supervised training are not essential for high-quality texture synthesis.

- Primary reason: thorough, careful analyses isolate what matters (multi-scale + nonlinearity) and what does not (depth, supervised pretraining).

- Problem framing and motivation are strong, situating the work within prominent CNN-style and texture synthesis literature.

- Approach is principled, exploring filter types, map counts, scales, and nonlinearities; multi-scale random model captures a range of structures without learned hierarchies.

- Claims are supported by strong visual results and VGG-loss evaluations, with plausible explanations about optimization robustness and soft vs hard constraints.

- Experimental protocol is transparent with clear ablations; discussion addresses computational efficiency and the role of hierarchical nets for speed.

- Broader impact: establishes a valuable minimal baseline and reframes assumptions about what deep features provide in texture synthesis.

- Evaluation needs strengthening: add human psychophysics (e.g., 2AFC) and explicit sample diversity metrics across multiple synthesized samples.

- Clarify multi-scale vs depth: quantify contributions of larger receptive fields vs scale combination and test dilated convolutions as an efficient alternative.

- Optimization confounds: provide ablations on L-BFGS settings (scaling, iterations, box constraints) to demonstrate robustness.

- Computational analysis: add runtime/memory benchmarks for single-scale, multi-scale, and shallow hierarchical approaches and discuss practical deployment optimizations.

- Reproducibility: release code, filter seeds, texture set, and detailed preprocessing and hyperparameters.

- Question 1: Report variability across multiple synthesized samples and effects of early stopping/noise initialization on the similarity-variability trade-off.

- Question 2: Test independence of conclusions using alternative perceptual distances (scattering, LPIPS, human 2AFC) and check model rankings.

- Question 3: Do results generalize to non-stationary natural images or style transfer—does the random multi-scale+Gram recipe remain competitive?

- Question 4: Can dilated convolutions or steerable filter banks replicate multi-scale benefits more efficiently?

- Question 5: Have other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, modulus, squaring) been tested and how do they affect quality and variability?

- Question 6: How sensitive are outcomes to the 1e7 loss scaling and iteration count, and can first-order optimizers (Adam) match results?

- Question 7: What texture classes consistently fail the random multi-scale model, and do failures indicate missing higher-order statistics?

- Question 8: How does color handling and cross-channel statistics affect results, and would color decorrelation (e.g., YUV) improve synthesis?
