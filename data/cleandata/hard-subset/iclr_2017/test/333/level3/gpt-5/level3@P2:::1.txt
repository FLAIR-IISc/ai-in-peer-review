1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper asks what aspects of CNN representations are truly necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It proposes a minimal yet surprisingly strong baseline: a single-layer convolutional model with ReLU nonlinearity, no pooling, and random filters, extended to a multi-scale variant that mixes multiple filter sizes. Textures are synthesized by matching Gram matrices of feature activations, as in Gatys et al., but without deep, pretrained features. The authors report:
- High perceptual quality from single-layer, random-filter models, with multi-scale variants often rivaling VGG-based results.
- Depth, pooling, and pretraining are not indispensable; instead, nonlinearity and multi-scale coverage are crucial.
- A broad empirical comparison across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA, with/without whitening) and scales.
- A quantitative evaluation using a VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual distance, supplemented by qualitative comparisons.
- A useful discussion linking practical synthesis to maximum-entropy modeling and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

The work is clearly written, easy to reproduce, and provides valuable insight into what makes Gram-based texture models work.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong and well-executed insight: a simple random, single-layer multi-scale model challenges the perceived necessity of depth and supervised training for this task.
- Broad and careful empirical ablation demonstrating what matters (multi-scale coverage and nonlinearity) and what often does not (learned filters), which is valuable to the community.

3. Supporting arguments
- What is the problem? Identify the minimal ingredients needed for natural texture synthesis, and assess whether depth, pooling, and pretraining are necessary.
- Is the approach well-motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It builds directly on Gatys et al.’s Gram-based model, situates against classic parametric models (Portilla & Simoncelli; Heeger–Bergen), scattering, and modern deep methods (VGG-based textures, texture networks), and contrasts with GAN/PixelCNN lines. The “minimal baseline” lens is timely and well argued.
- Do results support the claims?
  Strengths:
  - Convincing qualitative results across many textures for single-layer and multi-scale random filters; multi-scale captures longer-range structure; ReLU is shown to be necessary.
  - Quantitative VGG-based loss comparisons broadly track perceived quality and occasionally show the random multi-scale model outperforming a VGG-optimized synthesis, highlighting optimization issues and the similarity/variability trade-off.
  - The ablation across filter banks (random vs learned) is thorough and somewhat surprising, supporting the central claim.
  Limitations:
  - The main quantitative metric is the VGG-based loss, which is an imperfect proxy and potentially biased toward VGG-like statistics; human studies are absent. The paper notes this partially and offers thoughtful discussion, but the strong conclusions about depth/pretraining would benefit from psychophysical validation.
  - The discussion acknowledges computational inefficiency with large filters; while the authors report faster hierarchical variants without quality gains, the details are brief and the efficiency–quality trade-off is not rigorously quantified.
  - The samples from both the proposed and VGG models are still imperfect on some textures, and non-parametric patch-based methods can produce more faithful inpainting and texture extension in some cases. Figure 7 suggests VGG-based features may perform better on inpainting.

Overall, despite limitations in evaluation design and breadth of baselines, the evidence is sufficient to substantiate the main insight: random, shallow, multi-scale Gram models can be very strong parametric texture synthesizers.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help, not required for acceptance)
- Perceptual evaluation: Add a small-scale human study (e.g., 2AFC or Turing test on discrimination between real vs synthesized patches) to support claims about perceptual quality, especially on the most regular textures where VGG loss seems misaligned with human judgments.
- Variability: Quantify sample diversity vs similarity. Report distributions of VGG-loss distances among multiple samples per texture, and cross-sample distances (and possibly SSIM as a reference), to characterize mode collapse vs diversity. Consider early stopping or explicit noise to balance the trade-off.
- Efficiency and hierarchy: Provide concrete runtime/MACs/memory measurements across single-layer multi-scale vs hierarchical models achieving similar receptive fields; describe the hierarchical architectures tested and show matched-quality comparisons. This will clarify whether a hierarchical design can deliver similar quality more efficiently.
- Baselines: Include non-parametric patch-based baselines (e.g., image quilting) for a few textures to contextualize perceptual fidelity. Also consider scattering-based synthesis as a parametric comparator.
- Clarify claims: Temper “none of the above aspects are indispensable” by emphasizing the scope (parametric texture synthesis with Gram-based statistics). Depth and training may still matter for broader image generation or for efficiency/regularization in parametric texture synthesis.
- ReLU and scale ablations: Provide more systematic plots showing quality (VGG-loss and exemplar visuals) vs filter size, number of feature maps, and with/without nonlinearity.
- Inpainting: Since Figure 7 suggests VGG features may be better for inpainting, consider reporting a small controlled inpainting benchmark (e.g., user study or boundary consistency metrics) to illustrate where each approach excels.

Questions for the authors
- Human study: Can you provide a small psychophysical evaluation to validate that perceived quality improvements of the multi-scale random model over single-scale, and parity with VGG, hold for human observers?
- Diversity: How variable are the synthesized samples per texture? Can you report intra-model, intra-texture distance distributions (e.g., VGG-based loss among synthesized samples, and to the reference), and show how early stopping or added noise affects variability?
- Metric robustness: How sensitive are your conclusions to the perceptual metric? Do results hold under alternative deep-feature metrics (e.g., different VGG layers, average vs max pooling, or scattering distances)?
- Hierarchical efficiency: What exact multi-layer architectures did you test as efficient surrogates for large filters, and how do their FLOPs/runtime and quality compare to the single-layer multi-scale model?
- Filter count vs type: When controlling for the number of feature maps, does any learned filter bank (k-means/PCA) consistently outperform random under any setting, or are differences within noise?
- Scope of claims: Do you observe any texture classes (e.g., stochastic vs highly regular man-made) where depth or training clearly helps, beyond optimization stability? Could you add failure cases and analysis?
- Inpainting: Figure 7 suggests VGG-based features yield better inpainting. Can you quantify this and discuss why the random multi-scale features fall short in that setting?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, model definitions, and seeds to facilitate replication of all ablations and figures?
