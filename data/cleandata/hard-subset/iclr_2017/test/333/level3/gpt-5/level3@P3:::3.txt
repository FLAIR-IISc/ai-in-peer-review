1. Summary of contributions
The paper asks a clear, focused question: within the successful Gram-matrix texture-synthesis framework of Gatys et al., what properties of the feature extractor are actually necessary? The authors show that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining is indispensable. Using a single convolutional layer with ReLU and random filters—and, importantly, a multi-scale version aggregating filters at several receptive fields—they produce textures of high perceptual quality that often rival those obtained with VGG-19. They systematically explore various shallow filter banks (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means on ImageNet or on the target texture, and multi-scale variants), identify the importance of sufficient channel count, ReLUs, and multi-scale filters, and argue that Gram-matrix statistics themselves are the key driver. An informative discussion articulates the strengths/limitations of Gram-based methods, including the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, optimization challenges in VGG-19, finite-size effects, and the need for metrics beyond VGG loss. Figure 4 highlights a striking result: optimizing an image w.r.t. shallow filter banks can yield a lower VGG-19 Gram loss than directly optimizing the VGG objective, likely due to the harder optimization landscape in VGG. The supplementary shows that initializing VGG optimization with shallow-network outputs improves VGG synthesis, reinforcing that optimization—not just representation—matters. The paper calls for psychophysical evaluation to assess both quality and diversity, noting entropy-based diversity measures are impractical and do not reflect visual quality.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong, surprising empirical finding: a single-layer, random, multi-scale filter bank with Gram statistics can match or surpass VGG-19 on many textures, overturning a widely held belief about the necessity of depth/pretraining.
- Clear, well-motivated analysis of what Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis is “using,” with practical insights into optimization vs. representation and a simple, strong baseline that the community should adopt.

3. Supporting arguments
- Q1: What problem is tackled? Determining which aspects of deep representations are crucial for Gram-matrix texture synthesis and whether shallow, even random, features suffice.
- Q2: Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It builds directly on Gatys et al. and classic texture modeling (Heeger & Bergen; Portilla & Simoncelli), and connects to MaxEnt/ergodic random field views (Zhu et al.). The motivation—decompose contributions of depth, pooling, and training—is timely and important for understanding “why Gram matrices work.”
- Q3: Do the results support the claims rigorously? Largely yes for the main claim. The authors present broad qualitative comparisons across many textures and architectures, ablations (filter size, number of features, multi-scale, ReLU), and a quantitative proxy metric (VGG Gram loss). The Figure 4 phenomenon (shallow optimization achieving lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization) is compelling and coherently explained via optimization hardness; the supplementary result that shallow initializations improve VGG synthesis strengthens this. The limitations are candidly discussed: VGG loss is an imperfect metric; diversity is not rigorously quantified; and the evaluation does not settle whether models capture the true texture distribution vs. overfitting a single exemplar.

Strengths
- Conceptual: Demonstrates that Gram statistics, not depth/training, are the core ingredient in this framework; reframes prior assumptions.
- Empirical: Extensive exploration of shallow filter banks (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means), clear role of multi-scale aggregation and ReLUs, and evidence that more channels help.
- Optimization insight: VGG’s landscape is harder; a good initialization matters.
- Honest, informative discussion of variability, finite-size effects, and MaxEnt perspectives.

Limitations
- No systematic, quantifiable assessment of diversity and perceptual quality beyond VGG loss and visual inspection; difficult to conclude whether models learn the texture distribution rather than reproducing a single example.
- The proposed entropy-based diversity notion is acknowledged as impractical; psychophysical tests are deferred.
- Computational efficiency: single-layer multi-scale with large filters is slower than hierarchical alternatives (though authors note depth improves efficiency, not quality).

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Quantify diversity: Report intra-model variability by synthesizing multiple samples per texture and measuring dispersion in VGG Gram space and pixel space; complement with patch-level nearest neighbor analyses against the reference to detect trivial copying.
- Psychophysics: Even a small two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) or inpainting-detection pilot on a subset of textures would significantly bolster claims about perceptual quality and variability.
- Optimization analysis: Provide ablations on L-BFGS-B settings (iterations, scaling factor 1e7, box constraints), alternative optimizers (Adam), and multistart variance. Quantify how often shallow initializations improve VGG synthesis and by how much.
- Efficiency: Include runtime/memory comparisons for single-layer multi-scale vs. shallow hierarchical stacks with equivalent receptive fields, and an engineering note on implementing large filters efficiently (FFT convs).
- Scope: Clarify texture categories where shallow models excel (regular, man-made patterns) vs. where VGG remains superior (stochastic, high-order structures), and quantify across a larger, categorized texture set.
- Mitigate trivial reproduction: Demonstrate that generated samples are not patchwise copies by showing low cross-correlation peaks with the reference; report boundary conditions and any tiling artifacts.

Questions for the authors
- How many textures were tested in total for the comparisons in Figures 1 and 4? Can you provide aggregate statistics (median/quantiles of VGG loss) across the full set?
- How sensitive are results to the number of feature maps? Is there a clear performance–channels saturation curve for single-scale and multi-scale variants?
- Could you quantify the optimization difficulty in VGG (e.g., loss landscape curvature, gradient norms, loss vs. iteration traces) and show how shallow-initialization shifts these?
- Do shallow models ever exactly reconstruct the reference (finite-size effect)? How often and under what conditions (filter count, scales)?
- How does color handling (channelwise mean removal; filter color mixing) impact results? Are grayscale textures a special case?
- Can you release code and seeds to facilitate adoption of the shallow multi-scale baseline?
- Beyond VGG loss, did you explore any other proxy metrics (e.g., spectrum constraints as in Liu et al.) to triangulate quality?

Overall: A clear, insightful paper with strong empirical findings and a valuable baseline. The lack of psychophysical evaluation and systematic diversity metrics is a weakness but does not, in my view, outweigh the contribution. I recommend acceptance; a follow-up with psychophysics would significantly strengthen the claims.
