1. Summary
This paper asks a clear, specific question: which ingredients are truly necessary to generate natural-looking textures? Challenging prevailing assumptions, the authors show that depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not indispensable; instead, a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and multi-scale receptive fields can synthesize textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality. The analysis is thorough: the paper ablates filter count, scale, filter type (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), and nonlinearity, and situates findings within both Gram-matrix–based texture modeling and the MaxEnt perspective. The work offers a strong minimal baseline, a careful optimization protocol, and a thoughtful discussion of similarity-versus-variability trade-offs.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) The results are surprising and impactful, overturning a common belief that deep, pretrained features are required for high-quality texture synthesis. (ii) The empirical analysis is systematic and convincing, establishing clear causal factors (multi-scale + ReLU + sufficient channel count).

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well grounded in prior literature (Gatys et al., Portilla–Simoncelli, scattering, GANs/PixelCNN), and the question is timely and fundamental. A minimal model that rivals VGG-based synthesis is both scientifically interesting and practically useful.
- Evidence: Extensive qualitative comparisons across many textures and architectures, along with VGG-loss as a proxy metric, support the claims. The ablation isolating scale (3×3, 11×11, 55×55) versus multi-scale is particularly compelling, as is the demonstration that ReLU is essential.
- Rigor: The paper is careful about optimization details (L-BFGS-B, scaling) and offers a principled discussion of why Gram statistics over large random banks can suffice.

4. Feedback to improve (helpful, not decisive)
- Evaluation needs strengthening. VGG-loss is a reasonable proxy, but it can bias results and miss human percepts; please add psychophysical tests (e.g., 2AFC discrimination, inpainting plausibility), report inter-sample variability, and quantify diversity (e.g., pairwise VGG/LPIPS between samples, MMD). Consider a standardized texture benchmark (e.g., DTD, Brodatz).
- Report compute: wall-clock time, memory use, and scaling with filter size and feature-map count; include hierarchical variants for efficiency trade-offs.
- Sensitivity analyses: optimization hyperparameters, initialization, and random seeds; provide confidence intervals over multiple seeds.
- Reproducibility: release code and pretrained configurations; document exact Gram normalization choices.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to the precise Gram construction (centering, per-channel normalization, layer-wise weighting)?
- What is the distribution of performance across random seeds and feature counts? Are there diminishing returns past ~1k channels?
- Can you quantify the similarity–variability trade-off (e.g., early stopping curves, sample diversity metrics)?
- Do alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, GELU) or normalization (instance/batch) alter the conclusions?
- Can you provide quantitative compute/memory comparisons versus VGG-based synthesis?
My assessment aligns with other reviewers: this is a promising direction that sharpens our understanding of what truly matters in texture generation.
