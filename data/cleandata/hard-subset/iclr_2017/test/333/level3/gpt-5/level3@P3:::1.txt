Summary of the paper’s claims and contributions
- The paper proposes a minimal yet strong parametric texture-synthesis baseline: a single-layer convolutional model with ReLU and multi-scale random filters, using Gram-matrix statistics of feature maps, optimized with L-BFGS-B.
- It systematically contrasts random filters against unsupervisedly learned, Fourier, and PCA filters, and single-scale versus multi-scale variants, showing that multi-scale random filters often match or rival VGG-based multi-layer, pre-trained models in perceptual quality.
- It argues that neither deep hierarchical representations, pooling, nor supervised pretraining are indispensable for texture synthesis; multi-scale coverage and nonlinearity are more critical.
- It introduces a VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual evaluation and discusses maximum-entropy perspectives and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.
- It highlights practical aspects (e.g., ReLU is essential; optimization with single-layer models is easier and can help initialize VGG optimization).

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
- Reason 1: A simple, well-executed baseline that challenges a dominant narrative (necessity of depth/pretraining) with compelling evidence.
- Reason 2: Breadth of comparisons and clear ablations (scales, number of maps, nonlinearity) provide actionable insight and a reference point the community can build on.

Supporting arguments
Answers to the three key questions
1) Problem tackled: What aspects of deep CNN representations are necessary for parametric texture synthesis? Specifically, are depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining required, or are simpler representations sufficient?
2) Motivation and placement: The work is well motivated by the success of Gatys et al. and the longstanding literature on parametric texture models (Heeger–Bergen, Portilla–Simoncelli) and scattering. It is timely and well positioned as a “minimalist test” of the deep-feature hypothesis.
3) Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. Empirically, multi-scale random single-layer models often rival VGG-based synthesis qualitatively and under a VGG-derived loss. Ablations convincingly show the role of multi-scale filters and ReLU. However, the strength of the “depth not needed” conclusion is tempered by evaluation limitations: heavy reliance on a VGG-based metric, limited psychophysics, and no systematic comparison to strong non-parametric patch-based methods.

Strengths
- Conceptual clarity: isolates what matters (multi-scale coverage + nonlinear feature maps) and what does not (depth/pretraining), at least for this task.
- Methodological thoroughness: many filter constructions, controlled changes in number of maps, clear demonstrations of the importance of multi-scale.
- Practical insight: single-layer optimization is easier; single-layer outputs can warm-start VGG optimization; multi-scale random features are a robust, competitive baseline.
- Thoughtful discussion of variability vs similarity and the MaxEnt framing.

Limitations and concerns impacting the conclusions
- Evaluation: The main quantitative metric is VGG-based loss (itself a deep hierarchical representation), which risks circularity and can under- or over-estimate perceptual quality for certain textures (as the paper notes). Human studies are absent.
- Comparison set: While Portilla–Simoncelli is included qualitatively, the paper does not systematically compare against modern non-parametric patch-based methods (e.g., patchmatch / graphcut textures). In many cases, samples from both the VGG model and the single-layer models appear imperfect and substantially worse than strong non-parametric approaches, especially for complex global regularities and man-made textures.
- Inpainting evidence: Appendix Figure 7 suggests the VGG-based model performs better on inpainting, a perceptually meaningful proxy task. This weakens the generality of the “depth not needed” claim and calls for more systematic quantification.
- Efficiency: The proposed multi-scale random model relies on very large kernels (up to 55×55), which is computationally inefficient. The authors note hierarchical models are more efficient but claim no quality gains; details and parameter-matched comparisons are sparse.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Add a small psychophysical study (2AFC or Turing-test style) for a representative set of textures. Report effect sizes and inter-subject agreement.
  - Quantify variability explicitly (e.g., distribution of VGG-loss distances among multiple samples; nearest-neighbor analysis to the exemplar; diversity–fidelity curves via early stopping).
  - Include a modern non-parametric baseline with tuned parameters and report both qualitative and quantitative comparisons.
  - Report per-texture metrics and confidence intervals over multiple random seeds; analyze sensitivity to number of feature maps and filter-size distribution (scaling curves).
- Clarify the inpainting findings:
  - Provide a controlled, quantitative inpainting benchmark showing that VGG-based models outperform the single-layer variants. If so, qualify the main claim accordingly (e.g., “depth not necessary for many textures in unconditional synthesis, but beneficial for conditional tasks like inpainting”).
- Address efficiency and multi-scale modeling:
  - Provide parameter- and compute-matched comparisons between large-kernel single-layer multi-scale and hierarchical (dilated/strided) models. If quality is comparable, emphasize the efficiency advantages of hierarchy.
  - Explore dilated convolutions to cover large receptive fields without huge kernels.
- Reduce metric circularity:
  - Complement VGG-loss with alternative feature spaces (e.g., scattering transform, steerable pyramids) as evaluation metrics.
  - When possible, include human ratings to arbitrate disagreements between metrics.
- Reproducibility and ablations:
  - Report sensitivity to optimization details (e.g., the 1e7 scaling of gradients, iteration budget, initialization distributions).
  - Detail the exact multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, counts per scale) and release code and seeds.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide quantitative results on the inpainting task (Appendix Fig. 7) across multiple textures, comparing single-layer multi-scale vs VGG, including user studies or classifier-based discrimination?
2) How sensitive are your conclusions to the number of feature maps? Is there a clear scaling law where single-layer random models only catch up to VGG beyond a certain capacity?
3) Could you include parameter- and compute-matched comparisons between the multi-scale large-kernel model and an explicitly hierarchical model (possibly with dilations) to test whether hierarchy can achieve similar quality more efficiently?
4) How robust are results to the choice of random filter distribution and to the presence/absence of per-channel mean subtraction? Any failure cases where training or structured filters (e.g., scattering) clearly outperform random ones?
5) Can you add a controlled comparison to strong non-parametric methods and report where parametric models still fall short?
6) How many random seeds were used for each texture, and what is the variability (mean ± std) of VGG-loss and human preference across seeds?
