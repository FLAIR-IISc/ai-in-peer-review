- Core claim: Depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not required—single-layer random-convolutional networks with Gram-matrix statistics (especially multi-scale) can synthesize high-quality textures.
- Experimental breadth: Systematic evaluation of filter types (random, Fourier, k‑means/PCA), channel counts, filter sizes (11×11 to 55×55), and multi-scale vs single-scale variants.
- Key ablations: Shows ReLU nonlinearity and multi-scale coverage are crucial, while training and depth are not.
- Theoretical framing: Situates Gram statistics in a maximum-entropy modeling perspective and discusses the perceptual-similarity vs sample-variability trade-off.
- Quantitative evaluation: Proposes using VGG Gram-matrix loss as a complementary quantitative metric to visual inspection.
- Empirical performance: Multi-scale random model often matches or sometimes surpasses the VGG19-based Gatys et al. baseline across diverse textures, including some man-made patterns.
- Practical insight: Initializing VGG optimization with single-layer outputs can improve VGG-based synthesis results.
- Reproducibility: Synthesis objective, optimization details (L-BFGS-B, scaling, constraints), and layer-wise VGG losses are clearly documented.
- Decision: Accept — paper presents a strong, surprising, and well-supported minimal baseline likely to influence future work.
- Community impact: Establishes a useful minimal baseline that clarifies where complexity is actually beneficial (efficiency vs quality).
- Metric limitation: VGG Gram loss is an imperfect proxy and can bias comparisons, particularly since it is the VGG baseline's objective.
- Variability underexplored: The paper does not quantify sample diversity across multiple syntheses per texture, leaving open concerns about overfitting or near-reconstruction.
- Computational cost: Multi-scale single-layer models with large filters are expensive; runtime/memory profiling is missing.
- Scope limitation: Claims apply to stationary textures and the approach is not intended for images with global structure; failure modes and boundary artifacts could be flagged more explicitly.
- Suggested additions: Run a small human perceptual study (2AFC/oddity) to validate perceptual equivalence to VGG outputs on challenging textures.
- Suggested additions: Quantify variability by generating multiple samples per texture and reporting distributions (VGG loss, Gram-distance, power spectrum, nearest-neighbor checks).
- Suggested additions: Provide summary statistics across the texture set (median/percentiles of VGG loss, per-texture deltas), not just exemplars.
- Suggested additions: Report runtime and memory for single-scale vs multi-scale vs hierarchical variants with hardware/image-size details.
- Suggested additions: Expand ablation curves (quality vs number of maps), compare nonlinearities and padding choices, and test sensitivity to initialization and stopping criteria.
- Suggested additions: Ensure VGG baseline is well-optimized (multiple restarts, learning-rate/regularization checks) and consider alternative metrics (e.g., MS-SSIM) as sanity checks.
- Open questions for authors: Sensitivity to number of feature maps and chosen scales—can fewer/smaller filters retain quality while reducing computation?
- Open questions for authors: Empirical variability across samples—distributions of VGG loss and evidence of near-copying?
- Open questions for authors: Robustness to nonlinearity (leaky ReLU), padding (circular vs zero), and input normalization—do these materially affect quality?
- Open questions for authors: Quantitative runtime/memory comparisons across models and practical strategies to make multi-scale synthesis more efficient?
- Open questions for authors: How many initializations/restarts were used for VGG baseline and how do different optimization settings affect comparisons?
- Open questions for authors: Do findings extend to larger images, weakly stationary textures, or textures with long-range correlations, and what are systematic failure cases?
- Open question: Have nonlearned alternatives like scattering transform features been tested within the Gram-matrix framework as comparators to random filters?
