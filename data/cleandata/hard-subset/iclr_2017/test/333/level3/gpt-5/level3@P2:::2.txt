Summary of contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: which aspects of recent deep learning–based texture models are truly necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis? It argues, and demonstrates empirically, that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining are indispensable. Instead, a single convolutional layer with ReLU nonlinearity and random filters, matched via Gram-matrix statistics, can synthesize textures with perceptual quality comparable to the VGG-based state of the art. The paper:
- Introduces a strong minimal baseline built from a single layer with random filters, and a multi-scale extension that combines different receptive field sizes.
- Systematically compares random, hand-crafted (Fourier), and unsupervised (k-means, PCA) filters at two capacities (363 vs 3267 feature maps), and studies the roles of scale and nonlinearity.
- Proposes a quantitative evaluation using the VGG Gram-matrix loss to replace purely qualitative judgments, and reports results across 20 textures.
- Offers a thoughtful discussion connecting practical texture synthesis to maximum entropy modeling and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.
Overall, the contribution is both surprising and clarifying: it provides a minimal, well-analyzed baseline that advances understanding of what the Gram-matrix constraints capture and how far we can go without hierarchical, pretrained features.

Decision
Accept. The paper delivers a surprising and well-supported insight with broad implications, and provides a minimal yet strong baseline that will be valuable to the community.

Key reasons for decision
- Conceptual clarity and novelty: It challenges widely held assumptions by showing high-quality synthesis from single-layer random CNNs, especially with multi-scale filters.
- Thorough empirical analysis: Careful ablations (scale, number of filters, nonlinearity, filter types) and consistent optimization/evaluation lend credibility to the claims.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled?
- The paper tests which ingredients of DNN-based texture synthesis (depth, pooling, supervised training) are necessary, isolating the role of Gram-matrix statistics from representational depth and pretraining.

2) Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature?
- The work is well-situated among Gatys et al., Portilla-Simoncelli, Heeger-Bergen, scattering-based approaches, and recent extensions (e.g., style transfer, spectrum constraints). The choice to focus on textures provides a well-defined testbed for probing feature representation and statistics.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Evidence includes: (a) Single-layer random filters synthesize convincing textures, (b) multi-scale random filters substantially improve long-range structure, (c) ReLU is essential, (d) increased channel count improves quality, (e) random often matches or outperforms unsupervised or hand-crafted filters, and (f) VGG-based loss is generally aligned with human perception for many textures. The observation that initializing VGG synthesis with the single-layer result improves final outcomes strengthens the case.
- The paper is honest about limitations: dependence on VGG-based evaluation, computational inefficiency of large filters, and the similarity/variability trade-off.

Weaknesses and limitations (did not preclude acceptance)
- Evaluation is dominated by a VGG-based loss; while pragmatic, it is not a substitute for a psychophysical assessment, especially for highly regular, man-made textures where VGG may mis-rank quality. A small user study would bolster claims.
- The fairness of the VGG baseline comparison could be further clarified (pooling type, iteration budget, initialization, and tuning parity).
- Limited statistical analysis across textures (e.g., average loss differences, variance across seeds) and lack of formal significance testing.
- Computational efficiency: single-layer with very large kernels is slower than deep, small-kernel alternatives; the exploration of faster hierarchical stacks is mentioned but not detailed.
- Scope is limited to texture synthesis; implications for style transfer or other generative tasks are discussed but not empirically tested.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide quantitative summaries across the texture set (mean/median VGG loss and variability across multiple seeds and runs), not only per-image exemplars; include basic significance tests where applicable.
- Report runtime, memory, and iteration counts for all models (single-scale vs multi-scale vs VGG) to contextualize practical trade-offs.
- Expand the ablation on capacity: plot quality vs number of feature maps for random filters to illustrate the capacity-quality curve and the point of diminishing returns.
- Clarify the exact multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, number of channels per scale, initialization ranges) and sensitivity to these choices.
- Ensure the VGG baseline uses comparable settings (average vs max pooling, same iteration budget, same optimizer and step scaling, same initialization where relevant), and report the impact of initialization from the random model.
- Include a short user study or a psychophysical proxy (e.g., the proposed inpainting discrimination task) for at least a subset of textures to triangulate the VGG metric.
- Discuss potential failure cases and texture families where the approach underperforms (e.g., complex structured scenes, non-stationary patterns).
- Release code and pretrained configurations to establish the baseline for the community.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: What exact filter sizes and channel allocations per scale were used? How sensitive is performance to this allocation and to the maximum receptive field?
- Capacity and seeds: How does performance vary with the number of feature maps and random seeds? Please report mean/variance over multiple runs.
- Optimization: How sensitive are results to the gradient/loss scaling (1e7), iteration budget, and choice of optimizer? Could Adam (with tuned learning rate) match L-BFGS-B?
- VGG baseline parity: Did you match pooling type, iteration count, and initialization between your baseline and Gatys-style synthesis? Can you provide side-by-side numbers with identical settings?
- Nonlinearity: Beyond ReLU, did you try leaky-ReLU or tanh? Is the qualitative improvement primarily due to rectification sparsity or any nonlinearity?
- Fourier and k-means filters: Why do random filters outperform unsupervised and hand-crafted banks at equal capacity? Any diagnostic on activation distributions or Gram spectra that explains this?
- Variability: Can you quantify variability across samples from the same target (e.g., inter-sample VGG-loss, pixel-space diversity) and relate it to stopping criteria?
- Efficiency: Can you provide details and results for the hierarchical small-kernel implementation you mention, including speed and quality comparison?
- Generality: Do the conclusions extend to style transfer when texture statistics are combined with content constraints, or to non-stationary textures?

Overall, this work is a clear, well-executed, and thought-provoking contribution. Establishing a minimal, strong baseline helps recalibrate our understanding of what matters in texture synthesis and will likely influence subsequent research.
