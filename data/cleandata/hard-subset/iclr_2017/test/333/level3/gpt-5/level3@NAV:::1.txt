Summary and contributions
This paper proposes a minimal yet strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis: a single-layer CNN with multi-scale random filters optimized to match Gram statistics. Empirically, the baseline often rivals the popular VGG-based, multi-layer pre-trained model on diverse textures, suggesting that neither deep hierarchical representations nor learned features are strictly necessary for perceptually plausible texture generation. The work is thoughtfully executed, includes extensive variants (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA; single vs multi-scale; role of ReLU), and offers clear insights into what matters: multi-scale coverage and nonlinearity, more than depth or supervised pretraining. I found the paper interesting, surprising, and insightful.

Decision: Accept (weak)
Key reasons: (1) A compelling and simple baseline with strong empirical performance that will recalibrate the field’s assumptions; (2) Careful ablations yielding clear design principles (multi-scale + ReLU) and a useful discussion of variability vs. perceptual similarity.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper asks which aspects of CNN feature representations are essential for parametric texture synthesis—depth, pooling, or supervised training—and whether simpler constructions suffice.
- Is the approach well motivated and situated? Yes. It connects to Gatys et al., Portilla & Simoncelli, scattering/MaxEnt perspectives, and recent speed/quality improvements. The motivation to find minimal ingredients is timely and well framed.
- Do results support the claims? Largely, but some conclusions should be tempered. The multi-scale random baseline is strong and sometimes rivals VGG; however, evaluation leans heavily on a VGG-based loss and visual inspection without a user study. Both VGG and single-layer samples remain imperfect and, in many settings, still lag high-quality non-parametric patch-based synthesis. Also, inpainting examples (Fig. 7) suggest the VGG model may handle structured completion better.

Additional feedback (to help improve; not all decisive)
- Please add human perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC, ABX) to validate claims about “rivaling” VGG and to assess variability vs. similarity.
- Include comparisons to modern non-parametric/patch-based baselines to calibrate absolute quality.
- Report computational cost vs. quality: FLOPs, memory, wall-clock for single-layer multi-scale vs. hierarchical (including dilated/atrous) alternatives. A hierarchical model might capture multi-scale structure more efficiently than many large filters even if quality is similar.
- Clarify whether the apparent superiority on some textures is due to optimization ease (fewer local minima) rather than representation quality; control synthesis with matched stopping criteria.
- Consider metrics beyond VGG-loss (retrieval-based, LPIPS, or psychophysics).

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide a user study quantifying perceptual equivalence and variability?
- How do results change when controlling compute (same FLOPs/params) for single-layer multi-scale vs. shallow hierarchical (e.g., dilated) models?
- Do results hold on a broader, more structured set (man-made vs. organic textures), and across random seeds?
- In inpainting, why does VGG appear stronger—does depth help with global consistency?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of feature maps vs. the range of scales?
