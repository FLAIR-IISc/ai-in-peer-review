- Paper asks which aspects of deep features are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis and proposes a minimal parametric model using Gram statistics from a single conv layer with rectified random filters.
- Extends the model to multi-scale by mixing several filter sizes to capture different spatial ranges.
- Main claim: high-quality textures can be synthesized without depth, pooling, or supervised training—random filters + Gram statistics + ReLU suffice.
- Multi-scale random filtering substantially improves quality over single-scale, capturing small-, mid-, and long-range structure.
- A careful optimization setup (L-BFGS-B, gradient scaling, box constraints) is crucial and reconciles earlier negative results with random networks.
- Quantitative evaluation uses a VGG-based texture distance as a proxy for perceptual similarity, supplemented by qualitative comparisons.
- The paper connects the method to the MaxEnt viewpoint and discusses the trade-off between variability and fidelity, advocating soft constraints and an explicit distance metric.
- Overall assessment: insightful, provocative baseline that simplifies architecture while retaining strong results and provides a useful community reference.
- Decision: Weak Accept.
- Reason for accept 1: strong, simple baseline with compelling empirical evidence challenging assumptions about depth and learned features for texture synthesis.
- Reason for accept 2: broad ablations (filter types, scales, nonlinearity) and thoughtful discussion offer valuable guidance despite evaluation limits and some overclaims.
- Positioning: well-placed relative to parametric texture models and deep-feature Gram models; focus on textures is appropriate.
- Approach: single-layer random CNN + Gram stats is principled for testing necessity of depth/training; multi-scale extension is well motivated.
- Empirical ablations show ReLU is indispensable, more feature maps improve quality, multi-scale outperforms single-scale, and random filters rival learned/hand-crafted banks.
- Caveat: VGG-loss is not a ground-truth perceptual metric and may favor VGG-like statistics; human perception can diverge for structured textures.
- Missing comparison: no quantitative/qualitative comparison to strong non-parametric exemplar/patch-based methods, which can be superior for regular patterns.
- Overgeneralization: claim that depth and training are unnecessary should be limited to textures and Gram-based gradient synthesis.
- Suggested evaluation improvements: small-scale human study, comparisons to patch-based baselines, report sample diversity and variability metrics.
- Efficiency suggestions: report runtimes/memory for variants, explore separable/FFT implementations for large kernels.
- Analysis suggestions: plot performance vs number of feature maps, explain why Fourier/PCA/k-means don’t beat random, test sensitivity to padding/color preprocessing/input size.
- Method clarity: justify gradient scaling, report sensitivity, ensure VGG baseline got equal optimization tuning to avoid bias.
- Open questions requested: rationale for chosen multi-scale filter sizes/channel allocations and where returns saturate.
- Open questions requested: provide hierarchical net details, runtimes, and matched-quality comparisons if hierarchical variants are faster.
- Open questions requested: confirm optimization/equilibration for VGG baseline and quantify initialization advantages from single-layer results across textures.
- Open questions requested: report VGG-loss distributions across multiple samples/stopping criteria to characterize variability and overfitting.
- Open questions requested: compare to exemplar-based patch methods on highly regular textures both visually and by VGG-loss.
- Open questions requested: explain why unsupervised/Fourier filters do not consistently outperform random and whether whitening/color mixing help in low-channel regimes.
- Open questions requested: identify failure modes (stochastic microtextures vs man-made periodic) and whether simple global constraints (e.g., power spectrum) can fix artifacts.
- Open questions requested: quantify inpainting performance and boundary consistency between the random multi-scale and VGG-based models.
