1. Summary of contributions
The paper tackles a clear question: which aspects of deep features are actually necessary for high-quality texture synthesis? It proposes a deliberately minimal parametric model based on Gram statistics computed from a single convolutional layer with rectified random filters, extended to a multi-scale variant by mixing several filter sizes. The authors show:
- High-quality textures can be synthesized without depth, pooling, or supervised training of features; random filters suffice when coupled with Gram statistics and ReLUs.
- Multi-scale random filtering substantially improves quality over single-scale, capturing small-, mid-, and long-range structure.
- A careful optimization setup (L-BFGS-B, scaling, box constraints) is crucial, reconciling earlier negative results with random networks.
- Quantitative evaluation uses a VGG-based texture distance as a proxy for perceptual similarity, alongside broad qualitative comparisons.
- The paper neatly connects practice to the MaxEnt viewpoint and discusses variability vs. fidelity, arguing for soft constraints and an explicit distance metric.

Overall, this is an insightful and provocative baseline that simplifies the architecture while retaining surprisingly strong results. It provides a useful reference point for the community and raises important questions about what deep representations contribute to synthesis.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Reason 1: A strong, simple baseline with compelling empirical evidence that challenges prevailing assumptions about the necessity of depth and learned features for texture synthesis.
Reason 2: Broad ablations (filter types, scales, nonlinearity) and thoughtful discussion offer valuable guidance and a reference for future work, despite limitations in evaluation and some overgeneral claims.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem/positioning: The work is well placed in the literature on parametric texture models (Portilla & Simoncelli) and deep-feature Gram models (Gatys et al.), and contrasts with generative models (GANs, PixelCNN) by isolating which feature properties matter for texture statistics. The focus on textures is clear and appropriate.
- Approach/motivation: Using a single-layer random CNN with Gram statistics is a principled way to test necessity of depth/learning while preserving stationarity and feature correlations. The multi-scale extension is well motivated by the need to capture structures beyond the receptive field of a single scale.
- Empirical support: The paper presents wide-ranging comparisons across random, Fourier, PCA, k-means filters; small vs. large channel counts; and single- vs. multi-scale. Key ablations show:
  - ReLUs are indispensable.
  - More feature maps improve quality.
  - Multi-scale outperforms single-scale filters of any size.
  - Random filters rival or surpass learned/hand-crafted banks.
Results often rival the VGG-based model qualitatively and sometimes in VGG-loss, and the single-layer synthesis appears more robust to optimization pitfalls. The evidence generally supports the paper’s claims for texture synthesis. However:
  - The main quantitative metric relies on VGG-loss, which is not a ground-truth perceptual metric and may favor VGG-like statistics. In some structurally regular textures human perception diverges from the VGG proxy.
  - Comparisons to non-parametric exemplar-based methods (e.g., patch-based inpainting/quilting) are absent; visually those methods can be superior for exacting regularity. The inpainting examples (Fig. 7) suggest the VGG model fares better in some settings.
  - The broader conclusion that depth and training are not necessary should be limited to textures and this particular synthesis objective; it does not generalize to other generative tasks.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the acceptance decision)
- Calibrate claims: Emphasize the scope—textures with Gram-based losses and gradient-based synthesis. Clarify that multi-scale and nonlinearity are crucial, and that many feature maps are required.
- Evaluation:
  - Include a small-scale human study (2AFC or inpainting Turing test) to validate the VGG-loss as a perceptual proxy and assess variability vs. fidelity.
  - Add qualitative/quantitative comparison to a strong non-parametric baseline (e.g., patch-based quilting/PatchMatch-based texture synthesis) to contextualize “perceptual quality.”
  - Report diversity: show multiple samples per texture with a simple variability measure (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss distribution) and discuss the fidelity-variability trade-off more concretely (e.g., early stopping schedules).
- Efficiency:
  - Provide wall-clock runtimes and memory use for single-scale, multi-scale, and hierarchical variants under matched quality. The discussion hints that hierarchical models are faster; numbers would be valuable.
  - Explore separable or FFT-based implementations for large kernels to mitigate the 55×55 cost.
- Analysis/ablations:
  - Plot performance vs. number of feature maps per scale; show where returns diminish.
  - Detail why Fourier/PCA/k-means filters do not outperform random. Are there regimes (few filters, specific textures) where learned filters help?
  - Examine sensitivity to padding, color preprocessing (DC removal), and input size.
- Methodological clarity:
  - Justify the gradient scaling (1e7) and report sensitivity. Confirm that the VGG-based baseline was given equally favorable optimization settings to rule out optimization bias.
  - Elaborate on why multi-scale samples sometimes achieve lower VGG-loss than direct VGG synthesis—does the single-layer landscape ease optimization?

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: How were the specific filter sizes and per-scale channel counts chosen? Is there a systematic way to allocate channels across scales, and where do returns saturate?
- Hierarchical efficiency: You state hierarchical nets synthesize faster but don’t improve quality. Please provide architecture details, runtimes, and matched-quality comparisons.
- Optimization bias: Did you tune L-BFGS-B and initialization equally for the VGG-based model? Can you replicate the “initialize VGG from single-layer result” advantage across many textures and quantify it?
- Variability: Can you report distributions of VGG-loss across multiple samples per texture at different stopping criteria? Do random multi-scale models overfit (exact reconstruction) for certain textures/sizes?
- Non-parametric baselines: How does your method compare visually and in VGG-loss to exemplar-based patch methods on strongly regular textures?
- Feature choice: Why do unsupervised (k-means/PCA) and Fourier filters not consistently improve over random? Does whitening or color mixing help more in low-channel regimes?
- Failure modes: Which texture classes systematically fail (e.g., stochastic microtextures vs. man-made periodic)? Can adding simple global constraints (e.g., power spectrum) fix typical artifacts?
- Inpainting: The paper mentions inpainting as a perceptual probe. Do your models or the VGG-based one yield better boundary consistency and perceptual plausibility in that task? Can you quantify it?
