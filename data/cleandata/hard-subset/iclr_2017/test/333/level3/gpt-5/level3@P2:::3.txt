1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates which ingredients of Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis are truly necessary. Building on Gatys et al., the authors replace deep, supervised feature hierarchies with extremely simple architectures and show that:
- A single convolutional layer with random filters (no pooling) can synthesize high-quality natural textures.
- A random multi-scale single-layer model (concatenating multiple filter sizes) often rivals VGG-19-based texture synthesis and sometimes even achieves lower VGG-loss than directly optimizing the VGG objective, likely due to a friendlier optimization landscape.
- Filters trained on natural images (k-means/PCA) or hand-crafted (Fourier) do not consistently outperform random filters; increasing the number of feature maps and combining multiple scales are more important.
- ReLU nonlinearities are necessary; without them, synthesis degrades markedly.
- The paper offers a thoughtful discussion on the maximum-entropy view, the role of soft constraints, and the difficult trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability. It also suggests using psychophysics as a robust evaluation path.

Overall, this is a clear, well-motivated deconstruction of a widely used approach and establishes a strong, minimal baseline with practical implications.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong and surprising empirical finding: depth and supervised pretraining are not indispensable for Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis; a simple random, single-layer multi-scale model works very well.
- Careful ablations (filter type, count, scale, nonlinearity) and insightful analysis of optimization effects provide valuable understanding and a solid baseline for the community.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely addresses a timely question—what aspects of deep feature representations matter for texture synthesis? The scope is focused and well-placed within literature (Gatys et al., Portilla–Simoncelli, Bruna–Mallat, GANs/PixelCNN).
- Approach and placement: The authors systematically test single-layer models with diverse filter families (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA, texture-sampled), different numbers of maps, and multi-scale combinations. This is an appropriate and well-motivated ablation of architectural priors.
- Evidence and rigor:
  - Qualitative results across a reasonably broad set of textures show strong performance, especially for regular, large-scale structures.
  - A quantitative proxy (VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss) is used to compare methods, and the authors explicitly discuss its limitations and failure modes (e.g., for some man-made patterns).
  - The observation that VGG-based synthesis can be improved by initializing with shallow-model outputs is convincing evidence that optimization, not just representation, matters.
  - The analysis of the role of multi-scale receptive fields and the necessity of nonlinearities is clear and well supported.
- Limitations: The evaluation largely relies on the VGG-loss proxy and visual inspection; there is little statistical analysis (no error bars or variability across random seeds), limited reporting on diversity, and no human study. These limit the strength of claims about “perceptual quality” and about modeling texture distributions beyond a single exemplar. The authors acknowledge these issues and outline psychophysical evaluation as future work.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Report variability: For each texture/model, sample multiple outputs with different seeds and report mean±std of the VGG-based loss and pairwise distances among samples (diversity). Show within-texture vs across-texture distance distributions to evidence that the model captures a texture family, not just a single instance.
- Expand quantitative evaluation: Include SSIM and L2 for completeness (even if imperfect) and consider multi-resolution VGG features or scattering-based distances to reduce bias toward VGG statistics. A 2AFC human study on a subset of textures would strongly bolster claims.
- Optimization analysis: Provide more detail on the L-BFGS-B setup (line search, stopping criteria, gradient scaling rationale) and sensitivity analyses. Compare alternative optimizers (Adam/L-BFGS hybrids, multi-resolution pyramid) and multi-start to further separate representation from optimization.
- Multi-scale details: Specify the exact filter sizes, number of filters per scale, normalization of filter norms, and any per-scale weighting. Include a parameter sweep to relate performance to number of maps and scale coverage.
- Broaden comparisons: Add scattering-transform baselines (Bruna–Mallat) quantitatively on the same textures. Given your Fourier and k-means variants, this would contextualize the role of structured vs random bases.
- Efficiency: Provide run-time and memory comparisons (single-layer vs VGG) and discuss how a shallow multi-layer hierarchy might recover efficiency while preserving performance.
- Regular textures: Since the VGG-loss sometimes disagrees with perceptual judgments on grids/walls, supplement with periodicity/auto-correlation metrics or shift-invariant frequency-domain measures for those cases.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale architecture: What are the exact filter sizes, counts per scale, and normalization? How are filters initialized (distribution, variance), and are they re-normalized to equal energy?
- Sensitivity and stability: How sensitive are results to the gradient scaling (1e7), iteration budget, and random seed? Please report variance across seeds and early-stopping effects.
- Diversity: For a fixed texture and model, what is the distribution of pairwise VGG distances among 10–20 synthesized samples? How does this compare to distances between non-overlapping patches from the source texture?
- Optimization gap: Can you improve direct VGG optimization using (a) your shallow-model initialization, (b) multi-resolution coarse-to-fine, or (c) different optimizers? How much of the “random beats VGG” effect disappears?
- Nonlinearity: Did you test leaky-ReLU or other nonlinearities? How do they affect synthesis quality relative to ReLU/no nonlinearity?
- Scattering baseline: How does a scattering-based Gram model perform under your evaluation protocol?
- Scope: Do the conclusions extend to style transfer or non-stationary textures (e.g., structured scenes), or do you expect the shallow random approach to degrade without stationarity?

Overall, this paper provides a valuable, minimal baseline and deepens our understanding of what drives Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis. With modest additions on quantitative rigor and diversity, it would be even stronger.
