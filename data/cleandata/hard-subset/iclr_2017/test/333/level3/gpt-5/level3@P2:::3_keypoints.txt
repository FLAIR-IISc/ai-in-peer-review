- Single convolutional layer with random filters (no pooling) can synthesize high-quality natural textures.
- A random multi-scale single-layer model often rivals VGG-19 synthesis and sometimes achieves lower VGG-loss, likely due to a friendlier optimization landscape.
- Trained filters (k-means/PCA) or hand-crafted Fourier filters do not consistently outperform random filters; number of feature maps and multi-scale combination matter more.
- ReLU nonlinearities are necessary; removing them markedly degrades synthesis.
- Paper provides a thoughtful discussion of the maximum-entropy view, soft constraints, and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability; recommends psychophysics for evaluation.
- Overall contribution: clear deconstruction of Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis and a strong, minimal baseline with practical implications.
- Recommendation: Accept, based on surprising empirical findings and careful ablations.
- Scope and motivation: Focused question on which aspects of deep features matter for texture synthesis, well placed in prior literature.
- Methodology: Systematic tests across filter families, map counts, and multi-scale combinations are appropriate and well-motivated.
- Qualitative evidence: Strong visual results across a broad set of textures, especially regular and large-scale structures.
- Quantitative proxy: Uses VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss for comparison but explicitly notes its limitations and failure modes.
- Optimization insight: Initializing VGG-based synthesis with shallow-model outputs improves results, indicating optimization effects matter.
- Multi-scale and nonlinearity analysis: Clear, well-supported evidence for the importance of receptive-field scale and nonlinearities.
- Main limitations: Heavy reliance on VGG-loss and visual inspection; lacks statistical analysis (error bars), diversity metrics, and human studies.
- Authors acknowledge limitations and propose psychophysical evaluation as future work.
- Suggestion: Report variability across random seeds and provide mean±std of VGG-loss and pairwise sample distances to quantify diversity.
- Suggestion: Expand quantitative evaluation with SSIM, L2, multi-resolution VGG features, scattering distances, and consider a 2AFC human study.
- Suggestion: Provide detailed optimization settings (L-BFGS-B line search, stopping criteria, gradient scaling) and sensitivity analyses; compare other optimizers and multi-start strategies.
- Suggestion: Specify exact multi-scale filter sizes, filters per scale, normalization, and include parameter sweeps relating maps/scale to performance.
- Suggestion: Add scattering-transform baselines to contextualize structured vs random bases.
- Suggestion: Provide run-time and memory comparisons between single-layer and VGG approaches and discuss shallow-hierarchy efficiency trade-offs.
- Suggestion: For periodic/regular textures, supplement VGG-loss with periodicity/autocorrelation or frequency-domain metrics.
- Question: What are the exact filter sizes, counts per scale, initialization distribution/variance, and any energy normalization?
- Question: How sensitive are results to gradient scaling (1e7), iteration budget, and random seed—please report variance and early-stopping effects?
- Question: What is the distribution of pairwise VGG distances among 10–20 synthesized samples versus non-overlapping patches from the source texture?
- Question: Can direct VGG optimization be improved via (a) shallow-model initialization, (b) multi-resolution coarse-to-fine, or (c) different optimizers, and does that reduce the “random beats VGG” effect?
- Question: Were other nonlinearities (e.g., leaky-ReLU) tested, and how do they compare to ReLU/no nonlinearity?
- Question: How does a scattering-based Gram model perform under the same evaluation protocol?
- Question: Do conclusions extend to style transfer or non-stationary textures, or is the shallow random approach limited to stationary textures?
