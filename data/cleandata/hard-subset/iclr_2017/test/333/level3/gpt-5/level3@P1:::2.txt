This paper investigates which ingredients of CNN-based texture synthesis are truly necessary. The authors ask whether depth, pooling, or supervised pretraining are essential, and demonstrate that none is: a single-layer convolutional network with random filters (especially in a multi-scale configuration) and Gram-matrix statistics can synthesize natural textures of high perceptual quality, in many cases rivaling the VGG19-based model of Gatys et al. The study is thorough: it evaluates multiple single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA-learned filters; 363 vs. 3267 maps; 11×11 filters; and a multi-scale variant up to 55×55) and ablates key factors (filter scale, nonlinearity). It also presents a principled discussion situating Gram statistics within maximum-entropy modeling and emphasizes the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability. As a further contribution, the paper proposes a quantitative evaluation based on VGG Gram-matrix loss to complement visual inspection.

Decision: Accept, because the paper provides a strong, surprising, and well-supported minimal baseline that clarifies what matters in texture synthesis and will influence future work.

Supporting arguments:
- Clear problem framing and impactful finding: Contrary to common assumptions, depth and supervised training are not required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. This shifts the focus toward the nature of summary statistics (Gram matrices) and multi-scale coverage rather than learned hierarchical features.
- Thorough ablation and breadth: The paper systematically varies filter types (random vs. principled vs. unsupervised), numbers of channels, scales, and nonlinearities. The evidence that ReLU nonlinearity and multi-scale coverage are crucial, while training and depth are not, is compelling.
- Competitive performance: Across a diverse set of textures, the multi-scale random model often matches or occasionally surpasses the VGG baseline, including for man-made regular patterns. The observation that initializing VGG optimization with single-layer outputs improves results underscores the robustness of the proposed approach.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: The synthesis objective, optimization details (L-BFGS-B, scaling, constraints), and evaluation (layer-wise VGG Gram losses) are explicit. The study situates results in a broader theoretical context (MaxEnt, stationarity) and carefully discusses the similarity–variability trade-off.
- Community value: Establishing a strong minimal baseline helps disentangle where complexity is actually beneficial (efficiency vs. quality) and guides principled design of future models.

Additional arguments and caveats:
- Evaluation metric limitations: The VGG Gram-based loss is a reasonable proxy for perceptual similarity and aligns with prior work, but it is imperfect and may bias comparisons, especially since it doubles as the objective for the VGG baseline. The authors acknowledge cases where multi-scale random achieves lower VGG loss than VGG-optimized textures, suggesting optimization effects and metric mismatch.
- Variability underexplored: While the paper rightly calls attention to sample diversity, it does not quantify variability across multiple samples per texture. This leaves some uncertainty about overfitting/degeneracy (e.g., near-reconstruction under certain settings).
- Computational cost: Multi-scale single-layer models with large filters are expensive. Although the authors note that depth can improve efficiency without improving quality, some empirical runtime/memory profiling would strengthen the practical message.
- Scope: The claims are appropriately restricted to stationary textures; the approach is not intended for images with global structure. The paper could more explicitly flag failure modes and boundary artifacts.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive for acceptance):
- Add a small-scale human study (2AFC or oddity) to validate that the multi-scale random model’s outputs are as confusable with references as VGG outputs, especially for the difficult man-made textures where VGG loss disagrees with perception.
- Quantify variability: for each texture, generate multiple samples and report within-model distances (VGG-loss distributions, Gram-distance distributions, power spectrum/auto-correlation statistics). Include nearest-neighbor analyses to rule out near-copying.
- Provide summary statistics across the texture set (e.g., median/percentiles of VGG loss for each model, with per-texture deltas), not only exemplars.
- Report runtime and memory usage for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. hierarchical variants; include image sizes and hardware.
- Expand ablations: curves showing quality vs. number of feature maps; comparisons of nonlinearities (ReLU vs. leaky ReLU) and padding choices; sensitivity to initialization and stopping criteria (demonstrating the similarity–variability trade-off).
- Clarify evaluation fairness by ensuring the VGG baseline is well-optimized (e.g., multiple restarts, learning-rate/regularization sensitivity) and, if feasible, include alternative metrics (e.g., multi-scale SSIM as a sanity check) despite known limitations.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are the results to the number of feature maps per scale and the exact set of scales? Could a narrower multi-scale design (fewer, smaller filters) retain most of the quality while reducing computation?
- What is the empirical variability across samples for a fixed texture and model? Can you report distributions of VGG loss among multiple samples and any evidence of near-reconstruction?
- How robust are the conclusions to changes in nonlinearity (e.g., leaky ReLU), padding (circular vs. zero), or input normalization? Do these choices materially affect quality or failure modes?
- Can you provide quantitative runtime/memory comparisons across models and image sizes, and discuss practical strategies to make multi-scale synthesis more efficient?
- For fairness, how many initializations and optimization restarts were used for the VGG baseline, and how do different optimization settings affect its results versus the proposed models?
- Do the findings extend to larger images and to textures with weak stationarity or subtle long-range correlations? Are there systematic failure cases you can categorize?
- Have you tried scattering transform features as a nonlearned comparator to random filters within the same Gram-matrix framework, and if so, how do they fare?

Overall, this is a strong and clarifying contribution that establishes a compelling minimal baseline and opens useful directions for evaluation and efficiency.
