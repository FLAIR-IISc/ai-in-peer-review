1. Summary of contributions
The paper asks a focused and timely question: within Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis (Gatys et al.), which ingredients—depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining—are truly necessary? The core contribution is a careful empirical study showing that very shallow architectures, including a single-layer random filter bank (especially multi-scale), can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality, sometimes visually rivaling and even outperforming VGG-19 on simple/regular textures. The paper offers insightful analysis of optimization: Figure 4 shows optimizing with respect to shallow filter banks can yield lower VGG-19 loss than directly optimizing the VGG-19 objective, plausibly due to VGG-19’s harder optimization landscape; and initializing VGG optimization with shallow-model outputs improves VGG results (supplementary). The discussion convincingly lays out strengths and limits of Gram-matrix methods and frames the open problem of measuring diversity in texture generation.

2. Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper delivers a surprising, well-motivated, and impactful finding that clarifies what matters in Gram-based texture synthesis and challenges common assumptions about depth and pretraining. The analysis of optimization dynamics is especially informative.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: Identify which aspects of CNN representations are crucial for texture synthesis using Gram statistics.
- Motivation and placement: Solidly grounded in prior work (Gatys et al., MaxEnt perspectives) with thorough ablations across filter types, sizes, and scales. The work is well situated relative to prior parametric and learning-based approaches.
- Do results support claims? The qualitative evidence is extensive, with consistent trends (e.g., multi-scale > single-scale; many channels help; random filters surprisingly strong). The VGG-loss analysis and the VGG-initialization result substantiate the optimization argument. However, the study lacks a systematic, quantifiable, multi-metric comparison across methods and does not rigorously assess sample diversity; thus, claims about modeling the underlying texture distribution remain tentative.

4. Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not gating)
- Add diversity/coverage evaluation: multi-sample LPIPS/MMD in Gram space, patch-level novelty (nearest-neighbor/plagiarism analysis), and variability under multiple random seeds.
- Include a small psychophysical study (2AFC discrimination or inpainting) to validate perceived quality and diversity.
- Report dataset-level statistics (number and categories of textures; results split by regular vs stochastic).
- Control for optimization: compare L-BFGS-B vs Adam; ablate gradient/loss scaling; ensure fair iteration budgets across methods.
- Clarify failure cases and provide more quantitative summaries, not only cherry-picked visuals.

Questions for authors
- How consistent are gains across texture types? Can you quantify performance by regular vs stochastic categories?
- How sensitive are results to nonlinearity (ReLU vs leaky/ELU) and to feature/Gram normalization?
- With equal parameter counts, what is the contribution of multi-scale vs single large filters?
- Can you report statistics on the VGG-initialization benefit across all textures (final loss, convergence speed, success rate)?
- Do shallow models ever exactly reproduce references? If so, can you quantify and guard against memorization?
