- Main claim: Single convolutional layer with random filters (plus ReLU), especially in a multi-scale variant, can synthesize textures rivaling or exceeding VGG-19 Gram-matrix synthesis for many textures.
- Contribution: Introduces a minimal baseline using random single-layer filter banks and shows high perceptual-quality texture synthesis.
- Key empirical finding: Depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not indispensable for Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis.
- Multi-scale importance: Combining multiple receptive-field scales is crucial to capture both local and long-range texture structure.
- Nonlinearity matters: ReLU (or similar nonlinearity) is necessary; linear-only models perform worse.
- Filter choice: Random filters are competitive with learned (k-means, PCA) and hand-crafted (Fourier) filter banks.
- Feature map count: Increasing the number of feature maps improves synthesis quality.
- Optimization insights: Optimization choices (gradient scaling, L-BFGS-B, initialization) strongly affect results; shallow-model initialization improves VGG optimization.
- VGG-loss proxy: Proposes VGG texture loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity; shallow models can achieve lower VGG loss than direct VGG-optimization due to optimization difficulties.
- Theoretical framing: Connects empirical results to summary statistics, maximum entropy modeling, and the similarity–variability trade-off.
- Rigor and reproducibility: Methodology is clear and reproducible in principle (Lasagne/Theano), with informative ablations.
- Decision: Accept — due to a clear, surprising negative result, careful ablations, and a useful minimal baseline expected to influence practice.
- Suggested quantitative improvements: Report summary statistics across a larger texture set (e.g., DTD), means/variances of VGG-loss across seeds, and statistical tests.
- Diversity evaluation: Provide intra-method diversity curves (pairwise VGG-loss or Gram distances) and show diversity–similarity trade-offs via early stopping or noise annealing.
- Human evaluation: Recommend a small controlled 2AFC human study on a subset of textures to strengthen perceptual claims.
- Dataset clarity: Specify the texture set used, number of textures, stationarity assumptions, and list sources in the appendix.
- Baseline comparisons: Include quantitative side-by-side comparisons with Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) and feedforward texture networks (Ulyanov et al.).
- Efficiency analysis: Provide runtime/memory comparisons for single-scale vs multi-scale shallow models and against hierarchical variants, plus iterations to reach comparable VGG-loss.
- Implementation details: Clarify filter scaling/normalization, random seed control, feature-map normalization before Gram computation, and effects of biases or alternative nonlinearities.
- Failure cases: Highlight textures where shallow models fail (e.g., non-stationary or structural textures) to delineate method scope.
- Reproducibility questions: Request details on texture set release, code, exact filter sizes/counts/initialization, normalization, and seed usage.
- Optimization questions: Ask about sensitivity to loss/gradient scaling (1e7), iteration cap (2000), box constraints, and comparisons to Adam or other solvers.
- Model design questions: Inquire about experiments with alternative nonlinearities, pooling/normalization, cross-channel coupling for color, and evidence of over-constrained convergence across seeds.
- Hierarchical variants: Request quantification of hierarchical model speedups and description of best-performing hierarchical configurations.
