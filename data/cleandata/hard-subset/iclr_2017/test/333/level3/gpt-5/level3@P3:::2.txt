Summary of contributions
The paper asks a clear and important question: which aspects of CNN-based representations are truly necessary to generate natural-looking textures? Focusing on parametric texture synthesis, the authors systematically strip down the standard VGG-based method and show that high-quality textures can be obtained without depth, pooling, or supervised pretraining. A single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix statistics is sufficient, and a random multi-scale variant often rivals the VGG-based state of the art. The paper provides:
- A strong minimal baseline using single-layer random (and other) filter banks, including Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters.
- Careful ablations across number of feature maps, filter sizes, and multi-scale vs single-scale designs, highlighting the importance of multi-scale and nonlinearity.
- An analysis of optimization choices (L-BFGS-B, re-scaling) that resolves prior negative results with random weights.
- A quantitative evaluation proxy using VGG-based Gram losses to complement qualitative inspection.
- A thoughtful discussion linking the findings to maximum-entropy texture models and the critical trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Surprising and significant result: depth and supervised training are not indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis; a well-designed shallow, random model can rival the state of the art.
- Thorough, careful analysis and ablations that isolate what actually matters (multi-scale receptive fields and nonlinearity) and what does not.
This assessment aligns with other reviewers and points to a promising, simplified direction for future work on generative modeling and perceptual metrics.

Supporting arguments
- Problem framing and motivation: The paper targets a highly visible line of work (CNN Gram-statistics for textures and style). It is well-placed in the literature and contrasts “add more structure/constraints” with “how little is actually needed.”
- Approach: The design space is explored in a principled way: filter types (random vs learned vs Fourier), number of feature maps, single vs multi-scale, and with/without ReLU. The multi-scale random model convincingly captures small-, mid-, and long-range structure despite having no learned hierarchy.
- Claims vs evidence: Visual results are strong and often competitive with Gatys et al. The VGG-based loss, while imperfect, generally tracks perceptual quality and sometimes favors the random model—underscoring that optimization and variability matter. The paper provides plausible explanations (optimization robustness; soft vs hard constraints) and connects to MaxEnt theory.
- Rigor: The experimental protocol is transparent, with implementation details and hyperparameters. The ablation on nonlinearity and scale is clear, and the discussion squarely addresses computational efficiency and the role of hierarchical nets for speed (not quality).
- Broader impact: Establishing a minimal, robust baseline is valuable for the community. It reframes what we think deep features “buy” us in texture synthesis and encourages focusing on perceptual metrics and variability rather than depth per se.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Evaluation methodology needs strengthening:
  - The VGG-based loss is not a fully independent metric and can bias comparisons. Please add a small human psychophysics study (e.g., 2AFC discrimination, inpainting oddity) to validate perceptual similarity and variability. Even a pilot with a few textures and participants would help.
  - Report sample diversity explicitly. For each texture, generate multiple samples and quantify variability (e.g., pairwise VGG-distance distributions, LPIPS-like proxies, or entropy proxies on patch-level features).
- Clarify the role of “multi-scale” vs “depth”: The multi-scale single-layer model is crucial; please quantify how much of the gain comes from larger RFs vs combining scales in one objective (beyond the figure), and whether dilated convolutions could match the multi-scale effect more efficiently.
- Optimization confounds: Provide an ablation on L-BFGS settings (scaling factor, iteration budget, box constraints) to show robustness and to clarify the discrepancy with previous “random weights fail” observations.
- Computational analysis: Add runtime/memory benchmarks for single-scale vs multi-scale vs shallow hierarchical approximations, and discuss practical deployment (e.g., precomputing features, FFT-based convolutions for large kernels).
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code, filter seeds, and the texture set used; specify all preprocessing (e.g., channel mean subtraction) and parameter choices in an appendix.

Questions for the authors
1. Variability vs similarity: Can you report variability across multiple synthesized samples per texture (e.g., distributions of VGG-loss or other proxies), and show how early stopping or noise initialization affects the trade-off?
2. Independence of the metric: How do your conclusions change with alternative perceptual distances (e.g., scattering transform distances, LPIPS-like metrics, or human 2AFC)? Do the rankings of models remain?
3. Generality: Do results transfer to non-stationary natural images or to style transfer? Is the “random multi-scale + Gram” recipe competitive there as well?
4. Architectural efficiency: Could dilated convolutions or steerable filter banks recover the multi-scale benefits more efficiently than large kernels? Any experiments?
5. Nonlinearity: You show ReLU is indispensable. Have you tried other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, modulus, squaring) and can you comment on their impact on both quality and variability?
6. Optimization details: How sensitive are outcomes to the 1e7 loss scaling and iteration count? Can momentum-based first-order optimizers (e.g., Adam) achieve comparable results?
7. Failure cases: Which texture classes consistently defeat the random multi-scale model? Do these failures point to missing statistics beyond second-order Gram matrices (e.g., phase alignment, higher-order cross-correlations)?
8. Color handling: The Fourier-3267 design mixes color channels. Do cross-channel statistics materially affect results, and could color decorrelation (e.g., in YUV) improve synthesis?

Overall, this paper provides a compelling, carefully executed analysis with surprising results. The evaluation methodology would benefit from stronger perceptual validation and explicit diversity measurements, but the core contribution is solid and impactful. I recommend acceptance.
