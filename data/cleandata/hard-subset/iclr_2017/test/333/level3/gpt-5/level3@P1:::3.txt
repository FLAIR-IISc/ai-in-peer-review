The paper investigates what aspects of CNN feature representations are necessary for Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis, and makes the striking claim that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining are indispensable. It introduces a minimal baseline using a single convolutional layer with random filters (and ReLU), including a multi-scale variant, and shows that these models produce textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality—often rivaling, and on some highly regular textures even surpassing, synthesis from VGG-19 features. The paper systematically varies filter banks (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random), number of feature maps, receptive field sizes, and nonlinearity, and connects empirical findings to a clear discussion of summary statistics, maximum entropy, and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. It also proposes using the VGG texture loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and reports that shallow models can achieve lower VGG loss than direct VGG-optimization, likely due to optimization difficulties; initializing VGG optimization with shallow-model results improves the outcome.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, surprising, and well-supported negative result that reduces the perceived necessity of depth and supervised training for texture synthesis within the Gram-matrix framework; (2) Careful ablations and insights into optimization and multi-scale design, yielding a strong and useful minimal baseline that will influence practice and future work.

Supporting arguments:
- Problem: The paper directly targets the widely held belief (from Gatys et al.) that hierarchical deep, pretrained features are crucial for natural texture synthesis. It asks whether depth, pooling, or supervised training are actually necessary in this framework.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well positioned within the texture synthesis and style transfer literature (Julesz, Heeger & Bergen, Portilla & Simoncelli, Gatys et al., Ulyanov et al.). It connects to MaxEnt texture modeling and clarifies how Gram statistics build stationary feature spaces that capture pairwise correlations.
- Claims supported: The experiments convincingly show that single-layer random filter banks with ReLUs, especially in a multi-scale configuration, can reproduce both local and longer-range structure, with quality improving with the number of feature maps. Ablations demonstrate: (i) multi-scale combinations are crucial (single scale trades off short vs long-range structure); (ii) nonlinearity matters (without ReLU, synthesis degrades); (iii) random filters are competitive with learned/hand-crafted filters; and (iv) optimization matters (gradient scaling, L-BFGS-B, and initialization effects). The proposed VGG-loss-based evaluation, while imperfect, is a reasonable quantitative proxy in this domain and aligns broadly with visual assessments. The discussion thoughtfully frames the similarity–variability trade-off and the limitations of hard constraint MaxEnt in finite images.
- Rigor: The methodology is clear, reproducible in principle (Lasagne/Theano implementation, well-specified filter banks and optimization), and the ablations are informative. That said, the evaluation would benefit from broader, more systematic quantification (see feedback).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all items affect my decision):
- Quantitative evaluation: Report summary statistics across a larger texture set (e.g., DTD or a curated stationary texture suite). Provide means/variances of VGG-loss across multiple seeds per texture and method, and include simple statistical tests to substantiate claims of parity/superiority.
- Diversity/variability: While a full psychophysical study may be out of scope, add a practical proxy: e.g., intra-method diversity curves by reporting pairwise VGG-loss (or Gram distances) among multiple samples per method, alongside reference-to-sample distances. Show diversity–similarity trade-offs by early stopping or noise annealing schedules.
- Human evaluation: A small, controlled 2AFC study (human indistinguishability of patches) on a subset of textures would significantly strengthen conclusions about perceptual quality and variability.
- Dataset clarity: Specify the texture set used (number, source, stationarity assumptions) and provide the full list in the appendix.
- Baselines: Include side-by-side comparisons and quantitative scores versus Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) and feedforward texture networks (Ulyanov et al.), if feasible, to situate the minimal model more completely.
- Efficiency: Provide runtime and memory comparisons for single-scale vs multi-scale shallow models and against hierarchical variants; discuss how many iterations are needed to reach comparable VGG-loss.
- Implementation details: Clarify filter scaling/normalization, random seed control, and whether feature maps are normalized before Gram computation. Report the effect of adding biases or alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU) if tried.
- Failure cases: Highlight textures where shallow models systematically fail relative to VGG (e.g., strongly non-stationary or near-structural textures), to delineate scope.

Questions for the authors:
- Texture set and reproducibility: How many textures were evaluated, from which source, and are they released? Will you release code, filter definitions, and seeds to enable exact reproduction?
- Evaluation protocol: How many random initializations per texture were used to compute the reported VGG-loss numbers? Could you provide aggregated statistics (mean ± std) across seeds?
- Filter bank design: For the multi-scale random model, what exact filter sizes, counts per scale, and initialization distributions were used? Were filters normalized (e.g., unit norm) and were channels whitened or standardized beyond mean subtraction?
- Optimization: You scale the loss/gradients by 1e7 to avoid L-BFGS-B early stopping. How sensitive are results to this choice, iteration cap (2000), and box constraints? Have you compared Adam or LBFGS with strong line search?
- Nonlinearity and pooling: Did you explore alternative nonlinearities or local response normalization? What happens if you add average pooling to increase invariance within the single layer?
- Variability: Do shallow models tend to converge to the same sample across seeds for some textures, indicating over-constrained behavior? Any evidence of exact reconstruction beyond the anecdotal cases mentioned?
- Color handling: For Fourier-3267 and KMeans variants that mix channels, how much does cross-channel coupling contribute relative to per-channel filtering?
- Hierarchical speedups: You note hierarchical nets improve efficiency without quality gains. Can you quantify speedups and describe the best-performing hierarchical configuration?
