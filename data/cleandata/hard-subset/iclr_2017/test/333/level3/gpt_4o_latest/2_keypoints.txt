- Main claim: Single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can synthesize high-quality, natural-looking textures, challenging the need for deep, hierarchical, or pre-trained networks.  
- Contribution: Provides a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis and shows depth/supervised training are not indispensable.  
- Impact: Results simplify texture synthesis requirements and open new research directions.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: Novel findings and thorough, rigorous analysis.  
- Comparison to prior work: Well-motivated contrast with state-of-the-art (e.g., Gatys et al. 2015a).  
- Experimental strength: Extensive experiments across single-layer architectures and filter types demonstrate robustness, including surprising effectiveness of random filters.  
- Evaluation limitation: Heavy reliance on VGG-based loss as perceptual proxy; human evaluations or alternative perceptual metrics are needed.  
- Computational concern: Method is computationally inefficient due to large filters; improvements (hierarchical designs or optimized filters) are suggested.  
- Variability issue: Trade-off between perceptual similarity and diversity of synthesized textures is underexplored and needs metrics/strategies.  
- Suggested evaluations: Include psychophysical assessments, diversity metrics, and alternative distance/loss functions.  
- Questions for authors: How do textures fare in human discrimination tasks (real vs generated)?  
- Questions for authors: Can the single-layer approach extend to more complex image generation tasks beyond textures?  
- Questions for authors: Have alternative loss functions been considered to better capture perceptual similarity vs variability?
