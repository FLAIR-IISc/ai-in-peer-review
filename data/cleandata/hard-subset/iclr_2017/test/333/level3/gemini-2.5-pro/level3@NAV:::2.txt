This paper presents a thorough and surprising investigation into the fundamental components required for high-quality natural texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical networks with features pre-trained on large datasets are necessary for this task. The authors compellingly demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art method from Gatys et al.

Recommendation: Accept.

My decision is based on two key factors: the impactful and counter-intuitive nature of the core findings, and the rigorous empirical analysis that supports them. The paper provides a strong minimal baseline for the field and forces a re-evaluation of what architectural components are truly essential for generative modeling of textures.

The paper’s main strength is its clear and well-supported central claim. The authors systematically dissect the problem by conducting a comprehensive set of experiments that compare various single-layer architectures, ablating filter types (random, learned, hand-crafted), the number of feature maps, and filter scales. This rigorous approach convincingly isolates the crucial elements—namely, the use of multi-scale filters and non-linearities—from those that are less critical, such as network depth and supervised feature learning. The finding that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained VGG network is a significant contribution.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors reconsider their quantitative evaluation methodology. The primary metric used is a loss calculated from the very VGG-based model the paper argues is not essential. This circular evaluation is potentially biased and weakens the quantitative claims, even though the qualitative visual results are strong. Future work would benefit greatly from incorporating human perceptual studies or exploring alternative, independent metrics to validate the quality of the synthesized textures more objectively. The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability is also very insightful and highlights a promising direction for future research.

I have two questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1. Could you elaborate on the potential biases of using the VGG-based loss as your primary quality metric? Have you considered other quantitative measures that are independent of the models being compared?
2. The paper notes that scaling the loss by a large factor was critical for optimization. Could you provide more intuition on why this was necessary and how sensitive the results are to this hyperparameter?
