- Central finding: a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters can synthesize high-quality, naturalistic textures.  
- Key challenge to prior belief: depth and supervised pre-training are not strictly necessary for parametric texture synthesis.  
- Empirical result: multi-scale random filter banks often match or outperform VGG-19 outputs, especially on regular patterns.  
- Methodological strength: systematic ablation of depth and learning clarifies why Gatys et al.’s approach works.  
- Optimization insight: optimizing shallow random-network statistics can yield images with lower VGG-based loss than direct VGG optimization.  
- Practical implication: initializing VGG synthesis from shallow-model outputs can improve VGG-based results, suggesting difficult deep optimization landscapes.  
- Evaluation caveat: reliance on qualitative inspection and the imperfect VGG-loss metric weakens claims about perceptual quality.  
- Suggested improvement: include controlled psychophysical experiments (e.g., 2AFC) to rigorously assess perceptual similarity.  
- Generative concern: current approach matches a single exemplar’s statistics and may not demonstrate true sampling diversity from the texture distribution.  
- Suggested improvement: quantify sample variability across multiple random initializations to rule out near-deterministic reconstructions.  
- Reviewer decision: Accept — the result is surprising, significant, and supported by strong empirical evidence and discussion.  
- Open questions for authors: how variable are samples across seeds, why shallow optimization outperforms direct VGG optimization (local minima vs implicit regularization), and sensitivity to filter counts/sizes in the random multi-scale model.
