### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that state-of-the-art results, such as those from Gatys et al. (2015a), depend critically on deep, hierarchical representations from networks (like VGG) pre-trained on large-scale image recognition tasks. The authors systematically test this hypothesis by proposing a surprisingly simple and minimal baseline: a single-layer convolutional network with no pooling and random, data-independent filters. Through extensive empirical evaluation, the paper demonstrates that this model, particularly a multi-scale variant, can generate textures of high perceptual quality that often rival those produced by the 19-layer VGG network. Based on these findings, the authors conclude that neither network depth, pooling, nor learned features are indispensable for this task, suggesting that the core mechanism may be the capture of stationary feature correlations across multiple scales.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong and Insightful Baseline:** The paper introduces a simple yet remarkably effective baseline model that serves as an important point of reference for the field. The finding that a single layer of random filters can be competitive with a deep, pre-trained network is a significant and non-obvious result.
2.  **Challenges Conventional Wisdom:** The work provides compelling evidence to question the necessity of deep, hierarchical, and learned features for texture synthesis. This forces a valuable re-evaluation of *why* deep models are successful and opens new avenues for creating simpler, more efficient generative models.

### Supporting Arguments

The main strength of this paper lies in its direct and effective challenge to the complexity of the state-of-the-art model for parametric texture synthesis. By systematically removing key components—depth, pooling, and learned weights—the authors isolate what might be the core ingredients for success. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling. The multi-scale random filter model consistently produces high-quality textures and is particularly impressive on textures with regular, large-scale structures (e.g., the brick wall), where it sometimes perceptually outperforms the VGG-based model. This provides strong empirical support for the central claim that deep, learned hierarchies are not strictly necessary.

The work is interesting and insightful because it suggests that the power of the Gatys et al. model may derive less from the semantic knowledge encoded in its deep layers and more from its ability to provide a rich set of multi-scale feature statistics. The proposed random-filter baseline achieves a similar effect not through a hierarchy, but by explicitly using filters of varying sizes. This finding has important implications for understanding image generation and could inspire simpler models for related tasks like style transfer.

However, the conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not required should be interpreted with some caution, and this is where the paper could be strengthened. As the review key points note, the samples generated from *both* the VGG and the single-layer models are often imperfect and visibly inferior to the target texture, and certainly less perfect than what non-parametric methods can achieve. Furthermore, the paper's own appendix (Figure 7, inpainting) provides an interesting counter-example where the VGG-based model appears to perform better at filling in missing regions. This suggests that while a shallow model can capture stationary statistics well, a hierarchical structure might be more adept at modeling complex spatial relationships and long-range consistency, which are crucial for tasks beyond pure texture synthesis. These limitations do not undermine the paper's core contribution but call for a more nuanced discussion of the relative strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the acceptance decision.

1.  **Nuancing the Role of Hierarchy:** The conclusion that deep hierarchies are not necessary is strong, but perhaps too general. Could you elaborate on the potential benefits that they *do* offer? For instance, your inpainting example in Figure 7 seems to show VGG producing a more coherent result. Does this imply that hierarchical representations are better for capturing non-local structure or context, even if a shallow multi-scale model can better capture stationary statistics? A more detailed discussion on the trade-offs would be valuable.

2.  **Efficiency vs. Receptive Field:** You note in the discussion that a hierarchical net would be a more efficient way to achieve large receptive fields compared to using large filters (e.g., 55x55). This is a crucial point. Could a shallow hierarchical model (e.g., 2-3 layers) be constructed to explicitly and efficiently match the receptive field sizes of your multi-scale model? Comparing such a model to your single-layer baseline could help disentangle whether the key ingredient is simply access to multi-scale statistics or the specific single-layer architecture you propose.

3.  **The Gap to Non-Parametric Methods:** The paper focuses on a comparison between two parametric models. It would strengthen the context of your work to briefly acknowledge that both models produce results with visible artifacts and fall short of the quality achieved by classic non-parametric methods (e.g., Efros & Leung, 1999). This would help frame the contribution as a fundamental insight into the *relative complexity* of parametric models, rather than a claim to have solved texture synthesis.

4.  **Quantitative Evaluation:** The use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a clever idea. However, as you observe, it sometimes yields paradoxical results (e.g., the random model achieving a lower VGG loss than the VGG model itself). This may point to optimization challenges in the very deep VGG network. Have you considered a small-scale human perceptual study to more robustly validate the claim that your baseline "often rivals" the state-of-the-art? This could provide stronger, more direct evidence for your central claims.
