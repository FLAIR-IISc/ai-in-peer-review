- Main finding: A single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters can synthesize high-quality textures, often rivaling 19-layer pre-trained VGG results.  
- Challenge to field: Depth and supervised feature learning are not necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis.  
- Methodology: Systematic empirical study varying filter initializations, numbers of channels, filter sizes/scales, and filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA).  
- Multi-scale importance: Multi-scale receptive fields are identified as the most critical architectural component for texture quality.  
- Model capacity: Clear effect of channel count shown by comparisons (e.g., 363 vs 3267 feature maps).  
- Nonlinearity: Ablation study on ReLU supports claims about necessary model components.  
- Quantitative metric: Introduces using VGG-based synthesis loss as a proxy for perceptual quality to provide objective evaluation.  
- Surprising result: Multi-scale random models sometimes achieve lower VGG-loss than VGG-based synthesis, suggesting optimization issues with deep models.  
- Conceptual contribution: Suggests deep models’ success may come from multi-scale filter banks rather than semantic learned features.  
- Trade-offs discussed: Thoughtful analysis of perceptual similarity vs. sample variability and limitations of Maximum Entropy for finite images.  
- Recommendation: Accept — paper provides a significant, surprising, and well-supported contribution.  
- Human evaluation suggestion: Add a small-scale psychophysical study (e.g., 2AFC) to validate perceptual claims beyond VGG-loss.  
- Visualization suggestion: Show multiple synthesis samples per texture to illustrate variability differences between models.  
- Efficiency suggestion: Quantify computational cost (synthesis time or FLOPs) comparing random multi-scale models to VGG for practical context.  
- Questions for authors: Did you try different optimizers/initializations for VGG synthesis; what architectural properties cause over-constrained reconstruction in deeper random nets; how might a principled/perceptual metric be learned?
