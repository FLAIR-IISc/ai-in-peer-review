### Summary

This paper investigates the necessary components for parametric texture synthesis based on the statistics of neural network feature activations, a framework popularized by Gatys et al. (2015a). The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical, and pre-trained networks are essential for this task. The paper's main contribution is to demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures of high perceptual quality. Through extensive experiments, the authors show that these simple models, particularly a multi-scale random filter variant, can often rival or even surpass the quality of textures generated from the deep VGG-19 network, especially for textures with regular structures. The paper also provides an insightful discussion on the strengths and weaknesses of Gram-matrix-based synthesis, introduces the VGG-loss as a quantitative quality metric, and thoughtfully examines the crucial trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability in generative texture models.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a surprising and impactful finding, backed by compelling visual evidence and a thorough ablation study. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Strong Counter-intuitive Result:** The central claim that a single layer of random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis provides a powerful new baseline for the field. It fundamentally questions what architectural properties (depth, pre-training) are truly necessary for capturing texture statistics, prompting a re-evaluation of the role of deep hierarchies in this specific generative task.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Analysis and Discussion:** The paper systematically explores a variety of single-layer architectures and provides a clear analysis of the factors that do matter, such as the number of filters, the use of multiple scales, and the presence of non-linearities. The discussion section is particularly valuable, as it thoughtfully addresses the difficult and often-overlooked problem of evaluation, the challenges of the optimization landscape, and the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's core strength lies in its effective deconstruction of the state-of-the-art texture synthesis model. By showing that neither depth nor learned features are indispensable, the authors isolate the core mechanisms at play. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling; the textures generated by the multi-scale random model are often perceptually indistinguishable from or superior to those from the much more complex VGG-19 model.

A particularly insightful finding is that the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow models can lead them to find better solutions, even when measured by the VGG-19 loss itself (as noted for the grid textures). This is a subtle but important point about the interplay between model capacity and optimization difficulty. The authors support this claim with an experiment in the appendix, showing that initializing the VGG-19 optimization with a result from a shallow model improves the final outcome, strengthening their argument.

Furthermore, the discussion on the trade-off between "perceptual similarity" and "variability" is excellent. The authors correctly point out that simply matching the summary statistics of a single reference image is not the ultimate goal; the true goal is to model the underlying distribution of the texture. While the paper does not solve the problem of measuring variability, its clear articulation of the challenge and its limitations is a valuable contribution to the community.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

The primary limitation of the work is the lack of a systematic, quantitative evaluation of the generated textures by human observers. The evaluation relies on the authors' qualitative assessment and the proposed VGG-loss metric. While VGG-loss is a reasonable proxy, the paper itself demonstrates its imperfections. A psychophysical study, where subjects are asked to distinguish between real and synthesized textures (or to rate the quality of syntheses from different models), would provide much stronger evidence for the claims of perceptual quality and would make the paper's conclusions more definitive. The authors themselves allude to such an evaluation (e.g., an inpainting task), and including even a small-scale study would significantly bolster the work.

The paper mentions that for some over-constrained models, the synthesis can exactly reconstruct the reference texture. This touches on the critical issue of whether the model is learning a texture distribution or simply memorizing an instance. It would be beneficial to include a more direct analysis of sample diversity. For instance, generating multiple samples from the same reference texture (starting from different random noise seeds) and showing them side-by-side would give a clearer picture of the variability captured by the different models.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Your work highlights that the goal is to model a texture *distribution*, not just reproduce a single example. However, a sufficiently powerful set of shallow statistics could also perfectly reconstruct a reference image, thus exhibiting zero variability. How can you be certain that your best-performing models (e.g., random multi-scale) are not simply operating in a regime that is very close to this "trivial reproduction" case, especially for the highly regular textures where they excel?
2.  You convincingly argue that the VGG optimization landscape is more difficult than that of your shallow models. Could you provide more intuition on why this is the case? Is it purely a function of depth and the number of parameters, or are there other properties of the pre-trained VGG features that contribute to the challenging, non-convex nature of the loss surface?
3.  You state that you experimented with various hierarchical (deep) random-filter networks but found they did not improve synthesis quality over the single-layer multi-scale model. This is a very interesting result that supports your core thesis. Could you provide more details on the architectures you tested and the results you observed?
