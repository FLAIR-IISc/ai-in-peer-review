### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that the success of recent methods, such as Gatys et al. (2015a), is owed to the deep hierarchical structure and supervisedly learned features of networks like VGG. The authors systematically deconstruct this model and propose a surprisingly effective and simple baseline: a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters. Through extensive empirical evaluation, the paper demonstrates that this minimal model, particularly one using multi-scale random filters, can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with, and sometimes superior to, those produced by the 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network. The core contributions are providing a strong, simple baseline for texture synthesis and showing that depth and learned features may not be as indispensable for this task as previously believed.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Insightful Minimalist Investigation:** The paper provides a clear and valuable insight by questioning the necessity of complex, deep, and pre-trained models for texture synthesis. The finding that a single layer of random filters can produce high-quality results is surprising and important, forcing a re-evaluation of what makes deep generative models effective for this task.
2.  **Strong Empirical Baseline:** The work establishes a simple yet powerful baseline (the multi-scale random filter model) that future research in parametric texture synthesis should be compared against. The systematic experiments comparing various filter types (random, k-means, Fourier) and architectures are thorough and effectively support the main claims.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its direct and effective challenge to conventional wisdom. The authors systematically isolate key architectural components—depth, pooling, and learned weights—and demonstrate that none are strictly necessary for generating compelling textures. The visual results in Figure 1 and Figure 4 are striking; the single-layer random multi-scale model produces textures that are qualitatively on par with the much more complex VGG-based model, and in cases of highly regular patterns (e.g., brick walls, grids), they appear perceptually superior. This suggests that the core mechanism for this style of texture synthesis is the matching of second-order statistics (the Gram matrix) in a sufficiently rich, overcomplete feature space, which does not necessarily need to be learned.

The analysis of filter scale in Figure 2 provides a compelling argument for why the multi-scale approach is effective. It clearly illustrates that different filter sizes capture statistics at different spatial frequencies, and their combination is crucial for modeling the full texture. This is a well-supported and intuitive conclusion.

However, it is important to contextualize the claims. While the proposed model rivals the specific VGG-based parametric approach, the samples from both models are still visually imperfect and exhibit noticeable artifacts compared to the original textures. They are also qualitatively inferior to results from some non-parametric methods. Furthermore, the inpainting example in Figure 7 suggests that the VGG-based model may have an advantage in capturing more complex, non-local structural information, a point which tempers the strong conclusion that deep representations are not useful. Despite this, the paper's core finding—that a surprisingly simple model works remarkably well—remains valid and highly valuable to the community.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are meant to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision.
*   The conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not "necessary" could be nuanced. Your best-performing model uses large filters (up to 55x55), which are computationally expensive. A deep architecture can be seen as a computationally efficient method for achieving large receptive fields and multi-scale analysis. Your claim would be stronger if the discussion more thoroughly engaged with this efficiency trade-off. You mention experimenting with hierarchical random networks without success; including more details about these experiments (e.g., architectures, results) in the appendix would be very valuable, as it directly addresses this alternative hypothesis.
*   The paper introduces the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric, which is a good step towards more objective evaluation. However, you also show cases where it fails to align with human perception (e.g., for regular grid structures). It would be beneficial to explicitly state these limitations of the proposed metric in the main text to give the reader a more balanced view of its utility.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. It touches upon a critical and often overlooked aspect of generative modeling. This could be highlighted even more as a key direction for future work.

### Questions for the Authors
*   In your discussion, you state that you ran experiments with hierarchical random networks that were faster but did not improve synthesis quality. Could you provide more details on the architectures you tested? This is a crucial point for validating your claim that depth is not required for quality, only for efficiency.
*   The inpainting result in Appendix Figure 7 is intriguing. The VGG-based synthesis seems to produce a more coherent completion. Does this suggest that while random filters suffice for capturing stationary texture statistics, learned hierarchical features are better at modeling more complex spatial relationships and contextual information?
*   Could you elaborate on the relationship between your multi-scale single-layer model and a deep hierarchical model? One could argue a deep CNN is simply a more parameter- and computationally-efficient method for implementing a multi-scale analysis. How do your findings fit within this perspective?
