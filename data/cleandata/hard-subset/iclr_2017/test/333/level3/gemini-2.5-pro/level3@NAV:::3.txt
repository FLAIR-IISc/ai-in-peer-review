### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper presents a compelling investigation into the core components required for parametric texture synthesis using deep neural networks. Building on the seminal work of Gatys et al., which established the effectiveness of Gram matrix statistics from deep features, this work systematically questions the necessity of network depth, hierarchical representations, and supervised pre-training. The central and surprising finding is that a very shallow architecture—a single convolutional layer with a random, untrained filter bank—can generate textures of remarkably high perceptual quality. For certain regular textures, these simple models are shown to visually outperform the much deeper VGG-19 network. The paper provides a valuable discussion on the strengths and limitations of Gram-matrix-based methods, including an insightful analysis of the optimization landscape, suggesting that shallow models may benefit from a simpler objective function.

**Recommendation: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are (1) its significant and counter-intuitive main finding that challenges prevailing assumptions about the necessity of depth and learned features for texture synthesis, and (2) its insightful analysis of the optimization landscape, which contributes to a deeper understanding of deep generative models.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's strength lies in its clear and impactful core result. By demonstrating that a single layer of random filters is sufficient for high-quality synthesis, it establishes a strong and simple baseline for future work in this area. This finding forces a re-evaluation of why deep, pre-trained models work well, suggesting that much of their power may derive from the general convolutional structure and the statistical summary of Gram matrices, rather than from learned hierarchical features alone.

Furthermore, the analysis of the optimization process is a valuable contribution. The observation that optimizing with a shallow network can paradoxically achieve a lower VGG-19 loss than optimizing with VGG-19 directly (as noted for Figure 4) is a fascinating result. It highlights the difficulty of navigating the optimization landscape of very deep models. This claim is further substantiated by supplementary results showing that VGG-19 synthesis can be improved by initializing it with the output of a shallow model, providing strong evidence for the authors' hypothesis.

**Additional Feedback and Questions:**

While the visual results are strong, the paper's primary limitation is the lack of a systematic, quantitative evaluation of synthesis quality and diversity. The reliance on qualitative visual assessment and the VGG-loss proxy makes it difficult to rigorously compare methods or conclude that the models are capturing a true texture distribution rather than trivially reproducing the reference sample. The authors astutely discuss this limitation, correctly identifying that the goal is to model a distribution, not just match statistics for one image. A psychophysical study, as the authors suggest for future work, would have significantly strengthened the paper's claims.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify their thoughts on the following:
*   Could you elaborate on the trade-off between perceptual quality and sample diversity in your framework? Does the simpler optimization landscape of shallow models inadvertently favor solutions with lower variability that are perceptually closer to the reference image, thus explaining their superior performance on highly regular textures?
*   How might one design a practical, quantitative metric (short of a full psychophysical study) to measure sample diversity? While entropy is impractical, would a measure based on the average distance between multiple generated samples provide a useful signal?
