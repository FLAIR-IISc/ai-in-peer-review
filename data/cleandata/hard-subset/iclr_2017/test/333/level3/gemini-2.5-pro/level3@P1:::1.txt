### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that the success of recent methods, such as Gatys et al. (2015a), relies on deep, hierarchical representations and features learned from large-scale image recognition tasks. Through a series of systematic experiments, the paper demonstrates that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters and no pooling—is capable of generating textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from the 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network. The key contributions are the proposal of this strong and simple baseline, the empirical evidence that depth and learned features are not indispensable for this task, and a thoughtful discussion on evaluating texture quality and the theoretical underpinnings of parametric models.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The work provides a surprising, insightful, and valuable contribution to the understanding of generative texture models. Its two key strengths are:
1.  It compellingly argues against the necessity of deep hierarchies and learned features for texture synthesis by demonstrating the remarkable effectiveness of a much simpler model. This finding forces a re-evaluation of what components are truly fundamental to the task.
2.  It establishes a strong, minimal baseline (the multi-scale random filter model) against which future, more complex parametric texture synthesis models should be compared.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous empirical validation. The authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art VGG-based model and test the importance of its core components. The experiments comparing various single-layer architectures (Random, K-means, PCA, Fourier) are well-designed and lead to the striking conclusion that a sufficient number of random filters can perform on par with or even exceed filters learned from natural image statistics.

The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 provide strong support for the central claim. The textures generated by the multi-scale random model are of high quality and, particularly for textures with regular or repeating structures (e.g., the brick wall), are often visually superior to the VGG-based syntheses. The analysis in Figure 2, which ablates the contribution of different filter scales, convincingly demonstrates that the combination of multiple scales is crucial for capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, a task previously attributed to the hierarchical nature of deep networks.

Furthermore, the paper's discussion is nuanced and thoughtful. The authors correctly identify the limitations of their proposed VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric and engage with the deeper theoretical challenges of balancing perceptual similarity and sample variability within the Maximum Entropy framework. This adds significant depth to what could have been a purely empirical paper.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not change my overall positive assessment.

1.  **Nuance on the Role of Hierarchy:** The paper's conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not "necessary" is well-supported. However, it could be slightly overstated. As the authors themselves note, a deep architecture is a more efficient way to achieve large receptive fields than using very large convolutional filters. The appendix (Figure 7) also hints that the VGG-based model may produce more coherent results on an inpainting task. This suggests that while a deep hierarchy might not be strictly necessary for matching the second-order statistics used in this texture model, it may still be beneficial for capturing more complex, long-range structural dependencies or simply for computational efficiency. A more nuanced conclusion might state that while a surprisingly powerful texture model can be built *without* depth, the role of hierarchy as an efficient mechanism for building multi-scale representations remains important.

2.  **Context of Overall Synthesis Quality:** The paper rightly shows that its simple model can rival the VGG-based *parametric* model. However, it would be beneficial to briefly acknowledge that samples from both models, while impressive, still exhibit artifacts and do not achieve the fidelity of state-of-the-art *non-parametric* methods (e.g., patch-based synthesis). This would help to better contextualize the work within the broader field of texture synthesis and manage reader expectations.

3.  **Optimization Details:** The detail about scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 to prevent early stopping is crucial for reproducibility. It might be helpful to provide a brief sentence of intuition as to why the default L-BFGS-B optimizer fails without this scaling. Is it due to extremely small gradient magnitudes in this particular loss landscape?

**Questions for Authors**

1.  The multi-scale random model achieves its success through a bank of filters of varying sizes, including computationally expensive 55x55 filters. A deep network with smaller filters can achieve a similarly large receptive field more efficiently. Have you experimented with a deep (e.g., 3-5 layer) network with *random, untrained* weights? Would such a model achieve comparable quality to your single-layer multi-scale model but with greater computational efficiency? This could help disentangle the benefits of a large receptive field from the benefits of a learned, deep hierarchy.

2.  In the inpainting results in Appendix A (Figure 7), the VGG-based model appears to generate a more structurally coherent completion for the "pebbles" texture. This seems to contradict the idea that the VGG model's strengths are not essential. Do you agree with this qualitative assessment, and if so, does it suggest that the learned, deep features of VGG are indeed better at capturing certain types of higher-order statistics or long-range consistency that are not fully captured by the Gram matrix statistics of the random multi-scale model?

3.  The paper focuses on matching Gram matrices, which capture second-order statistics of the feature maps. Do you believe the core conclusion—that a shallow, random network is sufficient—would hold if the model were extended to match higher-order statistics of the feature activations?
