This paper presents a simple yet surprisingly powerful baseline for parametric texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical, and pre-trained networks are essential for this task. The authors demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters can generate textures whose quality often rivals that of state-of-the-art models based on the VGG network. The core contribution is the finding that merely matching the second-order statistics (Gram matrices) of a sufficiently rich, albeit random, filter bank is enough to capture the perceptual essence of many natural textures. This work provides a valuable and thought-provoking data point for the field of generative modeling.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. The key reasons for my decision are:
1.  It introduces a strong, simple, and important baseline that forces the community to reconsider the indispensable components for texture synthesis.
2.  The work is highly insightful, sparking a critical discussion about whether the success of deep generative models stems from learned hierarchical features or from the power of matching statistics in a high-dimensional feature space.

The primary strength of this paper lies in its compelling empirical results, which show a minimal model performing on par with a much more complex one. This finding elegantly isolates the statistical matching process as a core mechanism for success. However, the conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for texture synthesis may be too strong and requires further validation. For instance, the VGG-based model appears to perform better on the inpainting task shown in Figure 7, suggesting that hierarchical representations might be superior for capturing non-local context and structure. Additionally, it is important to acknowledge that the samples generated by both the proposed model and the VGG baseline are still imperfect and perceptually inferior to those from leading non-parametric methods, which provides important context for the paper’s claims.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest nuancing the claims regarding the role of deep hierarchies. While your model demonstrates they are not strictly *necessary* for impressive results, they might offer other advantages. This leads to my questions for the authors:

1.  Your multi-scale model achieves success by explicitly using filters of many different sizes. Could a hierarchical model achieve similar multi-scale receptive fields more efficiently? Could it be that the primary benefit of depth is computational efficiency for capturing multi-scale structure, rather than the learned nature of the features?
2.  How do you reconcile your conclusion about deep hierarchies with the VGG model's stronger performance on inpainting? Does this not suggest that for tasks requiring more contextual understanding beyond stationary statistics, a hierarchical representation remains advantageous?
