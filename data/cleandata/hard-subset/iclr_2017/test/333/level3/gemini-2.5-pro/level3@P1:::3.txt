### Summary

This paper investigates the necessary components for parametric texture synthesis, building upon the successful framework of Gatys et al. (2015a) which uses summary statistics (Gram matrices) from deep, pre-trained convolutional networks. The authors question the necessity of two commonly assumed critical elements: network depth and supervised pre-training of the features. The paper's primary and surprising contribution is to demonstrate that high-quality, naturalistic textures can be synthesized using a remarkably simple model: a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. Through a series of well-designed experiments, the authors compare various shallow architectures (using random, Fourier, or unsupervisedly learned filters) and show that a multi-scale random filter bank can generate textures that are often visually on par with, and sometimes superior to, those produced by the deep VGG-19 network. The paper also provides a valuable discussion on the limitations of the Gram-matrix-based synthesis paradigm, including the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative, albeit imperfect, metric for texture quality.

### Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:

1.  The central finding—that a single layer of random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis—is a simple, surprising, and significant result. It challenges the prevailing intuition in the community that deep, hierarchical, and learned feature representations are indispensable for such generative tasks.
2.  The paper provides a strong set of empirical results and complements them with an insightful discussion about the underlying mechanisms and limitations of this class of texture models, including the crucial role of the optimization process and the challenge of evaluating sample diversity.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its clear and impactful core message. The work by Gatys et al. was groundbreaking, but it left open the question of *why* it worked so well. This paper provides a compelling answer by systematically removing components. The demonstration that depth and learning are not strictly necessary is a valuable contribution to our understanding. The visual results presented in Figure 1 and Figure 4 are convincing; the "Random multi-scale" model, in particular, produces textures that are highly competitive, especially for regular patterns like brick walls and grids.

Furthermore, the authors uncover a fascinating subtlety in the optimization process. The observation that synthesizing an image to match a shallow random network's statistics can result in an image with a *lower* VGG-based loss than directly optimizing the VGG loss itself is a very interesting result. This, along with the finding that VGG-based synthesis can be improved by initializing from the shallow model's output (Appendix A), strongly suggests that the optimization landscape for deeper models is significantly more challenging. This insight is valuable beyond the specific application of texture synthesis and speaks to broader challenges in optimizing deep generative models.

Finally, the discussion in Section 7 is thoughtful and mature. The authors correctly identify the fundamental tension between achieving "perceptual similarity" to a reference texture and capturing the "variability" of the underlying texture distribution. They candidly acknowledge the limitations of their evaluation and point towards the need for more robust perceptual metrics and psychophysical experiments, which grounds the paper's claims appropriately.

### Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, its claims regarding the quality of the synthesized textures could be made more rigorous. The primary weakness is the reliance on qualitative visual inspection and an imperfect quantitative metric (the VGG-loss).

1.  **Lack of Perceptual Evaluation:** The core claim is about generating textures of "high perceptual quality." While the figures are illustrative, visual assessment by the authors is subjective. The paper would be substantially strengthened by a controlled psychophysical experiment. For example, a two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task where human subjects are asked to identify which of two synthesized images (e.g., one from the proposed random multi-scale model and one from the VGG-based model) is more similar to a reference texture would provide rigorous, quantitative evidence to support the claims of competitive or superior performance.

2.  **Modeling a Distribution vs. Reproducing an Exemplar:** The current methodology matches the statistics of a single exemplar image. A key desideratum of a texture model is to capture the underlying distribution from which that exemplar was drawn. The paper touches upon this as "variability" but does not experimentally investigate it. While difficult, showing a diversity of high-quality samples from different random initializations would help allay concerns that the model is simply finding a very good reconstruction of the reference image rather than sampling from a broader texture model. Trivial statistics (e.g., a very large number of random filters) could, in principle, perfectly constrain the solution to be the original image, which misses the point of generative modeling. A more explicit characterization of the diversity would be beneficial.

These points are suggestions for strengthening the work and do not undermine the core contribution, which is already sufficient for publication.

### Questions for Authors

1.  The paper highlights the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. Could you comment on the variability of the textures generated by your random multi-scale model? Figure 3 shows some variability, but across multiple runs with different random seeds, how diverse are the resulting textures? Is there a risk that the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow model leads it to consistently find very similar optima, thus exhibiting less diversity than a more complex model like VGG?

2.  Your finding that optimizing a shallow-model loss can yield a better VGG-loss than directly optimizing the VGG-loss is very interesting. Do you have a clearer hypothesis for why this occurs? Is it primarily about avoiding poor local minima in the VGG loss landscape, or could it be that the gradients from the shallow network provide a form of implicit regularization that is beneficial for the VGG metric?

3.  For the random multi-scale model, how were the number of filters and their respective sizes chosen? How sensitive is the model's performance to these hyperparameters? For instance, does performance degrade gracefully if fewer large filters are used?
