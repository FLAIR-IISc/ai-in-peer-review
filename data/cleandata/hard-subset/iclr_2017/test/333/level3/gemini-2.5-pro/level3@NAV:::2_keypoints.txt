- Core claim: A single convolutional layer with random filters can synthesize natural textures often perceptually competitive with Gatys et al.’s deep, pre-trained approach.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Rationale: Findings are impactful, counter-intuitive, and supported by rigorous empirical analysis.
- Contribution: Provides a strong minimal baseline and prompts re-evaluation of which architectural components are essential for texture generation.
- Experimental strength: Systematic experiments and ablations on filter types (random, learned, hand-crafted), feature map counts, and filter scales convincingly isolate important factors.
- Key technical insight: Multi-scale filters and non-linearities are crucial, while depth and supervised feature learning are less critical.
- Significant result: A shallow network with random filters can rival outputs from a deep pre-trained VGG network.
- Critique: Quantitative evaluation relies on a VGG-based loss (potentially circular and biased), weakening quantitative claims despite strong qualitative results.
- Suggested improvement: Incorporate human perceptual studies or independent metrics to validate texture quality objectively.
- Noted insight/future direction: The trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability is important and promising to explore further.
- Questions for authors: (1) Clarify potential biases of using a VGG-based loss and other independent metrics considered. (2) Explain why scaling the loss by a large factor was necessary and sensitivity to that hyperparameter.
