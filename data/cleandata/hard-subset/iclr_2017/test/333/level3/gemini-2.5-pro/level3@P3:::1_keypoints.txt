- Paper shows a single-layer, no-pooling, random-filter multi-scale model can synthesize textures often rivaling 19-layer VGG-based methods.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strong baseline: a simple single-layer random-filter model is surprisingly effective and serves as an important reference.  
- Challenges conventional wisdom: depth, pooling, and learned features may not be essential for parametric texture synthesis.  
- Empirical support: multi-scale random model produces high-quality textures and can outperform VGG on regular, large-scale structures (e.g., brick wall).  
- Interpretation: Gatys-style success may come from capturing rich multi-scale stationary feature statistics rather than semantic hierarchical features.  
- Limitations: both parametric models produce imperfect samples that are visibly inferior to classic non-parametric methods.  
- Counterexample: inpainting results (Fig. 7) suggest VGG may better capture non-local structure and long-range consistency.  
- Recommendation: nuance claims about hierarchy — discuss scenarios where hierarchical representations provide benefits.  
- Suggestion: compare to a shallow hierarchical (2–3 layer) model to separate effects of receptive field size from single-layer architecture.  
- Contextualization: acknowledge the quality gap to non-parametric texture synthesis methods.  
- Evaluation: consider a small human perceptual study since VGG-loss can give paradoxical quantitative results.
