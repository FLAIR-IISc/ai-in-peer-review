### **Summary**

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for generating high-quality natural textures using parametric models based on convolutional neural network features. The authors systematically challenge the prevailing assumption that the success of recent state-of-the-art methods, such as that of Gatys et al. (2015a), relies critically on deep, hierarchical architectures and features learned from large-scale natural image datasets (e.g., ImageNet). Through a comprehensive set of experiments, the paper presents the surprising and significant finding that a single convolutional layer with no pooling and, most remarkably, random, data-independent filters is sufficient to synthesize textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a deep, pre-trained VGG-19 network. The authors thoroughly explore various single-layer architectures, comparing random filters against hand-crafted (Fourier) and unsupervisedly learned (K-means, PCA) filters, and demonstrate that a multi-scale random filter bank performs exceptionally well. The work also contributes a thoughtful discussion on the limitations of current texture synthesis frameworks, particularly regarding the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and proposes a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG model's loss.

### **Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper for two primary reasons:

1.  **Surprising and Impactful Main Result:** The paper's core finding—that depth and learned features are not indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis—is counter-intuitive and provides a crucial new perspective to the field of generative image modeling. It establishes an incredibly simple yet powerful baseline that future work must be compared against.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Experimental Analysis:** The authors do not simply make their claim but support it with a systematic and convincing empirical study. The ablation of different filter types, filter numbers, and scales provides strong evidence for their conclusions and deepens the community's understanding of what makes these models work.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its clear and focused research question: what is truly essential for texture synthesis? Instead of proposing a more complex model, the authors deconstruct the current state-of-the-art to find its minimal core. This leads to their main, surprising discovery. The fact that a single-layer network with random filters can capture complex, long-range correlations in textures (e.g., the pebble and wall textures in Fig 1) fundamentally challenges the narrative that deep, hierarchical feature extractors are required to model such structures. This finding forces a re-evaluation of the role of depth and pre-training in this domain, suggesting they may contribute more to computational efficiency (by building large receptive fields cheaply) or fine-tuning rather than being a fundamental necessity.

The experimental methodology is sound and thorough. The authors systematically compare a wide array of single-layer models, isolating the contributions of different filter-learning strategies (random, unsupervised, hand-crafted). The results convincingly show that having a sufficiently large number of filters is important, but their origin (random vs. learned) is less critical. The investigation into multi-scale filters (Fig 2) is particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating that combining filters of different sizes is key to capturing structures at all scales simultaneously, which a single-scale model cannot do. This analysis provides a compelling explanation for the success of their best-performing random model.

Finally, the discussion section is a significant contribution in itself. The authors' reflections on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and their critique of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images, are sophisticated and important. They correctly point out that simply matching statistics is not enough, and that the choice of a perceptual metric and the allowed deviation (the "epsilon-environment") are critical components of a texture model. This provides a valuable conceptual framework for thinking about future research in this area.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help further improve it. These are suggestions for refinement and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

1.  **Evaluation Metric:** The use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric for perceptual quality is a clever and pragmatic choice. However, as the authors acknowledge, it is not perfect and is inherently biased towards the very model they are trying to establish a baseline against. The paradoxical result where the multi-scale model sometimes achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG model itself highlights this limitation. While a full psychophysical study is likely out of scope, even a small-scale human evaluation (e.g., asking subjects to pick the more realistic synthesis in a two-alternative forced-choice task) on a subset of the textures would have provided a much stronger and more objective validation of the claim that the synthesized textures "rival" the state-of-the-art.

2.  **Importance of Optimization:** The authors mention in passing that the discrepancy with earlier reports (where random networks failed) might be due to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically scaling the loss and gradients. This seems like a critical detail that enables their entire result. It might be beneficial to highlight this point more prominently, perhaps with a small ablation study in the appendix showing synthesis with and without this scaling, to underscore its importance for practitioners seeking to reproduce these findings.

3.  **Visualizing Variability:** The paper discusses the importance of variability between generated samples, but does not visualize it. Including a figure (perhaps in the appendix) that shows multiple different samples generated from the same source texture using both the random multi-scale model and the VGG model would be very illustrative. This would help readers visually assess the diversity of outputs from each model and better appreciate the similarity-variability trade-off being discussed.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the instances where your single-layer multi-scale model achieved a lower VGG-based synthesis loss than the VGG model that directly optimizes this loss? Do you believe this is primarily due to the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow network, allowing L-BFGS to find a better minimum, or does it point to a more fundamental issue with the expressivity or constraints of the deeper model?

2.  Your work focuses entirely on texture synthesis. The model of Gatys et al. (2015b) was famously also applied to artistic style transfer, which uses the same Gram matrix-based style representation. Have you performed any preliminary experiments to see if a single-layer random network can also replicate artistic style effectively when combined with a content loss term?

3.  The paper argues that deep architectures may primarily offer computational efficiency for creating large receptive fields. Did you measure the practical runtime difference between synthesizing a texture with your best multi-scale model (with large 55x55 filters) versus the VGG-19 model? This would provide a concrete data point for the trade-off between model simplicity and computational performance.
