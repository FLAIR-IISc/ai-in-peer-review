- Core finding: a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters can synthesize high-quality textures that often rival deeper pretrained networks (e.g., VGG).  
- This result challenges the assumption that deep, hierarchical, pre-trained features are necessary for parametric texture synthesis.  
- The paper systematically deconstructs Gatys et al. (2015a) to establish a strong minimal baseline.  
- Extensive experiments compare filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned), filter counts, and scales.  
- Multi-scale filters are crucial to capture both fine-grained detail and long-range structure; a single large filter is insufficient.  
- Non-linearities (ReLU) and a sufficient number of feature maps are indispensable for good synthesis.  
- The paper introduces a VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric to supplement subjective visual evaluation.  
- The VGG-loss metric is imperfect and can fail to capture perceptual advantages of the simpler model on highly structured textures.  
- The “less is more” finding has important practical implications, suggesting complex learned features may be less critical for texture statistics.  
- Reviewer recommendation: Accept, based on the significance of the finding and the thorough, methodical analysis.  
- Suggestion: authors should elaborate on optimization details (loss/gradient scaling), which may explain why random-weight synthesis succeeded here but failed in prior work.  
- Recommendation for future work: develop more robust perceptual metrics that balance perceptual similarity and sample variability.  
- Question: Is the VGG-loss landscape harder to optimize due to depth, pooling, or other architectural factors?  
- Question: Did the authors experiment with optimizing to non-zero loss thresholds or analyze sample diversity within a VGG-loss bound to formalize the perceptual-equivalence framing?
