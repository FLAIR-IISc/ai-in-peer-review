- Paper shows a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters (no pooling) can synthesize high-quality textures, challenging the need for deep learned features.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key strength: Compelling evidence that deep hierarchical representations and learned features are not strictly necessary for parametric texture synthesis.  
- Key strength: Establishes a strong, minimal baseline (multi-scale random filter model) for future work.  
- Experiments systematically deconstruct VGG-based models and compare single-layer variants (Random, K-means, PCA, Fourier), showing random filters can match or exceed learned filters.  
- Visual results (Figs. 1 & 4) demonstrate the random multi-scale model produces high-quality textures and can outperform VGG on regular/repeating structures.  
- Ablation (Fig. 2) shows that combining multiple filter scales is crucial for capturing both fine detail and long-range structure.  
- Authors provide a thoughtful discussion criticizing VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric and exploring perceptual similarity, sample variability, and Maximum Entropy theory.  
- Suggestion: Temper the claim that hierarchy is unnecessary—deep nets provide an efficient way to achieve large receptive fields and may aid coherence (e.g., inpainting).  
- Suggestion: Acknowledge both parametric models still exhibit artifacts and do not match fidelity of state-of-the-art non-parametric (patch-based) methods.  
- Suggestion: Clarify optimization detail (scaling loss/gradients by 1e7) and explain why L-BFGS-B fails without it for reproducibility.  
- Question: Have you tried a deep (3–5 layer) network with random weights to achieve large receptive fields more efficiently and compare quality/compute?  
- Question: Does the VGG model’s more coherent inpainting (Appendix Fig. 7, "pebbles") indicate learned deep features better capture higher-order or long-range statistics?  
- Question: Would the core conclusion hold if the model matched higher-order feature statistics instead of only Gram (second-order) matrices?
