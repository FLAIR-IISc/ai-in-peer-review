### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for generating high-quality natural textures using parametric models based on neural network features. The work challenges the prevailing assumption in the field—popularized by the successful texture synthesis method of Gatys et al. (2015a)—that deep, hierarchical architectures and features learned via supervised object recognition are indispensable for this task.

The central and surprising contribution is the demonstration that a very simple model, consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters, is sufficient to synthesize textures of high perceptual quality. These results often rival those produced by the 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network. The authors conduct a thorough and systematic empirical analysis, exploring a range of single-layer architectures with varying numbers of filters, filter sizes (including a multi-scale approach), and filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA). Furthermore, the paper introduces a quantitative evaluation method using the synthesis loss of the VGG-based model as a proxy for perceptual quality, providing a more objective measure than qualitative inspection alone.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a compelling and counter-intuitive result: that depth and learned features are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The success of a single-layer network with random filters provides a powerful new baseline and forces a re-evaluation of why deep generative models are effective, suggesting their power may stem more from the multi-scale filter bank architecture than from the semantic content of the learned features.

2.  **Thorough and Methodical Investigation:** The claims are supported by a rigorous and extensive set of experiments. The authors systematically dissect the problem by comparing different filter initializations, network capacities (number of channels), and the crucial role of filter scale. This careful analysis provides strong evidence for their conclusions and offers valuable insights into which components (e.g., multi-scale receptive fields, non-linearities) are truly critical.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The main strength of this work lies in its "less is more" approach. By questioning the necessity of complex, deep, pre-trained models, the authors uncover a fundamental insight. The fact that a random multi-scale filter bank, when combined with a Gram matrix-based statistical summary, can reproduce complex, long-range structures in textures is a significant finding. This simplifies our understanding of parametric texture modeling and opens up new avenues for research that do not depend on large, pre-trained networks.

The empirical analysis is exemplary. The paper does not just present its best model but carefully builds its case. The comparison between models with 363 and 3267 feature maps clearly shows the effect of model capacity. The experiments in Figure 2, which isolate the contribution of different filter scales, convincingly argue for the importance of a multi-scale representation, which is perhaps the most critical architectural element identified. The inclusion of an ablation study on the ReLU non-linearity further solidifies the authors' claims about the necessary components of the model. This systematic approach lends high credibility to the work and makes the results easy to interpret and build upon.

Finally, the discussion section is particularly insightful, touching upon the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, the practical limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images, and the connection between texture synthesis and building better quantitative models of human perception. This thoughtful analysis elevates the paper from a simple empirical result to a significant conceptual contribution.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the decision to accept.

*   **Human Evaluation:** The use of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric is a clever proxy for perceptual quality. However, as the authors rightly note, it is not perfect and is circular when comparing against the VGG model itself. The claims of "high perceptual quality" would be substantially strengthened by a small-scale psychophysical study. For instance, a simple two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task asking participants which of two synthesized images is a better match for a reference texture could provide direct evidence to support the visual examples.

*   **Visualizing Variability:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. To make this point more concrete, it would be helpful to include a figure showing multiple different samples generated from the same reference texture for the proposed multi-scale model and the VGG baseline. This would provide a qualitative sense of the diversity (or lack thereof) produced by each model.

*   **Computational Efficiency:** The discussion mentions that while hierarchical models are not necessary for quality, they are more computationally efficient than using large filters in a single layer. Quantifying this would be a useful addition. A brief comparison of synthesis times or floating-point operations (FLOPs) for the best random multi-scale model versus the VGG model would provide valuable context for practitioners.

### Questions for the Authors

*   The paper observes that for some textures, the VGG-based loss is lower for samples generated by your multi-scale model than for samples generated by the VGG model itself. This suggests the optimization of the deep VGG model is challenging. Did you experiment with different optimization algorithms (e.g., Adam) or initialization strategies for the VGG-based synthesis to see if this result holds?

*   You mention anecdotally that in deeper multi-layer random networks, the reference texture was sometimes perfectly reconstructed, indicating an over-constrained model. Could you elaborate on the architectural properties (e.g., depth, number of parameters) that led to this behavior?

*   The discussion on defining a texture model via a perceptual metric `d(x,y)` and an `ε`-environment is very insightful. Beyond using a fixed network like VGG, do you have thoughts on how such a metric might be learned or formalized in a more principled way?
