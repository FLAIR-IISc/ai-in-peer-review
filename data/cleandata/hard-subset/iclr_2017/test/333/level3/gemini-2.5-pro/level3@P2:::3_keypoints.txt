- Paper shows a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters can synthesize high-quality textures, challenging the need for deep, pre-trained networks.  
- Multi-scale random-filter variants often match or exceed VGG-19 results, especially on regular textures.  
- Authors analyze Gram-matrix-based synthesis, introduce VGG-loss as a quantitative metric, and discuss perceptual similarity vs. sample variability trade-offs.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: The counter-intuitive finding that shallow random filters suffice provides a strong new baseline and questions the role of depth and pre-training for texture synthesis.  
- Key reason 2: Thorough empirical study and ablation of single-layer architectures, examining filters, scales, non-linearities, and evaluation challenges.  
- Supporting: Visual results are compelling; shallow models can find better solutions due to a simpler optimization landscape.  
- Supporting: Initializing VGG optimization with a shallow-model result improves outcomes, reinforcing the optimization argument.  
- Supporting: Clear discussion emphasizing that matching one image’s statistics is not equivalent to modeling the underlying texture distribution.  
- Major weakness: No systematic human psychophysical evaluation to robustly validate perceptual quality claims.  
- Major weakness: Insufficient direct analysis of sample diversity—risk of models trivially reproducing reference images rather than capturing a distribution.  
- Requested clarification 1: How to ensure best models aren’t merely reproducing the reference image with zero variability?  
- Requested clarification 2: Why is the VGG optimization landscape harder—depth/parameters only, or properties of pre-trained features?  
- Requested clarification 3: More details on the hierarchical random-filter architectures tried and their (lack of) improvement over the single-layer multi-scale model.
