- Main finding: a single convolutional layer with no pooling and random filters can synthesize high-quality textures often competitive with VGG-19.  
- The paper systematically challenges the assumption that depth and ImageNet pretraining are essential for texture synthesis.  
- Multi-scale random filter banks perform exceptionally well by capturing structures at multiple scales simultaneously.  
- A sufficiently large number of filters is important; whether filters are random or learned matters less.  
- Depth and pretraining may primarily provide computational efficiency (large receptive fields) rather than fundamental modeling necessity.  
- The authors back claims with thorough ablation studies across filter types, counts, and scales.  
- The discussion emphasizes the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability and critiques Maximum Entropy for finite images.  
- The paper proposes a VGG-based quantitative metric for perceptual quality but acknowledges its bias toward the VGG model.  
- Recommendation: accept, based on the surprising, impactful result and rigorous experiments.  
- Suggestion: add a small-scale human psychophysical evaluation to more objectively validate perceptual claims.  
- Suggestion: highlight and ablate the optimization scaling/tuning that enabled random networks to succeed.  
- Suggestion: visualize sample variability by showing multiple synthesized outputs per texture for both models.  
- Question: why does the single-layer multi-scale model sometimes achieve lower VGG-loss than the VGG model—better optimization or a deeper issue with expressivity/constraints?  
- Question: can a single-layer random network replicate artistic style transfer when paired with a content loss?  
- Question: provide runtime comparisons between the multi-scale large-filter model and VGG-19 to quantify the computational trade-off.
