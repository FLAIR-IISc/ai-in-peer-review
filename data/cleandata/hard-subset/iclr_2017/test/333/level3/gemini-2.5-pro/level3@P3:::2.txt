### **Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. Challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical, and pre-trained networks (like VGG) are essential, the authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art model by Gatys et al. (2015a). The core contribution is the surprising and powerful demonstration that a very simple model—a single convolutional layer with no pooling and random, untrained filters—can generate natural textures of remarkable perceptual quality. These results often rival or even surpass those from much deeper, more complex architectures. The authors conduct a comprehensive analysis across various filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and scales, establishing a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis. Furthermore, the paper introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality, moving beyond purely subjective visual assessment.

### **Recommendation: Accept**

This paper should be accepted. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The central result—that a shallow network with random filters can generate high-quality textures—is a highly significant and counter-intuitive finding. It fundamentally challenges the community's understanding of what is necessary for this generative task and forces a re-evaluation of the role of deep, learned features.
2.  **Thorough and Methodical Analysis:** The authors support their claims with a rigorous and extensive set of experiments. By systematically testing a wide range of single-layer architectures and ablating components like filter type, filter count, and scale, they provide compelling evidence for their conclusions and establish a valuable new baseline.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this paper lies in its "less is more" approach. While much of the field was focused on adding complexity to generative models, this work provides a crucial insight by stripping the model down to its bare essentials. The finding that a single-layer, random, multi-scale network often performs on par with a 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network is a major contribution. This result is not only surprising but also has practical implications, suggesting that the complexity of deep learned features may be less critical for capturing textural statistics than previously believed.

The analysis is methodical and convincing. The authors do not simply present their best random model; they compare it against a variety of sensible alternatives, including filters derived from Fourier bases, K-means, and PCA. This thorough comparison strengthens their claim that randomness itself is sufficient and that complex feature learning is not a prerequisite. The investigation into filter scale (Fig. 2) is particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating why a multi-scale approach is crucial for capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, and that simply using a single large filter is insufficient. The paper correctly identifies that non-linearities (ReLU) and a sufficient number of feature maps are indispensable components, providing a nuanced picture of what truly matters.

Finally, the work is well-positioned in the literature. It directly addresses a central question left open by Gatys et al. (2015a) and provides a crucial counterpoint to the narrative that deep, supervised features are the key to unlocking natural image statistics.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

**Evaluation Metric:** I commend the authors for introducing a quantitative metric (the VGG-based loss) to supplement qualitative human inspection. This is a positive step towards more rigorous evaluation in the field. However, as the authors rightly point out, this metric is imperfect and can fail to capture the perceptual advantages of the simpler model on highly structured textures (e.g., brick walls, grids). The discussion on the trade-off between "perceptual similarity" and "variability" is excellent and points to a critical challenge. Developing more robust perceptual metrics that can quantify both of these aspects is a vital area for future work.

**Role of Optimization:** The paper notes that a discrepancy with earlier work that failed with random weights might originate from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically by scaling the loss and gradients. This seems like a critical detail that may have enabled the paper's success. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this point. Was this scaling factor the key difference that allowed the optimization to succeed where others failed? A brief ablation or discussion on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would strengthen the paper's practical reproducibility and impact.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Your VGG-loss evaluation reveals that for some regular textures, your multi-scale random model achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-based model that directly optimizes this loss. You suggest this points to the VGG optimization getting stuck in local minima. Do you have any further intuition as to why the VGG model's loss landscape is more difficult to optimize? Is it primarily depth, the use of pooling, or another architectural factor?

2.  In your discussion, you propose a more formal definition of a texture model based on a distance metric `d(x, y)` and an epsilon-environment of perceptually equivalent images. This is a very insightful framing. Did you perform any experiments to explore this idea, for example, by synthesizing textures by optimizing to a non-zero loss or by analyzing the diversity of samples found within a certain VGG-loss threshold?
