This paper proposes a generative model of natural textures based on a single-layer convolutional neural network with completely random filters. The authors demonstrate that this model can qualitatively capture the perceptual differences between natural textures and often rivals the current state-of-the-art. The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, as it addresses the question of what aspects of feature representation are crucial for natural image generation.

I decide to accept this paper, with the main reasons being the paper's ability to support its claims with thorough experiments and the significance of its findings. The paper provides a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis that solely relies on a single-layer network and random, data-independent filters. The authors also present a wide range of single-layer architectures with different filter sizes and types of filters, and they compare these models against the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al.

One of the key strengths of the paper is its thorough evaluation of the proposed model. The authors use a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model to replace the common-place evaluation of texture models through qualitative human inspection. They also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including the importance of combining multiple filter sizes in one model and the role of non-linearities in texture synthesis.

The paper also provides a formal generalization of maximum entropy models to account for the natural variability of textures with limited spatial extent. The authors discuss the limitations of the current approach and suggest potential avenues for future work, including the development of more robust measures of variability and the use of psychophysical assessments to evaluate the quality of synthesized textures.

One potential limitation of the paper is the lack of a systematic, quantifiable comparison across methods. While the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their proposed model, they do not provide a direct comparison with other state-of-the-art methods. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed model, particularly in comparison to deeper hierarchical architectures.

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational efficiency of the proposed model, including a comparison with deeper hierarchical architectures.
* Consider adding a direct comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as Gatys et al. and Ulyanov et al.
* Provide more details on the optimization procedure used to tune the parameters of the model.
* Consider adding more examples of synthesized textures to the paper, particularly for more complex textures.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the optimization procedure used to tune the parameters of the model?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of the current approach in terms of measuring variability and developing more robust measures of texture quality?
* Can you provide more examples of synthesized textures, particularly for more complex textures?
* How do you think the proposed model could be extended to more complex tasks, such as image generation or style transfer?
