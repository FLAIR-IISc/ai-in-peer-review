This paper proposes a simple yet effective baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer convolutional neural network with multi-scale random filters. The authors demonstrate that their approach can sometimes rival the state-of-the-art VGG-based models, which rely on a multi-layer, pre-trained architecture. The paper's main contribution is to show that texture synthesis may not require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps, challenging previous assumptions in the field.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper presents an interesting and insightful analysis of the role of deep hierarchical representations in texture synthesis. The authors' conclusion that these representations may not be necessary for high-quality texture synthesis is well-supported by their empirical results and has significant implications for the field. Secondly, the paper is well-written and clearly organized, making it easy to follow the authors' argument and understand their contributions.

The paper's strengths lie in its thorough evaluation of the proposed approach, which includes a comparison with the state-of-the-art VGG-based model. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the importance of multi-scale structure in texture synthesis and demonstrate that their single-layer model can capture this structure effectively. Additionally, the paper's discussion of the limitations of the VGG-based model and the potential benefits of using a single-layer architecture is well-reasoned and informative.

However, there are some limitations to the paper that prevent me from giving it a perfect score. Firstly, while the authors' conclusions about the role of deep hierarchical representations are intriguing, they require further validation. In particular, it would be useful to see a more systematic evaluation of the importance of these representations in texture synthesis, including a comparison with other architectures and techniques. Secondly, the paper's evaluation of the generated samples is limited to a visual inspection, which may not be sufficient to fully assess the quality of the synthesized textures. Finally, the authors note that the generated samples from both the VGG and single-layer models are imperfect and substantially worse than non-parametric methods, which suggests that there is still room for improvement in texture synthesis.

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following additional feedback. Firstly, it would be useful to provide more details about the optimization procedure used to train the single-layer model, as this may be an important factor in its success. Secondly, the authors could consider evaluating their approach on a wider range of textures and datasets, to demonstrate its generalizability. Finally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the implications of the authors' findings for the field of texture synthesis and beyond.
