- Proposes a generative model for natural textures using a single-layer CNN with completely random, data-independent filters.  
- Shows the model qualitatively captures perceptual differences in textures and often rivals state-of-the-art methods.  
- Well-motivated study that probes which aspects of feature representation are crucial for natural image generation.  
- Accepted because of thorough experiments and the significance of demonstrating a strong minimal baseline.  
- Presents a wide range of single-layer architectures (different filter sizes and types) and comparisons to Gatys et al.  
- Uses a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG synthesis loss instead of relying solely on human inspection.  
- Provides detailed analysis showing benefits of combining multiple filter sizes and the importance of non-linearities.  
- Offers a formal generalization of maximum entropy models to account for texture variability with limited spatial extent.  
- Acknowledges limitations and suggests future work on more robust variability measures and psychophysical evaluation.  
- Criticism: lacks systematic, quantifiable direct comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.  
- Criticism: insufficient analysis of computational efficiency compared to deeper hierarchical architectures.  
- Suggested improvements: add computational-efficiency analysis, direct comparisons to Gatys/Ulyanov, details on optimization, and more complex texture examples.  
- Questions for authors: clarify optimization procedure, plans for measuring variability and quality, provide more complex examples, and discuss extensions to image generation or style transfer.
