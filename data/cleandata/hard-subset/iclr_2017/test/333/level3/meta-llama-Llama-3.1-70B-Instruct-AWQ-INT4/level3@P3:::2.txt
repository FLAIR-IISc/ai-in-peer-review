The paper "What does it take to generate natural textures?" presents a thought-provoking analysis of the conditions that enable the generation of natural-looking textures. The authors' primary contribution is a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis that relies solely on a single-layer network and random, data-independent filters. This work challenges the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and supervised training of feature spaces are crucial for natural image generation.

I recommend accepting this paper, primarily due to its thorough analysis and surprising results. The authors' experiments demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated from networks with only a single layer, no pooling, and random filters. This finding is unexpected, as previous reports suggested that networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images.

The paper's strengths lie in its rigorous analysis and well-motivated approach. The authors carefully evaluate the performance of their single-layer model against the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al. (2015a) and provide a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model. The results show that the single-layer model often rivals the state-of-the-art approach, suggesting that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable for natural image generation as previously thought.

However, there is room for improvement in the evaluation methods. While the authors provide a quantitative texture quality measure, they also rely on human inspection to evaluate the quality of the synthesized textures. To strengthen their claims, they could consider using more objective evaluation metrics, such as the Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) or the Visual Information Fidelity (VIF) measure.

Additionally, the authors could provide more insights into the optimization procedure used to tune the single-layer model. They mention that a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure is responsible for the discrepancy between their results and previous reports, but a more detailed explanation would be helpful.

Some questions that I would like the authors to address in their response:

1. Can you provide more details on the optimization procedure used to tune the single-layer model? How did you arrive at the specific hyperparameters used in the experiments?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of variability in texture synthesis? The paper mentions that the single-layer model tends to favor perceptual similarity over variability, but a more comprehensive discussion of this trade-off would be helpful.
3. Can you comment on the potential applications of your single-layer model? Are there any specific use cases where this model would be particularly useful?

Overall, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of natural image generation and texture synthesis. Its thorough analysis and surprising results make it a valuable addition to the literature, and I believe it has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
