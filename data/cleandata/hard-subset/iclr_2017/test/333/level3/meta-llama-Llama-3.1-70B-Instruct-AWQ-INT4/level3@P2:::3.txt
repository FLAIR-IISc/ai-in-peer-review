This paper explores the problem of natural texture synthesis, specifically examining the necessary conditions for a deep neural network to generate high-quality textures. The authors build upon the work of Gatys et al., who demonstrated that deep-feature Gram matrix statistics can effectively characterize visual textures. The main contribution of this paper is the finding that very shallow networks, including single random filter banks, can perform surprisingly well in this texture-synthesis framework, sometimes visually outperforming the VGG-19 model on simple and regular textures.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper presents a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer network and random, data-independent filters. This is a significant contribution, as it challenges the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained feature spaces are necessary for natural image generation. Secondly, the paper provides an informative discussion of the strengths and limitations of Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis methods, highlighting the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis.

The paper's main finding is supported by a wide range of experiments, including comparisons with the state-of-the-art VGG-based model. The authors demonstrate that their single-layer model can generate high-quality textures that often rival the perceptual quality of the VGG-based model. Furthermore, the paper provides a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model, which allows for a more objective evaluation of texture quality.

However, I also have some concerns regarding the paper's limitations. One major limitation is the lack of systematic, quantifiable comparison across methods, making it hard to conclude whether the methods capture the true texture distribution rather than trivially reproducing examples. Additionally, the paper highlights the difficulty of measuring diversity in texture synthesis, suggesting that entropy is impractical and does not capture visual quality. While the authors recommend psychophysical evaluation as a practical way to assess visual quality and diversity, this is deferred to future work.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors provide more quantitative comparisons across methods, including metrics such as mean squared error or peak signal-to-noise ratio. Additionally, the authors could explore other measures of diversity, such as the use of generative adversarial networks or variational autoencoders, to better capture the texture distribution. Finally, the authors could provide more details on the optimization procedure used in their experiments, including the choice of hyperparameters and the number of iterations.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the optimization procedure used in your experiments, including the choice of hyperparameters and the number of iterations?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of measuring diversity in texture synthesis, and what alternative methods do you propose to use?
* Can you provide more quantitative comparisons across methods, including metrics such as mean squared error or peak signal-to-noise ratio?
* How do you think the use of Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis methods can be extended to more complex textures, such as those with irregular structures or multiple scales?
