**Summary of the paper's claims and contributions**

This paper proposes a simple yet effective baseline for parametric texture synthesis using a single-layer convolutional neural network with random, multi-scale filters. The authors demonstrate that this approach can generate high-quality textures that often rival those produced by state-of-the-art VGG-based models, which rely on deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained feature maps. The paper's main contributions include: (1) a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, (2) empirical results showing that texture synthesis may not require deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps, and (3) a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model.

**Decision and key reasons**

I decide to accept this paper, with the key reasons being:

1. The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to parametric texture synthesis, which challenges the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained feature maps are necessary for high-quality texture generation.
2. The empirical results are impressive, showing that the proposed baseline can sometimes rival the performance of state-of-the-art VGG-based models.

**Supporting arguments for the decision**

The paper's contributions are significant, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach using various metrics and comparisons with state-of-the-art models. The results are well-presented, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The authors also provide a thoughtful discussion of the implications of their findings, including the potential for simpler texture synthesis models and the importance of considering both perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis.

**Additional feedback for improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where the authors could further improve their work:

1. **Further validation of the conclusions**: While the empirical results are impressive, it would be helpful to see more extensive validation of the authors' conclusions about the role of deep hierarchical representations and learned feature maps in texture synthesis.
2. **Comparison with non-parametric methods**: The authors note that the generated samples from both VGG and single-layer models are imperfect and substantially worse than non-parametric methods. It would be interesting to see a more detailed comparison with non-parametric methods to better understand the strengths and limitations of the proposed approach.
3. **Inpainting evaluation**: The authors observe that the VGG-based model seems to perform better on inpainting (Figure 7). It would be helpful to see a more detailed evaluation of the proposed approach on inpainting tasks to better understand its performance in this area.
4. **Efficiency of hierarchical models**: The authors propose using a single-layer model with multiple filter sizes to handle multi-scale structure. However, it would be interesting to explore whether a hierarchical model (rather than many filter sizes) might handle multi-scale structure more efficiently.

**Questions for the authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the optimization procedure used in the paper, including the choice of hyperparameters and the number of iterations?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of variability in texture synthesis, which is an important aspect of texture modeling?
3. Can you provide more insights into why the proposed approach seems to work well for certain types of textures (e.g., those with regular structures) but not as well for others?
