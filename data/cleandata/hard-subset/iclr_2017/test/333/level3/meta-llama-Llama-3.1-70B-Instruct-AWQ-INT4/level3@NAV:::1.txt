**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a simple yet effective baseline for parametric texture synthesis using multi-scale random filters. The authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated from networks with only a single layer, no pooling, and random filters. They show that their approach often rivals the state-of-the-art VGG-based model, suggesting that deep hierarchical representations or learned feature maps are not indispensable for natural image generation. The paper's main contributions include presenting a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, showing that textures synthesized from this baseline are of high quality, and testing and comparing various single-layer architectures against the state-of-the-art texture model.

**Decision: Accept with Reservations**

I decide to accept this paper with some reservations. The paper presents an interesting and insightful approach to parametric texture synthesis, and the results are impressive. However, I have some concerns regarding the conclusions drawn by the authors, particularly with respect to the necessity of deep hierarchical representations.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's approach to texture synthesis is well-motivated, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the existing literature. The experimental results are also convincing, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach. However, I am not entirely convinced by the authors' conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for natural image generation. While the results do suggest that a single-layer network with random filters can generate high-quality textures, it is not clear whether this approach would generalize to more complex images.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

To strengthen the paper, I would like to see more rigorous validation of the conclusions, particularly with respect to the necessity of deep hierarchical representations. The authors could provide additional experiments or analysis to demonstrate the limitations of their approach and the potential benefits of using deeper networks.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address:

* Can the authors provide more insight into why the multi-scale model outperforms the single-scale model, particularly in terms of capturing long-range structures?
* How do the authors plan to address the issue of variability in texture synthesis, which is currently not well-captured by their approach?
* Can the authors provide more discussion on the potential benefits and limitations of using random filters versus learned feature maps?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and thought-provoking approach to parametric texture synthesis, and with some additional work, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
