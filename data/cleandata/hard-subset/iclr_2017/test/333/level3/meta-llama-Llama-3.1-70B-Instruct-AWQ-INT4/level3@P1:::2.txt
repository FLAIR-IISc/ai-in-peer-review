This paper proposes a generative model of natural textures based on a single-layer convolutional neural network with completely random filters. The authors demonstrate that this simple model is able to qualitatively capture the perceptual differences between natural textures, often rivaling the current state-of-the-art. The paper provides a thorough analysis of the conditions that enable the generation of natural-looking textures, and the results are surprising in that they suggest that deep hierarchical representations and the training of feature maps are not necessary for natural texture synthesis.

The paper's contributions are threefold. First, it presents a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis that solely relies on a single-layer network and random, data-independent filters. Second, it shows that textures synthesized from this baseline are of high quality and often rival state-of-the-art approaches. Third, it tests and compares a wide range of single-layer architectures with different filter sizes and types of filters against the state-of-the-art texture model.

The evaluation methods used in the paper are thorough, but could benefit from more work. The authors use a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model, which is a good start. However, they also note that this measure is not perfect and that finding the right balance between perceptual similarity and variability is challenging.
