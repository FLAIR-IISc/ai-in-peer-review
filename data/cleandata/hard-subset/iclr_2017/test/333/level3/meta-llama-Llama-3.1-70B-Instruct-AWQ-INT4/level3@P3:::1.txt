**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?" proposes a simple yet strong baseline for parametric texture synthesis using multi-scale random filters. The authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated from networks with only a single layer, no pooling, and random filters, often rivaling state-of-the-art approaches. They also show that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable for natural image generation as previously thought. The paper's main contributions include presenting a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, demonstrating the high quality of textures synthesized from the baseline, and testing and comparing various single-layer architectures against the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al.

Based on the paper's content and the conference reviewing guidelines, I decide to **Accept** this paper. The two key reasons for this choice are:

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's use of a single-layer network with random filters is a clever way to isolate the effect of the network architecture on texture synthesis.

The empirical results presented in the paper are impressive, and the authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach using various metrics, including visual inspection and quantitative measures. The comparison with the state-of-the-art texture model by Gatys et al. is particularly useful, as it provides a baseline for evaluating the performance of the proposed approach.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

While the paper is well-written and easy to follow, there are a few areas where the authors could improve the presentation:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the proposed approach and other texture synthesis methods, such as non-parametric methods.
* The authors could provide more insight into why the random multi-scale network performs better than the single-scale networks, and what the implications of this result are for texture synthesis.
* The paper could include more examples of textures synthesized using the proposed approach, to give the reader a better sense of the method's capabilities.

Some questions that I would like the authors to address in their response include:

* How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex textures, such as those with multiple scales or non-stationary statistics?

* How do the authors think their approach could be used in practice, for example, in computer-aided design or video game development?
