This paper presents a thorough analysis of the conditions that enable the generation of natural-looking textures. The authors investigate the role of various factors, including the depth of the network, the use of pooling, and the training of features on natural images, in generating high-quality textures. The results are surprising, as they show that a single-layer network with random filters can generate textures that often rival the perceptual quality of state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of the current state of the art in parametric texture modeling. They also provide a detailed description of their methodology, including the architecture of their single-layer network and the optimization procedure used to generate textures.

The analysis is thorough, and the authors provide a wide range of experiments to support their claims. They evaluate the quality of the generated textures using a variety of metrics, including a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model. They also provide a detailed comparison of their results with those of other state-of-the-art approaches.

The evaluation methods used in the paper are thorough, but could benefit from more work. The authors rely heavily on visual inspection to evaluate the quality of the generated textures, which can be subjective. They also use a quantitative texture quality measure, but this measure is not perfect and can be improved.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of their methodology and results. The paper aligns with other reviewers and suggests a promising direction for future work.
