- Decision: Accept — paper presents a compelling, novel result with thorough experiments and scientific rigor.
- Main claim: Single-layer CNNs with random filters can rival deep, supervised models for natural texture synthesis.
- Key contributions: systematic comparison of single-layer architectures, introduction of a VGG-based quantitative evaluation metric, and analysis of multi-scale filters and non-linearities.
- Novelty: Challenges the assumption that deep, pre-trained networks are necessary for high-quality texture generation.
- Experimental rigor: Extensive comparisons across filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervised) and architectures; shows multi-scale random filters and non-linearities are crucial.
- Practical impact: Simplifies texture synthesis by reducing reliance on deep models and supervised training, beneficial for limited-compute settings.
- Clarity issue: Mathematical formulations (e.g., Gram matrix derivation and role) need clearer explanations for non-expert readers.
- Limitation: Computational inefficiency of large filter sizes is acknowledged but not deeply explored or optimized.
- Variability vs. perceptual similarity: Paper focuses on perceptual similarity at the potential cost of variability; balancing these merits further study and psychophysical evaluation.
- Generalization: Unclear whether findings extend to other image-generation tasks (style transfer, inpainting); further exploration suggested.
- Question 1: How does the method handle highly irregular or non-repetitive textures; what are specific failure modes?
- Question 2: Can computational inefficiencies from large filters be mitigated (e.g., approximations, dilated convolutions)?
- Question 3: How sensitive is synthesis quality to different random filter initializations and variability across runs?
- Overall: Strong baseline and significant insight into the role of shallow architectures in texture synthesis, with minor clarity and limitation-addressing improvements needed.
