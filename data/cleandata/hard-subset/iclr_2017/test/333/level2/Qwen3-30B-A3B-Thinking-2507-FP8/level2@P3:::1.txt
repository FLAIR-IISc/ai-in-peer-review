

The paper investigates the essential components required for high-quality natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks with supervisedly trained features are indispensable. It demonstrates that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters—without pooling or pre-training—can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a). The work systematically evaluates diverse single-layer architectures (e.g., Fourier, K-means, random filters) and identifies that a multi-scale random model (with 1024 filters across 3×3, 11×11, and 55×55 kernels) achieves superior perceptual quality, particularly for regular structures like brick walls. Crucially, it shows that random filters outperform trained ones, attributing prior failures to suboptimal optimization rather than inherent limitations. The paper also introduces a quantitative metric using VGG-based reconstruction loss to supplement qualitative human evaluation, and discusses theoretical implications for maximum entropy texture modeling.

Accept. The work addresses a fundamental question with rigorous experimentation and clear evidence that depth and pre-training are not necessary for natural texture synthesis, directly contradicting prior assumptions. The contributions are novel, well-motivated, and supported by thorough ablation studies.

The paper’s core strength lies in its precise experimental design. The ablation of filter types (random vs. trained), filter sizes (3×3 to 55×55), and non-linearities (ReLU vs. no ReLU) conclusively demonstrates that the *number* of feature maps and *multi-scale filtering*—not network depth or filter training—are critical. For instance, Fig. 2 shows that single-scale large filters (55×55) capture long-range structures but miss fine details, while multi-scale combinations resolve this trade-off. The quantitative VGG-loss metric (Eq. 7) provides a reproducible evaluation method, and the observation that random filters outperform trained ones (e.g., K-means-3267) is robustly validated across 20 textures (Fig. 4). The theoretical discussion on the MaxEnt framework’s limitations for finite-size textures (Section 7) further elevates the contribution beyond empirical results.

Minor revisions would strengthen the paper. First, clarify the computational efficiency claim: while the paper notes multi-scale models are "computationally inefficient" due to large filters, it lacks runtime comparisons against hierarchical alternatives (e.g., VGG’s 19 layers). Quantifying synthesis time (e.g., "X seconds per texture") would substantiate this point. Second, explicitly address *why* random filters outperform trained ones. The paper suggests it stems from optimization tuning, but a brief analysis (e.g., comparing convergence rates or loss landscapes) would deepen the insight. Finally, the VGG-loss limitation for regular textures (e.g., walls, grids) is noted in Fig. 4 but not resolved; a sentence on how this might inform future perceptual metrics would be valuable.

Key questions for clarification:  
1. How does the multi-scale model’s receptive field (55×55) compare to the effective receptive field of a hierarchical network with similar total kernel size? This contextualizes the "efficiency" claim.  
2. For the random filter success, was the filter count (3267) or randomness the decisive factor? A baseline with 3267 *trained* filters (e.g., K-means-3267) would isolate the impact of randomness.  
3. The paper states that "the VGG-based loss is generally acceptable" but fails for regular textures. Could this be linked to the loss’s sensitivity to global regularity, and how might this inform the design of future loss functions?  

The paper’s core contribution—demonstrating that natural texture synthesis requires neither depth nor supervised training—challenges a foundational assumption in the field. The experimental rigor, quantitative evaluation, and theoretical discussion make this a significant advance. The minor suggestions are reasonable and would enhance clarity without requiring major rework.
