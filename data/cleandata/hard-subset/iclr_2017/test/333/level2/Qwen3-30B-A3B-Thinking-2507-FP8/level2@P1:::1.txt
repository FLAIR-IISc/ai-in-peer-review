

The paper investigates the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks, challenging the prevailing assumption that hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are essential. The authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized from a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, without pooling or supervised training, rivaling state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). Key contributions include a systematic comparison of filter types (Fourier, K-means, random), a quantitative evaluation via VGG-based loss, and insights into the role of filter scale and non-linearities.

**Decision: Reject**  
The paper presents an intriguing and potentially impactful finding, but the evidence supporting the core claim—random filters outperforming pre-trained ones—is insufficiently validated against critical baselines. The evaluation lacks comparison to non-DNN texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000), which were state-of-the-art before deep learning. Without this, it remains unclear whether the proposed approach genuinely advances the field or merely replicates prior work with a different implementation. Additionally, the VGG-based loss metric, while novel, is acknowledged to fail for certain regular textures (e.g., grids), yet the paper does not quantify how often this occurs or how it impacts overall conclusions.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
1. **Missing Baseline Comparison**: The authors claim their random-filter model "often rivals the current state-of-the-art" but omit comparison to hand-crafted methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000), which were the prior SOTA for texture synthesis. This omission weakens the novelty claim, as the paper’s contribution could be interpreted as merely "reimplementing existing DNN approaches with random weights" rather than introducing a fundamentally new paradigm.  
2. **VGG Loss Limitations**: While the authors propose using VGG loss as a quantitative metric, they admit it fails for regular textures (e.g., brick walls). The paper does not report how frequently this failure occurs across their 20 textures or provide human evaluation to corroborate the VGG loss rankings. Without this, the quantitative claims lack robustness.  
3. **Optimization Tuning Ambiguity**: The claim that "random filters perform better with refined optimization" contradicts Gatys et al. (2015a)’s assertion that random weights fail. However, the paper does not detail *how* the optimization differs (e.g., learning rate, initialization, regularization), making it unclear whether the improvement stems from the filters or the tuning. This undermines the reproducibility of the core finding.  

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**  
- **Include Non-DNN Baselines**: Compare results against Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) and other pre-DNN methods to establish whether the random-filter approach is genuinely superior or merely comparable.  
- **Quantify VGG Loss Reliability**: Report the percentage of textures where VGG loss correlates with human perception (e.g., via user studies) and discuss failure cases in detail.  
- **Clarify Optimization Details**: Explicitly state hyperparameters (e.g., L-BFGS-B settings, scaling factor) and how they differ from Gatys et al. (2015a) to justify the "refined tuning" claim.  
- **Address Variability**: The discussion on perceptual similarity vs. variability (Section 7) is insightful but lacks empirical support. Consider adding metrics for sample diversity (e.g., entropy of generated textures) to strengthen this argument.  

**Questions for Authors:**  
1. How does the perceptual quality of the random-filter model compare to Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) on standard texture datasets (e.g., Brodatz)?  
2. For the textures where VGG loss fails (e.g., grids), what do human evaluations reveal about the synthesized samples?  
3. Can you provide a table comparing key hyperparameters (e.g., optimization steps, filter size) between your method and Gatys et al. (2015a) to clarify the "refined tuning" claim?  

This work has strong conceptual merit but requires more rigorous validation to establish its significance. With the suggested revisions—particularly adding non-DNN baselines and clarifying optimization details—it would be a compelling contribution. As submitted, the evidence does not fully justify the claim that random filters are "not indispensable" or that the approach surpasses prior work.
