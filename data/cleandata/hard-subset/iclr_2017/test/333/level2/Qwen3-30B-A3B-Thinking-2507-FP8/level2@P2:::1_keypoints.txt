- Paper claims high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved with a single-layer CNN using random filters, and compares single-layer variants (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) with prior work.
- Authors introduce a VGG-based quantitative loss for evaluation and propose a multi-scale model that outperforms single-scale versions.
- Decision: Reject — core claim lacks sufficient evidence due to ambiguous experimental setup and evaluation methodology.
- Main methodological flaw: optimization details (e.g., scaling loss/gradients by 10^7, 2000 iterations) are not controlled or matched to Gatys et al., so improvements may be due to hyperparameter tuning rather than filter design.
- Without identical optimization settings, the claim that random filters rival state-of-the-art is unsubstantiated.
- Evaluation relies solely on VGG-based loss despite admitting it fails for structured textures (e.g., brick walls), undermining perceptual quality claims.
- No human studies are provided, leaving perceptual claims unsupported where VGG loss is misleading.
- Authors must explicitly report all optimization hyperparameters (loss scaling, iterations, initialization) for both their method and baselines.
- The paper fails to explain why random filters work: is success due to large capacity (e.g., 3267 filters) or inherent properties of random filters?
- Clarify whether multi-scale gains come from scale diversity or simply larger receptive fields; current analysis is incomplete.
- Requested evidence: correlation between VGG-based loss and human perception (e.g., table of VGG loss vs. human preference scores).
- Suggested fixes: disclose optimization parameters, run human evaluations (20–30 pairs), and analyze the “random filter” paradox to isolate contributing factors.
