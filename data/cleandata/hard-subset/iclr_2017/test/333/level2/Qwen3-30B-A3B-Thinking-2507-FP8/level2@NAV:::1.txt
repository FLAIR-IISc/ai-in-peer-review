

This paper makes a compelling argument that natural texture synthesis can be achieved using remarkably simple architectures: a single convolutional layer with random filters, no pooling, and no pre-training on natural images. The authors demonstrate that such minimal models can produce textures rivaling state-of-the-art approaches, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical representations are essential for natural image generation. Their key contribution is establishing a strong minimal baseline that reveals the fundamental requirements for texture synthesis, with thorough empirical evaluation across multiple filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and architectures (single-scale vs. multi-scale).

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that the depth and pre-training of feature spaces are not indispensable for natural texture synthesis, which is a surprising and valuable insight for the field. The experimental support is robust, with comprehensive comparisons against state-of-the-art methods and a novel quantitative evaluation metric using VGG-based loss. The results consistently show that random filters perform as well or better than trained filters, and the multi-scale model particularly excels at capturing long-range structures.

To strengthen the paper, I suggest adding a brief discussion of computational efficiency trade-offs between single-layer and multi-layer approaches, as the authors mention computational inefficiency but don't quantify it. Also, while the paper acknowledges limitations, explicitly stating the specific scenarios where their approach underperforms compared to VGG-based methods would provide better context.

The authors should clarify: How does the optimization process differ between their single-layer model and the VGG-based approach to explain the reduced susceptibility to local minima? Additionally, could the authors provide more insight into why random filters perform better than trained filters, which seems counterintuitive given the field's assumptions?
