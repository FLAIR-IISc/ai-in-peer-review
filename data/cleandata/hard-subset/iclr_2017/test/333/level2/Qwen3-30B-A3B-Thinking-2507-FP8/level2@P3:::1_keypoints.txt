- Main claim: Single-layer convnets with random filters (no pooling or pre-training) can synthesize natural textures matching state-of-the-art methods.  
- Method: Systematic evaluation of single-layer architectures including Fourier, K-means, and random filters.  
- Best model: A multi-scale random model (1024 filters across 3×3, 11×11, 55×55) achieves superior perceptual quality, especially for regular structures.  
- Key finding: The number of feature maps and multi-scale filtering—not network depth or supervised pre-training—drive synthesis quality.  
- Random vs trained: Random filters outperform trained ones; prior failures attributed to suboptimal optimization rather than inherent limitations.  
- Ablations: Varying filter types, sizes, and nonlinearities (ReLU vs none) convincingly isolates important factors (Fig. 2, Fig. 4).  
- Quantitative metric: Introduces a VGG-based reconstruction loss (Eq. 7) to complement qualitative human evaluation.  
- Limitation: VGG-loss struggles on highly regular textures (walls, grids), noted but not resolved.  
- Theory: Discusses implications for maximum entropy texture modeling and finite-size limitations (Section 7).  
- Recommendation: Accept—novel, well-motivated contributions with rigorous experiments and ablation studies.  
- Requested clarification 1: Compare multi-scale model receptive field (55×55) to effective receptive fields of hierarchical networks to contextualize efficiency claims.  
- Requested clarification 2: Is success due to filter count (e.g., 3267) or randomness—provide a baseline with equivalent-count trained filters.  
- Requested clarification 3: Explain why random filters outperform trained ones (e.g., analyze convergence or loss landscapes).  
- Minor revision: Quantify computational efficiency/runtime versus hierarchical alternatives and suggest how future perceptual metrics might address VGG-loss failures on regular textures.
