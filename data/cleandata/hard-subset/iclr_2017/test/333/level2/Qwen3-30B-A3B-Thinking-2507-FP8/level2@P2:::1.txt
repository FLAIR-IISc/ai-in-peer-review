

Summary: The paper investigates the necessity of deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features for natural texture synthesis, claiming that high-quality textures can be generated using a single-layer CNN with random filters. The authors systematically test various single-layer architectures (including Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters) and demonstrate that random filters with sufficient capacity (e.g., 3267 filters) often rival state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). They introduce a quantitative VGG-based loss for evaluation and propose a multi-scale model that outperforms single-scale variants.

Decision: Reject. The paper's core claim—that random filters suffice for high-quality synthesis—lacks sufficient evidence due to unresolved ambiguities in experimental setup and evaluation methodology.

Supporting arguments: The paper's central contribution hinges on contradicting Gatys et al. (2015a), who claimed random filters fail to generate perceptually meaningful textures. However, the authors do not clearly establish that their success stems from the random filters themselves rather than undisclosed optimization tweaks. Section 4 states they "scale the loss and gradients by 10^7" and use 2000 iterations, but Gatys et al. (2015a) did not specify these parameters. Without rigorous comparison of identical optimization settings (e.g., loss scaling, iteration count), the claim that random filters "perform on par or better" is unsubstantiated. This is a critical flaw: the result may merely reflect better hyperparameter tuning, not a fundamental insight about filter design.

Furthermore, the evaluation relies solely on the VGG-based loss (Eq 7) as a proxy for perceptual quality, despite the paper admitting this metric fails for structured textures (e.g., brick walls in Fig. 4). The authors acknowledge that "the VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage" in some cases, yet they use it to claim their method "rivals state-of-the-art." Without human evaluation—especially for the textures where VGG loss is misleading—the claim of perceptual quality is unsupported. The paper's own Fig. 4 shows clear perceptual differences between samples, yet it uses VGG loss to assert competitiveness. This undermines the primary contribution.

Additional feedback: 
1. **Clarify optimization parameters**: Explicitly state all hyperparameters used for both your method and Gatys et al. (2015a), including loss scaling, iteration count, and initialization, to isolate the effect of filter randomness.
2. **Include human evaluation**: Conduct a small-scale human study (e.g., 20-30 texture pairs) to validate that VGG loss correlates with perceptual quality. At minimum, provide a qualitative discussion of where VGG loss misleads (e.g., "For brick textures, VGG loss is lower for our method, but human observers prefer the Gatys sample").
3. **Address the "random filter" paradox**: Explain why random filters (which lack statistical alignment with natural images) outperform hand-crafted or unsupervisedly learned filters. Is it solely due to capacity (3267 filters), or do random filters inherently capture sufficient statistics? The paper does not distinguish these factors.

Questions for authors:
1. What specific optimization parameters (beyond loss scaling) differ from Gatys et al. (2015a) that enable random filters to work? Without this, the paper cannot claim the filter design is the key factor.
2. How does the VGG-based loss (Eq 7) correlate with human perception? Provide evidence (e.g., a table of VGG loss vs. human preference scores for key textures).
3. Why does the multi-scale model (with receptive fields up to 55×55) outperform single-scale models with equivalent filter counts? Is the improvement due to scale diversity or simply larger receptive fields? The paper's Fig. 2 suggests the former, but the analysis is incomplete.
