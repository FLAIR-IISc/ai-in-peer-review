## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper investigates the core requirements for generating natural textures using deep neural networks, challenging the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are essential. The authors demonstrate surprisingly strong results can be achieved with a single-layer convolutional network employing random filters, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods. They systematically explore different single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based synthesis loss, replacing traditional human evaluation. The work also offers a discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis within the maximum entropy framework.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core finding – that surprisingly simple networks can generate compelling textures – is a significant and counterintuitive result. This challenges established practices and opens up new avenues for research in generative modeling. Furthermore, the rigorous experimental setup, comparing numerous architectures and introducing a quantitative evaluation metric, strengthens the validity of the claims.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The extensive experimentation with various filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and network configurations convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness of the single-layer approach. The visual comparisons in Figure 1 and 4, alongside the quantitative results, clearly showcase the quality of the generated textures. The ablation studies, particularly regarding multi-scale filters, further solidify the understanding of the key factors influencing performance. The use of the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is a sensible approach, and the authors acknowledge its limitations.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification. While the authors mention the optimization procedure being more elaborate, a more detailed explanation of the specific tuning parameters (beyond the maximum iterations and scaling factor) would be valuable. It would also be helpful to understand *why* random filters work so well – is it simply the large number of filters, or is there a more fundamental principle at play? Additionally, the discussion on the MaxEnt framework feels somewhat disconnected from the main experimental results and could be streamlined or expanded with more concrete connections to the findings.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   Could the authors explore the computational cost of the multi-scale model in more detail, comparing it to the VGG-based approach?
*   What is the sensitivity of the results to the choice of the reference texture? Are some textures easier to synthesize than others?
*   The paper mentions a potential finite-size effect in the MaxEnt discussion. Could this be explored further with experiments using different image sizes?
