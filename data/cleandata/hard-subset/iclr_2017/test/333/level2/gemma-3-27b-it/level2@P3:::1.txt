This paper investigates the question of what is necessary for generating natural textures using deep neural networks. It challenges the common assumption that depth and pre-training are crucial, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be synthesized from a single-layer network with random filters. The project offers a compelling minimal baseline and a quantitative evaluation metric, contributing a valuable perspective to the field of texture synthesis.

The decision is to **accept** the paper. The core argument – that surprisingly simple networks can achieve impressive texture synthesis results – is well-supported by the presented experiments and analysis. The work presents a clear challenge to prevailing assumptions and offers a compelling alternative approach. Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation using VGG-based loss provides a more objective assessment than traditional human inspection.

The paper is strong in its empirical validation. The authors systematically explore various single-layer architectures and convincingly demonstrate that random filters, combined with a multi-scale approach, can rival state-of-the-art methods. The ablation studies, comparing different filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and filter sizes, are particularly helpful in understanding the contributions of each component. The comparison against the VGG-based model, both visually and quantitatively, is thorough and effectively conveys the results. The justification for the optimization procedure tuning is also provided and seems reasonable. The detailed description of the different network architectures (Fourier-363, Kmeans-3267, etc.) enables reproducibility.

Several areas could benefit from further clarification and potential expansion. The discussion regarding the theoretical connection to the MaxEnt framework is intriguing but could be fleshed out. Specifically, the authors mention a mismatch between the constraints in the MaxEnt framework and practical implementations; a more detailed explanation of this discrepancy and its implications would be valuable. The choice of the 10^7 scaling factor in the optimization procedure should be further justified. While stated as a necessary adjustment, the reason for this specific value is unclear. Exploring the sensitivity of the results to this scaling factor would be beneficial. The paper focuses heavily on visual similarity using the VGG-based loss. While acknowledged as imperfect, it would be insightful to explore other metrics, even briefly, to assess the diversity or originality of the generated textures. The claim that the multi-scale model often *rivals* the state-of-the-art could be strengthened by providing statistical significance tests for the quantitative comparisons. Providing error bars or p-values would add more rigor to the evaluation. Finally, while the authors mention a failure of the VGG-based loss for textures with regular structures, further analysis of these specific cases would offer deeper insight into the limitations of the chosen metric.
