- Investigates which components are necessary for natural texture synthesis with deep networks.  
- Challenges the view that depth and pre-training are essential; shows single-layer networks with random filters can produce high-quality textures.  
- Provides a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures against state-of-the-art methods.  
- Introduces a quantitative texture quality metric based on VGG-based synthesis loss to move beyond subjective assessment.  
- Main contribution: establishes a surprisingly strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis.  
- Recommendation: accept — the finding is significant and could steer research toward more efficient approaches.  
- Strength: thorough and systematic experimentation across many single-layer designs, yielding robust comparisons.  
- Strength: multi-scale model analysis highlights the importance of capturing different detail levels.  
- Strength: insightful discussion of the MaxEnt framework and its limitations for finite-size images.  
- Weakness: optimization procedure is insufficiently detailed (hyperparameters, initialization, sensitivity).  
- Suggestion: include a small-scale human evaluation to complement the VGG-based quantitative metric.  
- Open question: why do random filters work so well — capacity via many feature maps or an inherent benefit of randomization?  
- Weakness: the discussion of the "environment" around the reference texture is interesting but remains abstract and underexplained.  
- Questions for authors requested: details on L-BFGS-B settings and other optimizers tested, human eval results, and explanations for random-filter performance.
