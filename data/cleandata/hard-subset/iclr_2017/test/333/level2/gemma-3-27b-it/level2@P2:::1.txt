This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in deep neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly good textures can be generated using a single-layer CNN with random filters, challenging the prevailing assumption that hierarchical representations learned from natural images are crucial. They comprehensively evaluate various single-layer architectures against a state-of-the-art approach, introducing a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG-based synthesis loss.

**Reject.** While the findings are interesting and potentially impactful, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and the experimental validation isn’t entirely convincing given the current state of the art. The core idea – that simpler networks can perform surprisingly well for texture synthesis – has been hinted at in prior work (though perhaps not as thoroughly investigated) and the improvements over existing methods are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance at a competitive conference like ICLR. The quantitative results, while presented, don't consistently demonstrate superior performance and the reliance on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is a potential limitation.

The primary argument for rejection lies in the limited impact of the findings. The paper essentially shows that a more complex, pre-trained model isn’t *absolutely* necessary, rather than demonstrating a significant leap in texture synthesis quality. Several existing works already explored alternatives to deep hierarchical representations, and while this work offers a systematic exploration with random filters, the results don't fundamentally shift the landscape.  The claim of "rivaling state-of-the-art" is too strong based on the presented evidence.

Furthermore, the experimental setup, while extensive, feels somewhat exploratory. The paper tests a multitude of single-layer configurations, but the justification for choosing specific filter sizes or the number of feature maps feels somewhat arbitrary. The multi-scale model shows promise, but its performance gains aren't fully explained or convincingly linked to the combination of scales. The discussion of the MaxEnt framework and its limitations, while insightful, feels tangential to the core contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Novelty:** The idea of using simpler models for texture synthesis isn't entirely new. Previous work has investigated alternatives to deep, pre-trained networks. The paper doesn’t sufficiently contextualize its contribution within this existing body of work.
*   **Modest Improvement:** While the single-layer models perform surprisingly well, the quantitative and qualitative results do not demonstrably surpass the state-of-the-art. The VGG-loss as a metric is potentially flawed and doesn’t fully capture perceptual quality.
*   **Lack of Clear Mechanistic Insight:** The paper demonstrates *that* random filters can work, but it doesn’t provide a compelling explanation *why*. What properties of the random filters are crucial for capturing texture statistics?
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The large number of experiments feels somewhat ad-hoc. More focused investigations on key architectural choices (e.g., filter size, number of feature maps) would strengthen the conclusions.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Contextualize the work:** Thoroughly discuss related work, specifically highlighting how this paper builds upon or diverges from existing approaches to texture synthesis. Address previous attempts to simplify texture generation models.
*   **Strengthen Quantitative Evaluation:** Explore alternative metrics beyond the VGG-based loss. Consider user studies to directly assess perceptual quality. Perform a more rigorous statistical comparison with the state-of-the-art.
*   **Investigate Filter Properties:** Analyze the learned filters (even random ones) to identify any recurring patterns or properties that contribute to good texture synthesis.  Are certain filter distributions more effective?
*   **Focus the Scope:** Streamline the experimental setup and focus on a smaller set of well-motivated architectural choices.
*   **Clarify the Multi-Scale Benefit:** Provide a more detailed analysis of why the multi-scale model performs better. Are the different scales capturing different frequency components of the texture?
*   **Address Computational Cost:** Acknowledge the potential computational cost of large filters and discuss potential optimizations.



**Questions for the authors:**

*   What specific prior work hinted at the possibility of using random filters for texture synthesis, and how does this paper extend those findings?
*   Why was the VGG-based loss chosen as the primary evaluation metric, and what are its known limitations? Have you considered using other metrics or conducting user studies to validate the perceptual quality of the generated textures?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the optimization procedure used for texture synthesis? Were any specific techniques employed to avoid local minima?
*   Is there a correlation between the specific distribution of the random filters and the quality of the generated textures? Have you explored different filter distributions?
*   How does the computational cost of the single-layer multi-scale model compare to the state-of-the-art approach, and are there any potential optimizations that could reduce the computational burden?
