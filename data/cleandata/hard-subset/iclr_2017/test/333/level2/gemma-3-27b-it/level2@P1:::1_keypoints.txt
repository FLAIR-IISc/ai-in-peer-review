- Positive summary: paper shows surprising result that single-layer random-filter networks can synthesize high-quality textures and is well-written.
- Recommendation: reviewer recommends rejection despite interesting results.
- Main concern: claims that depth and pre-training are not indispensable are overstated given comparisons mainly to a single-layer baseline.
- Baseline critique: comparisons limited to one minimal architecture; robustness across other single-layer initializations and shallow trained nets is not established.
- Quantitative evaluation concern: using VGG-based loss is circular since it’s a deep-network perceptual metric, weakening the claim that depth isn’t important.
- Optimization details lacking: the specific tuning changes versus Gatys et al. are not fully explained and their impact is unclear.
- Multi-scale model explanation incomplete: paper does not convincingly explain why multi-scale combination succeeds beyond increased receptive field speculation.
- Ablation suggestion: run ablations (e.g., remove ReLU) to isolate contributions of components in the multi-scale model.
- Statistical rigor: provide statistical significance tests for quantitative comparisons (e.g., VGG-loss differences).
- Computational cost: discuss computational costs of single-layer and multi-scale models, especially with large filters.
- Language/claim refinement: soften wording from “indispensable” to “not strictly necessary” or similar.
- Limitations: expand discussion of limitations, especially the evaluation circularity using VGG features.
- Question for authors: detail the exact optimization changes made relative to Gatys et al.
- Question for authors: report experiments with other initialization schemes beyond random and K-means.
- Question for authors: identify alternative non–deep-learning texture quality metrics and justify metric choices.
- Question for authors: provide computational cost comparison between multi-scale and VGG-based models.
- Question for authors: clarify the “finite size effect” and how it may influence results.
