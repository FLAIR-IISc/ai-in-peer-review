## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper investigates the necessary components for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing notion that depth and pre-training are crucial, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated from a single-layer network with random filters. They present a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures against state-of-the-art methods, and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based synthesis loss. The core contribution lies in establishing a surprisingly strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The work presents a clear and well-motivated investigation into a fundamental question in image generation. The finding that complex architectures aren’t *necessary* for generating plausible textures is a significant one, potentially shifting focus toward more efficient approaches.

The paper’s strength lies in its thorough experimentation. The authors systematically test a wide range of single-layer architectures, providing a robust comparison against existing methods. The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric—using VGG-based loss—is valuable as it moves beyond purely subjective human assessment. The results clearly demonstrate the capability of the proposed baseline, and the analysis of multi-scale models highlights the importance of capturing different levels of detail. The discussion of the MaxEnt framework and its limitations in the context of finite-size images is also insightful.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification. The optimization procedure, while mentioned, lacks detail; a more thorough description of hyperparameter tuning and potential sensitivity to initialization would strengthen the findings. Furthermore, while the quantitative evaluation is useful, a small-scale human evaluation alongside it would provide additional validation. It would be helpful to understand *why* random filters perform so well – is it simply a matter of sufficient capacity with a large number of feature maps, or is there something inherent in the randomization process that is beneficial? Finally, the discussion of the "environment" around the reference texture is intriguing, but remains somewhat abstract.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you provide more detail on the optimization process (L-BFGS-B parameters, learning rate schedules, initialization strategies)?
*   Have you explored other optimization algorithms beyond L-BFGS-B?
*   Did you conduct any small-scale human evaluation to complement the VGG-based loss metric?
*   Could you elaborate on the potential reasons behind the surprisingly good performance of random filters?
