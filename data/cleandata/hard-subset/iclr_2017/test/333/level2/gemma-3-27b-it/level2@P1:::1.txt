This paper investigates the necessary components for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing notion that depth and pre-training are crucial, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated from a single-layer network with random filters. This is a surprising and potentially significant finding. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation, methodology, and results.

**Reject**

While the results are interesting and the paper is generally well-executed, I believe the claims are slightly overstated and the evaluation, while improved over purely subjective assessments, still lacks sufficient rigor to definitively support the strong conclusions drawn. Specifically, the claim that depth and pre-training are *not indispensable* feels too strong, given the comparison is primarily to a single-layer network. The performance gap between the proposed method and state-of-the-art (VGG-based) approaches, though sometimes small, is consistently present.

Here's a more detailed breakdown of my reasoning:

1. **Comparison Baseline:** The core argument hinges on the comparison to a single-layer network. While demonstrating that a single layer *can* achieve surprisingly good results is valuable, it doesn’t necessarily negate the benefits of deeper, pre-trained networks. The authors compare to a very specific minimal baseline. A more compelling argument would involve comparisons to other single-layer architectures with different initialization schemes (e.g., different random seeds, other distributions) and possibly a shallow network trained with a small dataset. Establishing the robustness of the findings across various single-layer configurations would strengthen the claim.

2. **Quantitative Evaluation:** The use of the VGG-based loss is a step in the right direction for objective evaluation, but it’s still fundamentally a perceptual metric derived *from* a deep network. Using this metric to “validate” the finding that depth isn’t important feels circular. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion section, but it remains a concern. Are there alternative quantitative metrics that could be used to assess texture quality independently of deep learning features?

3. **Optimization Procedure:** The paper mentions a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure as a reason for the discrepancy with earlier work (Gatys et al.). However, the details of this tuning are not fully explained. What specifically was changed in the optimization procedure, and how significant was its impact? More details on this aspect are needed.

4. **Multi-Scale Model:** While the multi-scale model performs well, the paper doesn't fully explain *why* it’s successful. Is it simply the increased receptive field size, or is there something more fundamental about combining filters at different scales? The analysis hints at the importance of both, but a more in-depth investigation would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies on the multi-scale model. For example, remove the ReLU non-linearities to see the impact on performance.
* **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests for the quantitative comparisons. Are the differences in VGG-based loss statistically significant?
* **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost associated with the single-layer models, especially the multi-scale model with large filters, would be helpful.
* **Clarify "Indispensable":** The term “indispensable” is strong. Perhaps rephrasing the claims to state that depth and pre-training are “not strictly necessary” or “can be circumvented” would be more accurate.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** Expand on the discussion of limitations, specifically regarding the circularity of using a VGG-based loss to evaluate a claim about the importance of VGG features.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you provide more details about the specific changes made to the optimization procedure compared to the original Gatys et al. implementation?
2. Have you experimented with other initialization schemes for the single-layer networks besides random and K-means?
3. Are you aware of any alternative quantitative texture quality metrics that are independent of deep learning features? If so, why weren't they used in the evaluation?
4. What is the computational cost of generating a texture with the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based model?
5. The paper mentions a potential “finite size effect”. Could you elaborate on this concept and how it might influence the results?
