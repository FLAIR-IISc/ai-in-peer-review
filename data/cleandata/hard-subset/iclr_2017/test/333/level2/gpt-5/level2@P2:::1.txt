Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a focused and timely question: which components of CNN-based texture models are actually necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis? Contrary to prevailing assumptions, the authors show that a single-layer CNN with random filters (no training, no pooling) and Gram-matrix statistics can synthesize textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality, often competitive with the VGG-based multi-layer method of Gatys et al. They present:
- A strong minimal baseline: single-layer, random filters, ReLU, Gram constraints, optimized with L-BFGS.
- A thorough single-layer ablation over filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA; with/without whitening; ImageNet vs target-only sampling) and channel counts (363 vs 3267), plus a random multi-scale model (up to 55×55 filters).
- Evidence that multi-scale random models capture small-, mid-, and long-range structure better than single-scale; ReLU nonlinearity is crucial.
- A quantitative measure using the VGG-based loss to assess perceptual similarity; qualitative comparisons on ~20 textures.
- A thoughtful discussion on MaxEnt modeling for finite-size textures and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The empirical finding that depth, pooling, and supervised training are not indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis is novel, insightful, and relevant; (2) The experimental ablations are careful and the qualitative gains of multi-scale random features are convincing; (3) The paper provides a useful, minimal baseline that should influence how future work is evaluated. The main weakness is the evaluation: it relies heavily on the VGG loss as a proxy for perception without a human study, and some claims (“rivals SOTA”) are texture-dependent.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The question is clear, important, and well-positioned in the literature (Gatys, Ulyanov, Johnson, Portilla-Simoncelli, GAN/PixelCNN). The paper directly challenges a widely-held hypothesis (necessity of deep, pretrained features) and narrows down which elements are essential (Gram statistics + multi-scale + ReLU).
- Methodological soundness: The synthesis setup closely follows Gatys but reduces architecture to a single conv layer (or multi-scale variant) and varies only filter banks. The loss normalization and optimization details are spelled out. The ablations (filter sizes, number of maps, learned vs random, whitening, single- vs multi-scale, with/without ReLU) are appropriate to support the core claims.
- Claims vs evidence:
  - “Depth and pretraining are not indispensable”: Supported by qualitative comparisons and by VGG-loss evaluation; multi-scale random often matches or surpasses VGG-synthesis for certain textures, especially highly regular ones.
  - “Random filters rival learned filters”: Supported across several filter constructions; random performs on par or better than k-means/PCA/Fourier in many cases when channel counts are matched.
  - “Multi-scale is critical”: The single-scale 1024-channel controls at different sizes convincingly show complementary scales; no single size suffices.
  - “ReLU matters”: Ablation shows failure without nonlinearity.
  - Limitations acknowledged: The authors recognize that VGG loss is an imperfect perceptual metric, that optimization differences can confound comparisons, and that variability vs similarity is not adequately measured.
- Rigor and placement: The paper is careful in dissecting what works and why, with a cogent conceptual link to the MaxEnt view. However, the “formal generalization” remains qualitative rather than a concrete new framework with theory or algorithms. The evaluation could be stronger (no human study; limited dataset; no runtime/memory measurements; no statistical analysis over random seeds).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Evaluation and metrics:
  - Include a small-scale user study (e.g., AB preference or a 2AFC discriminability test) to corroborate the VGG-loss results and quantify perceptual similarity. Your inpainting-based psychophysics suggestion is promising; even a pilot study would strengthen the claims.
  - Report diversity/variability across multiple random seeds and syntheses per texture. Quantify intra-model variability (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss between multiple samples, patch-wise statistics) to address the “overfitting to a single image” concern.
  - Expand the texture set and consider a standardized benchmark (e.g., DTD) for broader coverage and to mitigate selection bias. Provide per-texture results in a supplement.
- Comparisons and baselines:
  - Add a comparison to the scattering transform-based texture model (Bruna & Mallat), which is also shallow and hand-designed but structured; this would contextualize “random vs designed” features.
  - Consider Portilla–Simoncelli as a quantitative baseline (even if perceptual) or discuss why a fair quantitative comparison is hard.
  - For VGG-based synthesis, try initializing from your random-model outputs systematically and report consistent gains to separate optimization from representational effects.
- Analysis and ablations:
  - Specify the multi-scale architecture precisely: filter sizes used, number of maps per scale, normalization, and how features are concatenated. Provide a parameter count, FLOPs, and runtime/memory vs VGG-synthesis.
  - Explore other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, elu) and investigate whether modest pooling or dilations affect results at constant receptive field.
  - Study performance vs number of feature maps to show saturation behavior and the trade-off with compute.
- Methodological clarity:
  - Clarify the loss scaling by 1e7 and termination criteria in L-BFGS-B; consider reporting gradient norms and final objective values to show convergence, and show that re-scaling does not alter the minima.
  - Discuss preprocessing: removing per-channel ImageNet means for target textures might bias color; consider matching color histograms or learning per-texture DC offsets.
  - Provide code and filters to ensure reproducibility.
- Theory/discussion:
  - The MaxEnt discussion is insightful; consider formalizing the “ε-environment” perspective with a concrete sampling or optimization scheme (e.g., a constrained optimization or noise-injected objective) and demonstrate its effect on diversity.
  - Elaborate on why random features + Gram suffice: e.g., arguments about random feature embeddings preserving local structure and how convolutional overlap imposes higher-order constraints.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are results to the random seed (filters and initialization)? Please provide statistics over multiple runs per texture.
2. What is the exact multi-scale configuration (filter sizes and channels per scale)? How critical is the allocation across scales?
3. Can you provide runtime and memory comparisons (per 512×512 texture) vs VGG-based synthesis, and how do they scale with the number of feature maps?
4. How often does the VGG-based loss conclude lower for your random multi-scale outputs than for VGG-optimized outputs, and is this attributable to local minima? Does initializing VGG synthesis from your outputs consistently fix this?
5. How do your models fare on textures with strong global perspective or non-stationarity? Any failure modes you can characterize?
6. Could you compare against scattering-based texture synthesis to disentangle “random vs structured shallow” representations?

Overall, this paper offers a compelling, minimalist perspective with solid empirical support and valuable insights. Strengthening the evaluation and clarifying multi-scale details would make it a strong contribution.
