Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: which aspects of deep CNN representations are actually necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis? The authors show that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised pretraining is indispensable. Instead, a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching suffices to produce textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality. They systematically compare several single-layer choices (random, Fourier, PCA, K-means, with different channel couplings and numbers of feature maps) and a multi-scale variant that combines multiple filter sizes in one layer, and evaluate quality using the VGG-based texture loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity. They find that (i) more feature maps generally help, (ii) multi-scale random filters substantially improve long-range structure without sacrificing local detail, and (iii) random filters perform on par or better than handcrafted or unsupervised filters. The paper also discusses the role of optimization (L-BFGS-B with appropriate scaling and box constraints), the necessity of nonlinearities, and a perspective on reconciling maximum entropy texture models with finite-size variability.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, well-motivated, and surprising empirical finding that challenges common assumptions (depth and pretraining) while offering a strong, simple baseline for texture synthesis.
- Thorough experimental exploration (filter types, scales, number of channels) and careful analysis of why multi-scale and nonlinearity matter; results often rival a strong VGG-based baseline.

Supporting arguments
Strengths:
- Focused question with strong relevance: understanding what matters for image generation, specifically textures, is valuable to both theory and practice.
- Methodological clarity: the model is simple (single-layer CNN + Gram stats + ReLU + L-BFGS optimization) and the comparisons are straightforward.
- Breadth of ablations: variants include random vs. learned vs. handcrafted filters, low vs. high channel counts, single-scale vs. multi-scale, and the effect of removing the nonlinearity.
- Insightful observations: the multi-scale combination is critical (not merely larger receptive fields), and optimization difficulties in deeper models can partly explain the VGG model’s shortcomings; initializing VGG synthesis with single-layer solutions improves results.
- Evaluation: using VGG texture loss as an automatic proxy is sensible and aligns with current practice; the paper openly discusses its limitations and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Concerns / limitations:
- Evaluation relies primarily on the VGG-based loss and visual inspection. While common, this may bias comparisons and is not a perfect surrogate for perception, especially for highly regular, man-made patterns where the metric is acknowledged to be imperfect.
- Computational aspects are underquantified. The multi-scale single-layer with large spatial kernels (up to 55x55) is potentially expensive. Exact runtime, memory (especially for large Gram matrices), and comparisons to VGG-based optimization or feedforward texture nets (e.g., Ulyanov et al.) would help.
- Variability vs. overfitting: the paper raises the important issue that with many constraints one risks exact reconstruction for finite images. Concrete measurements of sample diversity (e.g., inter-sample VGG distance, coverage of patch histograms) are largely deferred.
- Formal contribution on MaxEnt generalization is more conceptual than formal. The discussion is interesting but lacks a concrete algorithmic or theoretical result.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Could you provide quantitative timing and memory profiles for the key settings (e.g., Random-363, Random-3267, and the multi-scale model), including Gram computation and L-BFGS optimization, and compare to VGG-based synthesis and a feedforward baseline (Ulyanov et al.)?
- How robust are the findings to image size? Do multi-scale random models still perform comparably for larger textures (e.g., 512x512), and does the optimization still converge reliably?
- Could you include a small-scale human study (e.g., 2AFC on matching source vs. synthesized textures) to validate the VGG-loss proxy, especially for the regular man-made textures where qualitative gaps are noted?
- Ablation on nonlinearity: you show ReLU is essential. Have you tried leaky-ReLU, ELU, or tanh? Does the specific nonlinearity or its degree of sparsity/rectification change outcomes?
- Initialization and optimization: you mention scaling the loss/gradients by 1e7 avoids early stopping. Please include an ablation quantifying the impact of iteration count, scaling factor, and initialization (noise vs. different seeds) on both convergence speed and final quality.
- Multi-scale architecture details: how are the 1024 feature maps distributed across filter sizes? Is performance sensitive to this allocation or to the exact set of scales?
- Random vs. learned filters: do learned filters (e.g., k-means) catch up with random ones as the number of channels increases, or is the random advantage consistent? Any insight into why random features plus Gram suffice—e.g., are we effectively approximating pairwise correlations over a near-universal random feature map?
- Diversity: could you report inter-sample distances (in VGG space) across 10–20 samples per texture to quantify variability? If you stop optimization earlier (as you suggest), how does the diversity vs. quality trade-off look quantitatively?
- Color handling: some variants filter RGB jointly with different weights; others per channel. Do cross-channel correlations in the Gram matrix play a significant role in textures with strong color structure?
- Normalization in the loss: you normalize Gram distances by the Frobenius norms of Gram matrices. Have you tried alternative normalizations or layer weightings (for the VGG-based evaluation), and how stable are the rankings to these choices?
- Reproducibility: code and model configurations would be valuable for the community given the role of optimization details in your success.

Overall, this is a well-executed and thought-provoking paper that provides a strong minimal baseline and sharpens our understanding of what is essential for texture synthesis. With a bit more on quantitative efficiency and a small human evaluation, the work would be even stronger, but as it stands it makes a clear, impactful point and is suitable for ICLR.
