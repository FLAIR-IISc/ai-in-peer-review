Summary of claims and contributions: The paper argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis. Using a single convolutional layer with ReLU nonlinearities, no pooling, and even random filters, the authors match Gram matrix statistics and produce textures that often rival the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. They further show multi-scale single-layer models improve perceptual quality, provide ablations over filter sets (Fourier, k-means/PCA, random), filter sizes, and nonlinearity, and propose a quantitative evaluation using the VGG Gram-loss as a surrogate for human judgment. They also discuss a MaxEnt perspective and finite-size considerations.

Decision: Accept. Reasons: (1) A strong and surprising minimal baseline with compelling empirical evidence advances understanding of what is essential for texture synthesis. (2) Careful ablations isolate the roles of scale, number of channels, and nonlinearities, yielding actionable insights for the community.

Supporting arguments: The paper’s question is clear and well motivated against prior work relying on deep, pretrained features. The literature positioning is appropriate (Gatys, Portilla–Simoncelli, GAN/PixelCNN texture lines). Experimental support is substantial: comparisons across many single-layer architectures, qualitative results on ~20 textures, and ablations that convincingly show multi-scale and ReLU necessity. The proposed VGG-based loss offers a pragmatic, reproducible metric, though it risks circularity. While human psychophysics would strengthen claims, the breadth of ablations and consistent qualitative improvement suggest the core conclusion is robust. Usefulness is high: the work establishes a simple, interpretable baseline and clarifies that depth/pretraining mainly improve efficiency, not quality. Methodological details (LBFGS setup, normalization, filter construction) appear sufficient for reproduction.

Additional feedback (to improve, not decisive for acceptance):
- Add a small-scale human study (e.g., 2AFC discrimination or inpainting) to validate the VGG-loss proxy and assess variability vs. similarity.
- Quantify diversity across multiple seeds; report runtime/memory and compare to VGG-based synthesis and hierarchical speedups.
- Provide statistical summaries over the texture set (means/variances of VGG-loss), and test sensitivity to loss normalization and gradient scaling.
- Release code and a fixed evaluation suite.

Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are results to the number of feature maps and to per-scale weighting in the multi-scale model?
- Does the main conclusion hold under alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., different pretrained backbones or pooling choices)?
- What is the seed-to-seed variability in quality/diversity? Any failure cases?
- Can circularity in the VGG-loss be mitigated (e.g., cross-model evaluation)?
- What are the exact compute costs per texture, and how do hierarchical approximations change quality vs. speed?
