- Paper shows high-quality texture synthesis is possible with a single conv layer + ReLU, no pooling, and even random filters using Gram-matrix matching.
- A multi-scale single-layer model (filters of several spatial sizes) often matches or surpasses VGG-based multi-layer synthesis.
- Systematic comparison of random, Fourier, k-means, and PCA filters examines importance of feature-map count, spatial scale, and nonlinearity.
- Introduces a quantitative evaluation using VGG feature-space loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity.
- Conceptual discussion frames texture synthesis in a maximum-entropy perspective, highlighting soft constraints and the fidelity–variability trade-off.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Surprising, practically relevant baseline showing depth and supervised pretraining are not required.
- Key reason 2: Thorough empirical analysis identifying essential elements (multi-scale, nonlinearity) and non-essential ones (training, depth, pooling).
- Strong placement in literature with comparisons to Gatys et al., Portilla & Simoncelli, and related work on handcrafted and unsupervised filters.
- Empirical support: extensive qualitative comparisons; trends show more feature maps help, multi-scale captures long-range structure, and ReLU is necessary.
- Reproducibility: architectural and optimization details are provided and many variants are tested.
- Caveat: Reliance on VGG-based loss may introduce circularity and fails on some structured man-made textures.
- Caveat: Variability across synthesized samples is discussed conceptually but not quantitatively measured.
- Caveat: Multi-scale single-layer with large filters is computationally heavy; no timing benchmarks provided.
- Essential suggestion: Report aggregate quantitative results (mean/median/variance of VGG-loss) and significance tests across the texture set.
- Essential suggestion: Detail exact multi-scale configuration (filter sizes, counts, initializations) and include runtime/memory comparisons versus VGG-based synthesis.
- Optional suggestion: Run a small human perceptual study (2AFC or inpainting) to validate the proxy metric and identify failure modes.
- Optional suggestion: Quantify within-method sample diversity and illustrate the fidelity–diversity trade-off (e.g., via early stopping experiments).
- Optional suggestion: Ablate other nonlinearities (leaky-ReLU, absolute value) to test the necessity of ReLU and links to scattering models.
- Optional suggestion: Include broader metrics (SSIM, pixel MSE) in an appendix and consider an independently trained perceptual metric.
- Optional suggestion: Report sensitivity to random seeds for filters and synthesis initialization, including failure rates and VGG-loss variance.
- Optional suggestion: Clarify scope and limitations, especially regarding stationary textures and behavior on non-stationary/global patterns.
- Questions: Exact multi-scale settings (filter sizes, counts per scale, initialization ranges) and sensitivity to allocation?
- Questions: Typical optimization iterations and wall-clock times for single-scale vs multi-scale vs VGG-based models on standard hardware?
- Questions: Robustness to random seeds for filters and synthesis initialization, and whether some textures systematically fail?
- Questions: Can early stopping induce greater variability without severe perceptual loss, and is there quantitative evidence for a controllable fidelity–diversity trade-off?
- Questions: Performance on grayscale textures or with decorrelated/whitened color channels?
- Questions: Is code and the texture set available to facilitate reproduction?
