Summary
The paper investigates which components of recent deep-learning-based texture synthesis are truly necessary. Using the Gram-matrix matching framework, it demonstrates that high-quality natural textures can be synthesized with a single convolutional layer, ReLU nonlinearity, no pooling, and even completely random filters. A multi-scale single-layer variant (combining filters of several spatial sizes) further improves quality and sometimes rivals or surpasses the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. The work systematically compares random, hand-crafted (Fourier), and unsupervisedly learned (k-means, PCA) filters; analyzes the importance of feature-map count, spatial scale, and nonlinearity; and introduces a quantitative evaluation based on the VGG feature-space loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity. A conceptual discussion links the practice of texture synthesis to the maximum entropy framework, emphasizing the role of “soft” constraints and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A strong, surprising, and practically relevant baseline showing that depth and supervised pretraining are not required for high-quality texture synthesis; (2) thorough empirical analysis identifying which elements are essential (multi-scale and nonlinearity) and which are not (training, depth, pooling), with compelling visual results and a quantitative metric.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance: Understanding the minimal ingredients for natural texture generation is important scientifically and practically. Establishing a simple, strong baseline clarifies the contribution of architectural depth and training in a widely used framework.
- Novelty and insight: While Gram-based synthesis is known, showing that random single-layer CNNs—especially multi-scale—can often rival the VGG-based model is non-trivial. The analysis that larger receptive fields alone are insufficient and that combining scales is key adds concrete design guidance.
- Placement in literature: The paper contrasts with Gatys et al. (2015a,b), Portilla & Simoncelli (2000), and recent extensions (e.g., Berger & Memisevic; Liu et al.; Ulyanov et al.), and relates to handcrafted feature banks (Fourier) and unsupervised filters. The discussion referencing the MaxEnt perspective and the role of stationarity is appropriate.
- Empirical support: Extensive qualitative comparisons across many textures show consistent trends: more feature maps help; multi-scale captures both local and long-range structure; ReLU is necessary. The quantitative VGG-loss metric generally aligns with perception and reveals interesting behavior (e.g., lower VGG-loss from the random multi-scale model on some textures).
- Reproducibility and clarity: Architectural and optimization details are described (filter sizes, counts, L-BFGS-B with gradient scaling, box constraints). The set of tested variants is broad.

Caveats (not blocking)
- Evaluation metric: Reliance on a VGG-based loss as a stand-in for human perception introduces potential circularity and known failure cases (e.g., man-made regular structures). The paper acknowledges this and motivates future psychophysics; nonetheless, the central claims rest primarily on visual evidence and this proxy metric.
- Variability assessment: The work focuses on maximizing perceptual similarity; variability across samples is discussed conceptually but not quantified. This affects conclusions about the trade-off between fidelity and diversity.
- Efficiency: Single-layer multi-scale with large filters is computationally heavy. The paper notes speedups possible with hierarchical implementations but does not provide timing benchmarks.

Suggestions to improve the paper (for camera-ready)
Essential
- Provide aggregate quantitative results: in addition to per-image examples, report summary statistics (mean/median and variance) of the VGG-loss across the full texture set for each method, and simple significance tests to substantiate “often rivals” claims.
- Detail the multi-scale configuration: list exact filter sizes, number of feature maps per scale, and initialization distribution per scale. Include runtime/memory comparisons versus VGG-based synthesis.

Recommended but optional
- Human perceptual study: a small-scale 2AFC or inpainting discrimination test on a subset of textures would substantiate the proxy metric and clarify the failure modes (especially for highly regular, man-made textures).
- Variability quantification: report within-method sample diversity as a function of optimization budget or early stopping (e.g., distribution of VGG-loss distances between multiple samples and the reference, and between samples), and illustrate the fidelity–diversity trade-off.
- Alternative nonlinearities: ablate other pointwise nonlinearities (e.g., leaky-ReLU, absolute value) to connect with scattering-based models and test robustness of the “nonlinearity is essential” conclusion.
- Broader metrics: include SSIM and pixel MSE in an appendix (with caveats) to contextualize the VGG-loss, and, if feasible, a simple learned perceptual metric trained independently of VGG-based Gram losses.
- Initialization sensitivity: report variability across random seeds for filter initialization and synthesis noise, including failure rates and variance in VGG-loss.
- Scope clarification: explicitly delineate limitations to stationary textures, and discuss behavior on strongly non-stationary or globally structured patterns.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact multi-scale settings (filter sizes, counts per scale, and initialization ranges)? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- How many optimization iterations are typically required for convergence for single-scale vs multi-scale vs VGG-based models, and what are typical wall-clock times on a standard GPU/CPU?
- How robust are results to different random seeds for both filters and synthesis initialization? Are there textures where random filters systematically fail?
- Could early stopping be used to induce greater variability without severe perceptual degradation? Any quantitative evidence for a controllable fidelity–diversity trade-off?
- How does the method perform on grayscale textures, or when color channels are decorrelated/whitened at input?
- Is code and a list of textures used available to facilitate reproduction?

Overall, the paper presents a compelling, well-executed study with clear takeaways for texture synthesis: multi-scale random features plus Gram statistics and ReLU suffice for surprisingly strong results. The findings will interest the community and provide a valuable baseline; the suggested additions would further strengthen empirical support and clarity.
