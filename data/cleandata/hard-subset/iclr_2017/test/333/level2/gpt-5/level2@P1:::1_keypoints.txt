- Main claim: A single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU, and Gram-matrix matching can synthesize high-quality textures, showing depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not indispensable.

- Methods: Systematic comparison of single-layer choices (random, Fourier, PCA, K-means), channel couplings, feature-map counts, and a multi-scale single-layer variant.

- Evaluation: Quality measured primarily by VGG-based texture loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, supplemented by visual inspection.

- Key findings: Increasing the number of feature maps generally improves texture quality.

- Key findings: Multi-scale random filters substantially improve long-range structure while preserving local detail.

- Key findings: Random filters perform on par with or better than handcrafted or unsupervised learned filters.

- Optimization: L-BFGS-B with careful scaling and box constraints is important; optimization behavior affects results.

- Nonlinearity: ReLU (or other nonlinearities) is necessary for good synthesis; removing it degrades performance.

- Theoretical angle: Paper offers a conceptual perspective reconciling maximum-entropy texture models with finite-size variability, but without formal results.

- Decision: Reviewer recommends acceptance.

- Strength: Clear, well-motivated, and surprising empirical result that provides a strong, simple baseline for texture synthesis.

- Strength: Methodological clarity and simplicity (single-layer CNN + Gram stats + ReLU + L-BFGS).

- Strength: Broad and thorough ablation studies across filters, scales, channel counts, and nonlinearities.

- Insight: Multi-scale combination matters beyond merely increasing receptive field; single-layer initialization can improve deeper VGG synthesis.

- Evaluation caveat: Reliance on VGG-loss and visual inspection may bias comparisons and is imperfect for regular, man-made patterns.

- Computational concern: Multi-scale large kernels (up to 55x55) may be computationally expensive; runtime, memory, and Gram-matrix costs are underreported.

- Variability concern: Risk of overfitting / low diversity with many constraints; quantitative diversity measurements are largely missing.

- Theoretical limitation: MaxEnt generalization discussion is conceptual rather than offering concrete algorithmic or theoretical contributions.

- Request: Provide quantitative timing and memory profiles for key configurations and comparisons to VGG-based and feedforward baselines.

- Request: Test robustness to image size and optimization convergence on larger textures (e.g., 512x512).

- Request: Include a small human study (e.g., 2AFC) to validate the VGG-loss proxy, especially for regular textures.

- Request: Ablate other nonlinearities (leaky-ReLU, ELU, tanh) to assess sensitivity to nonlinearity type and sparsity.

- Request: Provide ablations on initialization/scaling/iteration counts to quantify optimization effects on convergence and quality.

- Request: Clarify multi-scale architecture details (allocation of 1024 maps across filter sizes) and sensitivity to that allocation.

- Request: Analyze whether learned filters catch up to random ones as channels increase and why random features suffice (possible link to random feature universality).

- Request: Report inter-sample VGG distances or patch-histogram coverage to quantify synthesized sample diversity and the trade-off when stopping early.

- Request: Clarify color handling (joint vs per-channel filtering) and the role of cross-channel Gram correlations.

- Request: Test alternative Gram normalization/layer weighting schemes and report stability of rankings.

- Reproducibility: Release code and configurations given sensitivity to optimization details.
