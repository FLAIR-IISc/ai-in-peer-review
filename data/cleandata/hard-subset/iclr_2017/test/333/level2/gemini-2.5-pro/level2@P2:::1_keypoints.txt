- Paper shows a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters can synthesize textures rivaling deep VGG-based models.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Significant, surprising finding: shallow random-filter model matches complex pre-trained deep networks for texture synthesis.
- Thorough empirical evaluation comparing filter initializations, counts, and scales that isolates key ingredients for success.
- Main necessary components identified: multi-scale filters, non-linearities (ReLU), and sufficient feature count; learned depth is not essential.
- Ablation studies demonstrate small filters miss long-range structure and large filters miss fine detail.
- Visual results show random multi-scale model sometimes perceptually outperforms VGG (e.g., bricks, grids).
- Use of VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is clever but circular and biased toward VGG; should be interpreted cautiously.
- Discussion connects variability trade-offs to Maximum Entropy and highlights limitations of VGG loss in capturing perceptual advantages.
- Suggestion to clarify optimization details (why loss scaled by 10^7) for reproducibility.
- Suggestion to report synthesis runtimes to compare computational efficiency versus hierarchical architectures like VGG.
- Recommend emphasizing cases where VGG loss fails as strong evidence for the proposed model.
- Proposed inpainting experiment as a stronger human-in-the-loop evaluation of texture variability.
- Questions: did authors try alternative optimizers (e.g., Adam) or initializations to close VGG gap?
- Question: how sensitive are results to filter initialization distributions (Glorot vs Gaussian)?
- Question: elaborate how optimizer choice and convergence criteria define the ε-environment in the Maximum Entropy interpretation.
