### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The work challenges the prevailing assumption, established by state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a), that deep, hierarchical models with features learned from large-scale object recognition tasks are necessary. The authors systematically deconstruct this paradigm and convincingly demonstrate that neither depth, pooling, nor supervisedly pre-trained filters are indispensable.

The main contribution is a powerful minimal baseline for texture synthesis: a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters. The authors show that when using a sufficient number of filters across multiple scales, this simple model can generate textures of remarkably high perceptual quality, often rivaling or even exceeding the results from a 19-layer VGG network. Furthermore, the paper introduces a quantitative method for evaluating texture quality using the VGG-based synthesis loss, providing a more objective measure to complement qualitative human inspection. The work is rounded out by a thoughtful discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and the practical limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a strong and counter-intuitive result that forces a re-evaluation of what is necessary for generative texture modeling. The discovery that a shallow network with random filters can perform on par with a very deep, pre-trained network is a significant contribution to our understanding of neural generative models.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by extensive and well-designed experiments. The authors systematically compare various filter types (random, learned, hand-crafted), filter quantities, and scales, providing compelling visual and quantitative evidence for their conclusions. The quality of the generated textures speaks for itself.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The strength of this paper lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous investigation. It moves beyond simply presenting a new method and instead asks a fundamental scientific question about an existing, highly successful one.

The core claim—that depth and learned features are not strictly necessary—is convincingly demonstrated. The visual results in Figure 1 and Figure 4 clearly show that the random multi-scale model produces textures that are highly competitive with the much more complex VGG-based model. In some cases, particularly with regular, structured patterns (e.g., the brick wall), the proposed simple model produces visually superior results.

The experimental design is sound. By ablating components like network depth, filter training, and pooling, the authors isolate the crucial elements. The experiment in Figure 2, which compares single-scale models of varying filter sizes to the multi-scale model, effectively proves that the combination of features at different scales is critical for success, rather than just having a large receptive field. This provides a clear and actionable insight.

The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a valuable contribution. While the authors are careful to note its limitations, it represents a step towards more reproducible and objective evaluation in a field that often relies heavily on subjective assessment. The observation that their model can sometimes achieve a lower VGG-loss than the VGG model itself is a fascinating result, highlighting potential optimization challenges in very deep generative models.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the decision to accept.

*   **Role of Optimization:** The paper suggests in the introduction that the failure of previous attempts with random networks might be due to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Specifically, the authors note scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7. This detail seems quite important. Could you provide more intuition on why this scaling is necessary? Does it prevent premature convergence due to small gradient magnitudes? Clarifying whether the success is due to the model architecture alone, or a combination of the model and a carefully tuned optimization process, would strengthen the paper's conclusions.
*   **Computational Cost:** The authors correctly note that using large convolutional filters is computationally inefficient compared to a hierarchical approach. It would be beneficial to quantify this trade-off. A brief comparison of the synthesis time for the random multi-scale model versus the VGG-based model for a representative texture would give readers a clearer understanding of the practical implications.
*   **Discussion on Style Transfer:** The texture synthesis method by Gatys et al. is the foundation for their highly influential work on artistic style transfer. It would be interesting to see a short discussion on whether the authors believe their findings might extend to that domain. Is texture synthesis a special case where random projections are sufficient, or could a similar random-filter model potentially succeed at style transfer?
*   **Variability:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. The paper argues that the simpler model is less prone to local minima and thus might produce samples with higher perceptual similarity but lower variability. This is a compelling idea. Visualizing this by showing several different synthesized samples for the same texture from both the proposed model and the VGG model could provide qualitative support for this claim.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   How sensitive are your results to the choice of the L-BFGS optimizer? Have you experimented with other common optimizers like Adam, and if so, how did they perform in comparison?
*   You demonstrate that the VGG-loss metric sometimes fails to align with human perception for highly regular textures. Do you have an intuition for why this might be the case? Does it suggest that VGG features, trained for object recognition, are less adept at capturing long-range spatial periodicity compared to your multi-scale random filters?
*   The choice of filter sizes in the multi-scale model appears to be key. Could you elaborate on how this set of scales was chosen? Was it the result of a systematic search, or based on prior knowledge? How robust is the model's performance to changes in the number or sizes of these scales?
