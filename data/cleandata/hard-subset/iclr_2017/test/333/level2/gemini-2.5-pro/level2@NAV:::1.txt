This paper investigates the fundamental components required for neural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained networks are essential. The main contribution is the demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. which uses a 19-layer VGG network. The authors systematically show that neither network depth nor supervised feature learning is indispensable, proposing this simple architecture as a strong minimal baseline for the task.

I strongly recommend **Accept**. This work delivers a significant, surprising, and well-supported finding that provides a fundamental insight into neural generative models. The paper's core strength lies in its "less is more" approach, performing a crucial ablation study that was missing in the literature. By questioning the necessity of complexity, the authors reframe the understanding of what makes these texture models effective, identifying the key mechanism as the matching of second-order statistics in a sufficiently rich, non-linear filter bank.

The claims are convincingly supported through extensive experimentation. The authors compare a wide range of single-layer models with different filter initializations (random, Fourier, K-means) against the deep baseline, using both qualitative visual examples and a quantitative metric based on the VGG model's loss. The visual results are compelling, particularly for the multi-scale random model. This rigorous evaluation clearly demonstrates that architectural depth and learned features, while potentially beneficial for efficiency, are not the primary drivers of synthesis quality. The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and correctly positioned within the existing literature.

For potential improvement, while the discussion on the trade-off between the model's computational inefficiency (large filters) and simplicity is noted, a brief quantitative comparison of synthesis time versus VGG-loss could further strengthen this point.

I would like to ask the authors two clarifying questions:
1.  You mention that the optimization for the single-layer model is more robust. Could you provide more intuition or evidence for this? Is it due to a smoother loss landscape compared to deeper models?
2.  Have you considered a small-scale human study to validate how well the proposed VGG-loss metric correlates with human perceptual judgments of texture quality?
