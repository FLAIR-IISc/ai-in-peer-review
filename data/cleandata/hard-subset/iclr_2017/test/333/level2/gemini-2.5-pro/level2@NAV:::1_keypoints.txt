- Main claim: A single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters can synthesize high-quality textures, often rivaling Gatys et al.'s 19-layer VGG-based method.
- Contribution: Demonstrates depth and supervised pretraining are not essential for neural texture synthesis, proposing a simple model as a strong minimal baseline.
- Insight: Identifies matching second-order statistics in a sufficiently rich, non-linear filter bank as the key mechanism behind texture synthesis.
- Experimental rigor: Systematic comparisons across single-layer models with different filter initializations (random, Fourier, K-means) against the deep baseline.
- Evaluation: Uses both qualitative visual examples and a quantitative VGG-based loss metric to support claims.
- Results: Multi-scale random filter models produce particularly compelling visual results, showing architectural depth and learned features are not primary drivers of synthesis quality.
- Framing: Paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and properly situated within prior literature, filling a missing ablation study.
- Caveat: Notes a trade-off between model simplicity and computational inefficiency (large filters) versus efficiency advantages of deeper/learned models.
- Suggested improvement: Add a brief quantitative comparison of synthesis time versus VGG-loss to strengthen the computational trade-off discussion.
- Clarifying question 1: Request more intuition/evidence on why optimization for the single-layer model is more robust—possible smoother loss landscape?
- Clarifying question 2: Ask whether a small-scale human study was considered to validate how well the VGG-loss metric correlates with human perceptual judgments.
