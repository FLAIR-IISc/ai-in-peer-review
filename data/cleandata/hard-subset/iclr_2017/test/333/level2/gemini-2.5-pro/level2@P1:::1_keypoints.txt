- Paper shows a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent multi-scale filters can synthesize high-quality textures, often matching or beating 19-layer VGG results.  
- Challenges the necessity of depth, pooling, and supervised pre-trained filters for texture synthesis.  
- Main contribution: a minimal, powerful baseline using many random filters across scales for parametric texture synthesis.  
- Introduces a VGG-based quantitative metric (synthesis loss) to objectively evaluate texture quality.  
- Demonstrates a trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and limitations of Maximum Entropy for finite images.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Significant and surprising finding forcing re-evaluation of assumptions about generative texture models.  
- Key reason 2: Thorough, systematic empirical validation across filter types, quantities, and scales with strong visual and quantitative evidence.  
- Ablation experiments isolate that multi-scale features, not large receptive fields or depth, are critical for success.  
- Empirical results show the random multi-scale model can sometimes achieve lower VGG-loss than the VGG-based model, suggesting optimization or model issues in deep networks.  
- Request for clarification on optimization: scaling loss/gradients by 1e7 and whether success depends on tuned optimization versus architecture alone.  
- Ask to quantify computational cost: compare synthesis time of random multi-scale model versus VGG-based model.  
- Suggest brief discussion on applicability to style transfer: could random-filter models extend beyond texture synthesis?  
- Recommend visualizing sample variability by showing multiple synthesized samples from both models to support claims about variability and local minima.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to L-BFGS vs other optimizers (e.g., Adam); why VGG-loss misaligns with perception for regular textures; how filter scales were chosen and robustness to scale/number changes.
