### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption in recent literature that deep, hierarchical representations from supervisedly pre-trained networks (like VGG) are essential for this task. The authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art model of Gatys et al. (2015a) by testing a wide range of much simpler models. The core contribution is the surprising and powerful demonstration that a single convolutional layer with random, multi-scale filters, and no pooling, can generate textures of a perceptual quality that often rivals or even exceeds the deep VGG-based model. The paper supports this claim with extensive qualitative examples and introduces a quantitative metric based on the VGG model's own loss function to provide a more objective evaluation. The work provides a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis and forces a re-evaluation of the roles of depth and feature learning in generative models for textures.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a compelling and counter-intuitive result: that a very simple, shallow architecture with random weights can match the performance of a complex, deep, pre-trained network on texture synthesis. This is a fundamental contribution that simplifies our understanding of the problem and provides a much more interpretable baseline for future work.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are not based on a single cherry-picked result. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing various single-layer models with different filter initializations (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), numbers of filters, and filter scales. This systematic approach effectively isolates the key ingredients for success (multi-scale filters, non-linearities, and a sufficient number of features) and convincingly demonstrates that learned weights and deep hierarchies are not among them for this task.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its "less is more" conclusion, which is cleanly demonstrated through well-designed experiments. The state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. left open the question of *why* it worked so well, with depth and learned features being the commonly accepted reasons. This paper performs a crucial scientific ablation study, showing these factors to be non-essential. The results in Figure 1 and Figure 4 are striking; the quality of textures from the random multi-scale model is visually excellent and, in some cases (e.g., the regular brick wall and grids), perceptually superior to the VGG-based synthesis.

The investigation into what *is* crucial is also well executed. Figure 2 provides a clear demonstration of the importance of combining filters of multiple scales, as small filters miss long-range structure and large filters miss fine detail. The ablation showing the necessity of the ReLU non-linearity further strengthens the analysis.

Furthermore, the paper’s discussion is insightful. The authors astutely point out the paradox of using the VGG loss to evaluate their own model and are transparent about its limitations, noting its failure to capture the perceptual advantage of their method on some textures. The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and the connection to the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images, adds significant intellectual depth and points toward important future research directions.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a clever way to introduce objectivity, but it is also inherently circular, as the authors acknowledge. It might be beneficial to further emphasize in the main text that this metric should be interpreted with caution, as it is a proxy for human perception that is biased towards the very model being challenged. The cases where it fails (e.g., the grid textures) are actually strong evidence for the authors' model and this point could be made more forcefully.
*   The optimization details are a bit sparse. The scaling of the loss by 10^7 is mentioned as a practical necessity to avoid early stopping. A brief sentence explaining *why* this is necessary (e.g., due to the precision of the optimizer or the typical magnitude of the gradients) would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   The paper mentions that a hierarchical architecture would be more computationally efficient than using large filters in a single layer. To make this point more concrete, it would be useful to report the approximate synthesis times for the random multi-scale model versus the VGG model. This would help readers better understand the practical trade-offs.
*   The discussion on variability is excellent. The proposed inpainting experiment is a very creative idea for a more robust, human-in-the-loop evaluation of a texture model's ability to capture the underlying distribution.

**5. Questions for the Authors**
*   You note that initializing the VGG-based synthesis with the output of your single-layer model improves results, suggesting the VGG optimization is prone to poor local minima. Did you experiment with alternative optimization strategies or optimizers (e.g., Adam instead of L-BFGS) for the VGG model to see if this performance gap could be closed?
*   The success of random filters is a key finding. Does the specific distribution from which the filters are drawn matter? For instance, have you compared the uniform initialization from Glorot & Bengio with a simple Gaussian initialization? It would be interesting to know how sensitive the results are to this choice.
*   In your discussion of the Maximum Entropy framework, you introduce the concept of an ε-environment around the reference statistics. Could you elaborate on how the choice of optimizer and its convergence criteria (e.g., number of iterations, tolerance) implicitly defines this ε for your synthesis procedure?
