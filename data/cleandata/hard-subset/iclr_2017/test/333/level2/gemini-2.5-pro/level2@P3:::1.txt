**Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. It challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical models with features pre-trained on large-scale datasets (such as VGG) are indispensable for this task. The central contribution is the demonstration that a strikingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate natural textures of high perceptual quality. The work systematically explores a range of single-layer architectures, comparing filters derived from different principles (random, Fourier basis, k-means) and scales. Through extensive qualitative examples and a novel quantitative metric based on the VGG synthesis loss, the paper shows its minimal model often produces results that are competitive with, and occasionally superior to, the state-of-the-art deep learning approach.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. Its primary strengths are:
1.  It presents a surprising and significant finding that challenges the community's assumptions about the necessity of depth and learned features for a key generative task. This provides a crucial and powerful baseline for future research in texture synthesis and related areas.
2.  The claims are supported by a thorough and convincing set of experiments, with strong visual evidence and a thoughtful attempt at quantitative evaluation.

**Supporting Arguments**

The work makes a significant contribution by deconstructing a state-of-the-art method and identifying a far simpler alternative that retains high performance. The core finding—that a single layer of random filters is sufficient to model complex texture statistics—is counter-intuitive and forces a re-evaluation of why deep, trained networks have been so successful. This type of analysis, which isolates the truly essential components of a complex model, is of high value.

The empirical investigation is comprehensive. The paper does not merely present a single successful random model but compares a variety of filter initialization strategies (random, k-means, PCA, Fourier) and architectural choices (number of filters, filter scale). The development of the multi-scale random model is particularly effective, as it demonstrates how long-range correlations can be captured without resorting to a deep hierarchy. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling and strongly support the paper's main claims.

The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality is a welcome addition to a field that often relies solely on subjective visual inspection. While the paper appropriately acknowledges the metric's limitations, its use adds a layer of rigor to the comparisons and provides a more objective basis for the claims.

The discussion is insightful, touching upon the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, the connection to the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images, and the role of the optimization procedure. This demonstrates a deep engagement with the problem and its broader context.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Elaboration on Optimization:** The introduction notes that the discrepancy with prior work on random networks may stem from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically scaling the loss function. This is a critical detail. The paper would be enhanced by a more detailed discussion of this point. For instance, clarifying how sensitive the synthesis quality is to this scaling factor and whether the optimization landscape of the shallow model is qualitatively different from that of the deep VGG model could provide valuable insights for practitioners.
*   **Characterizing the VGG-loss Metric:** The paper correctly notes that the VGG-loss is an imperfect measure of perceptual quality, especially for regular, man-made textures. It would be beneficial to further characterize these failure modes. A more formal discussion of what types of structures the VGG feature space fails to properly penalize could help guide the development of better perceptual metrics in the future.
*   **Quantifying Computational Trade-offs:** The discussion mentions that the single-layer multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to its large filters. Quantifying this would be helpful for readers. A small table comparing the wall-clock time required for synthesis with the proposed multi-scale model versus the VGG-based model would make the practical trade-offs clearer.
*   **Connection to Style Transfer:** The Gram matrix-based statistics explored here are also central to the task of neural style transfer. A brief comment in the discussion on whether these findings might generalize—i.e., whether a shallow random network could also be sufficient to capture artistic style—would broaden the impact of the work.

**Clarifying Questions**

Answering the following questions could further clarify some aspects of the work.

*   The paper notes that initializing the VGG-based synthesis with textures from the single-layer model "consistently yields superior synthesis results." This suggests the deep model's optimization is prone to poor local minima. Does this imply that the primary benefit of the deep VGG model might be its feature representation, but its optimization landscape is a significant weakness for this synthesis task?
*   The observation that deeper random networks could sometimes exactly reconstruct the reference texture is fascinating. Could more details be provided on the architectures that exhibited this behavior? This seems highly relevant to the discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and how model capacity (in terms of the number of constraints) impacts this trade-off for finite-sized images.
