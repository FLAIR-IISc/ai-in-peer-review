- Main claim: A single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters can produce high-quality parametric texture synthesis, challenging the need for deep pretrained networks.
- Methodology: Systematic comparison of single-layer architectures and filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA) and scales, including a multi-scale random model.
- Quantitative metric: Introduces a VGG-based synthesis loss as a proxy for perceptual quality to complement visual inspection.
- Empirical result: The simple single-layer multi-scale model often matches or outperforms state-of-the-art deep methods on perceptual quality.
- Contribution: Provides a powerful, surprising baseline that forces re-evaluation of why depth and learned features help in generative texture tasks.
- Experimental rigor: Thorough empirical evaluation with strong visual examples and thoughtful quantitative comparisons.
- Insightful discussion: Addresses trade-offs between perceptual similarity and sample variability, links to Maximum Entropy for finite images, and considers optimization’s role.
- Optimization concern: Paper should elaborate how loss scaling and optimization tuning affect results and whether the shallow model’s optimization landscape differs from VGG’s.
- Metric limitations: VGG-loss can fail for regular, man-made textures; more characterization of these failure modes is advised.
- Practical costs: The multi-scale single-layer model is computationally inefficient due to large filters; quantifying wall-clock time would clarify trade-offs.
- Broader impact: Suggests potential implications for related tasks like neural style transfer; a brief discussion of generalization would strengthen the paper.
- Clarifying questions: Request clarification on whether VGG synthesis benefits mainly from representation or suffers from poor local minima, and details on deeper random networks that sometimes exactly reconstruct textures.
