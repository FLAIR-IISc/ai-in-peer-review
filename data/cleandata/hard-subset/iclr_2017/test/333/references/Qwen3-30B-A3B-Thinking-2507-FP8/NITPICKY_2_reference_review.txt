

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims

This paper investigates whether hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are truly necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures of perceptual quality rivaling the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), challenging the assumption that deep networks with trained filters are indispensable. They present a strong minimal baseline using various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters) and demonstrate that random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images. The paper further introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based synthesis loss and discusses implications for maximum entropy texture modeling.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and methodological flaws that undermine its core claims and scientific rigor, making it difficult to assess the validity of the central contribution.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The most serious issue is a fundamental mathematical error in the paper's methodology. In section 2, the authors describe "Fourier-3267" as having "3 · 3 · 3 · 121 = 3267 feature maps." However, with 3 color channels (R, G, B) and 3 weighting terms (wR, wG, wB ∈ [1, 0, -1]), the calculation should be 3 channels × 3 weightings × 121 Fourier basis elements = 1,089 feature maps, not 3,267. This error indicates a fundamental misunderstanding of the architecture and casts doubt on the paper's technical credibility.

The paper also makes unsupported claims based on "anecdotal evidence" (Section 7): "we found some anecdotal evidence (not shown) in deeper multi-layer random CNNs where the reference texture was exactly reconstructed during the synthesis." Scientific papers cannot rely on unverified, unreported observations to support their claims.

Additional significant issues include:

- A broken reference to VGG implementation (https://github.com/Lasagne/Recipes/blob/master/modelzoo/vgg19.py as accessed on 12.05.2016), which is problematic for reproducibility.
- The Glorot & Bengio citation is incomplete ("Glorot & Bengio, )").
- The paper claims random filters "perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" without providing statistical significance tests for these comparisons.
- The paper uses "VGG-based loss" as a quantitative measure but doesn't establish its correlation with human perception, which is the standard evaluation metric in texture synthesis.
- The reference list has inconsistent formatting (some entries use "et al." while others don't), and the citations in the text don't match the reference list formatting (e.g., "Gatys et al., 2015a" vs. "Gatys et al.,? \Q2015\E" in references).

These issues collectively undermine the paper's scientific rigor and make it impossible to confidently assess the validity of the central claim that random filters can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis.

## 4. Additional feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the mathematical error in the Fourier-3267 architecture description and recalculate all affected numbers.
2. Replace the "anecdotal evidence" with proper quantitative results or remove the claim.
3. Fix the broken VGG reference and provide a complete, reproducible implementation.
4. Include statistical significance tests for all comparative claims.
5. Provide a thorough comparison between the VGG-based loss metric and human evaluation results.
6. Standardize all citation formats throughout the paper and reference list.
7. Clarify which specific VGG architecture was used (e.g., VGG-19 with average pooling) and justify the choice.

## Questions for authors

1. Can you provide a corrected calculation for the number of feature maps in the Fourier-3267 architecture, and explain how this error affects your experimental results?
2. What specific evidence supports your claim that random filters perform better than trained filters? Please provide quantitative results with statistical significance testing.
3. How does the VGG-based loss metric correlate with human perception of texture quality? Can you provide a quantitative comparison between the two?
4. Why does the paper claim that random filters work better than trained filters, given that the filters were not adapted to the specific textures being synthesized? This seems counterintuitive to the standard understanding of filter learning.
5. What specific optimization parameters or techniques did you use to achieve better results with random filters compared to Gatys et al. (2015a), which reported that random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images?
