

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling and surprising contribution to the field of texture synthesis by challenging a fundamental assumption in the literature. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random filters can generate textures of quality that rivals state-of-the-art methods, including the influential work by Gatys et al. (2015a). The paper systematically tests various single-layer architectures with different filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and shows that random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images. The authors also introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based loss to complement traditional human evaluation. Their key contribution is establishing a strong minimal baseline that demonstrates neither hierarchical multi-layer representations nor supervised training of feature spaces are necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis. The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation across multiple textures and architectures, with visually compelling results that challenge the prevailing wisdom in the field.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental question in texture synthesis that has been overlooked by the field, challenging a key assumption that has been accepted without sufficient scrutiny.
2. The experimental results are strong, well-documented, and visually compelling, with clear evidence that random filters can outperform trained filters in some cases.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

This paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable for natural texture generation as previously thought. The authors provide a well-structured and thorough experimental evaluation that systematically tests various single-layer architectures, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. It clearly builds upon the work of Gatys et al. (2015a) while challenging the key assumptions of that work. The authors effectively position their contribution as going in the opposite direction of previous efforts (which added elements to improve quality) by asking what is truly indispensable.

The experimental methodology is sound and well-described. The use of VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure for texture quality is a useful contribution that could be adopted by other researchers. The visual examples in Figure 4 are particularly compelling, showing that the random filter model often generates textures that are visually indistinguishable from state-of-the-art methods.

The paper makes a significant theoretical contribution by challenging the prevailing view that deep hierarchical representations are necessary for natural image generation. This could influence future work in generative models and texture synthesis, potentially leading to more efficient and interpretable models.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to enhance its impact and rigor:

1. **Clarify the evaluation methodology**: The paper claims that the VGG-based loss is a good proxy for human perception, but it doesn't provide sufficient evidence for this claim. The authors should include a comparison between the VGG-based loss and human evaluations on a subset of the synthesized textures. How many human subjects were involved? What was the protocol? How was the correlation between VGG-based loss and human perception measured?

2. **Explain the paradox of random filters**: The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." This is a surprising finding that deserves more thorough explanation. The authors should discuss potential reasons for this phenomenon, such as the properties of random filters that make them particularly well-suited for texture synthesis.

3. **Provide computational efficiency data**: The paper mentions that "a more efficient way to achieve receptive fields of similar size would be to use a hierarchical multi-layer net" but doesn't provide concrete timing comparisons. The authors should include actual computational time measurements for different architectures to support this claim.

4. **Detail the optimization procedure**: The paper mentions "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" but doesn't provide details. The authors should clarify what specific changes they made to the optimization (beyond the standard L-BFGS-B settings) that led to better results with single-layer networks.

5. **Discuss limitations**: The paper should acknowledge potential limitations of their approach, such as whether this method generalizes to other types of image generation beyond texture synthesis, or whether it works equally well for all types of textures.

## Questions for the Authors

1. How did the authors ensure that the VGG-based loss they use for evaluation is a good proxy for human perception? Did they conduct a human evaluation study to validate this metric?

2. The paper claims that "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." What specific properties of random filters might explain this surprising result?

3. The paper states that "the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Can the authors explain this paradox, and does it suggest that the VGG-based loss is not a perfect metric for texture quality?

4. The paper mentions that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state of the art." What is the quantitative difference between the multi-scale model and the state-of-the-art in terms of the VGG-based loss? How often does it outperform, and by how much?

5. How do the authors explain the observation that "the reference texture was exactly reconstructed during the synthesis" in some deeper multi-layer random CNNs? Does this suggest a limitation of the Gram matrix as a constraint?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field by challenging a key assumption in texture synthesis. With the suggested revisions, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
