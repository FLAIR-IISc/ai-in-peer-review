- Paper challenges the assumption that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training are necessary for natural texture synthesis.  
- Main finding: a single convolutional layer with random filters can produce textures rivaling state-of-the-art deep pre-trained methods.  
- Contributions: establishes a strong minimal baseline, argues depth/pre-training aren’t essential, introduces a VGG-based quantitative metric, and frames texture synthesis in a maximum entropy context.  
- Decision: Accept — addresses a fundamental question with thorough, convincing experiments.  
- Experimental rigor: systematic testing of single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA) and varying filter counts shows random filters often outperform trained ones.  
- Qualitative results: multi-scale random models frequently match or surpass Gatys et al. (2015a) and hand-crafted parametric methods in perceptual quality.  
- Quantitative metric: introduces VGG loss as an objective evaluation tool while acknowledging its limitations and imperfect correlation with perception.  
- Ablations: multi-scale filter combinations are crucial for modeling multiple correlation ranges and outperform single-scale alternatives.  
- Theoretical framing: situates results in the maximum entropy framework, offering broader theoretical insight.  
- Requested improvements: add a fair computational time/memory comparison to assess efficiency vs. deeper models.  
- Requested improvements: provide a deeper analysis of VGG-loss failure cases (e.g., regular textures like brick walls).  
- Requested improvements: clarify optimization procedure details to separate effects of optimization from filter type.  
- Requested improvements: more systematic exploration of how performance scales with filter bank size beyond two tested sizes.  
- Question: why do 55×55 single filters and multi-scale 11×11 filters with equivalent cumulative receptive fields perform differently?  
- Question: how much of performance gain is due to the optimization procedure versus using random filters, and would applying the same optimization to Gatys et al. change results?  
- Question: for cases where multi-scale samples have lower VGG loss but look better, what perceptual differences (e.g., sample variability) explain this paradox?  
- Question: how do authors reconcile that depth isn’t necessary for texture quality but deep networks remain broadly successful and more efficient in other vision tasks?
