

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling challenge to a fundamental assumption in the field of natural texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical representations with supervised pre-training are essential for generating high-quality natural textures. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random filters can synthesize textures that rival the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), which relies on a deep VGG network with pre-trained weights. The paper's key contributions include:

- Proposing a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer CNN with random filters
- Demonstrating that textures synthesized from this baseline often rival or exceed the quality of state-of-the-art methods
- Systematically testing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned)
- Introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss
- Providing theoretical insights into the relationship between summary statistics, perceptual similarity, and texture variability

The paper is well-structured, clearly motivated, and makes a significant contribution to understanding the fundamental requirements for natural texture generation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental question in texture synthesis that has been largely unchallenged in the literature.
2. The experimental evidence is robust, comprehensive, and well-organized, with clear comparisons to state-of-the-art methods.
3. The findings challenge a commonly held assumption in the field and provide new insights into what aspects of deep networks are truly necessary for texture synthesis.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's central claim is well-supported by rigorous experiments. The authors systematically test multiple single-layer network configurations (with different filter types and numbers) against the VGG-based texture synthesis model. Their results consistently show that single-layer networks with random filters can generate textures of comparable or superior quality to the state-of-the-art. This is particularly striking given that the VGG model relies on a deep network with supervised training.

The paper makes a strong case for the following insights:
- A single-layer network with random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis
- The depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable as previously thought
- The combination of multiple filter scales (multi-scale architecture) is crucial for capturing both small- and long-range texture statistics
- Non-linearities (ReLUs) are essential for capturing statistical dependencies in textures

The paper's quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss is particularly valuable as it provides an objective measure of texture quality, complementing the traditional human inspection approach. The authors also thoughtfully discuss the limitations of this measure and the importance of balancing perceptual similarity with texture variability.

The theoretical discussion in Section 7 about the relationship between the MaxEnt framework and practical texture synthesis is insightful and adds depth to the paper's contributions. The authors provide a nuanced understanding of why their simple model works well, connecting it to the broader context of texture modeling.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance its quality:

1. **Clarify the "random" filter generation process**: The paper mentions filters are drawn from a uniform distribution according to Glorot & Bengio, but doesn't specify the exact distribution parameters. Providing the specific initialization parameters (e.g., Glorot uniform with range [-sqrt(6/(fan_in+fan_out)), sqrt(6/(fan_in+fan_out))]) would make the experiments more reproducible.

2. **Expand the discussion of computational efficiency**: The paper mentions the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters, but doesn't quantify this. A brief comparison of training times or computational requirements between the single-layer random model and the VGG-based model would strengthen this point.

3. **Include more quantitative comparison metrics**: While the VGG-based loss is a good measure, including additional quantitative metrics like SSIM (Structural Similarity Index) or LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity) would provide a more comprehensive evaluation.

4. **Address potential limitations**: The paper focuses on natural textures but doesn't discuss whether these findings generalize to artistic style transfer or other image generation tasks. A brief discussion of potential limitations or future directions for applying these insights to other domains would be valuable.

5. **Improve the visualization quality**: The figures (particularly Figure 4) could benefit from higher resolution and more consistent presentation to better highlight the differences between the models.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it because the random filters happen to capture the relevant statistics naturally, or is it due to the optimization procedure?

2. In Figure 2, the multi-scale model with 1024 filters (3×3, 5×5, 7×7, 9×9, 11×11) outperforms single-scale models. Could you explain why the specific combination of these filter sizes (rather than, say, 3×3, 7×7, 11×11) was chosen, and whether the results would change with different combinations?

3. The paper notes that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Could you elaborate on why this paradoxical result occurs, and what it might imply about the limitations of the VGG-based loss as a perceptual metric?

4. The paper mentions that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus loses variability." How would you modify the optimization procedure to better balance perceptual similarity and variability, and what would be the expected impact on the synthesis quality?

5. The authors state that "for finite size images it is well possible that only the reference image can exactly reproduce all the summary statistics." How does this relate to the concept of "overfitting" in texture synthesis, and what implications does this have for the design of future texture synthesis models?
