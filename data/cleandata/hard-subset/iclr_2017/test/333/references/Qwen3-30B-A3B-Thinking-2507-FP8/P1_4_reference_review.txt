

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper challenges a fundamental assumption in the field of texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are essential for generating high-quality natural textures. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random, data-independent filters can synthesize textures that often rival the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a). Their key contributions include:

- Presenting a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer network with random filters
- Systematically testing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), sizes (3×3 to 55×55), and numbers (363 to 3267 feature maps)
- Proposing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss, replacing traditional human inspection
- Demonstrating that both depth and pre-training of multi-layer representations are not as indispensable as previously thought
- Providing a thoughtful discussion connecting their findings to the Maximum Entropy framework and the balance between perceptual similarity and variability

The paper makes a compelling case that the hierarchical multi-layer representation and supervised training of feature spaces, which have been considered essential in previous work, are not actually necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging a widely held assumption in the field and providing robust empirical evidence to support its claims. The key reason for acceptance is that the authors demonstrate with comprehensive experiments that single-layer random filter networks can generate textures of comparable quality to the state-of-the-art, fundamentally changing our understanding of what's required for natural texture generation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's claims are well-supported by thorough experimentation. The authors systematically test multiple single-layer architectures across different filter types, sizes, and numbers, comparing them against the VGG-based state-of-the-art. Their results consistently show that random filters can produce textures matching or exceeding the quality of more complex models, with the multi-scale model (using filters of multiple sizes) particularly excelling.

The paper's methodological contribution of introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based loss is valuable and well-justified. The authors demonstrate that this metric correlates well with perceptual quality, with only a few exceptions for highly regular textures. This addresses a significant limitation of previous work that relied solely on subjective human evaluation.

The theoretical discussion connecting texture synthesis to the Maximum Entropy framework is insightful and provides a meaningful context for their empirical findings. The authors thoughtfully address the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, which is crucial for texture synthesis. Their observation that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages for highly regular textures (like brick walls) is particularly important for understanding the limitations of current evaluation metrics.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

### Suggestions for Improvement

1. **Comparison to More Recent Methods**: While the paper focuses on Gatys et al. (2015a), it would be valuable to briefly compare with more recent texture synthesis methods (e.g., those using GANs or other generative models) to better position the contribution in the current landscape.

2. **Computational Efficiency Discussion**: The paper mentions that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters. A brief comparison of runtime and memory requirements between the single-layer random model and the VGG-based model would strengthen the practical relevance of their findings.

3. **Variability Analysis**: The paper mentions that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus looses variability" but doesn't provide concrete analysis of this trade-off. Adding a quantitative measure of variability across different models would be valuable.

### Questions for Authors

1. In Figure 4, the multi-scale model outperforms the VGG-based model on some textures (e.g., brick wall, grids) but underperforms on others (e.g., pebble texture). Can the authors provide more insight into why this pattern occurs? Is it related to the specific statistical properties of these textures?

2. The paper states that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Why would a model that directly optimizes the VGG-based loss produce higher VGG-based loss values than a completely different model? Is this related to the optimization process or the nature of the textures?

3. The authors note that "the scale of these structures extends far beyond the receptive fields of the single units" in the single-layer models. How do they explain this phenomenon, given that the filters are only 11×11 in size? What mechanisms allow the single-layer model to capture such long-range structures?

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field by demonstrating that the depth and pre-training of feature spaces are not essential for natural texture synthesis. The thorough experimental evaluation, thoughtful theoretical discussion, and introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric make this work a valuable addition to the literature. I believe it will have significant impact on how we understand and approach texture synthesis and potentially other image generation tasks.
