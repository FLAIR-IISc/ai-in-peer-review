

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper investigates whether hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are truly necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors propose a simple single-layer CNN with random filters as a strong baseline for texture synthesis, demonstrating that it can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art approaches. The paper introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss and challenges the common belief that deep networks with pre-trained features are essential for natural texture generation. The authors also explore the importance of multi-scale filters, non-linearities, and the number of feature maps.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting idea but has several methodological issues and lacks sufficient quantitative support for its claims. The paper's claims about the superiority of random filters over trained filters are not statistically validated, and the paper includes numerous minor technical inconsistencies that undermine its credibility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of rigorous statistical validation for the key claims. The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" but provides no statistical significance tests for these comparisons. Without such tests, it's impossible to determine if the observed differences are meaningful or merely due to random variation in the texture synthesis process.

The paper also makes ad hoc adjustments without justification. The claim that "we scale the loss and the gradients by a factor of 10^7 in order to avoid early stopping of the optimization algorithm" is particularly concerning. This heuristic adjustment is not properly validated or explained, potentially masking convergence issues or making the optimization procedure less reliable. The paper should provide evidence that this scaling factor is necessary and not masking convergence problems.

Additionally, the paper's evaluation is primarily qualitative, relying on visual comparisons without providing quantitative metrics for the performance differences between models. The VGG loss is used as a metric, but the paper doesn't establish a strong correlation between this metric and human perception beyond the examples shown. The claim that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" lacks supporting evidence.

The paper also contains numerous minor technical inconsistencies that suggest insufficient proofreading:
- Inconsistent citation formatting throughout the reference list (e.g., "Glorot & Bengio, )" with a missing closing parenthesis)
- Inconsistent notation (M used for both vectorized texture size and number of feature maps)
- Inconsistent use of "VGG network" vs. "VGG network"
- In the Fourier-3267 description, the paper states "3 · 3 · 3 · 121 = 3267 feature maps" but doesn't clearly explain why three 3's are used (3 color channels × 3 weightings)
- The paper refers to "Appendix A, Fig. 5" but the appendix isn't provided

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons between different filter types. This would require multiple trials per texture and proper hypothesis testing.

2. Justify all heuristic choices (like the 10^7 scaling factor) with proper analysis or validation. Explain why this scaling is necessary and provide evidence that it doesn't mask convergence issues.

3. Include quantitative metrics (e.g., mean VGG loss across multiple textures) to support the qualitative claims about performance differences.

4. Clarify all notation to avoid confusion (e.g., don't use M for both vectorized texture size and number of feature maps).

5. Fix all citation formatting inconsistencies and missing parentheses in the references.

6. Provide a clearer explanation of why the multi-scale model (with 1024 feature maps) was chosen as the comparison point, rather than using the same number of feature maps as the other models (363 or 3267).

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" but doesn't specify the statistical test used to determine this. What statistical tests were performed to validate this claim?

2. The paper mentions "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models" but doesn't provide quantitative metrics for this claim. Can you provide mean VGG loss values for the multi-scale model versus single-scale models across all tested textures?

3. The paper uses VGG loss as a proxy for perceptual quality but doesn't establish a correlation between this loss and human ratings. Can you provide evidence that this loss metric correlates with human perception?

4. The paper states "we scale the loss and the gradients by a factor of 10^7 in order to avoid early stopping of the optimization algorithm." What evidence do you have that this scaling factor is necessary and not masking convergence issues?
