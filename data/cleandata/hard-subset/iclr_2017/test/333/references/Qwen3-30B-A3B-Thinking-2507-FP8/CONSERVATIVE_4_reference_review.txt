

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper challenges the established view that deep hierarchical CNNs with pre-trained weights are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures that rival or even surpass the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), which uses a deep VGG network. Their main contributions include presenting a minimal baseline using random filters, demonstrating that multi-scale models outperform single-scale ones, and proposing a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG loss. The paper also argues that neither the hierarchical structure nor supervised training of features is imperative for texture modeling.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper raises interesting questions about the necessity of deep networks for texture synthesis, it fails to provide rigorous evidence to support its bold claims. The paper's main contribution is not sufficiently validated against established baselines or with scientifically rigorous evaluation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary claim—that random filters can rival pre-trained deep networks for texture synthesis—lacks sufficient empirical validation. The evidence provided is primarily qualitative (human inspection) with limited quantitative support. Several key concerns undermine the paper's claims:

First, the paper acknowledges that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" but fails for textures with regular structures like walls and grids. This directly contradicts their claim that their method "often rivals the current state of the art." The authors show that for some textures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from their multi-scale model than for samples from the VGG-based model (which directly optimized the VGG-based loss), yet they present this as evidence of superiority without addressing the perceptual mismatch.

Second, the paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison against established baselines. The only baseline mentioned is Gatys et al. (2015a), but the paper fails to compare against the previous state-of-the-art (Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000), which was the standard before deep learning approaches. Without this comparison, the claim that their method "surpasses parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli" (2000) lacks evidence.

Third, the paper does not adequately explain why their optimization procedure is superior to previous work. The authors claim that "this discrepancy originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," but provide no systematic comparison of optimization parameters or convergence behavior. The claim that "the single-layer model is less prone to local minima" is unsupported by any statistical analysis or comparative evidence beyond a single example in Appendix A.

Fourth, the paper's quantitative evaluation is limited to a few examples (Fig. 4) without statistical significance testing. The claim that their method "often rivals the current state of the art" requires broader evaluation with proper statistical validation across multiple textures and metrics.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct a comprehensive comparison against established baselines, including Portilla & Simoncelli (2000), and provide quantitative metrics beyond the VGG-based loss (e.g., SSIM, human evaluation with proper methodology).
2. Provide statistical validation for their claims, including significance testing across multiple textures.
3. More thoroughly explain the optimization differences and provide evidence that their method is less prone to local minima.
4. Address the discrepancy between their VGG-based loss metric and perceptual quality more directly.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images," yet the VGG-based loss is consistently higher for random filter models compared to Gatys et al. (2015a) on the same textures. How do you reconcile this with your claim that your method "rivals" the state-of-the-art?
2. The paper claims that "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures" (Fig. 1), but Fig. 4 shows cases where the VGG model performs better. Can you clarify this apparent contradiction?
3. The paper acknowledges that "the VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for regular textures. How would you address this limitation in practice when deploying your method?
4. The authors claim their optimization is "less prone to local minima," but provide no evidence beyond a single example. What systematic evidence do you have for this claim?
5. The paper states that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state of the art," but the evidence is limited to a few examples. How many textures were evaluated, and what statistical tests were performed to support this claim?

This paper makes an interesting observation but fails to provide the rigorous validation required to overturn established wisdom in the field. The authors have presented an intriguing hypothesis but have not yet provided sufficient evidence to support their bold claims.
