

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of texture synthesis by challenging fundamental assumptions about what is necessary for generating natural textures. The authors argue that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are not essential for high-quality texture synthesis, contrary to prevailing beliefs. Their key contributions include:

- Demonstrating that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art approaches (Gatys et al., 2015a)
- Showing that random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images
- Introducing a multi-scale model that combines different filter sizes to capture textures across multiple spatial scales
- Proposing a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based loss to complement traditional human evaluation
- Providing a thoughtful discussion of the relationship between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a fundamental question in the field with significant implications for our understanding of deep representation learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental question about what aspects of deep networks are truly necessary for texture synthesis, challenging established assumptions in the field.
2. The empirical evidence is strong, comprehensive, and well-structured with multiple ablation studies that support the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a compelling case that challenges the conventional wisdom in texture synthesis. The authors systematically test the necessity of key components in the Gatys et al. (2015a) approach by evaluating various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and filter sizes.

The most significant evidence comes from their multi-scale model, which combines different filter sizes (3×3, 11×11, 55×55) and outperforms both single-scale models and the VGG-based model for many textures. Their ablation studies showing that the combination of different scales is crucial (Fig. 2) and that ReLU non-linearities are necessary (Fig. 2, right column) provide strong support for their claims.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution by introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based loss, which addresses a key limitation of the field (reliance on qualitative human evaluation). The authors thoughtfully discuss the trade-offs between perceptual similarity and variability, acknowledging that their approach leans more toward perceptual similarity than the state-of-the-art, which explains their superior performance on some samples.

The paper is well-organized, clearly written, and places its work in proper context with a thorough literature review. The experimental design is rigorous, with multiple texture examples, various filter configurations, and careful ablation studies.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, these suggestions could further enhance its quality and impact:

1. **Clarify the "random filters" paradox**: The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better then filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" but only briefly suggests this is due to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." A more detailed explanation of why random filters work so well, and how the optimization procedure differs from previous work, would strengthen the paper significantly.

2. **Quantify the "rivaling" claim**: The paper states that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state of the art" but provides mostly qualitative comparisons. Including specific metrics (e.g., average VGG loss across multiple textures, or a brief human evaluation study) would make this claim more concrete and persuasive.

3. **Expand on the paradoxical VGG loss results**: The paper mentions that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." This is a fascinating observation that deserves more detailed discussion, as it potentially reveals limitations in the VGG-based loss as a perceptual metric.

4. **Include computational efficiency analysis**: The paper mentions the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model but doesn't provide any timing comparisons. A brief analysis of the time required for synthesis with different models would be valuable for practical applications.

5. **Clarify the relationship between filter size and receptive field**: The paper states that the multi-scale model's success comes from "the combination of different scales," but it's not entirely clear how the receptive field size (55×55) relates to the ability to capture long-range correlations. A more detailed explanation of this relationship would strengthen the argument.

## Questions for the Authors

1. The paper suggests the discrepancy between their results and Gatys et al. (2015a) regarding random filters "originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Could you elaborate on exactly what aspects of the optimization procedure were different, and how you determined these were the key factors?

2. The paper states that "random filters perform on par or better then filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Given that the filters themselves are random and data-independent, could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it simply that the optimization procedure was better tuned, or is there something about the random filters that makes them more suitable for texture synthesis?

3. You mention that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Could you elaborate on why this might occur, and what it implies about the VGG-based loss as a perceptual metric?

4. The paper discusses the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. How might one systematically adjust the optimization procedure to better balance these two aspects for different texture types, and what metrics could be used to measure this balance objectively?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of what is truly necessary for natural texture synthesis. By challenging established assumptions and providing strong empirical evidence, it opens up new avenues for research in generative modeling and deep representation learning. The work is scientifically rigorous, well-executed, and addresses an important question in the field. I recommend acceptance.
