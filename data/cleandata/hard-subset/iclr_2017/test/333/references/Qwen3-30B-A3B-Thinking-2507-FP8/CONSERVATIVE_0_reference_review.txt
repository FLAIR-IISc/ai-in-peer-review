

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper claims to investigate what is truly necessary for natural texture synthesis by challenging the established view that deep hierarchical representations with pre-trained features are essential. The authors present a single-layer convolutional neural network with random filters as a strong minimal baseline that can generate textures rivaling the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), which relies on a deep 19-layer VGG network. They argue that neither hierarchical multi-layer representations nor supervised training of feature spaces is indispensable for texture modeling, and they demonstrate that random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images. The paper also proposes a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss to replace human inspection for texture quality assessment.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a strong claim that contradicts established prior work without providing sufficiently rigorous evidence to support this contradiction. The evidence presented lacks statistical significance testing and comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim—that random filters in a single-layer network can rival deep network approaches—directly contradicts Gatys et al. (2015a), which established the necessity of deep representations for high-quality texture synthesis. However, the paper fails to provide compelling evidence for this contradiction. Key shortcomings include:

1. **Insufficient statistical evidence**: The paper relies on qualitative visual comparisons (Figures 1-4) without statistical significance testing to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to random chance or specific to the test images. The claim that "random filters perform on par or better" requires rigorous statistical validation.

2. **Flawed quantitative evaluation**: The paper uses a VGG-based loss as a proxy for human perception, but acknowledges in Section 7 that this metric "fails to capture the perceptual advantage" for certain textures (e.g., wall, grids). This undermines their main quantitative evaluation method, as they cannot claim their method outperforms the state-of-the-art when their evaluation metric itself is imperfect for key cases.

3. **Inadequate explanation of the discrepancy**: The authors attribute their success to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" compared to prior work, but fail to specify exactly what this tuning entails or how it differs from previous approaches. Without this clarification, their claim that random filters work better than previously reported remains unsubstantiated.

4. **Incomplete baseline comparisons**: The paper does not provide a comprehensive comparison with recent texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a), nor does it address how their method compares to more recent alternatives that might have emerged since the Gatys paper.

5. **Lack of ablation studies**: The paper does not conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the contribution of random filters from other methodological choices (e.g., number of feature maps, multi-scale approach). This makes it impossible to determine whether the improvement comes specifically from the random filters or other aspects of their approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide rigorous statistical significance testing for all claims about perceptual quality improvements, not just visual comparisons.

2. Conduct a more comprehensive comparison with relevant baselines, including recent texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a).

3. Address the limitations of their VGG-based loss metric more thoroughly and consider alternative evaluation methods that might better correlate with human perception.

4. Provide a detailed explanation of the optimization procedure differences that enable random filters to work, with clear comparisons to prior work.

5. Include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of random filters from other methodological choices.

6. Discuss the computational efficiency of their method in comparison to the baseline, as this is a critical factor for practical applications.

7. Analyze failure cases more thoroughly to understand when and why their method underperforms compared to the state-of-the-art.

**Questions for authors:**

1. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the visual comparisons shown in Figures 1-4, demonstrating that the observed differences in perceptual quality are not due to random chance?

2. How does your "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" specifically differ from what was used in previous work that reported random filters failing, and can you provide evidence that this isn't simply due to your specific optimization parameters?

3. Given that your VGG-based loss metric is acknowledged to fail for certain textures (wall, grids), how can you claim that your method "rivals the current state-of-the-art" when the metric used to support this claim is known to be imperfect for key cases?

4. Can you provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a), particularly those that might have addressed limitations of the original Gatys approach?
