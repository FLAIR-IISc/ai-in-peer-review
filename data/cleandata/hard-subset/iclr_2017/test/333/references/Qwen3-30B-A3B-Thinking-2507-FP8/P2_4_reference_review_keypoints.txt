- Main claim: Depth and supervised training are not essential for high-quality natural texture synthesis.  
- Key result: A single-layer CNN with random filters can produce textures rivaling deep-network approaches.  
- Contributions: Introduces a minimal baseline, shows pre-training/depth are dispensable, proposes a VGG-loss quantitative measure, and discusses theoretical implications.  
- Empirical evaluation: Thorough testing across Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters with varying feature map counts.  
- Multi-scale model: Combining filter sizes outperforms single-scale models and rivals Gatys et al. (2015a), especially for regular textures.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance due to addressing a fundamental question and robust empirical evidence.  
- Novelty/significance: Challenges the assumed necessity of deep supervised networks for texture synthesis with a well-designed experimental protocol.  
- Counterintuitive finding: Random filters (with many feature maps) often outperform trained K-means filters.  
- VGG-loss utility: The proposed quantitative VGG-loss generally correlates with perceptual quality and offers an alternative to human inspection.  
- Theoretical placement: Situates work relative to Gatys et al. and discusses MaxEnt framework limitations for finite-size textures.  
- Clarify why random filters work: Reviewer asks for a deeper theoretical explanation of random filters’ success and their statistical alignment with natural textures.  
- Quantitative comparisons needed: Request for aggregated metrics (e.g., average VGG loss) to substantiate claims about multi-scale vs. state-of-the-art performance.  
- Computational efficiency: Ask for quantified runtime/inference comparisons between the multi-scale model and VGG-based approaches.  
- MaxEnt connection: Request to clarify how the single-layer model fits into the MaxEnt theoretical framework.  
- Questions for authors: Provide specific optimization differences from Gatys et al., quantify perceptual differences (possibly via human study) for cases where VGG-loss fails, analyze diminishing returns/minimum feature-map counts, and supply detailed quantitative results (e.g., average VGG loss) supporting multi-scale superiority.
