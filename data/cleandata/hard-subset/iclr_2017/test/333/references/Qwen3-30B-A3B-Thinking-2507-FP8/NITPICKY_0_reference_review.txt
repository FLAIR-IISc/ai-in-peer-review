

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper challenges the assumption that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are essential for natural texture synthesis. The authors propose a minimal baseline using a single-layer CNN with random filters that can synthesize textures rivaling the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a). They demonstrate that neither network depth nor pre-training is indispensable, and they introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based synthesis loss. The paper also explores the importance of filter scale and non-linearities, and discusses theoretical implications for maximum entropy texture modeling.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents an interesting challenge to established assumptions, the methodological inconsistencies and lack of rigorous validation of key claims undermine the paper's scientific contribution.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's core claim—that single-layer random filters can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis—relies on a quantitative metric (VGG-based loss) that the authors themselves admit may not fully correlate with human perception. In Figure 4, they show the multi-scale random model often fails to match the VGG-based model's quality (e.g., for "brick wall" texture), yet they claim it "rivals" the state-of-the-art. This is misleading without human evaluation to validate their metric.

Several methodological inconsistencies further weaken the paper:
- The arbitrary scaling of loss/gradients by 10^7 (Section 4) lacks justification and could artificially affect results.
- The paper uses average-pooling instead of max-pooling in VGG (Section 5) without explaining why this choice was made or how it affects comparisons.
- They claim "the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model" (Section 6), but provide no statistical evidence for this claim.
- The reference to GitHub code (https://github.com/Lasagne/Recipes/blob/master/modelzoo/vgg19.py) as a citation is unprofessional and non-permanent.

The paper also makes unsupported claims: "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures" (Section 6), yet Figure 1 shows inconsistent performance across textures (e.g., "pebble" texture). The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for their key claim that "a larger number of feature maps generally increases perceptual quality."

Furthermore, the paper's theoretical discussion (Section 7) contains logical inconsistencies. They claim "Gram matrices are not treated as hard constraints but as soft constraints," yet the optimization procedure (Equation 5) appears to treat them as hard constraints. This contradiction undermines their theoretical contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide human evaluation results to validate their quantitative metric, rather than assuming VGG-loss correlates with perceptual quality.
2. Justify the arbitrary loss scaling factor (10^7) and the choice of average-pooling.
3. Conduct proper statistical testing for key claims about feature map count and filter size.
4. Replace the GitHub code reference with a formal citation to the original VGG implementation.
5. Clarify the relationship between their optimization procedure and the "soft constraints" they claim to use.
6. Provide more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of multi-scale filters versus increased receptive fields.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide human evaluation results comparing the multi-scale random model and the VGG-based model for the textures where the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantages (e.g., "wall" and "grids")?

2. How did you determine the 10^7 scaling factor for the loss and gradients? Was this chosen to make the results match your expectations, or was it based on empirical testing?

3. The paper claims "the Gram matrices are not treated as hard constraints," yet the optimization procedure (Equation 5) minimizes the distance between Gram matrices. How does this differ from treating them as hard constraints?

4. For the "brick wall" texture (Figure 4), the VGG-based model appears clearly superior to the multi-scale random model, yet the authors claim the random model "rivals" the state-of-the-art. How do you reconcile this discrepancy?

5. What is the computational cost (in terms of time and memory) of your multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based model? The paper mentions "computational inefficiency" but doesn't quantify it.

These issues collectively undermine the paper's scientific rigor, and the lack of proper validation for key claims makes it difficult to assess the true contribution of this work. While the core idea is intriguing, the methodological shortcomings prevent this paper from being suitable for publication at ICLR.
