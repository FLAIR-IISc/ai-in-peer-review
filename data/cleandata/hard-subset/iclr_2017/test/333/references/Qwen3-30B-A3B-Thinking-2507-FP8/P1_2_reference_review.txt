

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling and significant contribution to the field of image generation by challenging a fundamental assumption in deep learning-based texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are not necessary for generating high-quality natural textures. Instead, they present a simple yet powerful baseline using a single-layer CNN with random filters that can generate textures rivaling the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a). 

The key contributions include:
- Establishing a strong minimal baseline using a single-layer network with random, data-independent filters
- Demonstrating that textures synthesized from this baseline often match or exceed the perceptual quality of more complex approaches
- Systematically testing various single-layer architectures with different filter sizes and types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA)
- Introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG loss to complement human evaluation
- Providing insights into the relationship between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis

The paper makes a strong case that the depth and pre-training of multi-layer image representations are not as indispensable for natural texture generation as previously believed, which is a significant conceptual shift in the field.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with significant implications for understanding what aspects of deep learning are truly necessary for image generation tasks. The empirical evidence is robust and the findings are well-supported by thorough experimentation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in several key areas that justify acceptance:

First, the question the paper tackles is highly relevant and timely. The assumption that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are essential for texture synthesis has been widely accepted in the community. By challenging this assumption with rigorous empirical evidence, the authors make a valuable contribution to the field's understanding of what makes deep learning effective for image generation tasks.

Second, the approach is exceptionally well-motivated and placed within the literature. The authors clearly situate their work in relation to Gatys et al. (2015a), the seminal paper in this area, and systematically address the claims made in that work. Their experimental design is comprehensive, testing a wide range of single-layer architectures with different filter types, sizes, and numbers of feature maps.

Third, the results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The paper provides extensive visual comparisons, quantitative analysis using the VGG loss metric, and detailed ablation studies. The key finding that random filters can outperform trained filters is particularly striking and well-demonstrated. The multi-scale model's performance, which often rivals the state-of-the-art, is especially impressive.

The paper also makes an important methodological contribution by introducing a quantitative metric (VGG loss) to complement the traditional human evaluation approach, which is a significant step toward more objective texture synthesis evaluation.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong as-is, the following suggestions would enhance its impact:

- **Clarify the random vs. trained filters comparison**: The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images," but the results in Figure 1 show that K-means filters sometimes outperform random filters. The authors should clarify under what conditions random filters outperform trained filters, and provide more detailed analysis of when and why this occurs.

- **Expand the comparison to traditional texture synthesis methods**: While the paper mentions that the results surpass parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000), a more detailed comparison would help readers understand the magnitude of the improvement over traditional methods.

- **Discuss computational efficiency more thoroughly**: The paper mentions that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters, but doesn't explore potential optimizations (e.g., using dilated convolutions to achieve larger receptive fields more efficiently). A brief discussion of potential computational improvements would strengthen the paper.

- **Expand the theoretical discussion**: The paper's discussion of the MaxEnt framework and its relationship to texture synthesis is insightful but somewhat brief. Expanding this section would provide deeper theoretical grounding for the empirical findings.

## 5. Questions for Clarification

To help me be more confident in my assessment, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper states that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus loses variability." How does the optimization procedure differ from that used in Gatys et al. (2015a), and how does this lead to the observed differences in variability? Specifically, what role do the L-BFGS-B parameters play in this difference?

2. The paper mentions that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Could you provide more specific examples of when this occurs and why it happens? What does this suggest about the limitations of the VGG loss as a perceptual metric?

3. The paper states that the multi-scale model "often rivals the current state of the art." Could you provide quantitative metrics (beyond the VGG loss) to support this claim, such as human evaluation scores or other perceptual metrics? The visual comparisons are compelling, but additional quantitative evidence would strengthen the claim.

4. The paper mentions that "the combination of different scales in the multi-scale network is important for good texture synthesis." Could you elaborate on why this is the case, and how the multi-scale model captures statistical dependencies that single-scale models cannot? The results in Figure 2 suggest this, but a more detailed explanation would be helpful.

These questions are intended to help strengthen the paper, not to undermine its contribution. The paper is already strong and makes a valuable contribution to the field, but addressing these points would make it even more impactful.
