

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes an interesting contribution by challenging the prevailing assumption that hierarchical deep networks with pre-trained features are essential for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures that rival the quality of the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a). The paper systematically evaluates various single-layer architectures (including random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters) and shows that a multi-scale model with random filters often outperforms both the single-scale models and the VGG-based approach. The authors also introduce a quantitative metric based on the VGG loss to evaluate texture quality, which is a useful alternative to human evaluation. The work is well-motivated, and the findings have significant implications for understanding the fundamental requirements for texture synthesis.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper addresses an interesting question with potentially important implications, the experimental design and evaluation methodology contain critical flaws that undermine the strength of the claims. The paper does not adequately control for model capacity when comparing different filter types, and the interpretation of results is not sufficiently supported by the evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim—that random filters can outperform trained filters for texture synthesis—relies on a flawed experimental comparison. The random filter models (particularly Random-3267) have significantly more parameters (3267 feature maps) compared to the K-means-based models (363 feature maps), yet the paper attributes the better performance to the randomness of the filters rather than to the increased capacity. This is a fundamental confounding factor that the authors fail to address. A proper comparison would require fixing the number of parameters across filter types to isolate the effect of randomness.

Furthermore, the paper's evaluation methodology has significant limitations. The authors use the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, but they acknowledge that this metric fails for certain textures (e.g., regular structures like walls and grids), where the VGG-based model actually produces lower loss for the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model. This suggests the metric is not a reliable measure of perceptual quality, yet the authors use it as the primary evaluation criterion. The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more robust evaluation framework that better aligns with perceptual quality.

The paper also fails to adequately address the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, which uses large filters (up to 55×55) that make the approach impractical for many applications. While the authors acknowledge this issue, they don't provide a compelling justification for why the improved perceptual quality outweighs the computational cost.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a fair comparison by fixing the number of parameters across different filter types. For example, compare Random-363 with K-means-363 rather than Random-3267 with K-means-363.

2. Provide a more comprehensive comparison with recent texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a). The field has advanced significantly since 2015, with methods like GAN-based texture synthesis that would provide a more relevant baseline.

3. Explore the limitations of the VGG-based loss metric in more depth. The authors acknowledge it fails for regular textures but don't explain why or propose a better alternative. A more nuanced evaluation framework would strengthen the paper's claims.

4. Address the computational inefficiency more thoroughly. The paper states that hierarchical networks are more efficient but doesn't demonstrate that they produce comparable quality to the single-layer approach. If hierarchical networks are more efficient but produce lower quality, this would significantly impact the practical relevance of the findings.

5. Clarify the initialization of the random filters. The paper mentions "random" filters but doesn't specify the initialization scheme (e.g., Glorot initialization), which is known to produce filters with certain statistical properties. This detail is crucial for reproducibility and interpreting the results.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How did you ensure that the random filters weren't inadvertently capturing some statistics of natural images through their initialization? (For example, Glorot initialization is known to produce filters with specific statistical properties.)

2. Could you provide more details about the filter initialization parameters for the random filters to ensure reproducibility?

3. How would you explain the better performance of random filters compared to trained filters when the trained filters were specifically designed to capture natural image statistics?

4. Could you provide a more detailed comparison with recent texture synthesis methods (e.g., GAN-based approaches) to better contextualize your results in the current state-of-the-art?

5. How do you plan to address the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, especially for larger textures, given that the paper acknowledges this as a drawback?
