

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary
The paper challenges the assumption that deep hierarchical networks with supervisedly trained features are necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis. It proposes a minimal baseline consisting of a single-layer CNN with random filters, demonstrating that this simple approach can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a). The authors systematically compare various single-layer architectures (with random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters) against the state-of-the-art, and provide a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss to complement traditional human evaluation. Their key findings are that neither network depth nor pre-training of features is essential for texture synthesis, and that random filters can outperform or match more sophisticated approaches.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging established assumptions in texture synthesis with well-supported empirical evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses a clear and important question in texture synthesis: what elements are truly necessary for high-quality results. The experimental design is comprehensive, featuring multiple architectures and ablation studies that thoroughly investigate the role of network depth, filter training, filter size, and multi-scale representations. The authors employ both qualitative human evaluation and a quantitative VGG-based loss metric, providing a more objective evaluation than typical human inspection alone.

The results consistently support the paper's claims: random filters outperform or match state-of-the-art methods across multiple texture types, and the multi-scale model particularly excels at capturing long-range structures. The ablation studies on filter size (Fig. 2) provide strong evidence that the combination of multiple scales is crucial for good texture synthesis, as single-scale models fail to capture both small- and long-range statistics simultaneously. The demonstration that non-linearities (ReLUs) are indispensable (Fig. 2, right column) further strengthens the paper's claims.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building directly on Gatys et al. (2015a) while challenging a key assumption. The authors clearly identify the gap in the literature (the claim that random weights fail, which they attribute to suboptimal optimization) and provide evidence to support their alternative explanation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational efficiency. While the authors note the multi-scale model's computational inefficiency, they don't provide quantitative comparisons of synthesis time against the VGG-based method.
- The connection between the findings and broader implications for generative modeling beyond texture synthesis could be strengthened. While the authors mention this in the discussion, a more concrete connection would enhance the paper's impact.
- The paper claims to "replace the common-place evaluation of texture models through qualitative human inspection" with their quantitative metric, but the discussion of the metric's limitations (particularly for regular structures like walls and grids) could be expanded. A more nuanced discussion of when the VGG loss works well versus when it fails would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How does the computational complexity of your multi-scale model compare to the state-of-the-art VGG-based method in terms of synthesis time? Could you provide specific benchmarks for synthesis time on standard hardware?

2. In section 7, you note that the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantages for textures with regular structures (e.g., walls, grids). Could you elaborate on why these regular structures pose a particular challenge for the VGG loss, and whether there are specific characteristics of these textures that make them difficult to evaluate?

3. The paper mentions that "the Gram matrices are not treated as hard constraints but as soft constraints only." Could you provide more details about how this is implemented in your optimization process (e.g., stopping criterion, how you balance perceptual similarity and variability)?
