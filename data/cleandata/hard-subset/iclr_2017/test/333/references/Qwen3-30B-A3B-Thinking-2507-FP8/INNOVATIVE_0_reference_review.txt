

# Review

## Summary of Contributions
This paper challenges a fundamental assumption in texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are essential for generating natural textures. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random, data-independent filters can synthesize textures that rival state-of-the-art methods. Their core contribution is a bold reexamination of what is truly necessary for natural texture generation, showing that neither the hierarchical multi-layer representation nor supervised training of feature spaces is indispensable. The paper introduces a minimal baseline that performs surprisingly well across multiple texture types, with the multi-scale random filter model often matching or exceeding the quality of Gatys et al. (2015a). This work fundamentally shifts our understanding of the requirements for natural image generation and challenges the field's prevailing assumptions.

## Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) The paper presents a highly novel and paradigm-shifting contribution that challenges a fundamental assumption in the field, (2) It demonstrates through extensive empirical evidence that a minimal model with random filters can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods.

## Supporting Arguments
This paper's novelty and boldness make it a strong candidate for ICLR. The field has long assumed that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are necessary for natural texture synthesis, based on the influential work of Gatys et al. (2015a). By directly challenging this assumption with a simple yet effective alternative, the authors make a significant conceptual contribution that could redirect the field's research direction.

The empirical evidence supporting their claim is compelling. They systematically test a wide range of single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and show that random filters often outperform or match pre-trained features. The multi-scale random model in particular generates textures that rival state-of-the-art, with perceptual quality that is often indistinguishable from Gatys et al. (2015a) in qualitative human evaluations. While the paper acknowledges the computational inefficiency of their approach, this is not a fundamental limitation to the conceptual contribution but rather a practical consideration that could be addressed in future work.

The paper's boldness is further demonstrated in its theoretical discussion of the MaxEnt framework for texture synthesis. By questioning the necessity of deep networks and highlighting the importance of the distance metric between textures, the authors provide a more fundamental understanding of texture synthesis that goes beyond the specific implementation details.

While the paper has some methodological limitations (e.g., the reliance on VGG loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, limited theoretical explanation for why random filters work so well), these do not undermine the core novelty and conceptual contribution. As a reviewer who prioritizes novel ideas over methodological perfection, I find the paper's central claim sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance.

## Additional Feedback
The paper would benefit from a more detailed theoretical explanation of why random filters work as well as they do. The authors could explore whether there's a connection between the statistical properties of random filters and the structure of natural textures.

The computational inefficiency of large filter sizes (up to 55×55) should be addressed more thoroughly. The authors could discuss how this limitation might be overcome (e.g., through hierarchical architectures) and why these alternatives don't improve texture quality despite being more efficient.

The paper could strengthen its quantitative evaluation by incorporating additional perceptual metrics beyond VGG loss, such as the ones mentioned in the appendix (SSIM). This would provide more robust evidence for their claim that their method rivals state-of-the-art.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that random filters "perform on par or better" than filters trained on natural images. Could the authors provide more specific quantitative evidence (e.g., mean VGG loss differences across all textures) to support this claim?

2. The paper mentions that "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures." However, Figure 4 shows some textures where Gatys et al. (2015a) appears superior. Could the authors clarify the circumstances where their method falls short and why?

3. The authors note that "the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model" for some textures. What do they think this indicates about the relationship between the VGG loss and perceptual quality?

4. The paper suggests that "for texture synthesis, both aspects rather seem to serve as fine-tuning of the image representation." Could the authors elaborate on what they mean by "fine-tuning" and how this perspective changes our understanding of deep networks in image generation?
