

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling and significant contribution to the field of texture synthesis by challenging a fundamental assumption in the literature: that deep hierarchical representations with pre-trained features are necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters can synthesize textures of perceptual quality that rival or even surpass the state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a). Their key findings include:

1. Random filters outperform or match filters trained on natural images (e.g., K-means, PCA)
2. The multi-scale model (using filters of different sizes) produces superior results, capturing both small- and long-range structures
3. The depth of the network and supervised pre-training are not necessary for good texture synthesis
4. They introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG-based loss that provides a more objective alternative to human inspection

The paper is well-structured, with thorough experimentation across multiple filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and scales. The results are visually compelling and supported by quantitative analysis. The authors provide a thoughtful discussion of the implications for maximum entropy texture modeling frameworks and perceptual metrics.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

My primary reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental question in texture synthesis that has been overlooked despite the field's rapid growth.
2. The experimental results are robust, comprehensive, and directly challenge a widely held assumption in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

### Strengths
- **Novel and impactful contribution**: The paper successfully challenges a key assumption in the field (that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are essential), which is a significant contribution that will likely influence future research directions.
- **Strong experimental design**: The authors test a wide range of single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) with different numbers of filters and scales, providing a thorough comparison against the state-of-the-art.
- **Quantitative evaluation**: The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric provides a more objective alternative to human evaluation, which is particularly valuable for large-scale comparisons.
- **Theoretical insight**: The paper provides a compelling explanation for why random filters work well (better optimization tuning) and connects to the maximum entropy framework, adding theoretical depth to the empirical findings.

### Key Strengths Beyond the Obvious
- The multi-scale model (using filters of different sizes) outperforms single-scale models, demonstrating that the combination of scales matters more than filter size alone, which is a subtle but important insight.
- The authors provide a clear explanation for why random filters work as well as trained filters: their optimization procedure is more carefully tuned than in earlier work, which previously reported that random filters fail to generate perceptually interesting images.
- The paper thoughtfully addresses the limitations of their quantitative metric (VGG loss) and acknowledges where it fails to correlate with human perception (e.g., for highly regular textures).

### Minor Concerns
- The paper doesn't conduct a formal human evaluation to validate their quantitative metric, though they acknowledge this limitation.
- The paper could more thoroughly explore why random filters work so well (beyond just stating it's due to better optimization tuning).
- The computational efficiency of their approach isn't quantified in comparison to the VGG-based method.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Major Suggestions
1. **Conduct a small-scale human evaluation**: While the paper acknowledges that human evaluation is the gold standard, it would significantly strengthen the paper to include a brief human study comparing the VGG-based loss with human perception. This could be a simple online survey with a few dozen participants rating the quality of synthesized textures from different models.

2. **Clarify the relationship between number of filters and performance**: The paper states "a larger number of feature maps generally increases the perceptual quality," but doesn't provide a systematic analysis of how the number of filters affects performance. A plot showing quality metrics as a function of filter count would be valuable.

3. **Better explain the optimization tuning**: The paper mentions "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" as the reason for the success of random filters, but doesn't provide details. A brief discussion of the specific parameters that were tuned (beyond the number of iterations and scaling factor) would help readers replicate the results.

### Minor Suggestions
1. **Quantify computational efficiency**: Include runtime comparisons between the single-layer random model and the VGG-based model to address the authors' concern about the multi-scale model's computational inefficiency.

2. **Discuss generalization to other image types**: While the paper focuses on textures, it would be valuable to briefly discuss whether the same approach might work for synthesizing other image types (e.g., natural scenes, objects).

3. **Clarify the "random" initialization**: The paper uses "random" filters, but doesn't specify the exact distribution. More detail about the initialization (e.g., Glorot uniform as cited) would be helpful for reproducibility.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To further clarify the paper and strengthen the authors' claims, I'd like to ask:

1. The paper states that random filters outperform trained filters, but the difference in the number of filters (363 vs. 3267) may be a confounding factor. Have you tried comparing models with the *same* number of filters (e.g., 363 random vs. 363 K-means)? This would isolate the effect of filter type from the effect of the number of filters.

2. You mention that "the success of the random multi-scale network arises from the combination of filters on multiple scales." Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why this combination is so effective, particularly in terms of how the receptive fields of different scales interact to capture both local and global texture statistics?

3. The paper uses the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, but acknowledges limitations for certain textures (e.g., brick walls, grids). How do you plan to address this limitation in future work, and what would be a more robust perceptual metric for texture synthesis?

4. The paper states that "for finite size images it is well possible that only the reference image can exactly reproduce all the summary statistics." How do you reconcile this with your observation that the multi-scale model sometimes achieves lower VGG-based loss than the VGG-based model itself? Does this suggest that the VGG-based loss is not a perfect metric, or that the multi-scale model is better capturing the texture statistics?

5. Have you explored whether the success of random filters generalizes to other tasks beyond texture synthesis, such as style transfer or image generation? If so, what were your findings?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field that challenges a fundamental assumption and opens up new research directions. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the implications are broad. With the suggested improvements (particularly the human evaluation), this paper would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
