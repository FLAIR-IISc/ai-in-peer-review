

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper challenges the established view that deep hierarchical networks with supervised training are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim that a single-layer CNN with random, data-independent filters can synthesize textures that rival the quality of the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a 19-layer VGG network with supervised training. They demonstrate that neither the hierarchical multi-layer representation nor the supervised training of feature spaces is essential for good texture synthesis, and that random filters can perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural image statistics. The authors also introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG loss to replace human evaluation, and they explore the role of multi-scale filters in texture synthesis.

## 2. Decision

Reject

Key reasons for rejection:
1. The paper makes a strong, counter-intuitive claim that random filters work as well as or better than trained filters for texture synthesis, but fails to provide sufficient empirical evidence beyond visual comparisons and a single quantitative metric.
2. The paper lacks a formal human evaluation study, which is the standard for texture synthesis research and essential for validating perceptual quality claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim—that random filters can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods—is not sufficiently supported. While the authors provide visual comparisons (Fig. 1 and Fig. 4), these do not constitute rigorous evidence for their claim. Texture synthesis is a perceptual task, and the standard for evaluating such methods has historically been human evaluation. The authors acknowledge this by stating "the quality of the synthesized textures is usually determined by human inspection," but they fail to conduct a proper human evaluation study to validate their claims.

Instead, they rely on a single quantitative metric (the VGG-based loss) to claim their method "often rivals the current state of the art." However, they do not demonstrate that this metric correlates with human perception. As they themselves note in Section 5, "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences," but they provide no evidence to support this claim beyond visual inspection.

The paper's attribution of their success to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" is insufficient. They don't systematically analyze how their optimization differs from Gatys et al. (2015a) or why this difference is so critical. Without this, their claim that random filters work well is not properly justified.

The paper also fails to compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a), which is essential for establishing the true standing of their approach. The claim that their method "rivals the current state of the art" is unsubstantiated without proper comparison to contemporary methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a formal human evaluation study with a sufficient number of participants and a well-designed experimental protocol to compare the perceptual quality of textures generated by their method versus Gatys et al. (2015a) and other relevant methods.

2. Provide statistical analysis of their results, including significance testing, to demonstrate that their method's performance is consistently better than or comparable to the state-of-the-art.

3. Systematically investigate the role of the optimization procedure. Specifically, they should compare their optimization setup with the one used by Gatys et al. (2015a) to identify which specific aspects of the optimization lead to the improved results with random filters.

4. Compare against more recent texture synthesis methods to establish the true standing of their approach in the current literature.

5. Provide a more detailed theoretical explanation for why random filters work as well as or better than trained filters, rather than attributing it to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure."

## Questions for the Authors

1. Can you provide statistical evidence (e.g., p-values from t-tests or ANOVA) demonstrating that your method's performance is significantly better than or comparable to Gatys et al. (2015a) using your VGG loss metric?

2. Have you conducted a formal human evaluation study to confirm that the textures synthesized by your method are perceived as equally good as those from Gatys et al. (2015a)? If so, please provide details of the study design and results.

3. What specific aspects of your optimization procedure (beyond the scaling factor of 10^7 mentioned in Section 4) are responsible for the improved results with random filters, compared to the optimization procedure used in Gatys et al. (2015a)?

4. How does your method compare to more recent texture synthesis approaches (e.g., those using GANs or other deep learning architectures) beyond Gatys et al. (2015a)?

5. Can you provide a theoretical explanation for why random filters work as well as or better than filters trained on natural image statistics, rather than attributing it to optimization tuning?
