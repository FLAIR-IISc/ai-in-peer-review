

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a bold claim that challenges a fundamental assumption in deep learning for texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that natural texture synthesis does not require deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features, as previously believed (Gatys et al., 2015a), but can be achieved with a single-layer convolutional network using completely random filters. The paper presents a strong minimal baseline showing that random filters, when combined with a multi-scale approach, can produce textures of perceptual quality rivaling state-of-the-art methods. The authors systematically test various filter types (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random) and architectures, revealing that the number of feature maps and filter scales matter more than filter training. They also introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss, which provides an objective alternative to human evaluation.

## 2. Decision: Accept

**Key reasons for acceptance:** The paper makes a highly novel and counterintuitive claim that fundamentally challenges a core assumption in the field. The authors demonstrate through compelling empirical evidence that simple, random, single-layer networks can achieve texture synthesis quality comparable to complex, deep architectures. This represents a significant conceptual shift in understanding what is truly necessary for natural texture generation, aligning with the reviewer's preference for bold, novel ideas over incremental improvements.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's novelty and conceptual contribution are its strongest assets. The claim that deep hierarchical representations and pre-training are not necessary for texture synthesis directly contradicts the prevailing wisdom in the field (Gatys et al., 2015a). The authors provide thorough experimental evidence across multiple textures and architectures to support this claim, showing that random filters often outperform hand-crafted or trained filters. The multi-scale approach they propose is particularly clever, demonstrating that combining filters of different sizes (3×3, 11×11, 55×55) is crucial for capturing textures at multiple spatial scales.

While the evaluation relies on VGG loss as a proxy for human perception (which has limitations, as the authors acknowledge), the results are consistent across multiple textures and show that their method often produces better perceptual results than the VGG-based model. The paper's theoretical discussion of the MaxEnt framework and the importance of the distance metric for texture synthesis adds significant depth to the contribution.

The paper's main strength is its conceptual boldness—it doesn't just improve texture synthesis, but fundamentally questions what is necessary for the task. This aligns perfectly with ICLR's emphasis on novel ideas over incremental methodological improvements.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Include human evaluation results:** While the VGG loss is a useful proxy, the paper would be significantly stronger with a small human evaluation study comparing the random model's output to the VGG-based model. This would directly address the paper's core claim about perceptual quality.

2. **Clarify the "randomness" aspect:** The paper states that random filters work well, but it's unclear how much randomness is necessary. Some exploration of how different random seeds affect results would help distinguish between "random" being a sufficient condition versus a necessary condition.

3. **Address the computational efficiency concern:** The paper notes that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters. While the authors mention that hierarchical architectures are faster but don't improve quality, a brief comparison of runtime would be valuable for practical applications.

4. **Clarify the relationship to existing work:** The paper mentions that earlier work (Gatys et al., 2015a) suggested random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images, but doesn't fully explain why their approach succeeds where others failed. A clearer explanation of the "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" would strengthen the paper.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you elaborate on the specific optimization tuning that enabled random filters to work well, compared to the approach used in Gatys et al. (2015a)?

2. How do you explain the fact that random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images? Is there a theoretical reason for this counterintuitive finding?

3. Given that the VGG loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual differences (particularly for regular structures like walls), how would you recommend evaluating texture synthesis models in the future?

The paper makes a significant conceptual contribution that challenges the field's understanding of what is necessary for natural texture synthesis. The novelty of the approach, combined with compelling empirical results, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
