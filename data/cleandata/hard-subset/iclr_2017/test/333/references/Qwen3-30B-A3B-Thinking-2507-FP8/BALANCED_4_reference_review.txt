

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a compelling exploration of the essential components required for natural texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are necessary for high-quality texture generation, demonstrating that single-layer CNNs with random filters can produce textures that rival state-of-the-art methods. Their key contributions include establishing a strong minimal baseline using random filters, conducting comprehensive comparisons across multiple single-layer architectures, introducing a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG-based synthesis loss, and providing theoretical insights into texture modeling. The work is well-organized, clearly written, and makes a meaningful contribution to the understanding of deep representation learning for texture synthesis.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution, but the evidence supporting its main claim is insufficient to justify the strong conclusions drawn, particularly regarding the necessity of hierarchical representations. The evaluation methodology lacks sufficient rigor to conclusively demonstrate that random single-layer networks outperform multi-layer approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim—that neither hierarchical representations nor pre-trained features are indispensable for natural texture synthesis—relies on evidence from qualitative comparisons and a single quantitative metric (VGG-based synthesis loss). While the results are intriguing, several critical limitations undermine the strength of the conclusions:

First, the evaluation methodology is problematic. The authors use the VGG-based synthesis loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, but as they acknowledge in Section 7, this metric fails for some textures (e.g., brick walls, grids). They show that for these textures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from their multi-scale model than for samples from the VGG-based model (which directly optimized this loss). This undermines the validity of their primary quantitative metric, as it suggests the metric doesn't reliably capture perceptual quality. The paper would be significantly strengthened by incorporating human evaluation, which is the standard for texture synthesis work.

Second, the paper doesn't sufficiently address the computational efficiency trade-off. While they acknowledge that their multi-scale model is computationally inefficient, they don't provide a thorough comparison of the time/quality trade-off between their approach and the VGG-based method. For practical applications, computational efficiency is crucial, and this omission weakens the claim that their approach is superior.

Third, the paper doesn't adequately address the "variability" aspect of texture synthesis, which is a key requirement for a good texture model. As noted in Section 7, their approach tends to produce samples with high perceptual similarity but low variability, which is a critical limitation for practical texture synthesis applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Include human evaluation studies to validate their quantitative metric. This is essential for texture synthesis work and would provide much stronger evidence for their claims.

2. Conduct a thorough comparison of computational efficiency (time, memory) between their method and the VGG-based approach, particularly for large textures.

3. Explicitly address the variability issue by either modifying their optimization procedure to introduce controlled variability or by developing a metric to quantify variability.

4. Clarify the relationship between their single-layer random filter approach and the MaxEnt framework they reference in Section 7. Specifically, how does their method relate to the theoretical framework they cite?

5. Provide statistical analysis of the quantitative results (e.g., standard deviations across multiple texture examples) to demonstrate that the observed differences are significant.

## 5. Clarification Questions

1. The paper claims that random filters "often rival the perceptual quality of Gatys et al. (2015a)." How many texture examples were evaluated in this claim, and what was the exact measure of "rivaling" (e.g., percentage of examples where random filters performed better, or average rank)?

2. The paper shows in Figure 4 that their multi-scale model produces competitive results, but the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for their model than for the VGG-based model for some textures. Can the authors explain this apparent contradiction between their quantitative metric and their own visual assessment of quality?

3. The authors mention that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus looses variability." What specific changes would they recommend to the optimization procedure to increase variability while maintaining perceptual similarity?

4. The paper states that "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures." However, Figure 1 shows that for the "pebble" texture, the multi-scale model appears to produce less detailed results than some single-scale models. Can the authors clarify this apparent contradiction?

The paper presents an interesting and potentially important contribution to texture synthesis, but the current evidence does not fully support the strong claims made. With more rigorous evaluation and additional analysis addressing the limitations noted above, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field.
