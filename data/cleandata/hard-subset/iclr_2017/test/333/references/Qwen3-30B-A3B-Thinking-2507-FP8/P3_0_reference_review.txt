

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling and counterintuitive claim that challenges a fundamental assumption in deep learning for texture synthesis: that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are essential for high-quality natural texture generation. The authors demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random filters can synthesize textures that rival the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), with the key being the use of a sufficiently large number of feature maps (3267) and careful optimization (2000 iterations, loss scaling by 10^7). 

The paper makes several significant contributions:
- It presents a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer network with random filters
- It shows that textures synthesized from this baseline often rival state-of-the-art approaches, suggesting depth and pre-training are not as indispensable as previously thought
- It comprehensively compares various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned)
- It introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss
- It provides a thoughtful discussion of the theoretical implications for maximum entropy texture modeling

The paper is well-motivated by a careful analysis of previous work and makes a clear, testable claim that challenges the field's conventional wisdom.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper makes a strong, counterintuitive claim that challenges previous assumptions in the field about the necessity of deep hierarchical representations for texture synthesis.
2. The experimental results are compelling and demonstrate that simple single-layer networks with random filters can produce textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling the state-of-the-art.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is highly significant because it fundamentally questions a widely accepted premise in the field. The authors provide careful evidence that random filters can work as well as trained filters, which contradicts the original Gatys et al. (2015a) claim that "networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images."

The experimental design is thorough and well-executed. The authors compare multiple filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) with different numbers of feature maps (363 vs. 3267) and evaluate the multi-scale model that combines filters of different sizes (3x3, 11x11, 55x55). The multi-scale model is particularly clever and effectively addresses the limitation of single-scale filters by capturing structures at multiple spatial scales.

The paper's theoretical discussion about the maximum entropy framework and finite-size effects is insightful and adds depth to the work. The authors correctly note that practical texture synthesis differs from the theoretical MaxEnt framework because it must account for the finite spatial extent of textures.

The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss is a valuable contribution to the field, moving beyond purely qualitative human evaluation to provide a more objective metric for texture quality.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, the following improvements would make it even better:

1. **Clarify optimization details**: The scaling factor of 10^7 is quite large and not well-justified. Please provide more details on how you determined this scaling factor and whether the results are sensitive to it. A sensitivity analysis would strengthen your claims.

2. **Include human evaluation**: While the VGG-based loss is a good proxy, it's not perfect. It would strengthen the paper to include some human evaluation results to confirm that the VGG loss correlates with human perception for the textures you're synthesizing. This would address the limitation you acknowledge in Section 5.

3. **Provide more ablation studies**: It would be helpful to see how much of the improvement comes from the number of feature maps versus the filter type. For example, how would Random-363 compare to Random-3267 with the same optimization?

4. **Address computational efficiency**: The multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters (55x55). Discuss potential more efficient alternatives (e.g., stacking multiple small filters) and why they might not work as well.

5. **Add statistical significance**: Include statistical tests to confirm that the differences in performance are significant, rather than relying solely on qualitative observation.

## Questions for the Authors

1. In your optimization, you scale the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7. How did you determine this scaling factor? Is the result sensitive to this scaling, and how did you verify that this scaling doesn't mask optimization issues?

2. Did you attempt to replicate Gatys et al. (2015a)'s random weights experiments with the same optimization parameters (2000 iterations, loss scaling)? If so, what were your results, and how do they differ from Gatys et al.'s?

3. You claim that the VGG-based loss is a reasonable proxy for perceptual quality, but it fails for some textures (e.g., regular man-made structures like walls and grids). Can you provide examples of these textures and explain why the VGG loss fails for them?

4. For the multi-scale model, you use filters of sizes 3x3, 11x11, and 55x55. Why did you choose these specific sizes? How would the results change if you used different filter sizes?

5. You mention that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus looses variability." How do you plan to address this issue of variability in future work, and what metrics would you use to measure it?

This paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of what is truly necessary for natural texture generation. The results challenge a fundamental assumption in the field and open up new possibilities for simpler, more efficient texture synthesis approaches. I believe this work will have a meaningful impact on the field of generative models.
