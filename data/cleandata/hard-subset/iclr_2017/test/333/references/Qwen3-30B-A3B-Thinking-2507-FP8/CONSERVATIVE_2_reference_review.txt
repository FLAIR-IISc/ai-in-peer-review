

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper investigates whether hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are truly necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random, data-independent filters can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality that rival or even surpass the state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a). They present a comprehensive comparison of various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned) and demonstrate that the multi-scale random filter approach often outperforms the multi-layer VGG-based model. The authors further propose a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG loss to replace human inspection as the primary evaluation method.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's main claim—that random filters can match or surpass the performance of trained deep networks for texture synthesis—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The results appear to be highly sensitive to the specific optimization procedure rather than representing a fundamental property of the method.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim is that "neither aspect [hierarchical multi-layer representation and supervised training] is imperative for texture modeling," but the evidence provided is insufficient to support this broad conclusion. The key issues undermining their claims are:

First, the paper fails to adequately explain the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a), which claimed that networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images. The authors attribute this to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," but they don't provide sufficient evidence that this tuning is robust or generalizable. Their optimization procedure (using L-BFGS-B with specific parameter choices) may be particularly well-suited to the single-layer random filter approach but not necessarily transferable to other methods or textures.

Second, the quantitative evaluation metric (VGG loss) they propose is acknowledged to be imperfect, especially for regular textures like walls and grids. The authors themselves note that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" but fails in specific cases. Using this imperfect metric to claim that their method "rivals the current state-of-the-art" is scientifically unsound.

Third, the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the random filter approach is robust across different textures and different random seeds. The results appear to be highly specific to the particular textures and optimization setups used. The authors don't demonstrate that their approach consistently outperforms the state-of-the-art across diverse textures or that the results generalize beyond their specific experimental setup.

Finally, the paper's claim that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" is particularly concerning. The evidence presented is limited to the specific optimization procedure they used, and they don't provide a rigorous comparison of the generalizability of their approach. The field has generally assumed that pre-trained filters capture useful image statistics, and this paper's claim contradicts that assumption without providing compelling evidence.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen this work, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough comparison against the state-of-the-art, including direct quantitative comparisons with metrics beyond the VGG loss (e.g., SSIM, human evaluation with larger sample sizes).

2. Address the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a) more thoroughly. Provide evidence that their optimization procedure is robust across different random seeds and textures, and explain why their results differ from previous work.

3. Investigate why the VGG loss fails for certain textures (like walls and grids) and how this impacts their claims about the superiority of their method.

4. Include ablation studies showing how sensitive their results are to specific optimization parameters (e.g., number of iterations, scaling factor).

5. Provide more evidence that the random filter approach is generally better than trained filters, not just in their specific experimental setup.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide evidence that your optimization procedure (L-BFGS-B with specific parameter choices) is robust across different random seeds and textures? How many random seeds and textures were tested?

2. In Figure 4, the multi-scale random model sometimes outperforms the VGG-based model, but the VGG-based model is directly optimized for the VGG loss. How can you be certain that this isn't simply an artifact of the optimization procedure rather than a fundamental advantage of the random filter approach?

3. The authors acknowledge that the VGG loss "fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for some textures. How do you address this limitation in your claims about the superiority of your method?

4. The paper claims "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images," but the evidence is limited to their specific experimental setup. Can you provide evidence that this holds across a broader range of textures and optimization conditions?
