

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a compelling challenge to the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks with supervised training are essential for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures of perceptual quality rivaling the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), which relies on a deep VGG network with pre-trained weights. The paper systematically evaluates various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random filters) with different filter sizes and numbers, introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG loss, and proposes a formal generalization of maximum entropy models to account for texture variability. The core contribution is the demonstration that neither hierarchical multi-layer representations nor supervised training of feature spaces are indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis.

## 2. Decision
**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in the field and providing rigorous evidence that simpler models can achieve comparable results. The work is well-positioned within the literature and makes a clear, testable claim that advances our understanding of what is truly necessary for texture synthesis.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main claim is well-supported by extensive experimental evidence. The authors provide a comprehensive comparison across multiple filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and demonstrate that random filters with sufficient quantity (3267) perform as well or better than more sophisticated filter designs. This is a notable contribution that corrects a misperception in the field (that random filters fail to generate perceptually interesting images).

The methodological rigor is strong, with the paper carefully addressing optimization procedures (including the critical importance of the L-BFGS-B implementation details) and providing both qualitative and quantitative evaluation. The multi-scale architecture is particularly clever, demonstrating that combining multiple filter scales (3×3, 11×11, 55×55) enables modeling of structures across different spatial scales, which single-scale models cannot achieve.

The paper's theoretical discussion of the maximum entropy framework and its limitations for finite-size textures is insightful and provides valuable context for interpreting their results. The authors correctly identify that the key challenge in texture synthesis is balancing perceptual similarity and variability, and they thoughtfully acknowledge the limitations of their VGG-based quality metric.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these minor revisions would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the "random filters" claim**: The paper states "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images," but this is primarily supported by visual inspection. A quantitative comparison of filter types using the VGG loss metric would strengthen this claim. For instance, comparing the average VGG loss across all test textures for each filter type would provide more concrete evidence.

2. **Address the filter quantity vs. quality question**: The paper demonstrates that more filters (3267 vs. 363) improve quality, but doesn't clearly separate the effect of quantity from the effect of filter type. A controlled experiment where filter type is fixed but number varies would help isolate the contribution of each factor.

3. **Elaborate on optimization differences**: The paper notes that "this discrepancy originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" but doesn't provide sufficient detail about what specific changes were made to the optimization process compared to Gatys et al. (2015a). A brief comparison of optimization hyperparameters would help readers reproduce and understand the results.

4. **Discuss computational efficiency**: While the paper mentions computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, it would be valuable to quantify the speed difference between the single-layer random model and the multi-layer VGG model, as this has practical implications for applications.

## Questions for Authors

1. In Figure 4, the multi-scale model appears to outperform the VGG model on certain textures (e.g., brick wall, grids), yet the VGG-based loss (7) is paradoxically lower for the VGG model. How do you reconcile this apparent contradiction between the quantitative metric and perceptual quality?

2. The paper claims random filters work well, but doesn't explore whether the specific distribution used (Glorot uniform) matters. Would filters from a different distribution (e.g., Gaussian) perform similarly, or is there something specific about the Glorot initialization that contributes to the success?

3. The authors note that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." What specific aspects of the grid textures cause this behavior, and how might this inform future work on texture metrics?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the field by challenging assumptions about the necessity of deep networks for texture synthesis. The results are scientifically rigorous, the methodology is sound, and the implications for understanding the fundamental requirements of natural image generation are significant. With minor clarifications, this paper would be an excellent addition to ICLR.
