

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the assumption that hierarchical deep networks with supervised training are essential. The authors present a minimal baseline using a single convolutional layer with random filters that can synthesize textures rivaling state-of-the-art approaches. They demonstrate that neither hierarchical multi-layer representation nor supervised training of feature spaces is necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The paper makes several contributions: introducing a strong minimal baseline, showing that random filters can achieve competitive results, conducting extensive comparisons across different single-layer architectures, proposing a quantitative texture quality measure, and discussing theoretical implications for maximum entropy models.

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the paper addresses an interesting question with potentially important implications, the evidence supporting the main claims is insufficiently rigorous due to methodological limitations in the evaluation framework. The paper relies on a quantitative metric (VGG-based loss) that the authors themselves acknowledge has limitations for texture synthesis evaluation, without providing complementary human evaluation to validate their claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim—that single-layer networks with random filters can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis—lacks sufficient empirical support. The authors acknowledge that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" but "fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for certain textures like "wall" or "grids." This directly undermines the paper's main conclusion that their method "often rivals the current state-of-the-art."

The evaluation methodology is the paper's most significant weakness. Texture synthesis quality is inherently subjective, yet the paper relies almost exclusively on a quantitative metric that the authors admit is imperfect. Figure 4 shows that for some textures, the VGG-based loss actually favors the random model over the Gatys model, but the paper does not provide human evaluation results to confirm whether these samples are indeed perceptually better. Without human evaluation, the paper cannot credibly claim perceptual quality.

The paper also lacks systematic comparison with previous methods. While it mentions surpassing "parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000)," it does not provide direct comparisons or quantitative results for these methods, making it difficult to assess the true improvement.

The claim that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" is overstated. The paper shows that random filters can work well, but the experiments don't conclusively prove consistent superiority. The phrase "often rival" in the abstract suggests performance is not consistently better, which weakens the main claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper has several promising elements that could be strengthened into a compelling contribution with more rigorous evaluation. First, the authors should conduct a proper human evaluation study to validate their quantitative claims. A small-scale user study with human observers would significantly strengthen the main claims. Second, the paper should provide more direct comparisons with previous methods, including quantitative metrics for methods like Portilla & Simoncelli.

The discussion of "variability" as a desideratum for texture synthesis is promising but underdeveloped. This could be expanded into a more substantial contribution by developing better measures of variability and exploring how to balance perceptual similarity with variability.

The paper would benefit from a more thorough analysis of the relationship between the number of feature maps and performance. The authors note that "a larger number of feature maps generally increases the perceptual quality" but do not provide systematic evidence for this claim.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How do you explain the discrepancy between the VGG-based loss metric (which suggests your method performs well) and the human perception (which the paper acknowledges may not align perfectly with this metric)? 

2. Could you provide more details about the human evaluation study (if any) that was conducted to validate the quality of the synthesized textures, or explain why such an evaluation was not included?

3. How do you address the concern that the random filters might be accidentally capturing some of the statistics of natural images through their specific distribution, rather than being truly random?

4. What are the computational costs of your method compared to the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al.), particularly for the multi-scale model with large filters (55×55), and how does this impact practical applications?
