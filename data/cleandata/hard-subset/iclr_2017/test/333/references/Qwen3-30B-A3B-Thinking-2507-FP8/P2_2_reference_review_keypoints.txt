- Main claim: Single-layer CNNs with random filters can generate natural textures comparable to state-of-the-art, challenging the need for hierarchical supervised representations.  
- Key contribution: Demonstrates random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images for texture synthesis.  
- Key contribution: Introduces a multi-scale model combining filter sizes (3×3, 11×11, 55×55) to capture textures across spatial scales.  
- Key contribution: Proposes a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG loss to supplement human evaluation.  
- Key contribution: Discusses the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis.  
- Decision: Recommend accept.  
- Primary reasons: Addresses a fundamental question, challenges established assumptions, and provides strong, comprehensive empirical evidence with ablation studies.  
- Supporting evidence: Ablations show multi-scale combination and ReLU non-linearities are crucial; multi-scale model often outperforms VGG-based model on many textures.  
- Feedback 1: Clarify why random filters work so well and how optimization tuning contributes to their performance.  
- Feedback 2: Quantify the “rivaling” claim with metrics (e.g., average VGG loss) or a brief human evaluation.  
- Feedback 3: Expand discussion on paradoxical cases where multi-scale samples have lower VGG loss than VGG-based model, exploring VGG loss limitations.  
- Feedback 4: Include computational efficiency/timing comparisons for practical relevance.  
- Feedback 5: Clarify relationship between filter size, receptive field, and capturing long-range correlations.  
- Question 1: Which exact optimization differences explain the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a) regarding random filters?  
- Question 2: Why do data-independent random filters perform as well or better than trained filters—optimization tuning or intrinsic suitability?  
- Question 3: Why does the VGG-based loss sometimes favor multi-scale samples over VGG-based model samples, and what does this imply about the metric?  
- Question 4: How can the optimization be adjusted systematically to trade off perceptual similarity and variability, and which metrics should measure that balance?
