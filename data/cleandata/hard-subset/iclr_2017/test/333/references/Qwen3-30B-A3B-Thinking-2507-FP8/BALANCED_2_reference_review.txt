

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper challenges the assumption that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors present a compelling minimal baseline using a single convolutional layer with random filters, demonstrating that such a simple model can generate textures that often rival state-of-the-art approaches. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures (with different filter sizes and types including random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters) against the VGG-based model of Gatys et al. (2015a). The paper introduces a quantitative evaluation measure based on the VGG loss to complement traditional human inspection, and provides thorough ablation studies on filter size and the importance of multi-scale filters. The main contribution is showing that depth and pre-training are not as indispensable as previously thought for natural texture generation.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

Key reasons: The paper's main claim that single-layer random filters "often rival" state-of-the-art texture synthesis is not sufficiently supported, and the quantitative evaluation method has significant limitations that undermine the main claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are clear: the experimental design is well-executed, the ablation studies on filter size and multi-scale architecture are particularly strong, and the findings challenge a common assumption in the field. The authors convincingly show that the spatial extent of filters and their combination across scales are crucial for texture synthesis, and that random filters can perform as well as trained filters.

However, the paper falls short in two critical areas that prevent it from being accepted:

First, the paper claims that their single-layer model "often rivals" the state-of-the-art, but the evidence is insufficient. The authors rely on a VGG-based loss as their quantitative measure, but they themselves acknowledge in section 6 that this loss "fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for some textures (particularly those with regular, man-made structures). Without a proper human evaluation study to complement their quantitative measure, it's impossible to be confident that their method actually produces better textures. The visual comparisons in Figure 4 are suggestive but not conclusive without human evaluation.

Second, the paper's main claim that "neither aspect [hierarchical representation or supervised training] is imperative" is overstated. The authors show that the single-layer model performs well on some textures but not all (as evidenced by Figure 4), and they don't adequately explain why the multi-layer model outperforms the single-layer model in certain cases. The suggestion that this is due to "more robust optimization" is speculative and lacks evidence.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper significantly, the authors should:

1. Conduct a proper human evaluation study to complement their quantitative measure. This would involve collecting perceptual judgments from human observers to determine if the textures generated by their single-layer model are indeed perceived as better than those from the VGG-based model.

2. Expand the comparison to include more recent texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a), such as those by Aittala et al. (2016) or Liu et al. (2016), which specifically address some limitations of the Gatys approach.

3. Provide a more nuanced analysis of when the single-layer model outperforms and when it fails, including a detailed discussion of the specific texture characteristics that lead to these differences.

4. Clarify the relationship between their quantitative measure (VGG loss) and perceptual quality. The paper acknowledges the limitations of this measure but doesn't provide a systematic way to address them.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper notes that the VGG loss "fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for some textures. Can you provide more details about these specific cases, and what perceptual differences were observed by human evaluators?

2. The paper claims that "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." However, Figure 1 shows that some trained filters (like K-means-3267) outperform random filters in some cases. Can you clarify the circumstances under which random filters outperform trained filters?

3. The paper suggests that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture" and thus "looses variability." How do you propose to balance perceptual similarity with variability in your framework, and what metrics would you use to evaluate this balance?

4. The paper mentions that "samples from the single-layer model still exhibit large perceptual differences" (Figure 3). Can you quantify these perceptual differences and explain why they occur despite the VGG loss being relatively low?
