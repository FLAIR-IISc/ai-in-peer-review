

# Review for "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper makes a compelling contribution to the understanding of texture synthesis by demonstrating that hierarchical deep networks and supervised training of feature spaces are not essential for high-quality natural texture generation. The authors present a strong minimal baseline using single-layer convolutional networks with random filters that can produce textures rivaling state-of-the-art approaches. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures (including Fourier bases, K-means clusters, PCA, and random filters) across different numbers of feature maps (363 vs. 3267) and filter sizes (3×3, 11×11, 55×55). The paper introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based loss to complement traditional qualitative human inspection, and provides a thorough analysis of the importance of multi-scale filters and non-linearities. The findings challenge previous assumptions about the necessity of deep hierarchical representations for texture synthesis.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that single-layer networks with random filters can generate high-quality textures, challenging the prevailing view that hierarchical deep networks are essential. The results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported by both quantitative and qualitative evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper addresses a clear, well-motivated question about the essential components of texture synthesis. The authors provide a thorough empirical analysis that systematically tests different architectural choices and demonstrates that neither multi-layer representations nor supervised training of features are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The experimental design is robust: they test multiple texture examples, multiple filter types, and systematically vary the number of feature maps and filter sizes. The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss (equation 7) provides a scientifically rigorous alternative to subjective human evaluation, and the results consistently show that random single-layer networks can produce textures of comparable quality to the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a). The ablation study on filter size (Figure 2) effectively demonstrates that the multi-scale approach is crucial for capturing both short- and long-range correlations in textures, and that non-linearities are indispensable. The paper's claims are well-supported by evidence across multiple experiments and visual comparisons.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following points:

- The paper claims that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." This is a strong claim that would be strengthened by a more direct quantitative comparison showing statistical significance across multiple texture examples.

- While the paper uses the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, it would be beneficial to include a brief human evaluation study to directly validate that the quantitative metric aligns with human perception, especially for the textures where the VGG loss fails to capture perceptual advantage (e.g., brick wall, grids).

- The computational efficiency claim about "deep hierarchical representations being not necessary but improving efficiency" needs clarification. The paper states that hierarchical architectures are "significantly faster" but doesn't provide quantitative runtime comparisons between the single-layer and hierarchical approaches.

- The discussion of the MaxEnt framework could be strengthened by explicitly connecting the authors' findings to the theoretical framework, particularly regarding how their approach handles the "finite size effect" mentioned in section 7.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper states that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" but notes failures for certain textures. Could you provide more details on which specific textures showed this discrepancy and why these textures might be particularly challenging for the VGG loss?

- The paper claims that the single-layer random filters "often rival the current state of the art" but doesn't provide a quantitative comparison of the VGG loss values between the single-layer model and Gatys et al. (2015a). Could you provide a table comparing the average VGG loss across all 20 textures tested in Figure 4?

- The computational efficiency claim about hierarchical architectures being "significantly faster" needs more concrete evidence. Could you include a table showing the actual runtime comparisons between the single-layer model and hierarchical alternatives for the same texture synthesis task?
