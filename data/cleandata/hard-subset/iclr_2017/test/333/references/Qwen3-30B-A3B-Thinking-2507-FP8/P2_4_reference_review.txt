

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling and counterintuitive claim: that the depth and supervised training of convolutional neural networks are not essential for high-quality natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures that rival state-of-the-art approaches based on deep networks. Their key contributions include presenting a minimal baseline for texture synthesis, showing that neither network depth nor pre-training is indispensable, introducing a quantitative measure for texture quality based on the VGG loss, and discussing the theoretical implications for texture synthesis models. The paper provides a thorough empirical evaluation across multiple filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) with different numbers of feature maps, and introduces a multi-scale model that outperforms single-scale approaches.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this recommendation are that (1) the paper addresses a fundamental question in texture synthesis that has been taken for granted in the literature, and (2) the empirical evidence is robust, well-structured, and challenges a key assumption in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's central claim is both novel and significant. The authors correctly identify that the prevailing assumption—that deep hierarchical networks with supervised training are necessary for texture synthesis—has not been rigorously tested. Their experimental design effectively challenges this assumption by systematically varying the network architecture and filter types while maintaining a consistent optimization procedure.

The empirical results are particularly convincing. The authors show that random filters (with 3267 feature maps) often outperform filters trained on natural images (K-means-3267), which is counterintuitive and directly contradicts the common wisdom. Their multi-scale model, which combines different filter sizes, consistently produces high-quality textures that rival the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), especially for textures with regular structures. The quantitative measure based on the VGG loss provides a valuable alternative to human inspection, and the paper demonstrates that this measure generally correlates with perceptual quality.

The paper is well-placed in the literature. It clearly builds on Gatys et al. (2015a) while challenging a key assumption, and it situates its work within the broader context of parametric texture modeling. The discussion of the MaxEnt framework and its limitations for finite-size textures is particularly insightful and adds theoretical depth to the empirical findings.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements:

- **Clarify the "why" behind random filters' success**: The paper states that random filters work well due to the large number of feature maps, but a more detailed theoretical explanation would strengthen the paper. The authors could explore how the random filters' statistical properties align with the natural texture statistics they're trying to capture.

- **Strengthen quantitative comparisons**: While the paper provides visual comparisons, adding quantitative metrics (e.g., average VGG loss across multiple textures) would make the claim that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state-of-the-art" more concrete. The paper mentions that the VGG loss is paradoxically lower for some samples from the multi-scale model than from the VGG-based model, but this needs more detailed analysis.

- **Expand the discussion of computational efficiency**: The paper notes that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters, but doesn't quantify this relative to the VGG-based approach. A brief comparison of inference times would be valuable for practitioners.

- **Clarify the relationship between the single-layer model and the MaxEnt framework**: The paper makes an interesting theoretical connection to the MaxEnt framework but doesn't fully elaborate on how their approach fits into this theoretical framework.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that random filters outperform trained filters, but the authors attribute this to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Could you provide more specific details about how the optimization differs from Gatys et al. (2015a), and why this particular tuning makes the single-layer model less prone to local minima?

2. For the textures where the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantage (e.g., brick wall, grids), what are the specific perceptual differences between the synthesized textures? Could these differences be quantified through a simple human study?

3. The paper claims that "a larger number of feature maps generally increases the perceptual quality of the generated textures." Is there a point of diminishing returns, or does quality continue to improve with more feature maps? What's the minimum number of feature maps needed to achieve good quality?

4. The authors note that "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures." Could you provide more detailed quantitative results to support this claim, such as average VGG loss across multiple textures for each model?

This paper makes a significant contribution to our understanding of what is truly necessary for natural texture synthesis. By demonstrating that simple single-layer networks with random filters can achieve high-quality results, the authors challenge a fundamental assumption in the field and open new avenues for research in generative models. The paper is well-written, well-structured, and the results are scientifically rigorous, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
