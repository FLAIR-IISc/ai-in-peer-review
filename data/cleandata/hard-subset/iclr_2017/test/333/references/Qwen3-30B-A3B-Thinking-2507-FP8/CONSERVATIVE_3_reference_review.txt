

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper challenges the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical networks with supervised training are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random filters can generate textures that rival or surpass the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a). They present a series of experiments comparing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) against the multi-layer VGG-based model, demonstrating that random filters can produce high-quality textures. The paper also introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss and discusses the relationship between texture synthesis, perceptual similarity, and variability.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes a bold claim that challenges established understanding in the field, but the evidence provided is insufficient to support this claim with the rigor required for ICLR. The primary issue is the lack of thorough validation against established baselines and the overreliance on a single quantitative metric that the authors themselves acknowledge has limitations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's central claim—that random single-layer networks can match or exceed the quality of multi-layer, pre-trained networks for texture synthesis—requires exceptionally strong evidence given its counterintuitive nature and the established literature (including Gatys et al., 2015a, which explicitly stated that random networks fail to produce perceptually interesting images).

Several critical issues undermine the paper's claims:
- The paper relies almost exclusively on visual comparisons and a single quantitative metric (VGG-based loss) to evaluate texture quality. The authors acknowledge this metric "fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for certain textures (e.g., brick walls, grids), yet they use it as the primary validation method. A proper evaluation of texture synthesis should include human perceptual studies, which the paper lacks.
- The claim that random filters perform better than trained filters is based on limited visual evidence and lacks statistical significance testing. The paper shows examples where random filters "outperform" trained filters, but it doesn't establish whether this is a consistent pattern across a large dataset of textures.
- The paper suggests the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a) is due to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." However, the paper provides no evidence that this tuning is systematically different from what was used in previous work, nor does it explain why the same optimization procedure would work better with random filters than with trained filters.
- The paper does not adequately address why their single-layer model would outperform multi-layer networks given the well-established fact that deeper representations capture more complex hierarchical structures in images. The authors acknowledge that "the combination of different scales in the multi-scale network is important," but this contradicts their main claim about the minimal requirements for texture synthesis.

The paper's methodology lacks the rigor expected for ICLR. While the experiments are well-described, the validation approach is insufficient for such a significant claim. The paper does not compare against established parametric texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000), which were state-of-the-art before deep learning approaches. The authors acknowledge that their method surpasses these older methods, but they don't provide evidence that it's better than other deep learning approaches beyond Gatys et al. (2015a).

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct human perceptual studies to validate texture quality claims, as the paper itself acknowledges that "the quality of the synthesized textures is usually determined by human inspection."
2. Provide statistical significance testing for their claims about random filters outperforming trained filters, rather than relying on a few visual examples.
3. Compare against additional established baselines beyond Gatys et al. (2015a), including both traditional parametric methods and other deep learning approaches to texture synthesis.
4. Better explain the optimization differences that supposedly enable random filters to outperform trained filters, and demonstrate that these differences aren't simply due to better hyperparameter tuning.
5. Address the contradiction between their claim that multi-layer networks aren't necessary and their own finding that multi-scale single-layer networks outperform single-scale ones, which suggests that some form of multi-scale representation is important.

## Questions for the Authors
1. Can you provide quantitative evidence (e.g., human perceptual study results) that demonstrates the textures generated by your random single-layer model are consistently perceived as better than those from Gatys et al. (2015a) across a large set of textures?
2. How do you account for the fact that Gatys et al. (2015a) explicitly stated that "networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images," and what specific changes in the optimization procedure explain this discrepancy?
3. Why does the paper show that multi-scale single-layer networks outperform single-scale ones (suggesting that multi-scale representation is important), while simultaneously claiming that deep hierarchical networks aren't necessary? How do these two findings reconcile?
4. Can you provide evidence that the "better" results with random filters aren't simply due to differences in optimization rather than the filter type itself? For example, would a trained network with the same optimization procedure perform as well as your random filters?
