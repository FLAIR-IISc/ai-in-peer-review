- Main claim: A single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures rivaling deep supervised VGG-based methods.  
- Contribution: Systematic evaluation of single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) across filter sizes/numbers.  
- Metric: Introduces a VGG-based quantitative texture quality measure and a formal generalization of maximum entropy models for texture variability.  
- Core finding: Neither hierarchical multi-layer representations nor supervised pretraining are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.  
- Experimental evidence: Random filters (with sufficient quantity, e.g., 3267) perform as well or better than trained/hand-crafted filters.  
- Methodology: Careful optimization tuning (including L-BFGS-B details) and both qualitative and quantitative evaluations support the results.  
- Architecture: Multi-scale design (3×3, 11×11, 55×55) captures structures across scales that single-scale models miss.  
- Theory: Insightful discussion on limitations of maximum entropy models for finite-size textures and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — significant, well-positioned, and testable claim.  
- Revise: Quantitatively compare filter types using the VGG loss instead of relying mainly on visual inspection.  
- Revise: Separate effects of filter quantity versus filter type with controlled experiments varying number while fixing type.  
- Revise: Provide more detail on optimization differences and hyperparameters relative to Gatys et al. (2015a) for reproducibility.  
- Revise: Quantify computational efficiency and runtime differences between the single-layer random model and multi-layer VGG.  
- Question: Why does VGG-based loss sometimes contradict perceptual quality (e.g., grids, brick wall) and how to reconcile metric vs perception?  
- Question: Does the choice of initialization distribution (Glorot uniform vs Gaussian) affect random-filter performance?
