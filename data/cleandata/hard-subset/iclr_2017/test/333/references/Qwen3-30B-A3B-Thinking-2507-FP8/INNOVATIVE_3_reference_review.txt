

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a bold and counterintuitive claim: the deep hierarchical representations and supervised training of feature spaces previously considered essential for natural texture synthesis are not actually necessary. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random, data-independent filters can generate textures of perceptual quality that rival or even surpass the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), which relies on a deep 19-layer VGG network. Their key contributions include presenting a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis, systematically comparing various single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, K-means, etc.), introducing a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss, and providing a theoretical discussion on the relationship between perceptual similarity and texture variability.

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated, challenging a fundamental assumption in the field with a clear experimental design. The authors rigorously test the necessity of deep representations and pre-trained features by comparing numerous single-layer models against the state-of-the-art, and their results consistently show that random filters can produce high-quality textures.

## 2. Decision

Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its bold conceptual contribution that challenges a fundamental assumption in the field. The finding that random single-layer CNNs can produce textures rivaling the state-of-the-art is not just an incremental improvement but a paradigm shift that reevaluates the role of deep hierarchical representations in natural image generation. This conceptual novelty is exactly what I prioritize as a reviewer.

The paper supports its claims through comprehensive empirical evidence across multiple datasets and texture types. The authors systematically compare 10 different single-layer architectures (including random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA-based filters) with both 363 and 3267 feature maps, demonstrating that random filters perform as well as or better than trained filters. The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss provides a more objective metric than human inspection alone, which is a significant methodological contribution.

Most importantly, the paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by questioning the necessity of deep networks for texture synthesis, suggesting that hierarchical representations may simply serve as "fine-tuning" rather than being fundamentally necessary. This reframing of the problem has implications beyond texture synthesis, potentially influencing how we think about the role of depth in generative models more broadly.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of why previous work (Gatys et al., 2015a) suggested random networks fail, as the authors mention this discrepancy but don't fully resolve it. A more detailed comparison of optimization procedures between the two papers would strengthen the argument.

2. While the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model is acknowledged, the paper could better address the practical trade-offs between quality and efficiency. For instance, how much better is the multi-scale model compared to the single-scale random model, and at what computational cost? This would help readers understand when to use which approach.

3. The discussion of variability versus perceptual similarity is insightful but could be expanded. The authors note that the single-layer model tends to favor perceptual similarity over variability, but they don't provide concrete examples of how this trade-off manifests in different texture types. A more detailed analysis of this trade-off across various texture categories would be valuable.

4. The paper mentions that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages for regular textures like walls and grids. It would be helpful to include a more systematic analysis of when the VGG loss fails and why, as this would inform future work on better perceptual metrics.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that random filters perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural images, yet the authors note that "completely random filters perform on par or better then filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, particularly given that the filters are data-independent?

2. The paper mentions that the multi-scale model often rivals the state-of-the-art, but Figure 4 shows some textures where the VGG-based model still performs better. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the texture types where the single-layer model excels versus where the VGG-based model is superior?

3. The paper introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss, but acknowledges its limitations. What are your thoughts on developing a more robust perceptual metric specifically designed for texture synthesis that could better capture the nuances of texture quality?

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the field of texture synthesis that challenges fundamental assumptions and opens new directions for research. The boldness of the claim, combined with rigorous experimental validation, makes it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
