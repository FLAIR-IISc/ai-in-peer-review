

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

1. **Summary of Contributions**: The paper investigates the essential components required for high-quality natural texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are necessary for effective texture generation. The authors present a strong minimal baseline using a single convolutional layer with random filters (no training required) and demonstrate that this simple model can synthesize textures rivaling or exceeding state-of-the-art methods. They test various single-layer architectures with different filter types (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random) and introduce a multi-scale model that combines multiple filter sizes. The paper provides both qualitative human evaluation and a quantitative VGG-based loss metric to assess texture quality, demonstrating that random filters often outperform learned filters and that the multi-scale approach significantly improves results.

2. **Decision**: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by rigorously challenging a fundamental assumption in texture synthesis and providing robust evidence to support its claims.

3. **Supporting Arguments**: The paper's central question—whether deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are truly necessary for texture synthesis—is clearly articulated and well-motivated by the literature. The authors place their work squarely within the context of prior texture synthesis methods, particularly building on Gatys et al. (2015a), and identify a clear gap in understanding the essential components of their approach. The paper supports its claims with extensive empirical evidence: they test a wide range of single-layer architectures, conduct thorough ablation studies (e.g., testing the importance of non-linearities and multiple filter scales), and provide both qualitative and quantitative evaluations. The results are scientifically rigorous, with careful comparison against state-of-the-art methods and appropriate baselines. The finding that random filters outperform learned filters is particularly noteworthy and well-supported by their experiments.

4. **Additional Feedback**: The paper could strengthen its contribution by providing a more detailed computational efficiency analysis of the multi-scale model compared to existing methods. While the authors mention computational inefficiency, a quantitative comparison would better contextualize the trade-offs. Additionally, a more systematic exploration of the relationship between the VGG-based loss metric and human perception would further validate their evaluation approach. The paper notes limitations in the VGG loss for certain textures but could elaborate on why these discrepancies occur and how they might be addressed.

5. **Questions for Authors**:
- How do you explain the counterintuitive finding that random filters outperform learned filters? Is this primarily due to the specific optimization procedure described in Section 4, or does it reflect a deeper characteristic of texture statistics?
- The paper notes that for some textures (like brick walls), the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantages of the multi-scale model. Could you provide more insight into why this discrepancy occurs for certain texture types but not others?
- The authors mention that initializing VGG-based synthesis with single-layer model outputs yields better results. Could this technique be leveraged to improve existing texture synthesis methods, and if so, what would be the practical implications?
