

# Review for "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of Claims

This paper investigates the necessity of deep hierarchical representations and supervised training for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim that a single convolutional layer with random filters can generate textures that rival the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a), challenging the prevailing view that multi-layer networks with pre-trained features are essential. They present a comprehensive comparison of various single-layer architectures (Fourier-based, K-means, PCA, random filters) with different filter sizes and numbers, and demonstrate that random filters often outperform trained filters. The paper also introduces a quantitative metric based on VGG loss to evaluate texture quality and discusses the theoretical implications for maximum entropy texture modeling.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting empirical finding that random single-layer networks can generate high-quality textures, the paper contains numerous technical inconsistencies, incomplete details, and methodological weaknesses that undermine confidence in the results and their interpretation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The primary reasons for rejection are:

1. **Critical technical inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple technical errors that compromise its credibility. The most glaring issue is the incomplete citation for Glorot & Bengio (2010), which appears as "Glorot & Bengio, )," with a missing year. This suggests poor attention to detail. The paper also uses inconsistent notation (e.g., "N∑ i,j=1" instead of proper summation notation) and contains grammatical errors ("better then" instead of "better than").

2. **Insufficient methodological details**: The paper claims to use "random filters drawn from a uniform distribution" but fails to specify the exact distribution parameters or random seed values. This severely impacts reproducibility. The paper states they "scale the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7" without justification, making it impossible to assess if this is a critical hyperparameter tuning step that might explain their results.

3. **Lack of rigorous evaluation**: The paper claims their method "rivals" the state-of-the-art but provides no statistical significance testing for their comparisons. The VGG-based loss metric is acknowledged to fail for some textures (e.g., "wall" and "grids"), yet the paper doesn't quantify how often this occurs or provide a more robust evaluation methodology.

4. **Incomplete comparisons**: The paper compares only to Gatys et al. (2015a) but fails to compare against other recent texture synthesis methods (e.g., Ulyanov et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2016), which could provide context for whether their approach truly represents a significant advancement.

5. **Inconsistent reporting**: The paper states "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images," yet Figure 1 suggests that for some textures, trained filters (K-means) outperform random filters. The paper doesn't reconcile this discrepancy.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. **Fix all technical inconsistencies**: Complete all citations, correct grammatical errors, and standardize mathematical notation. This is non-negotiable for a publication in a venue like ICLR.

2. **Provide complete methodological details**: Specify the exact distribution for random filters, the random seed used, and justify the 10^7 scaling factor. Include all hyperparameters used in the optimization process.

3. **Strengthen evaluation**: Provide statistical significance testing for all comparisons, and include a more comprehensive comparison against recent texture synthesis methods. Address the limitations of the VGG-based loss metric with concrete examples and analysis.

4. **Clarify theoretical claims**: The paper makes bold claims about "neither aspect being imperative" (hierarchical representation or supervised training) but doesn't provide sufficient theoretical justification. The discussion of maximum entropy models in Section 7 is too brief to support the main claims.

5. **Improve figure references**: The paper references Figures 1-4 but doesn't include them in the provided text. The reviewers need to see the actual results to evaluate the claims.

**Questions for authors:**

1. Could you specify the exact uniform distribution used for random filters (e.g., U[-0.5, 0.5]) and the random seed used in all experiments?

2. Why was the loss scaled by 10^7? Was this a critical hyperparameter, and how did you determine this value?

3. The paper claims random filters outperform trained filters, but Figure 1 shows K-means-363 outperforms Random-363 for some textures. How do you reconcile this with your main claim?

4. How many trials were conducted for each texture to ensure results aren't due to random initialization of the optimization process?

5. Why wasn't the multi-scale model compared against other state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a)?

This paper has an interesting core idea but falls short in execution due to numerous technical and methodological flaws. With the requested revisions, it could potentially be a strong contribution, but as currently written, it does not meet the standards expected for publication at ICLR.
