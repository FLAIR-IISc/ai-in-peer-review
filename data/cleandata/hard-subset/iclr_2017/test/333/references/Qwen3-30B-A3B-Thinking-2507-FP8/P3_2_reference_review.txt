

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a compelling challenge to a fundamental assumption in the field of texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical networks with supervised training are necessary for generating high-quality natural textures. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with completely random, data-independent filters can synthesize textures of perceptual quality that often rival or exceed the current state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a). Their key contributions include:

- Proposing a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis that relies solely on a single-layer network with random filters
- Systematically comparing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA)
- Showing that random filters outperform filters trained on natural image statistics
- Introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based loss to replace subjective human evaluation
- Providing a theoretical discussion connecting their findings to maximum entropy texture modeling frameworks

The most surprising finding is that random filters can match or exceed the quality of pre-trained networks, contradicting earlier reports (Gatys et al., 2015a) that suggested random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images. The authors attribute this to a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure rather than any inherent advantage of the network architecture.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

**Key reasons for acceptance**:
1. The paper addresses a fundamental question about the role of depth and pre-training in texture synthesis with significant implications for understanding generative models.
2. The experimental design is thorough, with extensive comparisons across multiple filter types and architectures, supported by both qualitative and quantitative evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

### Strengths

**Significant theoretical contribution**: The paper challenges a widely held assumption in the field and provides compelling evidence that deep hierarchical representations are not necessary for texture synthesis. This is a major conceptual contribution that could reshape how we think about generative models for natural image generation.

**Rigorous experimental evaluation**: The authors systematically compare multiple architectures (Fourier-363, K-means-363, Random-363, etc.) with varying filter types and numbers of feature maps. The multi-scale approach (with 1024 random filters) is particularly impressive, demonstrating that texture synthesis can be achieved with a much simpler architecture than previously thought.

**Quantitative evaluation**: The use of VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure for texture quality is a significant improvement over the traditional reliance on human inspection. This provides a more objective and reproducible way to evaluate texture synthesis methods.

**Theoretical grounding**: The paper provides a thoughtful discussion connecting their findings to the maximum entropy framework for texture modeling, addressing the limitations of the traditional approach when applied to finite-size images. This theoretical discussion adds significant depth to the empirical findings.

### Areas for Improvement

**Lack of statistical significance testing**: While the paper provides visual comparisons and quantitative VGG loss values, it doesn't include statistical tests to confirm that the differences in quality between methods are statistically significant. This is particularly important when claiming that "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images."

**Insufficient comparison with other baselines**: The paper compares against Gatys et al. (2015a) but doesn't directly compare with other texture synthesis methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) or Liu et al. (2016), which were state-of-the-art before the advent of deep learning approaches. A more comprehensive comparison would better establish the relative merits of their method.

**Optimization procedure details**: The paper mentions "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" as the reason for the success of random filters but doesn't provide specific details about what this tuning entails. This makes it difficult to reproduce the results or understand exactly what makes the random filters work.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Include statistical significance testing**: Add statistical tests (e.g., t-tests with p-values) to confirm that the differences in VGG loss between the methods are statistically significant, particularly for the claim that random filters outperform trained filters.

2. **Expand comparison with other methods**: Include a brief comparison with other texture synthesis methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) to better contextualize the performance of the proposed method. This would help readers understand the full significance of the results.

3. **Provide more details on optimization**: Elaborate on the specific parameters and tuning procedures that led to the success of the random filters. What exact changes to the optimization algorithm made the difference?

4. **Add computational cost analysis**: Include a table or figure comparing the computational cost (in terms of time and memory) of the proposed methods versus the state-of-the-art methods. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

5. **Clarify the multi-scale implementation**: The paper mentions a "multi-scale model" but doesn't fully explain how it's implemented. A more detailed description of the multi-scale architecture would help readers better understand the method.

## 5. Clarifying Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Could you provide more details about the specific random filter initialization and optimization parameters that led to this result, and how these differ from the optimization procedures used in previous work?

2. The paper mentions that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences between the reference and the synthesized texture. Only for a few textures, especially those with very regular man-made structures (e.g. the wall or the grids), the VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis." Could you elaborate on why this failure occurs specifically for these textures, and what might be a better metric for these cases?

3. In Figure 4, some samples from the multi-scale model appear to have artifacts (e.g., the brick wall texture). Could you explain why these artifacts appear and whether they're present in other textures as well? How do these artifacts compare to artifacts in the Gatys et al. (2015a) method?

4. The paper states that "a larger number of feature maps generally increases the perceptual quality of the generated textures." Is there an upper bound to this effect, or does the quality continue to improve with more feature maps? If there's a point of diminishing returns, what is it?

5. The paper suggests that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus loses variability." How does the authors' method address the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and what specific changes would you recommend to improve variability while maintaining perceptual quality?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field by challenging fundamental assumptions about the necessity of deep networks for texture synthesis. With minor revisions to address the statistical significance, implementation details, and comparative analysis, it will be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
