

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper makes a compelling contribution by challenging the widely held assumption that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are essential for natural texture synthesis. The authors present a strong minimal baseline that relies solely on a single-layer network with random, data-independent filters, demonstrating that such a simple model can generate textures that rival state-of-the-art approaches. They conduct a comprehensive evaluation across multiple single-layer architectures (including random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters) and establish that neither network depth nor pre-training is necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The paper also introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG loss, providing a more objective alternative to traditional human inspection for evaluation.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that random single-layer networks can achieve texture synthesis quality comparable to complex hierarchical models, challenging a fundamental assumption in the field. The experimental design is thorough, and the results are well-supported.

## 3. Supporting arguments for decision
The paper addresses a clear and important question in texture synthesis: whether deep hierarchical representations are truly necessary. The authors position their work well within the literature by explicitly contrasting with previous work (Gatys et al., 2015a) that claimed random networks fail for texture synthesis, attributing this discrepancy to better optimization procedures rather than architectural differences.

The experimental design is scientifically rigorous and comprehensive. The authors systematically compare multiple single-layer architectures with different filter types and sizes (363 vs. 3267 feature maps), including random, Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters. Their quantitative evaluation using VGG-based loss provides a valuable complement to traditional human evaluation, and their qualitative results consistently show that random filters perform as well as or better than trained filters. The exploration of multi-scale filters and the demonstration that combining multiple filter sizes is crucial for capturing different spatial structures further strengthens their argument.

The paper's claims are well-supported by both quantitative and qualitative evidence. The results in Figures 1-4 provide convincing evidence that random single-layer networks can generate textures that rival state-of-the-art methods. The authors also provide a thoughtful analysis of why random filters work well (the large number of feature maps enables sufficient statistical constraints) and discuss the limitations of their approach (e.g., computational inefficiency for large filters).

## 4. Additional feedback for improvement
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of the optimization procedure, particularly the scaling factor of 10^7 for the loss and gradients. It would be helpful to know how sensitive the results are to this parameter.
- While the paper mentions the VGG-based loss isn't perfect for all textures, providing a more detailed analysis of specific cases where the loss fails to capture perceptual quality would strengthen the discussion.
- An analysis of the statistical properties of the random filters that might explain their effectiveness would provide deeper insights into why they work so well.
- The computational efficiency discussion in Section 7 could be expanded to include specific runtime comparisons between the single-layer model and multi-layer approaches.

## 5. Questions for authors
1. The paper states that a scaling factor of 10^7 is used for the loss and gradients to prevent early stopping. Could you provide more details on why this scaling is necessary and how sensitive the results are to this specific value?
2. The paper mentions that the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantage for some textures like walls and grids. Could you provide specific examples of these cases and discuss the perceptual differences between the synthesized textures and the reference?
3. The paper demonstrates that random filters work well, but doesn't explore why. Could you provide an analysis of the frequency content or other statistical properties of the random filters that might explain their effectiveness compared to hand-crafted or learned filters?
