

# Peer Review for "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in the field of texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained weights are necessary for generating high-quality natural textures. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional neural network with random filters can generate textures that rival the state-of-the-art approach by Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a deep 19-layer VGG network with supervised training. The paper systematically explores various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and shows that random filters often outperform filters trained on natural images. The authors introduce a multi-scale model that combines filters of different sizes, which further improves performance. Additionally, they propose using a VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure of texture quality, providing an objective alternative to human inspection. The theoretical discussion connecting these findings to the Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling adds significant depth to the work. Overall, this paper offers a compelling re-evaluation of the role of deep representations in natural image generation.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The primary reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper makes a strong, well-motivated contribution that challenges a widely held assumption in the field of texture synthesis.
2. The experimental design is rigorous, with comprehensive comparisons across multiple model configurations and texture types.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's central claim—that random filters in a single-layer CNN can generate high-quality textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods—is well-supported by the evidence. The authors provide extensive visual comparisons across multiple textures and model configurations, showing that random filters often perform as well or better than filters trained on natural images. The multi-scale model in particular demonstrates impressive results, capturing both small-scale and long-range structures that single-scale models miss.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, building effectively on the seminal work of Gatys et al. (2015a) while challenging a key assumption in their approach. The authors correctly identify that the depth and pre-training of networks have been assumed critical for texture synthesis, and they provide a clear, well-executed test of this assumption.

The paper's theoretical discussion regarding the relationship between their findings and the Maximum Entropy framework is insightful and adds significant value to the work. This discussion helps contextualize their empirical findings within a broader theoretical framework.

The proposed VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure of texture quality is a valuable contribution that addresses a limitation in the field (reliance on subjective human evaluation). While not perfect, it provides a more objective standard for comparison.

The paper's contribution is particularly valuable because it suggests that the complex machinery of deep learning may not be necessary for texture synthesis, opening up new research directions and potentially simplifying texture generation approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

### Strengths to Highlight
- The paper effectively challenges a common assumption in the field, which is a valuable contribution.
- The systematic exploration of different filter types and scales is thorough and well-designed.
- The multi-scale model is particularly impressive and well-justified.

### Suggestions for Improvement

**Quantitative Analysis:** While the paper provides visual comparisons and the VGG-based loss metric, more quantitative analysis would strengthen the claim that random filters outperform trained filters. The authors should provide additional metrics (beyond the VGG-based loss) to support this claim. For example, they could include:
- Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) scores across different models
- Perceptual metrics like LPIPS (Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity)
- Statistical analysis of the VGG-based loss across multiple textures

**Evaluation Methodology:** The paper acknowledges that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages for certain textures (e.g., regular man-made structures). The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of these cases, including:
- Specific examples where the VGG-based loss fails
- Analysis of why it fails for these specific textures
- How the authors' model performs in these cases compared to Gatys et al. (2015a)

**Computational Efficiency:** The paper mentions that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters, but doesn't provide concrete comparisons. The authors should:
- Include runtime and memory usage comparisons between their models and the VGG-based model
- Discuss how these computational requirements impact practical applications

**Limitations and Scope:** The paper should more explicitly discuss the limitations of their approach:
- How their method handles non-stationary textures (textures that change across the image)
- Performance on textures with strong directional features
- Comparison with other recent texture synthesis methods (e.g., Aittala et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2016)

**Reproducibility:** The paper mentions "tuning the optimization procedure" but doesn't specify what changes were made. The authors should provide more details on:
- Specific optimization parameters that were adjusted
- How these changes differ from the standard implementation in Gatys et al. (2015a)
- Why these specific changes were necessary for random filters to work well

### Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that random filters outperform trained filters, but the evidence is largely visual. Can you provide more quantitative metrics to support this claim, beyond the VGG-based loss? For example, how do your models compare on SSIM, LPIPS, or other perceptual metrics?

2. The paper states that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages of the multi-scale synthesis, particularly for regular man-made structures. Can you provide specific examples of these textures and explain why the VGG-based loss fails for them?

3. The multi-scale model uses filters up to 55×55, which is computationally expensive. How do the computational requirements (time and memory) of your best model compare to the VGG-based model by Gatys et al. (2015a)? Do the visual results justify the increased computational cost?

4. The paper mentions that the single-layer model is more robust to local minima than deeper models. Can you provide evidence for this claim, such as analysis of optimization trajectories or comparison of convergence rates?

5. How does your approach handle textures that are not stationary (e.g., textures that change across the image)? For example, how would your model perform on a texture that transitions from one pattern to another?

This paper makes an important contribution to the field by challenging a key assumption in texture synthesis. With the suggested revisions, it will be an even stronger contribution that will likely influence future work in the area. The authors have provided a compelling argument that simpler models can achieve state-of-the-art results, which is a valuable insight for the broader field of generative modeling.
