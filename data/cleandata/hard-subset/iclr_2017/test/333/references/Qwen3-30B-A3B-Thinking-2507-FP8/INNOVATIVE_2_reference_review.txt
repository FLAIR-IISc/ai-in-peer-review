

# Review of "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper challenges a fundamental assumption in texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained features are necessary for generating natural textures. The authors present a surprising and bold finding that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters can synthesize textures of comparable quality to the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach (Gatys et al., 2015a). They systematically evaluate various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted Fourier basis, unsupervisedly learned K-means filters, PCA filters) and demonstrate that random filters often outperform learned ones. The paper introduces a quantitative measure for texture quality based on the VGG loss, and shows that a multi-scale single-layer model (with filters of different sizes) can rival the performance of deep hierarchical models. This work fundamentally questions the necessity of depth and pre-training in texture synthesis, potentially opening new directions for generative modeling.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper presents a highly novel and counterintuitive finding that challenges a core assumption in the field (that deep networks with pre-trained features are necessary for texture synthesis).
2. The minimal baseline they propose is surprisingly effective, with results that often rival the state-of-the-art, making this a significant conceptual contribution rather than an incremental improvement.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper excels in novelty and conceptual significance, which are the primary criteria I prioritize as an ICLR reviewer. The authors' main contribution—demonstrating that single-layer random CNNs can generate textures matching the quality of deep hierarchical models—is genuinely surprising and challenges the field's foundational assumptions. Previous work (including Gatys et al., 2015a) suggested that networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images, but the authors show this discrepancy stems from better optimization tuning rather than inherent limitations of random filters.

The experimental design is thorough and well-justified, systematically comparing multiple single-layer architectures against the state-of-the-art. Their finding that random filters outperform learned ones is particularly compelling, as it suggests the statistics of natural images might not be as crucial as previously thought for texture synthesis. The multi-scale model's superiority over single-scale models also provides valuable insights about the importance of receptive field sizes for capturing different texture scales.

While the paper could benefit from more human evaluation to complement the VGG-based loss metric, this is a minor limitation given the paper's conceptual contribution. The authors acknowledge this limitation in their discussion and propose future directions for better perceptual evaluation metrics. The paper's novelty and conceptual importance outweigh these minor methodological considerations.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. More detailed analysis of why random filters work as well as they do. A deeper exploration of the statistical properties of random filters that make them suitable for texture synthesis could provide additional theoretical insights.

2. A brief discussion of how these findings might extend to other generative tasks beyond texture synthesis (e.g., image generation, style transfer), to help position the work within the broader generative modeling landscape.

3. Clarification of the computational trade-offs: While the paper notes the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, a more explicit comparison of inference time versus quality would be helpful for practical applications.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Could you elaborate on why random filters might outperform learned filters in this context, given that the filters are data-independent?

2. The paper notes that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Could you provide more insight into why this paradoxical result occurs, and what it might imply about the limitations of the VGG-based loss as a perceptual metric?

3. The authors mention that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus looses variability." How might the optimization procedure be modified to better balance perceptual similarity and variability, and what would be the practical implications of such modifications?
