

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a bold and novel contribution to the field of texture synthesis by challenging a fundamental assumption in the field: that deep hierarchical representations and pre-trained feature spaces are essential for high-quality natural texture generation. The authors demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random filters can generate textures that rival or even surpass state-of-the-art methods that rely on deep networks with pre-trained weights. They systematically test various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and filter counts, showing that neither network depth nor supervised training of filters is necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The paper also introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss and provides a theoretical discussion on the relationship between texture synthesis and maximum entropy models. This work fundamentally challenges the prevailing direction in deep learning-based texture synthesis and opens up new avenues for simpler, more efficient texture generation models.

## 2. Decision: Accept

I recommend acceptance of this paper with two key reasons: (1) the paper makes a significant and counterintuitive contribution that fundamentally challenges a core assumption in the field, and (2) the empirical evidence supporting the claim is robust and compelling.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper's strength lies in its bold conceptual contribution, which aligns perfectly with ICLR's preference for novel ideas over methodological perfection. The authors ask a fundamental question: "What does it take to generate natural textures?" and provide a surprising answer that contradicts the conventional wisdom established by Gatys et al. (2015a), which has been the foundation for much subsequent work in the field.

The experimental evidence is particularly strong. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments comparing numerous single-layer network configurations against the state-of-the-art. Their results consistently show that random filters in a single-layer network can produce textures of comparable or superior quality to the deep network approaches. The multi-scale model with random filters particularly stands out, often rivaling the performance of the VGG-based model while using a much simpler architecture.

The paper's contribution is not merely incremental but fundamentally challenges the field's understanding of what is necessary for natural texture generation. The claim that "neither aspect [hierarchical multi-layer representation and supervised training] is imperative" is significant and has implications beyond texture synthesis to other image generation tasks. This is the kind of conceptual breakthrough that advances the field in meaningful ways.

While the paper acknowledges limitations in their quantitative evaluation metric (VGG loss), these do not undermine their main claim. The authors demonstrate that their simpler approach often outperforms the state-of-the-art in qualitative human evaluation, and their main contribution is the conceptual insight rather than the specific evaluation metric.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:

1. The discussion of the "variability" metric could be expanded. While you mention it's challenging to measure, providing a concrete example of how you might measure it (e.g., through human evaluation of texture diversity) would strengthen your argument.

2. The computational efficiency discussion could be more nuanced. While you state that deeper networks don't improve quality but are more efficient, it would be valuable to include a clear comparison of the actual time and resource requirements for your single-layer approach versus the multi-layer state-of-the-art.

3. The theoretical discussion of the MaxEnt framework could be more directly connected to your empirical findings. Specifically, how do your results challenge or refine the theoretical understanding of texture synthesis?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. You note that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages for certain textures (e.g., brick walls). Could you elaborate on which specific textures show this discrepancy and whether there's a pattern to when the VGG loss fails?

2. In your multi-scale model, you use filters of different sizes (3×3, 11×11, 55×55). How did you determine that these specific sizes were optimal, and would a different set of filter sizes yield even better results?

3. You mention that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus looses variability." How might you modify the optimization procedure to better balance perceptual similarity and variability without sacrificing quality?
