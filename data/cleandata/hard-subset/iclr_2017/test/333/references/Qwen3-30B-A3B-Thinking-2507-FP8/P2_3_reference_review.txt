

# Review of "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in the field of texture synthesis: that deep hierarchical networks with pre-trained filters are essential for generating natural textures. The authors present a strong minimal baseline using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, demonstrating that such a simple model can produce textures that often rival the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a). Their key contributions include:

- A rigorous empirical demonstration that neither depth nor pre-training of feature spaces is necessary for high-quality texture synthesis
- Comprehensive comparison of various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned)
- Introduction of a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss
- Theoretical discussion linking their findings to maximum entropy models and the relationship between perceptual similarity and variability

This work significantly advances our understanding of what is truly required for natural image generation and challenges established assumptions in the field.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a critical, previously untested assumption in the literature (that deep hierarchical representations are necessary for natural texture synthesis) with compelling empirical evidence.
2. The findings are surprising yet rigorously demonstrated through systematic experiments and clear visual comparisons.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is exceptionally well-motivated. The authors correctly identify that Gatys et al. (2015a) suggested multi-layer hierarchical representations and supervised training were critical, but they didn't provide rigorous evidence for this claim—they simply assumed it. This paper directly addresses that gap, which is a valuable contribution to the field.

The experimental design is thorough and well-executed. The authors systematically test various single-layer architectures with different filter sizes, types, and numbers of feature maps. Their comparison between random filters and filters trained on natural images (Figures 1-4) provides convincing evidence that random filters can perform as well as or better than trained filters. The discovery that multi-scale filters outperform single-scale ones (Figure 2) and that ReLU non-linearities are essential (Figure 2, right column) adds important nuance to the findings.

The paper also makes a significant methodological contribution by introducing a quantitative measure (VGG-based loss) to complement traditional human evaluation, which addresses a common limitation in texture synthesis evaluation. The discussion in Section 7 connecting their empirical findings to maximum entropy models provides valuable theoretical grounding.

The paper is well-written, with clear figures and explanations. The key findings—particularly that random filters can rival state-of-the-art—are surprising but well-supported by the evidence presented. The paper avoids overclaiming by noting that the VGG-based loss doesn't perfectly capture perceptual quality for all textures (e.g., regular man-made structures), which demonstrates intellectual honesty.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would make it even better:

1. **Quantitative Comparison**: The paper claims the random filter approach "often rivals" the state-of-the-art, but it would be more convincing with specific metrics. A table showing average VGG loss across all textures for each model would provide stronger evidence for the claims.

2. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper mentions that deep hierarchical representations "can improve the efficiency of texture synthesis," but doesn't provide timing data. Including synthesis times for different architectures would strengthen this point.

3. **Broader Implications**: While the paper focuses on texture synthesis, briefly discussing how these findings might extend to other image generation tasks (e.g., style transfer, image inpainting) would enhance the paper's impact.

4. **Limitations Section**: The paper acknowledges the VGG loss doesn't perfectly capture perceptual quality for all textures, but adding a dedicated limitations section would help readers understand the boundaries of the approach.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better assess the paper's claims and significance, I would appreciate clarification on these points:

1. The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim, such as quantitative comparisons of VGG loss across different filter types?

2. The paper claims that "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures." However, Figure 4 shows that for some textures (e.g., the "brick" texture), the VGG-based synthesis appears slightly better. Could you clarify how the authors determine which model "outperforms" in cases where the difference is subtle?

3. The authors note that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus loses variability." Could you elaborate on how they measured this variability, and how they plan to address it in future work?

4. The paper mentions that "a larger number of feature maps generally increases perceptual quality." What is the upper limit to this effect? Would increasing feature maps beyond 3267 continue to improve quality, or does it eventually plateau or degrade?

These questions would help me better understand the scope and significance of the paper's claims, which would strengthen my confidence in the paper's contribution to the field.

In conclusion, this paper makes a significant contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in texture synthesis with compelling evidence. The findings have important implications for our understanding of what is truly necessary for natural image generation, and the paper is well-executed and well-motivated. I recommend acceptance.
