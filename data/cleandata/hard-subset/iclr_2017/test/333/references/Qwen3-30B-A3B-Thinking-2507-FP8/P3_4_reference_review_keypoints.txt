- Challenges the assumption that deep, supervised networks are required for high-quality natural texture synthesis; single-layer CNNs with random filters can match or exceed state-of-the-art.

- Systematic experiments across single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, K-means, random filters) establish a strong minimal baseline producing remarkable perceptual quality.

- Introduces a VGG-based quantitative evaluation metric and offers theoretical insights connecting texture synthesis to maximum entropy modeling.

- Recommendation: weak accept due to a significant empirical contribution and well-designed experiments.

- Key claim: random filters sometimes outperform trained filters, contradicting prior work and suggesting new insights into important features.

- Empirical results (Figures 1–4) are generally convincing; the multi-scale model often rivals Gatys et al.'s approach.

- Major concern: the VGG-based loss fails on certain regular man-made textures (walls, grids), calling into question its reliability as a perceptual metric.

- Major concern: insufficient explanation/comparison for why the authors' optimization (L-BFGS-B tuning) succeeds where prior work reported random weights failed—could be an optimization artifact.

- Major concern: theoretical discussion of maximum entropy models feels disconnected from empirical results and lacks actionable modification guidance.

- Suggestion: clarify optimization differences with Gatys et al., providing detailed hyperparameters and comparative analysis.

- Suggestion: expand evaluation to characterize when the VGG loss fails and include human perceptual studies to validate results.

- Suggestion: address computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model and discuss alternative architectures or trade-offs to mitigate cost.

- Suggestion: explicitly connect empirical findings to how the MaxEnt framework should be modified for practical texture synthesis.

- Question: provide a nuanced analysis of the conditions under which random filters outperform trained filters and why.

- Question: explain why VGG-based loss fails for walls and grids—limitations of VGG or a fundamental issue with the approach?

- Question: detail how the optimization affects the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability and ways to control this balance.

- Question: clarify the conditions where the multi-scale model outperforms state-of-the-art despite some examples favoring the VGG-based model.
