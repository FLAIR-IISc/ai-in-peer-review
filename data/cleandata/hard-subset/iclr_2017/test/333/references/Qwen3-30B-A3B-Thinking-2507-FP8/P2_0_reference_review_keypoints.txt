- Main claim: A single-layer CNN with random filters can generate natural textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods (e.g., Gatys et al.).

- Systematic evaluation: Tested multiple single-layer architectures and filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random), finding random filters often outperform trained filters.

- New metric: Introduces a VGG-based quantitative texture quality measure to complement human evaluation.

- Key contribution: Establishes a strong minimal baseline, challenging the necessity of deep hierarchical representations and supervised pretraining for texture synthesis.

- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.

- Acceptance reasons: Addresses an overlooked fundamental question and provides strong, well-documented, visually compelling results.

- Methodology assessment: Experimental design and descriptions are sound; visual examples (e.g., Figure 4) are particularly convincing.

- Theoretical impact: Challenges prevailing assumptions about depth/hierarchy in image generation and could influence future generative-model research.

- Revision request — VGG validation: Provide human evaluation data and correlation analysis to validate the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perception (subjects, protocol, metrics).

- Revision request — random-filter explanation: Offer explanations or hypotheses for why random filters match or exceed trained filters for texture synthesis.

- Revision request — compute costs: Include concrete computational time/efficiency comparisons across architectures (single-layer vs. multi-layer/hierarchical).

- Revision request — optimization details: Specify the optimization/tuning changes beyond standard L-BFGS-B that improved single-layer results.

- Revision request — limitations: Discuss limitations and generalizability (other image generation tasks, texture types where method may fail).

- Key questions for authors: How was VGG-loss validated vs humans? What properties of random filters explain results? Why do multi-scale samples sometimes have lower VGG-loss than the VGG-model? Quantitative gap between multi-scale and SOTA? Why do some multi-layer random CNNs exactly reconstruct reference textures — a Gram-matrix limitation?
