- Paper challenges the assumption that deep, pre-trained hierarchical networks are necessary for natural texture synthesis, showing a single-layer CNN with random filters can produce high-quality textures.

- Key contributions: proposes a strong minimal baseline (single-layer, random filters), shows baseline matches/exceeds complex methods, systematically tests filter types/sizes, introduces a VGG-based texture quality metric, and analyzes perceptual similarity vs variability.

- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

- Relevance: tackles a timely, important question and challenges widely-held assumptions in the community.

- Motivation and positioning: well-motivated and clearly situated relative to Gatys et al. (2015a) and related work.

- Experimental design: comprehensive experiments across filter types, sizes, and feature map counts with extensive visual comparisons and ablations.

- Results rigor: scientifically rigorous results showing random filters can outperform trained ones and multi-scale models often rival state-of-the-art.

- Methodological contribution: introducing the VGG loss provides a more objective complement to human evaluations.

- Clarify random vs. trained filters: need conditions/analysis explaining when K-means or trained filters outperform random filters.

- Compare to traditional methods: expand comparisons to parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) to quantify improvements.

- Computational efficiency: discuss optimizations (e.g., dilated convolutions) to mitigate multi-scale model inefficiency from large filters.

- Theoretical discussion: expand the MaxEnt-framework discussion to better ground empirical findings.

- Question—optimization differences: clarify how their optimization differs from Gatys et al. (2015a) and the role of L-BFGS-B parameters in reduced variability.

- Question—VGG loss anomalies: provide examples where multi-scale samples have lower VGG loss than VGG-based models and explain implications for VGG as a perceptual metric.

- Question—additional metrics: supply quantitative evidence beyond VGG loss (e.g., human evaluation scores or other perceptual metrics) to support state-of-the-art claims.

- Question—multi-scale advantages: elaborate why combining scales improves synthesis and how multi-scale models capture dependencies that single-scale models miss.
