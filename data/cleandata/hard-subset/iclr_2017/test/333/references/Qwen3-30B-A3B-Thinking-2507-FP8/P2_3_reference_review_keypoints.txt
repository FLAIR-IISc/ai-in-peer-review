- Challenges the assumption that deep, pre-trained hierarchical networks are necessary for natural texture synthesis.  
- Shows a single-layer convolutional network with random filters can often rival Gatys et al. (2015a) in texture quality.  
- Provides a comprehensive comparison of single-layer architectures with random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned filters.  
- Introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG synthesis loss.  
- Offers theoretical discussion linking results to maximum entropy models and perceptual similarity vs. variability.  
- Recommends paper acceptance.  
- Argues the paper addresses a critical untested assumption with compelling empirical evidence.  
- Notes experiments are thorough and systematic, testing filter sizes, types, and feature-map counts.  
- Finds random filters can perform as well or better than trained filters, and multi-scale filters outperform single-scale; ReLU non-linearities are important.  
- Praises the use of VGG-based loss to complement human evaluation and the paper’s clear writing and intellectual honesty.  
- Suggests adding a table of average VGG losses across textures for stronger quantitative comparison.  
- Recommends reporting synthesis runtimes to support claims about computational efficiency.  
- Advises briefly discussing broader implications for other image-generation tasks and adding a dedicated limitations section.  
- Requests clarifications: quantitative comparisons of filter types (VGG loss), how multi-scale vs. single-scale superiority is determined in subtle cases, how variability was measured and will be addressed, and whether increasing feature-map counts has an upper limit.
