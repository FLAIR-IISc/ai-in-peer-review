

# Peer Review: "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling challenge to a fundamental assumption in deep learning-based texture synthesis: that hierarchical multi-layer networks with supervised training are essential for high-quality natural texture generation. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can generate textures that rival or even surpass state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a), effectively showing that neither deep hierarchical representation nor supervised training of feature spaces is necessary for natural texture synthesis. Through systematic experimentation with various single-layer architectures (using Fourier basis, K-means clusters, and random filters), the authors establish a strong minimal baseline that achieves remarkable perceptual quality. They also introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG features to supplement traditional human inspection, and provide valuable theoretical insights regarding the relationship between texture synthesis and maximum entropy modeling.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following key reasons:

1. The paper makes a significant empirical contribution by challenging a widely held assumption in the field, with well-designed experiments that thoroughly test the core claim.
2. The authors provide compelling evidence that random filters outperform trained filters for texture synthesis, which contradicts prior work and offers new insights into what features are truly important.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is highly valuable and well-executed. The systematic comparison across multiple filter types and sizes (including the impressive multi-scale model) provides strong evidence against the necessity of deep networks. The results in Figures 1-4 are generally convincing, with the multi-scale model often producing textures comparable to Gatys et al.'s state-of-the-art approach.

However, I have concerns about the evaluation methodology that weaken the paper's strongest claim. The paper asserts that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences," but Figure 4 demonstrates that for some textures (particularly regular man-made structures like walls and grids), the VGG-based loss fails to capture perceptual advantages of the multi-scale model. This undermines the paper's claim that their quantitative metric is reliable. The authors acknowledge this limitation but don't sufficiently address how it affects their main conclusion.

Another concern is the lack of clear explanation for why their optimization procedure (L-BFGS-B with specific parameters) outperforms Gatys et al.'s approach, which previously reported that "networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images." The paper attributes this to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," but doesn't provide sufficient detail or comparative analysis to convince the reader that this is a fundamental insight rather than an optimization artifact.

The theoretical discussion about maximum entropy models is interesting but feels disconnected from the empirical results. While the paper makes a good point about the limitations of the MaxEnt framework for finite-size textures, it doesn't clearly explain how the authors' findings specifically inform how we should modify the framework for practical texture synthesis.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this already strong paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the optimization differences**: Provide a detailed comparison of the optimization parameters and procedures between this work and Gatys et al. (2015a), including the specific hyperparameters that make the difference. This would address the key question of why random filters work here but not in prior work.

2. **Expand the evaluation**: Include a more comprehensive analysis of when the VGG-based loss fails (as noted in the paper) and provide additional evidence that the multi-scale model truly outperforms the state-of-the-art in perceptual quality, not just in the VGG loss metric. Consider including a human evaluation study to complement the quantitative results.

3. **Address computational efficiency**: While the paper notes that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient, it doesn't explore whether this inefficiency can be mitigated by alternative architectures (e.g., using multiple layers with smaller filters to achieve similar receptive fields). A brief discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the practical relevance of the findings.

4. **Strengthen theoretical connection**: Explicitly connect the empirical findings to the theoretical discussion about maximum entropy models. How do the authors' results specifically inform the modification of the MaxEnt framework for practical texture synthesis?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images," but the results in Figure 1 show mixed performance across textures. Could you provide a more nuanced analysis of the conditions under which random filters outperform trained filters, and why?

2. The paper acknowledges that the VGG-based loss fails for some textures (like walls and grids). Could you elaborate on why this failure occurs specifically for these textures, and whether it's a limitation of the VGG network or a fundamental issue with the approach?

3. The authors note that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus loses variability." How does the optimization procedure affect this trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and are there techniques to adjust this balance?

4. The paper claims that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state-of-the-art," but Figure 4 shows cases where the VGG-based model produces better samples. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the conditions under which the multi-scale model outperforms the state-of-the-art?

This paper makes an important contribution to our understanding of texture synthesis and challenges a key assumption in the field. With the suggested clarifications and expansions, it has the potential to become a highly influential work in the area of generative models and texture synthesis.
