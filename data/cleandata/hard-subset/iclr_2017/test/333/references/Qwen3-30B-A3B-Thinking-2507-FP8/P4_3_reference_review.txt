

# Review

## Summary of Contributions
The paper investigates whether hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random filters can synthesize textures that rival state-of-the-art approaches, challenging previous assumptions about the necessity of deep architectures and pre-trained features. They provide extensive experiments comparing various single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA filters) against the Gatys et al. (2015a) model, introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG features, and provide theoretical insights into the relationship between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis.

## Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that depth and pre-training are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis, which challenges previous assumptions in the field. The experimental design is thorough, with careful ablation studies on filter size, filter type, and multi-scale vs. single-scale architectures. The main claims are well-supported by empirical evidence: Figure 1 shows that random filters perform as well as or better than filters trained on natural images, and Figure 4 demonstrates that the multi-scale random model often rivals the state-of-the-art. The ablation studies in Figure 2 convincingly demonstrate that both multi-scale filters and non-linearities are crucial for good performance.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly building on Gatys et al. (2015a) while challenging some of its assumptions. The authors provide a strong motivation for their work by identifying the specific aspects of the Gatys approach they aim to test (hierarchical representation and supervised training). The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG loss (Eq. 7) provides a useful complement to qualitative human evaluation, and the theoretical discussion on the relationship between the MaxEnt framework and practical texture synthesis is insightful.

## Additional Feedback
1. The computational efficiency claims regarding the multi-scale model would be strengthened by providing specific timing numbers comparing the single-layer random model with the VGG-based model.

2. While the paper acknowledges limitations of the VGG-based loss metric, it would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how their approach might generalize to other perceptual metrics, and whether the insights about random filters would hold for different evaluation criteria.

3. The paper claims that "the multi-scale model often rivals the current state of the art" but lacks statistical analysis to support this claim (e.g., p-values for human evaluation comparisons). A brief statistical analysis would strengthen this important claim.

4. Some figures (e.g., Figure 3) are not well-explained in the text and could benefit from more detailed captions that clarify the specific comparisons being made.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences between the reference and the synthesized texture." Could you provide more specific examples where it fails (beyond the wall and grids mentioned), and what these failures might indicate about the limitations of the VGG-based loss for texture synthesis?

2. The authors mention that "for a few textures such as the grid structures, the VGG-based loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model." Can you elaborate on why this might happen, and whether this suggests an issue with the VGG-based loss metric or with the multi-scale model's optimization?

3. In the discussion, the authors state that "a more efficient way to achieve receptive fields of similar size would be to use a hierarchical multi-layer net." The paper then states "while the synthesis is indeed significantly faster, the quality of the synthesized textures does not improve compared to a single-layer model." Could you provide specific timing numbers to support this claim, as computational efficiency is an important practical consideration for texture synthesis?
