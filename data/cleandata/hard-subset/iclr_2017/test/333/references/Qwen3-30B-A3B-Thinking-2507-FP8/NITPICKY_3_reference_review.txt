

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper investigates whether hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training of feature spaces are truly necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors claim to present a strong minimal baseline using a single convolutional layer with random filters that can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods. They test various filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA) and demonstrate that random filters often outperform trained filters, challenging previous assumptions about the necessity of deep networks for texture synthesis. The paper also proposes a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG-based loss to complement traditional human inspection.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting claim but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its main claims. The lack of statistical analysis, methodological inconsistencies, and formatting errors undermine the paper's credibility.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim—that random filters can outperform trained filters in texture synthesis—lacks proper statistical support. The authors rely solely on qualitative visual comparisons, which is insufficient for making such a strong claim. The paper states "completely random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images" without providing any statistical significance tests or quantitative metrics beyond the VGG-based loss, which is the same metric used in the baseline method they're trying to challenge.

The paper contains multiple technical and formatting errors that raise concerns about the authors' attention to detail. Key examples include:
- Missing citation year in "Glorot & Bengio, )"
- Typographical error "max-pooling1" in section 5
- Inconsistent references to "Gatys et al., 2015a" vs "Gatys et al., 2015" in the reference list
- Unexplained scaling factor of 10^7 for loss and gradients in section 4
- Missing figures (Fig. 1-4) that are referenced throughout the text

The paper also makes a significant methodological claim without proper justification: "the VGG-based loss (7) appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences between the reference and the synthesized texture. Only for a few textures, especially those with very regular men-made structures (e.g. the wall or the grids), the VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis." This is a major claim that would require quantitative evidence, but the authors provide only qualitative examples.

Furthermore, the paper's experimental design lacks proper controls. The comparison between random filters and trained filters is not properly isolated, as the random filters are compared against filters trained on ImageNet (which contains textures), while the authors claim their random filters "rival" state-of-the-art methods that use trained filters. Without proper ablation studies controlling for the number of filters, filter sizes, and other hyperparameters, the comparison is not valid.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide complete statistical analysis with significance testing for all claims about performance differences between filter types. Qualitative comparisons alone are insufficient for such a strong claim.

2. Correct all formatting and technical errors throughout the paper, including the missing citation year, inconsistent references, and typographical errors.

3. Include all figures referenced in the text (Fig. 1-4) to enable proper evaluation of the visual comparisons.

4. Clarify the implementation details of the "multi-scale" model. The paper states "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models" but doesn't explain how the multi-scale model is constructed or implemented.

5. Provide a proper comparison of computational efficiency between their method and the state-of-the-art, including runtime and memory usage metrics.

6. Address the circularity in their evaluation: they use the VGG-based loss (which is the same metric used by Gatys et al.) to evaluate their method, which makes it difficult to claim that their method "rivals" the state-of-the-art.

7. Include a proper ablation study to isolate the effect of random filters from other factors (number of filters, filter size, etc.).

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide statistical significance tests showing that the performance differences between random filters and trained filters are meaningful? Without this, your claim that "random filters perform on par or better" lacks scientific rigor.

2. Why did you choose to scale the loss by 10^7? How sensitive are your results to this scaling factor? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis?

3. Could you clarify how the "multi-scale" model is implemented? The paper states it uses 1024 feature maps but doesn't explain how different filter sizes are combined.

4. The paper claims "the VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for some textures, but the VGG-based loss is the metric that Gatys et al. used to optimize their model. How can you claim perceptual advantage when you're using the same metric that the state-of-the-art was optimized for?

5. Why did you use different filter sizes (11×11 vs 55×55) in your experiments, and how did you determine that these specific sizes were appropriate for the problem?
