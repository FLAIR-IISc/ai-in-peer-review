- Challenges the assumption that deep pre-trained hierarchical networks are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.  
- Demonstrates a single-layer CNN with random, data-independent filters can synthesize textures rivaling Gatys et al. (2015a).  
- Presents a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis using single-layer random-filter networks.  
- Systematically evaluates single-layer architectures across filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), sizes (3×3–55×55), and numbers (363–3267 maps).  
- Proposes a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss to replace subjective human inspection.  
- Shows depth and pre-training of multi-layer representations are not as essential as previously believed; multi-scale single-layer model often excels.  
- Offers a theoretical discussion linking results to the Maximum Entropy framework and the perceptual similarity vs variability trade-off.  
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions due to strong empirical evidence and conceptual impact.  
- Experiments are thorough and consistently support the claim that random filters can match or exceed complex models.  
- The VGG-based metric generally correlates with perceptual quality but fails on some highly regular textures (e.g., brick walls).  
- Suggestion: include comparisons to more recent texture synthesis methods (e.g., GAN-based or other generative models).  
- Suggestion: provide runtime and memory comparisons to assess computational efficiency, especially for the multi-scale model.  
- Suggestion: add quantitative analysis of variability across synthesized samples to evaluate the perceptual-variability trade-off.  
- Question: why does the multi-scale model outperform VGG-based on some textures (e.g., bricks, grids) but underperform on others (e.g., pebbles)?  
- Question: why can samples from the multi-scale model have lower VGG-based loss than samples from the model that directly optimizes that loss?  
- Question: how do single-layer models capture long-range structures that extend beyond small receptive fields (e.g., 11×11 filters)?
