This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve texture synthesis quality comparable to state-of-the-art methods relying on deep, pre-trained networks like VGG. They explore various single-layer architectures, employing Fourier basis filters, k-means filters, PCA filters, and random filters, and propose a multi-scale model that further improves results. A VGG-based loss is introduced as a quantitative metric for texture quality, replacing the traditional reliance on human inspection. The core claim is that the hierarchical representation and supervised training commonly used in texture synthesis are not indispensable, challenging previous assumptions in the field.

**Reject.** While the core idea is interesting and the results show promise, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet pervasive, flaws in presentation, methodological rigor, and justification of design choices. These collectively detract from the overall credibility and impact of the work, despite the potentially valuable insights.

The primary issue is a lack of thoroughness and justification. For example, the choice of 11x11 filters feels somewhat arbitrary. Why this specific size? Is there an ablation study investigating the impact of different filter sizes on performance? Similarly, the scaling factor of 10^7 in the optimization procedure (Section 4) is presented as a necessary adjustment without clear explanation or justification. It appears to be a "magic number" tuned empirically. The hyperparameter tuning across all experiments feels similarly ad-hoc, and a more systematic approach would strengthen the claims.

Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation, while a welcome addition, is not entirely convincing. The VGG-based loss is presented as a proxy for perceptual quality, but the authors acknowledge its limitations (Section 7) and even provide evidence where it fails to align with perceptual assessment. Relying heavily on this metric when acknowledging its imperfections weakens the quantitative support for the claims. The comparison to human perception feels superficial, and a formal user study evaluating the synthesized textures would have significantly bolstered the conclusions.

The writing style is also problematic. The paper is dense and often repetitive, with convoluted phrasing. Numerous minor grammatical errors and awkward sentence constructions impede readability. The formatting is inconsistent. For example, the references aren’t consistently formatted and are frequently cited mid-sentence instead of with bracketed numbers. The notation is also inconsistent. Variable names are used without clear definition. The organization of the results section, especially Figure 1, is confusing. It's not immediately clear what each row represents and how the different models compare. Finally, the appendix feels tacked on rather than integrated, and the discussion of related work is not comprehensive. The citation of "Jaynes (1982)" is curiously placed at the end of the discussion, seemingly without direct relevance to the preceding paragraph.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to systematically evaluate the impact of different filter sizes, number of feature maps, and optimization parameters on synthesis quality.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Include a formal user study to assess the perceptual quality of the synthesized textures and validate the VGG-based loss.
*   **Justification of Design Choices:** Provide clear and concise justifications for all design choices, including filter size, scaling factors, and optimization parameters.
*   **Improve Writing and Formatting:** Revise the manuscript for clarity, conciseness, and consistency in writing style and formatting. Pay close attention to grammar, sentence structure, and notation.
*   **More Thorough Related Work:** Expand the related work section to provide a more comprehensive overview of relevant literature.
*   **Clarify the Appendix:** Integrate the appendix more effectively into the main text and provide a clearer explanation of the additional results.
* **Filter Whitening:** The effect of filter whitening (Kmeans-NonWhite vs Kmeans) is not adequately discussed. Is the improvement from whitening statistically significant?
* **Optimization Details:** Provide more details about the L-BFGS-B optimization, such as the specific implementation used and any modifications made.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the rationale behind choosing an 11x11 filter size, and how does it affect the results compared to other filter sizes?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the scaling factor of 10^7 used in the optimization procedure? What is its impact on convergence and synthesis quality?
3.  Were any formal statistical tests performed to compare the performance of the different models? If so, please provide the results.
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the multi-scale model to the choice of scales? Did you experiment with different scale combinations?
5.  Could you elaborate on the theoretical connection between the MaxEnt framework and the observed results? How does the proposed approach relate to the principles of maximum entropy?
