## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture generation, often rivaling the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015a).  The work systematically compares various single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, K-means, PCA, Random) against the established VGG-based method, introducing a VGG-based loss function for quantitative evaluation.  Crucially, they highlight the importance of optimization tuning in achieving good results with simpler models, and discuss the theoretical connection to maximum entropy models and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis.

**2. Decision**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** This paper presents a significant and counter-intuitive finding regarding the complexity required for texture synthesis. The empirical results are compelling, and the analysis is well-conducted. While the theoretical discussion could be deepened, the paper already offers a valuable contribution to the field, prompting a re-evaluation of existing methods and assumptions.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Impact:** The core finding – that random, single-layer CNNs can produce high-quality textures – is a notable departure from existing literature. This challenges the assumption that learned hierarchical representations are essential for this task.  If validated, it simplifies the approach to texture synthesis and opens up new avenues for exploration.
* **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors go beyond subjective visual assessment by introducing a quantitative metric based on the VGG-based loss function. This allows for a more objective comparison between different architectures. The inclusion of comparison against the well-established Gatys et al. (2015a) method further strengthens the evaluation.
* **Thorough Experiments:** The extensive ablation study, comparing various filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and sizes, provides valuable insights into the factors influencing texture synthesis quality.  The multi-scale model investigation is particularly compelling.
* **Theoretical Considerations:** The discussion of the connection to maximum entropy models and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability adds depth and context to the empirical findings. This moves the work beyond a purely empirical demonstration.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement)**

* **Theoretical Deepening:** While the connection to MaxEnt is discussed, it feels somewhat superficial. Exploring the implications of the finite size of images and the resulting constraints more thoroughly would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Quantifying the “mismatch” allowed in the constraints would be valuable.
* **Optimization Details:** The impact of the 10<sup>7</sup> scaling factor in the optimization procedure isn’t fully explained. A more detailed discussion of the optimization process, including sensitivity to learning rate and other hyperparameters, would be helpful. Is this scaling factor universal across all textures, or is it texture dependent?
* **Statistical Significance:**  While visually compelling, the comparisons rely heavily on qualitative assessment alongside the VGG loss. Performing statistical tests to demonstrate that differences in VGG loss are significant would provide stronger evidence.  Perhaps reporting confidence intervals on the VGG loss values would be useful.
* **Computational Cost:** Discussing the computational cost associated with the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based approach would be beneficial, especially considering the authors' point about efficiency.
* **Figure Clarity:** Some figures (e.g., Fig. 2) could benefit from clearer labeling and potentially larger image sizes for easier visual comparison.



**5. Questions for the Authors**

1. **Optimization Sensitivity:**  How sensitive are the results to the 10<sup>7</sup> scaling factor applied during optimization?  Did you experiment with different scaling factors, and if so, how did they impact the synthesized texture quality?
2. **Statistical Validation:** Have you performed any statistical tests to validate that the differences in VGG loss between different architectures are statistically significant? If so, could you please provide the results?
3. **MaxEnt Connection:**  Could you elaborate on how the practical implementation of your method deviates from the theoretical predictions of the MaxEnt framework, given the finite size of the images? Specifically, how do you account for the inevitable mismatch between the summary statistics?
4. **Filter Choice:** You found random filters performing surprisingly well. Did you investigate the statistical properties of these randomly generated filters (e.g., their frequency spectrum) to understand *why* they work?  Is there any discernible pattern in the filters that correlate with better texture synthesis performance?
5. **Generalization:**  Have you evaluated the performance of the single-layer models on textures significantly different from those used in training the K-means filters (e.g., medical images, satellite imagery)? Does the performance degrade significantly?




In conclusion, this is a well-written and insightful paper that challenges established assumptions about texture synthesis.  The empirical results are compelling and the analysis is thorough.  Addressing the minor suggestions and answering the questions above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution to the field.
