## ICLR 2024 Review: What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the requirements for generating realistic textures using deep convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are essential for this task, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods employing deeper architectures trained on ImageNet. They systematically explore different filter types (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random) and sizes, ultimately advocating for a multi-scale approach with random filters. The work introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model and discusses the theoretical connection to maximum entropy models, highlighting the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability in texture generation.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and counterintuitive result – the surprisingly strong performance of single-layer random CNNs for texture synthesis. The results are well-supported by experimentation and the thorough exploration of different network architectures strengthens the conclusions. The introduction of a VGG-based quantitative evaluation metric is also a valuable contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Impact:** The finding that deep pre-training is *not* essential for good texture synthesis is significant. It challenges existing assumptions in the field and opens up avenues for simpler, more efficient texture generation models.
* **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The authors perform a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures against the state-of-the-art.  Figures 1 and 4 visually demonstrate the quality of the generated textures and the competitiveness of the proposed methods. The ablation studies, like the comparison of single-scale vs. multi-scale, are insightful.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:**  The adoption of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a substantial improvement over relying solely on human evaluation, adding objectivity to the analysis. The finding that the VGG loss sometimes favors the synthesized image suggests a limitation of the VGG loss, which the authors acknowledge.
* **Theoretical Grounding:** The discussion connecting the work to maximum entropy models provides a theoretical framework for understanding the results and highlights future research directions.
* **Well-Written & Clear:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, despite the technical details. The organization is logical and the figures are helpful.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Elaborate on Optimization Tuning:** The paper mentions “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” as a key factor in their success with random networks. More details on *exactly* what this tuning entailed would be extremely valuable. What parameters were adjusted, and why? This is crucial for reproducibility.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors note the inefficiency of the multi-scale model, a more detailed discussion of the computational cost (training/synthesis time) for different architectures would be helpful. A quantitative comparison would be stronger.
* **Human Evaluation:** Although the VGG-based loss is useful, a small-scale human evaluation (e.g., a user study asking participants to distinguish real vs. synthesized textures) would further bolster the claims and provide validation for the quantitative metric.
* **Discussion of Failure Cases:** While the results are impressive, it would be helpful to discuss situations where the single-layer models *struggle*. What types of textures are particularly difficult to synthesize, and why?
* **Link to Related Work:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work on texture synthesis, particularly recent advances using GANs or diffusion models.  How do the results compare to these approaches in terms of quality, speed, and computational cost?




**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding optimization tuning:** Could you please provide specific details on the optimization parameters that were adjusted and how they differed from the original Gatys et al. implementation?
* **Regarding perceptual similarity and variability:** You mention balancing these two aspects. Could you elaborate on how the choice of different filter types (e.g., Fourier basis vs. K-means) influences this balance?
* **Regarding the VGG loss:**  You observe discrepancies between the VGG loss and perceived quality. Do you have any hypotheses as to *why* the VGG loss might sometimes be misleading? Is it due to limitations in the VGG architecture itself, or the way the Gram matrices are computed?
* **Reproducibility:** Will you release the code for this work?  This would greatly facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research in this area.
* **Generalization to other tasks:**  Do you see potential for applying these findings to other generative modeling tasks, beyond texture synthesis? For example, could similar principles be used to generate images of other types of natural scenes?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a novel and significant finding. Addressing the points raised in the additional feedback will further strengthen the work and increase its impact. I recommend acceptance.
