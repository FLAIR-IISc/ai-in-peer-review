This paper investigates the necessary components for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing notion that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, demonstrating that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results. They systematically explore various single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG feature space distance, replacing the traditional reliance on human inspection. The core claim is that depth and pre-training are not as critical for texture synthesis as previously believed, and a minimal baseline can yield high-quality results.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting investigation and raises valid points about the potential redundancy of deep networks for texture synthesis, the sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies, coupled with a lack of rigorous analysis in key areas, leads me to recommend rejection. The work feels incomplete and somewhat hastily presented, hindering a clear assessment of its true contribution.

The primary issue lies in the methodological details and their presentation. 
1. **Filter Size Justification:** The choice of 11x11 filters feels arbitrary. A more thorough ablation study varying filter sizes systematically, rather than just presenting multi-scale results, is crucial. Why this specific size? What are the trade-offs?
2. **Optimization Details:** The "scaling factor of 10^7" in the optimization process (Section 4) is presented without justification. This is a significant hyperparameter and its impact needs to be discussed. Is this scaling universal across all architectures? Is it sensitive to the initialization?
3. **Quantitative Evaluation Concerns:** The VGG-based loss, while a step towards objectivity, is treated as a near-perfect proxy for perceptual quality. The authors themselves acknowledge its limitations in the discussion (e.g., failure to capture perceptual advantages for regular structures). Relying heavily on this metric without acknowledging its flaws weakens the empirical support. The observation that VGG loss is *lower* for multi-scale model outputs despite human perception favoring those outputs raises serious concerns.
4. **Inconsistent Presentation:** The network architectures are described in a somewhat disorganized manner. The repeated listing of network configurations (e.g., at the end of Section 2) is redundant and clutters the paper. The naming convention, especially with the repetition of "11x11," could be more concise.
5. **Statistical Significance:** The results shown in Figure 4 lack statistical rigor. Simply *showing* samples does not constitute a convincing comparison. Quantitative metrics (beyond the VGG loss) and statistical tests are needed to demonstrate the claimed performance improvements.
6. **Missing Baselines:** Although the authors compare to Gatys et al. (2015a), it’s unclear if other more recent texture synthesis approaches were considered. A brief discussion on how their approach relates to recent generative models (GANs, diffusion models) would strengthen the work.
7. **Minor Formatting Issues:** Throughout the manuscript there are inconsistencies in the citation format and a couple of missing references (e.g., the Glorot & Bengio citation is incomplete).

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of Contributions:** The contributions list is somewhat verbose. It could be streamlined for better impact.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** The discussion on maximum entropy models is intriguing but feels disconnected from the main empirical work. Expanding on how the limitations of the MaxEnt framework relate to the observed results could be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Study on ReLU:** The importance of non-linearities is mentioned, but a more detailed ablation study removing ReLU from different architectures would strengthen this point.
*   **Robustness Analysis**: The results seem highly dependent on the optimization procedure. A discussion of how robust the optimization is to different initialization strategies or noise levels would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the rationale behind the choice of 11x11 filters, and how sensitive are the results to changes in filter size?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the "scaling factor of 10^7" used in the optimization process, and how this value was determined?
3.  Given the acknowledged limitations of the VGG-based loss, what other metrics are being considered to evaluate perceptual quality, and why were they not included in the final evaluation?
4.  Why are samples from the multi-scale model sometimes achieving lower VGG loss, but are still preferred perceptually? What does this suggest about the limitations of the VGG loss?
5. Could you share the code used for the experiments to enable reproducibility of the results?
