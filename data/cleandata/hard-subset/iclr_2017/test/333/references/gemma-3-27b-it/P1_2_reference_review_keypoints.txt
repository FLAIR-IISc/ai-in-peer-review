- Single-layer CNNs with random filters can generate high-quality textures rivaling deep, pre-trained models.
- The paper systematically compares single-layer architectures (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random) and introduces a VGG-based quantitative texture quality metric.
- Authors conclude depth and pre-training are less crucial than careful optimization; they connect results to maximum entropy models and the perceptual similarity–variability trade-off.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions — the finding is significant and experiments are thorough.
- Novelty/Impact: Challenges the dominant paradigm by showing simpler models can suffice for texture synthesis.
- Experiments: Comprehensive exploration of filter types and sizes strengthens the empirical claims.
- Quantitative evaluation: VGG-based loss provides a more objective measure that correlates reasonably with perceptual quality.
- Theory: The discussion linking to maximum entropy and trade-offs adds useful conceptual context.
- Presentation: Paper is well-written and figures effectively illustrate generated textures and comparisons.
- Improvement needed: Provide detailed optimization settings (learning rates, L-BFGS-B tuning) for reproducibility.
- Improvement needed: Discuss computational cost more fully, particularly for the multi-scale, large-filter models.
- Improvement needed: Include ablation studies to isolate contributions of components (e.g., ReLU, filter size).
- Improvement needed: Add direct side-by-side visual comparisons of synthesized versus original textures.
- Improvement needed: Report statistical significance testing for quantitative comparisons.
- Question: How sensitive are results to the loss/gradient scaling used during optimization?
- Question: Did different random filter initialization schemes or distributions affect results?
- Question: How were filter scales chosen for the multi-scale model—empirical or theoretical basis?
- Question: Provide examples where the VGG-based loss fails and explain alternative metrics considered.
- Question: How well does the approach generalize across a broader range of textures and which texture types fail?
