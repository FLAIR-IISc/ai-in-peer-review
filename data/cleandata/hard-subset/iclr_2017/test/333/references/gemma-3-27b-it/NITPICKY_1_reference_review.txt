This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods relying on deep, pre-trained networks like VGG. They explore various filter types (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random) and architectures, and introduce a VGG-based loss function for quantitative texture evaluation. The core claim is that depth and pre-training are not indispensable for this task, challenging prior assumptions in the field.

**Reject.** While the presented work explores an interesting direction and yields visually compelling results, the overall presentation lacks rigor and suffers from numerous minor flaws that, cumulatively, undermine confidence in the conclusions. The superficial treatment of optimization details and the lack of comprehensive statistical analysis raise serious concerns.

The paper’s main argument hinges on achieving comparable performance to Gatys et al. (2015a) with a drastically simplified model. However, the authors only *claim* a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure as the reason for their success, without providing sufficient detail. Section 4 states they “scale the loss and the gradients by a factor of 10^7”, but provides no justification for this value. Was this determined empirically? Through hyperparameter optimization? This is critical, as the optimization process heavily influences the results. Furthermore, the L-BFGS-B algorithm is acknowledged to have default parameters used without mention of whether these were optimal. 

The comparison to Gatys et al. (2015a) feels incomplete. While visual examples are provided, the quantitative comparison relies heavily on the VGG-based loss. However, the authors admit this loss isn’t perfect and can fail to capture perceptual advantages. The use of an average over only 5 layers (Eq. 7) seems arbitrary; why not explore the impact of including more or fewer layers, or weighting them differently? Similarly, the statement that “VGG-based loss appears to generally be an acceptable approximation” is weak and requires stronger support. A statistical analysis of the correlation between VGG-loss and human perceptual judgment would be far more convincing.

Numerous minor issues detract from the paper’s clarity and polish: 
*   Inconsistent notation (e.g., use of both G(x) and Gij(x) for the same quantity).
*   Typos and grammatical errors throughout the text (e.g., “19-layer convolutional network (Simonyan & Zisserman (2014); in the following referred to as VGG network)” is awkward).
*   The description of the Fourier-3267 filter is confusing and potentially incorrect. The statement "yielding 3 · 3 · 3 · 121 = 3267 feature maps in total" does not make sense given the prior definitions.
*   The explanation of the k-means filters lacks detail regarding the patch sampling process and the choice of 363/3267 clusters.
*   The appendix is referenced but not included in the submitted paper.

These seemingly minor issues contribute to a general impression of insufficient attention to detail, raising questions about the robustness of the presented results.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   Provide a detailed description of the optimization procedure, including the rationale for the scaling factor and the justification for using default L-BFGS-B parameters.
*   Conduct a thorough hyperparameter search for the optimization process and report the optimal settings.
*   Perform a statistical analysis to correlate the VGG-based loss with human perceptual judgments.
*   Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the number of layers used in the VGG-based loss calculation.
*   Address all the identified typos and grammatical errors.
*   Clarify the description of the Fourier-3267 filter and the k-means filter selection process.
*   Include the appendix in the submitted version.
*   Add ablation studies to show the effect of different components of the architecture (e.g. ReLU).

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  What was the rationale behind the choice of 10^7 as the scaling factor for the loss and gradients? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis on this value?
2.  How was the number of clusters (363 and 3267) chosen for the k-means filters? What effect did this have on the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the patch sampling process used for generating the k-means filters?
4.  What specific metrics were used to evaluate the perceptual quality of the synthesized textures beyond the VGG-based loss? Were any human subject studies conducted?
5.  Can you clarify the statement regarding the tensor product in the Fourier-3267 filter description? Is it correct that this yields 3267 feature maps?
