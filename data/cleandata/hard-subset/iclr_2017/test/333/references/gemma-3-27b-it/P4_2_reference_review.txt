The paper investigates the question of what is essential for natural texture generation, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are necessary. It proposes a minimal baseline using a single-layer CNN with random filters and demonstrates its surprising effectiveness, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods. The authors further explore various single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based synthesis loss.

**Reject.** While the findings are interesting and counterintuitive, the paper lacks sufficient rigor in its evaluation and analysis to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The reliance on visual comparisons, despite the introduction of a quantitative metric, weakens the claims. Furthermore, the justification for optimization choices and parameter settings remains superficial.

The core argument – that a single-layer network with random filters can produce textures comparable to those generated by deep, pre-trained networks – is compelling. However, the evidence presented feels anecdotal. While Figure 1 showcases visually appealing results, a more systematic quantitative comparison is needed. The authors introduce a VGG-based loss, but its use appears limited to evaluating textures in Figure 4, and its correlation with perceived quality isn't thoroughly investigated. The claim that the multi-scale model "often rivals" state-of-the-art results is not sufficiently substantiated. The discussion section hints at the complexities of balancing perceptual similarity and variability, but a deeper exploration of these trade-offs is missing.

The extensive experimentation with different single-layer architectures is valuable, but the rationale behind the specific choices of filter sizes and types (Fourier basis, K-means, PCA) isn’t always clear. The selection of 11x11 filters seems somewhat arbitrary; a sensitivity analysis exploring different filter sizes would strengthen the results. The optimization procedure, utilizing L-BFGS-B, is described briefly, but crucial details like the scaling factor of 10^7 are presented without proper justification. Why was this specific value chosen? How does it impact the optimization process and the resulting textures? The appendix offers some supporting evidence, but it feels supplementary rather than integral to the core argument. The conclusion suggests the need for better perceptual metrics, but doesn't build upon the VGG loss already introduced.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Provide a more comprehensive quantitative comparison against the state-of-the-art method (Gatys et al., 2015a) using the proposed VGG-based loss. Report mean scores and standard deviations, along with statistical significance tests.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to determine the contribution of each component of the multi-scale model (different filter sizes, non-linearities).
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different optimization parameters (learning rate, maximum iterations, scaling factor).
*   **Perceptual Study:** Consider conducting a small-scale perceptual study with human participants to validate the correlation between the VGG-based loss and perceived texture quality.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Elaborate on the theoretical basis for the success of random filters. Is there a connection to concepts like universality or random projections?
*   **Clarify "Often Rivals":** Define more precisely what "often rivals" means in quantitative terms.
*   **Explain Scaling Factor:** Provide a clear explanation for the choice of the scaling factor (10^7) in the optimization procedure.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a quantitative comparison (e.g., mean VGG loss and standard deviation) between the multi-scale model and the Gatys et al. (2015a) method across a larger and more diverse set of textures?
2.  What is the rationale behind using L-BFGS-B as the optimization algorithm, and why was the scaling factor of 10^7 chosen?
3.  Did you explore other filter sizes besides 11x11? If so, how did the performance vary?
4.  How does the performance of the proposed method vary with the number of feature maps? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
5.  The paper mentions potential limitations of the VGG-based loss. Could you elaborate on scenarios where it fails to accurately reflect perceived texture quality?
