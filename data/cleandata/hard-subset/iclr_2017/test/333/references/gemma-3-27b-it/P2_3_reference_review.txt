## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the requirements for successful natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture generation, often rivaling the results of the established Gatys et al. (2015a) method. They systematically explore different single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, k-means filters, PCA filters, random filters) and emphasize the importance of optimization tuning.  The authors also introduce a VGG-based loss function for quantitative texture evaluation and propose a generalization of maximum entropy models to account for the variability of textures. Overall, the paper presents a compelling argument for the surprisingly minimal requirements for effective texture synthesis.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a strong and counterintuitive result – that deep, pre-trained networks aren't *necessary* for high-quality texture synthesis.  The experimental setup is thorough, comparing a wide range of architectures, and the quantitative evaluation metric is a welcome addition to the field. The analysis is insightful, and the theoretical discussion about the relationship to maximum entropy models is intriguing.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Impact:** The finding that single-layer networks with random filters can perform competitively with state-of-the-art methods is a significant contribution. It challenges commonly held assumptions about the necessity of depth and pre-training in generative models, particularly in the context of texture synthesis. This is a valuable insight for the broader generative modeling community.
* **Experimental Rigor:** The authors explore a wide range of single-layer architectures, providing a comprehensive comparison. They meticulously detail the network configurations and optimization procedures. The inclusion of a quantitative evaluation metric (VGG-based loss) strengthens the results and moves beyond purely subjective human evaluation.  The comparison against the VGG-based approach is well-defined.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and organized. The introduction clearly motivates the research question, and the methodology is explained in detail. The results are presented with clear figures and descriptions.
* **Sound Theoretical Justification:** The discussion linking the findings to the maximum entropy framework provides a theoretical grounding for the observed results. The acknowledgement of the limitations of the MaxEnt framework in the context of finite-size images is commendable.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Optimization Details:** While the authors mention scaling the loss and gradients, a more detailed explanation of the tuning process and its impact would be beneficial.  Specifically, *why* was a factor of 10^7 chosen? Was this determined empirically or through a specific rationale?
* **Filter Size Analysis:** The discussion around multi-scale filters is strong, but a visualization of the receptive fields of the different filter sizes, overlaid on sample textures, would further strengthen the argument.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost associated with the different architectures (especially the multi-scale model with large filters) would be helpful.  Even a relative comparison would be valuable.
* **VGG-Loss limitations:** While the authors acknowledge the limitations of the VGG-based loss, expanding on *what* textures consistently show discrepancies between perceptual quality and VGG-loss score would provide further insight.
* **Connection to other work:** The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of related work on simpler generative models for texture synthesis (e.g., those based on Markov Random Fields), and how this work differs.




**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the scaling factor (10^7) used in the optimization?  Were other values explored, and how did they affect the quality of the synthesized textures?
2.  How does the computational time for generating a texture with the multi-scale model compare to that of the VGG-based model, and to other single-layer models?
3.  You mention that certain textures (e.g., grids) exhibit discrepancies between perceptual quality and the VGG-based loss. Could you provide specific examples of these textures and explain why you believe the VGG-based loss fails to capture their perceptual qualities?
4.  In the discussion section, you mention the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability.  Did you explore any methods to explicitly control this trade-off during the texture synthesis process? If so, could you briefly describe them?
5.  The paper focuses primarily on texture *synthesis*. Are there implications of these findings for other generative modeling tasks, such as image completion or super-resolution?



I believe this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of generative modeling and texture synthesis.  Addressing the suggested improvements would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
