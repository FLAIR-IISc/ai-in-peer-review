- Paper shows a single-layer CNN with random filters can synthesize high-quality natural textures, challenging the need for deep pretrained networks.  
- Authors propose a multi-scale random filter network as a strong, simple baseline for texture synthesis.  
- They systematically evaluate single-layer architectures and filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and optimization techniques.  
- Introduce a VGG-based quantitative texture quality measure to supplement subjective evaluation.  
- Provide a theoretical discussion linking results to maximum entropy models and finite-size texture effects.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions; results are compelling and counterintuitive with solid empirical support.  
- Strong empirical evidence: visual comparisons and VGG loss show single-layer models often rival VGG-based approaches.  
- Thorough exploration: wide-ranging ablations give insight into why simple models work.  
- Novel quantitative evaluation: VGG loss enables more rigorous, non-subjective comparisons.  
- Theoretical context: adds depth by relating empirical findings to statistical models of textures.  
- Reproducibility: methodology is clearly explained and should be reasonably reproducible.  
- Request to clarify optimization details, esp. sensitivity to the 10^7 scaling factor and L-BFGS-B settings.  
- Suggest adding a small-scale human (perceptual) evaluation to strengthen claims about perceptual quality.  
- Ask for a more detailed comparison of computational cost and synthesis time versus VGG-based methods.  
- Recommend expanding analysis on filter sizes by tying receptive field sizes to captured texture scales.  
- Specific questions: intuition and sensitivity of the 10^7 scaling factor, how multi-scale filter outputs are combined, consideration of other perceptual metrics beyond VGG loss, and whether code will be released.
