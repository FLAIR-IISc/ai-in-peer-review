This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling the performance of state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). They systematically compare different single-layer architectures and highlight the importance of combining filters at multiple scales. Furthermore, they introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model and discuss the limitations of maximum entropy models in the context of finite image sizes.

**Reject.** While the paper presents interesting findings and a valuable baseline, the claims regarding the “rivaling” performance of the single-layer networks require significantly more rigorous justification and the presented results are not convincing enough to overturn established knowledge in the field. The analysis, while extensive, feels somewhat exploratory and lacks a clear, focused argument beyond demonstrating that surprisingly good results *can* be obtained with a simple model.

The main issue lies with the evaluation. Relying heavily on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is problematic. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the discussion, stating the VGG-based loss isn’t perfect and fails to capture perceptual advantages in some cases. However, the main claim of "rivaling" performance is *directly* linked to this loss function. Visual inspection of the results (Fig. 1, 4, 6) suggests the synthesized textures, while decent, often lack the fine details and overall coherence of the Gatys et al. results. The claim that the multi-scale model "often rivals" the state-of-the-art feels overstated based on the presented visual evidence and the reliance on the VGG loss. Furthermore, while the introduction of quantitative measures is a positive step, simply demonstrating a correlation between VGG loss and perceptual quality does not validate the VGG loss as a reliable metric for comparing texture synthesis methods. The appendix offers limited further validation.

The exploration of different single-layer architectures is thorough and the ablation studies regarding filter size and learning methods are insightful. The identification of multi-scale filters as crucial is a valuable contribution. The discussion section is well-written and touches upon important theoretical considerations, such as the limitations of the MaxEnt framework and the need for improved perceptual metrics. However, this section, while interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the core experimental results. It reads more like future work than a strong conclusion supporting the paper's main claims.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **More Rigorous Evaluation:** Conduct a proper user study to assess the perceptual quality of the synthesized textures. This is crucial to substantiate the claim of "rivaling" performance. Employ metrics beyond the VGG loss, such as those based on the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) or Learned Perceptual Image Patch Similarity (LPIPS) which are commonly used in image generation tasks.
*   **Comparative Analysis:** Provide a more detailed and quantitative comparison with Gatys et al. (2015a). Don't just show example textures; report statistical comparisons of the VGG loss and potentially other metrics across a larger dataset of textures.
*   **Focus the Argument:** Sharpen the paper's core message. Instead of claiming to rival the state-of-the-art, focus on presenting the single-layer model as a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis and highlighting the surprising effectiveness of random filters.
*   **Clarify the Optimization:** Provide more details on the tuning of the optimization procedure. The authors mention "more elaborate tuning" as a reason for their improved results compared to earlier reports, but don't specify what these changes were.
*   **Explain the Discrepancy:** Explore the reasons why random filters perform so well. Is it simply a matter of sufficient capacity (number of feature maps)? Or is there something inherent in the random filters that makes them effective for capturing texture statistics?
*   **Address Computational Efficiency:** While the paper acknowledges the inefficiency of large filters, a more detailed discussion of potential optimization strategies would be valuable.

Finally, I have a few clarifying questions:

*   What specific parameters were changed in the optimization procedure compared to the original Gatys et al. (2015a) implementation?
*   Could you provide a more quantitative comparison of the VGG loss between the single-layer models, the multi-scale model, and the Gatys et al. model, including standard deviations?
*   Were any attempts made to train the single-layer filters in a self-supervised manner (e.g., using a texture discrimination task)? If so, what were the results?
*   Could you elaborate on the relationship between the number of feature maps and the quality of the synthesized textures? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
