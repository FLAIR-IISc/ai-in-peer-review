This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are essential for high-quality texture generation, demonstrating that a single-layer network with random filters can achieve competitive results. They present a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures, comparing them against the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. (2015a) model, and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss.

**Reject**

The paper presents a compelling argument and initial results suggesting that single-layer networks can perform surprisingly well in texture synthesis. However, the novelty is incrementally limited and the empirical validation, while extensive, lacks sufficient rigor to definitively support the strong claims made. Specifically, the results are highly sensitive to optimization parameters (mentioned in the introduction but not deeply explored) and the proposed quantitative metric relies on the very deep network the authors aim to circumvent, creating a circularity.

The primary justification for rejection lies in the lack of robustness in the results and the somewhat circular nature of the evaluation. While the authors demonstrate that a single-layer network *can* achieve results comparable to the state-of-the-art, it’s not clear *why* this is the case beyond stating it’s due to “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure”. The paper states that deeper networks are “not as indispensable,” which is a moderate claim, but then suggests the multi-scale random network “often rivals” the state-of-the-art, which implies a stronger equivalence. The figures supporting this claim (Fig. 4) show variability; some textures are better synthesized by the single-layer network, while others are clearly superior from the Gatys et al. approach. This inconsistency undermines the broader claims. The dependence on the VGG-based loss for evaluation, while practical, raises concerns. If the goal is to show single-layer networks are sufficient, evaluating against a metric derived from a deep network seems to validate the performance *relative* to that network, rather than demonstrating absolute quality. Moreover, the authors acknowledge limitations of the VGG-based loss, especially for structured textures.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The ablation studies exploring different filter types and sizes are thorough. The introduction of the quantitative texture quality measure is a valuable contribution, even with its limitations. The description of the various network architectures is precise. However, the paper would benefit from a deeper investigation into the optimization procedure and a more independent evaluation of the synthesized textures.

**Additional Feedback/Questions for Authors:**

1. **Optimization Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure is critical. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the optimization parameters and their impact on the results? A sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims about the single-layer network's effectiveness.
2. **Independent Evaluation:** How do the synthesized textures perform when evaluated using metrics *other* than the VGG-based loss, such as those based on statistical texture features (e.g., co-occurrence matrices, local binary patterns)? Human perceptual studies, even with a small number of participants, would provide stronger evidence of visual quality.
3. **Computational Cost:** The multi-scale model involves large filters and thus significant computational expense. How does the computational cost of the multi-scale model compare to that of the Gatys et al. model for generating textures of comparable quality?
4. **Generalization:** Is the performance of the single-layer network consistent across different datasets and texture types? Are there specific types of textures where it consistently underperforms?
5. **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are interesting, a more theoretical discussion of *why* a single-layer network can achieve such good results would be valuable. Is there a connection to concepts from signal processing or statistical texture modeling that could explain the observed phenomenon?
6. **Clarification on "Rivals":** The use of the word “rivals” is subjective. Can you quantify the improvement or lack thereof compared to Gatys et al., using a statistical test on the VGG loss or other metric? A clear definition of what constitutes “rivaling” performance would be helpful.
