This paper investigates the necessity of deep, pre-trained convolutional networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly good textures can be generated using a single-layer CNN with random filters, challenging the prevailing wisdom that depth and pre-training are crucial for this task. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures with different filter types against the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. (2015a) approach, using both a VGG-based quantitative metric and qualitative visual assessment. The key contributions include a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis, demonstrating the effectiveness of random filters, and a thorough comparison of different single-layer architectures.

**Reject**

While the paper presents interesting findings and a valuable investigation into the core components of texture synthesis, the overall impact and novelty are limited, and the experimental validation lacks sufficient rigor. The core finding – that a single layer with random filters can produce competitive textures – feels somewhat overstated, and the justification for its significance isn’t fully compelling.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of a truly convincing demonstration of the performance claims. While the authors *show* textures, and report VGG-based loss, the comparison to the state-of-the-art (Gatys et al.) is not consistently favorable. Several examples in Figure 1 appear to demonstrate clear visual superiority of the Gatys et al. method. The claim of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art feels unsubstantiated based on the provided evidence. The reliance on the VGG-based loss as a “more objective” metric is questionable, as the authors themselves acknowledge its imperfections in the discussion section. A more robust and statistically sound evaluation is needed, preferably including a rigorous user study to assess perceptual quality.

Secondly, the motivation for exploring this minimal baseline feels somewhat post-hoc. The paper frames the work as identifying “indispensable” elements, but doesn’t clearly articulate *why* the depth and pre-training were suspected to be so crucial in the first place, beyond referencing earlier work. A stronger theoretical justification for the investigation would be beneficial.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The idea of using random filters isn't entirely new (referenced in the introduction), and the core contribution is demonstrating it works “surprisingly well.” This is a valuable observation, but doesn’t represent a breakthrough.
* **Insufficient Quantitative Validation:** The VGG-based loss is used extensively, but its limitations are acknowledged. It serves as a proxy for perceptual quality, but doesn’t replace a genuine perceptual study. The statement about the multi-scale model achieving lower VGG loss than Gatys et al. for some textures is interesting but requires further investigation into *why* this happens.
* **Visual Assessment is Subjective:** The visual comparisons rely heavily on the reader's subjective judgment. While helpful, this isn’t a strong form of evidence.
* **Optimization Details:** The paper mentions careful tuning of the optimization procedure as being critical to achieving the reported results. However, the specific details of this tuning are not fully disclosed, hindering reproducibility. The scaling factor of 10^7 seems unusually large and warrants further explanation.
* **Discussion of MaxEnt and Variability:** The discussion towards the end regarding the limitations of the MaxEnt framework and the need for variability measures is insightful, but feels somewhat disconnected from the main empirical results. It points towards future research directions, but doesn't strengthen the current work.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **User Study:** Conduct a perceptual user study to directly compare the textures generated by the proposed method with those generated by Gatys et al. This would provide a much more compelling demonstration of the performance claims.
* **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical analysis to support claims of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art. Report confidence intervals or p-values for the VGG-based loss comparison.
* **Ablation Study:** Perform a more thorough ablation study to understand the contribution of different components of the multi-scale model (e.g., the combination of different filter sizes).
* **Optimization Details:** Provide more detailed information about the optimization process, including the learning rate schedule, and the impact of the scaling factor on the loss and gradients.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Strengthen the theoretical motivation for investigating the minimal baseline. Why were depth and pre-training initially considered so important?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the number of feature maps and the filter size.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the optimization procedure tuning (specifically, the rationale behind the 10^7 scaling factor)?
2.  Have you considered other quantitative metrics for evaluating texture quality beyond the VGG-based loss? If so, what were the results?
3.  Can you share the code used to generate the textures, to facilitate reproducibility?
4.  Could you elaborate on the cases where the multi-scale model achieved lower VGG loss than the Gatys et al. method? What characteristics do those textures share?
5.  What is the computational cost of the multi-scale model compared to the Gatys et al. approach? Could the computational overhead limit its practical applications?
