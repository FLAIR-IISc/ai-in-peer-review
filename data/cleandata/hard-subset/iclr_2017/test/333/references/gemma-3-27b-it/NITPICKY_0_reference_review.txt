This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods based on deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures, employing different filter types (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random) and sizes, and introduce a VGG-based loss function for quantitative evaluation. The core claim is that the hierarchical representation and supervised training previously considered crucial are not indispensable for this task.

**Reject.** While the presented work explores an interesting direction and yields promising results, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor – yet cumulatively substantial – shortcomings in methodology, presentation, and rigor. The lack of attention to detail significantly undermines confidence in the validity of the conclusions.

The paper's weaknesses are manifold. First, the description of the different filter types lacks sufficient clarity and justification. For instance, the weighting terms in "Fourier-3267" (wR, wG, wB ∈ [1, 0,−1]) feel arbitrarily chosen and are not adequately explained. Why these specific values? What is the reasoning behind exploring different color channel weightings?  Second, the experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice of 11x11 filters is presented without strong justification. A more thorough exploration of filter sizes, perhaps including smaller ones, would be beneficial. Similarly, the sampling strategy for k-means filters – both from ImageNet and the target texture – deserves more detailed explanation and analysis. Why is a sample size of 1e7 patches chosen? Is this number statistically significant? Third, the optimization procedure (L-BFGS-B with scaling factor 10^7) appears heavily tuned without a clear explanation of *why* this scaling is necessary. The authors mention avoiding early stopping, but a more systematic investigation of the optimization landscape would be ideal. The lack of ablation studies regarding hyperparameters hinders reproducibility and understanding of the results.

Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss, while a step in the right direction, is not presented convincingly. The authors claim it's a reasonable approximation of perceptual quality, but acknowledge its limitations, particularly for regular structures. This raises concerns about the validity of the quantitative comparisons. The assertion that the VGG-based loss is *lower* for some samples from the multi-scale model, yet these are not perceived as better, is particularly troubling and demands deeper investigation. The appendix section referencing figures 5 and 6 is incomplete and makes assessing the supplementary material difficult.

Finally, the writing style is often imprecise and repetitive. The discussion section is overly verbose and contains philosophical musings that detract from the core scientific contribution. The paper contains numerous minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, missing periods) that, while individually minor, contribute to a sense of carelessness. The reference list formatting is also non-standard.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Clarity of Methodology:** Provide a more detailed and justified explanation of each filter type and the experimental setup. Include ablation studies to demonstrate the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** Justify the sample sizes used in k-means clustering and other experiments. Consider performing statistical tests to assess the significance of the observed differences.
*   **Optimization Analysis:** Provide a more thorough analysis of the optimization landscape and the effect of the scaling factor. Consider using alternative optimization algorithms and comparing their performance.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:**  Conduct a user study to validate the VGG-based loss function and assess the perceptual quality of the synthesized textures.
*   **Conciseness and Precision:** Revise the writing style to be more concise and precise. Focus on the core scientific contribution and avoid unnecessary philosophical digressions.
*   **Formatting:** Carefully proofread the paper for formatting inconsistencies and ensure adherence to a standard referencing style.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why were the specific weighting terms (wR, wG, wB ∈ [1, 0,−1]) chosen for the Fourier-3267 filters?
2.  What is the justification for the scaling factor of 10^7 in the optimization procedure?  Did you explore alternative scaling factors or optimization algorithms?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why the VGG-based loss sometimes *decreases* while perceptual quality remains unchanged or even decreases?
4.  Could you provide the complete supplementary material, especially Figures 5 and 6?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of filter size (11x11)? Have you experimented with smaller filter sizes?
