This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. Challenging the prevailing wisdom established by Gatys et al., the authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be generated using a single convolutional layer with random filters, often rivaling the performance of deeper, pre-trained networks. They thoroughly explore various single-layer architectures, introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG reconstruction loss, and offer a discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of their findings within the context of maximum entropy models.

**Reject.** While the core idea is intriguing and represents a potentially valuable shift in perspective, the paper feels somewhat exploratory and lacks the depth of analysis needed to fully substantiate its claims. The results, while promising, are not overwhelmingly convincing, and the justification for certain design choices (e.g., the specific optimization tuning) feels insufficient.

The core strength of the paper lies in its boldness. The authors directly challenge established dogma and present a counter-intuitive finding – that simplicity can achieve surprisingly good results in texture synthesis. This is precisely the kind of work ICLR should encourage. The exploration of different filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) is commendable and provides a useful comparative landscape. The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a step in the right direction, moving away from purely subjective human evaluation. 

However, several aspects prevent a positive recommendation. Firstly, the discussion around “optimization tuning” that supposedly accounts for discrepancies with earlier work feels hand-wavy. The authors state it's more elaborate but don’t provide specific details of what was changed beyond maximum iterations and gradient scaling.  What specific learning rate or optimization strategies were employed? A more rigorous ablation study exploring the sensitivity to these hyperparameters would significantly strengthen the argument. 

Secondly, while the quantitative results are presented, the statistical significance of the improvements (or lack thereof) is not addressed. Simply showing that the multi-scale model sometimes rivals the VGG-based model isn’t enough. Proper statistical tests are needed to assess whether the observed differences are truly meaningful. The claim that the VGG loss sometimes *fails* to capture perceptual advantages is interesting but lacks supporting evidence beyond anecdotal observation.

Thirdly, the theoretical discussion, while insightful, feels disconnected from the empirical findings. The connection between the experimental results and the formalization of maximum entropy models is not fully articulated.  How exactly does the success of random filters relate to the idea of allowing a "small mismatch" in constraints? More concrete links between theory and practice are needed.

Finally, while the authors explore various filter sizes, the choice of 11x11 seems somewhat arbitrary. A more systematic exploration of different filter sizes, potentially including smaller and larger values, could reveal important insights into the optimal receptive field size for texture synthesis.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Detailed Optimization Procedure:** Provide a comprehensive description of the optimization procedure, including learning rate schedule, momentum, and any other relevant parameters.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Conduct statistical tests to assess the significance of the observed differences between the different models.
*   **Ablation Study:** Perform an ablation study to evaluate the impact of different components of the multi-scale model (e.g., the number of scales, the filter sizes).
*   **Theoretical Elaboration:** Strengthen the connection between the empirical findings and the theoretical discussion on maximum entropy models.
*   **Filter Size Exploration:** Investigate a wider range of filter sizes to determine the optimal receptive field size.
*  **Visual Comparison:** Include side-by-side visual comparisons of textures generated by each model, with clear annotations highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed description of the “more elaborate tuning” of the optimization procedure? Specifically, what parameters were changed, and how did you determine their optimal values?
2.  Have you performed any statistical tests to assess the significance of the differences in performance between the different models? If so, please provide the results.
3.  How does the choice of the VGG network (specifically the layers used for calculating the Gram matrices) influence the quantitative evaluation metric? Have you experimented with using different layers or different pre-trained networks?
4.   Could you elaborate on how the concept of allowing a "small mismatch" in the constraints relates to the observed success of the random filters in generating textures? Is there a theoretical justification for why random filters would be more effective in this context?
