## ICLR 2024 Review: What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods based on the Gatys et al. (2015) approach. The core contribution is a strong minimal baseline, alongside a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures and a novel quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss. The authors also provide a theoretical discussion linking their findings to maximum entropy models and the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability in texture generation.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and counterintuitive result—that depth and pre-training are not strictly necessary for surprisingly good texture synthesis. The experimental evaluation is thorough, the methodology is sound, and the theoretical discussion is insightful. The work significantly contributes to our understanding of what constitutes a good texture model and offers a simpler, more efficient alternative to existing methods.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Strong Counter-Intuitive Result:** The central finding – high-quality texture synthesis from a single-layer random CNN – is significant.  It challenges the assumption that the complexity of deep networks and learned features is essential for this task. This alone justifies publication.
* **Thorough Experimental Evaluation:** The authors systematically compare multiple single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) with different filter sizes and feature map numbers against the state-of-the-art. This provides a robust basis for their conclusions. The inclusion of a multi-scale random CNN is a particularly valuable addition.
* **Novel Evaluation Metric:** Replacing subjective human evaluation with a quantitative VGG-based loss provides a more objective and reproducible measure of texture quality. This is a key methodological contribution. While not perfect (acknowledged by the authors), it’s a step forward for the field.
* **Insightful Theoretical Discussion:** The paper connects its findings to the theoretical framework of maximum entropy models and highlights the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability, offering valuable insights for future research.
* **Reproducibility:** The implementation details are reasonably clear, and the code is likely accessible (although not explicitly stated).

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement)**

* **Clarify Optimization Procedure:** While the authors mention tuning the optimization, further detail on the "more elaborate tuning" compared to Gatys et al. would be beneficial.  Specifically, what parameters were adjusted, and why did this lead to improved results with the simpler models?
* **Visual Comparison Enhancement:** While the visual comparisons in Fig. 4 are informative, increasing the number of texture examples used for comparison would strengthen the argument. Consider adding more diverse textures.
* **Computational Cost:**  Quantify the computational cost (training and synthesis time) of the single-layer models compared to the VGG-based approach. This would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved.
* **Error Analysis:** Discuss the types of textures where the single-layer models *fail* to perform well.  This would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.
* **Further Exploration of Variability:** The discussion of balancing perceptual similarity and variability is excellent.  Could the authors briefly explore some simple methods to increase variability in the generated samples (e.g., adding noise, adjusting the optimization stopping criterion) and show the resulting textures?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Optimization Tuning:** Could you provide more specifics on the optimization procedure and the differences in tuning compared to the original Gatys et al. paper? Were any modifications made to the L-BFGS-B algorithm itself, or just to hyperparameters?
* **Scale Combination:** In the multi-scale model, how was the combination of different scales achieved? Was it a simple concatenation of feature maps, or was there a more sophisticated combination strategy?
* **Generalizability:**  Do you observe similar results when using different datasets for pre-training the K-means filters (e.g., other subsets of ImageNet, or a different dataset altogether)?
* **Limitations of VGG-loss:** You acknowledge that the VGG-based loss isn't perfect. What specific types of textures does it struggle to evaluate accurately? Could you propose alternative objective metrics for evaluating texture synthesis quality?
* **Code Availability:** Will you be releasing the code for this work to the public?



In conclusion, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field of texture synthesis. Its counter-intuitive results, thorough evaluation, and insightful discussion make it a strong candidate for acceptance. Addressing the minor feedback points above will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
