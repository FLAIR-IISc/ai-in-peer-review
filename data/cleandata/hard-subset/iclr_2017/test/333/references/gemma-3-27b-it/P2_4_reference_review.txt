## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results, often rivaling state-of-the-art approaches like Gatys et al. (2015a). They systematically explore different single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) with varying filter sizes and numbers, and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model’s synthesis loss. The work argues that the key to their success lies in refined optimization procedures and proposes a generalization of maximum entropy models to account for the inherent variability of textures.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and counterintuitive result demonstrating the surprising effectiveness of a very simple texture synthesis method. The experiments are thorough, the analysis is insightful, and the introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric is a valuable contribution. While the theoretical discussion regarding MaxEnt models feels slightly underdeveloped, the core empirical findings are strong.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The finding that random single-layer CNNs can produce high-quality textures challenges the commonly held belief that depth and pre-training are critical for this task. This is a significant contribution, potentially simplifying approaches to texture generation and reducing computational costs.
* **Thoroughness of Experiments:** The authors explore a wide range of single-layer architectures, filter types, and sizes. The comparison against the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. method is comprehensive and well-presented.  The ablation studies (e.g., multi-scale vs. single-scale) provide valuable insights into the importance of different components.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:**  The adoption of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a welcome improvement over relying solely on subjective human evaluation. This allows for more objective comparisons and facilitates reproducibility.  The acknowledgement of its limitations is also appreciated.
* **Clear Writing and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results. The figures are informative and help to illustrate the key findings.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Depth:** While the discussion of MaxEnt models is interesting, it feels somewhat disconnected from the empirical results. Expanding on how the authors' findings relate to the theoretical framework, specifically addressing the implications of finite size effects, would strengthen the paper.  Perhaps clarifying how their “soft constraints” approach relates to the theoretical limitations of MaxEnt in finite image spaces.
* **Optimization Details:** The paper mentions a scaling factor (10^7) applied to the loss and gradients during optimization. Providing more justification for this choice and exploring its sensitivity would be helpful. Was this value tuned?  How does it affect the optimization landscape?
* **Visual Examples:** While the figures are good, including a larger set of visual examples, potentially in an appendix, would further demonstrate the versatility and limitations of the proposed method.  Showing failures could be as insightful as successes.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost associated with the different architectures, particularly in comparison to the deeper VGG-based model, would be valuable.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The scaling factor of 10^7 applied to the loss and gradients during optimization is crucial for performance. Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind this choice and the results of any sensitivity analysis you performed?
2.  You mention that textures synthesized with the multi-scale model sometimes have lower VGG-based loss than those generated by the Gatys et al. model, yet appear perceptually superior. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why this occurs, perhaps linking it to the limitations of the VGG-based loss function in capturing perceptual similarity?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the initialization of the random filters? Did you experiment with different initialization schemes and did they significantly affect the quality of the synthesized textures?
4.  Regarding the MaxEnt framework, could you elaborate on the implications of allowing a “small mismatch” in the constraints for the synthesis process and how this connects to the observed variability in the generated textures?
