## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the necessary components for natural texture synthesis using deep convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are crucial, the authors demonstrate that surprisingly high-quality textures can be generated using a single-layer CNN with *random* filters. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random filters) to the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. (2015a) approach, introducing a novel quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss. They conclude that depth and pre-training are less vital than previously thought, and that careful optimization is key to achieving good results even with simple models. The paper also touches upon the theoretical connection to maximum entropy models and the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability in texture synthesis.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a counter-intuitive and significant finding – that strong texture synthesis performance can be achieved with surprisingly simple models. The experiments are thorough, the analysis is insightful, and the introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric is valuable. This work challenges assumptions in the field and opens new avenues for research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Impact:** The core finding – that random single-layer CNNs can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis – is a significant departure from existing approaches. This challenges the dominant paradigm and suggests that the complexity of deep networks may not be essential for this task.
* **Thoroughness of Experiments:** The authors systematically explore a wide range of single-layer architectures, including different filter types (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random) and sizes. This comprehensive evaluation strengthens their conclusions.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:** Replacing subjective human evaluation with a VGG-based loss function is a positive step towards more objective assessment of texture quality. The correlation between this metric and perceptual quality appears reasonable.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** The connection to maximum entropy models and the discussion of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability add depth and context to the empirical results.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and results. Figures are helpful in illustrating the generated textures and the comparison between different models.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Optimization Details:** While the authors mention scaling the loss and gradients during optimization, more detailed information about the optimization process (e.g., learning rate schedule, L-BFGS-B parameter tuning) would be helpful for reproducibility.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the different models, particularly the multi-scale model with large filters, would be valuable.
* **Ablation Studies:** More focused ablation studies investigating the contribution of individual components (e.g., ReLU non-linearity, filter size) within the multi-scale model would strengthen the analysis.
* **Visual Comparison:** While the figures are good, a more direct visual comparison (side-by-side) of the synthesized textures and the original textures would further highlight the quality of the results.
* **Statistical Significance:** While qualitative results are shown, it would be better to include some statistical significance testing of the quantitative evaluations to support the claims that the models are comparable.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Optimization Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the scaling factor applied to the loss and gradients during optimization? Did you explore a range of values, and if so, what were the effects?
* **Filter Initialization:** For the random filters, did you experiment with different initialization schemes (e.g., different distributions)? Could the specific uniform distribution used have an impact on the results?
* **Multi-Scale Filter Selection:** How were the different filter scales chosen for the multi-scale model? Was this determined empirically, or based on some theoretical considerations?
* **VGG-Loss Limitations:** You acknowledge that the VGG-based loss is not perfect. Could you provide examples where the VGG-loss fails to accurately reflect perceptual quality? Are there other metrics you considered and rejected, and why?
* **Generalization to other textures:** Have you tested the models on a broader range of textures beyond those shown in the paper?  Are there specific types of textures where the approach performs less well?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of texture synthesis. The findings are intriguing and potentially impactful, and the authors have conducted a thorough investigation. Addressing the points raised in the additional feedback would further strengthen the paper. I recommend acceptance for publication at ICLR.
