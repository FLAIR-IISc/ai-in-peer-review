This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors argue that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve results comparable to state-of-the-art methods relying on deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG. They present a variety of single-layer architectures, comparing their performance using a VGG-based loss function as a proxy for perceptual quality, and propose a generalization of maximum entropy models to account for finite texture extent.

Reject. While the exploration of simplified models for texture synthesis is a worthwhile endeavor, the paper presents a somewhat incremental contribution without sufficiently robust validation. The improvements over existing methods are not convincingly demonstrated, and the reliance on the VGG-based loss as a primary evaluation metric raises concerns about the true perceptual quality of the generated textures.

The core argument – that depth and pre-training are not *imperative* – is likely true to some extent. However, the evidence presented doesn't strongly support the claim that the proposed single-layer models *rival* state-of-the-art performance. The visual comparisons in Figure 1 and 4 appear subjective, and the quantitative results (VGG loss) are insufficient to establish a clear advantage. The claim of surpassing parametric methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) feels weak given the advancements in the field since that work. 

Several aspects of the methodology are also concerning. The extensive hyperparameter search and tuning of the optimization procedure, mentioned as a reason for differing results from Gatys et al. (2015a), isn't fully detailed. Without a clear understanding of these optimization choices, it's difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are due to the model architecture itself or simply better optimization. Furthermore, the paper largely *assumes* the validity of the VGG-based loss as a proxy for human perception. While the authors acknowledge its limitations, they rely heavily on it for evaluation.  The exploration of different filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) is interesting, but the rationale for choosing specific parameter settings (e.g., the number of feature maps – 363 vs 3267) isn't fully explained. The introduction of the multi-scale model is a positive step, but its computational inefficiency is acknowledged without a thorough discussion of potential trade-offs or mitigation strategies. The theoretical discussion on generalizing maximum entropy models feels disconnected from the empirical results and adds limited value to the core contribution.

Here are suggestions for improvement:

*   **More rigorous evaluation:** Conduct a proper user study to assess the perceptual quality of the generated textures. Compare the single-layer models directly against the VGG-based model in a blind test.
*   **Detailed optimization procedure:** Provide a complete description of the optimization parameters and tuning strategies used for all models. Perhaps a sensitivity analysis of these parameters.
*   **Justification for architectural choices:** Explain the rationale behind choosing specific filter sizes and numbers of feature maps.
*   **Computational complexity analysis:** Quantify the computational cost of the multi-scale model and discuss potential optimizations.
*   **Clarify the connection to MaxEnt:** Explain how the theoretical discussion on MaxEnt informs the design and interpretation of the empirical results.
*   **Address the limitations of VGG loss:** Acknowledge that VGG loss is not a perfect metric, and attempt to provide a more nuanced evaluation of the generated textures.

I have several questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide the exact optimization parameters used for each model? (Learning rate, number of iterations, L-BFGS-B parameters, scaling factor).
*   How sensitive are the results to the scaling factor of 10^7 used in the optimization?
*   What were the results of the ablation study regarding the ReLU non-linearity?
*   What is the computational time required to synthesize a texture using the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based model?
*   Beyond the VGG-based loss, what other quantitative metrics were considered, and why were they ultimately discarded?
