## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a *single-layer* CNN with *random* filters. They present a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures, comparing them to the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. (2015a) method. They also introduce a VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality, replacing the traditional reliance on human inspection. The key contribution is establishing a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis and questioning the necessity of depth and pre-training for this specific task.  The authors also discuss theoretical underpinnings relating to maximum entropy models and the limitations of current approaches to defining texture similarity and variability.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and counter-intuitive result – that surprisingly simple networks can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods. The thorough empirical evaluation, the introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric, and the theoretical discussion make this a significant contribution. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The visual comparison of synthesized textures (Figures 1 & 4) convincingly demonstrates the quality achievable with the proposed single-layer models, particularly the multi-scale version. The fact that random filters can perform so well is genuinely surprising and worthy of publication.  The ablation studies (Figure 2) provide insights into the importance of multi-scale filtering.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:** Replacing subjective human evaluation with the VGG-based loss is a valuable step towards a more objective assessment of texture synthesis quality. This provides a basis for comparing different methods and tracking progress. While acknowledging the limitations of this metric, the authors thoughtfully discuss its shortcomings.
* **Well-Placed in the Literature:** The paper clearly positions itself within the existing body of work on texture synthesis and generative modeling, accurately citing relevant research.  The critique of prior work (e.g., the perceived failure of random networks) is well-articulated and convincingly addressed.
* **Theoretical Insight:** The discussion connecting the work to maximum entropy models and the limitations of existing constraints on texture synthesis demonstrates a strong understanding of the underlying principles.  The analysis of perceptual similarity and variability is insightful and suggests directions for future research.
* **Reproducibility:** The paper provides sufficient detail on the network architectures, optimization procedure, and evaluation metrics to allow for reproducibility.  The use of publicly available datasets (ImageNet) and frameworks (Lasagne) further enhances reproducibility.



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify Optimization Sensitivity:** The authors mention more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure as a potential reason for discrepancies with earlier work. It would be beneficial to provide more details on these tuning parameters (learning rate, regularization, etc.) and perhaps a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate their impact.
* **Further analysis of the VGG loss:** While a good step, the VGG loss isn’t perfect. A visual demonstration of cases where it *fails* to capture perceptual quality would strengthen the paper.  For example, showing textures that have low VGG loss but are still perceptually distinct would highlight the metric’s limitations and open avenues for future research.
* **Computational Cost:** The multi-scale model’s computational inefficiency is noted. A brief discussion of potential strategies for mitigating this (e.g., filter pruning, sparse convolutions) could add value.
* **Expand Theoretical Discussion:** The discussion around the MaxEnt framework is excellent but could be expanded.  Specifically, exploring how the proposed approach relates to the “ad hockeries” identified by Jaynes would be a valuable addition.
* **Formatting:** While minor, the formatting in some sections (e.g., network descriptions) could be improved for clarity.  More consistent use of bullet points or tables could enhance readability.


**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Optimization Details:** Could you please provide a more detailed description of the optimization procedure, including the specific values used for the L-BFGS-B parameters (beyond just noting default values) and any techniques used to avoid local minima?
* **VGG Loss Limitations:** Can you share a specific example (perhaps visually) of a texture synthesized with low VGG loss but that is perceptually dissimilar to the reference texture?
* **Scalability:** Have you explored the scalability of this approach to larger textures or more complex texture datasets? Are there any limitations that emerge as the texture resolution increases?
* **Connection to Style Transfer:** Given that the Gatys et al. work originated from style transfer, how do you see the findings of this paper impacting style transfer applications? Could the single-layer approach be adapted for style transfer as well?




Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper that challenges a prevailing assumption in the field of texture synthesis. The strong empirical results, coupled with the thoughtful theoretical discussion, make it a valuable contribution to the ICLR community. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
