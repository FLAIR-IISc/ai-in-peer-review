This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results compared to state-of-the-art methods based on deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG. They explore various filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) and architectures, proposing a multi-scale random filter network that often rivals the VGG-based approach. A novel quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model’s synthesis loss is also introduced as an alternative to subjective human inspection.

**Reject.** While the core idea of demonstrating the effectiveness of a minimal baseline for texture synthesis is intriguing, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigor in its experimental evaluation that cumulatively detract from its overall impact and credibility. The issues range from imprecise language and missing details to questionable choices in experimental setup and a superficial discussion of the results.

The main issue lies in the lack of control and clarity in the experimental setup. For example, the “tuning of the optimization procedure” mentioned as a key difference from earlier work is described vaguely. The scaling factor of 10<sup>7</sup> applied to the loss and gradients feels arbitrary and lacks justification. Why was this value chosen? What sensitivity analysis was performed to determine its robustness? The statement that “all parameters of the optimization algorithm...except for the maximum number of iterations…were left at their default value” is similarly unhelpful. What *are* the default values? Different L-BFGS-B implementations have varying defaults. 

Furthermore, the comparison to the VGG-based model is not entirely fair. While the authors synthesize textures from VGG using the same optimization procedure, the pre-trained weights of VGG inherently provide a significant advantage. The authors acknowledge this but don’t thoroughly explore its implications. The claim that the multi-scale model often rivals VGG is also somewhat overstated. While the visual comparisons in Fig. 4 are suggestive, they are subjective and lack quantitative backing beyond the VGG-based loss, which the authors themselves concede is not a perfect metric. The VGG loss being *lower* for the multi-scale model on certain textures, while framed as paradoxical, isn't explored rigorously enough. Is this a genuine limitation of the VGG loss, or an artifact of the specific textures chosen or optimization parameters?

Several minor details also raise concerns. The use of “1e7” for 10<sup>7</sup> is unconventional and unprofessional. The description of the Fourier filters is unnecessarily complex. Why the weighting terms wR, wG, wB in Fourier-3267? What is the motivation for these specific values? The terminology is also inconsistent; sometimes filters are described as “randomly sampled,” other times as “drawn from a uniform distribution.” These are not synonymous. The appendix reference (Fig. 5) is dangling, and the discussion of the Minimax entropy principle feels tacked on and disconnected from the main results.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Optimization Details:** Provide a complete and precise description of the optimization procedure, including all hyperparameters used for each model, and justify the choices made.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Supplement the visual comparisons with more rigorous quantitative metrics, such as Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) or Kernel Inception Distance (KID), adapted for texture synthesis.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of specific design choices, such as the number of feature maps, filter size, and ReLU non-linearities.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical analysis to support claims of superiority. Are the differences in VGG loss statistically significant?
*   **Error Analysis:** Analyze the cases where the single-layer models perform poorly compared to VGG. What types of textures are particularly challenging?
*   **Clarity and Consistency:** Revise the manuscript to improve clarity, consistency, and precision in language and terminology.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed justification for the scaling factor used in the optimization procedure?
*   Have you performed a sensitivity analysis of the optimization hyperparameters?
*   Have you considered using alternative quantitative metrics to evaluate the quality of the synthesized textures?
*   What is the computational cost difference between the multi-scale model and a comparable deep hierarchical network?
*   Why are the weighting terms [1, 0, -1] chosen for the Fourier-3267 filters?
