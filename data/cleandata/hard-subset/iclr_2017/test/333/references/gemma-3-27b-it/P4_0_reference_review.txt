This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing notion that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential. The authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer CNN with random filters, rivaling the performance of state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). They perform an extensive comparison of different single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss.

**Reject.** While the paper presents interesting findings and a thorough investigation, the presented results, particularly the claim of rivaling state-of-the-art performance, are not convincingly demonstrated. The quantitative evaluation, while a step forward, appears heavily reliant on the VGG-based loss, which, as the authors acknowledge, isn't a perfect perceptual metric, and the subjective visual comparisons lack sufficient rigor.

The core argument – that depth and pre-training are not *imperative* – is reasonable. The paper effectively demonstrates a surprisingly strong baseline. However, the claim that the generated textures “often rival” state-of-the-art approaches is overstated. The visual examples in Figure 4, while showing competitive results in *some* cases, don’t consistently support this claim across the 20 presented textures. Some synthesized textures from the multi-scale model appear visibly different from the VGG-based synthesis, exhibiting artifacts or lacking the fine-grained detail. The authors’ admission that the VGG-based loss isn't perfect undermines the strength of their quantitative comparison. Specifically, the discussion in Section 7 notes the potential for the VGG loss to favor perceptual similarity over variability, raising questions about what the loss is actually measuring.

The exploration of different filter types and the introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric are valuable contributions. The ablation studies, investigating the importance of multi-scale filters, are well-conducted and provide clear insights. The discussion of the relationship to the MaxEnt framework is thoughtful and contextualizes the work within a broader theoretical landscape.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** While the VGG-based loss is a good starting point, exploring other quantitative metrics or conducting a formal human perceptual study would significantly strengthen the paper. The authors acknowledge limitations in the VGG loss; addressing these is crucial.
*   **Visual Comparison Rigor:** The visual comparisons should be more systematic. Consider using a paired comparison study (showing reference, VGG, and single-layer synthesis side-by-side) and collecting subjective ratings from multiple observers.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters, particularly the scaling factor in the optimization procedure (section 4), would be beneficial. How robust are the findings to changes in these parameters?
*   **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the single-layer vs. VGG synthesis would be helpful, given the authors' claim of efficiency.
*   **Clarification on "Rivaling":** The term "rivaling" is subjective. The authors should define more precisely what constitutes "rivaling" performance. Is it achieving a comparable VGG loss? Achieving indistinguishability in a perceptual study?

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the cases where the multi-scale model genuinely outperforms the VGG-based synthesis, beyond the qualitative examples in Figure 4? Specifically, are there particular texture characteristics that seem to favor the single-layer approach?
2.  You mention the potential for the VGG loss to prioritize similarity over variability. Could you elaborate on how you explored this tradeoff, and how it might influence your conclusions?
3.  How sensitive are your results to the scaling factor of 10<sup>7</sup> used during optimization? Did you explore a range of values, and what impact did that have on the quality of the synthesized textures?
4.  Could you provide more details on the computational resources (time, memory) required for synthesizing textures using both the single-layer and VGG-based models?
