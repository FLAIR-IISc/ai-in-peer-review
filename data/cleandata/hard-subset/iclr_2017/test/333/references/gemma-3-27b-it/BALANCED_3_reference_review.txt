This paper investigates the necessary components for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that depth and pre-training are crucial, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer network with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality results, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods. They present a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG features, replacing common human inspection.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting and counterintuitive result – that complex, pre-trained networks aren’t strictly *necessary* for good texture synthesis. However, the presented evidence, while extensive, doesn’t quite convincingly demonstrate the *sufficiency* of the proposed single-layer approach. The novelty is somewhat diminished by the fact that similar observations have been made previously (albeit perhaps not with the same thoroughness in architectural exploration), and the core contribution feels incremental rather than a paradigm shift. The writing is dense and at times difficult to follow, especially concerning the numerous network configurations.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Superiority:** While the results show that single-layer networks can *rival* state-of-the-art performance, they don’t consistently *outperform* them. The paper acknowledges this, but a stronger argument for the utility of the simpler models would require more compelling demonstrations of superiority, or at least a clearer articulation of the trade-offs (e.g., computational cost). The qualitative comparison in Fig. 1 appears to show the VGG model producing generally sharper, more detailed textures.

2. **Lack of Ablation on Optimization:** The authors highlight the importance of optimization procedure tuning. However, the paper lacks a rigorous ablation study specifically focused on the optimization parameters (L-BFGS-B settings) for *different* architectures. It's stated that the optimization was more elaborate than previous work, but the specific details of this elaboration and its impact on performance aren’t adequately presented. It is crucial to demonstrate that the success isn't solely due to hyperparameter tuning favoring the simpler model.

3. **VGG-Based Evaluation Concerns:** The adoption of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is commendable, but the authors themselves acknowledge its limitations, particularly for textures with regular structures. The inconsistency between VGG loss and perceptual quality, as noted in the discussion, raises concerns about the reliability of this metric for objectively comparing different approaches. While better than human inspection, it isn't a perfect substitute and its shortcomings need to be addressed more carefully in the interpretation of results.



**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Clarify the novelty:** Explicitly address prior work that hints at similar findings (networks with random weights can produce interesting images). Position the current work more precisely within the existing literature, highlighting the specific contribution beyond simply re-demonstrating this phenomenon.
*   **Simplify the presentation of network configurations:** The sheer number of network variants (Fourier, Kmeans, PCA, Random, with and without whitening, different filter sizes) is overwhelming. Consider a table summarizing the key parameters and performance metrics for each configuration.
*   **Provide a more detailed analysis of the multi-scale model:** The multi-scale model appears to be the most successful.  A deeper investigation into *why* it performs well—beyond simply stating that it combines different scales—would be valuable. For example, what are the specific features learned by the different filter sizes?
*   **Include more diverse texture examples:** The paper focuses on a relatively limited set of textures. Evaluating the approach on a wider variety of textures (e.g., biological textures, materials) would strengthen the generalization of the findings.
*   **Elaborate on the theoretical implications:** The paper touches upon the relationship to maximum entropy models and the notion of constraints. Expanding on these theoretical connections, and discussing how the findings inform our understanding of texture modeling, would add significant depth.
*   **Consider computational complexity:** A discussion of the computational costs associated with different architectures and optimization procedures would be useful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed description of the “more elaborate tuning” of the optimization procedure? What specific parameters were adjusted, and how did those adjustments impact the performance of different network architectures?
*   Have you experimented with other quantitative texture quality metrics besides the VGG-based loss? If so, how do those metrics correlate with perceptual quality and with each other?
*   Could you perform an ablation study specifically focused on the impact of different optimization parameters (e.g., learning rate, maximum iterations, box constraints) on the performance of both the single-layer and VGG-based models?
*   What are the limitations of the proposed approach? Are there specific types of textures for which it consistently fails to produce high-quality results?
*   The paper mentions the potential for a trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.  Have you explored methods for explicitly controlling this trade-off, such as adjusting the stopping criterion of the optimization algorithm?
