## Review

This paper investigates the requirements for generating natural textures, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods. They systematically explore various single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model, moving away from purely subjective human evaluation.

**Decision:** Accept. The work presents a counterintuitive but well-supported finding that challenges a common belief in the field. The experimental setup is comprehensive, and the introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric is a valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Novelty:** The core finding – that high-quality texture synthesis can be achieved with a single-layer random convolutional network – is a significant departure from prior work and potentially simplifies approaches to texture generation. While previous works have explored random networks, the detailed comparison with state-of-the-art methods and the analysis of filter properties (scale, type) provide novel insights.
2. **Technical Soundness:** The paper provides a clear description of the network architectures and the texture synthesis algorithm. The use of L-BFGS-B for optimization is appropriate, and the careful tuning of the optimization procedure (scaling loss and gradients) is highlighted as a key factor in achieving good results. The quantitative evaluation metric, while based on the VGG model, provides a more objective comparison than relying solely on human perception. The ablation studies (varying filter sizes, filter types) are well-designed and provide strong evidence to support the claims.
3. **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The concepts are explained clearly, and the experimental setup is thoroughly described. The use of figures and tables effectively illustrates the results. The connections to existing literature (parametric texture models, GANs, style transfer) are clearly established.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the authors acknowledge computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model, a more thorough discussion of the limitations of this approach would be beneficial. For example, the textures generated by the single-layer networks appear somewhat smoother than those generated by deeper networks. Investigating the reasons for this difference and exploring potential remedies could strengthen the work.
* **Generalization to other datasets:** The experiments primarily focus on a limited set of textures. Evaluating the approach on a wider range of textures and potentially other image domains (e.g., satellite imagery, medical images) could broaden the applicability of the findings.
* **Theoretical Connection to MaxEnt:** The discussion of the connection to maximum entropy models is insightful. Exploring this connection more formally, perhaps through a theoretical analysis of the entropy of the generated textures, could further strengthen the work.
* **Impact of Initialization:** The paper mentions the importance of optimization tuning. Investigating the influence of different random seeds and initialization strategies on the generated textures could reveal the robustness of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of random filters, yet also explores K-means and PCA based filters. What is the practical advantage (e.g., speed, memory) of using truly random filters versus using filters learned even in an unsupervised manner?
2. The loss function used for optimization is crucial. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the results to the scaling factor (10^7) applied to the loss and gradients? Was this value chosen empirically, and how would changing it affect the results?
3. The multi-scale model outperforms the single-scale models. Is there a principled way to determine the optimal scales to use in the multi-scale model, or is it primarily a matter of trial and error?
4. The paper mentions that deeper hierarchical networks do not improve texture quality but can improve efficiency. What specific architectures were explored, and what were the key challenges encountered when attempting to improve performance with deeper networks?
