This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results compared to the state-of-the-art, multi-layer VGG-based approach. They explore various single-layer architectures, including those with Fourier and K-means filters, and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model's synthesis loss. The core claim is that hierarchical representations and supervised training are not *imperative* for natural texture generation, challenging prior assumptions in the field.

**Reject.** While the findings are intriguing, the paper feels premature and lacks the rigorous validation expected for such a claim that fundamentally questions established practices. The improvement over prior work isn't substantial enough to warrant a paradigm shift, and the paper leans heavily on qualitative assessments despite attempting a quantitative metric.

The primary concern lies in the limited demonstrable advantage over the established Gatys et al. (2015a) method. The authors state their multi-scale model "often rivals" the state-of-the-art, but this is supported largely by visual comparisons (Fig. 4) which are subjective. The quantitative metric (VGG loss) also demonstrates mixed results, sometimes *favoring* the simpler model even when visual inspection suggests otherwise, hinting at limitations in its correlation with perceptual quality. The claim that the random multi-scale model outperforms the VGG model for "textures with extremely regular structures" is not convincingly demonstrated; the differences appear subtle and require more systematic evaluation.

Furthermore, the focus on a single-layer network feels somewhat contrived. While demonstrating a minimal baseline is valuable, the paper doesn’t adequately address *why* a single layer can perform reasonably well. Is it simply that textures are relatively low-complexity structures that don’t necessitate deep feature hierarchies? This isn't explored. The authors acknowledge the computational inefficiency of large filters and admit that hierarchical networks might offer practical advantages, undermining the central argument that depth isn't important. The optimization procedure (L-BFGS-B with scaling) is presented as a key factor enabling the success of the simpler models, but lacks a thorough comparative analysis against optimized implementations of the VGG-based method. It is unclear if the observed results are due to the inherent capabilities of the random filters or simply a more effective optimization strategy. 

The discussion section is overly ambitious in connecting the work to maximum entropy principles and perceptual distance metrics. These are interesting avenues for future research but feel tacked on and not fully integrated with the core experimental findings.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **More Rigorous Evaluation:** Implement a proper user study (e.g., a Turing test) to assess the perceptual quality of the generated textures. Relying solely on visual comparisons is insufficient.
*   **Benchmarking:** Conduct a comprehensive comparison with a wider range of texture synthesis methods, including more recent and sophisticated approaches.
*   **Ablation Study:** Perform a more detailed ablation study to isolate the contributions of different components of the multi-scale model (e.g., the number of filters, filter sizes, ReLU non-linearity).
*   **Optimization Analysis:** Provide a more thorough comparison of the optimization procedures used for the single-layer and multi-layer models. Investigate the sensitivity of the results to different optimization parameters.
*   **Computational Cost:** Report the computational time required to generate textures with the different models. This is particularly important given the inefficiency of the large filters.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Elaborate on the theoretical reasons why a single layer can be effective for texture synthesis. Relate the findings to the properties of natural textures and the representational capabilities of convolutional filters.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7. What is the rationale behind this specific scaling factor? How sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter?
2.  How does the performance of the multi-scale model compare to the VGG-based method when both are optimized using the same optimization algorithm and parameters?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with generating textures using the multi-scale model, especially compared to the VGG-based approach?
4.  While you present the VGG loss as a quantitative measure, its correlation with perceptual quality appears imperfect. What are the limitations of this metric, and what alternative evaluation metrics could be considered?
5.  You claim the multi-scale model rivals the current state-of-the-art. Could you quantify this claim with a statistical comparison, such as a paired t-test or ANOVA, based on human evaluation scores or a more robust quantitative metric?
