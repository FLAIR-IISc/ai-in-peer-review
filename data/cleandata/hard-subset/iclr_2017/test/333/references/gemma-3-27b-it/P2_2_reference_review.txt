## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the necessary components for high-quality natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can generate textures rivalling state-of-the-art results achieved with deeper, pre-trained networks (like those based on Gatys et al., 2015a).  They systematically explore various single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, K-means filters, PCA filters, and random filters) and propose a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model.  The work further argues that, for finite-sized images, striving for *exact* reproduction of statistics (as in MaxEnt models) may be less important than allowing for some variability.  The core contributions are a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis, a demonstration of the surprisingly good performance of random filters, a comprehensive comparison of different single-layer architectures, and a novel evaluation metric.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling challenge to established assumptions in texture synthesis and provides a surprisingly effective, minimal baseline. The results are well-supported by both qualitative and quantitative evaluations, and the discussion of the limitations of the MaxEnt framework is insightful.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Impact:** The finding that random filters in a single-layer network can perform competitively with state-of-the-art methods is significant. This challenges the common assumption that deep, pre-trained representations are *necessary* for good texture synthesis and opens up interesting avenues for more efficient texture generation.
* **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors perform a thorough empirical comparison of various single-layer architectures, including different filter types and sizes. The inclusion of a quantitative evaluation metric (based on VGG loss) is a major strength, moving beyond purely subjective human evaluation. The results in Figures 1, 4, and 6 convincingly demonstrate the performance of the proposed methods.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates its motivation by questioning the assumed necessity of depth and pre-training in texture synthesis. It positions itself well within the existing literature on texture modeling and generative models.
* **Sound Methodology:** The methodology is clearly explained, and the implementation details are provided (e.g., optimization parameters, network architectures). The use of L-BFGS-B for optimization and the scaling of the loss function demonstrate a careful attention to detail.  
* **Insightful Discussion:** The discussion section raises important theoretical considerations regarding the interplay between statistical similarity and perceptual variability, and the limitations of the MaxEnt framework for finite-sized images.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify Multi-Scale Implementation:** The description of the multi-scale model could be more detailed. Specifically, how are the filters from different scales combined? Is it a simple concatenation of feature maps, or is there a more sophisticated integration mechanism?
* **Ablation Study on Optimization:** While the authors mention tuning the optimization procedure, a more formal ablation study exploring the impact of different optimization parameters (learning rate, number of iterations, etc.) would further strengthen the paper.
* **Visual Comparison with Alternatives:** Including a side-by-side visual comparison with textures generated by other classic texture synthesis methods (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, 2000) alongside the VGG-based method would provide a broader context for evaluating the performance of the proposed methods.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational cost of the different methods would be valuable, given the emphasis on simplicity and efficiency.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the multi-scale model:** Could you provide a more detailed explanation of how the filters from different scales are integrated during the synthesis process?
* **Regarding optimization:**  You mention scaling the loss function. What was the rationale behind the specific scaling factor (10^7)? Was this determined empirically, and if so, how?
* **Regarding the quantitative metric:**  While the VGG-based loss is a useful metric, you acknowledge its limitations.  Do you have any ideas for developing more robust perceptual metrics specifically tailored for texture synthesis?
* **Regarding variability:** You mention that completely reconstructing the reference texture can happen. Is there any evidence of this occurring during your experiments, and how did you address it? Could you elaborate on the potential benefits of deliberately introducing some level of randomness or noise during the synthesis process to promote variability?



Overall, this is a well-written and thought-provoking paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of texture synthesis. The clear presentation, strong empirical results, and insightful discussion make it a strong candidate for acceptance.  Addressing the minor feedback points would further enhance the quality of the paper.
