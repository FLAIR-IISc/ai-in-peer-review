## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can generate textures rivaling state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a). They systematically compare various single-layer architectures – Fourier basis, k-means learned filters, PCA filters, and purely random filters – and identify multi-scale filters as particularly effective.  The work introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model, replacing traditional human inspection, and discusses the theoretical connection to maximum entropy models. The core contribution is a surprisingly strong baseline for texture synthesis that questions the necessity of depth and pre-training.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and counterintuitive result – that surprisingly simple models can achieve high-quality texture synthesis. The experimental setup is thorough, the quantitative evaluation is a welcome addition, and the discussion connects the findings to broader theoretical concepts.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Impact:** The finding that random filters in a single-layer network can rival more complex approaches is significant. It shifts the understanding of what’s necessary for texture generation and suggests that the hierarchical representations learned during pre-training might be less crucial than previously thought, at least for this specific task.
* **Thorough Evaluation:**  The authors perform a comprehensive comparison of numerous single-layer architectures, controlling for factors like filter size and learning method. This provides a robust basis for their claims.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:** The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric (VGG-based loss) is a significant strength. While acknowledged as imperfect, it offers a more objective means of comparison than subjective human evaluation, common in the field.  The supplemental material showing comparisons to SSIM further strengthens this aspect.
* **Strong Baseline:** The established baseline provides a valuable point of comparison for future work in texture synthesis.
* **Clear Writing and Structure:** The paper is generally well-written and logically structured, making it easy to follow the authors’ line of reasoning.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Discussion of Optimization:** The authors mention a more “elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” as a reason for their success with random filters, differing from Gatys et al. (2015a).  More detail on these tuning parameters (learning rate schedules, regularization, etc.) and their impact would be valuable. A sensitivity analysis could be performed.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the inefficiency of the multi-scale filters, a more quantitative comparison of the computational cost (training and synthesis time) between the single-layer models and the VGG-based approach would be helpful.
* **Visual Examples:**  While Figure 1 and 4 offer good comparisons, adding a side-by-side comparison of the *optimization process* (e.g., showing intermediate images during the L-BFGS-B iterations) for both a random filter network and the VGG network could visually illustrate the difference in optimization landscape.
* **Limitations of VGG-Loss:** The authors rightly acknowledge the limitations of the VGG-based loss. Expanding on these limitations and discussing potential alternative metrics for evaluating perceptual quality would be beneficial.
* **Connection to MaxEnt:** The discussion on the MaxEnt framework feels a bit disconnected from the empirical results.  Explicitly linking the practical implications of allowing “mismatch in the constraints” to the observed behavior of the models would strengthen this section.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the “elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” that distinguishes your work from Gatys et al. (2015a)? Specifically, what optimization parameters were adjusted, and what impact did those adjustments have on the quality of the synthesized textures?
2.  What is the computational cost (wall-clock time) for synthesizing a single texture (e.g., 256x256 pixels) using the multi-scale random filter model versus the VGG-based model?
3.  You mention that the VGG-based loss isn’t perfect. What alternative perceptual metrics have you considered, and why did you ultimately choose the VGG-based loss for your quantitative evaluation?
4.  Can you provide more intuition as to *why* random filters work surprisingly well? Is it simply a matter of having a large number of filters to capture sufficient variations, or is there something inherent in the randomness that contributes to the success?
5.  In the discussion regarding the MaxEnt framework, you mention a potential link between allowing mismatch in constraints and the observed behavior. Could you provide a concrete example of how this "mismatch" manifests in the generated textures?
