This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly high-quality texture synthesis, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods employing deep, pre-trained networks. The work includes a comprehensive comparison of different single-layer architectures and introduces a VGG-based loss function for quantitative texture quality assessment.

**Reject**

The core findings of the paper – that a single-layer network with random filters can produce compelling textures – are interesting and counterintuitive. However, the presentation lacks sufficient rigor and clarity to confidently support the claims made. The experimental setup, while extensive, feels somewhat ad-hoc, and the interpretation of the results is not always well-justified. 

The paper’s primary weakness lies in the subjective assessment intertwined with the quantitative evaluation. While the introduction of the VGG-based loss is a step towards objective measurement, the authors heavily rely on visual comparisons and statements like "rivaling state-of-the-art approaches." The visual examples are useful, but lack a systematic, statistically-grounded comparison to truly substantiate these claims. The paper also doesn't adequately address the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters in the optimization process (L-BFGS-B), beyond mentioning scaling the loss and gradients. The choice of these values seems critical given the reported success, and justification for them is missing.

Furthermore, the discussion section, while attempting to place the work in a broader theoretical context (MaxEnt framework), becomes overly speculative and introduces concepts without fully developing them. The discussion of the limitations of the VGG-loss and the need for better perceptual metrics feels disconnected from the main experimental results.  The mathematical formulation of the texture synthesis as an ergodic random field is presented, but the practical implications of this formalization are not fully explored. The reference to a “finite size effect” as a potential reason for exact reconstruction feels like post-hoc rationalization.

Finally, several aspects of the methodology lack sufficient detail. For instance, the exact parameters used for the k-means clustering (number of iterations, initialization method) are not provided, which impacts reproducibility. The selection of the 20 textures used for comparison in Figure 4 is not justified, raising concerns about potential bias.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Replace the subjective "rivaling" statements with statistically significant quantitative comparisons. Conduct user studies or employ more robust image quality metrics alongside the VGG-based loss. A statistically powered comparison with the Gatys et al. method is vital.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Perform an ablation study to analyze the sensitivity of the results to the scaling factor in the optimization process. Demonstrate that the observed performance is not solely dependent on this specific parameter setting.
*   **Statistical Rigor:** Provide details on the statistical significance of any observed differences in performance between the different single-layer architectures.
*   **Clarity of Methodology:** Provide complete details regarding the experimental setup, including the number of k-means iterations, initialization method, and the selection criteria for the textures used for evaluation.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** Focus the theoretical discussion on the aspects directly supported by the experimental results. Avoid overly speculative arguments.
*   **Ablation Study**: How much does each component of the multi-scale model contribute to the overall results? E.g. what if only 3x3 and 11x11 filters were used?
* **Optimization details:** Why L-BFGS-B? Were other optimizers tried? What are the implications of using a second-order optimizer?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the justification for the scaling factor of 10^7 applied to the loss and gradients during optimization? Has a sensitivity analysis been performed on this value?
2.  How were the 20 textures used for comparison in Figure 4 selected? What criteria were used to ensure a representative sample?
3.  Can you provide statistical results supporting the claim that the single-layer models “rival” the performance of the Gatys et al. model, beyond visual inspection?
4. What is the computational cost of the multi-scale model in comparison to the VGG-based model?
5.  Were any attempts made to train the filters in the single-layer networks using a different learning paradigm (e.g., adversarial training)? If so, what were the results?
