This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results, often rivaling the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach. They systematically explore different filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and sizes, emphasizing the importance of multi-scale analysis. They also introduce a VGG-based loss function as a quantitative metric for texture quality, replacing subjective human evaluation.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting and counterintuitive finding – that surprisingly simple networks can perform well in texture synthesis – it feels somewhat exploratory and lacks the rigor expected for publication. The claims are strong (challenging established understanding), but the supporting evidence, while plentiful, doesn’t fully convince. The paper feels like a thorough investigation of *what* works, but less focused on *why*.

The core argument – that depth and pre-training aren’t essential – is compelling. The extensive ablation study with different filter types and scales is a strength. The results showing random filters performing on par with learned filters *is* noteworthy and challenges conventional wisdom. However, the justification for why this happens is relatively shallow. The authors posit that it relates to optimization landscape robustness, but this isn’t rigorously demonstrated. Furthermore, the gains over the VGG baseline, while sometimes present, aren't consistently significant and are heavily reliant on the chosen quantitative evaluation metric. The reliance on the VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is acknowledged as imperfect, and the paper’s own results (Fig. 4) show cases where the VGG loss doesn’t align with visual perception. This undermines the quantitative support for their claim of rivalling the state-of-the-art. The discussion section also feels somewhat unfocused, drifting into broader theoretical considerations without fully connecting them to the presented empirical work.

Here are additional suggestions for improvement:

*   **Deeper Dive into the Optimization:** The authors mention the importance of optimization tuning. A more detailed analysis of the optimization landscape – perhaps visualizing gradients or loss surfaces for both single-layer and deep networks – would strengthen the argument about robustness.
*   **Human Evaluation:** While the authors rightfully critique subjective human evaluation, *some* controlled human studies comparing the textures generated by different models would greatly bolster the claims. Even a simple A/B test could provide valuable insights.
*   **Theoretical Framework:** The paper touches upon the Maximum Entropy principle but doesn’t fully integrate it. A more formal connection to this theoretical framework would provide a stronger grounding for the findings.
*   **Filter Size Analysis:** While the multi-scale model performs best, the analysis of individual scale filters (Fig. 2) is limited. A more thorough exploration of the information captured at different scales, perhaps using activation visualization techniques, would be helpful.
* **Clarify the role of ReLUs:** The paper briefly mentions that removing ReLUs from the multi-scale model destroys performance. More detail on why this is the case is needed. Is it simply a matter of representational capacity, or is there something more fundamental at play?

Questions for the authors:

*   The paper argues that the VGG-based loss is an acceptable proxy for perceptual similarity, yet acknowledges its limitations. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of cases where the VGG loss *disagrees* with visual perception? What properties of the textures cause this discrepancy?
*   You mention the optimization landscape being more robust for single-layer models. Can you quantify this robustness in any way? (e.g., by measuring the number of local minima, or the sensitivity of the solution to initialization)
*   Could you elaborate on why random filters perform surprisingly well? Is it simply a consequence of having a large number of filters, or is there something special about the properties of random convolutions in this context?
* The discussion section mentions a trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. How did you attempt to balance these factors during the texture synthesis process, and what were the results?
