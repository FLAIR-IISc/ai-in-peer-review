This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential. The authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer CNN with random filters, often rivaling state-of-the-art results from deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG. They present a comprehensive comparison of different single-layer architectures and introduce a VGG-based loss function as a quantitative metric for texture quality, moving away from purely subjective human evaluation. The work also touches upon a more formal generalization of maximum entropy models for texture variability.

**Reject.** While the findings are interesting and challenge conventional wisdom, the paper feels exploratory and lacks a compelling, fully-developed argument. The novelty lies in demonstrating that deep networks *aren’t* necessary, but the paper doesn’t deeply explore *why* this is the case beyond stating the optimization procedure matters. The quantitative evaluation, while a step forward, feels somewhat circular (using VGG loss to evaluate textures generated to *look* like those understood by VGG).

The central claim—that depth and pre-training are not indispensable—is supported by the presented results, but the support isn’t overwhelming. While the multi-scale random network performs well, the comparisons to Gatys et al. aren’t always convincing, often relying on visual inspection alongside the VGG loss. The statement that the multi-scale model “often rivals” SOTA is a bit weak, and the examples provided don’t consistently demonstrate superiority.  The extensive ablation study of different single-layer filters is valuable, but the detailed analysis feels somewhat disconnected from a larger theoretical framework. The discussion section on MaxEnt and variability is intriguing, but underdeveloped; it feels like a direction for future work rather than an integral part of the current contribution. The computational efficiency arguments regarding hierarchical networks feel briefly addressed, and the conclusion that they aren’t *necessary* doesn’t necessarily mean they aren’t *beneficial* in other ways.

Here's additional feedback for improvement:

*   **Deeper Dive into Optimization:** The authors mention the importance of optimization procedure tuning.  A more detailed analysis of *how* the optimization differs and why it affects the results of the random networks would significantly strengthen the argument.
*   **Clarify the Role of Scale:** The multi-scale approach is a key finding. Elaborate on the specific benefits of combining different filter scales beyond simply increasing the receptive field.  How do different scales capture different aspects of texture?
*   **More Rigorous Quantitative Evaluation:** While the VGG loss is a good start, consider exploring other quantitative metrics beyond those directly tied to VGG’s feature space. A user study, even with a small number of participants, would provide more compelling evidence of perceptual quality.
*   **Connect to Existing Literature on Random Features:** There's a body of work on the effectiveness of random features (e.g., Random Kitchen Sinks) in various machine learning tasks. Positioning this work within that context would be valuable.
*   **Expand on the MaxEnt Discussion:**  The discussion of MaxEnt and the limitations of its application to finite-size images is thought-provoking.  Developing these ideas further, even as future work, would add depth to the paper.



I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed comparison of the optimization parameters used for the single-layer networks versus the VGG-based synthesis, specifically focusing on the learning rate schedule and the importance of the scaling factor of 10^7?
2.  You mention that the VGG loss may not perfectly capture perceptual quality, especially for regular structures.  Do you have any preliminary results on alternative metrics or user studies that might support this claim?
3.  Why do the random filters perform so well? Is there a specific property of the random distribution used that makes it well-suited for texture synthesis, or is it simply the large number of filters?
4.  The discussion touches on variability. Would incorporating a diversity-promoting term into the loss function (e.g., encouraging differences between independently generated samples) improve the results?
