This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN, even with random filters, can generate textures rivaling the quality of state-of-the-art methods relying on deeper, pre-trained networks like VGG. They explore various single-layer architectures, including Fourier basis filters, K-means learned filters, and random filters, and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model's synthesis loss. The core claim is that the hierarchical representation and supervised training previously considered crucial for texture generation are, in fact, dispensable.

**Reject.** While the exploration of minimal models for texture synthesis is a worthwhile endeavor, the paper's claims of challenging established paradigms are not convincingly supported. The results, while interesting, are presented with a lack of rigorous comparison and sufficient justification for the observed improvements. The presented evidence relies heavily on visual comparisons, and the quantitative metric, while a step forward, still hinges on the VGG model’s perceptual accuracy – effectively shifting the problem rather than solving it.

The primary concern lies with the insufficient benchmarking. The paper compares against Gatys et al. (2015a), but doesn’t thoroughly investigate *why* the proposed methods achieve comparable results. The authors attribute it to improved optimization, but the details of this “more elaborate tuning” are somewhat vague and lack detailed ablation studies. Simply stating the optimization was tuned without providing specific details (learning rate, regularization, etc.) or comparing against the original Gatys et al. implementation with identical tuning undermines the claim. The comparison to Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) feels dated and doesn't represent a meaningful benchmark given the rapid advancements in the field. 

Furthermore, the assertion that random filters perform “on par or better” than learned filters needs stronger evidence. While visually compelling, it’s crucial to statistically validate these observations. Are the improvements significant? Are they consistent across a diverse range of textures? The authors mention variability being a key factor, yet the evaluation doesn’t explicitly measure or control for this. The multi-scale model’s performance is also presented without a comprehensive analysis of the optimal scale combinations and their impact on different texture types.

The discussion section delves into theoretical considerations about maximum entropy models and perceptual metrics, but these discussions feel somewhat disconnected from the core empirical findings. The lack of a clear link between the theoretical framework and the observed results weakens the paper's overall impact. The conclusion that deep hierarchical representations are “not necessary” is a strong statement that requires much more definitive evidence than presented here.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies on the optimization procedure. Provide specific hyperparameters and compare performance against the original Gatys et al. implementation under identical settings.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform quantitative analysis (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the observed improvements in texture quality.
*   **Diversity Metrics:** Incorporate metrics to explicitly measure the diversity of generated textures.
*   **Broader Benchmarking:** Compare against more recent state-of-the-art texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al. (2015a).
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a detailed error analysis to identify the types of textures where the proposed methods struggle and why.
*   **Visualizations:** While the provided images are helpful, higher-resolution images and a more systematic comparison would be beneficial.
*   **Clarify Optimization:** Detail the "elaborate tuning" of the optimization procedure beyond simply stating it was performed. What specifically was changed, and why?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the optimization parameters used in your experiments, and how they differ from those used in the original Gatys et al. implementation?
*   Have you performed statistical tests to confirm the significance of the observed improvements in texture quality? If so, please provide the results.
*   What metrics did you consider for evaluating the diversity of the generated textures, and why did you ultimately choose the VGG-based loss as your primary evaluation metric?
*   Could you elaborate on the theoretical implications of your findings regarding the role of depth and pre-training in texture synthesis? How do these findings relate to existing theories of perceptual learning and representation?
