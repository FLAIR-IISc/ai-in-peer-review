## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in deep convolutional networks for natural texture synthesis. Challenging the prevailing belief that hierarchical, pre-trained networks (like VGG) are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can achieve surprisingly competitive texture synthesis results, often rivaling the state-of-the-art.  They systematically explore various single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, k-means filters, PCA filters, random filters) and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based reconstruction loss, moving away from purely subjective human assessment. The core contribution is a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis and a compelling argument that much of the perceptual quality can be achieved without the complexity of deep pre-trained networks. They also delve into a more theoretical discussion about the interplay between perceptual similarity, variability, and the Maximum Entropy framework in texture modeling.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a significant and counterintuitive finding. The results are compelling, the experimental setup is thorough, and the paper is generally well-written.  The demonstration that random filters in a single layer can achieve high-quality texture synthesis is a valuable contribution that challenges existing assumptions and opens up new avenues for research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Impact:** The finding that deep pre-training is not *essential* for good texture synthesis is a significant departure from the established literature.  This challenges the common wisdom that complex feature hierarchies are always necessary for generative tasks and suggests simpler models can be surprisingly effective.  The implications are potentially broad for areas beyond texture synthesis.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors provide a comprehensive comparison of different single-layer architectures, demonstrating the effectiveness of random filters and the multi-scale approach. The visual results (Figures 1, 4) are convincing, and the quantitative evaluation provides additional support for their claims. The comparison to the VGG-based method is fair and demonstrates competitive performance.
* **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors carefully control experimental parameters and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric. The choice of using the VGG reconstruction loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, while acknowledged as imperfect, is a reasonable approach and allows for more objective comparisons. The L-BFGS-B optimization is appropriately implemented and tuned.
* **Thoughtful Discussion:** The paper goes beyond empirical results and delves into a theoretical discussion about the connection to Maximum Entropy modeling, addressing the challenges of finite sample size and balancing perceptual similarity with variability.  This elevates the paper beyond a purely engineering contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarity of Multi-Scale Architecture:** While the concept of the multi-scale model is clear, the exact implementation details (how the outputs of different filter scales are combined) could be described more explicitly. A diagram illustrating the architecture would be very helpful.
* **Ablation Study of Optimization:** The authors mention careful tuning of the optimization procedure.  It would be valuable to provide more details about this tuning (e.g., learning rate schedules, regularization) and perhaps conduct a small ablation study to demonstrate the sensitivity of the results to the optimization parameters.
* **Qualitative Failure Cases:** While the results are generally impressive, it would be helpful to show some examples of failure cases – textures where the single-layer models struggle. This would provide a more balanced picture of the limitations of the approach.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the inefficiency of the large filters, a more detailed comparison of computational cost between the single-layer and VGG-based models would be beneficial.
* **Elaborate on "Perceptual Similarity" and Variability:** In the discussion section, the authors rightly highlight the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability. Providing a more concrete example of how these can be traded off during synthesis would strengthen the argument.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Optimization Tuning:** Could you provide more details about the specific tuning of the L-BFGS-B optimization procedure?  Were any other optimization algorithms explored, and if so, why was L-BFGS-B chosen?
2. **Multi-Scale Combination:**  How exactly are the feature maps from the different scales combined in the multi-scale model? Is it a simple concatenation, weighted sum, or something else?
3. **Failure Cases:** Could you provide a few examples of textures where the single-layer models perform poorly compared to the VGG-based model?  What characteristics do these textures have in common?
4. **Computational Resources:** What hardware was used for the experiments, and how long did it take to synthesize a texture of a given size using the single-layer and VGG-based models?
5. **Variability Quantification:** You mention the challenges of quantifying variability.  Have you considered any alternative metrics beyond the VGG-based loss or psychophysical experiments, such as measuring the entropy of the synthesized textures?



Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of texture synthesis. The authors have convincingly demonstrated that simpler models can achieve surprisingly good results, challenging conventional wisdom and opening up new avenues for research.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
