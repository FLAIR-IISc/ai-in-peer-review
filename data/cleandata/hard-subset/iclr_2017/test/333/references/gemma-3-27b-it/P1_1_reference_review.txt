## ICLR 2024 Review: What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for high-quality natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can achieve results competitive with state-of-the-art methods (Gatys et al., 2015a).  The authors systematically explore different single-layer architectures, filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random), and optimization techniques, ultimately proposing a multi-scale random filter network as a strong baseline. They further introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model and discuss a generalization of maximum entropy models relevant to finite-sized textures.  The core contribution is the surprising effectiveness of simplicity—demonstrating that depth and pre-training aren't as crucial for texture synthesis as previously thought.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and counterintuitive result with solid empirical support. The work challenges a commonly held assumption in the field and offers a valuable minimal baseline for future research in texture synthesis.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors provide extensive visual comparisons (Figures 1, 4) demonstrating that their single-layer models, particularly the multi-scale version, generate textures of surprisingly high quality, often rivaling the more complex VGG-based approach. The quantitative evaluation using VGG loss further corroborates these perceptual findings.
* **Thorough Exploration:** The paper systematically investigates a wide range of single-layer architectures and filter initialization methods, allowing for a nuanced understanding of the factors influencing performance.  This is a strength, as it goes beyond simply presenting a positive result and tries to understand *why* it works.
* **Novel Quantitative Evaluation:** Introducing a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG model is a smart move, moving beyond purely subjective human evaluation.  This allows for more rigorous comparison and analysis.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** The discussion relating the findings to maximum entropy models and finite-size effects adds depth and provides a valuable theoretical context for the empirical results.
* **Reproducibility:** The methodology is clearly explained and should be reasonably reproducible given the details provided.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Clarify Optimization Details:** While the optimization procedure is described, a little more detail could be helpful.  For example, how sensitive is the performance to the scaling factor (10^7) used during optimization? What are the consequences of different L-BFGS-B parameters? A short ablation study on this might be beneficial.
* **Human Evaluation:** Although the VGG loss is a good proxy, a small-scale human evaluation (e.g., a Turing test) comparing the multi-scale model to the state-of-the-art could provide stronger evidence of perceptual quality.
* **Computational Cost:** A more detailed discussion of the computational cost associated with the multi-scale model would be helpful. How does it compare to the VGG-based model in terms of synthesis time?
* **Connection to Filter Size:** The analysis of different filter sizes in Figure 2 is insightful. Expanding on this by explicitly relating the receptive field size of each filter size to the scales of texture features it captures would strengthen the argument.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the scaling factor in optimization (10^7):**  What is the intuition behind this scaling factor? Does its value significantly affect the quality of the synthesized textures?  Have you experimented with different values and observed any trends?
* **Regarding the multi-scale model:** Could you elaborate on the strategy used to combine the outputs of the filters at different scales?  Is it a simple averaging, or is there a more sophisticated weighting scheme?
* **Regarding the VGG loss:** While the VGG loss correlates with perceptual quality, you acknowledge it isn't perfect.  Have you considered other perceptual metrics or loss functions that might better capture human perception of texture similarity?
* **Regarding reproducibility:**  Will you be releasing the code used in this work? This would significantly increase the impact and allow others to build upon your findings.



In conclusion, this is a valuable and well-executed paper that challenges conventional wisdom in texture synthesis. The demonstration of high-quality results using such a simple model is surprising and impactful, and the paper’s exploration of different architectures and theoretical considerations strengthens its contribution.  Addressing the minor feedback points would further enhance the paper’s quality and impact.
