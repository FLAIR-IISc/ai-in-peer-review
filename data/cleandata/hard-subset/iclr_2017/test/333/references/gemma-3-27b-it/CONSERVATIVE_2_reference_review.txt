This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results compared to the state-of-the-art, VGG-based approach by Gatys et al. (2015a). They explore various single-layer architectures, including Fourier basis filters, K-means learned filters, and PCA filters, and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG reconstruction loss. The core claim is that the hierarchical representation and supervised training often considered crucial for texture generation are not, in fact, indispensable.

**Reject.** While the exploration of simplified texture synthesis models is a worthwhile endeavor, the presented work falls short of convincingly demonstrating its central claim. The results, while interesting, are presented without sufficient rigor and lack a thorough comparative analysis against established baselines beyond the single VGG model. The reliance on a modified optimization procedure without a clear justification for the scaling factor also raises concerns.

The primary issue lies in the relatively limited scope of comparison. The authors primarily benchmark against the Gatys et al. (2015a) model, and while improvements are shown, comparing solely to a single, somewhat dated approach isn't enough to establish the significance of the findings. There’s a noticeable absence of comparison to more recent texture synthesis techniques, including those leveraging GANs or more advanced probabilistic models. These methods represent the current state-of-the-art and would provide a more robust assessment of the proposed approach. The paper also lacks a clear explanation of *why* the single-layer models perform so well. Is it purely the number of filters, the optimization procedure, or some other underlying principle? Simply stating that depth and pre-training are "not as indispensable" is insufficient; a more in-depth analysis is needed.

Furthermore, the quantitative evaluation metric, while a step towards objectivity, is still based on the VGG model itself. This creates a circularity in the evaluation – the metric essentially measures how well the synthesized textures fool the same network used in the original state-of-the-art method. A more independent and perceptually aligned metric, ideally incorporating human evaluation, would strengthen the conclusions. The qualitative results (Fig. 1 & 4) while visually compelling, don’t include error maps or systematic comparisons to aid in objective assessment. The appendix section detailing the evaluation of SSIM, while demonstrating its inferiority to the VGG loss, does not offer a different, strong quantitative result.

The explanation regarding the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, though insightful, remains largely theoretical. The authors suggest further work on variability measures but do not offer any concrete steps towards addressing this crucial aspect of texture synthesis.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Optimization Details:** The justification for scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10<sup>7</sup> is weak. Provide a more detailed explanation of why this scaling is necessary and its potential impact on the results. Was an ablation study performed to confirm the sensitivity of the results to this parameter?
*   **Filter Selection:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the filter size selection (11x11). What rationale was used to choose this size? Was there an investigation into the impact of varying filter sizes on performance?
*   **Computational Cost:** While acknowledging the inefficiency of large filters, a more quantitative comparison of computational costs (training time, inference time) between the single-layer and multi-layer models would be beneficial.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Incorporate a human evaluation study to assess the perceptual quality of the generated textures. This would provide a more objective and reliable assessment than relying solely on the VGG-based loss.
*   **Comparison to other Texture Synthesis Methods:** Specifically, how do the results compare to methods employing GANs (e.g., TextureGAN) or other recent approaches?
* **Error Analysis**: Provide error maps to show where the single-layer models struggle compared to the state of the art.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The optimization procedure appears crucial for the success of the single-layer models. How sensitive are the results to the initial noise image and the choice of optimization parameters (other than the scaling factor)?
2.  You mention that the VGG-based loss may not perfectly capture perceptual similarity. Do you have any preliminary results or ideas for developing a more robust perceptual metric for texture evaluation?
3.  The paper focuses on parametric texture synthesis. How do the findings generalize to non-parametric approaches, such as those based on GANs?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why random filters perform so well, especially in comparison to filters trained on natural images? Is there a specific property of the random filters that contributes to their effectiveness?
