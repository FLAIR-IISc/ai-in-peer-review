This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods relying on deep, pre-trained networks like VGG. They systematically explore various single-layer architectures, comparing random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned filters. Additionally, they propose a VGG-based loss function as a more objective metric for evaluating texture quality, moving beyond solely relying on human inspection.

**Reject.** While the findings are intriguing and counterintuitive, the paper presents a somewhat limited exploration of the design space and lacks a deeper theoretical grounding for *why* these results are observed. The novelty is high, but the empirical validation, while extensive, doesn’t quite solidify the claims enough for acceptance, especially given the reliance on somewhat ad-hoc optimization tuning. 

The core argument – that depth and pre-training are less critical than previously believed – is compelling. The experimental setup is thorough, testing a range of filter types and sizes. The multi-scale random filter network is a particularly interesting finding, demonstrating that combining filters at different scales is key to achieving good performance. The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric (VGG loss) is a step in the right direction, although its limitations are acknowledged. However, the paper feels largely empirical. There is little discussion about *why* random filters work so well. Is it simply that a sufficient number of random filters can approximate any relevant feature distribution? Or is there something about the optimization landscape that makes simple, random networks easier to train for this specific task? The fact that the multi-scale model outperforms single-scale versions suggests the importance of receptive field size, but this isn’t deeply analyzed. The connection to maximum entropy modeling, mentioned in the introduction and discussion, feels underdeveloped. The authors state that the results suggest the depth and pre-training are "fine-tuning" but don't elaborate or provide empirical evidence for this claim beyond the observed performance. Furthermore, the success seems highly dependent on the specific optimization procedure ("more elaborate tuning"). This tuning isn't fully transparent; what specific changes were made compared to Gatys et al., and how sensitive are the results to these changes?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation studies on optimization:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the optimization process. How does performance change with different learning rates, optimization algorithms, and regularization techniques? A clear explanation of the "more elaborate tuning" is crucial.
*   **Theoretical discussion:** Expand on the theoretical implications of the results. Relate the findings to concepts like kernel methods, random feature maps, or the expressive power of shallow networks.
*   **Receptive field analysis:** Quantify the effective receptive field size of the different networks. How does this relate to the scale of the textures they can synthesize effectively?
*   **Comparison to other texture synthesis methods:** While the paper compares to Gatys et al., a broader comparison to other texture synthesis techniques (e.g., those based on Markov Random Fields, or more recent generative models) would be valuable.
*   **Generalization to other domains:** While the focus is on natural textures, could these findings generalize to other domains like material synthesis or image inpainting?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide more details about the specific changes made to the optimization procedure compared to Gatys et al. (2015a)? What is the sensitivity of the results to these changes?
2.  What is the computational cost of the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based model, and how does this impact its practical applicability?
3.  Do you have any intuition as to *why* random filters perform so well? Is it related to the high dimensionality of the filter space, or other factors?
4.  The paper mentions a potential connection to maximum entropy modeling. Can you elaborate on how the proposed approach relates to this framework and what future research directions could explore this connection further?
