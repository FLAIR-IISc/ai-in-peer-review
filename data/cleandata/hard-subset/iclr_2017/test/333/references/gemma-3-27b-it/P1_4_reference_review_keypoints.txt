- Paper shows single-layer CNNs with random filters can synthesize natural-looking textures rivaling deep pre-trained models.
- Method optimizes a noise image to match Gram matrix statistics of a target texture.
- Authors test multiple single-layer architectures: Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters.
- Introduces a VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative evaluation metric replacing solely human inspection.
- Connects results to a maximum entropy framework, offering theoretical grounding.
- Thorough ablation studies demonstrate the importance of multi-scale filters and compare filter types.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions; findings are surprising and impactful.
- Novelty: Challenges the assumption that depth and pre-training are necessary for texture synthesis.
- Strong empirical evidence: Visual and quantitative comparisons frequently show random multi-scale models comparable to Gatys et al. (2015).
- Quantitative evaluation: VGG-loss provides an objective metric addressing prior reliance on subjective tests.
- Recommendation to clarify optimization details, specifically sensitivity to the scaling factor (10^7) for reproducibility.
- Suggests a small-scale human evaluation (A/B test) to further validate perceptual quality.
- Request to provide computational cost comparison and quantify synthesis speed-ups versus deeper models.
- Ask for discussion and examples of failure cases where single-layer models do not produce realistic textures.
- Suggest expanding analysis of variability vs. perceptual similarity and exploring ways to encourage diversity in outputs.
- Key methodological questions: why optimization is less prone to local minima, explanation of the finite-size MaxEnt effect, principled choice of multi-scale settings, sensitivity of K-means initializations, and examples where VGG-loss fails.
