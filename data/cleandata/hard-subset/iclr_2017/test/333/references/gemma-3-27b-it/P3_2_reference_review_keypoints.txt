- Single-layer CNNs with random filters can synthesize high-quality natural textures, challenging the need for deep, pre-trained networks.  
- The authors introduce a VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality, reducing reliance on human evaluation.  
- The paper establishes a strong minimal baseline for texture synthesis and questions depth/pre-training necessity for this task.  
- Comprehensive empirical evaluation shows multi-scale single-layer models rival Gatys et al. (2015a), with ablations highlighting multi-scale importance.  
- The VGG-based quantitative evaluation is a valuable objective metric, though its limitations are acknowledged.  
- The work is well-situated in prior literature and convincingly addresses critiques about random networks.  
- Theoretical discussion connects results to maximum entropy models and examines limitations of current texture similarity/variability constraints.  
- Reproducibility is strong: architectures, optimization, and metrics are detailed, using public datasets and frameworks.  
- Request clarification and sensitivity analysis of optimization hyperparameters (e.g., L-BFGS-B settings, learning rates, regularization).  
- Suggest providing visual examples where VGG loss fails (low loss but perceptually dissimilar) to illustrate metric limitations.  
- Note the multi-scale model’s computational inefficiency and recommend discussing mitigation strategies (pruning, sparse convolutions).  
- Recommend expanding the MaxEnt discussion, specifically relating to “ad hockeries” identified by Jaynes.  
- Minor formatting improvements (clearer network descriptions, consistent bullets/tables) would enhance readability.  
- Questions for authors: provide detailed optimization parameters and techniques to avoid local minima.  
- Questions for authors: show examples of low VGG loss yet perceptually distinct textures.  
- Questions for authors: discuss scalability to larger textures and more complex datasets and any resolution-dependent limitations.  
- Questions for authors: comment on implications for style transfer and whether the single-layer approach can adapt to style transfer tasks.
