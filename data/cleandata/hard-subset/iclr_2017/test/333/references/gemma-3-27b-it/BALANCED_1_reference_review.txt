This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing belief that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly high-quality textures can be generated using a single convolutional layer with random filters, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods. They systematically explore different single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model’s synthesis loss, replacing traditional human evaluation. The work also offers a discussion on the generalization of maximum entropy models for textures with limited spatial extent.

**Reject** – While the findings are intriguing and contribute to a better understanding of texture synthesis, the paper suffers from a lack of sufficient novelty and a disconnect between the compelling visual results and the proposed quantitative evaluation. The claim that the results "rival" state-of-the-art methods isn't fully substantiated by the quantitative comparison, and the discussion feels somewhat tangential to the core contribution.

Here’s a breakdown of my assessment:

**1. Specific Question/Problem Tackled:** The paper directly addresses the question of what constitutes the minimal requirement for achieving high-quality natural texture synthesis. It aims to determine if the depth and pre-training of deep networks, commonly employed in texture synthesis, are truly indispensable.

**2. Motivation and Literature Placement:** The motivation is well-articulated. The paper is firmly grounded in the existing literature on texture synthesis, citing relevant works on parametric texture models, GANs, and style transfer. It positions itself as a counterpoint to existing assumptions about the necessity of deep representations and pre-training, directly engaging with the work of Gatys et al. (2015a) and subsequent extensions.

**3. Support for Claims:** The visual results presented in Figure 1 and 4 are compelling and demonstrate that single-layer networks with random filters *can* produce textures of surprisingly high quality. However, the quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss doesn’t fully support the claim of rivaling state-of-the-art performance. While the authors acknowledge limitations of this metric, relying heavily on it in the results section creates a disconnect. The argument that the multi-scale model often rivals the VGG-based synthesis is made without overwhelming statistical evidence. The justification for the chosen optimization parameters (scaling factor of 10^7) also lacks sufficient explanation. Furthermore, the discussion regarding the generalization of maximum entropy models feels somewhat disconnected from the core findings regarding single-layer networks and random filters. The connection to the main contribution feels tenuous.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The paper would benefit from a more robust quantitative evaluation. Consider incorporating established texture quality metrics (beyond the VGG-based loss) and conducting a formal user study to assess perceptual quality, even if it’s a smaller-scale experiment. This would strengthen the claim of rivaling state-of-the-art methods.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance testing for the quantitative comparisons. Simply stating that the multi-scale model “often rivals” state-of-the-art is insufficient.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to understand the contribution of different components of the multi-scale model (e.g., the number of filters, filter sizes).
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the results to the optimization parameters, especially the scaling factor used in the loss function.
*   **Discussion Clarification:** Streamline the discussion section. Focus more directly on the implications of the findings regarding single-layer networks and random filters, and either integrate the discussion on maximum entropy models more closely or move it to the appendix.
*   **Explain Optimization Choices:** Provide a clearer rationale for the chosen optimization algorithm (L-BFGS-B) and its parameters.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the scaling factor (10^7) in the optimization process? How sensitive are the results to this parameter?
*   What were the results of a formal user study comparing the perceptual quality of textures generated by the single-layer model, the multi-scale model, and the VGG-based model?
*   How do the results change if you use different texture quality metrics in addition to the VGG-based loss?
*   Can you elaborate on the connection between the findings regarding single-layer networks and the discussion on the generalization of maximum entropy models? How does the proposed work contribute to the understanding of MaxEnt models in the context of texture synthesis?
*   What is the computational cost of the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based model, and how does this impact its practical applicability?
