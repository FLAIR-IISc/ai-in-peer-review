## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the necessity of deep, pre-trained convolutional networks for natural texture synthesis. Challenging the prevailing paradigm established by Gatys et al. (2015a), the authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be generated using a single-layer convolutional network with *random* filters, often rivaling the quality of state-of-the-art methods.  The core contribution lies in a minimal baseline for texture synthesis, a thorough comparison of various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random), and the introduction of a VGG-based loss function for quantitative texture quality assessment.  The authors provide compelling evidence that depth and pre-training are not indispensable for capturing the essential characteristics of natural textures, suggesting that simpler models, when properly optimized, can be surprisingly effective. They also delve into the theoretical underpinnings of parametric texture synthesis and the need to balance perceptual similarity and variability.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a significant and counterintuitive finding that challenges established assumptions in the field of texture synthesis. The experimental results are convincing, and the analysis is thorough. The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric using the VGG-based loss is a valuable contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Impact:** The core finding – that random filters in a single-layer CNN can generate textures competitive with state-of-the-art methods – is genuinely novel. It fundamentally questions the necessity of complex, pre-trained features for this task, shifting the focus towards optimization and architectural considerations. This finding has the potential to influence future research in generative modeling and texture synthesis.
* **Strong Empirical Evidence:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation of different single-layer architectures. The visualizations in Figure 1 and Figure 4 clearly demonstrate the quality of textures generated by the random multi-scale model, often matching or surpassing the results obtained with the VGG-based approach. The quantitative VGG-based loss further supports these visual assessments.  The ablation study (Figure 2) clearly shows the importance of multi-scale filters and non-linearities.
* **Sound Methodology:** The experimental setup is well-defined and reproducible. The use of L-BFGS-B for optimization and the careful tuning of parameters are crucial for achieving the reported results. The comparison to established methods (Gatys et al., Portilla & Simoncelli) provides a solid benchmark.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** The paper goes beyond purely empirical results, providing a thoughtful discussion of the theoretical foundations of parametric texture synthesis and the importance of balancing perceptual similarity and variability. The connection to the MaxEnt framework and the limitations of current evaluation metrics are insightful.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **Optimization Details:** While the authors mention scaling the loss and gradients, a more detailed explanation of the tuning process would be helpful. Specifically, how was the scaling factor (10^7) determined? Was a grid search performed?
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper acknowledges the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model due to the large filter sizes. A brief discussion of the computational cost (e.g., training/synthesis time) compared to other methods would be valuable.
* **Qualitative vs Quantitative Discrepancies:**  The authors briefly mention situations where the VGG-based loss doesn't fully align with perceptual quality. It would be helpful to provide more examples and discuss potential reasons for this discrepancy in more detail.
* **Generalization to Other Domains:** While the paper focuses on textures, could these findings generalize to other image generation tasks, such as style transfer or image completion? A brief discussion of the potential implications for these domains would be interesting.
* **Human Evaluation:** Although the authors focus on the VGG-based loss, a small-scale human evaluation (e.g., a user study asking participants to distinguish between real and synthesized textures) would further strengthen the claims.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the parameter tuning process for the L-BFGS-B optimization, particularly the choice of the scaling factor for the loss and gradients?
2.  What is the approximate computational cost (e.g., seconds per iteration, total synthesis time) of the multi-scale model compared to the VGG-based approach for a fixed image size?
3.  Can you provide a few more examples where the VGG-based loss seems to be at odds with human perception, and speculate on the potential reasons for these discrepancies? Specifically, could the limitations of VGG’s feature space contribute to this mismatch?
4.  Do you foresee these findings having implications for other generative modeling tasks beyond texture synthesis, such as style transfer or image completion?  If so, could you briefly outline potential avenues for investigation?




Overall, this is a well-written and impactful paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of texture synthesis. The counterintuitive findings are supported by strong experimental evidence and insightful theoretical discussion. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
