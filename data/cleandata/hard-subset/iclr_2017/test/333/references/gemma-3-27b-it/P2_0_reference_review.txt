## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the requirements for generating natural-looking textures using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single-layer CNN with *random* filters. They perform an extensive comparison of various single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, k-means, PCA, random) against the established Gatys et al. (2015a) method, introducing a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model and discussing a generalization of maximum entropy models. The core finding is that depth and pre-training are surprisingly less crucial than previously believed, and that a properly tuned optimization procedure is key to success even with simple models.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a counterintuitive and significant finding—that surprisingly simple models can achieve impressive texture synthesis results.  The extensive experimentation and quantitative evaluation strengthen the claims.  The work challenges established beliefs in the field and opens up avenues for more efficient texture generation techniques.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The central finding – that random filters in a single-layer network can rival state-of-the-art texture synthesis – is genuinely surprising and represents a significant contribution. This directly challenges the common assumption about the necessity of deep, pre-trained networks.
* **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The paper isn't just theoretical; it presents a comprehensive experimental evaluation. The authors test a wide variety of single-layer architectures, systematically varying filter types and sizes. The visual comparisons in Figure 1 and 4 are compelling, and the quantitative VGG-based loss provides a more objective assessment than purely subjective human evaluation.
* **Well-Motivated & Placed in Literature:**  The introduction clearly outlines the context of the work, situating it within the growing field of natural image generation and texture synthesis.  The paper effectively contrasts its approach with existing methods like GANs, PixelCNNs, and style transfer techniques.
* **Rigorous Methodology:** The authors carefully detail their methodology, including network architectures, optimization procedures (L-BFGS-B with specific tuning), and evaluation metrics. The inclusion of the implementation details (Lasagne) is also appreciated. The justification for the optimization scaling factor is also a good touch.
* **Quantitative Evaluation:**  The adoption of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a valuable contribution, reducing reliance on subjective human assessment. While the authors acknowledge its limitations, it provides a consistent and comparable measure of quality.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Optimization:** The paper states that a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure is key, but it could benefit from a deeper dive into *what* specifically was tuned and *why* it made a difference. Providing more details about the sensitivity of the results to optimization parameters would be valuable.
* **Limitations of VGG Loss:** While a good step forward, the limitations of the VGG-based loss are acknowledged but could be explored further. Perhaps a discussion of specific texture types where it performs poorly would be helpful, and potential alternatives (e.g., learned perceptual metrics) could be mentioned.
* **Computational Cost:** While the authors mention the inefficiency of multi-scale models, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of different architectures would be beneficial.
* **Statistical Significance:** While visual results are strong, reporting some form of statistical significance for the quantitative results (e.g., using paired t-tests) would further strengthen the claims.
* **Clarity in Notation:** Some of the notation (e.g., within the mathematical formulations) could be slightly clarified for better readability.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  Could you elaborate on the specific optimization parameters that were tuned and the impact of these adjustments on the results?  Specifically, beyond the scaling factor mentioned, were learning rates, momentum, or other parameters adjusted, and how did those changes influence the synthesis quality?
*  The paper mentions a possible finite-size effect leading to exact reconstruction in deeper models.  Is there any empirical evidence to support this claim? Have you observed this behavior consistently?
* You mention a trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.  Have you experimented with modifying the optimization procedure (e.g., adding a regularization term) to explicitly encourage greater variability in the synthesized textures?
* The appendix mentions SSIM performing worse than VGG loss. Were there any other perceptual metrics explored and compared?



In conclusion, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of texture synthesis. The core finding is surprising and well-supported by the experimental results. Addressing the suggestions above would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
