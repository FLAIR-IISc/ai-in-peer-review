This paper investigates the requirements for natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained convolutional networks are essential. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly good textures can be generated using a single convolutional layer with random filters, often rivaling the quality of state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). They comprehensively evaluate various single-layer architectures and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG features.

**Reject** – While the results are interesting and counterintuitive, the paper lacks sufficient depth in analysis and justification for some of its design choices, and the improvements over the baseline (Gatys et al., 2015a) aren't substantial enough to warrant acceptance in its current form. Additionally, the connection between the theoretical discussion on maximum entropy models and the empirical results isn’t clearly established.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Incremental Improvement:** The core finding – that a single-layer network with random filters can generate textures competitive with deeper, pre-trained networks – is valuable. However, the improvement isn’t dramatic. Figures 1 and 4 show instances where the proposed method *matches* the quality of Gatys et al., but doesn't consistently *outperform* it. The claim of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art feels overstated without a more rigorous and consistent quantitative demonstration of superior performance. The VGG-based loss provides some quantification, but its limitations (discussed in the paper itself) weaken its conclusive power.

2. **Optimization Details:** The paper mentions an "elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" as a potential reason for discrepancies with earlier work. However, this tuning isn’t fully detailed. The scaling factor of 10^7 is mentioned, but the justification for its value is missing. A more thorough ablation study exploring the sensitivity of the results to different optimization parameters is needed. What happens if the scaling factor is different? What about the learning rate?  Without this, it's hard to discern whether the success is due to the architecture itself or the optimized optimization procedure.

3. **Theoretical Connection:** The final section attempts to connect the work to the MaxEnt framework. However, this connection feels superficial. While the authors acknowledge the limitations of applying MaxEnt to finite-size images, they don't fully explore how their approach aligns with or departs from this theoretical framework. The discussion of the "bipartition Px" feels disconnected from the practical results. The point about the constraints being "soft" is interesting but requires further elaboration.

4. **Filter Size and Multi-Scale:** The investigation of different filter sizes and the multi-scale approach is a strong point, but the motivation for the specific choices (e.g., 11x11, 3x3, 55x55) could be strengthened.  Why these particular sizes?  Is there a theoretical basis or a systematic exploration of a wider range of sizes?



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** Expand the quantitative evaluation beyond the VGG-based loss. Consider metrics that correlate better with human perception, even if they are more complex to compute.  A user study, even with a small number of participants, would significantly strengthen the claims.
*   **Ablation Study:** Perform a thorough ablation study on the optimization parameters (learning rate, scaling factor, number of iterations). Demonstrate the robustness of the results to variations in these parameters.
*   **Visualization:** Provide more visual examples of texture synthesis results, particularly those where the proposed method clearly outperforms Gatys et al. (2015a).
*   **Clarify Optimization:** Provide pseudocode or a more detailed explanation of the optimization process, including how the box constraints are implemented.
*    **Error Analysis:** Discuss cases where the proposed method *fails* to generate realistic textures. What types of textures are particularly challenging? What are the limitations of the single-layer approach?
*   **Theoretical Depth:**  Either fully develop the connection to the MaxEnt framework or remove the discussion if it’s not central to the contribution. If retained, provide a more rigorous analysis of how the proposed approach relates to the theoretical concepts.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific modifications were made to the optimization procedure beyond the scaling factor? Can you provide a more detailed explanation and justification for these changes?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the VGG network used for evaluation? Would using a different pre-trained network (e.g., ResNet) significantly alter the conclusions?
*   You mention that the multi-scale model often rivals the state-of-the-art. Can you provide a quantitative comparison (e.g., using the VGG loss) across a large dataset of textures demonstrating the statistical significance of this improvement?
*   Could you elaborate on why the specific filter sizes (3x3, 11x11, 55x55) were chosen for the multi-scale model? Was a systematic search performed?
*   What is the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) of the proposed method compared to Gatys et al. (2015a)?
