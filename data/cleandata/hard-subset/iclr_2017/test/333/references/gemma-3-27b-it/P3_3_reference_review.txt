## ICLR 2024 Review: WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for natural texture synthesis using convolutional neural networks. Challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential, the authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with *random* filters can generate textures of comparable quality to state-of-the-art methods based on deeper, pre-trained VGG networks. They systematically explore various single-layer architectures (Fourier basis, K-means filters, PCA filters, random filters) and propose a multi-scale random filter approach that performs exceptionally well. Crucially, they introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based reconstruction loss, moving away from subjective human inspection.  The work also contributes a discussion on the relationship between their findings and the maximum entropy modeling framework.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** This paper presents a surprising and important result – that high-quality texture synthesis doesn’t inherently require the complexity of deep, pre-trained networks. The well-designed experiments, clear writing, and novel quantitative evaluation strongly support this claim. The work provides a valuable baseline and challenges existing assumptions within the field of texture generation.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors convincingly demonstrate that single-layer networks, particularly the multi-scale random filter model, can produce textures that visually rival those generated by the established Gatys et al. (2015a) method. The figures (especially Fig. 1 & 4) are compelling.
* **Systematic Exploration:** The methodical comparison of different single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) provides valuable insights into the role of filter design and network capacity. The investigation into multi-scale filters is particularly insightful.
* **Novel Quantitative Evaluation:** The adoption of the VGG-based reconstruction loss as a quantitative metric is a significant contribution. This move towards objective assessment enhances the reproducibility and comparability of results. The supplemental comparisons with SSIM further bolster this point.
* **Theoretical Discussion:** The paper doesn't shy away from discussing the broader theoretical implications of their findings in relation to maximum entropy models and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. This elevates the work beyond a purely empirical demonstration.
* **Well-Written and Clear:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The methodology is explained clearly, and the results are presented in a concise and understandable manner.



**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Ablation Study on Optimization:** While the authors mention tuning the optimization procedure, a more detailed ablation study on the impact of different L-BFGS-B parameters (learning rate, maximum iterations, box constraints) would strengthen the conclusions.  How sensitive are the results to these hyperparameters, particularly for the random filter models?
* **Computational Cost:** While acknowledging the inefficiency of the multi-scale model due to large filters, a more quantitative comparison of the computational cost (training/synthesis time) between the single-layer models and the VGG-based approach would be useful.
* **Generalization to Other Datasets:** The experiments primarily focus on a limited set of textures. Investigating the performance of the models on a broader range of natural texture datasets (e.g., materials databases) would improve the generalizability of the findings.
* **Discussion of Filter Size Selection:** The choice of 11x11 filters seems somewhat arbitrary.  Is there a principled way to determine the optimal filter size(s) for a given texture?  A discussion of this would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the "scaling factor of 10^7" in the loss:**  Can you elaborate on the rationale behind this specific scaling factor? Is it crucial for achieving good results, and if so, why?  Is there a risk of instability or numerical issues with such a large scaling?
* **Concerning the VGG-based loss as a metric:** You rightly point out its imperfections.  Do you have any thoughts on how this metric could be further improved to better align with human perception of texture quality?  Perhaps incorporating metrics related to statistical texture features beyond the VGG representations?
* **About the MaxEnt framework discussion:** You mention that practical implementations allow for a small mismatch in constraints.  How does this relate to the optimization process – does the L-BFGS-B algorithm implicitly introduce this mismatch, or is it a more fundamental property of the random filter approach?
* **On the limitation of the paper:** What would be the next logical step to build on these results? Are there specific areas of texture synthesis (e.g. procedural texture generation, style mixing) where the insights from this work might be particularly impactful?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a compelling message and rigorous experimental support. Its acceptance would be a valuable contribution to the ICLR conference.
