This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that a single-layer CNN with random filters can achieve surprisingly competitive results, often rivaling state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a). They systematically compare various single-layer architectures—using Fourier, K-means, and PCA filters—and introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on the VGG-based texture model, moving away from purely subjective human assessment. They posit that the success stems from a more elaborate optimization procedure than previously employed.

**Reject.** While the findings are interesting, the paper feels premature and lacks the rigorous validation expected for a claim challenging established methodology. Specifically, the improvements demonstrated over Gatys et al. (2015a) appear marginal and, critically, are heavily reliant on a quantitative metric (VGG loss) that the authors themselves acknowledge is imperfect and may not fully capture perceptual quality. The reliance on ablation studies without exploring parameter tuning for the original Gatys et al. method weakens the comparison.

The core argument—that depth and pre-training are “not as indispensable” as previously thought—is too strong given the evidence presented. The authors demonstrate that they *can* be replaced, not that doing so doesn't represent a trade-off in performance or efficiency. The observation that random filters perform well is intriguing, but the mechanism behind this success remains largely unexplained. Simply stating that it's due to "more elaborate tuning" feels insufficient. The paper doesn’t adequately explain *why* a single layer with random filters can achieve results comparable to a deeply trained network—what properties of the optimization or network structure are enabling this?

The quantitative evaluation is a step in the right direction, but relying solely on VGG loss is problematic. The authors' own results demonstrate discrepancies between VGG loss and perceived quality for certain textures (grids, walls), casting doubt on its reliability as a comprehensive assessment tool. The appendix briefly mentions SSIM and inpainting tasks, but these are not incorporated into the main analysis. The claim that the VGG loss is an “acceptable approximation” of perceptual quality is not convincingly substantiated.  The exploration of multi-scale filters is valuable, but the conclusion that hierarchical networks aren't *necessary* feels like a leap, given that efficiency was a factor in not fully evaluating them. It would be more accurate to say they aren't *sufficient* for optimal results.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Stronger Baseline Comparison:** A more thorough comparison with Gatys et al. (2015a) is needed. Perform a parameter sweep of the optimization procedure for the original method to establish a truly fair baseline.
*   **Perceptual Evaluation:** Incorporate a formal perceptual study with human observers, even if it's a small-scale experiment, to validate the VGG loss metric.
*   **Mechanism Explanation:** Investigate *why* random filters work. Are specific filter properties emerging during optimization? Is there a connection to the statistical properties of natural textures?
*   **Efficiency Analysis:** Provide a detailed efficiency comparison (training and synthesis time) between the proposed method and the state-of-the-art.
*   **Generalization:** Discuss the limitations of the approach. Does it generalize well to different types of textures or image domains?
*   **Clarify the Optimization:** Provide more details on the optimization process beyond simply stating "more elaborate tuning". What specific changes were made, and why? How sensitive are the results to these changes?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific changes were made to the optimization procedure (L-BFGS-B parameters, loss scaling) compared to the original implementation by Gatys et al. (2015a), and how were these changes determined?
2.  Can you provide a detailed comparison of the computational cost (training and synthesis time) between your best-performing model and the original Gatys et al. (2015a) model?
3.  You acknowledge limitations in the VGG loss metric. What steps have you considered to address these limitations, beyond the brief mention of SSIM and inpainting in the appendix?
4.  What is the role of the ReLU non-linearity in the success of the single-layer models? How do the results change when ReLU is removed (as briefly shown in Figure 2), and can this be quantified beyond visual inspection?
5.  You mention a potential "finite size effect" regarding exact reconstruction. How does the size of the input image affect the results, and is there a point at which the synthesized image *always* converges to the reference image?
