### 1. Summary

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for parametric natural texture synthesis. The authors challenge two widely held assumptions derived from the state-of-the-art model (Gatys et al., 2015a): the necessity of a deep, hierarchical network architecture and the need for supervised pre-training on a large-scale object recognition task. The paper's main contribution is to demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling those produced by a 19-layer pre-trained VGG network.

To support this claim, the authors conduct a thorough empirical study comparing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), filter counts, and filter scales. They introduce a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model, complementing the standard qualitative visual inspection. The paper concludes that for texture synthesis, factors like multi-scale filter representations and non-linearities are crucial, whereas network depth and learned semantic features appear to be less indispensable than previously believed.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The central result—that a single-layer network with random filters can match the performance of a deep, pre-trained network on texture synthesis—is a significant and counter-intuitive finding. It provides a powerful and simple baseline for future work and forces a re-evaluation of the role of deep hierarchical features in this specific generative task.
2.  **Rigorous and Systematic Evaluation:** The paper supports its claims with a comprehensive set of experiments that systematically ablate different components of the model (filter type, number, scale). The introduction and honest assessment of a quantitative metric (the VGG-loss) adds a layer of scientific rigor beyond purely subjective visual comparisons, strengthening the authors' conclusions.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear and focused research question: what are the truly essential components for texture synthesis? The approach of starting with a complex, successful model and stripping it down to its bare essentials is a classic and effective scientific method.

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is sound. The texture model itself, based on matching Gram matrices of feature activations, directly follows the established state-of-the-art framework, ensuring a fair comparison. The synthesis process via L-BFGS optimization is standard practice. The experimental design is excellent; by testing a wide array of single-layer models (Random, Fourier, K-means, etc.) with both low and high filter counts, the authors convincingly demonstrate that the success of the random filter model is not an anomaly. The investigation into the importance of multi-scale filters (Fig. 2) is a particularly effective piece of analysis, demonstrating that a combination of filter sizes is key to capturing both fine-grained and long-range correlations, and that non-linearities are indispensable.

**Novelty and Impact:** The core novelty is the demonstration that depth and supervised feature learning are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. This stands in contrast to the original claims in Gatys et al. (2015a) and the general trend in the field. This result is impactful because it provides a much simpler and more interpretable baseline model. The paper also makes a valuable contribution by proposing a quantitative evaluation metric. While acknowledging its imperfections, using the VGG-loss as a proxy for perceptual quality is a pragmatic step towards more objective and reproducible evaluation in this domain. Finally, the discussion section thoughtfully connects the empirical results to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework, discussing the critical trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, which adds significant intellectual depth to the paper.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clearly stated, the methods are described precisely, and the results are presented logically with strong visual support. The figures are informative and directly support the text's claims.

In summary, the paper presents a clear, surprising, and well-supported result that advances our understanding of generative texture models. The experiments are thorough, and the analysis is insightful.

### 4. Additional Feedback

The following points are suggestions for improvement and are not critical to the decision.

*   In the introduction, the paper suggests that its success with random networks, contrary to earlier reports, "originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." However, Section 4 states that L-BFGS-B was used with default parameters, except for iteration count and a simple scaling of the loss. It would be helpful to clarify what specific tuning was "elaborate" and critical for success. Was the scaling factor the key element? This detail seems important for reproducibility and understanding why this attempt succeeded where others failed.
*   The discussion of computational inefficiency due to large filters is well-taken. The paper mentions that hierarchical architectures were explored but did not improve synthesis quality. This is a very interesting negative result. Including a brief summary of the tested architectures and their performance (perhaps in the appendix) would be highly valuable to the community, as it would provide evidence on where the trade-off between single-layer expressivity and multi-layer efficiency lies.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. The proposed inpainting task in the appendix is a creative idea for a future psychophysical evaluation of model variability.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure"? Specifically, was the loss scaling factor of 10^7 the primary change that enabled successful synthesis with random filters, preventing the early stopping of the optimizer that might have plagued previous attempts?
2.  The paper notes the paradoxical result that for some textures, the VGG-loss is lower for samples from your multi-scale model than for samples from the VGG model itself. The appendix shows that initializing the VGG synthesis with your model's output improves results. Does this initialization strategy also resolve the paradox for those specific textures (i.e., does the VGG model then achieve an even lower loss, as expected)? This could provide further insight into the optimization landscape.
3.  The paper finds that a random multi-scale model is superior to single-scale models. Does this advantage hold for other filter types as well? For instance, how would a multi-scale model using K-means or Fourier basis filters at different scales compare to the single-scale versions and the random multi-scale model?
