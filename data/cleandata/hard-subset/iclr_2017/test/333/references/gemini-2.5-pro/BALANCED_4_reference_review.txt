### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal requirements for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption, established by state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a), that successful texture generation relies critically on deep, hierarchical representations from networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition tasks (e.g., VGG on ImageNet). The authors compellingly demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures of remarkable perceptual quality, often rivaling those produced by deep, pre-trained networks.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) establishing a strong and simple baseline for texture synthesis that requires neither network depth nor supervised training; (2) showing through extensive experiments that this simple model is highly effective, thereby questioning the indispensability of learned hierarchical features for this task; (3) performing a systematic comparison of single-layer architectures with various filter types (random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned); and (4) proposing the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality, while also thoughtfully discussing its limitations.

### 2. Decision: Accept

This is a strong and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of generative models for images. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant and Surprising Core Finding:** The paper presents a clear, counter-intuitive, and impactful result: a single-layer network with random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis. This finding fundamentally simplifies our understanding of the problem and forces a re-evaluation of the role of depth and learned features in texture modeling.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Support:** The central claim is substantiated by a comprehensive set of experiments that are well-designed and convincingly presented. The authors systematically explore the design space of simple models, providing strong evidence for their conclusions through extensive qualitative and quantitative comparisons.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper exhibits several strengths that make it a clear accept.

**Strength 1: Importance and Impact of the Central Result.** The primary strength lies in its core finding. By demonstrating the surprising effectiveness of a minimal model, the paper acts as a crucial ablation study for the more complex state-of-the-art models. It suggests that the power of these methods may derive less from the specific learned hierarchical features of VGG, and more from the general architectural paradigm: a highly overcomplete, non-linear filter bank whose second-order statistics (Gram matrix) are matched. This is a foundational insight that clarifies the mechanism behind neural texture synthesis and has broader implications for building more efficient and understandable generative models.

**Strength 2: High-Quality Experimental Investigation.** The empirical work is excellent. The authors do not merely show that one random network works; they conduct a thorough investigation into what makes it work.
*   **Systematic Comparison:** They compare a wide variety of filter initializations—random, Fourier basis, K-means, PCA—and numbers of filters, demonstrating robustly that random filters perform competitively.
*   **Ablation of Key Components:** Their analysis of filter scale (Fig. 2) provides a clear explanation for why their multi-scale random model is superior, showing that a combination of receptive field sizes is necessary to capture correlations at different ranges. The demonstration that the non-linearity (ReLU) is essential further isolates the critical model components.
*   **Direct and Fair Comparison:** The qualitative comparison against the VGG-based model in Figure 4 is extensive (20 textures) and persuasive. The results from their proposed model are often visually indistinguishable from or even superior to the state-of-the-art, particularly for highly regular textures.

**Strength 3: Insightful Discussion and Analysis.** The paper goes beyond just presenting results. The discussion in Section 7 is particularly strong. The authors astutely identify optimization as a key factor explaining their success where others had reported failure with random networks. Their discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability is nuanced and important for the field. Furthermore, connecting their practical findings to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework and highlighting its limitations for finite-sized images demonstrates a deep conceptual understanding of the problem.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Demonstrating the Optimization "Trick":** The paper hypothesizes that its success with random networks, contrary to prior reports, is due to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization," specifically scaling the loss and gradients. This is a crucial point. The paper would be even more convincing if it included a small experiment directly demonstrating this: show a failed synthesis from a random network *without* this scaling. This would provide a direct "reproduction" of the previously reported failure mode and solidify the authors' explanation.
*   **Quantifying Variability:** The discussion on the similarity/variability trade-off is excellent. The authors speculate that their model might produce less variable samples due to a smoother optimization landscape. This is a testable hypothesis. While a full psychophysical study is beyond scope, a simple quantitative analysis could be illustrative. For example, generating N>5 samples for a given texture with both the proposed model and the VGG model and then reporting the average pairwise distance (e.g., using LPIPS or SSIM) within each set of samples could provide preliminary evidence to support this claim.
*   **Efficiency Claims:** The paper notes that deep architectures would be more computationally efficient for achieving large receptive fields than using large kernels. This is an important practical point. Adding concrete timing numbers (e.g., seconds per synthesis iteration for the multi-scale random model vs. the VGG model) would make this trade-off more tangible for the reader.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the rebuttal period:

1.  Regarding the VGG-loss metric, you show cases where your random multi-scale model achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-based synthesis, even though the latter directly optimizes this objective. Do you attribute this solely to the VGG synthesis getting trapped in a poor local minimum, or could there be a more fundamental reason related to the differing model architectures and their respective optimization landscapes?
2.  Could you provide more detail on your claim that your success with random networks is due to careful optimization? Specifically, can you confirm if removing the gradient scaling factor of 10^7 leads to the kind of "perceptually uninteresting images" reported in prior work?
3.  You convincingly argue for the importance of multi-scale filters. Have you considered alternative ways of achieving multi-scale receptive fields within a single-layer framework, such as using dilated convolutions with different rates, and do you have any intuition on how that might compare to your approach of using different kernel sizes?
