### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumptions that deep, hierarchical representations and supervisedly pre-trained features are necessary. The central claim is that a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters is sufficient to generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art method of Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a 19-layer pre-trained VGG network. The paper presents a comprehensive comparison of various single-layer models (using random, Fourier, k-means, and PCA filters) and introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model itself. Finally, the authors provide a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, linking practical algorithms to the Maximum Entropy framework and the importance of defining explicit perceptual metrics.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a surprising and potentially impactful core finding, I recommend rejection due to a significant number of methodological inconsistencies, a lack of crucial implementation details, and a general lack of rigor that undermines the paper's claims. The manuscript, in its current state, does not meet the high standards required for publication at ICLR. The two primary reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Serious Methodological Flaws:** There is a fundamental and unexplained discrepancy between the distance metric defined in the paper (Eq. 2) and the loss function that is actually optimized for synthesis (Eq. 5). Furthermore, the optimization procedure relies on an arbitrary scaling factor ("a hack") that is not justified, suggesting potential numerical instability.
2.  **Lack of Scientific Rigor and Reproducibility:** The paper omits critical details about the experimental setup (e.g., whitening method, random filter initialization) and makes strong claims without providing any supporting evidence (e.g., about experiments with other deep random architectures). The quantitative evaluation is based on a metric that the authors themselves demonstrate to be unreliable, and the manuscript is marred by severe formatting errors, particularly in the bibliography.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Inconsistent Formulation of Distance and Loss:** In Section 3, the authors define a specific normalized Euclidean distance `d(x,y)` between Gram matrices (Eq. 2). However, in Section 4, the synthesis procedure minimizes a different objective function `E(y)` (Eq. 5), which is a squared relative error. These two formulations are not equivalent. The paper never acknowledges or justifies this switch. This is a critical methodological flaw. A principled approach would require using the defined metric as the objective function or providing a clear reason for using a different one.

2.  **Opaque and Unjustified Optimization "Tricks":** The authors state they "scale the loss and the gradients by a factor of 10^7 in order to avoid early stopping". This is a significant intervention in the optimization process. Why is this necessary? What is the sensitivity of the results to this exact value? This suggests that the optimization landscape is problematic or that the default L-BFGS-B tolerances are inappropriate for this task. Without a proper explanation or ablation study, this appears to be an arbitrary hack that raises questions about the stability and generality of the synthesis procedure and harms reproducibility.

3.  **Missing Experimental Details:** The paper lacks sufficient detail for other researchers to reproduce the work.
    *   For the `Kmeans` filters, the paper states patches were whitened, but fails to specify the whitening method (e.g., ZCA, PCA whitening).
    *   For `Random` filters, the paper cites "(Glorot & Bengio, )" with a missing year and does not specify the exact distribution used (e.g., the precise uniform or normal parameters derived from fan-in/fan-out).
    *   A strong claim is made in the discussion that "extensive experiments with various hierarchical architectures" were conducted and showed no quality improvement. No evidence (architectures, results, figures) is provided to support this claim, rendering it anecdotal.

4.  **Flawed Quantitative Evaluation:** The authors propose using the VGG-based loss as an "objective quantification of texture quality." However, they later report that for some textures, their random multi-scale model achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than the VGG-based model, which directly optimizes this loss. This paradoxical result fundamentally undermines the reliability of the proposed metric. While the authors commendably acknowledge this, they still rely on it as the primary quantitative support for their claims. Given the perceptual nature of the task, a small-scale human evaluation would have been a far more convincing and rigorous way to support claims of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art.

5.  **Poor Manuscript Preparation:** The reference list is riddled with severe formatting errors (e.g., `Gatys et al\.,? \Q2015\E, Gatys et al\.,`). This indicates a lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript and is unacceptable for a conference submission. In-text citations are also incomplete (e.g., "(Glorot & Bengio, )").

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite the issues raised, the core idea of this paper is valuable. The finding that random filters in a shallow network can produce high-quality textures is counter-intuitive and important for the community. I encourage the authors to revise the paper addressing the following points:

*   Reconcile the distance metric (Eq. 2) and the loss function (Eq. 5). Please use a consistent formulation and justify your choice.
*   Provide a thorough explanation for the 10^7 scaling factor. An ablation study showing its effect would be highly beneficial.
*   Fill in all missing experimental details to ensure the work is fully reproducible.
*   To make convincing claims about perceptual quality, a user study is strongly recommended.
*   The finding that single-layer model outputs can serve as better initializations for the VGG-based synthesis is very interesting. This point, currently in the appendix, could be brought into the main paper to better frame the discussion around optimization landscapes.
*   Thoroughly proofread the entire manuscript, paying special attention to formatting and the reference list.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the reason for the discrepancy between the distance metric defined in Equation (2) and the loss function minimized in Equation (5)?
2.  Could you provide the exact method used for whitening image patches for the k-means filters and the precise distribution from which the random filters were sampled?
3.  What analysis led to the choice of 10^7 as the scaling factor for the loss? How do the final results change if this value is, for example, 10^5 or 10^9?
4.  Regarding the claim that deeper random networks did not improve quality: could you provide details of at least one or two representative hierarchical architectures you tested and show their corresponding synthesis results?
