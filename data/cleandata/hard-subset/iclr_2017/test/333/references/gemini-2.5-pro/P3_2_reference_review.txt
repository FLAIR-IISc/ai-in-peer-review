### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the widely held belief that both deep, hierarchical architectures and features learned via supervised pre-training are essential for this task. The authors' central claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually indistinguishable from, and sometimes superior to, those produced by the state-of-the-art model of Gatys et al. (2015a), which uses a deep, pre-trained VGG-19 network.

The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It establishes a strong and surprisingly effective minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer, random-filter CNN.
2.  Through extensive experiments, it systematically compares various single-layer models with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and shows that random filters are highly competitive, and that a multi-scale filter bank is crucial for capturing structures at different ranges.
3.  It introduces a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model, providing a more objective evaluation tool than qualitative human inspection alone.
4.  It offers a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, critiquing the standard Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images and proposing a more practical view based on perceptual distance metrics.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that provides significant and surprising insights into the nature of texture synthesis. It conducts a rigorous and well-designed set of experiments to convincingly argue its main thesis. The findings have important implications, forcing a re-evaluation of what architectural components are truly fundamental for modeling natural image statistics.

**Key Reasons for Acceptance:**

1.  **Impactful and Surprising Main Finding:** The core result—that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained network—is counter-intuitive and impactful. It provides a powerful lesson in the value of questioning assumptions and establishing strong, simple baselines. This finding significantly advances our understanding of what neural networks learn and what properties are inherent to their structure.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors do not just present one random model; they systematically explore a wide space of single-layer architectures. The comparisons between different filter types (random, learned, engineered) and different scales are highly informative. The visual results presented in Figures 1, 2, and 4 are compelling and strongly support the paper's claims.
3.  **Methodological and Conceptual Contributions:** Beyond the main empirical result, the paper introduces a pragmatic quantitative evaluation metric (the VGG-loss) that can help standardize comparisons in the field. Furthermore, the discussion in Section 7 provides valuable conceptual clarity on the limitations of the MaxEnt framework for practical synthesis and the crucial trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this work lies in its simplicity and the clarity of its conclusion. By going "the opposite way" of most research—simplifying rather than adding complexity—the authors uncover a fundamental property of CNN-based texture models: the combination of a sufficiently diverse, overcomplete filter bank (even a random one), a point-wise non-linearity, and second-order statistics (the Gram matrix) is powerful enough to capture complex, multi-scale texture statistics.

The experiments are well-designed to isolate the contributions of different factors. The analysis in Figure 2, which ablates filter scale, convincingly demonstrates that the success of their best model ("Random multi-scale") stems from its ability to simultaneously model correlations at different spatial frequencies, a principle long-known in texture analysis but cleanly demonstrated here in the context of random CNNs.

The paper also highlights a subtle but important point about optimization. The finding that their single-layer model can sometimes achieve a lower VGG-loss than the deep VGG model itself (which directly optimizes this loss) suggests that the optimization landscape of deeper models is substantially more complex and prone to poor local minima. This is a valuable practical insight for anyone working on optimization-based image generation.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is already very strong, but the following points could help improve its clarity and impact:

1.  **Clarity on the Multi-Scale Model:** The "Random multi-scale" model is the star performer, yet its architecture is only vaguely described in Section 6. It would be beneficial to specify its exact architecture in Section 2 along with the other models. How many filters are used for each scale (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55)? Are the feature maps from different scales simply concatenated before computing the Gram matrix? This detail is crucial for reproducibility.
2.  **Elaboration on the Optimization "Trick":** The authors mention scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 to prevent early stopping in L-BFGS-B. This seems to be a critical implementation detail that enabled their success, contrasting with prior work that found random networks to be ineffective. A brief discussion on why this is necessary (e.g., related to the optimizer's tolerance parameters or floating-point precision issues with small gradients) and how this scaling factor was chosen would be valuable.
3.  **Discussion on the VGG-loss Metric:** While the VGG-loss is a useful contribution, the paper could be more explicit about its limitations. Since it is the objective function of the model being compared against, it is inherently biased. The authors astutely note cases where this metric fails to align with perception (e.g., for regular, man-made structures). A more consolidated discussion of these limitations would further strengthen the paper's argument for developing better perceptual metrics.
4.  **Variability vs. Similarity:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. The idea of using an inpainting task for evaluation (Appendix A, Figure 7) is very clever. This concept is interesting enough that it could be briefly mentioned in the main text as a promising direction for future evaluation protocols, as it directly probes a model's ability to capture the conditional distribution of texture patches.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the exact composition of the "Random multi-scale" model, specifically the number of random filters applied at each of the different spatial scales?
2.  Your finding that the single-layer model can sometimes find a "better" minimum for the VGG-loss than the VGG model itself is fascinating. As mentioned in the appendix, you tried initializing the VGG synthesis with your model's output. Does this approach consistently lead to lower final VGG-loss and/or better perceptual quality across a wide range of textures? This could suggest a powerful two-stage synthesis pipeline.
3.  Have you considered the effect of the number of filters in the random models? The paper compares 363 vs. 3267 filters. Is there a point of diminishing returns, and how does the required number of random filters compare to the number of filters in the VGG layers used by Gatys et al.?
