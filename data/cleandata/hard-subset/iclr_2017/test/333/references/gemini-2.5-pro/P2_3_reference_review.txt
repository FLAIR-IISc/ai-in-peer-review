### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumptions in the field, established by the influential work of Gatys et al. (2015a), that both deep, hierarchical network architectures and features learned via supervised pre-training are necessary. The authors' central claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with, and sometimes superior to, those produced by a deep, pre-trained 19-layer VGG network.

The paper's key contributions are:
1.  **A strong, minimal baseline:** It establishes that a single-layer CNN with random filters and Gram matrix statistics is a powerful model for texture synthesis, fundamentally questioning the necessity of depth and learned features for this task.
2.  **A comprehensive empirical evaluation:** The authors systematically compare a wide range of single-layer architectures, varying filter types (random, Fourier, K-means), filter sizes, and the number of feature maps, providing convincing evidence for their claims. The multi-scale random model is shown to be particularly effective.
3.  **A quantitative evaluation metric:** The paper proposes using the loss function of the VGG-based model (the "VGG-loss") as a proxy for perceptual quality, offering a quantitative method to complement the standard qualitative human inspection.
4.  **A thoughtful discussion:** The paper provides a nuanced discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and connects the practical aspects of texture synthesis to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework, highlighting the importance of the chosen distance metric.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that provides a surprising, impactful, and rigorously supported result. It serves as a model for how to conduct scientific inquiry in deep learning by questioning assumptions, simplifying models to their core components, and performing careful, controlled experiments.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The core discovery that a shallow network with random weights can rival a deep, pre-trained network is a highly significant contribution. It forces a re-evaluation of *why* the Gatys et al. model works so well, suggesting that the power may lie more in the general structure of convolutional feature spaces and the Gram matrix summary statistic rather than the specific features learned for object recognition. This provides a crucial insight into the nature of texture representation.
2.  **Methodological Rigor:** The paper's claims are backed by extensive and well-designed experiments. The authors do not just show one successful random model; they systematically explore a variety of filter initializations and architectures. The ablation study in Figure 2, which dissects the importance of multi-scale filters versus large receptive fields and the necessity of non-linearities, is particularly compelling and strengthens their conclusions considerably.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The strength of this paper lies in its clear hypothesis and the thoroughness with which it is tested. By going "the opposite way" of previous work (i.e., simplifying instead of adding complexity), the authors isolate the indispensable elements of the texture synthesis pipeline.

The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are striking. The "Random multi-scale" model consistently produces high-quality textures that are, to the naked eye, often indistinguishable from or even better than the VGG-based synthesis, especially for highly regular patterns like the brick wall. This visual evidence is the most powerful argument for the paper's claims.

The introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric, while not perfect (as the authors honestly acknowledge), is a valuable contribution. It attempts to make the evaluation more objective than simple side-by-side comparison. The authors' observation that their model sometimes achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than the model directly optimizing that loss is a fascinating result, pointing towards complexities in the optimization landscape of deep models that their simpler model avoids. This adds another layer of depth to their analysis.

Finally, the discussion in Section 7 is insightful. It correctly frames the problem not just as minimizing a loss function, but as navigating a trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. The connection to the Maximum Entropy framework and the argument for explicitly defining a perceptual metric and an equivalence set (the ε-environment) is a sophisticated take that elevates the paper beyond a simple empirical result.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to improve it further.

*   **Model Specification:** The "Random multi-scale" model is the hero of the paper, yet its precise architecture is not specified in Section 2 alongside the other models. It would be beneficial to explicitly state the different filter sizes used, the number of filters at each scale, and how their outputs are combined before computing the Gram matrix.
*   **Role of Optimization:** The paper notes that "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" and the "more robust optimization of the single-layer model" might be key factors. This is a crucial point that could be explored more deeply. Is the success of the shallow model primarily due to its representational capacity or its much simpler, better-behaved optimization landscape? A brief analysis of the loss curves or gradient norms during optimization could provide valuable insight.
*   **Computational Cost:** A practical consideration is the computational efficiency. A single-layer model with very large filters (e.g., 55x55) could be computationally more expensive than a deep network that achieves a large receptive field through pooling. A brief comment on the relative synthesis times would be a useful addition for practitioners.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide the exact architectural details for the "Random multi-scale" model used in the experiments? Specifically, what were the filter sizes and the number of feature maps per scale?
2.  You suggest that the simpler optimization landscape of the single-layer model may contribute to its success, allowing it to avoid local minima that affect the deeper VGG model. Have you performed any analysis to confirm this? For example, does the single-layer model converge more consistently across different random initial noise images compared to the VGG model?
3.  The paper makes an excellent point about the need to balance perceptual similarity and variability. The VGG-loss measures the former. How might you begin to quantitatively measure the latter? Is the proposed inpainting task in the appendix the most promising direction, and could you elaborate on how that might be implemented in a scalable way?
