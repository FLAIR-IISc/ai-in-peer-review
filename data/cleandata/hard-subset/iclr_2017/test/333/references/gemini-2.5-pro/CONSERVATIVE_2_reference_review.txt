### **Summary**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that state-of-the-art performance, as exemplified by Gatys et al. (2015a), requires deep, hierarchical representations from networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition tasks. The central contribution is the demonstration that a shallow, single-layer convolutional network with random, untrained filters can produce textures that are often perceptually comparable to those from the VGG-19 based model. The paper systematically explores various single-layer architectures, varying filter types (random, Fourier, learned via K-means) and the number of feature maps. It concludes that a multi-scale architecture with random filters is a critical component, outperforming single-scale variants. To quantitatively support their qualitative findings, the authors propose using the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model of Gatys et al. as a proxy for perceptual quality.

### **Decision: Reject**

I recommend rejecting this paper. While the core finding is intriguing and counter-intuitive, the submission fails to support its strong claims with a methodologically sound evaluation. My primary concerns are:

1.  **Circular and Unconvincing Quantitative Evaluation:** The main quantitative metric used to validate the proposed simple model is the loss function of the very state-of-the-art model (Gatys et al.) it aims to challenge. This approach is inherently circular and cannot serve as objective proof that the new model is "competitive" or "rivals" the baseline.
2.  **Insufficient Comparison with Established Baselines:** The paper focuses its comparison on the deep learning-based SOTA but omits a rigorous comparison against classic, highly influential parametric texture models like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000). Such a comparison is essential for contextualizing the performance of a simple, random-filter model.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**1. On the Evaluation Metric:**
The paper's primary quantitative evidence rests on using the VGG-based loss (Eq. 7) to evaluate textures generated by non-VGG models. This is a flawed methodology for proving superiority or competitiveness. The VGG-loss measures fidelity to a set of statistics derived from the VGG network; it is not a universally accepted, ground-truth measure of perceptual texture quality, but rather the objective function for one particular model.

The authors themselves report instances where their multi-scale random model achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than samples generated by the VGG model, which directly optimizes this loss. This finding does not necessarily imply the random model is perceptually superior. A more parsimonious explanation is that the optimization landscape for the proposed single-layer model is simpler and less susceptible to poor local minima than the deep VGG-based model. Therefore, the results may speak more to the relative ease of optimization than to the representational power of the underlying features. To claim that a model with random filters "rivals" one with carefully trained features requires independent, objective evidence, which is lacking here. The strong perceptual claims made throughout the paper demand a formal human study (e.g., psychophysical experiments) for validation, which is a standard requirement for work in this area.

**2. On the Choice of Baselines:**
The paper positions itself as a search for the "indispensable" elements of texture modeling. A central part of this investigation should involve placing the proposed method in the context of prior, established work. While Gatys et al. (2015a) is the correct contemporary SOTA baseline, the omission of any comparison to Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) is a significant weakness. The Portilla & Simoncelli model is arguably the most important pre-deep learning parametric texture model, relying on meticulously hand-crafted statistics of wavelet coefficients.

Given that the authors' proposed model also relies on a shallow filter bank (albeit a random one), a direct comparison is not only relevant but crucial. Does a single layer of random filters outperform the carefully engineered filter statistics of Portilla & Simoncelli? If so, by how much and on which textures? Without this comparison, it is difficult to assess the true contribution. The paper demonstrates that a simpler deep-learning-inspired model works surprisingly well, but fails to establish where this simple model stands relative to decades of research in parametric texture modeling.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the manuscript.

*   **Implementation Details:** The paper mentions scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7 to avoid early stopping in the L-BFGS-B optimizer. This seems like a critical implementation detail. Was this same scaling applied when optimizing the VGG-based baseline model? If the optimization hyperparameters were tuned differently for each model, it confounds the comparison, as performance differences could be attributed to the optimization procedure rather than the model's representational capacity. Please clarify the exact optimization setup for all models compared.
*   **Analysis of Multi-Scale Model:** The results in Figure 2 compellingly argue for the importance of multi-scale filters. However, the largest filter used is 55x55, which is quite large and computationally expensive for a single convolutional layer. A more efficient way to achieve large receptive fields is through a deeper, hierarchical architecture. The authors state that they "conducted extensive experiments with various hierarchical architectures" and found no quality improvement. This is a strong and important negative result that should be substantiated with data and details in the main paper or appendix, as it is central to the claim that depth is not "indispensable."
*   **Connection to Random Features Literature:** The surprising effectiveness of random filters has parallels in other areas of machine learning, such as the work on Random Kitchen Sinks (Rahimi & Recht, 2007). Situating the findings within this broader context would strengthen the paper's discussion and theoretical grounding.
*   **Variability vs. Similarity:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability is thoughtful. The proposed inpainting experiment (Appendix A, Figure 7) is an interesting idea for a more objective evaluation of variability. Including results from such a pilot study would significantly bolster the paper's claims.
