### 1. Summary

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges two prevailing assumptions in the field: that a deep, hierarchical network architecture and supervisedly trained features are necessary. The authors propose and systematically evaluate a range of simple baseline models, culminating in a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent, multi-scale filters.

The paper's primary contribution is to demonstrate, through extensive empirical evaluation, that this minimal model can synthesize textures that are often perceptually competitive with, and sometimes superior to, the state-of-the-art model from Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a 19-layer VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet. The authors conduct a thorough ablation study, comparing filters derived from various sources (random, Fourier basis, K-means, PCA) and investigating the impact of filter size, the number of feature maps, and non-linearities. To move beyond purely subjective evaluation, the paper introduces a quantitative quality metric based on the synthesis loss of the VGG-based model. Finally, the paper discusses the results in the context of maximum entropy models for textures, highlighting the practical importance of defining a perceptual distance metric rather than enforcing exact statistical constraints, especially for finite-sized images.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a compelling and counter-intuitive result: a single convolutional layer with random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis. This fundamentally challenges the community's understanding of why deep neural networks are effective for this task and establishes a powerful, simple baseline that provides significant insight into the core mechanisms of parametric texture modeling.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are substantiated by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments. The authors systematically compare a wide variety of single-layer architectures, isolating the contributions of different components (e.g., filter type, filter scale, non-linearity) and providing strong qualitative and quantitative evidence for their conclusions.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous methodology. It takes a well-established, complex model and systematically simplifies it to discover the essential ingredients.

*   **Novelty:** The central finding is highly novel and impactful. Prior work, including the seminal Gatys et al. (2015a) paper, suggested that random-weight networks failed to produce compelling results, attributing success to the hierarchical features learned during large-scale object recognition training. This paper convincingly refutes that conclusion, showing that with careful optimization, a much simpler architecture suffices. This forces a re-evaluation of the role of depth and learned features in generative texture models. The key insight appears to be that the Gram matrix computed over the outputs of a sufficiently diverse, non-linear filter bank is the primary driver of synthesis quality, rather than the specific, learned hierarchy of VGG.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The experimental setup is sound and the comparisons are fair. The texture model, based on Gram matrices of feature activations, and the L-BFGS optimization are standard for this line of work, allowing for a direct comparison of the feature extractors. The authors test an impressive variety of models (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) with different capacities (363 vs. 3267 features) and scales. The experiment in Figure 2, which isolates the effect of filter scale, is particularly effective in demonstrating why a multi-scale approach is crucial. Furthermore, the introduction of a quantitative metric using the VGG-based loss, while acknowledged as imperfect, is a laudable step towards more objective and reproducible evaluation in a field that often relies solely on visual inspection. The paper’s identification of the optimization procedure (specifically, scaling the loss) as a critical factor for success is a valuable technical contribution.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The research question is stated plainly, the methods are described with sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the results are presented clearly through well-organized figures. The discussion section provides a thoughtful interpretation of the empirical results and connects them to broader theoretical concepts in texture modeling, such as the Maximum Entropy framework, enriching the paper's contribution.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions that the single-layer model with large filters is computationally inefficient. Quantifying this would be helpful. A small table comparing the average synthesis time per image for the proposed "Random multi-scale" model versus the VGG-based model would provide readers with a concrete understanding of the trade-off between architectural simplicity and computational efficiency.
*   **Terminology for Evaluation Metric:** Using the "VGG-based loss" as a quality metric is a clever idea. However, the terminology might be slightly confusing, as the paper's main point is to move away from VGG for synthesis. Consider renaming it to something like "VGG Perceptual Distance (VPD)" to more clearly distinguish its role as an external evaluation tool from its role as an objective function in the baseline model.
*   **Details of the Multi-scale Model:** The composition of the "Random multi-scale" model is crucial to its success. While Figure 2 implies the scales used, it would be beneficial to explicitly state the filter sizes included (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55) and how the 1024 filters were distributed among these scales within the main text of Section 6.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The paper attributes the success of its random-filter model, in contrast to previous negative reports, to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically the scaling of the loss and gradients by 10^7. How sensitive is the synthesis quality to this specific scaling factor? Was this value found through systematic tuning, and does it need to be adapted for different models or texture types?
*   The results show that for some highly regular textures (e.g., brick wall), the multi-scale model achieves a lower VGG-based loss than the VGG model itself, which is paradoxical since the latter directly optimizes this loss. The paper suggests this relates to the VGG model losing variability. Could you elaborate on this? Does this imply the loss landscape for the VGG model is more complex, leading the optimizer to a poorer local minimum compared to what the single-layer model can generate?
*   The finding that initializing the VGG synthesis with a sample from the single-layer model yields superior results is fascinating. Does this suggest that the primary benefit of the deep VGG model lies in its representational capacity (i.e., its feature space defines a good perceptual metric), but its corresponding loss surface is difficult to optimize directly from a noise initialization?
