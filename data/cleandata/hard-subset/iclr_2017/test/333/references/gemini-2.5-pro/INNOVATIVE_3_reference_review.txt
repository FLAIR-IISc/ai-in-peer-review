### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. It challenges the widely-held belief, established by the influential work of Gatys et al. (2015a), that high-quality texture generation necessitates deep, hierarchical features from networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition datasets like ImageNet. The authors' core contribution is to demonstrate, through extensive experiments, that neither network depth nor supervised feature learning is indispensable. They propose a powerful yet simple baseline: a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters. The paper shows that this minimal model, particularly a multi-scale variant, can synthesize textures of remarkably high perceptual quality, often rivaling or even surpassing the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach. The work also introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for texture quality and provides a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, connecting their findings to the Maximum Entropy framework and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a bold, surprising, and fundamentally important result. Its primary value lies in its direct and effective challenge to the prevailing assumptions in a popular subfield of generative modeling. By demonstrating that a simple, random, and shallow architecture can be so effective, the paper forces a significant re-evaluation of what mechanisms are truly responsible for the success of neural texture models. This is not an incremental improvement but a conceptual breakthrough that simplifies our understanding of the problem and provides a crucial new baseline for all future work. The claims are substantiated with compelling visual evidence and a thorough set of ablation studies.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

*   **Exceptional Novelty and Impact:** The paper's central thesis is refreshingly counter-intuitive. In a field often characterized by a push towards deeper and more complex models, this work powerfully argues for the opposite: radical simplicity. The finding that random projections, when combined with a Gram matrix summary statistic, are sufficient to capture complex texture statistics is a profound insight. This result demystifies the texture synthesis process, suggesting that the magic may lie less in the learned, semantic features of VGG and more in the general properties of overcomplete, multi-scale filter banks and the stationarity imposed by the Gram matrix. This has major implications, suggesting that powerful priors for natural images might be achievable with far simpler and less data-hungry methods than previously believed.

*   **Strong and Convincing Empirical Support:** The authors substantiate their claims with extensive and well-designed experiments. The visual comparisons in Figures 1 and 4 are striking; the quality of textures from the random multi-scale model is undeniably high and, in several cases (e.g., the brick wall, grids), perceptually superior to the VGG-based method. The systematic comparison across different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and numbers of feature maps effectively isolates the key ingredients for success. The ablation study in Figure 2, which teases apart the contributions of different filter scales, provides a clear and intuitive explanation for why the multi-scale approach works best: it simultaneously captures statistics at fine, medium, and coarse granularities.

*   **Insightful Analysis and Discussion:** The paper goes beyond a mere presentation of results. The authors correctly identify a key detail—scaling the optimization loss—that likely explains the discrepancy between their success and previous failures with random networks. Furthermore, the discussion in Section 7 provides valuable conceptual framing. By highlighting the practical limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite images and reframing the problem in terms of a perceptual metric and an ε-environment, the authors connect their empirical work to deeper theoretical questions. This elevates the paper from a simple "look what I can do" result to a thoughtful inquiry into the nature of generative texture modeling.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While these points do not detract from my decision to accept, they could further strengthen the paper:

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** The use of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric is a commendable step towards more objective evaluation. However, as the authors note, it has its own biases and paradoxes (e.g., when the single-layer model achieves a lower VGG-loss than the model directly optimizing it). It would be beneficial to explicitly frame this metric as an imperfect *proxy* for perceptual similarity and perhaps include a brief discussion of cases where it clearly fails, reinforcing the argument that developing better perceptual metrics remains a critical open problem.

*   **Computational Cost vs. Model Depth:** The paper notes that the large filters in the multi-scale model are computationally inefficient and that deep networks are a more efficient way to achieve large receptive fields. The authors mention that they tried deep *random* networks but that quality did not improve. This is a very interesting negative result. Even a brief summary of these experiments (e.g., what architectures were tested, why the authors speculate they failed) in the appendix would be highly valuable, as it would proactively address a natural question from the reader.

*   **The Role of Overcompleteness:** The results show a clear improvement when moving from a smaller number of filters to a larger one. This suggests that a highly overcomplete set of projections is key. A short discussion on this aspect could be illuminating. Is the goal simply to have a basis so large that it captures all relevant local image structures, with the Gram matrix then enforcing the correct co-occurrence statistics?

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   The optimization trick of scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 appears to be a critical factor in enabling the synthesis from random networks. Could you provide more intuition on the optimization landscape? Does this scaling effectively increase the learning rate or prevent the L-BFGS optimizer from prematurely converging in what might be a very flat loss region near the initialization?

*   Regarding your experiments with deep random networks that did not yield improved quality: could you elaborate on the architectures you tried? Was the issue related to optimization challenges (e.g., vanishing/exploding gradients in an untrained deep network) or did the resulting summary statistics simply prove less effective than the single-layer multi-scale model?
