### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the fundamental components necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The authors challenge two widely held beliefs in the field: that successful synthesis requires (1) deep, hierarchical feature representations, and (2) features learned via supervised pre-training on large datasets. The central and surprising claim is that a very simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—is sufficient to generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art method of Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a deep, pre-trained VGG network.

The paper systematically evaluates a range of single-layer models with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and demonstrates that a multi-scale random filter model is particularly effective. To move beyond purely subjective evaluation, the authors propose using the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality. Finally, the paper provides a thoughtful discussion on the implications of these findings, touching on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and re-contextualizing texture synthesis within the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that provides a significant and surprising contribution to our understanding of texture modeling and generative methods. The core claims are supported by strong and thorough empirical evidence.

1.  **Significant and Counter-intuitive Finding:** The paper compellingly demonstrates that a single-layer network with random filters can serve as a powerful texture model. This finding is highly impactful as it questions the necessity of deep hierarchies and learned features for this task, suggesting that the architectural prior of a convolutional filter bank combined with second-order statistics is incredibly effective on its own. This provides a fundamental insight and establishes a crucial minimal baseline for the field.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experiments:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments that systematically deconstruct the problem of texture synthesis. The comparisons across different filter origins (random vs. learned vs. hand-crafted) and the ablation study isolating the importance of multi-scale filters are particularly well-executed and lead to clear, interpretable conclusions about what components are truly essential for success.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary strength of this work lies in its clear hypothesis and elegant experimental design. By "going the opposite way" and simplifying the state-of-the-art model, the authors reveal a core truth about texture modeling: for stationary signals like textures, a sufficiently rich, overcomplete, and multi-scale set of random projections is enough to capture the statistics needed for high-quality synthesis. This is a profound result that forces a re-evaluation of the role of deep learning in this domain; perhaps depth and training serve more as efficiency improvements and fine-tuners rather than essential ingredients.

The experimental support is robust. Figure 1 immediately showcases the impressive quality of the random filter models. The follow-up analysis in Figure 2, which demonstrates that combining multiple filter scales is crucial for capturing both fine-grained details and long-range structures, is a highlight of the paper. This analysis provides a clear explanation for the success of their proposed multi-scale model. Furthermore, the direct visual comparisons in Figure 4 against the VGG-based model are fair and effectively illustrate the competitiveness of their much simpler approach, particularly on regular or geometric textures.

The introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric, while acknowledged as imperfect, is a commendable step towards more objective evaluation in a field that often relies on subjective visual inspection. The authors' honesty about the limitations of this metric (e.g., the paradoxical cases where their model achieves a lower VGG-loss than the model directly optimizing for it) adds to the credibility of the work.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help further improve it. These are suggestions for refinement and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Role of Optimization:** The paper notes that scaling the loss and gradients was crucial, and that the simpler single-layer model may have a more favorable optimization landscape. This is a very interesting point. A little more discussion on the interplay between model complexity and ease of optimization would be valuable. Is it possible that the deep VGG model's performance is limited not by its representational power but by the difficulty of optimization, and that the single-layer model succeeds in part because it is easier to optimize to a good minimum?
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The authors correctly state that a single-layer model with large filters is computationally inefficient compared to a hierarchical model. Quantifying this would be helpful. A simple table or note comparing the typical synthesis time for the multi-scale random model versus the VGG model would give readers a practical sense of this trade-off.
*   **Sample Variability:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. To make this point more visually concrete, it might be helpful to show a few more random samples (e.g., 2-3 more seeds) for a single texture from both the proposed multi-scale model and the VGG baseline side-by-side. This would give a clearer impression of the diversity produced by each method.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  In the discussion, you mention testing various hierarchical architectures with random filters and finding no quality improvement. Could you elaborate on the types of architectures you explored? For example, did you test a VGG-like deep architecture where all convolutional filters were initialized randomly and kept fixed? This would be a critical experiment to fully disentangle the contributions of network depth from pre-trained weights.
2.  You report that initializing the VGG-based synthesis with textures from the single-layer model yields "superior synthesis results" (Appendix A). Does this initialization strategy also consistently lead to a lower final VGG-loss compared to starting from noise? This would provide strong evidence for your hypothesis that the deep model's optimization is prone to poor local minima.
3.  The choice of 1024 feature maps for the multi-scale model seems effective. How sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter? Is there a clear point of diminishing returns where adding more random filters ceases to improve perceptual quality?
