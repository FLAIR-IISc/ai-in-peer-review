### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical representations from supervisedly pre-trained networks (like VGG) are essential for this task. The authors propose a significantly simpler model: a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters. The core claim is that this minimal model, particularly a multi-scale variant, can generate textures that are perceptually competitive with, and in some cases superior to, the state-of-the-art method of Gatys et al. (2015a). To support this, the paper presents a wide range of experiments comparing different single-layer architectures (using random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned filters) and introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on the synthesis loss of the VGG-based model itself.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Insufficient and Flawed Evaluation Methodology:** The paper's primary claims of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art are supported by subjective visual inspection and a quantitative metric that is circular and potentially misleading. Using the VGG-based loss to evaluate a non-VGG model measures its ability to replicate VGG-based statistics, not necessarily its perceptual quality. The authors themselves note paradoxical results with this metric, which undermines its validity as a tool for comparison.
2.  **Overstated Claims without Rigorous Validation:** The assertion that a simple random-filter model challenges a well-established, deep, pre-trained model is a significant one that requires extraordinary evidence. The provided evidence, consisting of a curated set of qualitative examples, is insufficient. A formal human perceptual study is necessary to substantiate claims of comparable or superior quality, but is not provided.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's central premise is a strong and interesting one: that much of the complexity in current texture synthesis models may be unnecessary. The visual results from the random multi-scale model are indeed surprisingly compelling. However, the work falls short of the rigorous standards required to validate its claims against established methods.

My primary concern lies with the evaluation. The authors rightly note that evaluation is traditionally done by human inspection, and they attempt to introduce a more objective measure with the VGG-based loss (Eq. 7). This choice is methodologically unsound. The VGG-based loss is, by definition, the objective function that the Gatys et al. model optimizes. Using it to score a different model creates a circular argument. A low score for the proposed random model does not necessarily mean it produces a perceptually better texture; it means the statistics it generates happen to align well with the specific statistics captured by the VGG network's layers.

This flaw is made explicit in Section 6, where the authors report that for some textures, the single-layer model achieves a *lower* VGG-based loss than the VGG-based model which directly optimizes this loss. While they suggest this points to optimization issues in the deeper model, it more critically highlights the unreliability of their chosen metric for comparing disparate models. It suggests the optimization landscape is non-convex and that their model finds a different local minimum which happens to score well on the VGG metric, but this does not equate to a definitive improvement in quality. Without a principled, independent measure of perceptual quality, the quantitative results are difficult to interpret and cannot be taken as strong evidence.

Secondly, the qualitative evidence is not sufficient to support the paper's strong claims. While the random multi-scale model performs very well on highly regular or crystalline textures (e.g., brick walls, grids), the VGG-based model appears to retain more subtle and naturalistic detail in more stochastic textures. The claim of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art is not uniformly supported across the examples in Figure 4. Such a claim would need to be substantiated with a carefully designed psychophysical experiment (e.g., a two-alternative forced choice task asking participants which synthesis is more like the original). Relying on the authors' own visual assessment is insufficient for a rigorous comparison.

Furthermore, while the paper is positioned as finding a "minimal baseline," its comparison to other established baselines is lacking. For instance, the model of Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) is mentioned as the pre-DNN state-of-the-art, but no direct visual comparisons are provided in the main figures. A convincing demonstration would show the proposed random model, the VGG model, and the Portilla & Simoncelli model side-by-side.

In summary, while the paper presents a fascinating and counter-intuitive result, it fails to provide the rigorous evidence needed to endorse its conclusions. The work is more of an exploratory study or an interesting ablation than a validated replacement or competitor for established SOTA methods.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The finding that random filters can be this effective is genuinely interesting and valuable. The paper would be much stronger if it were framed as an analysis of *why* this is the case, rather than an attempt to claim SOTA performance. It serves as an excellent "sanity check" or minimal baseline for the field.
*   To truly validate the claims of perceptual quality, a user study is non-negotiable. This would provide the necessary objective evidence to support the visual arguments.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is thoughtful and raises important points. This could be a stronger focus of the paper. Exploring *why* the simpler optimization landscape of the single-layer model seems to produce more perceptually regular results on certain textures is a promising direction.
*   The tuning of the optimization procedure seems critical. The authors mention that scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 was necessary to avoid early stopping. This feels like a very brittle, ad-hoc hyperparameter. A sensitivity analysis or a more detailed explanation of this choice would improve the methodological rigor.

**Questions for Authors:**
*   Could you elaborate on the discrepancy reported by Gatys et al. (2015a) that random weights failed to generate interesting images? You attribute your success to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Can you specify what this tuning entails beyond scaling the loss, and why you believe it is the key factor?
*   Have you considered applying your VGG-loss metric to samples generated by established non-DNN methods like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000)? This would help calibrate the metric and better position your model relative to both older and newer baselines.
*   In Section 7, you mention that hierarchical architectures did not improve quality over a single-layer model in your experiments. This is a very strong and counter-intuitive claim. Could you provide more details on the architectures tested and the results that led to this conclusion?
