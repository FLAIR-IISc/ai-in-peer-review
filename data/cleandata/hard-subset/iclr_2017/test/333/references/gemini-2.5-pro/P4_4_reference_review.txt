### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal architectural requirements for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption, established by state-of-the-art methods like Gatys et al. (2015a), that both a deep, hierarchical network architecture and supervised pre-training on large-scale datasets are essential for success. The central claim is that a surprisingly simple model, consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters, can generate textures of a quality that is often comparable to, and sometimes surpasses, those produced by deep, pre-trained VGG networks.

The paper's key contributions are:
*   It establishes a strong and minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis, demonstrating that a single-layer, multi-scale convolutional network with random filters is sufficient to capture complex texture statistics.
*   Through extensive empirical evaluation, it shows that neither network depth nor supervised feature learning are indispensable for this task, fundamentally simplifying our understanding of the underlying mechanisms.
*   It conducts a systematic comparison of various single-layer architectures, exploring different filter types (random, hand-crafted Fourier, unsupervisedly learned via K-means/PCA) and quantities, concluding that a multi-scale random filter bank performs remarkably well.
*   It proposes using the synthesis loss from a VGG-based model as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality, offering a more objective evaluation metric than qualitative human inspection alone.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents compelling evidence for a counter-intuitive result: that a shallow network with random weights can rival a deep, expertly-trained network on the complex task of texture synthesis. This finding forces a re-evaluation of what components are truly critical for capturing natural image statistics and provides a much simpler, more fundamental explanation for the success of CNN-based parametric models.
2.  **Methodological Rigor:** The claims are substantiated by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments. The authors systematically ablate key components of the model (filter type, filter scale, non-linearities, number of features) to isolate the factors responsible for high-quality synthesis. This rigorous approach lends strong support to their conclusions.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear hypothesis and the rigorous way it is tested. By "going the opposite way" from the trend of increasingly complex models, the authors perform a valuable service to the community by establishing a minimal, yet powerful, baseline.

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is a straightforward adaptation of the Gatys et al. (2015a) framework, replacing the multi-layer VGG network with various single-layer architectures. The core mechanism—matching the Gram matrix of feature activations via L-BFGS optimization—is sound and clearly explained. The experimental design is excellent. Figure 1 provides a comprehensive comparison across a wide range of filter types, demonstrating the surprising efficacy of random filters. Figure 2 presents a crucial ablation study, effectively showing that the success of their best model stems from the combination of multiple filter scales, not merely a large receptive field, and that non-linearities (ReLU) are indispensable. These controlled experiments are scientifically robust and directly support the paper's central claims.

**Clarity and Novelty:** The paper is exceptionally clear in its motivation, methodology, and conclusions. The writing is direct and the figures are highly illustrative. The core finding is highly novel and impactful. While Gatys et al. (2015a) briefly reported that random networks failed to produce interesting results, this paper demonstrates the opposite, attributing the difference to careful optimization. This discovery fundamentally alters the interpretation of why deep CNNs work for this task, suggesting the key ingredients are an overcomplete, multi-scale, non-linear filter bank and second-order statistics, rather than learned hierarchical features. The introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric, while acknowledged as imperfect, is also a valuable contribution towards more reproducible and objective evaluation in this domain.

The discussion section is also a highlight, thoughtfully considering the implications of the findings, including the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and connecting the empirical work to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling. This demonstrates a deep engagement with the problem beyond just the empirical results.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Computational Analysis:** The paper notes that the single-layer multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filter sizes. It would strengthen the paper to quantify this. A simple table comparing the synthesis time (e.g., time per 100 iterations) and memory usage for the proposed random multi-scale model versus the VGG-19 model would provide a more concrete sense of the trade-offs.
*   **Failure Modes:** The presented results are very strong. However, a brief discussion of any observed failure modes would be enlightening. Are there specific categories of textures (e.g., quasi-crystalline patterns, textures with nested regularities) where the shallow random model consistently underperforms the deep VGG model? This would help delineate the boundaries of the proposed method's effectiveness.
*   **Optimization Details:** The paper mentions that scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 was important to avoid early stopping. This seems like a critical implementation detail that enabled the main discovery. Expanding slightly on this—for instance, explaining why the optimization might fail with smaller scaling factors—would be very helpful for reproducibility and for understanding the optimization landscape.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The success of the random multi-scale model is attributed to its ability to capture correlations across different spatial frequencies. Have you considered the relationship between the number of filters allocated to each scale and the quality of the results? Is the current distribution (equal number per scale) optimal, or could performance be improved by, for example, allocating more filters to finer scales?
*   In the appendix, you show that initializing the VGG-based synthesis with an image from the single-layer model leads to superior results. This is a fascinating result, suggesting the single-layer model's optimization landscape is perhaps smoother or less prone to poor local minima. Could you comment on whether you see this as a practical "warm-start" technique for more complex generative models, and what it implies about the relationship between the loss landscapes of the shallow and deep models?
*   The VGG-loss is used as a quantitative metric. How well does this metric correlate with human perceptual judgments? For instance, in Figure 4, the VGG-loss for the brick wall texture is lower for the VGG synthesis (0.015) than the multi-scale synthesis (0.021), yet the multi-scale result appears perceptually superior in its regularity. This suggests a potential misalignment. Have you considered a small-scale user study to validate how well the VGG-loss aligns with human preference?
