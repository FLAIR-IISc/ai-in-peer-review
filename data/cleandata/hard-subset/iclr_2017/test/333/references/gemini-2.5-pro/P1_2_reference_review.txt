### Review

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal components necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption, established by the state-of-the-art model of Gatys et al. (2015a), that both deep, hierarchical network architectures and features learned via supervised pre-training are essential for the task.

The central claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with, and sometimes superior to, those produced by a deep, pre-trained VGG network. The authors demonstrate this through extensive experiments, comparing various single-layer models with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and filter counts. They find that a multi-scale random filter model is particularly effective.

Furthermore, the paper makes two other notable contributions. First, it proposes a quantitative metric for texture quality, the "VGG-loss," which uses the objective function of the Gatys et al. model to evaluate synthesized images, providing a more objective alternative to purely qualitative human inspection. Second, it provides a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, connecting the practical results to the Maximum Entropy framework and highlighting the crucial trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability for finite-sized images.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Main Result:** The paper's core finding—that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained SOTA model—is a significant contribution. It provides a powerful and much simpler baseline for texture synthesis and forces a re-evaluation of what components are truly necessary for modeling natural image statistics. This result is counter-intuitive and has important implications for understanding feature representations in generative models.

2.  **Rigorous and Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors don't just show that one random network works; they systematically compare different filter initializations (random, learned, engineered), filter counts, and scales. The inclusion of ablation studies that demonstrate the importance of multi-scale filters and non-linearities further strengthens their conclusions.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis and the methodical way it is tested. By starting with the complex state-of-the-art model and systematically stripping away components (depth, learned weights), the authors convincingly isolate the core mechanisms at play.

The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling. The "Random multi-scale" model, in particular, produces textures of remarkably high quality. Its superior performance on regular, man-made structures (e.g., brick walls, grids) compared to the VGG-based model is a striking piece of evidence. The authors plausibly attribute this to a more well-behaved optimization landscape for the simpler, shallower model. This is further supported by the experiment in the appendix (Fig. 5) showing that initializing the VGG synthesis with the output of the single-layer model yields better results.

The introduction and use of the VGG-loss as an evaluation metric is a clever and practical contribution. While the authors are transparent about its limitations (i.e., its failure to align with human perception on certain textures), it represents a valuable step towards more reproducible and quantifiable evaluation in a field that has heavily relied on subjective visual assessment.

Finally, the discussion in Section 7 elevates the paper from a purely empirical study to one with theoretical depth. The authors' analysis of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and their critique of the direct application of the Maximum Entropy framework to finite-size images, is insightful. Their proposed reformulation—defining a texture model via a distance metric `d(x,y)` and an epsilon-environment—is a more pragmatic and realistic view of the texture synthesis problem.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened:

*   **Definition of the "Random multi-scale" model:** The architecture of the best-performing proposed model, the "Random multi-scale" network, is not formally defined in Section 2 along with the other models. It is introduced in the results section with only a mention of 1024 feature maps and receptive fields up to 55x55. For clarity and reproducibility, a precise definition (e.g., number of filters at each specific scale) should be included in the methods section.
*   **The Role of Optimization:** The authors mention that they scale the loss and gradients by 10^7 to avoid early stopping and suggest this may explain the discrepancy with prior work that found random networks to be ineffective. This is a crucial detail. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this point. Is the success highly sensitive to this specific scaling factor? A brief discussion or ablation on this hyperparameter could further solidify the claim that optimization subtleties, not just the model, were key to unlocking the potential of random features.
*   **Quantifying the VGG-loss Failure Cases:** The paper rightly notes that the VGG-loss sometimes fails to capture perceptual quality. It would strengthen the paper to quantify this observation, even anecdotally. For example, stating for how many of the 20 textures in Figure 4 the multi-scale model achieved a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-synthesis, or where the perceptual difference was most starkly at odds with the VGG-loss values.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide the exact architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model, specifying the different filter sizes used and the number of random filters allocated to each scale?

2.  You convincingly argue that depth and pre-training are not *indispensable*. However, the VGG-based model still appears perceptually superior on some of the more stochastic, less structured textures (e.g., the pebbles or flowers in Figure 4). Do you have a hypothesis for why the deep, learned features still hold an advantage in these specific cases?

3.  The discussion on perceptual similarity versus variability is excellent. Your model appears to excel at similarity, sometimes at the cost of variability (as hinted by the "exact reconstruction" anecdote). Have you experimented with deliberately stopping the optimization early as a simple heuristic to trade some similarity for increased variability, and if so, how did it affect the results?
