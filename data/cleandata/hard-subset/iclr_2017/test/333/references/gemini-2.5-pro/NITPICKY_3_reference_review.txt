### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The central claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a deep, pre-trained VGG network. The authors conduct a series of experiments comparing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and sizes. They argue that neither a deep hierarchical architecture nor supervised feature learning is indispensable for this task. The paper also introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality and concludes with a theoretical discussion on the limitations of the maximum entropy framework for finite-size images.

**Decision: Reject**

While the core finding that random filters in a shallow network can produce high-quality textures is surprising and potentially impactful, the paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological omissions, technical inconsistencies, and presentational flaws. The credibility of the central claims is undermined by a lack of crucial experimental details and unsupported assertions. The overall lack of rigor and polish makes the paper unsuitable for publication in its current form.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Critical Methodological Omissions:** The paper's best-performing model, the "random multi-scale" network, is central to the main claims of rivaling the state-of-the-art. Shockingly, this model is never defined in the methodology (Section 2). It appears for the first time in the results section (Section 6), leaving the reader to guess its architecture, filter sizes, number of feature maps, and how outputs from different scales are combined. This is a critical omission that makes a core part of the work entirely irreproducible and unverifiable. Furthermore, other methodological details are missing: the whitening procedure for K-means filters is unspecified, and the number of patches sampled for the "Kmeans-Sample" model is not stated, making it impossible to assess whether the model is learning statistics or simply memorizing patches.

2.  **Technical Inconsistencies and Errors:** The mathematical formalism presented is careless and inconsistent.
    *   **Equation Typos:** Equation (3) for pixel distance contains a blatant error in the summation index, using `N` (the number of feature maps) instead of `M` (the number of pixels). This demonstrates a lack of careful proofreading.
    *   **Inconsistent Definitions:** The definition of the distance metric `d(x,y)` in Equation (2) is inconsistent with the error function `E(y)` used for optimization in Equation (5) and evaluation in Equation (6). Equation (2) uses a normalization factor of `1/(||G(x)||_F * ||G(y)||_F)` and is not squared, whereas the error function in Equation (5) is a relative squared error normalized by `1/||G(x)||_F^2`. These subtle but important differences are not explained or justified, creating confusion about what is actually being optimized and measured.
    *   **Imprecise Notation:** In Section 2, the weights for the Fourier-3267 model are described as `wR, wG, wB ∈ [1, 0,−1]`, which incorrectly denotes an interval rather than the discrete set `{−1, 0, 1}`.

3.  **Unsupported Claims and Flawed Evaluation:** The paper makes several strong claims without providing the necessary evidence. In the discussion, the authors state, "We conducted extensive experiments with various hierarchical architectures... the quality of the synthesized textures does not improve." This is a significant finding that supports their minimalism argument, yet no results, figures, or even a description of these experiments are provided in the paper or appendix. Such "results not shown" are unacceptable. Furthermore, the decision to use the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is questionable. It is circular to use the objective function of the model you are comparing against as the primary evaluation metric. The authors themselves later undermine this choice by stating that for some textures, the "VGG-based loss fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis," which makes their quantitative evaluation framework appear self-contradictory and unreliable.

4.  **Poor Presentation and Lack of Focus:** The paper's structure and presentation are lacking. The lengthy discussion in Section 7 on the maximum entropy framework and `epsilon`-environments feels disconnected from the paper's empirical core and reads like an afterthought intended to add unearned theoretical depth. Most critically, the bibliography is in a state of disarray, filled with BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `\Q2015\E`), question marks, and inconsistent formatting. This level of carelessness in the final presentation suggests a rushed and unpolished submission.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The core idea is valuable. A revised manuscript should focus squarely on the empirical investigation of minimal texture models.
*   The "random multi-scale" model must be precisely and completely defined in the methodology section.
*   All mathematical equations and notation must be carefully reviewed for correctness and consistency. The relationship between the distance metric and the loss function must be clarified.
*   The "hack" of scaling gradients by 10^7 should be better justified. Was tuning the optimizer's own tolerance parameters insufficient? This detail suggests the optimization is brittle.
*   All claims must be supported by evidence. The results from the "extensive experiments" with deeper random networks must be included, at least in an appendix.
*   The discussion section should be revised to focus on the direct implications of the presented results, rather than broad, speculative theoretical arguments.
*   The entire manuscript, and especially the reference list, requires meticulous proofreading and formatting correction.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the complete architectural details for the "random multi-scale" model, including the specific filter sizes used, the number of filters per scale, and the mechanism for combining their feature maps before computing the Gram matrix?
2.  Can you clarify the discrepancy between the distance metric in Eq. (2) and the loss function in Eq. (5)? Why were different normalizations used? Which formulation was used for the optimization reported in the paper?
3.  Regarding the claim that the VGG-based synthesis optimization is "less prone to local minima," what evidence supports this? Could the paradoxical result where your model achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG model be explored more rigorously, perhaps by analyzing the loss landscape or running the VGG optimization from more random seeds?
4.  Can you provide a summary of the results from your experiments with deeper, hierarchical random networks that you mention in the discussion?
