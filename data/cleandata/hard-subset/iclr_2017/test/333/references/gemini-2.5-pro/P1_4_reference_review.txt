### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, a problem for which the state-of-the-art (at the time of writing) relies on summary statistics from deep, pre-trained convolutional neural networks (Gatys et al., 2015a). The authors challenge the prevailing assumptions that both the hierarchical multi-layer structure and the supervised pre-training of the network are essential for success.

The main contribution is the demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures of a quality that often rivals the deep VGG-based model. The paper systematically explores various single-layer architectures, comparing filters derived from Fourier bases, K-means clustering on natural images, and random initialization. It finds that a multi-scale random filter model is particularly effective.

Furthermore, the paper introduces a quantitative metric for texture quality, the "VGG-loss," which uses the loss of the state-of-the-art model as a proxy for perceptual distance. Finally, it provides an insightful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, critiquing the standard maximum entropy (MaxEnt) framework for finite-size images and proposing a more practical view based on a perceptual distance metric and an epsilon-environment of acceptable textures.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Empirical Finding:** The paper's central result—that a single layer of random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis—is a strong, counter-intuitive, and important finding. It forces a re-evaluation of why deep models are successful in this domain, suggesting that the core mechanism may be the generic structure of convolutional filter banks and Gram matrix statistics, rather than learned hierarchical features. This provides a powerful and minimal baseline for a fundamental problem in generative modeling.
2.  **Strong Conceptual Contribution:** Beyond the empirical results, the paper offers a valuable conceptual discussion in Section 7. The framing of texture synthesis as a trade-off between "perceptual similarity" and "variability," and the critique of the direct application of the MaxEnt framework to finite-size images, elevates the paper from a simple ablation study to a thoughtful piece of scientific work that clarifies the problem definition and its practical challenges.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's claims are well-supported by a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors compare their random filter models not only against the VGG-based SOTA but also against several plausible alternatives for single-layer filters (K-means, PCA, Fourier). This thorough comparison strengthens their conclusion that sophisticated, data-driven filters offer little to no advantage over random ones, a remarkable result. The visual evidence presented in Figure 1 and Figure 4 is compelling, clearly showing the high quality of the textures generated by the multi-scale random model. The results on highly structured textures like brick walls and grids are particularly impressive and visually support the claim that their model can sometimes outperform the VGG-based approach.

The introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric is a pragmatic and useful contribution. While the authors are rightly cautious, acknowledging its imperfections (as seen in cases where visual quality and the loss value diverge), it provides a more objective and reproducible evaluation tool than qualitative human inspection alone. Their honesty about the metric's limitations adds to the scientific rigor of the paper.

The discussion about the optimization process is also a key strength. The authors note that the simpler optimization landscape of a single-layer model may contribute to its success, as it is less prone to poor local minima. The suggestion that VGG-based synthesis can be improved by initializing from their model's output (Appendix A) is a clever piece of evidence supporting this hypothesis. This highlights a crucial, often overlooked, interplay between the model (the choice of statistics) and the synthesis algorithm (the optimization).

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Multi-Scale Model Architecture:** The "Random multi-scale" model is the paper's best-performing model, yet its architecture is not explicitly defined in the methods (Section 2) alongside the other models. It is introduced somewhat abruptly in the results. It would be beneficial to add a clear description in Section 2 specifying the different filter sizes used (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55) and how the 1024 total feature maps are distributed across these scales.
*   **Elaboration on Optimization Trick:** The paper mentions scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7 "in order to avoid early stopping." This is a crucial practical detail. A brief sentence explaining *why* this prevents early stopping (e.g., by keeping gradient norms above the optimizer's tolerance threshold for small loss values) would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding.
*   **Visualizing Variability:** The discussion on the "perceptual similarity vs. variability" trade-off is excellent. To make this point even more concrete, it would be powerful to include a figure (perhaps in the appendix) showing multiple different samples generated from the same source texture for both the multi-scale random model and the VGG model. This would allow readers to visually assess the variability of each method.
*   **Citation Formatting:** There appear to be some minor issues with the bibliography formatting (e.g., repeated author names, question marks). A proofread of the reference list would be beneficial.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model? Specifically, what are the filter sizes used and how many filters are allocated to each size?
2.  You argue that your single-layer model is less prone to local minima. Does this manifest as more stable optimization runs? For instance, do different random initializations for the synthesis procedure lead to more consistent final loss values with your model compared to the VGG-based model?
3.  The discussion on variability is very insightful. Qualitatively, did you observe that the samples generated from your multi-scale model were less diverse (i.e., more similar to each other) than those generated by the VGG model, as might be suggested by your model's better performance on highly regular textures?
