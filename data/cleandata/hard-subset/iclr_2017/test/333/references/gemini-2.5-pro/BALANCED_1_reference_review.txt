### **Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It critically re-examines the highly successful model of Gatys et al. (2015a), which uses statistics from a deep, pre-trained VGG network. While subsequent work has focused on adding complexity to this model, this paper takes the opposite approach, seeking a minimal model by asking which components are truly indispensable. The authors' central claim is that neither a deep hierarchical architecture nor features learned via supervised training are necessary. They demonstrate this by proposing a simple yet powerful baseline: a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. The texture is modeled by the Gram matrix of the feature activations from this single layer. Through extensive experiments, the paper shows that this minimal model, particularly a multi-scale variant, can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from the state-of-the-art VGG-based model. As part of their evaluation, the authors also propose a novel quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model, providing a more objective measure than visual inspection alone.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that provides a surprising and important result, backed by a thorough and systematic empirical investigation. It represents a significant contribution to our understanding of generative models for images.

1.  **Significant and Counter-intuitive Finding:** The core result—that a single layer of random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained network for texture synthesis—is a powerful and unexpected finding. It provides a crucial, simple baseline for the field and challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, learned hierarchies are a prerequisite for capturing complex natural image statistics. This kind of ablation is fundamental to scientific progress.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors do not merely present their best result but conduct a meticulous study of the design space. They compare various filter initialization strategies (random, learned, engineered), filter quantities, and scales. The ablation studies clearly isolating the importance of multi-scale receptive fields and non-linearities are particularly insightful. The introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative evaluation metric is a clever and valuable contribution in its own right.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous:

*   **Clarity of Hypothesis and Scientific Value:** The paper is motivated by a clear and fundamentally important question: "What does it take to generate natural textures?". By systematically stripping down a complex, successful model to its bare essentials, the work provides deep insight into *why* the model of Gatys et al. works. This analysis is far more valuable than a purely incremental improvement.
*   **Effectiveness of the Minimalist Model:** The proposed "random multi-scale" model is remarkably simple, yet its qualitative results (Fig. 4) are highly compelling. The fact that random filters perform on par with or better than filters learned unsupervisedly from natural images is a particularly striking result that warrants rethinking the role of feature learning in this context.
*   **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are well-supported by the evidence provided. The visual comparisons in Figures 1 and 4 are convincing. The systematic exploration of different model configurations provides a robust foundation for the conclusions. The quantitative analysis using the VGG-loss, while acknowledged as imperfect, adds a layer of objectivity that strengthens the paper's claims.
*   **Insightful Discussion:** The paper concludes with a thoughtful discussion section that connects the empirical findings to broader theoretical concepts, such as the Maximum Entropy framework. The authors' reflections on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and the limitations of existing frameworks for finite-sized images, are nuanced and elevate the paper beyond a simple empirical report.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Clarity on the "Random Multi-scale" Model:** The best-performing model, "Random multi-scale," is central to the paper's claims but is not described with the same level of detail as the other models in Section 2. It would be beneficial to add a precise description of its architecture. For example: How many filters are used at each scale (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55)? Are the feature maps from parallel convolutions concatenated before the Gram matrix calculation?
*   **The Role of Optimization:** The detail about scaling the loss by 10^7 to prevent early stopping is interesting. Is this also necessary for the baseline VGG synthesis? A brief comment on why this might be necessary (e.g., related to the magnitude of gradients in Gram matrix matching) could provide additional insight into the optimization landscape of these models.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The discussion mentions that hierarchical networks are more efficient for achieving large receptive fields. This is a key trade-off. A brief quantitative comparison of the synthesis time or FLOPs for the random multi-scale model (with its large 55x55 convolutions) versus the VGG model would make this point more concrete and the comparison between the models more complete.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to solidify my understanding:

1.  Could you please clarify the exact architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model, as mentioned in the feedback above?
2.  Your finding that initializing VGG-based synthesis with the output of your single-layer model yields superior results is fascinating. Does this suggest that the VGG model has a more expressive representation but is hindered by a more challenging, non-convex optimization landscape that the single-layer model navigates more effectively?
3.  You convincingly show that unsupervisedly learned filters (from K-means or PCA) offer no advantage over random filters. This is a very strong result. What is your primary hypothesis for why this is the case? Does it suggest that for the Gramian statistic, a large, diverse, overcomplete basis is more important than a basis that is "optimal" for representing natural image patches?
4.  The discussion on perceptual similarity vs. variability is excellent. As a simple experiment, have you tried using early stopping during optimization as a heuristic to increase variability, and if so, how did it affect the qualitative results?
