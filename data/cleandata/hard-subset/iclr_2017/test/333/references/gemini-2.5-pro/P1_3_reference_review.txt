### Paper Review

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that the success of state-of-the-art models, like that of Gatys et al. (2015a), is contingent on both a deep, hierarchical network architecture and features learned via supervised pre-training on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. The authors systematically demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a deep, pre-trained VGG-19 network.

The core contributions are:
1.  Establishing a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer, random-filter CNN.
2.  Showing through extensive experiments that neither network depth nor supervised feature learning are indispensable for this task.
3.  Demonstrating that a combination of filters at multiple spatial scales is crucial for capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, and that non-linearities (ReLUs) are essential.
4.  Proposing and utilizing a quantitative metric for texture quality, the "VGG-loss," to supplement subjective human evaluation.
5.  Providing a thoughtful discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture synthesis, framing the problem in the context of maximum entropy models and perceptual distance metrics.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that makes a clear, surprising, and valuable contribution to the field of generative image modeling. My decision to accept is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Simplification:** The paper's main finding—that a shallow network with random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis—is a significant result. It fundamentally reframes our understanding of what makes CNN-based texture models work, shifting the focus from learned hierarchical features to the inherent properties of the architecture (convolution, non-linearity) and the statistical summary (Gram matrix). It provides a crucial "Occam's razor" baseline that any more complex texture model should be measured against.
2.  **Rigorous and Insightful Experiments:** The authors support their claims with a thorough and well-designed set of experiments. They don't just show that random filters work; they compare them systematically against various alternatives (hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and ablate key architectural choices (filter size, number of filters, non-linearities). This rigorous approach convincingly isolates the essential ingredients for success.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this paper lies in its focused and methodical investigation. The central claim is counter-intuitive and goes against the grain of much of the deep learning literature, which often emphasizes the importance of depth and learned features. The authors provide compelling visual evidence across a wide range of textures in Figure 1 and Figure 4 to support this claim. The "Random multi-scale" model, in particular, produces impressively high-quality results that are often indistinguishable from or even superior to the VGG-based model, especially for textures with regular, long-range structures.

The ablation study presented in Figure 2 is particularly effective. It cleanly demonstrates that a combination of multiple filter scales is necessary to simultaneously capture local statistics and global structure, and that removing the non-linearity (ReLU) completely breaks the model. This provides a clear and interpretable explanation for why their proposed minimal model is successful.

Furthermore, the introduction and use of the VGG-loss as a quantitative evaluation metric is a commendable step towards more objective assessment in a field that has historically relied on qualitative inspection. The authors are honest about the metric's limitations, noting its failure to capture the perceptual quality of some regular textures, which adds to the credibility of their analysis. The finding that their simpler model can sometimes achieve a lower VGG-loss than the VGG model itself is a fascinating insight into the optimization landscape of these deep models.

Finally, the discussion in Section 7 is a highlight. It thoughtfully explores the tension between achieving perceptual similarity and generating sufficient variability, and correctly points out that practical synthesis algorithms operate not by finding exact statistical matches but by exploring an "epsilon-environment" around the target statistics. This provides a mature perspective on the theoretical underpinnings and practical realities of texture modeling.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

These points are intended to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Multi-Scale Model Architecture:** The paper is very clear about the single-scale models, but the exact architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model is not explicitly defined in Section 2. It is implied that it is a single-layer model with a collection of filters of different sizes (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55). Please clarify precisely how these filters are combined. Are they simply concatenated to form a single, larger set of filters in one convolutional layer? A sentence of clarification in Section 2 would be beneficial.
*   **Optimization Details:** The authors mention scaling the loss and gradients by a large factor (10^7) to prevent premature stopping of the L-BFGS optimizer. This is a critical detail for reproducibility. A brief note explaining *why* this is necessary (e.g., are the raw gradients too small for the optimizer's default tolerance?) would provide valuable insight for practitioners.
*   **Visualizing Variability:** The discussion touches on the important trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability. This could be made more concrete with a small visual experiment. Showing several different synthesized samples for the same source texture from both the proposed multi-scale model and the VGG model would provide a direct visual comparison of their respective variability.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper notes that the multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters. It would be helpful to provide a brief quantitative comparison of synthesis time (e.g., time per 2000 iterations) for the single-scale 11x11 model, the multi-scale model, and the VGG-based model to give the reader a concrete sense of the trade-offs.

**Questions for Authors:**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following:

1.  Could you please elaborate on the exact architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model? How are the filters of different spatial extents implemented and combined within the single convolutional layer?
2.  The discussion on perceptual similarity versus variability is excellent. In your experiments, did you qualitatively observe a noticeable difference in the diversity of generated samples between your single-layer models and the deep VGG model, as your discussion suggests?
3.  Regarding the VGG-loss metric, you note that for some regular textures, your model produces a lower loss than the VGG model that directly optimizes it. You attribute this to the VGG optimization getting stuck in local minima. Did you find that initializing the VGG synthesis with your model's output (as in Appendix A) consistently allowed it to find a better minimum (i.e., lower loss) than starting from noise?
