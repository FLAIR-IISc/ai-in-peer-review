### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing view that state-of-the-art results, such as those from Gatys et al. (2015a), depend critically on deep, hierarchical network architectures and features learned via supervised object recognition training. The authors make the surprising and significant claim that a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters is sufficient to synthesize textures that are often perceptually competitive with those generated from a 19-layer pre-trained VGG network.

The paper's main contributions are: (1) establishing a strong and simple baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer random-filter CNN; (2) demonstrating through extensive experiments that this baseline rivals state-of-the-art methods, suggesting depth and learned features may not be indispensable; (3) systematically comparing a variety of single-layer architectures, including those with random, hand-crafted (Fourier), and unsupervisedly learned (K-means, PCA) filters; and (4) introducing a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model, moving beyond purely qualitative human inspection.

### **Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to **Accept** this paper. The core reasons are:

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The central result—that a shallow network with random filters can effectively model complex natural textures—is a highly valuable contribution. It forces a reconsideration of the necessary ingredients for generative texture models and provides a crucial, simplified baseline for future research.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support:** The claims are substantiated by a thorough and convincing set of experiments. The visual results are compelling, and the systematic comparison across different filter types and model complexities provides a clear and insightful analysis of what factors are most important for synthesis quality.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous empirical investigation, which leads to an impactful conclusion.

*   **Disentangling Key Components:** The work successfully disentangles the contributions of network architecture (depth), feature representation (learned vs. random), and the statistical matching procedure (Gram matrices). By showing that the combination of a simple convolutional projection (even a random one) and second-order statistics is remarkably powerful, the paper clarifies that much of the representational capacity for textures lies in the statistical model itself, rather than requiring deeply nested, learned non-linearities.

*   **Thorough Investigation:** The authors do not simply present a single successful random model. They methodically explore the design space of single-layer models, varying the number of filters, the filter type (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), and the filter scale. This comprehensive approach strengthens their conclusion that random filters are not just viable but competitive. The finding that a multi-scale random filter bank performs best is particularly insightful, demonstrating that capturing correlations across different spatial frequencies is key, a principle that can be achieved without a deep hierarchy.

*   **Quantitative Evaluation:** While acknowledging its limitations, the introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a commendable step towards more objective evaluation in this field. The authors are intellectually honest about the circularity of using the VGG model's own loss for evaluation and wisely report cases where their simpler model paradoxically achieves a lower loss, prompting a thoughtful discussion on the optimization landscape and the nature of perceptual similarity.

*   **Insightful Discussion:** The discussion extends beyond the core results to contemplate important related concepts. The authors' reflections on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, the computational aspects of shallow vs. deep models, and the limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images add significant depth and context to the work.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Clarifying the "Multi-Scale" Model:** The architecture of the "random multi-scale" model, which is central to the best results, is not explicitly defined in Section 2. Please clarify how filters of different scales are combined. Is it a single convolutional layer that uses a concatenated bank of filters with varying kernel sizes (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55)? A more precise definition would improve the paper's clarity and reproducibility.

*   **Substantiating the Optimization Claim:** The paper attributes its success with random networks, in contrast to previous reports, to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." However, the only specific detail provided is scaling the loss and gradients. While this is a useful practical trick, calling it "elaborate tuning" may be an overstatement. It would be more impactful to either provide more detail on other crucial optimization settings or to frame this contribution more directly as a key practical insight for stabilizing the synthesis process.

*   **Visualizing Variability:** The discussion on sample variability is excellent. To visually support this point, it could be beneficial to show a few more samples generated from different random initializations for a single texture, perhaps comparing the multi-scale model against the VGG model. This would give readers a more intuitive feel for the diversity of outputs from each model.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate your response to the following questions to help clarify my understanding:

1.  Could you elaborate on the optimization procedure? You mention that scaling the loss was a key factor. Were there any other modifications to the L-BFGS-B optimization (e.g., learning rate, momentum, initialization strategies) that you found were critical to successfully generating high-quality textures from random-filter networks?
2.  Could you please provide a precise architectural description of the "random multi-scale" model used to generate the results in Figures 1, 2, and 4?
3.  Given that the VGG-loss sometimes fails to align with perceptual quality for highly regular textures, have you considered supplementing your evaluation with a small-scale human preference study, even for a subset of the textures, to further validate that your model is indeed competitive with or superior to the VGG-based approach?
