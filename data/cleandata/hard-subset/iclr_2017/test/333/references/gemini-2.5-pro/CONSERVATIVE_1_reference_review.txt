### **Review**

**Summary of the paper:**
This paper investigates the minimal requirements for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption, established by Gatys et al. (2015a), that a deep, hierarchical network pre-trained on a supervised task is essential for this task. The authors propose a significantly simpler model based on a single convolutional layer. They demonstrate, through extensive experiments, that even with random, data-independent filters, this shallow architecture can generate textures that are visually comparable, and in some cases superior, to those produced by the 19-layer VGG network. The key contributions include establishing this simple random-filter model as a strong baseline, analyzing the impact of filter size and type, and introducing a multi-scale variant that proves particularly effective. The authors also propose using the loss function from the VGG-based model as a quantitative metric for texture quality, supplementing the standard qualitative visual inspection.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The claims of "rivaling state-of-the-art approaches" are not substantiated by a sufficiently rigorous evaluation framework. The primary quantitative metric proposed (the VGG-based loss) is demonstrated to be unreliable by the authors' own results, and the qualitative visual evidence, while intriguing, is subjective and does not consistently support the claim of comparable or superior quality across diverse textures.
2.  The methodological contribution is undermined by its practical limitations. The best-performing proposed model relies on computationally inefficient large-kernel convolutions, a problem that the established deep, hierarchical models are specifically designed to overcome efficiently. The paper argues that deep hierarchies are not *necessary* but fails to argue that they are not *preferable*.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision:**

The central thesis of this paper is a bold one: that the depth and supervised training of models like VGG are not "indispensable" for texture synthesis. While the results showing the surprising effectiveness of a single layer of random filters are interesting, the paper's claims of competitiveness with established state-of-the-art methods are not convincingly supported.

My primary concern lies with the evaluation methodology. The authors rightly note that evaluation is typically done by subjective human inspection and attempt to introduce a more objective measure by using the reconstruction cost of the VGG-based model (Gatys et al., 2015a). However, this choice of metric is problematic. The paper reports that for some textures, the authors' multi-scale model achieves a *lower* VGG-based loss than the VGG-based model itself, which directly optimizes this very loss function. This is a paradoxical result that severely undermines the validity of the metric for comparing these two different architectures. It suggests that the metric is either not a faithful proxy for perceptual quality or that the optimization of the baseline VGG model is fundamentally flawed, making a fair comparison of the models' representational power impossible. Without a reliable quantitative metric, the paper falls back on subjective visual assessment. While the results in Figure 4 for the multi-scale model are impressive for highly regular patterns (e.g., brick wall, grids), the VGG model appears to produce more faithful results for more stochastic and complex textures (e.g., the flowery pattern, pebbles). The claim of "rivaling" the state-of-the-art is therefore not consistently demonstrated. A formal psychophysical study would be required to make such a strong claim.

Secondly, the paper positions its model as a "minimal baseline," but this minimality is purely conceptual, not practical. The best-performing model, the random multi-scale network, uses filters with sizes up to 55x55. Such large convolution kernels are notoriously inefficient. The established paradigm of using deep stacks of small-kernel convolutions (e.g., 3x3) was developed precisely to achieve large receptive fields in a computationally and parameter-efficient manner. The authors acknowledge this, stating that a hierarchical net would be more efficient. This admission detracts from the significance of the proposed method as a practical alternative. The work successfully shows that a large, diverse set of random projections followed by a Gram matrix calculation is a powerful statistical descriptor, but it does not present a compelling case for abandoning the efficient, hierarchical architectures that are standard practice.

Finally, the paper hints that the improved performance on some textures may be due to a simpler optimization landscape for the single-layer model. This is a plausible explanation. However, this reframes the contribution from one about representational power ("what it takes to generate textures") to one about optimization dynamics. If the established VGG-based method is simply getting stuck in poor local minima, the appropriate solution is to improve its optimization, not necessarily to discard the model itself. The experiment showing that VGG synthesis improves when initialized from the single-layer model's output supports this interpretation.

**Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper:**

*   The core finding—that a single layer of random filters can be this effective—is genuinely surprising and valuable. The paper would be stronger if it were framed more as an analysis and ablation study that provides insight into *why* the Gatys et al. model works, rather than an attempt to propose a competitive new method.
*   The discussion in Section 7 regarding the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and the formalization of texture synthesis in terms of a perceptual metric `d(x,y)`, is insightful. This could be developed further and made a more central part of the paper's contribution.
*   The ablation study on the importance of filter scale and non-linearities (Figure 2) is well-executed and provides clear, valuable insights.
*   Instead of relying on the flawed VGG-loss metric, consider either conducting a small-scale user study (e.g., two-alternative forced choice test) to formalize the perceptual evaluation or toning down the claims of SOTA-competitiveness.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the VGG-loss paradox? Do you believe this invalidates the loss for comparing different generator architectures, or does it point to a systematic failure in the optimization of the deeper VGG-based model that goes beyond standard initialization issues?
*   You argue that your model is less prone to local minima. How can you disentangle the effects of the model's representation from the effects of the optimization dynamics? Could the VGG model, with a more sophisticated optimization scheme, consistently outperform your single-layer model?
*   Given the computational cost of large kernels, what is the envisioned practical use case for this model over an efficient, deep, hierarchical network?
