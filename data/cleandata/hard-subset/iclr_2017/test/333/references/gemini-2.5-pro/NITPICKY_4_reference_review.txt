### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that state-of-the-art results, such as those from Gatys et al. (2015a), depend critically on deep, hierarchical representations from networks trained on large-scale object recognition tasks. The primary contribution is a compelling demonstration that a simple, single-layer convolutional network with random, untrained filters can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a 19-layer VGG network. The paper systematically explores various single-layer architectures, comparing filters derived from different principles (random, Fourier basis, k-means). Furthermore, it proposes using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality and includes a thoughtful, if somewhat speculative, discussion on the limitations of the maximum entropy framework for finite-sized images.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Reproducibility:** The paper's central claims hinge on a series of experiments, but critical methodological details are either omitted or described ambiguously. This is most apparent in the description of the optimization procedure, where a "magic number" scaling factor is introduced without justification, and in the setup of the baseline models (e.g., whitening procedure for k-means).
2.  **Insufficiently Careful Manuscript Preparation:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of formatting errors, inconsistencies, and a completely corrupted reference section. This demonstrates a lack of attention to detail that undermines confidence in the scientific diligence with which the work was conducted.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

While the core thesis of this paper is provocative and potentially significant, the execution and presentation are not yet at a level suitable for publication at ICLR.

**Methodological Ambiguities:**
*   **Optimization:** In Section 4, the authors state they "scale the loss and the gradients by a factor of 10^7 in order to avoid early stopping." This is a crucial detail, as the authors themselves suggest that their success, where prior work with random networks failed, is due to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." However, this scaling factor is presented without any analysis or justification. How was this value chosen? Is the synthesis quality sensitive to this parameter? This ad-hoc tuning lacks scientific rigor and makes the results difficult to interpret and reproduce. Furthermore, stating that L-BFGS-B parameters are left at their "default value" is insufficient for reproducibility, as defaults vary across implementations.
*   **Model Descriptions:** In Section 2, the description of the models lacks precision. For the `Kmeans` models, the paper mentions that patches are whitened, but the specific whitening method (e.g., ZCA) is not specified. The citation provided for k-means, (Rubinstein et al., 2009), appears to be for K-SVD, which is not the same algorithm. For the `Random` models, the paper cites "(Glorot & Bengio, )" but fails to specify the exact uniform distribution used, which is a key part of that initialization scheme.
*   **Evaluation Metric:** The use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative quality metric (Section 5) is circular. The paper argues that the VGG network is not indispensable, yet uses it as the arbiter of quality. The authors astutely note the "paradox" that their simpler model can achieve a lower VGG loss than the VGG-based synthesis itself, correctly attributing this to optimization challenges. However, this finding should have prompted a more critical discussion of the metric's validity, rather than its general acceptance as an "acceptable approximation."

**Presentation and Formatting Flaws:**
*   **References:** The reference section is unusable in its current state. Nearly every entry is corrupted with extraneous characters (e.g., `\.,? \Q2016\E,`) and repeated author blocks. This is a major oversight that should have been caught by any level of proofreading.
*   **Citations:** The in-text citation style is often clumsy, e.g., `(Simonyan & Zisserman (2014); in the following referred to as VGG network)`. The footnote for the VGG implementation relies on an "accessed on" date, which is not a stable reference for a scientific artifact; a specific commit hash should be provided.
*   **Clarity and Consistency:** The paper introduces a normalized distance metric `d(x,y)` in Equation 2 but then uses a different (though related) loss function `E(y)` in Equation 5 for the actual synthesis. The purpose of defining `d(x,y)` is never made clear, adding unnecessary confusion. The title and section headings use all-caps, which deviates from standard formatting conventions.

Collectively, these issues, ranging from minor formatting choices to major methodological gaps and a broken reference list, create an impression of a rushed and unpolished submission. Even with a novel core idea, the sheer number of these flaws makes it difficult to endorse the paper.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The central idea in this paper is valuable, and I encourage the authors to revise and resubmit. The following points are meant to be constructive:

*   **Rigorize the Methodology:** Provide precise, unambiguous details for all experimental steps. Justify the choice of the 10^7 scaling factor, perhaps with a small ablation study showing its effect. Specify the L-BFGS-B implementation and its parameters. Clarify the whitening procedure and correct the k-means citation.
*   **Strengthen the Evaluation:** Acknowledge the circularity of the VGG-loss metric more directly. The finding that a simpler model can better optimize this loss is interesting and deserves more prominent placement and discussion. While human studies are expensive, they remain the gold standard. A small-scale user study comparing the best single-layer model to the VGG baseline on a few representative textures would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Refine the Manuscript:** Thoroughly proofread the entire paper. Re-compile the bibliography to fix the reference section. Streamline the citation style. The lengthy discussion in Section 7 on the MaxEnt framework, while interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the empirical core of the paper and could be condensed to improve focus.
*   **Figures:** The results are discussed entirely through references to figures that were not included in the provided text. In a revision, ensure these figures are clear, well-labeled, and effectively support the claims made in the text.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you provide the specific implementation (e.g., SciPy) and parameters used for the L-BFGS-B optimizer?
*   Could you elaborate on the exact whitening transformation applied to the image patches before k-means clustering?
*   What is the sensitivity of the synthesis quality to the `10^7` loss scaling factor? What happens if this value is `10^5` or `10^9`?
*   The VGG-based model was modified to use average-pooling instead of max-pooling. What was the motivation for this change, and how does it affect the resulting textures compared to the original formulation by Gatys et al.?
