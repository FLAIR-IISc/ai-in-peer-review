### Summary of the Paper

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumptions that both deep, hierarchical network architectures and features learned via supervised pre-training are necessary for this task. The authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art model of Gatys et al. (2015a) by exploring a range of much simpler models. Their core contribution is demonstrating that a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a deep, 19-layer VGG network pre-trained on ImageNet. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical study comparing various filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), filter quantities, and filter scales. It establishes a strong minimal baseline—a single-layer network with random multi-scale filters—and introduces the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality to complement qualitative human inspection. Finally, the paper offers a nuanced discussion on the interplay between summary statistics, optimization, and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture modeling.

### Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant and Surprising Core Finding:** The paper's central discovery—that a surprisingly simple model consisting of a single layer of random convolutional filters can generate high-quality textures—is a fundamental contribution. It challenges the community's understanding of what is necessary for generative texture modeling and provides a crucial insight that the power may lie more in the statistical summary (the Gram matrix over a convolutional feature space) than in the learned, hierarchical nature of the features themselves.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Investigation:** The authors substantiate their claims with a comprehensive and well-designed set of experiments. They systematically explore the design space of simple models and include critical ablation studies that isolate the importance of factors like filter scale and non-linearities. The comparisons are fair and the results are presented clearly, providing strong evidence for the paper's conclusions.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis and rigorous methodology. It takes a complex, high-performing model and asks a simple, powerful question: "What parts of this are truly essential?"

The empirical evaluation is exemplary. Instead of just showing that one random model works, the authors compare a wide variety of single-layer architectures. This includes different methods for generating filters (random, hand-crafted Fourier basis, unsupervisedly learned k-means/PCA) and varying the number of filters. This breadth makes their conclusion—that purely random filters perform on par with or better than others—much more convincing. The ablation study in Figure 2, which teases apart the effect of filter scale, is particularly insightful. It clearly demonstrates that a combination of different receptive field sizes is key to capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, directly supporting their choice of a multi-scale random model as their strongest baseline.

Furthermore, the introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric, while not perfect, is a pragmatic step towards more objective evaluation in a field that often relies solely on subjective visual assessment. The authors are commendably transparent about the metric's limitations, even highlighting the paradoxical cases where their model scores better on the VGG loss than the VGG model itself. This observation insightfully points towards the role of the optimization landscape, suggesting that the simpler single-layer model is less prone to poor local minima.

The work is well-positioned and provides a valuable counterpoint to the trend of building ever-deeper and more complex models. By establishing a strong, minimal baseline, this paper forces the community to reconsider the sources of success in neural generative models and provides a more solid foundation for future work.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

1.  **Clarification on Optimization:** In the introduction, you suggest that your success with random networks, in contrast to earlier reports, originates from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." However, Section 4 states that L-BFGS-B was used with default parameters, except for a scaling factor of 10^7 applied to the loss and gradients. This scaling factor seems to be the crucial trick. It would be very helpful to briefly explain *why* this scaling is necessary. Is it to ensure the gradients have sufficient magnitude to avoid premature termination by the optimizer's tolerance settings? A sentence of clarification would strengthen the paper's reproducibility and insight.
2.  **Discussion of the Quantitative Metric:** The use of the VGG-loss as a metric is clever but also circular, as it measures performance using the very system you are comparing against. It would be beneficial to frame this more explicitly. You could state that this metric quantifies "perceptual similarity as defined by the VGG feature space" rather than as a universal ground truth. This would add nuance and precision to the interpretation of your quantitative results.
3.  **Empirical Analysis of Variability:** The discussion in Section 7 regarding the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent and shows deep thought. The paper could be strengthened by adding a small, simple quantitative analysis to accompany this discussion. For instance, you could generate multiple samples for a given texture from different random initializations and report a simple metric of diversity, such as the average pixel-wise standard deviation across the generated set. This would provide some empirical data to ground the otherwise theoretical discussion of variability.
4.  **Efficiency as a Motivation for Depth:** You correctly note in the discussion that while deep architectures are not necessary for quality, they are a more computationally efficient way to achieve large receptive fields than using large kernels. This is a very important point that could be highlighted more prominently, perhaps even in the introduction or abstract. It elegantly reframes the role of depth as one of practical efficiency rather than fundamental necessity for this task.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate your response to the following questions to help clarify my understanding:

1.  Could you elaborate on the role of the 10^7 scaling factor in the optimization? Was this the primary change that enabled successful synthesis with random networks? Do you have a hypothesis for why this might be particularly critical for random-filter models?
2.  You note that your multi-scale model sometimes achieves a lower VGG-based loss than the VGG model that directly optimizes this loss. You suggest this is due to the simpler optimization landscape of your model. Have you experimented with other optimizers (e.g., Adam) for the VGG-based synthesis to see if this is an issue specific to L-BFGS in deep, complex energy landscapes?
3.  Your discussion of the similarity-variability trade-off is very insightful. Do you have any thoughts on how the choice of summary statistics itself (i.e., second-order statistics via the Gram matrix) might inherently bias the synthesis towards perceptual similarity at the expense of variability, independent of the optimization process?
