### 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper investigates the minimal requirements for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge two widely-held assumptions from prior work: that a deep, hierarchical representation and supervised pre-training on natural images are essential for this task. The paper's primary claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can synthesize textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art method based on a 19-layer pre-trained VGG network.

The main contributions are:
*   Establishing a strong and minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis that does not require network depth or learned features.
*   Presenting extensive empirical results showing that a single-layer network with random multi-scale filters can generate high-quality textures, often rivaling the VGG-based model.
*   Conducting a systematic comparison of various single-layer architectures, testing different filter types (random, hand-crafted Fourier basis, unsupervisedly learned via k-means/PCA), filter sizes, and quantities.
*   Introducing the use of the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model as a quantitative, albeit imperfect, proxy for perceptual quality, moving beyond purely subjective visual inspection.
*   Providing a thoughtful discussion that connects the empirical findings to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling, highlighting the importance of the chosen perceptual distance metric for finite-sized images.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper demonstrates convincingly that deep, pre-trained hierarchical features are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. The success of a simple, single-layer random filter model is a strong, counter-intuitive result that provides a crucial new baseline and forces a re-evaluation of the principles underlying generative texture models.
2.  **Rigorous and Systematic Empirical Support:** The central claim is supported by a comprehensive set of experiments that systematically ablate key components of the model. The authors thoroughly investigate the impact of filter type, filter scale, and non-linearities, providing clear evidence that a multi-scale representation and non-linear activation functions are critical, while filter training and network depth are not.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision
The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis, rigorous methodology, and impactful conclusion. It follows a classic scientific approach of simplification: taking a complex, successful model and stripping it down to its essential components.

**Technical Soundness:** The methodology is sound and clearly described. The core texture model, based on matching Gram matrices of feature activations, is a direct adaptation of the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a). The novelty lies not in a complex new technique but in applying this method to a radically simplified architecture. The experiments are well-controlled and provide compelling evidence. The comparison across different filter types (Random, K-means, PCA, Fourier) in Figure 1 effectively shows that random filters are surprisingly competitive, decoupling synthesis quality from features learned from natural image statistics. The ablation study on filter scale in Figure 2 is particularly strong, clearly demonstrating that combining multiple scales is crucial for capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, and that neither small nor large filters alone are sufficient. The inclusion of an ablation on the ReLU non-linearity further solidifies the analysis of which components are indispensable.

**Novelty and Impact:** The central finding is highly novel and impactful. At the time of this work, the prevailing narrative credited the success of neural texture synthesis to the rich, hierarchical features learned by deep networks trained for object recognition. This paper refutes that narrative, suggesting instead that a sufficiently overcomplete, multi-scale, random convolutional filter bank, when combined with second-order statistics (the Gram matrix), provides a powerful enough image representation for this task. This result simplifies the problem and provides a much more accessible and computationally lighter baseline for future research. Furthermore, the introduction and use of the VGG-loss as a quantitative evaluation metric, despite its noted flaws, represents a valuable step towards more objective and reproducible evaluation in the field.

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The motivation is laid out clearly in the introduction, the models and methods are described precisely, and the results are presented logically with both qualitative and quantitative support. The discussion section is a particular highlight, thoughtfully interpreting the results and connecting them to deeper theoretical concepts like the Maximum Entropy framework and the practical challenges of modeling finite-size textures. This elevates the paper from a simple empirical report to a more profound contribution to the understanding of generative image models.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Questions:**
1.  **Optimization Details:** You mention that scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7 was critical to avoid early stopping. This seems like a crucial implementation detail that enabled your success where others had previously reported failure with random networks. Could you elaborate on your hypothesis for why this scaling is so important? Is it due to the numerical precision of the optimizer, or does it relate to the gradient magnitudes being fundamentally smaller in a shallow network compared to a deep one?
2.  **Multi-Scale Model Architecture:** The "Random multi-scale" model is shown to be your best-performing model, but its specific architecture is not detailed in Section 2. Figure 2 implies it uses filters of size 3x3, 11x11, and 55x55. Could you please clarify the exact architecture? Specifically, how are the 1024 feature maps distributed across these different filter scales?
3.  **VGG-Loss Paradox:** You note that for some textures, the VGG-loss is lower for your multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model, which directly optimizes this loss. This strongly suggests the optimization landscape for the deep VGG model is much more challenging, with many poor local minima. To what extent do you believe your model's success is due to its superior performance versus the VGG model's failure to find a good optimum? Is it possible that the single-layer model's simpler optimization landscape allows it to find solutions that are coincidentally also good in the VGG feature space?
4.  **Sample Variability:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. While Figure 3 shows some sample diversity, it would be more impactful to directly visualize this. Could you consider adding a figure that shows, for a single reference texture, a 2x2 grid of samples generated from different random initializations for both your multi-scale model and the VGG model? This would provide a clearer qualitative comparison of the variability produced by each method.
