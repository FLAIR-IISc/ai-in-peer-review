This paper investigates the minimal architectural components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption in the field that the success of models like Gatys et al. (2015a) relies critically on both the deep, hierarchical architecture and the features learned through supervised pre-training on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. The authors convincingly demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually on par with, and occasionally superior to, those produced by the 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network. The key contributions include establishing this strong, minimal baseline; a systematic ablation study of different filter types, sizes, and quantities; the proposal of using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality; and a thoughtful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis concerning the Maximum Entropy framework.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper with a clear, surprising, and impactful core result. The main reasons for my decision are:
1.  **Significant Finding:** The discovery that a shallow network with random weights can rival a deep, pre-trained network for texture synthesis is a fundamentally important contribution. It provides a powerful and much-needed sanity check on the community's assumptions about why deep learning models work for this and related generative tasks, suggesting that much of the heavy lifting may be done by the convolutional structure and the statistical matching procedure itself, rather than learned hierarchical features.
2.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The authors do not simply present their best model but systematically explore the design space. They compare random filters to unsupervisedly learned (K-means, PCA) and hand-crafted (Fourier) ones, and convincingly show that random filters are sufficient. The investigation into the effect of filter scale (Fig. 2) is particularly insightful, clearly demonstrating that a multi-scale receptive field structure is crucial for capturing correlations at different ranges, thus motivating their best-performing multi-scale model.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's claims are well-supported by strong empirical evidence. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling and clearly illustrate the high quality of textures synthesized from the random multi-scale model. The comparison is fair, pitting the proposed minimal models directly against the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. model, synthesized using the same optimization procedure.

The introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative evaluation metric is a clever, pragmatic choice. While the authors rightly acknowledge its limitations (namely, that it is the objective of the competitor model), it provides a more principled quantitative measure than pixel-wise error or SSIM, and largely correlates with the visual quality observed in the figures. The cases where it fails (e.g., for regular, man-made structures) are themselves interesting and highlight the shortcomings of existing perceptual metrics.

Finally, the discussion in Section 7 elevates the paper beyond a purely empirical study. The authors connect their findings to the broader theoretical context of the Maximum Entropy framework. Their argument that practical synthesis on finite-sized images requires moving from exact statistical matching to defining a small "volume" of acceptable textures around the reference (an "epsilon-environment") is a nuanced and important point. This insight helps explain why over-parameterized models can still produce variable results and provides a valuable conceptual lens for future work.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions for refinement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

1.  **Quantitative Evaluation with Human Subjects:** The paper's core claim is about perceptual quality. While the visual evidence is strong and the VGG-loss is a reasonable proxy, the gold standard remains human evaluation. A small-scale psychophysical study (e.g., a two-alternative forced-choice task asking participants which synthesized texture is more similar to the original) would provide definitive evidence for the claim that the random multi-scale model rivals the VGG-based model and would significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions.

2.  **Clarification on Optimization:** The paper notes that the loss and gradients were scaled by 10^7 "to avoid early stopping." This is a critical implementation detail that feels somewhat magical. A brief explanation of why this is necessary would be very helpful. Is it due to the optimizer's tolerance parameters interacting with a very flat loss landscape, or a numerical precision issue? Elucidating this could provide further insight into the nature of the optimization problem posed by these models.

3.  **Visualizing Variability:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. This point could be made more tangible by including a figure in the appendix that shows multiple different samples generated from the same source texture for both the proposed multi-scale model and the VGG model. This would allow readers to visually assess the difference in variability that the authors allude to in the text.

4.  **Broader Context of Random Features:** The finding that random projections are powerful has echoes in other areas of machine learning (e.g., random kitchen sinks, compressed sensing). Briefly situating the work in this broader context could help readers appreciate that this phenomenon, while surprising in this specific domain, is part of a larger pattern of the "unreasonable effectiveness" of random features.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Your results show that a multi-scale architecture is critical. Have you investigated whether a single-scale model could eventually match the multi-scale performance if given a sufficiently large number of filters (e.g., far more than 3267)? In other words, is the explicit multi-scale structure fundamentally necessary, or is it a more efficient way of achieving what a massive single-scale model could eventually learn?

2.  You observe that for some textures, your multi-scale model achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than the VGG model that directly optimizes this loss. You attribute this to the simpler optimization landscape of your model. Could you elaborate on this? Does this suggest that the VGG-loss landscape is fraught with poor local minima that the optimization for the VGG model gets stuck in, whereas the landscape for the random model is better behaved?

3.  Regarding your proposed framework of defining a texture as an epsilon-environment based on a perceptual metric, how do you envision choosing a principled value for epsilon? Could the natural variance of statistics computed over different patches of a large, stationary texture sample inform this choice?
