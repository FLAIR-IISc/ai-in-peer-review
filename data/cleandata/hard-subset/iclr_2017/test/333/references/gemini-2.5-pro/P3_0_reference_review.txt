### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal ingredients required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It directly challenges the prevailing assumption, established by the influential work of Gatys et al. (2015a), that both a deep, hierarchical network architecture and features learned via supervised object recognition are essential for this task. The authors' central and surprising claim is that neither is indispensable. They demonstrate this by proposing a strong baseline model consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters.

The paper's key contributions are:
*   It shows that a simple, single-layer CNN with random multi-scale filters can generate textures of remarkable perceptual quality, often rivaling or even exceeding the state-of-the-art VGG-based model.
*   It provides a systematic and comprehensive empirical study, comparing a wide range of single-layer models with different filter types (random, hand-crafted Fourier, unsupervisedly learned K-means/PCA) and architectures, strengthening the claim that the specific nature of the filters is less important than their statistical properties and multi-scale coverage.
*   It introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative, albeit imperfect, proxy for perceptual quality, moving beyond purely qualitative visual inspection.
*   It offers a thoughtful discussion that connects the empirical results to the Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling, insightfully framing the problem as a trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, particularly for finite-sized images.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant and Surprising Result:** The paper presents a fundamental and counter-intuitive finding that a very simple model (a single layer of random filters) can be as effective as a complex, deep, pre-trained network for a key generative task. This result provides a crucial new perspective on what aspects of deep networks are responsible for their success in texture modeling and serves as an essential baseline for future work.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support and Rigorous Analysis:** The claims are well-supported by extensive experiments that systematically ablate the core components of the model (depth, filter type, filter scale). The comparison against numerous baselines and the state-of-the-art is convincing. The analysis of the importance of multi-scale filters and non-linearities (Fig. 2) is particularly insightful.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's main strength lies in its clear and impactful core message. By demonstrating the effectiveness of a minimal model, it forces a re-evaluation of the community's understanding. Before this work, the success of Gatys et al. was largely attributed to the rich, hierarchical features learned by VGG on ImageNet. This paper convincingly argues that much of that power can be replicated by a sufficiently expressive, stationary feature space derived from a random, multi-scale filter bank. The Gram matrix, which captures second-order statistics in this feature space, appears to be the most critical component.

The experimental methodology is sound. The authors' choice to explore a wide variety of filter initialization strategies (K-means, PCA, Fourier, Random) is excellent, as it isolates the effect of pre-training and demonstrates that the model's success is not contingent on a specific type of "unsupervisedly learned" feature but is a more general property of overcomplete filter banks. The ablation study in Figure 2, which teases apart the contributions of filter size, is a highlight of the paper. It clearly illustrates that a combination of filters at different scales is necessary to capture both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, elegantly explaining the success of their multi-scale model.

Furthermore, the discussion in Section 7 elevates the paper beyond a purely empirical contribution. The authors connect their findings to the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis (MaxEnt framework) and astutely point out the limitations of this framework for finite-sized images. Their proposal to define texture models via a perceptual metric and an epsilon-environment is a thoughtful conceptual contribution that addresses the critical trade-off between perceptual fidelity and sample diversity.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Evaluation Metric:** The primary weakness is the reliance on the VGG-based loss as the quantitative metric for evaluation. This is circular, as it uses the objective function of the competitor model as a ground-truth measure of quality. While the authors acknowledge its imperfections, the claims of "rivaling" or "surpassing" the state-of-the-art would be much more powerfully substantiated with a human perceptual study (e.g., A/B testing where users choose the more realistic texture). This is especially important for the cases (like the grids) where the authors note a paradox between the VGG loss and visual quality.
*   **Optimization vs. Representation:** The paper suggests that the single-layer model's success might be partly due to a simpler optimization landscape. This is a crucial point that deserves more exploration. The current work conflates the representational power of the model with the ease of optimizing its loss function. It would be valuable to include more discussion on whether the VGG model is simply harder to optimize with L-BFGS and might perform better with different optimization strategies. The experiment in Appendix A (Fig. 5) is a good step in this direction, but a more direct analysis in the main paper would be beneficial.
*   **Reproducibility of the Multi-scale Model:** The "Random multi-scale" model is the paper's best-performing proposed model, yet its architecture is not explicitly defined in the list of models in Section 2. For clarity and reproducibility, the exact filter sizes and the number of filters per scale should be detailed.
*   **Generalization of Claims:** The conclusion states that "natural image generation does not necessarily depend on deep hierarchical representations." This is a very strong claim based solely on the task of texture synthesis. Textures are a special class of images characterized by stationary statistics. It would be more precise to qualify this claim, acknowledging that hierarchical representations may still be indispensable for generating images with complex, non-stationary structures like objects and scenes.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you further elaborate on the relationship between model simplicity and optimization? How confident are you that the performance gains of the single-layer model are due to its representational capacity rather than simply being a consequence of a more convex or easier-to-navigate loss landscape for the L-BFGS optimizer compared to the deep VGG model?
2.  Given the noted discrepancies between the VGG-loss and perceived quality, have you considered even a small-scale user study to validate that the textures generated by your random multi-scale model are indeed preferred or considered of equal quality to those from the VGG model?
3.  What is the precise architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model used in Figures 1 and 4? Specifically, what are the different filter sizes used, how many random filters are allocated to each size, and are their resulting Gram matrices weighted or simply concatenated?
