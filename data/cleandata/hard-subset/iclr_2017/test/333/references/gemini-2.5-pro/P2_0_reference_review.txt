### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption, established by the influential work of Gatys et al. (2015a), that deep, hierarchical representations from networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition are essential for this task. The core contribution is the demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with, and sometimes superior to, those from a deep 19-layer VGG network.

The paper systematically explores a variety of single-layer architectures, comparing filters derived from Fourier bases, k-means clustering on image patches, PCA, and random initializations. It finds that a multi-scale random filter model performs exceptionally well. Furthermore, the authors introduce a quantitative metric for texture quality, the "VGG-loss," which uses the objective function of the Gatys et al. model to evaluate synthesized samples. Finally, the paper provides a thoughtful discussion that connects these empirical findings to the broader Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling, highlighting the crucial trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Strong and Surprising Primary Result:** The paper's central claim—that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained network for texture synthesis—is a significant and counter-intuitive finding. This "less is more" result provides a powerful new baseline for the field and forces a re-evaluation of what architectural components are truly necessary for capturing texture statistics. It simplifies our understanding of the problem, suggesting that a sufficiently diverse, overcomplete set of filter responses, combined with second-order statistics (the Gram matrix), is the key ingredient, rather than learned hierarchical features.

2.  **Rigorous and Thorough Empirical Investigation:** The authors do not simply present their best model; they conduct a comprehensive set of experiments to support their claims. The comparison across various filter generation strategies (random, learned, hand-crafted) is insightful. The ablation study in Figure 2, which teases apart the contributions of filter size versus the combination of multiple scales, is an excellent example of rigorous scientific investigation. This analysis convincingly shows that the combination of filters at different scales is critical for capturing both fine-grained and long-range structures, and that non-linearities (ReLUs) are indispensable.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. It positions itself effectively against the state-of-the-art and follows a logical "subtractive" approach to scientific inquiry—removing components to find the minimal sufficient model.

The visual evidence presented in Figures 1 and 4 is compelling. For many textures, particularly those with regular or semi-regular structures (e.g., brick wall, grids, scales), the proposed "Random multi-scale" model produces results that are arguably superior to the VGG-based synthesis. The textures appear sharper and more globally coherent. While the VGG model may still have an edge on more stochastic or complex textures, the performance of the simple random model is consistently impressive and strongly supports the central claim.

The discussion in Section 7 is a strength of the paper. It shows a deep engagement with the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis. The authors correctly identify the tension between perceptual similarity (which they optimize for) and variability. Their framing of texture synthesis models in terms of a perceptual metric `d(x,y)` and an ε-environment of acceptable textures is a nuanced and valuable contribution that moves beyond a simplistic view of constraint matching. This discussion elevates the paper from a simple empirical report to a more thoughtful piece of research.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, there are areas that could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **The VGG-loss Metric:** The use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative evaluation metric is a double-edged sword. While the desire for an objective measure is commendable, using the loss function of the model you are trying to outperform is inherently circular. The authors commendably point out the paradoxical result that their model sometimes achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than the VGG model itself. This strongly suggests that the VGG synthesis is struggling with a difficult, non-convex optimization landscape, and that the VGG-loss is therefore more a measure of "optimisability" or similarity within the VGG feature space than an objective measure of perceptual quality. The authors should be more explicit about this limitation in the main text and frame the metric more cautiously.

2.  **Optimization Details:** The paper mentions scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 to avoid early stopping in L-BFGS. This feels like an important practical detail that is somewhat glossed over. A brief explanation of why this is necessary (e.g., due to floating-point precision issues in the optimizer when gradients become very small) would improve the paper's reproducibility and clarity.

3.  **Computational Cost:** The authors note that their multi-scale model is computationally inefficient due to large filters. It would be helpful to quantify this. A brief comparison of synthesis time (e.g., time per 2000 iterations) for the multi-scale random model vs. the VGG model would give readers a concrete sense of the trade-offs involved.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on your interpretation of the VGG-loss results? Given that the single-layer model can achieve a lower loss, do you believe this is primarily due to a smoother, more easily optimized loss landscape compared to the deep VGG model? If so, does this imply that while VGG may provide a rich *feature space*, its depth presents significant optimization challenges that can harm the final synthesis quality?

2.  Your results show that random filters are sufficient. However, pre-trained filters also work very well. What is your intuition on what pre-training *does* provide, if not necessity? Does it simply learn a particularly effective basis of multi-scale filters, or is there a more subtle structural benefit from the hierarchical arrangement of these learned features that your model does not capture?

3.  The paper focuses on Gram matrices (second-order statistics). Have you considered whether this simple random-filter architecture could be extended by incorporating higher-order statistics, and do you have any hypotheses about what kinds of textures might require such an extension?
