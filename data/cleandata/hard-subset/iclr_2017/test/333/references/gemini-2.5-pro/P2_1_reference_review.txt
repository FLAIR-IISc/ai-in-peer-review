### Paper Summary

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, a task for which the state-of-the-art (at the time of writing) relies on deep, pre-trained convolutional neural networks (e.g., VGG). The authors challenge two widely-held assumptions: that a deep, hierarchical representation and that supervisedly trained features are indispensable for this task. The core contribution is the demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from the much more complex VGG-based model of Gatys et al. (2015a). Through extensive experiments, the authors show that a multi-scale architecture with random filters is particularly effective, capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure. They also introduce the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality, complementing the standard qualitative visual inspection.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a strong and counter-intuitive result that fundamentally challenges the community's understanding of what makes neural texture synthesis effective. By demonstrating the power of a single layer of random filters, it convincingly argues that the hierarchical structure and learned features of deep networks like VGG may be sufficient but are not necessary. This provides a crucial minimal baseline and forces a re-evaluation of the core principles at play.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors' claims are supported by a thorough set of experiments. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), numbers of filters, and filter scales. The ablation study on the importance of multi-scale filters versus a single large receptive field (Fig. 2) is particularly insightful and strongly supports their conclusion that combining scales is key.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this work lies in its clear and impactful "less is more" argument. At a time when the field was rapidly moving towards deeper and more complex models, this paper provides a valuable sanity check. It reframes the problem of texture synthesis: rather than being about leveraging semantically rich, hierarchical features learned for object recognition, it might be more fundamentally about projecting the image into a high-dimensional feature space (which even random filters can do) and then capturing stationary second-order statistics via the Gram matrix. The fact that random filters perform on par with, or even better than, filters learned from natural image statistics (K-means, PCA) is a particularly striking result that underscores this point.

The methodology is scientifically sound. The synthesis process follows the established optimization-based approach, making the comparison to the Gatys et al. model fair. The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure is a valuable contribution. While the authors rightly acknowledge its imperfections (e.g., for highly regular structures), it provides a more objective benchmark than purely subjective visual assessment, strengthening the paper's scientific rigor. The analysis of failure modes and successes is balanced; for instance, they show where the multi-scale model excels (regular structures) and where VGG still has an edge.

The discussion in Section 7 is also a highlight. It thoughtfully connects the empirical results to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling. The authors correctly point out the tension between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and the practical issues of applying these theoretical models to finite-sized images. This discussion elevates the paper from a simple empirical study to a more profound exploration of the underlying principles of generative texture modeling.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Optimization Details:** The authors mention scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7 to avoid premature stopping of the L-BFGS optimizer. This feels like a somewhat ad-hoc hyperparameter. A brief explanation of why this is necessary (e.g., due to the scale of the Gram matrix values or the precision limits of the optimizer) would be helpful for reproducibility and understanding the optimization landscape.
2.  **Discussion on Computational Cost:** The paper mentions in the discussion that hierarchical networks are more efficient than their single-layer model with large filters. This is a crucial practical point. Quantifying this difference (e.g., synthesis time for a sample image for the multi-scale random model vs. the VGG model) would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved.
3.  **Generalization to Style Transfer:** The Gatys et al. model is famous for both texture synthesis and artistic style transfer. While this paper focuses on texture, the findings have clear implications for style transfer. A brief discussion on whether they expect these results to hold for style transfer (which involves an additional content loss) would be a valuable addition. Would a random network be sufficient to separate content and style? This could be framed as an exciting avenue for future work.
4.  **Clarity of Figure 1 Caption:** The caption for Figure 1 could be more explicit in guiding the reader. For instance, clearly labeling which rows correspond to 363 vs. 3267 feature maps would improve readability.

### Questions for Authors

1.  You report a fascinating and paradoxical finding: for some textures, your multi-scale random model achieves a *lower* VGG-based loss than the VGG-based synthesis model, which directly optimizes that exact loss. You suggest this is due to the simpler optimization landscape of your model. Could you elaborate on this? How frequently did this occur? Does this imply that the VGG-loss landscape is highly non-convex and that better optimization, rather than a better model, is a key challenge for the VGG-based approach?
2.  Your best-performing model relies on multi-scale filters. This seems to functionally mimic the multi-scale receptive fields found in a hierarchical CNN. Have you considered whether a shallow, multi-layer random CNN (e.g., 2-3 layers) could achieve similar quality to your multi-scale single-layer model but with greater computational efficiency due to smaller kernels?
3.  The paper makes a compelling case against the necessity of learned features. However, the VGG network was trained on ImageNet, an object-centric dataset. Do you think features learned on a texture-specific dataset might offer a more significant advantage over random filters than the ImageNet-trained features did?
