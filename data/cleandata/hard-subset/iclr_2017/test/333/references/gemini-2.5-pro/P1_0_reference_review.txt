### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that state-of-the-art results, exemplified by Gatys et al. (2015a), depend critically on deep, hierarchical network architectures and features learned via supervised pre-training (e.g., on ImageNet). The authors' central claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a 19-layer VGG network.

The paper systematically supports this claim through extensive experiments. It compares a variety of single-layer models with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and sizes, demonstrating that random multi-scale filters perform exceptionally well. To move beyond purely subjective evaluation, the authors propose using the synthesis loss of the VGG-based model as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality. Finally, the paper includes a thoughtful discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, framing texture synthesis within a maximum entropy context and highlighting the importance of the chosen perceptual distance metric for finite-sized images.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper's core result—that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained network for texture synthesis—is a significant contribution. It establishes a powerful and unexpectedly simple baseline, forcing a re-evaluation of what architectural components are truly essential for this task. This finding has important implications for understanding the implicit biases of CNN-based generative models.

2.  **Rigorous and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claims are supported by a well-designed and thorough set of experiments. The authors don't just show that one random network works; they systematically ablate filter types, the number of filters, filter scales, and the role of non-linearities. The visual results presented are compelling and strongly corroborate the main thesis.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The strength of this paper lies in its clear hypothesis and the rigorous way it is tested. By going "the opposite way" of most research—simplifying rather than adding complexity—the authors provide fundamental insights.

The central finding is impactful because it suggests that the power of the Gatys et al. model comes less from the specific, learned hierarchical features of VGG and more from the general structure of using Gram matrix statistics from a convolutional filter bank. The fact that random filters suffice implies that a sufficiently overcomplete, non-linear projection is enough to capture the necessary statistics for perception. The authors' insight that previous failures with random networks might be due to optimization subtleties (i.e., scaling the loss) is a valuable practical contribution in itself.

The experimental setup is excellent. The comparison between models with 363 and 3267 filters clearly shows the benefit of a larger number of features. The ablation study in Figure 2, which compares single-scale models of varying filter sizes against the multi-scale model, is particularly illuminating. It convincingly demonstrates that combining multiple scales is crucial for capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, rather than just having a large receptive field. This provides a clear explanation for the success of their best-performing "Random multi-scale" model.

The proposal to use the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a commendable step towards more objective evaluation in a field that often relies on subjective inspection. The authors are commendably transparent about its limitations, noting instances where the metric contradicts visual perception, which adds credibility to their analysis.

The discussion in Section 7 is also a highlight. It thoughtfully connects the empirical results to the theoretical underpinnings of maximum entropy models for textures. The discussion of finite-size effects and the need to define a perceptual metric with an ε-environment around the reference statistics is nuanced and forward-looking, posing important questions for the future of the field.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

1.  **Details of the "Random multi-scale" model:** The "Random multi-scale" model is the hero of the paper, yet its architecture is not explicitly defined in Section 2 like the other models. Could you please clarify the exact filter sizes used and how the 1024 feature maps are distributed across these scales? This information is crucial for reproducibility.

2.  **Elaboration on Optimization:** The paper states that scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7 was critical to avoid early stopping. This is a very interesting practical finding. Could you elaborate slightly on the potential reason for this? Does L-BFGS-B's convergence criteria struggle with the very small, noisy gradients of this loss landscape, and does scaling effectively change the optimizer's tolerance? A little more intuition here would be very helpful.

3.  **Quantifying Computational Cost:** In the discussion, you mention that hierarchical networks are more computationally efficient for achieving large receptive fields than using large, single-layer filters (e.g., 55x55). It would strengthen this point to provide some rough numbers. For instance, what is the relative synthesis time for the best single-scale model (e.g., 11x11), the multi-scale model, and the VGG-based model?

4.  **Variability vs. Perceptual Similarity:** The discussion on the trade-off between variability and similarity is excellent. You note that the single-layer models might be less prone to local minima, leading to lower VGG-loss but potentially less variability. This is a fascinating observation. While a full psychophysical study is beyond the scope, have you considered visualizing the diversity of samples by generating multiple textures from the same source image with different random initializations? Showing a 2x2 grid of diverse samples for the multi-scale model alongside a 2x2 grid for the VGG model could visually illustrate this trade-off more directly than Figure 3 alone.
