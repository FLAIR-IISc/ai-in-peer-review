### **Summary of Contributions**

The paper investigates the essential components for parametric natural texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical neural networks with pre-trained features (such as VGG, used in the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al., 2015a) are necessary for this task. The authors propose a minimal baseline model consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. The core contribution is demonstrating that this simple model, particularly a multi-scale variant, can generate textures of remarkably high perceptual quality, often rivaling or even surpassing the complex VGG-based model. The paper systematically evaluates different filter types (random, learned, hand-crafted) and sizes, concluding that a multi-scale representation is crucial for capturing both fine details and long-range structures. Additionally, it introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality and provides a thoughtful discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture synthesis.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper presents a compelling and counter-intuitive result: a very simple model (a single-layer CNN with random filters) can achieve state-of-the-art performance on a complex generation task. This finding forces a re-evaluation of the roles of depth and feature learning in texture synthesis, making it a highly impactful contribution.
2.  **Rigorous Empirical Investigation:** The authors support their central claim with a thorough set of experiments. They systematically compare various single-layer architectures, ablate the importance of filter scale, and provide strong qualitative and quantitative evidence. The visual results are particularly convincing.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's main strength lies in its "less is more" argument, which is convincingly demonstrated. The prior art, exemplified by Gatys et al. (2015a), attributed its success to the rich, hierarchical features learned by a deep network trained for object recognition. This paper systematically dismantles that assumption.

First, the experiments in Figure 1 show that even single-scale, random filters can capture significant texture structure, especially with a sufficient number of feature maps (e.g., Random-3267). This immediately casts doubt on the necessity of learned features.

Second, the introduction of the multi-scale random filter model is the paper's strongest empirical point. The results in Figure 2 and Figure 4 are striking. The multi-scale model often produces textures that are perceptually superior to the VGG-based model, particularly for highly regular patterns like brick walls and grids. The ablation study in Figure 2, which compares single-scale models with varying filter sizes against the combined multi-scale model, provides a clear and rigorous justification for why the multi-scale approach is effective: it simultaneously captures correlations at different spatial frequencies.

Third, the paper proposes a novel way to quantify texture quality using the loss function of the VGG model itself. While this metric has its own biases (as the authors rightly acknowledge), it is a creative attempt to move beyond purely subjective human evaluation. The observation that their simpler model can sometimes achieve a lower VGG loss than the VGG model itself is a fascinating result, suggesting that the optimization landscape for the deep VGG model is more challenging, which is an important insight for the community.

Finally, the discussion section elevates the paper beyond a simple empirical study. It thoughtfully considers the implications of its findings, discussing the trade-off between computational efficiency and architectural depth, the fundamental role of Gram matrices as stationary descriptors, and the critical balance between perceptual similarity and sample variability. The proposed extension of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-size images is a valuable conceptual contribution.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, a few points could be clarified or strengthened. These suggestions are intended to help improve the final version and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Details of the Multi-Scale Model:** The "Random multi-scale" model is central to the paper's best results, but it is not formally defined in Section 2 alongside the other models. Its composition (number of filters per scale, specific filter sizes used) is only mentioned implicitly in Section 6 and the Figure 2 caption. An explicit definition in the methodology section would improve clarity and reproducibility.
*   **The Role of Optimization:** The introduction mentions that the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a) regarding random weights may stem from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Section 4 specifies scaling the loss and gradients by a factor of 10^7. This feels like a critical implementation detail that may be responsible for making the random-filter approach work at all. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this. For instance, was a small ablation study performed on this scaling factor? Highlighting the importance of this "trick" could be a valuable contribution in itself, as it might explain previous failures and guide future work.
*   **Variability Analysis:** The discussion on variability is excellent, but the paper provides limited empirical evidence of it. Figure 3 shows some variability for a single texture. It would strengthen the paper to show a few more examples of variability for different textures, perhaps comparing the diversity of samples from the proposed model versus the VGG model. This would lend more empirical weight to the excellent discussion in Section 7.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the exact architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model? Specifically, what are the filter sizes used, and how many of the 1024 total filters are allocated to each size?
2.  Regarding the optimization, how sensitive are the results to the loss scaling factor of 10^7? Does the synthesis fail entirely without it? Did you experiment with other optimization algorithms besides L-BFGS-B, and did you observe similar behavior? This would help clarify whether the success of random filters is tied to a specific optimization setup.
3.  The paper notes that for some textures, the multi-scale model achieves a lower VGG-based loss than the VGG model itself. You suggest this is due to optimization difficulties in the deeper model. Could this also imply that the single-layer model, perhaps due to its simpler loss landscape, is better at capturing certain types of global, stationary statistics that the layer-wise VGG model struggles to optimize for jointly?
4.  You mention that deeper multi-layer random CNNs sometimes exactly reconstructed the reference texture. This is a very interesting "failure case" related to overfitting the statistics. Could you elaborate on the conditions under which this happened (e.g., network depth, number of filters)? This observation seems highly relevant to the discussion on balancing similarity and variability.
