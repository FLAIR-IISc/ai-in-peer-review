### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a compelling and surprising investigation into the minimal requirements for parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The central claim is that contrary to the prevailing view at the time—heavily influenced by the work of Gatys et al. (2015a)—neither a deep hierarchical architecture nor features learned via supervised pre-training are essential for generating high-quality natural textures. The authors support this claim by demonstrating that a simple, single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters can produce textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a 19-layer, pre-trained VGG network.

The paper's key contributions are:
1.  The proposal of a strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis that relies only on the Gram matrix statistics of a single convolutional layer with random filters.
2.  A thorough empirical ablation study comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures. This includes different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), filter counts, and, crucially, the use of multi-scale filters, which is shown to be highly effective.
3.  The introduction and use of the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model (termed "VGG-loss") as a quantitative metric for texture quality, providing a more objective evaluation method than qualitative human inspection alone.
4.  A thoughtful discussion connecting their empirical findings to the theoretical underpinnings of texture modeling, such as the Maximum Entropy framework, and raising important questions about the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

### **Recommendation: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work is well-motivated, the experiments are rigorous, and the main conclusion is both surprising and significant.

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant and Insightful Finding:** The paper effectively challenges the community's nascent assumptions about why deep neural networks are effective for texture synthesis. By showing that a shallow network with random filters is sufficient, the authors isolate the core mechanism: Gram matrix statistics computed over a sufficiently diverse, non-linear filter bank are powerful descriptors of texture, independent of learned hierarchical features. This is a fundamental insight that simplifies our understanding of the problem.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The authors do not just present one random model; they systematically explore a variety of design choices. The comparison between different filter initializations (Random, K-means, Fourier), filter counts, and the critical demonstration of the importance of multi-scale filters (Fig. 2) provides strong evidence for their claims. The qualitative results presented are compelling and directly support the conclusion that the simple model often rivals the state-of-the-art.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength lies in its clear and falsifiable hypothesis and the comprehensive experiments designed to test it. At a time when deep learning research was heavily focused on increasing depth and leveraging large-scale supervised training, this work provides a valuable counter-narrative, showing that for certain tasks, architectural complexity and learned features may be less critical than the statistical objectives imposed on the representation.

The ablation study in Figure 2 is particularly effective. It clearly demonstrates that neither small nor large filters alone are sufficient; it is the *combination* of multiple scales that allows the model to capture both fine-grained detail and long-range structure. This, combined with the demonstration that non-linearities (ReLUs) are essential, elegantly dissects the necessary components of their model.

Furthermore, the introduction of the VGG-loss as a quantitative metric is a commendable step forward. While the authors rightly point out its imperfections (e.g., its failure to capture the perceptual advantage of their model on highly regular textures), it provides a repeatable and objective baseline for comparison, moving the field beyond purely subjective "eyeballing" of results. The observation that their simpler model sometimes achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than the VGG-based model itself is a fascinating result, pointing towards complex optimization dynamics and the potential for deep models to get stuck in less optimal local minima.

The discussion section is also a major strength. The authors connect their findings to the broader theoretical context of the Maximum Entropy framework and astutely discuss the practical challenges of balancing perceptual similarity and variability in finite-sized images. This elevates the paper from a simple empirical result to a more thoughtful piece that contributes to the conceptual understanding of generative modeling for textures.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened:

1.  **Nuance in "Rivaling State-of-the-Art":** The claim that the random model "often rivals" the VGG model is well-supported, but the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of *when* it does so. Visually, the random multi-scale model seems to excel on textures with strong geometric regularity (bricks, grids, scales), where its more constrained optimization might prevent the "wobbly" artifacts sometimes seen in the VGG results. Conversely, the VGG model appears superior for more stochastic or intricate textures. Acknowledging this distinction would make the claims even more precise.
2.  **The Role of Optimization:** The paper states that scaling the loss by 10^7 and careful optimization were key to achieving their results, in contrast to previous reports. This is a critical detail. A little more analysis or intuition on *why* this is necessary would be valuable. Does the loss landscape of a random, shallow network have fundamentally different properties (e.g., flatter regions, poorer conditioning) than that of a deep, pre-trained network?
3.  **Computational Efficiency:** The authors correctly note that large filters (e.g., 55x55) are computationally inefficient compared to achieving a similar receptive field with a deeper network. While they focus on a conceptually minimal model, this practical trade-off is significant and could be highlighted more prominently earlier in the paper, rather than being left primarily for the discussion.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the VGG-loss metric, you observe that for some textures, the multi-scale model achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-based synthesis itself. You suggest this might be due to the VGG optimization being more prone to local minima. Could you elaborate on this? Is it possible to verify this, for example, by showing that initializing the VGG synthesis with the output from your model leads to a better final VGG-loss? (I see this is in the appendix, perhaps it is worth mentioning in the main text).
2.  How sensitive are the results of the `Random multi-scale` model to the specific random seed used to generate the filters? Is one draw of random filters generally sufficient, or did you find that some random initializations perform significantly better than others?
3.  In your discussion of the MaxEnt framework, you propose a model definition based on a metric `d(x, y)` and an ε-environment. How does the choice of network architecture (e.g., single-layer vs. deep) influence the geometry of this metric and the resulting perceptual quality within that ε-ball? Does your work suggest that the metric induced by the random network is, in some ways, "better" behaved for optimization than the one from VGG?
