### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the minimal components necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, directly challenging the assumptions underlying the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a). The authors' central claim is that neither a deep, hierarchical network architecture nor features learned via supervised pre-training are essential for this task. Instead, they demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with those from a deep, pre-trained VGG network.

The main contributions are:
1.  A strong, minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer CNN with random filters, showing surprising performance.
2.  Rigorous empirical evidence suggesting that depth and pre-training are not indispensable for this problem, contrary to previous assumptions.
3.  A systematic comparison of various single-layer architectures, exploring different filter types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) and sizes, culminating in a highly effective multi-scale random model.
4.  The introduction and use of a quantitative texture quality metric (the "VGG-loss") based on the reconstruction error of the Gatys et al. model, providing a more objective evaluation tool than qualitative inspection alone.
5.  An insightful theoretical discussion connecting practical texture synthesis to the Maximum Entropy framework, highlighting the importance of the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, and proposing a more formal definition of a texture model based on a perceptual distance metric.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a surprising and impactful core result: that a simple, single-layer CNN with random filters can rival the state-of-the-art in texture synthesis. This finding fundamentally challenges the prevailing assumption that deep, pre-trained hierarchies are essential and provides a much simpler, more interpretable model for the task.
2.  The claims are supported by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments. The authors systematically compare a wide range of filter types and architectures, and crucially include ablation studies (e.g., comparing multi-scale vs. single large-scale filters) that isolate the key factors for their model's success.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper is its clear and falsifiable central hypothesis, which it proceeds to test rigorously. The authors go against the trend of adding complexity and instead ask what can be removed, which is a valuable scientific contribution. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling; the textures generated by the "Random multi-scale" model are of remarkably high quality and, in some cases (e.g., the brick wall, grids), perceptually superior to the samples from the much more complex VGG-based model.

The experimental methodology is sound. By comparing random filters to other simple alternatives like Fourier bases, PCA, and K-means, the authors make a convincing case that it is the presence of a sufficiently rich, overcomplete filter bank, rather than any specific learned structure, that is critical. The ablation study in Figure 2, which demonstrates that the combination of multiple filter scales is more effective than a single large scale, is an excellent piece of analysis that strengthens their claims about the multi-scale model.

Another significant contribution is the thoughtful discussion on evaluation and the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis. The authors' proposal to use the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric, while potentially circular, is a pragmatic and useful step towards more reproducible research in this area. Even more impressively, they identify a paradox where their simpler model sometimes achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-model itself, leading to a nuanced discussion about optimization landscapes, local minima, and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability. This discussion, along with the proposed formalization of a texture model via a distance metric and an epsilon-environment, elevates the paper beyond a simple empirical result to a conceptually rich contribution.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be addressed to further improve it:

*   **The VGG-Loss Metric:** The paper rightly notes that the VGG-loss is not perfect. It would be beneficial to expand on this. The metric essentially uses the model being simplified as the "oracle" of perceptual quality. When the single-layer model achieves a lower VGG-loss, it suggests one of two things: either the single-layer model's optimization landscape is simpler, allowing it to find a better minimum, or the VGG-loss itself is an imperfect proxy for human perception in those cases. A more direct discussion of these possibilities would be valuable.
*   **Analysis of Random vs. Learned Filters:** The result that random filters often outperform unsupervisedly learned filters (K-means) is quite surprising. The paper states this observation but doesn't delve deep into why this might be. A brief hypothesis would be welcome. For example, could it be that the diversity and orthogonality of a random basis provides better coverage of the feature space required for matching second-order statistics, compared to centroids from K-means which might collapse onto a few dominant modes?
*   **Quantifying Variability:** The discussion of the similarity-variability trade-off is excellent. To make it more concrete, it might be interesting to include a simple quantitative measure of sample diversity. For example, the authors could generate several samples for the same texture (from different random initializations) and report the average pairwise pixel-wise L2 or SSIM distance for their model versus the Gatys et al. model. This could provide quantitative evidence for their claim that their model favors similarity, potentially at the cost of variability.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Regarding the VGG-loss metric: Could you elaborate on its potential pitfalls? Given that your simpler model can sometimes achieve a lower VGG-loss than the model that directly optimizes it, does this suggest that the VGG-loss is a flawed perceptual metric for certain types of textures (e.g., those with strong global regularity), or that the optimization of the deep VGG model is significantly more challenging?
2.  Your results show that the multi-scale random filter model is crucial for success. Have you explored whether the relative number of filters at each scale matters? For example, is it better to have more small-scale filters to capture fine detail, or more large-scale filters for global structure?
3.  The paper focuses on synthesis by optimization. Have you considered whether these single-layer random feature extractors could be used in a feed-forward generative network, similar to the work of Ulyanov et al. (2016)? It seems your findings would suggest that a generator network could also be much simpler than previously thought.
