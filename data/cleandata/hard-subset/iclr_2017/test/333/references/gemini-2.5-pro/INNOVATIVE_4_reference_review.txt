### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It directly challenges two prevailing assumptions in the field: the necessity of deep, hierarchical network architectures and the importance of features learned via supervised pre-training. The authors systematically deconstruct the state-of-the-art model of Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a deep, pre-trained VGG network. Through extensive empirical evaluation, the paper demonstrates that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent, multi-scale filters—is capable of generating textures that are often perceptually competitive with, and occasionally superior to, those produced by the VGG-based approach. The work establishes a new, powerful, and minimal baseline for texture synthesis and compellingly argues that much of the structure required to model textures is inherent in the convolutional architecture and the statistical constraints (Gram matrices), rather than in learned, hierarchical features.

**Decision: Strong Accept**

This is an outstanding paper that exemplifies the kind of bold, curiosity-driven research that pushes a field forward. Its core contribution is both simple and profound.

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Impact:** The paper's primary strength lies in its contrarian and well-executed approach. At a time when the community was largely focused on the power of deeper, pre-trained models, this work courageously asks "what if we don't need all that complexity?" The finding that a single layer of random filters can perform so well is a significant and counter-intuitive result. It forces a re-evaluation of *why* deep learning models are effective for this task and suggests that the power may lie more in the architectural priors (convolution, non-linearities) and the choice of statistical summary (Gram matrix) than in the learned hierarchy of features.
2.  **Establishes a Critical Baseline:** By demonstrating the remarkable effectiveness of such a simple model, the authors provide an essential baseline for all future work on parametric texture synthesis. Any new, more complex model must now justify its added complexity by demonstrating a significant improvement over this "random multi-scale" approach. This is a vital contribution to ensuring scientific rigor in the field.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The claims made in the paper are well-supported by thorough and convincing empirical evidence.

*   **Systematic Evaluation:** The authors don't just present one random network. They meticulously compare a wide range of single-layer architectures with varying filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), numbers of filters, and filter scales. This systematic approach lends significant weight to their conclusions, particularly the finding that a multi-scale architecture with a large number of random filters is the key to success.
*   **Compelling Visual Results:** The visual evidence presented in Figures 1 and 4 is extremely persuasive. The textures generated by the random multi-scale model are of exceptionally high quality, capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure. The fact that this simple model can outperform the deep VGG network on highly regular textures (e.g., brick walls, grids) is a striking demonstration of its power.
*   **Insightful Discussion:** The paper goes beyond merely presenting results. The discussion section provides a thoughtful analysis of the findings, contextualizing them within the broader Maximum Entropy framework. The authors' reflections on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, the role of optimization, and the limitations of finite-size images add significant intellectual depth. This demonstrates a mature understanding of the problem space.

While the proposed quantitative metric (VGG-loss) is inherently circular for comparing against a VGG-based model, the authors are transparent about its limitations. Given the strength of the visual results and the conceptual breakthrough, this methodological imperfection is a minor flaw that does not detract from the paper's core message.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**
The following points are suggestions aimed at strengthening an already excellent paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   A small-scale human perceptual study would have provided definitive evidence for the claims of rivaling state-of-the-art quality. While the visual results are compelling on their own, a forced-choice experiment ("which sample looks more like the original texture?") comparing the random multi-scale model to the VGG model would have made the paper's conclusions unassailable.
*   The discussion on the optimization being "less prone to local minima" for the single-layer model is intriguing. This could be briefly substantiated by, for instance, showing loss curves for both models over several runs from different initializations to demonstrate lower variance in the final loss for the simpler model.
*   The discussion on the trade-off between similarity and variability is excellent. It could be enhanced with a visual demonstration, perhaps by showing synthesized results at different stages of the optimization process (e.g., after 100, 500, and 2000 iterations) to illustrate how the sample evolves from high variability to high perceptual similarity.

**Questions for the Authors:**
I would be interested to hear the authors' thoughts on the following points:

1.  In the introduction, you state that the discrepancy between your success with random filters and the failures reported by Gatys et al. (2015a) may stem from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." The main text mentions scaling the loss and gradients. Were there other critical settings in the L-BFGS optimizer or initialization strategies that you found were essential to making random filters work effectively?
2.  You briefly mention that you experimented with deep hierarchical networks using random filters but did not see an improvement in quality over the single-layer model. Could you elaborate on this? Was the issue that deeper random models tended to fall into poor local minima, or did they perhaps over-constrain the problem and reproduce the original image, as hinted at in your discussion?
3.  The success of the multi-scale approach is very clear. Did you explore whether the distribution of filters across scales mattered? For instance, does performance change if you use more small filters and fewer large ones, or vice-versa, while keeping the total number of filters (1024) constant?
