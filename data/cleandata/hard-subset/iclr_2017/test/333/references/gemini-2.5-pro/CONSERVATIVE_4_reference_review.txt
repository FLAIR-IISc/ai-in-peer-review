### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The authors challenge two widely held beliefs that emerged from the success of Gatys et al. (2015a): that a deep, hierarchical architecture and supervised pre-training on natural images are essential for high-quality results. The paper's main contribution is to demonstrate, quite surprisingly, that a simple single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters can generate textures of high perceptual quality. This model is presented as a strong minimal baseline for the task. The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and spatial scales. They find that a multi-scale architecture with random filters is particularly effective, often producing results that are visually competitive with the state-of-the-art VGG-based model. Additionally, the paper proposes a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model, and includes a thoughtful discussion on the limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-size images.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper's extraordinary central claim—that a simple random network can rival a deep, pre-trained one—is not substantiated with sufficiently rigorous evidence. The evaluation relies primarily on visual examples and a newly proposed, unvalidated quantitative metric, rather than established evaluation protocols like user studies.
2.  The methodological contribution is confounded by a critical, under-analyzed implementation detail in the optimization procedure. It is unclear if the success of the random network is due to its representational power or simply because its optimization landscape is more amenable to a specific optimization setup.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's premise is fascinating, and the visual results are undeniably impressive. However, for a claim that seeks to overturn the prevailing understanding in the field, the standard of evidence must be exceptionally high. Unfortunately, the current manuscript does not meet this standard.

My primary concern lies with the evaluation methodology. The established method for assessing the quality of synthesized textures is through human perceptual studies, as acknowledged by the authors. Instead of conducting such a study, the paper introduces a new quantitative metric: the reconstruction loss of the VGG-based model (Gatys et al., 2015a). This choice is problematic for several reasons. First, using the objective function of the baseline model as a metric for evaluating a new model is circular and potentially biased. It measures proximity in the VGG feature space, not necessarily perceptual quality. Second, the authors themselves demonstrate cases where this metric fails to align with visual perception (e.g., for regular, man-made structures). An unproven metric with known failure modes cannot be the basis for a strong claim of rivaling the state-of-the-art. The qualitative evaluation, while compelling, is susceptible to cherry-picking and cannot replace a systematic, controlled comparison.

Furthermore, the paper's comparison to prior work is insufficient. While the VGG-based model is the primary baseline, other highly influential parametric models like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) are mentioned but never directly compared against. A claim of presenting a "strong minimal baseline" should be validated by showing its performance relative to a wider range of established techniques.

My second major concern relates to methodological soundness. The authors attribute their success with random filters, in contrast to prior failures reported by Gatys et al., to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically the scaling of the loss and gradients by a large constant (10^7) to prevent early stopping. This is a critical detail that is not adequately explored. It is entirely plausible that the observed success is less about the surprising efficacy of random features and more about the discovery of an optimization technique that works well for the simple, convex-like loss landscape of a single-layer model. The finding that initializing the VGG synthesis with the single-layer model's output improves results further suggests that optimization, not representation, is a dominant factor. The paper fails to disentangle these effects. A proper ablation study on this scaling factor and an analysis of its effect on the baseline VGG model are necessary to support the paper's core claims.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The most critical addition needed to make this paper's claims credible is a formal user study. A two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) experiment, where participants are asked to judge which of two syntheses (one from the proposed model, one from a baseline) is a better match to a reference texture, would provide the necessary rigorous evidence. Baselines should include both the VGG-based model and the classic Portilla & Simoncelli (2000) model.
*   The role of the optimization procedure needs to be clarified. I would strongly recommend an ablation study on the loss scaling factor. Does a range of values work? How sensitive is the synthesis quality to this parameter? More importantly, does applying the same scaling factor to the original VGG-based synthesis also improve its results, potentially closing the performance gap?
*   The discussion in Section 7 regarding the limitations of the MaxEnt framework and the proposal for a metric-based definition of a texture model is insightful. However, it feels somewhat disconnected from the empirical core of the paper. Tying this theoretical discussion more closely to the empirical findings would strengthen the paper's narrative. For instance, could the VGG-loss be framed as an imperfect attempt at the perceptual metric `d(x,y)` that the discussion calls for?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for the specific choice of 10^7 as the scaling factor for the loss and gradients? What was the process for finding this value, and what happens when it is significantly altered?
2.  Have you applied the same optimization procedure (i.e., loss scaling by 10^7) to the VGG-based model from Gatys et al. when synthesizing from a random noise initialization? If so, how do the results compare? This is crucial for understanding whether your contribution is a new model or an improved optimization strategy.
3.  Given that your proposed VGG-loss metric sometimes yields paradoxical results (e.g., your model achieving a lower VGG-loss than the VGG model that directly optimizes it), do you believe this points to optimization difficulties in the deep model rather than the superiority of your shallow model's representation? How does this affect its validity as a general-purpose metric for perceptual quality?
4.  Considering the history and importance of psychophysical evaluation in texture synthesis, what were the reasons for not including a user study to provide definitive support for your claim that your model's quality "rivals state-of-the-art approaches"?
