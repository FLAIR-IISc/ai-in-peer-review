### **Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental components necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption, established by the state-of-the-art work of Gatys et al. (2015a), that deep, hierarchical representations from networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition are indispensable. The authors systematically deconstruct this model and demonstrate, quite surprisingly, that a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters is sufficient to generate textures that are often perceptually indistinguishable from, and sometimes superior to, those produced by a 19-layer pre-trained VGG network.

The core contributions are: (1) establishing a powerful yet simple baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer, multi-scale random filter bank; (2) providing strong empirical evidence that neither network depth nor supervised feature learning is a prerequisite for capturing complex texture statistics; (3) introducing a quantitative evaluation metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model; and (4) offering an insightful discussion that connects these empirical findings to the Maximum Entropy framework and the crucial trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

### **Decision and Justification**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong and insightful paper that makes a clear, surprising, and significant contribution to our understanding of generative models for textures. It follows a classic scientific approach by questioning the core assumptions of a state-of-the-art model and using carefully designed experiments to isolate the truly necessary components. The findings are impactful, well-supported, and have broader implications for the field of representation learning.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The central result—that a shallow network with random filters can rival a deep, pre-trained one—is counter-intuitive and forces a re-evaluation of what makes deep representations effective for this task. It suggests that much of the descriptive power comes from the convolutional structure and the statistical aggregation (Gram matrix), rather than learned hierarchical features. This is a foundational insight.
2.  **Rigorous and Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical investigation. They don't just test one random network; they systematically compare a wide range of single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA), filter sizes, and numbers of feature maps. The ablation study on the importance of multi-scale filters versus simply large receptive fields is particularly convincing and clearly demonstrates that a combination of scales is key to their model's success.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis-driven research and execution. The authors identify two key aspects of the Gatys et al. model—depth and supervised training—and design a minimal model that lacks both, yet still succeeds. This approach provides a much clearer understanding of the problem.

The introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric is a valuable contribution. While using the state-of-the-art model as a benchmark metric has potential biases (which the authors transparently acknowledge), it represents a significant step up from purely subjective visual inspection and allows for more systematic model comparison. The authors' honesty about its failures on certain regular textures strengthens the paper's credibility.

Furthermore, the discussion in Section 7 is excellent. It elevates the paper from a simple empirical result to a more profound commentary on generative modeling. The framing of texture synthesis as a balance between perceptual similarity and variability, and the critique of the classical Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images, demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem. The proposal to think in terms of an ε-environment around the target statistics, defined by a perceptual metric, is a conceptually valuable idea for the community.

Finally, a small but crucial detail that enhances the work's reproducibility and explains its success where others failed is the explicit mention of scaling the loss function during optimization to prevent premature convergence.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper correctly notes that its multi-scale model, with filters up to 55x55, is computationally inefficient and that deep architectures are a more efficient way to achieve large receptive fields. This point could be made more concrete with a brief analysis comparing the FLOPs of the single-layer 55x55 convolution against a stack of smaller convolutions (e.g., 3x3) that yield a similar receptive field. This would quantify the trade-off between model simplicity and computational efficiency.
2.  **Ablation on Optimization Scaling:** The authors note that scaling the loss by 10^7 was critical. An ablation study in the appendix showing synthesis results for different scaling factors (e.g., 1, 10^3, 10^7, 10^9) would powerfully illustrate this point and provide practical guidance for others implementing similar models.
3.  **Variability Visualization:** The paper discusses the difficulty in measuring variability. While a full psychophysical experiment is beyond scope, a simple visualization could be helpful. For example, showing 4-5 different samples generated from the same source texture for both the proposed model and the VGG model could give readers a qualitative sense of the difference in variability between the two methods.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The VGG-loss is an interesting proxy for perceptual quality, but it is inherently tied to the features of the VGG network. Could this introduce a bias that favors representations similar in style to those learned by VGG, potentially underestimating the quality of textures with statistics that VGG is not tuned for? Have you considered supplementing this with a small-scale human study (e.g., A/B testing on a few examples) to validate that the VGG-loss correlates well with human preference?
2.  Your finding that initializing VGG synthesis with your model's output improves the final result is fascinating. It suggests your model's optimization landscape is smoother or that it finds a better basin of attraction. Could you elaborate on this? Does this imply that the single-layer model captures the global structure more robustly, providing a superior starting point for the deep model to refine the finer details?
3.  How does the number of parameters/constraints in your best-performing random models (e.g., Random multi-scale with 1024 filters) compare to the number of constraints used from the VGG network in the original Gatys et al. method? Is it possible that the sheer quantity of constraints is a more dominant factor than the "quality" (i.e., trained vs. random) of the filters themselves?
