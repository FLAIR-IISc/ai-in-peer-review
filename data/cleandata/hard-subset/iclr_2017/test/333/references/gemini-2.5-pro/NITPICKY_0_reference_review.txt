### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal architectural and feature-learning requirements for parametric natural texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption, established by Gatys et al. (2015a), that successful texture synthesis necessitates deep, hierarchical representations from networks pre-trained on large-scale supervised tasks like object recognition. The central claim is that a surprisingly simple model—a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling the state-of-the-art.

The paper systematically explores various single-layer architectures, comparing filters derived from Fourier bases, k-means clustering on natural image patches, PCA, and random initialization. A key contribution is the "random multi-scale" model, which combines random filters of varying sizes and demonstrates impressive performance. To move beyond purely qualitative evaluation, the authors propose a quantitative metric based on the synthesis loss of the VGG-based model they aim to supplant. Finally, the paper includes a theoretical discussion on the limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-size images and proposes a more general definition of a texture model based on a distance metric and a perceptual equivalence partition.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Fundamentally flawed evaluation methodology:** The primary quantitative metric used to validate the proposed simple models is the reconstruction loss from the very deep, pre-trained VGG network that the paper argues is unnecessary. This circular reasoning critically undermines the paper's central thesis.
2.  **Lack of methodological rigor and reproducibility:** The optimization procedure relies on a critical but entirely unjustified ad-hoc hyperparameter (a scaling factor of 10⁷), and key architectural details of the best-performing model are omitted.
3.  **Numerous presentation and formatting issues:** The paper is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, inconsistent citations, and a poorly compiled bibliography. This lack of attention to detail erodes confidence in the overall scientific rigor of the work.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

My decision to recommend rejection is based on the accumulation of numerous flaws which, despite the paper's interesting premise, call into question the validity and reliability of its conclusions.

**3.1. Circular and Contradictory Evaluation**

The paper's most significant weakness is its choice of a quantitative evaluation metric. The authors propose using the multi-layer VGG reconstruction cost (Eq. 7) to assess the quality of textures generated by their single-layer random models. This approach is self-defeating. If the VGG-based loss is indeed a reliable proxy for human perceptual quality (as the authors assume for their metric), then the features and hierarchical structure of the VGG network must be meaningful for capturing texture statistics. This directly contradicts the paper's main argument that such features and depth are "not as indispensable as has previously been thought."

The authors even acknowledge this paradox in Section 6, noting that for some textures, their random multi-scale model achieves a *lower* VGG-based loss than the VGG-based synthesis model which directly optimizes this loss. Instead of investigating this critical discrepancy, which suggests issues with either the optimization or the metric itself, they dismiss it and proceed. A successful argument against the necessity of VGG would require an evaluation metric independent of VGG, such as a comprehensive human perceptual study, which is the standard for this field.

**3.2. Methodological Opacity and Ad-Hoc Optimization**

The experimental methodology lacks sufficient detail for reproduction and contains troubling "tricks."

*   In Section 4, the authors state they "scale the loss and the gradients by a factor of 10⁷ in order to avoid early stopping." This is a massive, arbitrary scaling factor. There is no justification for this specific value, no ablation study showing its effect, and no explanation for why the standard L-BFGS-B algorithm would require it. This suggests a fragile optimization process and undermines the claim that the method is a "strong minimal baseline." It reads as a post-hoc hack to make the experiment work, rather than a principled component of the model.
*   The architecture of the best-performing "Random multi-scale" model is not specified. The paper states it uses 1024 feature maps and Figure 2 analyzes its component scales (3x3, 11x11, 55x55), but the precise composition of the model—how many filters are allocated to each scale—is never described. This is a critical missing detail for a central component of the paper's contribution.

**3.3. Pervasive Lack of Polish and Professionalism**

The manuscript is littered with minor, yet collectively significant, errors that indicate a lack of careful preparation.

*   The bibliography is exceptionally poorly formatted. Entries contain extraneous characters (e.g., `? \Q2016\E`), incorrect tags (`[cs]`), inconsistent author naming, and other artifacts suggestive of a failed BibTeX compilation.
*   Citation style is inconsistent throughout the text (e.g., `Simonyan & Zisserman (2014)` vs. `(Russakovsky et al. (2015))`, with the latter having improperly nested parentheses).
*   A footnote in Section 5 includes an archival access date of "12.05.2016," which is highly unusual and unprofessional for a formal conference submission.

While individually minor, these numerous errors project an image of carelessness that makes it difficult to trust the meticulousness of the underlying research.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite my recommendation, I believe the core idea is valuable and the empirical results are genuinely surprising. The paper could be substantially improved and made suitable for publication with the following revisions:

*   **Rethink Evaluation:** The most crucial change is to replace the VGG-based loss with an independent evaluation. The gold standard would be a formal psychophysical experiment (a user study) asking participants to distinguish real from synthesized textures. This would provide direct, undeniable evidence for the perceptual quality of the generated samples.
*   **Justify and Clarify Methodology:** The 10⁷ scaling factor must be thoroughly explained and justified, ideally with an ablation showing its impact. The full architecture of the multi-scale model must be specified. Details on the whitening process for k-means should also be included.
*   **Refine the Discussion:** The theoretical discussion in Section 7 is interesting but feels disconnected. It could be strengthened by more directly linking it to the empirical findings. For instance, how does the observed "over-similarity" in the random model's outputs relate to the proposed ε-environment framework?
*   **Thorough Proofreading:** The paper requires a complete pass for proofreading and reference correction. The quality of the presentation must be brought up to the standards of a top-tier conference.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a justification for the 10⁷ loss scaling factor? What is the sensitivity of the synthesis quality to this specific value, and what happens when it is removed?
2.  What is the exact filter distribution (number of filters per scale) for the "Random multi-scale" model used to generate the results in Figures 1 and 4?
3.  How do you reconcile the central claim that VGG's features and depth are not essential with the decision to use the VGG network itself as the ultimate arbiter of synthesis quality in your quantitative analysis?
