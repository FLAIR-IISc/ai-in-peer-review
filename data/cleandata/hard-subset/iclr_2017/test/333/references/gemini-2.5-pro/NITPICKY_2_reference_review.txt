### Review

**Summary of Contributions**

The paper investigates the minimal components necessary for parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumptions that deep, hierarchical architectures and supervised pre-training are essential for generating high-quality natural textures. The primary contribution is a strong baseline model for texture synthesis that relies on a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. Through a series of experiments, the paper demonstrates that this minimal model can generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art method by Gatys et al. (2015a), which uses a deep, pre-trained VGG network. The authors also introduce the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative metric for texture quality and conclude with a discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, framing it within the Maximum Entropy principle and considering the practical implications of finite-size images.

**Decision: Reject**

I recommend rejecting this paper. While the core finding—that a single random layer can be surprisingly effective for texture synthesis—is interesting and counter-intuitive, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of methodological ambiguities, formatting errors, and a fundamentally flawed evaluation protocol. These issues collectively undermine the scientific rigor and reproducibility of the work, making it impossible to confidently accept the paper's strong claims.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Detail and Reproducibility:** The paper omits several critical details required to reproduce the results.
    *   **Optimization:** The authors state that the success of their approach, in contrast to previous reports, "originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." However, the details provided are insufficient. They use L-BFGS-B with most parameters at their "default value" and scale the loss by a constant factor of 10^7 "to avoid early stopping." Default optimizer parameters are not standard across implementations (e.g., SciPy versions), and the choice of the scaling factor 10^7 is presented without any justification or analysis. This is a crucial hyperparameter that appears to be key to their success, yet its selection is not explained.
    *   **Filter Generation:** For the `Kmeans` models, the paper states that image patches were whitened, but the specific whitening method (e.g., ZCA, PCA) is not specified. This is a non-trivial detail that affects the resulting filter basis.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** In Section 7, the authors claim to have "conducted extensive experiments with various hierarchical architectures" which did not improve quality. No evidence (architectures, results, figures) is provided to support this significant claim.

2.  **Flawed Quantitative Evaluation:** The authors propose using the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model of Gatys et al. (2015a) as a quantitative metric for texture quality. This approach is problematic for several reasons:
    *   **Circularity and Bias:** Using the objective function of a competing model as an evaluation metric is inherently biased towards architectures and representations similar to that model. It does not serve as an independent, objective measure of perceptual quality.
    *   **Demonstrated Unreliability:** The authors themselves show the metric is unreliable. They report cases where their multi-scale model achieves a *lower* VGG-loss than the VGG-based model, which directly optimizes this loss. While they interpret this as a sign of better optimization, it more directly demonstrates that the VGG-loss is not a reliable proxy for perceptual quality and can be manipulated by the optimization process. This finding invalidates its use as a primary quantitative support for their claims. With the quantitative metric being unreliable, the paper's conclusions rest almost entirely on subjective, qualitative visual assessment.

3.  **Pervasive Formatting and Technical Errors:** The manuscript is unacceptably sloppy for a top-tier conference submission.
    *   **Citations and References:** The reference section is completely broken, filled with BibTeX artifacts (`\Q`, `\E`), question marks, incorrect years (e.g., citing SciPy as Jones et al., 2016), and inconsistent formatting. A citation in the main text to `(Glorot & Bengio, )` is incomplete. This level of carelessness is alarming.
    *   **Missing Figure Captions:** The text refers to Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7, but no captions are provided in the manuscript. This makes it impossible to interpret the results presented in the figures.
    *   **Typographical Errors:** There are technical typos that create ambiguity. For example, Equation (3) uses `N` in the summation limit, but the input vector `x` is defined as belonging to `R^M`. This should likely be `M`.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The core idea presented in this paper is valuable and worth pursuing. A revised version could be a strong contribution if the aforementioned issues are addressed.
*   The discussion in Section 7 regarding the Maximum Entropy framework and perceptual metrics is interesting but feels disconnected from the paper's empirical results. This section should be more tightly integrated with the experiments or significantly condensed. Citing "personal communication" is not appropriate for a formal publication.
*   Given the demonstrated flaws of the VGG-loss as a metric, the authors should consider a more principled evaluation method. A well-designed psychophysical study with human participants would provide much stronger evidence for their claims about perceptual quality.
*   The manuscript requires a thorough proofreading and a complete overhaul of its reference management. Adherence to the conference's formatting guidelines is not optional.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please specify the exact implementation and version of the L-BFGS-B optimizer used? What are the specific values of the parameters that were left at "default"?
2.  Could you provide a justification for the choice of 10^7 as the loss scaling factor? Was a sensitivity analysis performed to understand its effect on the optimization and final texture quality?
3.  Regarding the claim that initializing the VGG-based synthesis with the single-layer model's output yields superior results (Appendix A), could this not be interpreted as evidence that the complex loss landscape of the deep VGG model simply requires a better initialization strategy, rather than being an indication of a fundamental flaw in its texture representation?
