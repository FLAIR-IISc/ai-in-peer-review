### Summary of Contributions

The paper investigates the minimal requirements for parametric natural texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that state-of-the-art performance, as established by Gatys et al. (2015a), relies critically on deep, hierarchical representations from networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition tasks (e.g., VGG-19 on ImageNet). The core contribution is a demonstration that a surprisingly simple model—a single-layer convolutional network with random, untrained filters—can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality. The authors show that by matching the Gram matrix of feature activations from this simple model, the generated textures often rival those produced by the much more complex VGG-based approach. The paper also introduces a quantitative evaluation metric based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model itself and explores a variety of filter initialization strategies and architectural choices (e.g., multi-scale filters).

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Flawed Evaluation Methodology:** The central claim that a simple random-filter model can "rival" the state-of-the-art is primarily supported by a circular quantitative metric. The paper proposes using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, but using the very model you aim to supplant as the objective standard for evaluation undermines the claim that it is unnecessary.
2.  **Confounding Factors in Comparison:** The paper attributes its model's success to its architectural simplicity, but it also introduces significant modifications to the optimization process (e.g., scaling the loss by 10^7) which are not properly disentangled from the model's contribution. It is plausible that these optimization improvements, rather than the model itself, account for much of the observed performance gain, but this is not rigorously tested.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper presents a bold and counter-intuitive claim: that the deep, trained features of a network like VGG are largely superfluous for texture synthesis. While questioning established beliefs is a valuable scientific endeavor, the evidence provided here is not sufficiently rigorous to support such a strong conclusion.

First, the evaluation methodology is a major concern. The primary quantitative metric used to compare the proposed model against the VGG baseline is the reconstruction cost defined in the VGG feature space (Eq. 7). This creates a circular dependency: to prove that a VGG network is not needed, the authors rely on a VGG network to score the results. This metric is inherently biased towards feature representations that align with VGG's. While the authors admit the metric is imperfect, its central role in the evaluation makes the quantitative results unconvincing. Strong claims against an established method require independent and robust verification, which in this case would necessitate a large-scale psychophysical study to assess perceptual quality, a standard practice in the field before the advent of deep-learning-based metrics. The qualitative visual results are compelling for some structured textures but are subjective and insufficient on their own to validate the general superiority or equivalence of the method.

Second, the paper itself suggests that its success, contrary to previous reports of random networks failing, may stem from "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Specifically, the loss and gradients are scaled by a large constant (10^7) to prevent premature termination of the L-BFGS optimizer. This is a crucial detail that is not adequately controlled for in the comparison. For the claims to be valid, the authors must demonstrate that the VGG-based baseline does not also improve significantly when subjected to the same optimization enhancements. Without an ablation study that applies identical optimization procedures to both the proposed model and the baseline, one cannot confidently attribute the performance to the random-filter architecture. The results are confounded, and it remains an open question whether the paper has discovered a better model or simply a better way to optimize this class of synthesis problems.

Finally, while the concept of a single-layer model is appealing in its simplicity, the best-performing proposed model (random multi-scale) relies on filters with very large spatial extent (up to 55x55). The authors correctly note that deep architectures are a more computationally efficient method for achieving large receptive fields. Therefore, the argument against hierarchical representation is weakened. The model does not eliminate the need for large-receptive-field features; it merely implements them in an inefficient manner. The contribution is thus less about disproving the value of deep representations and more about demonstrating an alternative, albeit impractical, way to achieve a similar effect.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The exploration of various filter types (Fourier, K-means, etc.) is a valuable contribution. The finding that random filters are competitive with or superior to filters learned via unsupervised methods is an interesting result in its own right and warrants further investigation.
*   The discussion in Section 7 on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is thoughtful. However, claims such as the single-layer model being "less prone to local minima" are speculative and presented without empirical evidence. This line of reasoning could be strengthened with experiments designed to quantify sample diversity or analyze the loss landscape.
*   The paper's observation that initializing VGG-based synthesis with textures from the single-layer model yields superior results is an interesting practical finding. This could be framed as a useful initialization strategy, which is a more defensible and less ambitious claim than stating the single-layer model rivals the VGG one on its own.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide an ablation study that compares your random-filter models and the VGG baseline under identical optimization conditions? Specifically, what is the performance of the Gatys et al. (2015a) method when the loss and gradients are also scaled by 10^7? This is essential for disentangling the effects of the model architecture from the optimization procedure.
2.  Given the methodological concerns with using VGG-loss as an evaluation metric, have you considered conducting a formal human evaluation (e.g., a two-alternative forced-choice task) to more rigorously compare the perceptual quality of samples from your best model against the VGG baseline?
3.  You report that for some textures, the VGG-loss is lower for your model's synthesis than for the synthesis produced by directly optimizing that same loss. This suggests a failure in the optimization of the VGG-based model. Does this not point to the optimization scheme being the more critical factor, rather than the choice of features (random vs. trained)?
