### **Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing assumption that the success of recent neural methods hinges on deep, hierarchical representations and features learned via supervised training on large-scale datasets like ImageNet. The authors compellingly demonstrate that a surprisingly minimal model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—is sufficient to generate textures that are often perceptually competitive with the state-of-the-art model from Gatys et al. (2015a). Through a series of well-designed experiments, the paper systematically explores different filter types (random, Fourier, K-means), filter sizes, and the number of feature maps, concluding that a multi-scale architecture with a large number of random filters is a key ingredient for success. The work provides a powerful new baseline for texture synthesis and forces a re-evaluation of the role of depth and learning in generative texture models.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Impact:** The paper's central finding is both surprising and profound. By showing that a shallow network with random weights can rival a very deep, pre-trained model, it questions the established narrative about why neural texture synthesis works. This is not an incremental improvement but a bold conceptual challenge that simplifies the problem and opens up new avenues for thinking about generative models. It suggests the power may lie more in the architectural prior (convolutional structure, non-linearities, and Gram matrix statistics) than in the learned feature hierarchies themselves.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evidence:** Despite the simplicity of the proposed model, the empirical results are remarkably strong. The visual comparisons provided in the figures are clear and persuasive. The authors conduct a thorough ablation study, convincingly demonstrating the importance of multi-scale filters and non-linearities, while simultaneously showing that learned or engineered filters offer little to no advantage over random ones. This comprehensive analysis provides strong support for their central claim.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper lies in its "less is more" approach. The authors take a complex, state-of-the-art model and systematically strip it down to its bare essentials, discovering in the process that much of the perceived complexity was not essential for the core task. This is a significant scientific contribution. Earlier work (Gatys et al., 2015a) had suggested that random networks were ineffective, a conclusion this paper successfully overturns, attributing the difference to careful optimization and, crucially, the use of a multi-scale architecture.

The introduction of the multi-scale random model is a key technical contribution. The ablation in Figure 2, which shows how different filter scales capture different levels of structure, provides a clear and intuitive justification for why combining them is so effective. This insight is valuable on its own.

Furthermore, the paper introduces a pragmatic approach to quantitative evaluation by using the loss of the state-of-the-art VGG model as a proxy for perceptual quality. While imperfect—a point the authors rightly acknowledge—it represents a thoughtful attempt to move beyond purely subjective visual inspection. The finding that their simpler model can sometimes achieve a *lower* VGG-loss than the VGG model itself is a fascinating result, highlighting potential optimization difficulties in very deep generative models and reinforcing the practical benefit of their simpler approach.

The discussion in Section 7 is also a highlight. The authors thoughtfully consider the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, and correctly frame texture synthesis in the context of defining a perceptual metric and an equivalence class of textures around a reference image. This shows a deep understanding of the problem space that goes beyond just generating pretty pictures.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Human Evaluation:** The core claim is that the generated textures "rival" the state-of-the-art in perceptual quality. While the figures are compelling, this claim would be significantly strengthened by a small-scale human perceptual study. For example, a two-alternative forced-choice (2AFC) task asking participants which of two synthesized images (one from the proposed model, one from Gatys et al.) is a better match to a reference texture would provide definitive evidence.

*   **Exploration of Deep Random Networks:** The authors mention in the discussion that they experimented with hierarchical multi-layer random networks but that the quality did not improve. This is a very interesting negative result that preempts a natural question from the reader. It would be beneficial to include a brief summary of these experiments and some example images in the appendix. This would further solidify the central claim that depth, even in a random network, is not a necessary component.

*   **Details on Optimization:** The paper notes that a key difference from prior work is "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure," specifically the scaling of the loss and gradients. This seems to be a critical detail for reproducibility and for understanding why this work succeeded where previous attempts with random nets may have failed. A bit more elaboration on the sensitivity to this scaling factor and the intuition behind it would be helpful.

*   **Sample Variability:** While Figure 3 shows some variability, the exploration is limited. It would be interesting to see a more direct comparison of the variability of samples from the proposed model versus the VGG model. Does the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow model lead to more or less diversity in the final results for a given VGG-loss? This could be a fruitful direction for future work.
