### **Review**

#### **1. Paper Summary**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the widely-held assumption, established by work like Gatys et al. (2015a), that successful texture modeling requires features from deep, hierarchical convolutional networks pre-trained on large-scale object recognition tasks. The authors compellingly demonstrate that a surprisingly simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters—can generate textures of remarkable perceptual quality, often rivaling the state-of-the-art.

The core contributions are: (1) establishing a strong and minimal baseline for texture synthesis using a single-layer random network; (2) showing through extensive experiments that neither network depth nor supervised feature learning are indispensable for this task; (3) introducing a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG-based model, moving beyond purely qualitative evaluation; and (4) providing an insightful discussion on the theoretical underpinnings of texture synthesis, particularly on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability for finite-sized images, and proposing a refinement to the Maximum Entropy framework.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant and Surprising Main Result:** The central finding—that a shallow network with random filters can perform on par with a deep, pre-trained network—is a significant contribution that provides a powerful new baseline for texture synthesis. It forces a re-evaluation of the role of depth and learned features in generative modeling of stationary signals and is likely to influence future research in this area.
2.  **Rigorous Empirical Analysis and Conceptual Depth:** The paper supports its claims with a thorough empirical study comparing various filter initialization strategies (random, learned, engineered) and architectures (single-scale vs. multi-scale). The analysis is complemented by a thoughtful and nuanced discussion that elevates the paper beyond a mere empirical report, offering valuable conceptual insights into the nature of parametric texture models.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its clear and falsifiable central hypothesis and the rigorous way it is tested. The authors identify two key factors previously thought to be critical—depth and pre-training—and systematically dismantle the case for their necessity.

*   **Strength of the "Less is More" Argument:** The visual results, particularly for the random multi-scale model (Fig. 4), are striking. This model's ability to capture both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, sometimes even better than the VGG-based model on highly regular textures (e.g., brick walls), is convincing evidence for the authors' claims. The work provides a crucial insight: a sufficiently overcomplete, multi-scale random projection followed by second-order statistics (Gram matrix) is a powerful enough representation for modeling textures.

*   **Methodological Soundness:** The authors perform careful ablation studies. The comparison between single-scale models of varying filter sizes in Figure 2 clearly demonstrates why a multi-scale approach is necessary: different scales capture different structural properties of the texture. The demonstration that the ReLU non-linearity is essential further strengthens their analysis of which components are truly indispensable.

*   **Explanation of Discrepancy with Prior Work:** A key technical contribution is the identification of an optimization-level detail (scaling the loss and gradients for L-BFGS) that enables random networks to succeed where they previously failed. This is an excellent example of the scientific rigor needed to overturn a previously reported negative result.

*   **Conceptual Contribution:** The discussion in Section 7 is a highlight. The framing of texture synthesis as a trade-off between "perceptual similarity" and "variability" is lucid. The critique of the Maximum Entropy framework's application to finite images, and the proposal to instead define texture models via a perceptual metric `d(x,y)` and an epsilon-environment of acceptable samples, is an important and sophisticated conceptual step forward for the field.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to increase its impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the recommendation to accept.

*   **Model Specification:** The "Random multi-scale" model is the authors' best-performing proposed model, yet its architecture is not explicitly defined in Section 2 alongside the other models. The paper should clearly state the different filter sizes used (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55) and how the 1024 filters are distributed among these scales.
*   **Comparison to Classic Methods:** The paper claims its method "surpasses" classic parametric models like Portilla & Simoncelli (2000). While this is highly plausible, the claim would be much stronger if supported by a direct visual comparison in Figure 4. Including results from this seminal baseline would better contextualize the performance of both the random CNN and the VGG-based models.
*   **Demonstrating the Optimization "Trick":** The insight about scaling the loss function to avoid premature convergence is crucial. A small experiment in the appendix showing the visual difference in synthesis results from a random network with and without this scaling would powerfully illustrate this point and solidify the explanation for the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a).
*   **Variability:** The discussion of variability is excellent. To give readers a more intuitive feel for this, the authors could include a figure in the appendix showing, for a single reference texture, multiple synthesized samples from different random initializations for both their multi-scale model and the VGG model. This would help visualize the diversity of outputs from each model.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

*   You note that your single-layer model is less prone to local minima than the deep VGG model. The appendix is mentioned to contain results from initializing the VGG synthesis with your model's output. Does this initialization strategy provide a consistent and significant improvement across a diverse set of textures? This could suggest a practical two-stage synthesis pipeline.
*   In your experiments with hierarchical random networks (mentioned in the discussion), did you observe a degradation in synthesis quality compared to the single-layer large-filter model, even when the effective receptive field sizes were matched? If so, this might imply that capturing long-range correlations via a single large convolution is fundamentally different or more effective than composing them hierarchically through multiple smaller ones, which would be a very interesting finding.
