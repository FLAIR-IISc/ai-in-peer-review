**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, challenging the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical, and pre-trained feature representations are necessary. The current state-of-the-art, exemplified by Gatys et al. (2015a), relies on matching second-order statistics (Gram matrices) of feature maps from a VGG network trained on ImageNet. The authors question this paradigm by systematically stripping away its core components. Their central and surprising finding is that a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent, multi-scale filters is sufficient to generate textures of a quality that often rivals or even surpasses the deep VGG-based model. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison across various filter types (random, learned, hand-crafted) and sizes, and introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss itself as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality.

**Recommendation: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. Its primary strength lies in its bold, counter-intuitive, and well-supported central claim. By demonstrating the remarkable effectiveness of a simple, random, shallow model, it forces a re-evaluation of the community's understanding of why deep models succeed at generative tasks like texture synthesis. This work provides a crucial new baseline and a significant conceptual contribution, prioritizing a novel and impactful idea over incremental refinement of existing methods.

**Supporting Arguments**

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and Conceptual Impact:** The core finding—that neither network depth nor learned features are indispensable for high-quality texture synthesis—is a significant departure from the prevailing wisdom. At a time when the field was rapidly embracing the idea that features learned for discriminative tasks were the key to generative modeling, this paper provides a powerful and elegant counterexample. It suggests that a sufficiently rich, overcomplete, and multi-scale random projection, combined with the matching of second-order statistics, is enough to capture the perceptual essence of many textures. This is not an incremental improvement; it is a fundamental insight that challenges assumptions and opens up new avenues for research into the nature of texture representations.

2.  **Strong and Systematic Empirical Validation:** The authors do not simply present a single result with a random network. They conduct a rigorous series of experiments comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures. They test filters derived from Fourier bases, k-means clustering on natural images, PCA, and random initialization, across different filter counts. This systematic approach convincingly demonstrates that the success is not due to some accidental property of a specific filter set, but rather a more general principle. The ablation study on filter scales in Figure 2 is particularly effective, clearly illustrating why a multi-scale representation is crucial for capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure, and why simply using a large single-scale filter is suboptimal. The visual evidence presented in Figures 1 and 4 is compelling; the textures generated by the random multi-scale model are often qualitatively indistinguishable from or superior to those from the much more complex VGG model.

3.  **Thoughtful Analysis and Discussion:** The paper goes beyond just presenting results. The authors propose using the VGG-loss as a pragmatic, quantitative measure of texture quality, acknowledging its limitations while using it to support their claims. The discussion in Section 7 is particularly strong, connecting their empirical findings to the broader Maximum Entropy (MaxEnt) framework for texture modeling. They astutely observe the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability and discuss the challenges of finite-size images in this context. This demonstrates a deep understanding of the problem space and elevates the paper from a simple empirical report to a more profound conceptual contribution.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While these points do not detract from my recommendation, they could further strengthen the paper:

*   **Details of the Multi-scale Model:** The architecture of the "Random multi-scale" model, which is the hero of the paper, is not explicitly defined in the methods section. I infer it's a single layer that concatenates feature maps from random filters of different sizes (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, 55x55). Explicitly stating the scales used and the number of filters per scale would improve clarity and reproducibility.

*   **The Role of Optimization:** The authors mention that they scale the loss and gradients by a large factor (10^7) to prevent early stopping and that their simpler model may be less prone to local minima. This is an interesting point. Could the authors elaborate on this? Is the superior performance on regular textures (like brick walls) primarily due to a better representation, or is it that the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow model allows it to find a better minimum for the Gram matrix loss? A brief discussion on the interplay between the model's representational capacity and the optimization dynamics would be insightful.

*   **Exploring Variability:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. While a full psychophysical study is beyond the scope of this work, a simple quantitative experiment could hint at this. For example, by generating multiple samples from the same source texture using different random seeds, what is the average pairwise distance (e.g., in VGG-loss) between them for the proposed model versus the VGG model? This could provide a preliminary quantitative handle on the diversity of the generated samples.

*   **Clarification on VGG-loss Paradox:** The paper notes that for some textures, the VGG-loss is paradoxically lower for samples from the multi-scale model than for the VGG-based model (which directly optimizes that loss). This is a fascinating observation that points to optimization challenges in the deeper model. It would be beneficial to highlight which specific textures in Figure 4 exhibit this behavior to help the reader connect the text to the visual evidence.
