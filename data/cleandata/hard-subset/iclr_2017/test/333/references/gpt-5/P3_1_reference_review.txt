1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper asks what is minimally required to synthesize natural textures using Gram-matrix matching. Contrary to the prevailing belief that depth and supervised pretraining are crucial (as in Gatys et al.), the authors show that a single convolutional layer with random filters and ReLU nonlinearity, coupled with Gram-matrix matching, can produce textures that often rival VGG-based multi-layer models. They:
- Propose a strong minimal baseline: single-layer CNNs with random (and other) filters, sometimes multi-scale via different filter sizes, optimized with L-BFGS-B to match Gram statistics.
- Systematically compare filter types (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means; both 363 and 3267 channels) and a random multi-scale model (up to 55x55) against the VGG-based model.
- Introduce a quantitative evaluation using the VGG Gram loss across layers as a proxy for perceptual similarity (and discuss its limitations).
- Provide qualitative evidence that random multi-scale single-layer models capture small-, mid-, and long-range structures and sometimes obtain lower VGG-loss than VGG-based synthesis (attributed to optimization and reduced variability).
- Discuss the MaxEnt perspective, finite-size effects, and the need to balance perceptual similarity with variability.

2) Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The paper offers a surprising and valuable negative result: depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis when using Gram-based statistics, and a carefully designed single-layer random multi-scale model provides a strong baseline.
- The work is well motivated, clarifies a misconception in prior literature, and explores design choices (filter size, number, nonlinearity, multi-scale) with meaningful insights.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well grounded in prior texture modeling (Julesz; Portilla & Simoncelli; MaxEnt) and deep texture synthesis (Gatys et al.). It also acknowledges related attempts to introduce constraints or improve optimization. Positioning a minimal baseline against this literature is timely and important.
- Methodological clarity: The core pipeline is clear: single-layer convolution + ReLU, compute Gram statistics, optimize an input image via L-BFGS-B under box constraints to match a reference. The exploration of different filter banks and the emphasis on multi-scale and nonlinearity are appropriate and informative. The observation that nonlinearity is essential aligns with the idea that second-order statistics in a nonlinear feature space capture higher-order image statistics of the original signal.
- Empirical support: The qualitative comparisons are convincing as described; the finding that random multi-scale can rival or even outperform VGG-based synthesis under the VGG-loss is important, and the explanation via optimization landscapes and variability is plausible. The ablation suggesting that a mix of filter sizes is crucial (vs. a single large filter bank) is valuable.
- Insights: The MaxEnt discussion and the emphasis on finite-size effects and soft constraints make a useful conceptual contribution. The clarity that Gram statistics are generically useful for stationary feature spaces is helpful.

4) Additional feedback and suggestions (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Evaluation metric bias: Relying primarily on VGG Gram loss to evaluate all methods risks bias, especially when the methods are optimized against/through that representation. Please report cross-metric evaluations (e.g., Gram losses computed from other networks or features, scattering-based distances) and provide aggregate statistics over many textures, not just per-figure losses. A small-scale human 2AFC or inpainting-based psychophysical assessment would strengthen claims about perceptual quality.
- Variability: You rightly raise the similarity–variability trade-off but do not quantify variability. Even simple proxies (diversity across multiple random initializations, patchwise entropy, or distances among samples under multiple feature spaces) would illuminate the trade-off and help interpret the counterintuitive cases where your samples achieve lower VGG-loss than VGG-optimized samples.
- Reproducibility: Please release code and pretrained filter configurations. Provide exact multi-scale architecture details (filter sizes used, number of channels per scale, any normalization of feature maps before Gram computation), random seeds, and full optimization hyperparameters (tolerances and their interaction with the 1e7 scaling).
- Fairness of comparisons: Clarify parameter budgets. VGG-based synthesis uses many more features across multiple layers than your 1024-channel multi-scale model. Report performance curves as a function of number of features to contextualize “minimality.”
- Nonlinearity choice: You show ReLU is necessary. It would be informative to test other common nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, ELU, tanh) to verify robustness.
- Color handling: Some filter banks process channels separately; others mix channels. Please analyze the impact of color mixing on texture quality and confirm that removing the per-channel DC component does not degrade natural color statistics.
- Computational cost: You acknowledge inefficiency of large-kernel convolutions. Provide concrete timing/memory comparisons and detail the hierarchical architectures you tried (depth, pooling, dilation) and why they did not improve quality, even if they improved speed.
- Theoretical framing: The paper would benefit from an explicit connection between Gram matching with random features and kernel methods (e.g., second-order matching in a randomized nonlinear feature space approximates higher-order statistics), and to scattering transforms, to better situate the findings.

Questions for the authors
- What exact filter sizes and channel allocations are used in the multi-scale model, and how sensitive are results to this allocation? Please provide performance vs. number of channels per scale.
- How robust are results across random seeds? Are there failure cases where certain random draws produce poor textures?
- Do you normalize feature maps (e.g., per-channel variance normalization) before computing Gram matrices? If not, how do scale differences across filters affect Gram statistics and synthesis quality?
- Can you report VGG-loss and alternative metrics averaged across a larger texture set with confidence intervals? Do your conclusions hold under alternative feature spaces (e.g., AlexNet, scattering)?
- How many independent samples can you generate per texture before noticeable mode collapse? Can you quantify variability in any way?
- In the Fourier filter experiments, how do results change with and without ReLU? Do you see a direct connection to spectral constraints (power spectrum) when ReLU is removed?
- Can you clarify the “exact reconstruction” anecdote in deeper random CNNs? Under what settings does it occur, and how often?

Overall, this is a thoughtfully executed, insightful paper that establishes a strong minimal baseline and challenges assumptions about the necessity of depth and supervised features in Gram-based texture synthesis. With strengthened evaluation and reproducibility details, it could have substantial impact.
