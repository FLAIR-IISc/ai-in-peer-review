1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper studies what is minimally required to synthesize natural textures via parametric models. It revisits Gram-matrix-based texture synthesis (as in Gatys et al., 2015a) and argues that two elements previously considered critical—depth (multi-layer hierarchies) and supervised pretraining—are not necessary. The authors propose a strong minimal baseline:
- A single-layer CNN with ReLU nonlinearity and random filters, matched via Gram matrices of feature activations.
- A multi-scale single-layer variant with large receptive fields.
They compare several single-layer variants (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA-learned filters, with different numbers of feature maps), and report that random filters can match or surpass alternatives. They use the VGG texture loss (Gram matrices from multiple VGG layers) as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, supplemented by qualitative visual comparisons. They also discuss the balance between perceptual similarity and variability, and relate practical synthesis to maximum-entropy perspectives with soft constraint matching.

2. Decision
Reject, primarily due to:
- Limited novelty: the core mechanism (Gram matching) is known; the main new claim is that single-layer random multi-scale filters suffice, which is an incremental ablation/negative result rather than a substantive new algorithm or theory.
- Insufficient empirical rigor: the evaluation relies heavily on the VGG-based loss (including paradoxes where optimizing it does not produce the lowest value), with no human perceptual study or robust large-scale quantitative analysis to substantiate “rivaling state-of-the-art” claims.

3. Supporting arguments
On the key questions:
- What problem is tackled? Identifying the minimal ingredients required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, and whether depth and supervised pretraining are necessary.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The paper is well situated among texture models (Julesz; Heeger & Bergen; Portilla & Simoncelli) and neural approaches (Gatys et al., Johnson et al., Ulyanov et al., Berger & Memisevic, Liu et al.). Re-examining the necessity of depth and pretraining is a meaningful question. The discussion relating to MaxEnt perspectives and soft constraints is a useful framing.
- Do results support the claims with rigor? Partially. The qualitative samples suggest that single-layer (especially multi-scale) random filters can produce convincing textures on some images. The observation that more feature maps improve results and that ReLUs are necessary is sensible. However:
  - The primary quantitative metric is the VGG-based Gram loss. This is both the proxy for human perception and the optimization target for the comparison model (Gatys et al.), yet the paper notes cases where their method attains lower VGG loss than direct optimization, implicating optimization difficulties. This raises concerns about the validity and stability of the metric as a sole quantitative measure and undermines the fairness of the comparison without deeper analysis.
  - There is no human study (e.g., 2AFC, MUSHRA, inpainting discrimination) to validate perceptual claims.
  - The scope of textures appears limited (~20), and no aggregate statistics (means, variances, significance tests) are provided across a standardized dataset.
  - Some architectural details of the multi-scale model (exact filter sizes per scale, allocation of the 1024 feature maps, normalization) and optimization settings are not fully specified, limiting reproducibility.
Novelty assessment:
- The core idea that untrained/random features with Gram statistics can support texture synthesis has been discussed in prior work (with mixed conclusions). Showing that improved optimization and multi-scale single-layer configurations yield strong results is useful, but primarily as a baseline/ablation rather than a new method. The theoretical discussion on MaxEnt and ε-environments is conceptual and not formalized into new algorithms or theorems.

Clarity and technical soundness:
- The formulation (Gram matrix distance, optimization via L-BFGS-B) is standard and sound. The paper is generally clear, but key details of the multi-scale architecture and evaluation protocol are missing or relegated to figures not present here. References have formatting issues.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Include a human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC between reference vs. synthesized; inpainting-based discrimination) across a larger, standardized texture set. Report inter-rater agreement and statistical significance.
  - Provide aggregate quantitative results across many textures, not just selected examples. Include distributions of VGG loss and, if possible, alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., learned perceptual metrics).
  - Analyze the “optimization paradox”: why does direct optimization of the VGG loss yield higher loss than your method on some textures? Compare optimizers (Adam vs. L-BFGS), initialization schemes, and convergence diagnostics to ensure a fair comparison.
- Clarify and ablate architecture:
  - Provide exact multi-scale configuration: list all filter sizes, number of filters per scale, normalization/initialization, and padding/stride. Include ablations showing the contribution of each scale and total feature count in a controlled manner.
  - Compare against classical multi-scale, oriented filter banks (e.g., steerable pyramids/Gabor/wavelets) to contextualize the role of orientation and multi-scale design vs. randomness. Also compare to scattering transform features (Bruna & Mallat) with Gram matching if feasible.
- Variability vs similarity:
  - Quantify sample diversity across seeds. For example, report within-model distances (VGG loss) among multiple samples conditioned on the same texture, and visualize trade-offs via early stopping or explicit slack variables on Gram constraints.
  - Consider introducing a tunable constraint slack or regularization that explicitly trades off matching strength and diversity; analyze its effect systematically.
- Efficiency and reproducibility:
  - Provide runtime and memory comparisons for single-scale vs multi-scale vs deeper alternatives, and discuss practical deployment.
  - Release code and pretrained filter configurations (even for random seeds) to facilitate replication.
- Theoretical framing:
  - If advancing the MaxEnt perspective, formalize the ε-environment idea with a principled selection of ε (e.g., via concentration bounds or calibration to perceptual thresholds), and analyze how it scales with image size and number of constraints.

Questions to the authors
- Multi-scale architecture: What exact filter sizes and number of filters per scale were used in the multi-scale model? How are feature map counts distributed across scales to reach 1024 total?
- Normalization: How are filters and activations normalized across scales to prevent dominance by larger receptive fields? Are Gram matrices layer- or feature-normalized?
- Optimization: What is the sensitivity to the L-BFGS-B scaling factor (1e7), iteration budget, and initialization? Did you try other optimizers or learning-rate schedules?
- Dataset and statistics: How many textures were used in total? Can you provide aggregate quantitative results (means, variances) of VGG loss across all textures, and not just selected visuals?
- Human evaluation: Do you have any preliminary perceptual user studies (e.g., 2AFC) to support the claim of “rivaling state-of-the-art”? If not, would you consider adding one?
- Variability: How diverse are samples across different random seeds for the same texture at comparable VGG loss? Can you quantify this diversity and relate it to perceived variability?
- Comparisons: How does your random multi-scale model compare to multi-scale oriented filter banks (e.g., steerable pyramid) or scattering features when matched via Gram statistics?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code and random seeds to replicate the reported results and facilitate adoption of the baseline?
