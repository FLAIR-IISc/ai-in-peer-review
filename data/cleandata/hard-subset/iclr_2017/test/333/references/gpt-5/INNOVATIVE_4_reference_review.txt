Summary
This paper asks a fundamental question: what are the minimal ingredients needed to synthesize perceptually convincing natural textures? The central claim is bold and surprising: neither depth nor supervised pretraining is necessary. A single convolutional layer with random filters, combined with Gram-matrix statistics and optimized with L-BFGS, can synthesize textures that often rival the VGG-based state-of-the-art (Gatys et al., 2015). The paper contributes: (1) a strong minimal baseline using single-layer random (and other) filter banks; (2) extensive comparisons across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), scales, and numbers of feature maps; (3) a quantitative evaluation using VGG-based texture loss in lieu of purely visual inspection; and (4) a thoughtful discussion connecting practical synthesis to maximum entropy modeling and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: The work makes a clear, novel, and conceptually impactful claim—showing that random, shallow features suffice for high-quality texture synthesis when paired with Gram statistics and appropriate optimization. This minimal baseline challenges prevailing assumptions about the necessity of depth and pretraining and will likely influence how the community evaluates texture models and interprets deep features. While some evaluations could be strengthened, the novelty and insight outweigh the shortcomings.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and clarity of insight: Demonstrating that a single-layer random, multi-scale filter bank plus Gram statistics can rival VGG-based synthesis is a crisp and important result. It reframes the role of depth/pretraining as largely efficiency and fine-tuning rather than necessity for this domain.
- Thorough ablations: The paper systematically varies filter types, counts (363 vs 3267 vs 1024 multi-scale), sizes (3 to 55), and the presence of nonlinearity. The findings—random ≥ learned filters, multi-scale > single-scale, ReLU essential—are informative.
- Quantitative proxy evaluation: Using VGG Gram-based loss to quantify perceptual similarity is reasonable and strongly correlated with visual quality in most cases. The observation that the minimal model can sometimes achieve lower VGG loss than VGG-optimized synthesis is intriguing and leads to a valuable discussion about variability vs overfitting to perceptual statistics.
- Conceptual contribution: The discussion around MaxEnt, finite-size effects, and explicitly modeling an ε-environment of acceptable deviations is thoughtful and opens new directions for principled texture evaluation and synthesis.

Limitations (not decision critical)
- Human perceptual validation is absent. The proposed VGG-based metric is a reasonable proxy, but the paper itself notes its failure modes (e.g., man-made regular structures) and the importance of variability. A small 2AFC/Turing-test-style study would substantiate claims.
- Variability vs similarity is not quantified. The paper argues convincingly why this matters but does not provide empirical diversity metrics or controlled early-stopping trade-offs.
- Efficiency claims are qualitative. Timing/complexity comparisons (single-layer large filters vs hierarchical) are not reported.
- Missing baselines: A comparison to scattering-based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat) would help position the random filter result relative to structured analytic filter banks.
- Scope: Experiments cover ~20 textures; broader coverage and systematic seed sensitivity could strengthen generality.

Suggestions to improve (to help the authors; not all are decision-critical)
- Add a lightweight human study (e.g., 2AFC on real vs synthesized, or the inpainting task you propose) to corroborate the VGG-loss metric and to probe variability.
- Report runtime and memory for main variants; include L-BFGS iteration counts, convergence behavior, and sensitivity to the 1e7 loss scaling.
- Include a diversity analysis: e.g., pairwise VGG/LPIPS distances among multiple samples per texture at different stopping criteria; visualize mode collapse tendencies.
- Compare against the scattering moments baseline and Portilla–Simoncelli with a modern optimizer, to situate “random vs structured” filters.
- Provide more diagnostics: effect of number of feature maps, receptives-field size, and ReLU on both VGG loss and human judgments (if added).
- Release code and seeds; document exact multi-scale configuration.
- Clarify why optimization from random noise with single-layer statistics is less prone to local minima than VGG; e.g., loss landscape visualizations or empirical success rates.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are your results to the number of feature maps? Is there a threshold after which random filters saturate quality, and how does this interact with scale choices?
2. Can you quantify variability vs similarity (e.g., LPIPS/FID among multiple samples) and show how early stopping or temperature-like mechanisms trade between them?
3. How do results change with other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, tanh) or without channel-wise mean subtraction?
4. Could you include a scattering-based synthesis baseline to disentangle “randomness vs structure” in single-layer models?
5. What are the actual runtimes and memory footprints for the main single-layer multi-scale setup vs a shallow hierarchical alternative?
6. The VGG loss sometimes favors your model over VGG-based synthesis. Can you provide intuition or experiments (e.g., initializing VGG with your outputs) to probe whether this is due to optimization or to the metric’s bias?
7. How robust are results across image resolutions and rescaling? Does the multi-scale configuration need to be adapted?

Overall, this is a compelling and provocative paper. It provides a strong minimal baseline and a valuable perspective on texture synthesis and perceptual statistics. I recommend acceptance.
