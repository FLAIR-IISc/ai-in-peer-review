Summary and contributions
The paper asks a focused and interesting question: what are the minimal ingredients needed to synthesize natural textures? It challenges the prevailing view that depth and supervised pretraining are essential for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. The main claims are: (i) a single-layer CNN with random filters, matching Gram matrices of feature maps, can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality, often rivaling Gatys et al. (2015); (ii) depth and pretraining are not indispensable; (iii) multi-scale random filters (still within a single layer) improve results versus single-scale; (iv) ReLU nonlinearities are important; (v) a VGG-based loss provides a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity; and (vi) a discussion on how finite-size images complicate the MaxEnt framing of texture models. The empirical exploration spans various filter banks (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random), channel couplings, and filter counts, and the paper emphasizes a “strong minimal baseline.”

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical validation is not rigorous enough to support the central claim; evaluation relies almost exclusively on a VGG-based metric and anecdotal visual inspection, without psychophysical/user studies or more neutral metrics. (2) Baseline comparisons against Gatys et al. do not convincingly control for optimization/tuning differences and appear confounded by the choice of evaluation metric. The novelty is primarily empirical and incremental, and the evidence falls short of ICLR standards.

Supporting arguments
- Evaluation metric and potential bias: The paper uses a VGG-based reconstruction loss as its main quantitative measure of perceptual quality. This is problematic because (a) it is not a ground-truth perceptual metric, and (b) it is intrinsically tailored to the Gatys/VGG representation. The paper itself notes cases where their images achieve lower VGG-loss than those produced by optimizing that very loss with VGG, which suggests either optimization/tuning issues or a misalignment between the metric and perceived quality. Without a systematic human study or a more neutral battery of metrics, the claim of “rivaling state-of-the-art” remains unsubstantiated.
- Baseline fairness and optimization confounds: The central narrative hinges on improved optimization (L-BFGS-B with scaled loss/gradients, box constraints). However, it is not clear that the VGG-based baselines are equivalently tuned. Initializing VGG synthesis from the proposed model’s outputs improves Gatys results; this underscores that optimization dominates here and should be controlled carefully. A thorough study is needed: vary optimizers (LBFGS/Adam), step sizes, iteration budgets, initialization schemes, normalization choices, and early-stopping criteria for BOTH methods, and report results across multiple seeds and textures.
- Limited and non-standardized evaluation set: Although the paper shows results on several textures, the set is small and curated. No standardized benchmark or systematic coverage of diverse categories is used. There is no statistical analysis across many seeds per texture to characterize variability versus fidelity.
- Variability not measured: The discussion correctly points out the fidelity–variability trade-off and potential finite-size effects (even exact reconstructions), but there is no concrete measurement or protocol to quantify variability (e.g., repeated syntheses, diversity scores, entropy proxies, or a human inpainting Turing test). This makes it hard to assess whether the proposed approach overfits to the reference statistics or produces genuinely diverse samples.
- Relation to prior art: The observation that non-learned multi-scale representations can be effective (e.g., scattering networks; Portilla-Simoncelli wavelets) is well-known. The novelty here seems to be that purely random filters + Gram matching can be surprisingly strong under careful optimization. While interesting, the conceptual advance is modest and not supported by theory beyond qualitative MaxEnt commentary.
- Efficiency claims lack data: The paper notes that single-layer multi-scale random filters with large receptive fields are inefficient and that hierarchical multi-layer architectures are faster without improving quality, but no runtime, memory, or convergence statistics are provided.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Include a controlled and thorough optimization study for all baselines, with matched compute budgets, initializations, and stopping criteria. Report sensitivity to: number of iterations, step sizes, loss scaling, and box constraints.
- Provide large-scale, standardized evaluation. At minimum: 50–100 textures spanning multiple categories; multiple seeds per texture; report means/variances. Make code, seeds, and texture set public.
- Add a psychophysical evaluation (e.g., two-alternative forced choice for discrimination or inpainting Turing tests) to corroborate claims about perceptual quality. If infeasible, include multiple objective metrics beyond VGG-loss and SSIM (LPIPS, DISTS), and analyze where metrics agree/disagree with human judgments.
- Quantify variability: generate many samples per texture, evaluate inter-sample distances under multiple perceptual metrics, and show trade-off curves between VGG-loss and diversity as stopping criteria vary.
- Compare against additional baselines: scattering-based texture synthesis, Portilla-Simoncelli (with modern implementations), spectrum-constrained CNN methods (e.g., Liu et al.), and nonparametric patch-based methods. Control for parameter count and compute.
- Clarify and fully specify the multi-scale architecture (exact filter sizes, number of filters per scale), normalization of Gram matrices, and pre/post-processing. Provide runtime and memory comparisons.
- Temper claims about “rivaling state-of-the-art” until supported by controlled baselines and user studies.

Questions for the authors
1) How many textures and seeds were used for the quantitative comparisons? Please provide aggregate statistics (mean/variance) for VGG-loss and other metrics across textures and seeds.
2) How were the VGG baselines tuned? Did you perform the same loss/gradient scaling, box constraints, and iteration budgets? Results with and without your improved initialization would be helpful.
3) Please report ablations on optimizer choice (LBFGS vs Adam), iteration count, and initialization (noise vs your model outputs) for both your method and Gatys.
4) What is the exact multi-scale design (filter sizes, counts per scale)? How sensitive are results to this design?
5) Can you provide runtime and memory benchmarks for single-scale, multi-scale, and hierarchical variants on standard image sizes?
6) How do your results compare quantitatively to scattering-based synthesis and Portilla-Simoncelli, beyond anecdotal visuals?
7) Can you quantify variability across multiple samples per texture and analyze the fidelity–diversity trade-off under different stopping criteria?

Overall, the question is timely and the minimal-baseline perspective is valuable, but stronger, controlled evidence is needed to support the central claims.
