Summary and contributions
- The paper asks what is minimally required to synthesize convincing natural textures. It claims that (i) neither depth nor supervised pretraining are necessary; (ii) a single convolutional layer with ReLU and Gram (feature-covariance) statistics suffices, and a random multi-scale single-layer model often rivals the VGG multi-layer style model of Gatys et al.; (iii) random filters can match or outperform hand-crafted or unsupervised filters; (iv) multi-scale filters and nonlinearity are the crucial ingredients; and (v) a VGG-based loss can serve as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity. It further offers a useful discussion reframing texture synthesis within a relaxed MaxEnt view that explicitly allows small mismatches in constraints.
- Empirically, the paper systematically compares filter banks (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random), numbers of channels, and scales, shows ablations (filter size, ReLU on/off), and analyzes optimization (L-BFGS-B with gradient scaling, box constraints). It also reports an interesting observation that initializing VGG-based synthesis from their single-layer results improves the VGG model’s outcome, pointing to optimization effects in deep losses.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons
1) Surprising and useful baseline: Demonstrates convincingly that a simple, single-layer, random, multi-scale Gram model produces high-quality textures, challenging prevailing assumptions and providing a strong minimal baseline for future work.
2) Systematic ablations isolate what matters (multi-scale and nonlinearity) and what does not (depth and pretraining), offering clear scientific insight for the community.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: While Gram-based synthesis is known, showing that a single random-layer with appropriate multi-scale filters often rivals a deep, pretrained VGG pipeline is both non-obvious and practically important. This lowers the bar for strong texture synthesis and clarifies the role of architectural depth vs. statistics used.
- Empirical support: The paper compares multiple filter types and capacities (363 vs. 3267 channels; multi-scale up to 55x55), and shows consistent qualitative gains from more channels and multi-scale, and that ReLU is necessary. The multi-scale random model often matches or surpasses VGG in both qualitative appearance and the reported VGG-based loss, and even improves VGG optimization when used for initialization—evidence that the result is not a fluke of one texture.
- Clarity of insight: The analysis that single-scale large filters capture long-range but miss fine-scale, whereas smaller filters do the opposite, and that multi-scale reconciles this, is compelling and actionable. The MaxEnt discussion usefully formalizes why allowing small mismatches in constraints is essential for variability and practical synthesis.
- Limitations and risks to claims: The main quantitative metric is the VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss, which, while reasonable, is not a neutral gold standard and is correlated with the mechanism under study. The authors are transparent about this and motivate it as a proxy for perception; still, lack of human studies or alternative perceptual metrics weakens the strongest versions of the claims. The test set appears modest (~20 textures), and runtime/efficiency results are limited (acknowledged as a concern for large filters). Some citation formatting is incomplete, and a few architectural specifics (exact per-scale channel counts) are light.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect my decision)
- Evaluation:
  - Add human perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC or inpainting Turing tests) to validate that the VGG-loss correlates with perceived similarity for your models, especially on highly regular man-made textures where you note discrepancies.
  - Report modern reference-free perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) and possibly FID/KID on patch distributions to complement VGG-loss.
  - Quantify variability: Show distribution of VGG-loss and LPIPS across multiple random restarts, and visual diversity grids. Even simple proxies (variance across seeds, nearest-neighbor distances in a patch feature space) would strengthen the variability discussion.
- Fairness and baselines:
  - Compare against scattering-based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat) and spectrum-constrained CNN methods (e.g., Liu et al.) with the same optimization budget.
  - Include the feedforward “Texture Networks” baseline (Ulyanov et al.) for speed-quality trade-offs.
- Method details and ablations:
  - Specify the multi-scale architecture precisely: filter sizes, number of filters per scale, normalization per scale, and initialization distributions. Provide sensitivity to these choices.
  - Explore alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, GELU) and filter normalizations; clarify the role of input mean subtraction and feature normalization on synthesis quality and optimization.
  - Analyze optimization sensitivity: effect of iteration count, gradient scaling (10^7 is a strong heuristic), optimizer choice (Adam vs. L-BFGS-B), and box vs. unconstrained optimization.
- Efficiency:
  - Provide wall-clock times, memory usage, and FLOP estimates for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. hierarchical variants. If a hierarchical version achieves similar quality faster, include it as a practical option.
- Reproducibility:
  - Release code and a texture benchmark set (or point to public ones), with scripts to reproduce all figures and losses. Clean up references and fix missing years/venues.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale design: What exact filter sizes and channel counts per scale were used in the best-performing random multi-scale model? How sensitive are results to reallocating channels across scales?
2) Optimization: How critical is the 10^7 scaling for L-BFGS-B? Could you show convergence curves and outcomes across a grid of scalings/optimizers? Does Adam with a tuned schedule reach comparable minima?
3) Variability: How diverse are samples across random initializations for a fixed target texture? Can you provide quantitative dispersion measures and example grids?
4) Metric dependence: Do your qualitative conclusions hold if you evaluate with LPIPS, SSIM, or scattering-distance metrics? Are there cases where VGG-loss ranks methods incorrectly relative to human judgments?
5) Over-constraint: You mention exact reconstructions in deeper random CNNs. Can you characterize when this happens (filter counts vs. image size) and how introducing an epsilon-tolerance or early stopping affects diversity?
6) Efficiency: What are typical runtimes for 512x512 textures for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. VGG-based synthesis under your settings? Any memory bottlenecks for 55x55 filters?

Overall, this paper offers a compelling, well-argued minimal baseline and clear insights into what matters for texture synthesis. Strengthening the evaluation and clarifying details would make it an even more impactful contribution.
