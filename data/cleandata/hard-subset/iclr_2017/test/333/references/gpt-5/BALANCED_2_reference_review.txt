1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper asks a focused question: which ingredients are truly necessary to synthesize convincing natural textures? The main claim is that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are indispensable. A single convolutional layer with random filters, combined with Gram-matrix matching and proper optimization, can synthesize textures that often rival the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. The authors:

- Propose a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random filters and ReLU, optimized to match Gram matrices.
- Explore a range of single-layer filter sets (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random), numbers of feature maps, and a multi-scale variant (filters up to 55×55).
- Show the importance of multi-scale filters and the ReLU nonlinearity.
- Evaluate using a VGG-based Gram loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, and report qualitative results on about 20 textures.
- Offer a conceptual discussion relating practical synthesis to MaxEnt and to the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Overall, the work is framed as a careful minimality study that questions the necessity of hierarchical pretrained representations for texture synthesis and provides a robust baseline.

2) Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- The empirical evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous: it relies heavily on qualitative figures and a VGG-based loss as the primary quantitative metric, without human studies or robust alternative measures.
- The novelty and placement in the literature are incomplete: the connection to existing shallow, fixed-filter approaches (e.g., scattering-based texture synthesis) and kernel/MMD perspectives is not deeply developed, and the contribution risks being seen as an optimization/tuning refinement of known Gram-matching ideas.

3) Supporting arguments
- On the question/problem: The question is clear and valuable—establishing minimal ingredients for texture synthesis is important for understanding and benchmarking. The paper convincingly asks whether depth and pretraining are necessary and isolates other factors (multi-scale, nonlinearity, number of features).
- Motivation and literature placement: The work is well motivated by Gatys et al.’s success and the desire for simpler baselines. However, the connection to fixed, hand-crafted representations (Portilla–Simoncelli; scattering networks) and to kernel/maximum mean discrepancy interpretations of Gram-based matching is underdeveloped. This matters because those lines of work already argue that second-order statistics in appropriate feature spaces can be powerful without learned deep hierarchies.
- Do the results support the claims? The qualitative results are strong and the multi-scale random model often looks competitive. Yet:
  - The VGG-based loss is a circular proxy (it privileges similarity in the very representation underlying the current state-of-the-art). The authors note paradoxes (their method sometimes yields lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization), highlighting the fragility of this metric as a ground truth.
  - No human perceptual evaluation is provided, despite the paper’s central claim being perceptual. Even a small-scale 2AFC study or an inpainting-based psychophysics test would strengthen claims dramatically.
  - Variability/diversity is acknowledged as important but not measured. The paper argues conceptually for an ε-environment rather than hard constraints, but does not quantify sample diversity or trade-offs (e.g., via patch discrimination tests, entropy bounds, or surrogate metrics).
  - Robustness and statistics: results are shown for ~20 textures, with no systematic statistical analysis across seeds, feature counts, filter scales, or optimization hyperparameters (e.g., the pivotal gradient scaling by 1e7). Claims that earlier failures arose from optimization differences deserve ablation.
  - Baselines and fairness: There is no quantitative comparison to Portilla–Simoncelli or scattering-based synthesis (Bruna & Mallat), and no timing/memory analysis. The single-layer model is acknowledged to be inefficient, but no measurements are given. 
  - Reproducibility: Implementation details are partially specified, but code/data availability is not clearly provided; some citations and references are incomplete.

In short, the paper provides compelling qualitative evidence and a welcome baseline, but lacks the quantitative and perceptual rigor expected for a definitive claim that depth and pretraining are not necessary.

4) Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Add a small psychophysical study (e.g., 2AFC or the proposed inpainting test) to correlate VGG-loss with human judgments and to measure variability. Even 20–30 participants over a curated texture set would be valuable.
- Provide systematic ablations:
  - Optimization: L-BFGS-B vs. Adam; effect of gradient scaling; iteration budgets; box constraints; initialization.
  - Architecture: ReLU vs. other nonlinearities; effect of biases; number of features per scale; exact multi-scale configuration; normalization/orthogonalization of filters; effect of color mean subtraction.
  - Randomness: report means and variances across random seeds for loss and perceptual scores.
- Compare quantitatively to Portilla–Simoncelli and scattering-based synthesis (including run time and resolution scaling). Given the conceptual proximity, this is essential for novelty.
- Theoretical framing: connect Gram matching with random features to kernel two-sample testing/MMD; discuss conditions under which random features plus Gram statistics approximate matching of higher-order pixel statistics (via the nonlinear lift and overlap constraints).
- Efficiency: quantify time/memory vs. VGG-based synthesis; assess whether a shallow hierarchical random net (with small kernels) can match the single-layer large-kernel performance with speed gains.
- Diversity: adopt a measurable diversity proxy (e.g., train a patch classifier to distinguish real vs. synthesized patches from the same texture; report its accuracy as a variability indicator at matched perceptual loss).
- Clarify dataset, image sizes, and failure cases; standardize and clean references.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are your results to the number of random features and to the random seed? Please provide averages and standard deviations of VGG-loss (and, ideally, perceptual judgments) across seeds.
- What is the exact multi-scale architecture (kernel sizes, number of filters per scale), and how does performance scale with each component? Is there an optimal allocation across scales?
- Can you provide timing/memory comparisons to the VGG-based method for the same output resolution and target loss? How does the hierarchical shallow random net compare in practice?
- How does your method compare quantitatively to scattering-based texture synthesis on the same texture set?
- How do different nonlinearities (ReLU, leaky ReLU, tanh) affect results? Is the benefit of ReLU due to sparsity or other properties?
- Can you report a simple variability measure (e.g., patch discrimination or inter-sample VGG-loss) to substantiate the similarity/variability trade-off?
- Are code and texture sets planned for release to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, this is an interesting and clarifying baseline study with appealing qualitative results. With stronger empirical rigor and broader comparisons, it could make a valuable contribution.
