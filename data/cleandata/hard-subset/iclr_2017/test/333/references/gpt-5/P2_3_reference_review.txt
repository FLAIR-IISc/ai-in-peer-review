1. Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: which elements are truly necessary to synthesize natural textures? It proposes a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random (data-independent) filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix statistics of feature maps. The authors show:
- Single-layer, even random-feature models can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality.
- A multiscale variant (mixed filter sizes up to 55×55) substantially improves results, often rivaling VGG-based multi-layer texture models (Gatys et al.).
- Training filters (k-means/PCA) is not essential; random features perform on par or better given enough channels.
- ReLU nonlinearity is necessary; depth helps efficiency but not quality.
- They introduce a VGG-based reconstruction loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity and discuss the MaxEnt perspective and the trade-off between similarity and variability.

Overall, the paper is thoughtfully positioned within the texture synthesis literature (Julesz; Heeger & Bergen; Portilla & Simoncelli; Gatys et al.) and offers an impactful conceptual message: depth and supervised pretraining are not indispensable for texture modeling when using Gram statistics.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient quantitative and perceptual evaluation to substantiate the central “often rivals state of the art” claim.
- Incomplete methodological details and presentation issues (important specifics of the multiscale model, optimization, and comparisons are missing/underspecified).

3. Supporting arguments
- Evaluation rigor: The paper relies primarily on qualitative figures and a VGG-based loss. While VGG-based loss is a reasonable proxy, it doubles as the objective for the Gatys baseline, raising concerns about circularity and sensitivity to architecture. No human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC) is provided, and there is no cross-network evaluation (e.g., using different backbones for assessment) to mitigate proxy bias. Claims like “often rival” and “outperform” need systematic quantification across a well-defined dataset, with multiple random seeds and statistical analysis.
- Variability vs. similarity: The discussion rightly emphasizes this trade-off, but the experiments optimize only for similarity. There is no attempt to quantify variability (sample diversity), even with simple proxies (e.g., multiple synthesis runs per texture with distances among samples). The anecdote about exact reconstruction in deeper random CNNs underscores the risk of over-constraining without demonstrating controlled variability.
- Methodological clarity: Key details of the multiscale random model are missing (scales used, number of filters per scale, initialization ranges, exact aggregation of Gram constraints across scales). The optimization “tuning” (scaling the loss by 1e7) is cited as critical, yet there is no ablation to show sensitivity to optimizer, step size, iterations, or box constraints. ReLU is shown to be important, but alternatives (leaky ReLU, tanh, modulus) are not explored.
- Fairness and efficiency: The proposed single-layer models use very large filters (up to 55×55) and thousands of channels. There are no runtime or memory comparisons against VGG-based synthesis. The claim that hierarchical models mainly improve efficiency is plausible, but it should be backed by empirical timing and convergence results.
- Comparisons: The paper cites Portilla & Simoncelli and scattering-based methods (Bruna & Mallat), but does not provide side-by-side results. The Fourier-basis and k-means comparisons are helpful, but the absence of a scattering baseline is a missed opportunity given its conceptual proximity (fixed multiscale filters plus second-order stats).
- Writing and presentation: Several references are incomplete, and some text has placeholders. Figures are central to the argument but the paper relies on them without robust quantification. This detracts from the scientific rigor needed for the strength of the claims.

4. Suggestions for improvement (constructive, not all required for acceptance)
- Evaluation
  - Add human perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC on real vs. synthesized patches, or inpainting tests as you suggest).
  - Report cross-network evaluation: assess with multiple pretrained nets (e.g., VGG, ResNet, AlexNet) and metrics not used in any objective to reduce circularity.
  - Quantify variability: generate multiple samples per texture and measure inter-sample distances in perceptual feature space; report trade-off curves (iterations vs. diversity).
  - Expand the texture set and provide statistical summaries (means, variances, significance tests).
- Method and ablations
  - Fully specify the multiscale model: filter sizes, number of filters per scale, initialization distribution, and how Gram constraints across scales are aggregated.
  - Provide optimizer ablations (L-BFGS-B vs. Adam, scaling factors, iteration budgets) and convergence plots for objective and evaluation metrics.
  - Explore nonlinearity choices (ReLU variants, modulus) and padding schemes; report their effects.
  - Measure and report compute cost (time, memory) vs. Gatys et al., and also for hierarchical versions you experimented with.
- Comparisons
  - Include a scattering transform baseline (fixed wavelet filters and second-order stats).
  - Provide direct comparisons to Portilla & Simoncelli under the same evaluation protocol.
- Theory and framing
  - Clarify the MaxEnt discussion by proposing a concrete epsilon-tolerant objective, or by introducing a principled stochastic synthesis scheme to sample from the epsilon-set.
  - Discuss how the number of channels and spatial overlap of convolutions relate to constraint redundancy and over/under-constrained regimes.
- Reproducibility
  - Release code, seeds, and a list of textures. Document preprocessing (mean subtraction, whitening), normalization, and exact loss normalizations used in Eqs. (2–7).
- Writing
  - Fix broken references and polish the exposition. Integrate Appendix material (e.g., grid examples, SSIM) into the main evaluation where relevant.

Questions for the authors
- Multiscale model details: Which filter sizes are used, how many filters per scale, and how are Gram matrices combined across scales? Are filters independent across scales?
- Optimization: How sensitive are results to loss scaling, optimizer choice, iteration count, and initialization? Can you provide convergence traces and failure cases?
- Variability: How diverse are multiple samples for a fixed texture? Can you quantify this and illustrate trade-offs between similarity and variability?
- Efficiency: What are the runtime and memory costs for single-layer multiscale vs. VGG-based synthesis on standard image sizes?
- Cross-network evaluation: Do your conclusions hold when evaluated with a different perceptual network than VGG-19 (e.g., ResNet-50) or with other metrics?
- Comparisons: How does your method compare to scattering-based synthesis on the same texture set and evaluation metrics?
- Generality: Do results hold for larger textures, out-of-distribution textures (e.g., stochastic vs. highly regular), and grayscale vs. color?
