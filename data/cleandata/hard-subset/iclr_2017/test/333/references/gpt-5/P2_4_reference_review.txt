1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper asks: what are the minimal ingredients needed for high-quality natural texture synthesis? It argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are necessary: a single convolutional layer with random filters, combined with Gram-matrix (second-order) statistics and ReLU, can synthesize textures that often rival Gatys et al. (2015). The authors:
- Establish a strong minimal baseline: single-layer CNNs (stride 1, ReLU, zero-padding), with various filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), primarily 11×11, and a multi-scale variant mixing several filter sizes (up to 55×55).
- Optimize an image to match feature Gram matrices, using L-BFGS-B with some practical tweaks (loss/gradient scaling, box constraints).
- Propose to use the VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity; present qualitative results across ~20 textures; analyze the roles of number of feature maps, scale, and nonlinearity.
- Discuss the MaxEnt perspective, the role of soft constraint matching in finite images, and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Overall, the paper is clear, well-motivated by the literature on parametric texture models and neural style/texture synthesis, and the central finding (random features + Gram suffice) is interesting as a minimal baseline and scientific insight.

2) Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- The central empirical claims (“rivals state-of-the-art”) are not rigorously substantiated: evaluation relies mostly on visuals and a single proxy metric (VGG-based loss) that is known to be imperfect and, paradoxically, sometimes favors their method over the model it is derived from. No human study or robust complementary metrics are provided.
- Important experimental details and quantitative summaries are missing or insufficient (e.g., precise multi-scale architecture, distribution of results across a large texture set, diversity/variability assessment), limiting reproducibility and confidence.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Strong. The question is timely and relevant; the paper is well placed among classic parametric models (Portilla & Simoncelli), style/texture with deep features (Gatys), and alternatives (scattering, feedforward texture nets).
- Novelty/insight: Moderate but meaningful. Showing that random, single-layer features with Gram statistics can provide high perceptual quality is a useful, counterintuitive baseline. The multi-scale observation (small, medium, large filters jointly needed) is plausible and valuable. The MaxEnt discussion is thoughtful.
- Methodological rigor: Mixed.
  - Positives: Ablations on number of feature maps, filter types (random vs learned/hand-crafted), and nonlinearity. Clear optimization description. The use of VGG-loss as a proxy is reasonable as one lens.
  - Concerns: The quantitative evaluation rests on the same Gram-loss family used for synthesis (and on a pretrained VGG); the paper itself notes failures on regular man-made textures and paradoxes (multi-scale having lower VGG-loss than VGG-based synthesis), which undermines relying on this metric alone. There is no user study (e.g., 2AFC perceptual indistinguishability) or alternative perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS) and no analysis of sample variability (diversity), despite discussing its importance. No statistical analysis across a standardized and broad texture benchmark; results are presented texture-by-texture without aggregate numbers.
  - Missing details: The exact multi-scale composition (filter sizes used, counts per scale, normalization/initialization per scale) is not fully specified; boundary handling (zero vs reflect/circular padding) may matter for textures; the set of textures and selection protocol is not described in detail. Code release is not indicated.
- Claims vs evidence: Statements such as “often rivals the state of the art” would require either a human study or stronger quantitative aggregates across many textures with multiple metrics and careful optimization parity (e.g., ensuring the VGG-based baseline is well-tuned). The paradox noted by the authors suggests optimization or metric mismatch rather than a clear superiority.

4) Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors; not all are gating)
- Evaluation:
  - Add a small-scale human study (e.g., 2AFC discrimination or inpainting-based test) to validate perceptual indistinguishability and assess variability/diversity. Even 15–20 participants on a curated set would strengthen claims.
  - Report aggregate quantitative results across a larger, standardized texture set; include multiple metrics (VGG-loss, LPIPS; possibly SSIM with caveats) and summaries (means, variances).
  - Quantify diversity: show distributions over distances between multiple samples for the same reference; report entropy proxies (e.g., patch retrieval diversity, inpainting fool rates).
- Baselines and parity:
  - Ensure optimization parity with the VGG baseline (iterations, initialization protocols, LBFGS vs Adam variants, loss scaling). Show that “initializing VGG with single-layer outputs” improves VGG results, but also try the reverse initialization to rule out unfair advantages.
  - Compare to scattering-based texture synthesis (fixed, multiscale filters with second-order stats) as a particularly relevant baseline for “no learning, multiscale fixed features.”
- Method/analysis details:
  - Precisely define the multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, number of filters per scale, initialization, normalization across scales), padding type, and per-scale regularization. Provide a quantitative ablation varying these.
  - Analyze the role of nonlinearity: compare ReLU to other monotone nonlinearities (leaky-ReLU, abs, tanh) to test whether symmetry-breaking or sparsity is the key.
  - Provide runtime/memory comparisons; discuss efficiency trade-offs and possible hierarchical speed-ups more concretely.
- Reproducibility: Release code and a script to reproduce figures and tables.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact composition of the multi-scale model (filter sizes used, number of filters per scale, initialization variance per scale, and padding type)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- How large is the texture set used in evaluation, and how were textures selected? Can you provide aggregate quantitative summaries across all textures?
- Did you attempt a small-scale user study (2AFC) or an inpainting-based psychophysics test, as proposed in the discussion? If not, can you include one in a revision?
- How do results compare to scattering-based texture synthesis (fixed wavelet filter banks with second-order statistics) under matched conditions?
- Can you quantify sample diversity for your model versus Gatys et al. (e.g., pairwise VGG/LPIPS distances across multiple synthesized samples per texture)?
- The paradox where multi-scale achieves lower VGG-loss than VGG-optimized synthesis: can you diagnose whether this is due to optimization issues (e.g., local minima, loss scaling) or metric mismatch? Have you tried stronger VGG synthesis settings or different pooling?
- What is the effect of padding choice (zero vs reflect/circular) on texture quality and border artifacts?

Despite the rejection, I find the core insight promising; with stronger, more rigorous evaluation and clearer methodological specification, this work could become a useful baseline and a clarifying contribution to the field.
