Summary and contributions
The paper asks whether depth and supervised pretraining are truly necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It proposes a minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU nonlinearity and random (or simple hand-crafted/unsupervised) filters, optimized to match Gram matrices of feature maps, à la Gatys et al. The authors explore several single-scale architectures (363 vs 3267 feature maps; 11×11 filters; Fourier, k-means, PCA, random) and a “multi-scale” single-layer model combining multiple filter sizes (up to 55×55; 1024 filters). They evaluate primarily with a VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss (as a proxy for perceptual similarity), and argue: (1) random single-layer models can synthesize textures of competitive quality; (2) multi-scale random models often rival a VGG-based model; (3) ReLUs and combining multiple filter sizes are crucial; (4) variability vs similarity is an underappreciated trade-off; (5) the MaxEnt perspective should be extended to allow finite-tolerance constraints.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical rigor and clarity: heavy reliance on a circular VGG-loss metric without human studies or robust alternative quantitative metrics; missing, inconsistent, or incorrect methodological details and equations; numerous formatting/citation errors impede reproducibility.
- Baseline fairness and ablations are incomplete: the VGG baseline is modified (avg pooling), multi-scale architecture is under-specified, optimization choices are ad hoc and seem central to the result but are not systematically analyzed.

Supporting arguments
- Evaluation validity and completeness: The main quantitative metric is the VGG texture loss; however, the paper notes cases where the single-layer model achieves lower VGG-loss than an image produced by optimizing that very loss with VGG, indicating optimization pathologies and undermining the metric as a reliable comparator across methods. No human perceptual study is provided. No alternative metrics (beyond a brief SSIM mention) or psychophysical protocols are actually executed. There is no quantitative analysis of sample diversity (variability), and no statistical summaries over a standardized texture dataset.
- Baseline comparability: The VGG baseline deviates from prior work (average pooling instead of max pooling), which is known to affect results. It is unclear whether the VGG synthesis uses the same weights, layer set, and normalizations as the original, making the comparison potentially unfair. No comparisons to scattering-based texture models or to classical Portilla & Simoncelli are shown quantitatively; claims of surpassing prior art are primarily qualitative.
- Methodological precision and reproducibility: The multi-scale model is insufficiently specified: exact set of filter sizes, number of filters per scale, filter normalization, and initialization distributions per scale are not documented. The L-BFGS-B setup uses a critical scaling of loss/gradients by 1e7 “to avoid early stopping,” but there is no ablation on scaling factor, stopping tolerances, or iteration counts. The sensitivity to initialization (random noise vs other) is not reported. The spatial sizes of textures, memory/computation costs, and timing are not reported.
- Equation and notation issues: x ∈ RM is used both for pixels and as the dimension defining F ∈ RN×M (number of spatial locations), creating ambiguity. In dp(x,y) (Eq. 3), the summation index uses N instead of M, likely a typo that makes the expression incorrect as written. In Eℓ, the notations for N and M per layer are ambiguous (Nℓ and Mℓ are not defined). Normalization constants differ from standard Gatys choices without justification. These issues hinder reproducibility and trust.
- Filter bank definitions: The Fourier basis filters (11×11) are claimed to be real; it is unclear how the DC component is handled given input mean subtraction. For Fourier-3267, the use of discrete RGB weightings [1, 0, −1] multiplies filters; redundancy and normalization across channels are not discussed. For k-means filters, the whitening method (ZCA/PCA, per-channel/per-patch) is unspecified; for PCA-363, whether channels are concatenated and how eigenvectors are reshaped/normalized is not detailed. Random initialization references Glorot & Bengio but does not state fan-in/out accounting for convolution.
- Scope and novelty: The central claim—that random single-layer CNN features with Gram statistics can be competitive—is interesting and potentially impactful. However, the paper’s own discussion acknowledges that optimization choices largely explain discrepancies with earlier negative results for random weights. The work would be strengthened by a systematic study showing when depth or pretraining help (e.g., speed/quality tradeoffs, sample variability) and by decoupling representational power from optimization artifacts.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide a standardized benchmark suite of textures, quantitative results (mean and variance across seeds), and a small-scale human study (2AFC or MOS) to validate the VGG-loss surrogate.
- Fully specify the multi-scale architecture: filter sizes, per-scale filter counts, initialization, normalization; provide code and seeds. Report runtime/memory vs image size.
- Replace ad hoc gradient scaling with principled optimizer settings; ablate stopping tolerances, iterations, and box constraints. Compare L-BFGS-B to Adam/L-BFGS without scaling.
- Ensure fair VGG baseline: match layers, pooling, and normalizations to Gatys et al., or report both max/avg pooling variants.
- Correct equations/notation, define all symbols per layer, and align normalization to prior work or justify deviations.
- Expand ablations: number of feature maps, effect of removing ReLU or using leaky-ReLU, filter normalization, effect of per-layer Gram normalization, and per-scale contributions.
- Improve references (many entries are malformed or incomplete), remove “personal communication” or anonymize per policy, and ensure figures referred to (Figs. 1–7) are present with captions and quantitative annotations.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact multi-scale filter sizes and the number of filters per scale? How are filters initialized and normalized per scale?
- How sensitive are results to the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling and L-BFGS-B tolerances? Can you reproduce your main claims without scaling by tuning optimizer parameters?
- Which VGG layers are used precisely? Are results different with max pooling (original) vs average pooling?
- What is the texture dataset, image sizes, and how many seeds per texture? Can you report mean±std VGG-loss and a simple human 2AFC study?
- How do you normalize Gram matrices across scales/channels? Do you whiten feature maps before Gram computation?
- Can you quantify variability (e.g., LPIPS diversity, patch classification accuracy, or inpainting test) to support the similarity–variability trade-off?
- Do Fourier filters include the DC component? Given input mean subtraction, does this create degenerate filters?
- In Eq. 3, should the summation index be over M (pixels) rather than N? Please correct and clarify the notation for all equations.
