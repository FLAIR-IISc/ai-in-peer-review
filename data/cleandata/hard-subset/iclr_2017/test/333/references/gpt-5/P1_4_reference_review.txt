Summary and contributions
The paper asks a focused question: what elements are truly necessary to synthesize natural textures? It claims that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are indispensable. The main contribution is a strong, minimal baseline: a single convolutional layer with ReLU nonlinearity, random filters, and Gram-matrix matching of feature activations can synthesize textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality. The authors conduct a broad set of comparisons across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA-trained, hand-crafted), numbers of feature maps (363 vs 3267), and receptive field sizes, including a multi-scale single-layer variant. They also propose to use the VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, replacing purely qualitative evaluation, and offer a thoughtful discussion linking their observations to the MaxEnt view and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The core empirical finding—that a shallow, random, multi-scale CNN with Gram statistics rivals the VGG-based state of the art on many textures—is both surprising and practically useful. It recalibrates assumptions about the necessity of depth and supervised pretraining for texture synthesis, and establishes a valuable baseline.
- The paper is clear, well-motivated in the literature, and contains informative ablations (filters, scales, nonlinearity, number of maps) that support the main claim.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work is well placed with respect to parametric texture synthesis, Gatys et al. (2015), and prior reports that random weights fail. The emphasis on minimality and on identifying indispensable pieces is valuable for understanding what makes Gram-matrix-based synthesis work.
- Approach: The use of single-layer CNNs with ReLU, standard convolution, and Gram matrices is straightforward and replicable. The multi-scale variant is a natural, principled way to capture correlations across spatial extents without hierarchical pooling.
- Evidence: Qualitative results (on ~20 textures) show small- and mid-scale structure captured well across multiple variants; multi-scale improves long-range consistency. The ablation indicating the necessity of both multi-scale filtering and nonlinearity is compelling. The observation that VGG-based optimization improves when initialized from the single-layer solution suggests part of the gap in prior negative results with random networks was due to optimization, not representational limits. Reports that the multi-scale model sometimes attains lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization are consistent with local minima in the deeper model.
- Rigor and limitations:
  - Strengths: Broad comparison across filter types and capacities; systematic manipulation of receptive field size; clear loss definition and optimization setup; discussion of the similarity–variability trade-off and finite-size effects in MaxEnt.
  - Weaknesses: Evaluation relies heavily on the VGG loss as a perceptual proxy and qualitative inspection; there is no human subject study or independent perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS) to corroborate claims. The dataset of textures and aggregate statistics across many examples are not systematically reported (e.g., average VGG loss across a standardized set). Computational efficiency and memory usage of large spatial filters are not quantified. Diversity/variability of samples is acknowledged but not measured. Comparisons with scattering-based or classic Portilla–Simoncelli models are not quantitatively presented. Finally, the claim that supervised pretraining and depth are not necessary is demonstrated for stationary textures; generality beyond textures is not addressed (which is fair but should be scoped accordingly).

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a quantitative summary over a standardized texture set: average and variance of VGG loss (and an alternative metric such as LPIPS) for each model variant, plus pairwise human preference on a small-scale study if possible.
- Report runtime and memory for single-scale and multi-scale random models versus VGG-based synthesis, and include the efficiency gains for hierarchical multi-layer random networks you mention (architecture and numbers).
- Clarify multi-scale architecture details precisely: scales used, number of filters per scale, total feature map count, and how feature maps are concatenated and normalized before Gram computation. Include sensitivity to the number of maps per scale.
- Include ablations on optimization: sensitivity to the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling, number of L-BFGS iterations, box constraints, and initialization schemes. This will better support the claim that optimization, not representation, caused prior failures with random nets.
- Add diversity analysis: e.g., intra-model VGG/LPIPS distance between multiple samples at matched loss thresholds; a simple 2AFC inpainting or oddity task would be informative (even with a small N).
- Compare quantitatively to Portilla–Simoncelli and scattering-based synthesis on the same texture set using the same metrics.
- Discuss failure cases: which texture classes (e.g., strong perspective, non-stationary patterns) fail, and why.
- Release code and texture list to facilitate adoption of the proposed strong baseline.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: What exact filter sizes and counts per scale are used? How are feature map variances normalized across scales before computing the Gram matrices? Did you try cross-scale Gram statistics?
- Optimization: How critical is the 1e7 scaling factor and the 2000-iteration budget? Do results degrade significantly with Adam or LBFGS settings closer to Gatys et al.? How many seeds/initializations were tried per texture, and how variable are outcomes?
- Capacity fairness: Your single-layer models sometimes have thousands of feature maps. How do results vary when you match the total number of constraints (i.e., Gram entries) across models, including the VGG-based one?
- Diversity: At a fixed target VGG loss, how diverse are multiple samples from your model? Could you report intra-set distances or show failure to exactly reconstruct the exemplar as the image size increases?
- Efficiency: For the hierarchical multi-layer random CNNs you explored, can you share concrete architectures, speedups, and whether quality truly matches the single-layer multi-scale variant?
- Generality: Do results hold across image resolutions and color spaces (e.g., luminance-chrominance)? How sensitive are conclusions to input mean subtraction/whitening choices?

Overall, this is a clear, insightful paper that provides a valuable minimal baseline and stimulates rethinking about what makes neural texture synthesis work. Addressing the evaluation and efficiency points would further strengthen it.
