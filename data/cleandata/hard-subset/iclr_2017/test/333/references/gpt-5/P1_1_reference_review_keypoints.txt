- Paper asks what is necessary for natural texture synthesis with CNN-derived parametric models, claiming depth and pretraining are not required.  
- Main claim: a single convolutional layer with random filters plus Gram-matrix statistics and proper optimization can rival VGG-based multi-layer models.  
- Contribution: proposes a minimal baseline—single-layer ReLU CNNs, including multi-scale random-filter variants optimized to match Gram statistics.  
- Empirical finding: comparing many single-layer feature banks (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means) shows more maps and multi-scale design matter more than learned features or depth.  
- Experimental scope: evaluates different filter counts (363 vs 3267), filter sizes, and multi-scale combinations across 20 textures with visual comparisons.  
- Quantitative evaluation: uses VGG-based Gram-loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity in addition to visual results.  
- Conceptual contribution: discusses perceptual similarity vs sample variability and frames texture modeling via distance metrics and ε-neighborhoods under MaxEnt constraints.  
- Decision: Accept (borderline) because the baseline is surprising and the empirical study is broad and careful.  
- Strength: Demonstrates that random, single-layer, multi-scale Gram-constrained models can be highly competitive and set a strong baseline for future work.  
- Strength: Ablations convincingly show ReLU is critical and that combining scales captures both local color/statistics and long-range structure.  
- Strength: Random filters perform on par or better than learned feature banks across tested settings.  
- Strength: Practical optimization improvements (L-BFGS-B with box constraints and loss scaling) help reproduce good results with random networks.  
- Observation: Initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer results can improve final synthesis outcomes.  
- Limitation: Heavy reliance on VGG Gram-loss risks circularity and does not ensure human perceptual equivalence; no psychophysical study is provided.  
- Limitation: Texture set is modest (20 images); aggregate quantitative summaries (means, stds) and seed variability are not systematically reported.  
- Limitation: Details of multi-scale architecture, filter normalization, and optimization sensitivity are insufficiently specified for full reproducibility.  
- Limitation: Multi-scale models with very large filters are computationally heavy; runtime/memory benchmarks vs VGG are missing.  
- Limitation: No theoretical analysis explaining why random features plus Gram suffice or how many features are needed per texture class.  
- Suggested improvements: add average VGG-loss ± std, failure cases, and sensitivity to random seeds.  
- Suggested improvements: provide full multi-scale design details and runtime/memory comparisons versus VGG synthesis.  
- Suggested improvements: include sensitivity analyses for loss scaling, iterations, initialization, alternative nonlinearities, and Gram normalization.  
- Suggested improvements: add a small user study or perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS) to validate VGG-loss correspondence to human judgments.  
- Suggested improvements: report sample diversity across multiple synthesized outputs and release code and filter definitions for reproducibility.  
- Key open questions posed to authors: exact filter sizes/counts per scale, filter normalization and DC treatment, whether filters mix RGB channels, sensitivity to optimization choices, tests of other nonlinearities, compute/memory costs, sample variability across seeds, and generality across image sizes and non-stationary/structured textures.
