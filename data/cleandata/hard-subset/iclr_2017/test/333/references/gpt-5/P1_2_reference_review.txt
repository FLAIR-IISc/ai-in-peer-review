1. Summary and contributions
The paper asks what is truly necessary to synthesize perceptually convincing natural textures within the “Gram-matrix matching” paradigm popularized by Gatys et al. The authors argue that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is necessary: a single convolutional layer with ReLU and randomly initialized filters, combined with Gram matrices of feature activations and L-BFGS-B optimization, can often match or rival textures produced by the standard VGG multi-layer model. They:

- Propose a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random filters (and a multi-scale variant) whose Gram matrices define the texture statistics.
- Systematically compare different single-layer filter banks (random, Fourier basis, k-means, PCA; 11×11 by default; 363 vs 3267 channels) and a random multi-scale model (up to 55×55 filters) against the VGG texture model.
- Provide ablations: number of features, filter size, impact of multi-scale vs single scale, role of ReLU.
- Quantify quality using the VGG-based multi-layer loss as a surrogate for perceptual distance, while also discussing the trade-off between “perceptual similarity” and sample “variability” and relating practical synthesis to maximum-entropy modeling.

Overall, the paper promotes a simple, insightful baseline, and empirically demonstrates that random, shallow, multi-scale convolutional features paired with Gram statistics are surprisingly powerful.

2. Decision: Reject (main reasons: limited empirical rigor and incomplete positioning)
- The main claim—that random, single-layer, multi-scale CNNs with Gram statistics can rival deep, pretrained VGG-based synthesis—is interesting and practically valuable. However, the evaluation is largely qualitative and uses a surrogate metric that is not independent of the competing method. There is no human study, limited diversity analysis, and little statistical evidence to support “rivals SOTA” beyond cherry-picked examples.
- The work is not sufficiently situated against closely related alternatives that already employ fixed, non-learned multi-scale features (e.g., scattering/wavelet-based texture models), and it lacks strong comparisons to those baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
- On motivation and placement: The question—what is indispensable for texture synthesis—is well-posed and relevant. The paper connects to prior parametric models (Portilla-Simoncelli), deep Gram-matrix approaches (Gatys), and discusses MaxEnt. However, the discussion underplays scattering-based models (Bruna & Mallat are cited but not compared), which are fixed multi-scale convnets with mathematically structured filters and moments, making them a very natural and strong baseline for the thesis “depth/pretraining aren’t necessary.”
- On methodology: The single-layer formulation, Gram statistics, and optimization are standard and clearly described. The multi-scale random conv approach is sensible. The ablations (filter size, ReLU on/off) support the narrative that multi-scale and nonlinearity are important; showing that a single very-large filter bank fails to capture small-scale statistics is a useful observation.
- On claims vs. evidence:
  - Quality evaluation relies predominantly on the VGG-based loss—an internal metric that is (i) biased toward VGG features and (ii) not independent of the main comparator. While the authors acknowledge its imperfections, the central quantitative claim would benefit from a metric independent of VGG (or, better, a human 2AFC test).
  - There is no quantitative variability/diversity assessment. The text discusses the trade-off between similarity and variability and mentions that their optimization may bias toward higher similarity (potentially reducing diversity), but there is no empirical characterization (e.g., pairwise VGG distances between multiple samples, entropy proxies).
  - The paper asserts that “rivals SOTA” on many textures and sometimes achieves lower VGG loss than VGG-optimized synthesis, but without statistically grounded comparisons across a clearly defined evaluation suite and without human study, this remains anecdotal.
  - Optimization details (e.g., scaling the loss/gradients by 1e7) appear crucial to success; yet there is no sensitivity analysis showing that results are robust to optimizer settings, number of iterations, or initialization.
- On completeness and clarity:
  - Multi-scale architecture specifics (scales used, number of channels per scale, normalization across scales) are not fully specified in the text provided.
  - Reproducibility would be improved with code, seeds, and a list of textures used.
  - Some references are incomplete/inconsistent.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide a broader and fairer baseline set: include scattering transform-based texture synthesis and a modern implementation of Portilla-Simoncelli with quantitative results, to directly test the hypothesis that fixed multi-scale representations + suitable moments suffice.
- Add a small-scale human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC “which looks closer to the reference?” or inpainting-based discrimination) on a curated texture set to validate the VGG-loss findings and assess diversity.
- Quantify variability: report intra-model diversity across multiple random initializations using (i) VGG-loss to the reference (distribution), (ii) pairwise distances between generated samples in VGG feature space, and (iii) perhaps LPIPS or DISTS metrics as independent perceptual proxies.
- Report runtime and memory for single-layer vs hierarchical multi-layer random nets, and discuss practical trade-offs.
- Provide a sensitivity analysis for key choices: number of feature maps, filter sizes per scale, ReLU vs other nonlinearities, effect of mean subtraction, and optimizer hyperparameters (including the 1e7 scaling).
- Clarify the multi-scale model (exact scales and channels), and whether per-scale Gram matrices are concatenated or aggregated.
- Improve references and positioning: more directly compare to or cite contemporaneous findings that random or fixed features can suffice in style/texture tasks, and strengthen the MaxEnt discussion with concrete experimental implications.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact details of the multi-scale model (filter sizes used, number of channels per scale, normalization across scales, and whether Gram matrices are computed jointly or per scale)?
- How robust are the results to optimizer settings (iterations, step sizes, the 1e7 scaling factor), initialization, and box constraints? Please provide sensitivity plots.
- Can you report quantitative diversity metrics across multiple samples per texture (e.g., pairwise VGG distances and an independent metric such as LPIPS), and show the trade-off with similarity?
- How does your method compare quantitatively and qualitatively to scattering transform-based texture synthesis on the same texture suite?
- Can you include a small human study (even N≈10–20 participants, 2AFC) to assess perceptual similarity and diversity?
- Do you normalize features or Gram matrices per scale/channel to balance contributions from different filter sizes?
- Is code, pretrained filters (for k-means/PCA models), and the texture set planned for release to ensure reproducibility?

In summary, the paper presents a compelling and useful minimal baseline and insightful ablations, but requires stronger, more independent evaluation and clearer positioning against closely related fixed-feature models to support its main claims.
