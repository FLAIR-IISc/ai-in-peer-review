Summary of contributions
- The paper investigates the minimal ingredients required for high-quality natural texture synthesis. It proposes a strong baseline that uses a single convolutional layer with random (data-independent) filters and Gram matrix statistics of feature activations, optimized via L-BFGS-B to match a reference texture’s statistics.
- It shows that such single-layer models, and especially a random multi-scale variant (filters up to 55×55), can synthesize textures that often rival the perceptual quality of the multi-layer, supervised VGG-based model of Gatys et al. (2015).
- It systematically compares a range of single-layer filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA; 11×11; with 363 vs. 3267 filters) and emphasizes that depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary for strong synthesis quality. Nonlinearity (ReLU) and multi-scale filters are identified as critical.
- It introduces a quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based Gram loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, along with qualitative comparisons, and discusses the balance between perceptual similarity and variability. It also provides a conceptual discussion relating practical synthesis to a relaxed Maximum Entropy view with ε-environments.

Decision: Reject, primarily due to (1) insufficiently rigorous and potentially confounded evaluation, and (2) limited novelty beyond a useful empirical baseline.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation are clear: What is minimally required to generate natural textures? The paper is well-positioned relative to Gatys et al., Portilla & Simoncelli, and related CNN/Gram-based and scattering approaches. The focus on minimality and ablations is timely and relevant.
- Technical soundness of the core method is solid: the synthesis objective is standard Gram matching; implementation details (single-layer CNNs, ReLU, padding/stride, L-BFGS-B with box constraints, gradient scaling) are specified. The architectural variants are described, and the identification of multi-scale filters and nonlinearity as crucial is supported by experiments.
- However, the evaluation is limited and confounded:
  - The primary quantitative metric is the VGG Gram loss—the same family of statistics used by the strong baseline they compare against. This favors methods that better optimize those statistics and can penalize perceptually better outputs that diverge from VGG’s feature statistics. The paper itself observes paradoxical cases where the VGG loss is lower for the proposed method than for VGG-optimized synthesis. This undermines the reliability of the metric as a general perceptual proxy.
  - No human psychophysical evaluation or standardized perceptual metrics independent of VGG (e.g., LPIPS) are provided. SSIM and pixel MSE are noted as inadequate, but no alternative quantitative validation is used.
  - The dataset appears small (examples from ~20 textures) with no principled train/test protocol or statistical analysis. Variability/diversity is discussed conceptually but not measured.
- The novelty is primarily empirical: demonstrating that shallow, random multi-scale filters with Gram matching suffice for strong texture synthesis. While this is informative and useful as a baseline, it is incremental relative to prior work establishing that fixed filter banks (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli, scattering) and Gram statistics are powerful for textures. The paper’s “formal generalization” of MaxEnt remains conceptual without new theory or algorithms.
- Reproducibility could be improved: while VGG details are cited, it is unclear whether code and exact settings for all architectures (especially the multi-scale configuration: filter sizes, counts per scale, channel mixing) will be released. Gradient scaling by 1e7 and its effect on convergence is mentioned but not systematically analyzed.
- Claims such as “often rival” state-of-the-art are based on qualitative inspection plus the potentially confounded VGG loss; they would benefit from independent perceptual assessment and larger-scale evaluation.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Include a human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC distinguishing real vs synthesized patches, or ranking) to validate “rivaling” claims and to assess the noted cases where VGG loss disagrees with perceived quality.
- Report quantitative diversity/variability across multiple samples per texture. Options include feature-space coverage metrics, pairwise LPIPS/Gram distances among samples, or a psychophysical inpainting test as proposed in the discussion.
- Evaluate with perceptual metrics independent of VGG (e.g., LPIPS with different backbones), and analyze correlation with human judgments.
- Provide comprehensive ablations:
  - Number of filters vs quality and compute cost.
  - Exact multi-scale design (filter sizes used, number per scale, channel mixing) and its sensitivity.
  - Role of ReLU vs other nonlinearities, and of normalization choices (e.g., feature normalization before Gram).
  - Optimization sensitivity (L-BFGS-B vs Adam; gradient scaling; iterations) and convergence behavior.
- Broaden the benchmark (more textures with diverse statistics, including man-made patterns where VGG loss underperforms).
- Compare against scattering-based texture synthesis (fixed wavelets) as a strong, closely related nonlearned baseline.
- Provide compute/memory/throughput comparisons: single-layer large-filter vs equivalent multi-layer architectures with small kernels to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Release code and pretrained filter definitions to ensure reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1. Multi-scale architecture: What exact filter sizes were used, how many filters per scale, and how were RGB channels handled across scales? Were filters normalized per scale?
2. Optimization: What motivates the 1e7 gradient/loss scaling? How sensitive are results to this scaling, iteration count, and optimizer choice? Is early stopping used to trade off similarity vs variability?
3. Evaluation:
   - How many textures were used in total? Can you provide aggregate statistics (mean/variance) of VGG loss across textures and multiple random seeds?
   - Did you evaluate with any perceptual metrics independent of VGG (e.g., LPIPS)? If so, how do results compare?
   - Can you provide a small user study to corroborate visual assessments, especially for cases where VGG loss is misleading?
4. Diversity: What is the variability across multiple runs from different initializations for the same texture? Can you quantify it (e.g., inter-sample distances in feature space)?
5. Comparisons: How does the proposed approach compare to scattering-based synthesis (Bruna & Mallat) in both quality and compute?
6. Efficiency: What are wall-clock times and memory use for single-layer large filters vs shallow multi-layer small-kernel networks reaching similar receptive fields?
7. Reproducibility: Will code and exact filter banks (including random seeds) be released?

Overall, the paper presents a useful empirical baseline and insightful observations about the sufficiency of shallow random feature banks for texture synthesis. Strengthening the evaluation with independent perceptual validation and clearer ablations would substantially improve its rigor and impact.
