Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: which ingredients of recent powerful texture synthesis methods are truly necessary? Contrary to the prevailing view that both deep, hierarchical features and supervised pretraining are essential (as in Gatys et al. 2015), the authors show that a single-layer CNN with random filters, combined with Gram matrix matching, can synthesize textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality. They provide:
- A minimal, strong baseline using one convolutional layer with ReLU, no pooling, and Gram-matching of feature covariances.
- Evidence that (i) pretraining and (ii) depth are not strictly necessary for high-quality texture synthesis; multi-scale random filters help significantly more than deeper networks per se.
- An extensive ablation over filter types (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA learned, hand-crafted), counts (363 vs 3267), scales, and nonlinearities, showing random filters are competitive or better, and ReLU is crucial.
- A quantitative proxy evaluation using the VGG-based texture loss as a distance metric, replacing purely qualitative judgement.
- A thoughtful discussion connecting practical synthesis to finite-sample MaxEnt reasoning, and the perceptual trade-off between similarity and variability.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The paper makes a strong, surprising, and well-supported negative result that challenges assumptions about the necessity of depth and supervised pretraining for texture synthesis, and offers a useful minimal baseline. (2) The ablation study is broad and yields actionable insights (multi-scale and nonlinearity matter far more than depth or supervised features). The main weaknesses are in evaluation: overreliance on a VGG-based metric and the lack of human studies or rigorous variability assessment. Nonetheless, the central claim is compelling and relevant to ICLR.

Supporting arguments
- Methodology: The single-layer model is precisely defined, as is the optimization (L-BFGS-B with box constraints and gradient scaling), and the loss (Gram matching). The ablations isolate important factors: number of maps, scale, nonlinearity. The multi-scale random model’s success and the failure without ReLU strongly support the conclusion that second-order feature correlations in a sufficiently rich random nonlinear feature space are highly expressive for stationary textures.
- Relation to prior work: The paper is well-situated in the texture literature (Julesz; Heeger & Bergen; Portilla & Simoncelli; Gatys et al.). It also acknowledges connections to MaxEnt models and filtering-based approaches (Bruna & Mallat). The empirical finding that random features work as well or better than k-means/PCA-trained filters is novel and practically important.
- Claims vs evidence: Qualitative results across many textures are convincing, especially the multi-scale model on regular, long-range patterns (walls, grids). The VGG-loss sometimes favors random multi-scale images even over VGG-optimized ones, consistent with simpler, better-conditioned optimization. The demonstration that increasing receptive field size without multi-scale does not suffice is well argued. The discussion about variability versus perceptual similarity is nuanced and honest about current limitations.
- Rigor and reproducibility: Most implementation details are given (filter sizes, padding, DC removal, iterations). The multi-scale architecture specifics and some optimization hyperparameters could be clearer (see questions). The choice of the VGG-loss as a proxy perceptual metric is reasonable but imperfect and introduces a potential bias.

Main weaknesses
- Evaluation bias and missing user studies: The reliance on VGG-loss risks circularity (it approximates perceptual similarity but is derived from a supervised network). While the authors acknowledge and discuss this, the paper would be stronger with a small-scale psychophysical 2AFC test or at least preference studies, especially for the categories where VGG-loss and perception diverge (e.g., man-made regular textures).
- Variability not rigorously quantified: The paper explicitly focuses on perceptual similarity. However, demonstrating that random single-layer models do not collapse to near-copies would benefit from quantitative measures (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss among multiple samples, patch-level diversity statistics) and visual grids of multiple samples per texture.
- Clarity on multi-scale configuration: The multi-scale random model is a key positive result, but its exact composition (scales, counts per scale, normalization across scales) is not fully specified in the main text.
- Limited baselines in quantitative terms: The claim of surpassing Portilla & Simoncelli is plausible visually but not quantified. Including PS results and scores under the same VGG-based metric (or other metrics) would strengthen the case.
- Efficiency: While the authors note the computational cost of large filters and that depth could be used for efficiency, no speed-quality trade-off is quantified.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not all required for acceptance)
- Add a brief human study (e.g., 20 participants, 20 textures, 2AFC real vs synth), focusing on cases where VGG-loss is inconclusive.
- Report variability: show 5–10 independent samples per texture, with quantitative dispersion (mean and variance of VGG-loss to the reference and between samples).
- Specify the multi-scale model precisely: list filter sizes, number of filters per scale, filter normalization, and any per-scale weighting in the Gram.
- Include a Portilla & Simoncelli baseline under the same evaluation, and optionally scattering-based synthesis.
- Provide an ablation on filter count (e.g., 256, 512, 1024, 2048) to characterize the trade-off between capacity and quality.
- Discuss the connection to kernel MMD/moment-matching: Gram-matching of random ReLU features approximates matching second-order moments in a random feature space, helping explain why random features suffice.
- Release code and trained filters (for the learned variants) and seeds to facilitate replication.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale details: What exact set of filter sizes were used, how many filters per scale, and how were features across scales normalized/weighted in the Gram? Did you concatenate feature maps before computing a single Gram, or average per-scale Grams?
- Optimization and stability: What motivated the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling? Can you show that replicating Gatys’ random-weight “failures” are fixed by your optimization settings? How sensitive are results to iteration count and initialization?
- Filter normalization: Were filters normalized (e.g., unit norm) per channel or per filter? For Fourier filters, did you include sine/cosine pairs and DC removal explicitly?
- Diversity: Do independent random seeds produce meaningfully different samples? Can you report inter-sample distances (VGG-loss) and show sample grids?
- Nonlinearity: Did you try leaky-ReLU or other nonlinearities? Is the necessity of ReLU specific, or is any nonlinearity that breaks phase sufficient?
- Non-stationary textures: How does the method fare on structured, non-stationary images (e.g., macro patterns, perspective), and could simple spatial tiling or position-dependent statistics help?
- Efficiency: Can a shallow multi-layer network with small kernels approximate the single-layer 55×55 multi-scale performance at lower cost, and what is the quality drop, if any?

Overall, this is a well-executed and thought-provoking paper that establishes a strong minimal baseline and clarifies what matters for Gram-based texture synthesis. With modest improvements in evaluation and clarity, it will make a valuable contribution.
