- Paper asks what minimal ingredients are needed to synthesize convincing natural textures and shows a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU, and Gram-matrix matching can rival VGG-based methods.
- Key contribution: a strong minimalist baseline using single-layer CNNs with random, data-independent filters optimized by L-BFGS-B to match Gram statistics.
- Systematic exploration of filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), channel counts (e.g., 363 vs 3267), and multi-scale setups identifies nonlinearity and multi-scale filters as crucial.
- Introduces a quantitative evaluation proxy using VGG-based texture loss and discusses the perceptual similarity vs variability trade-off in finite-sample MaxEnt terms.
- Ablations are comprehensive and show that random multi-scale features plus Gram matching suffice and are practically relevant as a new baseline.
- Decision: Weak accept, motivated by conceptual significance, clarity, and practical utility of the baseline.
- Conceptual significance: challenges the necessity of depth and supervised pretraining for high-quality texture synthesis with Gram statistics.
- Practical utility: offers a simple, adoptable baseline and clarifies roles of nonlinearity and multi-scale receptive fields.
- Methodologically sound: mirrors Gatys et al. but replaces learned features with random filters and explores multiple filter banks with insightful ablations.
- Empirical findings: more channels generally improve quality; multi-scale random filters outperform single-scale filters with equivalent parameters; ReLUs are necessary.
- Results: qualitative outputs are compelling across diverse textures, especially regular, large-scale structures, and sometimes achieve lower VGG loss than VGG-based synthesis.
- Optimization observations: discusses scaling the loss to avoid early stopping and that initializing VGG synthesis with random-model outputs can improve results.
- Theoretical discussion: thoughtful connection to finite-size effects and the need to allow small constraint mismatches in MaxEnt formulations.
- Suggestion: evaluation should include human 2AFC or inpainting psychophysics, alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS), and scattering-distance as a non-VGG baseline.
- Suggestion: explicitly quantify variability (e.g., pairwise distances among multiple samples) to substantiate the similarity vs variability trade-off.
- Suggestion: include empirical comparisons to scattering-based synthesis (Bruna & Mallat) and more systematic comparisons to Portilla & Simoncelli using common metrics.
- Reproducibility request: detail multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, filters per scale, initialization ranges, padding, channel allocation) and release code and configs.
- Robustness request: report sensitivity to random seeds, the minimum number of feature maps needed to reach VGG-loss thresholds, and runtime/memory costs.
- Clarify preprocessing: specify global ImageNet mean subtraction vs per-image mean, and whether grayscale textures were tested.
- Analysis request: provide intuition why random features outperform k-means/PCA/Fourier in Gram-matching, and analyze spectral/coverage properties of bases vs random.
- Suggestion: explore other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, absolute, squaring) and their effects on Gram statistics and synthesis.
- Suggestion: investigate boundary conditions, tiling artifacts, stationarity assumptions, and limitations on non-stationary textures.
- Questions for authors: exact multi-scale filter sizes and channel counts, sensitivity to allocation, and stability across random seeds.
- Questions for authors: why do random filters beat k-means/PCA/Fourier, and any spectral/coverage analysis to explain this?
- Questions for authors: how crucial is the 1e7 gradient scaling and were other optimizers or budgets tried; can you provide quantitative variability evidence and scattering baseline comparisons?
