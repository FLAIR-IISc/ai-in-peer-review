- Paper asks what minimal ingredients are required for natural texture synthesis and shows depth and supervised pretraining are not essential.
- Main contribution: a strong minimal baseline—single-layer random-filter CNNs matched via Gram-matrix statistics and optimized with L-BFGS.
- Contribution: multi-scale random-filter variant capturing short, mid, and long-range correlations significantly improves results; ReLU nonlinearity is critical.
- Contribution: systematic ablations across filter families, counts, and scales, finding random filters can outperform unsupervised/analytic filters.
- Contribution: use of VGG-based texture loss as a quantitative proxy and discussion linking Gram-based synthesis to maximum entropy with finite-sample effects.
- Decision: weak accept.
- Reason: provides a simple, well-executed, and impactful baseline that challenges assumptions about depth and pretraining.
- Reason: insightful conceptual link to MaxEnt and a practically useful multi-scale finding.
- Positioning: well placed among parametric texture models and focuses concretely on textures rather than general image generation.
- Methodology: standard synthesis objective and L-BFGS-B optimization; meaningful ablations (scales, feature counts, linear vs ReLU).
- Empirics: visual results convincing for many textures, especially regular man-made patterns; VGG-loss provides some quantitative structure.
- Empirics: single-layer optimization sometimes achieves lower VGG losses than VGG-space optimization due to differing optimization landscapes.
- Novelty: shows random, single-layer, multi-scale filters can yield competitive textures, providing an important community baseline.
- Weakness: heavy reliance on VGG-based loss introduces circularity and lacks a human perceptual study or robust diversity metrics.
- Weakness: missing architectural details for multi-scale setup, no reporting of variability across seeds, and no runtime/memory comparisons.
- Weakness: related-work gap—deeper connection to scattering transform and other analytic filterbanks (e.g., Gabor/wavelets) is missing.
- Suggested improvement: report variability by generating multiple samples per method and reporting VGG-loss distributions and pairwise sample distances.
- Suggested improvement: fully specify multi-scale details (filter sizes, counts, normalization) and ablate scale combinations.
- Suggested improvement: test sensitivity to number of feature maps, random seeds, and alternative filter initializations (e.g., orthogonal).
- Suggested improvement: extend baselines to include scattering transforms and spectrum-constrained Gram models with equal optimization budgets.
- Suggested improvement: report wall-clock runtimes and memory, and discuss efficient implementations (FFT/dilated convolutions).
- Suggested improvement: perform a small-scale perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC) to reduce reliance on VGG-loss.
- Suggested improvement: release code, hyperparameters, seeds, and exact preprocessing/whitening details for reproducibility.
- Key questions for authors: exact filter sizes/counts per scale and normalization strategy.
- Key questions: stability across random initializations and synthesis noise—report VGG-loss variance and qualitative failure modes.
- Key questions: quantify sample diversity and whether models sometimes collapse to deterministic reconstructions.
- Key questions: justify the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling for L-BFGS-B and whether alternative optimizers remove this need.
- Key questions: why do some random multi-scale samples get lower VGG-loss than VGG-optimized images and can VGG results be improved via initialization?
- Key questions: how do results relate to analytic filterbanks/scattering and could random orthogonal multi-scale filters approximate their performance?
- Key questions: provide runtimes for 2000 iterations at common resolutions and whether dilated convs or FFTs can reduce cost.
