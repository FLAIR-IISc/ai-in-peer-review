Summary
This paper asks a pointed and timely question: what is truly indispensable for high-quality natural texture synthesis? The authors demonstrate that a single convolutional layer with random filters, combined with Gram-matrix statistics and a well-tuned optimization, can synthesize textures that often rival the state-of-the-art VGG-based, multi-layer method of Gatys et al. They provide a strong minimal baseline, show that multi-scale random features are particularly effective, and analyze the roles of feature count, filter size, and nonlinearity (ReLU). They also propose a quantitative evaluation using VGG-based losses in lieu of pure visual inspection and offer a thoughtful discussion connecting practical synthesis to MaxEnt modeling, emphasizing the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold insight: depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary for strong texture synthesis; a single-layer, random, multi-scale model suffices. This challenges prevailing assumptions and establishes a compelling minimal baseline for future work.
- Clear empirical evidence and ablations support the central claim, with sensible analysis of what matters (multi-scale, number of features, ReLU) and what does not (architectural depth, learned filters).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Well-scoped and well-situated in the literature (Julesz; Portilla & Simoncelli; scattering; Gatys; feed-forward texture nets; GANs/PixelCNN). The question—necessity of depth and learned features—is important and underexplored despite the prevalence of deep, trained representations in synthesis.
- Methodology: The approach is deliberately simple and transparent: single layer conv with ReLU; Gram statistics; L-BFGS-B with minor but impactful optimization tuning; a multi-scale variant to cover long-range dependencies. The ablation on single-scale vs multi-scale convincingly shows why combining scales matters (small filters capture local statistics; large filters capture long-range structure; together capture both).
- Empirical support: Qualitative comparisons across many textures and filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA) consistently support the claim that random filters perform on par or better than learned/hand-crafted ones when feature dimensionality is large. The ReLU ablation shows nonlinearity is necessary. The use of VGG-based loss as an external evaluation metric is reasonable (the proposed model does not optimize this metric), and the observation that the random multi-scale model can sometimes achieve lower VGG loss than the VGG-synthesis itself is intriguing. The discussion of variability vs similarity and finite-size effects is thoughtful and situates the work in a broader theoretical context (MaxEnt).
- Impact: Establishes a strong, minimal baseline and reframes the perceived necessity of depth/training for texture models; likely to influence how future algorithms are designed and evaluated.

Concerns and limitations (not fatal)
- Evaluation relies primarily on VGG-based loss and qualitative inspection; no psychophysical or human preference study. The authors acknowledge this and outline directions for measuring variability (e.g., inpainting 2AFC). Given the conceptual contribution, this is acceptable but leaves room for stronger validation.
- Computational efficiency: Large spatial filters make the single-layer model slow; the authors note hierarchical nets can recover efficiency but did not show improved quality. Reporting wall-clock comparisons and memory use would make the trade-offs clearer.
- Some details are underspecified (exact multi-scale configuration, random initialization ranges, per-scale channel mixing, robustness across random seeds), and there are minor presentation/typo issues in references.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not central to decision)
- Provide precise multi-scale architecture details (filter sizes used, number of filters per scale, initialization distributions, padding choices) and report sensitivity to random seeds.
- Add quantitative runtime/memory comparisons versus VGG-based synthesis and feed-forward texture networks.
- Include small-scale human studies (2AFC similarity judgments or inpainting) to corroborate VGG-loss as a perceptual proxy and to assess variability.
- Explore alternative nonlinearities (Leaky ReLU, Softplus) and decorrelation (whitening vs non-whitening) to test robustness of the random feature claim.
- Evaluate on standard texture benchmarks (e.g., Brodatz, DTD) and report failure cases (highly non-stationary, strongly perspective-distorted, or semantic textures).
- Clarify the normalization in the Gram loss (differences from Gatys), and provide an analysis on how normalization and stopping criteria trade off similarity vs variability.
- Discuss connections and contrasts with the scattering transform: structured wavelets vs random filters; what each buys in invariance/stability vs expressivity.
- Release code and textures to cement the baseline’s utility.

Questions for the authors
- What exact multi-scale configuration was used (filter sizes, counts per scale, channel mixing) and how sensitive are results to these choices and to random seeds?
- How do runtime and memory compare to Gatys-style synthesis and to feed-forward texture networks (e.g., Ulyanov et al.) for 256–512 px images?
- Are there textures or categories where the single-layer model consistently fails? Can you characterize them (e.g., non-stationarity, regular global structure)?
- How does image resolution affect quality and variability? Do results scale to larger images without tiling artifacts? What padding scheme was used?
- Can you quantify the optimization “tuning” that made random features work where earlier reports failed (e.g., effect of gradient scaling, iteration count, box constraints)?
- Do alternative nonlinearities or simple contrast normalization layers alter the need for large numbers of features?
- Could you provide preliminary 2AFC results to validate the VGG-loss as a perceptual proxy, even with a small number of participants?

Overall, this paper makes a clear, surprising, and impactful contribution by establishing a strong minimal baseline and reframing assumptions about depth and learning in texture synthesis. I recommend acceptance.
