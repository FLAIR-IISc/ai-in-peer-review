Summary and contributions
The paper asks whether depth and supervised pretraining are truly necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. It claims:
- A strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random filters and ReLU, with Gram-matrix matching, is sufficient to synthesize textures of competitive perceptual quality.
- A multi-scale single-layer variant (multiple filter sizes concatenated) further improves quality and can rival VGG-based style/texture approaches.
- A systematic comparison across filter types (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA-learned, variants with/without whitening, dataset-specific sampling) and across numbers of feature maps (≈363 vs ≈3267 vs a 1024-feature multi-scale model).
- A quantitative evaluation via the VGG-based texture loss; discussion relating Gram statistics and MaxEnt, and the role of allowing small constraint mismatches.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient empirical rigor and potentially biased evaluation (chief metric is the VGG loss, yet even VGG-optimized images are not best by that metric; no human study, no statistical analysis, limited set, unclear selection). (2) Important methodological details are missing or ambiguous (multi-scale architecture specification, filter normalization, whitening, Fourier basis, optimization settings, data/protocol, reproducibility). The overall presentation also contains numerous technical and formatting issues that undermine confidence.

Supporting arguments
- Evaluation metric and fairness: The principal quantitative measure is the VGG Gram loss. However, the paper reports that the random multi-scale model sometimes achieves lower VGG-loss than images optimized directly in VGG space, attributing this to optimization issues. This severely undermines the metric’s interpretability and fairness. If the chosen evaluation cannot reliably certify its own optimization, it should not be the sole quantitative metric. No strong alternative evaluation is provided (no human study, no large-scale user preference test, no controlled psychophysics despite discussing inpainting, no robustness/variability analysis). The dataset and texture set (“20 different textures”) is not clearly defined, nor is a train/test protocol.
- Missing or unclear architectural details: The “Random multi-scale” model—which appears central to the best results—is not precisely specified. The paper references filter sizes as small as 3×3 and as large as 55×55, but does not give the exact set of scales, per-scale filter counts summing to 1024, filter initialization distributions, any per-feature normalization, or whether channels are normalized. Without these, results are not reproducible.
- Inconsistent comparisons: Many comparisons vary both the filter type and the number of feature maps (363 vs 3267 vs 1024), confounding capacity with feature design. A fair study should hold the number of feature maps constant (or sweep N and report curves) while varying filter type, and separately analyze the effect of multi-scale vs receptive field size given equal parameter budgets.
- Optimization details are unusual and under-justified: L-BFGS-B with a 10^7 loss/gradient scaling to avoid early stopping is a nonstandard trick; its effect on convergence, stability, and comparability is not studied. The box constraint [0,1] conflicts with subtracting ImageNet per-channel means (“remove DC”)—clarify ordering and whether clipping occurs in pixel space or preprocessed space. The choice of 2000 iterations with no reported per-texture convergence diagnostics makes it hard to assess whether differences are genuine or optimization artifacts.
- Methodological ambiguities: 
  - Fourier basis filters: Are these real-valued sine/cosine bases? Is the DC component included/excluded? How are bases normalized? Are complex components handled?
  - K-means “whitening”: PCA whitening or ZCA? Patch-wise or dataset-wise? Are filters normalized (e.g., unit l2 norm) before convolution?
  - PCA-363: PCA across 11×11×3 patches—are principal axes reshaped and normalized? Are low-variance components included?
  - Preprocessing: “remove the DC component” by subtracting dataset channel means does not remove DC per-image/patch. If DC filters are used, their Gram entries will still reflect local means.
- Lack of robustness/variability analysis: The text notes the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability but provides no quantification (e.g., inter-sample diversity, entropy proxies, patch-wise nearest-neighbor tests, or 2AFC user studies). The anecdotal evidence that deeper random CNNs can exactly reconstruct the reference indicates potential overfitting/pathological minima and warrants careful analysis.
- Theory section is exploratory, not substantiated: The discussion around MaxEnt and ε-environments is interesting but remains conceptual. No formal results, algorithms, or empirical tests are given to support the proposed perspective.
- Presentation and references: Numerous typos and formatting issues (e.g., “men-made,” “looses,” “inpaited,” “extend” vs “extent”), inconsistent or incomplete references (placeholders “? \Q2016\E,” missing venues/years, inconsistent author formatting), and an in-text footnote marker with a bare URL detract from clarity and professionalism.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide complete, precise architectural specifications for all models, especially the multi-scale variant (scales, per-scale filter counts, initialization, normalization). Release code and seeds.
- Rework evaluation:
  - Ensure the VGG baseline optimization is strong (careful convergence, multiple restarts, tuned hyperparameters). If not, do not use its loss as the primary metric.
  - Add human evaluations (small-scale 2AFC or MOS) to substantiate “rivals” claims; include statistical tests.
  - Report results across a larger, predefined texture set with clear selection criteria, and provide per-texture metrics and aggregate statistics (mean, variance).
  - Control for capacity: compare filter types at matched numbers of feature maps; provide performance vs N curves.
  - Analyze variability: e.g., pairwise VGG-loss between multiple samples from the same model, patch-based discrimination tasks, or diversity metrics.
- Clarify optimization choices (scaling, bounds, convergence criteria), and report runtime/memory on specified hardware for single- and multi-scale models.
- Tighten the theory section: either formalize the ε-environment framework with experiments, or shorten to focus on empirical findings.
- Thoroughly edit for language, notation consistency, and complete/correct references.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale model: What exact filter sizes and how many filters per scale were used to reach 1024 maps? Were filters per scale normalized? How were random filters initialized (distribution, variance scaling)?
2) Fourier basis: Are you using real sine/cosine bases? Is the DC filter included? How are filters normalized to equal energy?
3) K-means/PCA details: Define whitening precisely (PCA vs ZCA, per-patch vs global), filter normalization, and whether you re-scale cluster centers/eigenvectors. How do you handle color correlations?
4) Preprocessing and constraints: Are pixel values clipped in [0,1] before or after mean subtraction? If the input to the network is mean-centered, what is the effective feasible set during L-BFGS-B?
5) Optimization: Why 10^7 scaling? Did you tune L-BFGS-B settings per model? Provide convergence curves and sensitivity analyses.
6) Evaluation: How many textures total? From which dataset(s)? How were they chosen? How many random initializations per texture? Are reported VGG-loss numbers averaged?
7) Capacity control: Can you report performance as a function of number of feature maps for each filter type, and a matched-parameter comparison between single-scale 55×55 and multi-scale?
8) Variability: How diverse are multiple samples per texture? Please quantify inter-sample distances and, if possible, provide a small user study or patch discrimination test.
