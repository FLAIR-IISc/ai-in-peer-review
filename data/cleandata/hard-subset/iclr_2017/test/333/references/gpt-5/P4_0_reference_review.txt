1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper investigates the minimal requirements for high-quality natural texture synthesis using Gram-matrix-based statistics. Contrary to prior claims that deep, supervisedly trained feature hierarchies are necessary, the authors show that a single convolutional layer with random filters, especially in a multi-scale configuration, can synthesize textures that often rival a VGG-based multi-layer model. They:
- Propose a minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU, random filters, and Gram-matrix matching.
- Systematically compare diverse single-layer filter banks (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA from ImageNet patches, and variants) with different feature counts (363 vs 3267) and a multi-scale random model (up to 55×55 receptive fields).
- Provide ablations showing that (i) more feature maps improve quality, (ii) multi-scale is critical (single large filters miss fine structure; single small filters miss long-range structure), and (iii) ReLU nonlinearity is indispensable.
- Evaluate primarily by a VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, supplemented by qualitative comparison; discuss the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability and relate the approach to a MaxEnt perspective with soft constraints.

2. Decision
Reject, due to (1) insufficiently rigorous evaluation relying largely on a potentially circular proxy metric (VGG-based loss) without human perceptual validation, and (2) incomplete empirical analysis supporting key causal claims (e.g., optimization tuning vs architecture as the reason random filters succeed), making it hard to assess technical soundness and generality.

3. Supporting arguments
- On the problem tackled: The question—what architectural and training elements are truly necessary for texture synthesis—is clear and important. The paper is well positioned in the literature (Julesz; Heeger & Bergen; Portilla & Simoncelli; Gatys et al.) and articulates a strong baseline contribution.
- Motivation and placement: The idea of questioning depth and supervision is well motivated. The exploration of single-layer, random, and multi-scale models fills a gap and is a potentially valuable baseline for the community.
- Evidence and rigor:
  • The primary quantitative evaluation uses the VGG-based Gram loss. This metric is optimized by the VGG model and is known to correlate with perceptual similarity, but the reliance on it as the main quantitative measure introduces circularity and may bias conclusions. The paper acknowledges imperfections of this metric; however, no alternative robust evaluation (e.g., psychophysical user study, 2AFC, inpainting Turing tests, or at least crowd-sourced evaluations) is provided. SSIM is noted to be poor; pixel distances are not reported.
  • Claims that multi-scale random sometimes “outperforms” the VGG-based synthesis are not strongly substantiated without human studies. Lower VGG loss does not guarantee better perceived quality and may reflect optimization ease rather than model adequacy.
  • The critical claim that prior failures with random weights stem from “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” is plausible but not convincingly demonstrated. An ablation isolating the effect of gradient scaling, constraints, initialization, and optimizer settings on synthesis quality is missing.
  • The multi-scale architecture is not fully specified in the excerpt (e.g., exact filter sizes per scale, number of filters per scale, normalization of filters, and whether feature map variance is controlled), which limits reproducibility and leaves ambiguity about fairness vs single-scale comparisons.
  • Efficiency claims (single-layer slower; hierarchical multi-layer faster but not better) lack quantitative runtime/complexity measurements.
  • Variability vs similarity is discussed conceptually, but variability is not measured. The evidence that the proposed models may reduce variability (e.g., lower loss, occasional near-reconstruction) is anecdotal; this limits the strength of claims about the model’s suitability as a generative texture model rather than an exemplar-matching procedure.
- Novelty: The main novelty lies in demonstrating that a single-layer, random, multi-scale Gram model is a strong baseline. This is conceptually interesting but incremental relative to Gatys et al. and earlier single-layer texture models; the paper’s contribution would be substantially strengthened by rigorous evaluation and clearer causal ablations.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all items affect the decision)
- Provide a thorough ablation study on optimization: test different gradient scalings, box constraints, initializations, optimizers (L-BFGS-B vs Adam), iteration budgets, and stopping criteria. Show that random filters fail under prior settings but succeed under yours.
- Specify the multi-scale model unambiguously: list all filter sizes, counts per scale, normalization (e.g., unit norm), any whitening/zero-mean constraints, and how feature maps across scales are balanced in the Gram objective.
- Include quantitative perceptual evaluation: small-scale 2AFC user studies or inpainting-based tests would substantiate claims and address potential biases of the VGG metric.
- Measure variability: e.g., intra-model loss dispersion across multiple seeds, LPIPS distances between samples, patch entropy proxies, or human diversity ratings.
- Report computational metrics: wall-clock time, memory, FLOPs, and scalability vs filter size and number of features; compare to VGG-based synthesis and to a shallow hierarchical model with smaller filters achieving equivalent receptive field.
- Fairness of comparisons: equalize the number of feature maps when possible; include additional baselines (e.g., scattering transform, Portilla-Simoncelli, spectrum-constrained CNN) with the same evaluation metric.
- Clarify the role of nonlinearity: add quantitative results comparing ReLU vs other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, tanh) and linear models, with statistics on multiple textures.
- Release code and pretrained filter sets to ensure reproducibility.

5. Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale architecture: What exact filter sizes and counts per scale are used? How are filters initialized and normalized across scales? Are feature map activations rescaled to balance contributions to the Gram matrix?
- Optimization sensitivity: How sensitive are results to the 1e7 scaling of loss/gradients, iteration count, and L-BFGS parameters? Can you show that without these settings, random filters fail, and with them they succeed, controlling for other factors?
- Evaluation robustness: How well do conclusions hold under alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, DISTS)? Do human 2AFC judgments align with the VGG-based loss ranking across your models?
- Variability: What is the diversity between samples (e.g., LPIPS between multiple synthesized images from the same reference)? Do some models collapse toward the reference (near verbatim reconstructions)?
- Runtime: What are the average synthesis times and memory footprints for single-scale (363 vs 3267), multi-scale, and VGG-based models on the same hardware?
- Filter learning baselines: For k-means/PCA filters, are filters whitened/normalized? Why do learned filters underperform random ones—could this be due to a mismatch in filter energy or lack of decorrelation?
- Generality: How do results extend to a broader, standardized texture dataset (e.g., DTD/Brodatz) with quantitative summaries over many textures rather than a handful?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and potentially impactful minimal baseline, but stronger, more rigorous evaluation and clearer ablations are needed to support the central claims.
