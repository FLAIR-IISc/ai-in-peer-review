Summary and contributions
The paper asks what elements are indispensable to synthesize natural textures with parametric models. It proposes a surprisingly strong minimal baseline: a single convolutional layer with ReLU and random filters, combined with Gram-matrix matching of feature activations, can synthesize textures of high perceptual quality. A multi-scale variant (a single layer containing multiple filter sizes) further improves results, often rivaling the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. The paper systematically compares single-layer models with different filter types (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA-learned) and sizes, and argues that (i) neither depth nor supervised pretraining is necessary for strong texture synthesis and (ii) combining multiple scales in one layer is more important than simply increasing the receptive field of a single scale. It also proposes to use the VGG Gram-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and discusses the MaxEnt perspective and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The empirical support is primarily qualitative and the quantitative evaluation relies on a non-independent proxy (VGG Gram loss) that is closely tied to a competing baseline, without a broader or human-based validation; (2) Important architectural and experimental details are underspecified, and comparisons to closely related fixed-filter methods (e.g., scattering transform) and rigorous baselines are incomplete, limiting confidence and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: The central observation—that Gram-matching with a shallow random-feature bank works well—is interesting and challenges the common wisdom from Gatys et al. While this minimal baseline is useful, the conceptual ingredients (Gram matching, random features, single-layer CNN) are direct variants of established methods. Related fixed-filter approaches (e.g., scattering-based synthesis, Portilla–Simoncelli) are acknowledged but not compared quantitatively. The paper would be stronger with head-to-head comparisons to scattering networks (fixed, non-learned, multi-scale/multi-orientation filters) and modernized P&S implementations.
- Empirical rigor: The main evidence is visual. The VGG-based loss is used to score outputs, but (i) it is not independent of the VGG-based baseline; (ii) aggregate metrics across the 20 textures (means, variance, statistical tests) are not reported; (iii) perceptual evaluation (user study) is absent; (iv) variability of samples for a fixed texture (a central topic in the discussion) is not measured. The paper itself notes cases where VGG loss disagrees with perception, especially for man-made regular patterns.
- Methodological clarity and reproducibility: The multi-scale architecture is not fully specified (exact set of filter sizes, number of channels per scale, initialization ranges), and several training/synthesis details likely critical to the strong performance (LBFGS-B settings, gradient scaling of 1e7, constraints) are given but not analyzed for sensitivity. Code availability is not stated. The k-means/PCA pipelines require 10^7 patches from ImageNet, but this ultimately underperforms random filters; this is a useful negative result but the resource cost is high.
- Scope of claims: The claim that depth and pretraining are “not indispensable” for texture synthesis is supported within the specific Gram-matching framework and a limited set of textures, but the conclusion is somewhat broad. The results do not decisively show parity with VGG across a wide texture distribution or task variations (tilings, inpainting, exemplar size). The discussion on MaxEnt and finite-sample variability is insightful but primarily conceptual, with no corresponding experiments probing the perceptual trade-off.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Provide aggregated quantitative results over the full texture set (mean/median VGG loss per method with confidence intervals; also pixel-domain baselines and SSIM for completeness, despite their limitations).
  - Include a small-scale human study (pairwise preference or 2AFC discrimination) to calibrate the VGG-loss proxy, especially on regular, man-made textures where it is known to fail.
  - Measure sample variability: e.g., report distribution of VGG losses among multiple seeds vs. reference and between synthesized samples; consider a simple inpainting or jigsaw discrimination test as suggested.
- Comparisons and baselines:
  - Include scattering transform–based synthesis (fixed, non-learned wavelet filters) and a modern Portilla–Simoncelli baseline with the same optimizer and runtime budget.
  - Compare against spectrum-constrained variants (e.g., Liu et al.) and show whether adding a spectrum constraint benefits the random multi-scale model.
- Clarity and completeness:
  - Specify the multi-scale layer precisely: filter sizes used (e.g., 3, 11, 21, 55), number of filters per scale to reach 1024 total, exact initialization (Glorot ranges), normalization, and ReLU placement.
  - Report sensitivity analyses for: number of feature maps, filter sizes, presence/absence of ReLU, initialization scale, and LBFGS-B settings (especially the gradient scaling factor and iteration budget).
  - Discuss compute: wall-clock time, memory, and comparisons to VGG synthesis, and to hierarchical multi-layer random CNNs that you mention as faster but not better.
- Scope and caveats:
  - Temper general claims about depth/pretraining; emphasize the finding as a strong minimal baseline within Gram-matching. Clarify failure modes (e.g., structured scenes, non-stationary textures, very high-frequency microtextures).
  - Consider alternative, more independent metrics (LPIPS, DISTS) to supplement VGG Gram loss.

Questions for the authors
1. What exact filter sizes and channel allocations are used in the multi-scale model, and how sensitive are results to this allocation?
2. How many random seeds per texture were tried per model, and what is the variability in the VGG loss and in human-perceived diversity?
3. Do results hold if the evaluation metric is changed to LPIPS/DISTS or a small user study? For the grid and brick textures where VGG loss misranks, how often do humans prefer your outputs?
4. How does your model compare quantitatively to scattering-based texture synthesis under the same optimization and runtime budget?
5. Can you report aggregate quantitative summaries across the 20 textures (means, standard deviations, significance tests) rather than per-image figure captions?
6. How critical is the gradient scaling (1e7) and the LBFGS-B iteration budget? Do alternative optimizers (Adam) with matched compute produce similar results?
7. Could you release code and model configurations to ensure reproducibility?

Overall, this work highlights a valuable and perhaps underappreciated baseline and provides thoughtful discussion. With stronger, more independent evaluation and clearer specifications, it could make a solid contribution.
