Summary and contributions
- The paper asks: what are the minimal ingredients needed to generate high-quality natural textures? It challenges two widely held assumptions from Gatys et al. (2015) that (i) hierarchical multi-layer representations and (ii) supervised pretraining are necessary.
- The core contribution is a strong minimal baseline: a single convolutional layer with random filters (and ReLUs), using Gram matrices of feature maps as stationary summary statistics, optimized with L-BFGS-B. A multi-scale single-layer variant (with filters up to 55×55) further improves performance.
- The paper systematically compares different single-layer choices (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA filters, number of feature maps, and multiscale vs single-scale) and argues that random filters are competitive or superior, and that nonlinearity and multiscale coverage are critical.
- It proposes a quantitative evaluation using a VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, supplementing qualitative comparisons. It also discusses the MaxEnt framework and the critical trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) Insufficient and partially circular evaluation: heavy reliance on a proxy metric (VGG-based loss) that is not validated here as a perceptual measure, limited qualitative evidence, and no human study or robust quantitative assessment of variability. Claims that depth and pretraining are unnecessary are not convincingly supported beyond the specific proxy metric and selected visual examples.
2) The reported advantages appear largely attributable to optimization ease and large receptive fields rather than a principled demonstration that depth/training are redundant. The paper itself notes that deeper models are prone to local minima and that initializing VGG synthesis from the proposed method improves results, which muddies causal interpretation of the main claim.

Supporting arguments
- Evaluation rigor and potential circularity:
  - The main quantitative metric is the VGG-based Gram loss (a close variant of the metric that underpins Gatys-style synthesis). This makes conclusions contingent on the alignment between this proxy and human perception, which the paper itself acknowledges is imperfect. No user study or psychophysical evaluation is provided. The claim that the proposed method “often rivals SOTA” remains largely anecdotal.
  - For several textures, the proposed method achieves lower VGG loss than the VGG-optimized synthesis itself; the authors attribute this to optimization issues in deeper models. This suggests the comparison conflates model capacity with optimizer pathologies. Without a stronger optimization baseline (e.g., better initialization, multistart, modern optimizers, or continuation strategies), the fairness of the comparison is questionable.
  - Variability is not measured. The paper argues that the proposed method may trade off variability for perceptual similarity but does not quantify entropy/diversity (e.g., distributions of VGG-loss across multiple seeds, patch-level discrimination tests, or nearest-neighbor analyses across samples). This undermines the stated goal of understanding what it takes to generate natural textures, where variability is central.

- Scope and baselines:
  - The dataset size and diversity are unclear; qualitative figures show around 20 textures. No systematic benchmark or standardized suite (e.g., Brodatz, KTH-TIPS) is used to assess generality.
  - The paper omits comparison to classic non-parametric patch-based synthesis (Efros–Leung, image quilting), which are longstanding baselines for texture quality and variability. The omission weakens claims about “rivaling state of the art” beyond the Gram-statistics-based family.
  - Given the conceptual proximity, a stronger comparison to scattering-based texture models (Bruna & Mallat) is warranted.

- Claims about depth and training:
  - The proposed multi-scale single-layer model uses large filters (up to 55×55) and many channels (e.g., 1024 or 3267), which increases capacity and compute. The assertion that depth is unnecessary is weakened if equivalent receptive fields and capacity are simply achieved via large, random filters; the paper acknowledges hierarchical models can be more efficient but does not provide rigorous speed/quality trade-off analysis.
  - The finding that random filters work well is interesting, but the dependency on ReLU, number of features, and multiscale coverage suggests the design space matters. A broader ablation (type of nonlinearity, normalization schemes, channel mixing strategies, different filter size allocations) would better substantiate generality.

- Reproducibility and clarity:
  - Many optimization details are ad hoc (e.g., scaling gradients by 1e7 to prevent early stopping). It is unclear how sensitive results are to such choices. Compute/memory costs, runtime at typical resolutions, and scaling behavior are not reported.
  - Some references and formatting are inconsistent; key baselines (Efros–Leung/quilt) are missing.

Suggestions to improve (not necessarily part of the decision)
- Provide a human perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC, inpainting discrimination) on a standardized texture set to validate that VGG-loss correlates with perceived quality. Measure both similarity and variability.
- Compare against classic non-parametric methods and scattering-based texture synthesis on the same dataset using human studies and multiple proxy metrics.
- Strengthen the optimization baseline for the VGG model (better initializations, continuation, or improved solvers) to separate model capacity from optimizer issues. Report multistart variability and best-of-k outcomes.
- Quantify compute: per-iteration cost, total runtime, memory footprint for different image sizes, and scaling vs. hierarchical alternatives.
- Provide broader ablations: nonlinearity types (ReLU/leaky/tanh), per-filter normalization, channel coupling strategies, filter size distributions in multiscale, number of features vs. quality curve.
- Release code and pretrained filter sets; specify seeds and all optimizer hyperparameters for reproducibility.
- Clarify the theoretical discussion: formalize the proposed -environment in the MaxEnt framing and illustrate its practical impact (e.g., how tolerance affects variability and perceived quality).

Questions for the authors
1) How robust are your conclusions to the choice of optimizer and initialization? If you use stronger optimization for the VGG-based synthesis (e.g., initialization from multiple seeds, continuation, Adam/L-BFGS hybrids), do your relative results persist?
2) How does the method scale to higher resolutions (e.g., 512×512, 1024×1024)? Please report runtimes and memory use for single- and multi-scale settings.
3) Can you provide a human perceptual study quantifying both similarity and variability? For instance, a 2AFC matching task and an inpainting-based discrimination test across a broader set of textures.
4) How sensitive are results to the number of feature maps and filter-size distribution in the multi-scale model? Is there a clear quality–compute Pareto curve?
5) How does your approach compare to patch-based non-parametric textures and to scattering-based synthesis on the same benchmark set?
6) Do you observe exact reconstructions (overfitting) frequently? Under what conditions (image size, number of features, filter sizes)? How do you mitigate this while preserving similarity?
7) Will code, filters, and evaluation scripts be released to ensure reproducibility?
