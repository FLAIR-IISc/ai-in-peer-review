- Paper asks what minimal ingredients are needed to synthesize convincing natural textures, arguing depth and supervised pretraining are unnecessary.
- Claims a single convolutional layer with ReLU and Gram (feature-covariance) statistics often suffices, with a random multi-scale single-layer model rivaling VGG multi-layer models.
- Shows random filters can match or outperform hand-crafted or unsupervised filters.
- Identifies multi-scale filters and nonlinearity as the crucial ingredients for texture quality.
- Proposes a VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity.
- Frames texture synthesis within a relaxed MaxEnt view allowing small mismatches in constraints.
- Empirically compares filter banks (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random), channel counts, and scales with systematic ablations.
- Analyzes optimization details (L-BFGS-B with gradient scaling, box constraints) and reports practical heuristics.
- Observes initializing VGG-based synthesis from single-layer results improves VGG outcomes, suggesting optimization effects in deep losses.
- Decision: weak accept.
- Key reason: provides a surprising, useful minimal baseline showing single-layer random multi-scale Gram model produces high-quality textures.
- Key reason: systematic ablations isolate what matters (multi-scale and nonlinearity) and what does not (depth and pretraining).
- Novelty/significance: lowers bar for strong texture synthesis and clarifies role of architectural depth vs. employed statistics.
- Empirical support: multi-scale random model often matches or surpasses VGG in appearance and VGG-loss, with benefits from more channels and multi-scale filters.
- Insight: single-scale large filters capture long-range structure but miss fine detail; multi-scale reconciles scales effectively.
- Limitation: main quantitative metric is VGG-based Gram loss which is not neutral and may correlate with the studied mechanism.
- Limitation: lack of human perceptual studies and alternative perceptual metrics weakens strongest claims.
- Limitation: modest test set (~20 textures) and limited runtime/efficiency analysis; some architecture specifics are under-specified.
- Suggested evaluation improvements: add human perceptual tests, LPIPS, FID/KID, and report variability across random restarts.
- Suggested baselines: compare to scattering-based methods, spectrum-constrained CNNs, and feedforward “Texture Networks” for speed-quality trade-offs.
- Requested method details: precise multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, per-scale channel counts, normalizations) and sensitivity analyses.
- Suggested ablations: alternative nonlinearities, filter normalizations, input/feature normalization effects, and optimizer/grid searches.
- Efficiency request: provide wall-clock times, memory usage, FLOPs for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. VGG-based synthesis.
- Reproducibility request: release code, texture benchmark, and scripts to reproduce figures and losses.
- Questions for authors: exact multi-scale filter sizes and per-scale channel allocations, sensitivity to reallocating channels across scales.
- Questions for authors: importance of 1e7 gradient scaling for L-BFGS-B, convergence curves, and whether Adam can match results.
- Questions for authors: quantitative measures of sample diversity across initializations and example grids.
- Questions for authors: do conclusions hold under LPIPS, SSIM, or scattering-distance, and are there cases where VGG-loss mis-ranks methods?
- Questions for authors: when do deeper random CNNs achieve exact reconstructions and how does epsilon-tolerance or early stopping affect diversity?
- Questions for authors: typical runtimes for 512×512 textures across methods and memory bottlenecks for very large filters.
