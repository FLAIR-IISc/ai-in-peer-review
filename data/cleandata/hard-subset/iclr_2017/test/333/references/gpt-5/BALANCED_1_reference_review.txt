Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: What elements are actually necessary to synthesize convincing natural textures? It challenges two commonly assumed necessities in recent texture synthesis work (depth and supervised pretraining), and argues that neither is essential. The authors propose a minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU nonlinearity and Gram-matrix matching of feature maps, using either random, hand-crafted (Fourier), or unsupervised (k-means/PCA) filters. They further present a multi-scale single-layer variant that mixes filters of different sizes (up to 55×55). Texture synthesis is done via L-BFGS optimization to match Gram statistics of a reference texture. For evaluation, they report a VGG-based reconstruction loss (average over 5 layers) as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and provide extensive qualitative comparisons.

Key claims supported by experiments and analysis:
- Single-layer models with random features can synthesize textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality, often comparable to Gatys et al. (2015).
- Multi-scale random features substantially improve quality, capturing long-range structure without requiring depth or supervised training.
- Nonlinearities (ReLU) are important; without them synthesis degrades.
- The choice of filters (random vs trained) is less critical than the number of feature maps and multi-scale coverage.
- The paper discusses the MaxEnt perspective and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, arguing for soft constraints and improved perceptual metrics.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Primary reasons:
1) Insufficiently rigorous evaluation to support strong claims of “rivaling state-of-the-art,” relying primarily on qualitative figures and a single proxy metric (VGG-loss) that is known to be imperfect and sometimes paradoxical even by the authors’ own admission.
2) Limited ablation and quantitative analysis of design choices and robustness (e.g., number of features, filter size distribution, seeds, runtime), and incomplete specification of the multi-scale model architecture, which undermines reproducibility and the strength of the conclusions.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The question is well posed and the motivation is strong. The paper is well situated with respect to classical (Heeger-Bergen; Portilla-Simoncelli) and modern (Gatys et al.) texture models, and it is valuable to establish strong minimal baselines.
- Novelty: Showing that random single-layer features can work well, especially with multi-scale filters, is an interesting and potentially impactful observation. However, the paper largely relies on qualitative evidence, and several important baselines (e.g., spectrum-matching constraints, scattering transform–based models) are not compared quantitatively.
- Evidence and rigor:
  - The primary quantitative metric (VGG-loss) is closely related to the synthesis objective of Gatys et al., and the paper itself shows cases where the metric disagrees with perception or favors reduced variability. There is no user study, no psychophysical proxy (beyond discussion), and no correlation analysis to justify the metric as a general perceptual proxy.
  - Ablations are partial: ReLU vs no-ReLU and single-scale vs multi-scale are shown, but there is no systematic exploration of (i) number of feature maps vs quality; (ii) distribution across scales; (iii) sensitivity to optimization hyperparameters (e.g., gradient scaling, stopping criteria); (iv) variability across random seeds; (v) texture size dependence; or (vi) runtime and computational efficiency.
  - Details of the multi-scale architecture (exact filter sizes, counts per scale, padding/stride per scale, normalization per scale) are not fully specified. The authors emphasize that “more elaborate tuning of the optimization” is essential, but do not provide a careful ablation to substantiate which elements matter and why.
  - Some filter sets (k-means from the target texture) risk overfitting; this should be discussed quantitatively.
- Broader claims: The conceptual discussion around MaxEnt, soft constraints, and the -environment is insightful, but remains largely qualitative and does not translate into an operational evaluation protocol of similarity vs variability. As such, it does not yet support claims about the generality of the approach as a texture model.

Constructive feedback and questions for the authors
The following suggestions are intended to help strengthen the work; they are not all required for a decision, but addressing several would materially improve the paper.

- Evaluation and metrics
  - Provide a small-scale user study or a psychophysical proxy (e.g., oddity or inpainting tasks) to assess whether the proposed models truly “rival” VGG-based synthesis perceptually, and to quantify similarity–variability trade-offs.
  - Report additional quantitative metrics (LPIPS, SSIM, spectral statistics) and analyze their correlation with human judgments, at least on a subset.
  - Quantify variability: show intra-model diversity across multiple seeds/initializations and measure dispersion in the chosen metric(s).

- Ablations and robustness
  - Systematically vary the number of feature maps and report how quality scales with N for single-scale and multi-scale setups.
  - Detail the multi-scale design: precise filter sizes used, number of filters per scale, any per-scale normalization, stride/padding, and whether dilations could achieve similar effects.
  - Study sensitivity to optimization (gradient scaling factor, number of iterations, stopping criteria, box constraints). Is the conclusion about random features robust to using Adam vs L-BFGS?
  - Compare different nonlinearities (ReLU, leaky-ReLU, tanh), and explain why ReLU is essential.
  - Quantify runtime and memory vs VGG-based synthesis; include a speed/quality Pareto plot.

- Baselines and comparisons
  - Include quantitative comparisons to (i) spectrum-constrained CNN texture models; (ii) scattering-based texture synthesis; (iii) Heeger–Bergen/Portilla–Simoncelli on the same set of textures.
  - Clarify how “Kmeans-Sample” (trained on the target texture) avoids trivial memorization, and show it does not outperform random filters in a way that suggests overfitting.

- Reproducibility
  - Release code and pretrained filter banks; provide exact seeds and all hyperparameters.
  - Specify the set of textures used and include more diverse, challenging cases (including non-stationary or strongly structured man-made patterns).

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale architecture: What exact scales (filter sizes) and how many filters per scale were used? Were filters normalized per scale to equalize contributions? Did you consider dilated convolutions?
2) How many random seeds were tried per texture/model, and how stable are results across seeds (both in VGG-loss and visually)?
3) Can you provide a curve of VGG-loss vs number of feature maps for single- and multi-scale models? Is there a clear saturation point?
4) How do your models perform on larger textures or with periodic boundary conditions? Do boundary/padding choices matter perceptually?
5) How sensitive are results to the gradient scaling (1e7) and to L-BFGS specifics? Would Adam with tuned step sizes reach similar optima?
6) For k-means/PCA filters: how exactly was whitening performed? Do these filters consistently underperform random filters, and if so, why?
7) Could you quantify variability (e.g., VGG-loss dispersion across samples) and provide a mechanism to trade off similarity vs variability (e.g., early stopping schedules)?
8) Do your models ever exactly reconstruct the reference texture? Under what conditions (image size, number of constraints) does this occur?

Overall, the paper presents a compelling and provocative empirical observation and a useful minimal baseline. With stronger quantitative evaluation, clearer architectural/optimization details, and broader comparisons, it could make a solid contribution.
