- Main contribution: a single convolutional layer with ReLU and random filters plus Gram-matrix matching can synthesize high-quality textures; a multi-scale variant often rivals VGG-based multi-layer models.
- Claim: neither depth nor supervised pretraining is necessary for strong texture synthesis within the Gram-matching framework.
- Claim: combining multiple scales in one layer matters more than simply increasing a single-scale receptive field.
- Empirical work: systematic comparisons of single-layer models with random, Fourier, and k-means/PCA-learned filters and various sizes.
- Proposal: use the VGG Gram-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity.
- Conceptual discussion: frames results in a MaxEnt perspective and highlights a trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary rejection reason 1: empirical support is mainly qualitative and quantitative evaluation relies on a non-independent VGG Gram loss tied to a competing baseline.
- Primary rejection reason 2: important architectural and experimental details are underspecified, and comparisons to related fixed-filter methods are incomplete.
- Novelty critique: the idea is interesting but largely a straightforward variant of established methods; lacks head-to-head quantitative comparisons to scattering and modern Portilla–Simoncelli.
- Empirical rigor critique: no aggregate metrics, confidence intervals, statistical tests, user study, or measurements of sample variability are provided.
- Methodological clarity critique: multi-scale architecture, filter counts/sizes, initialization, and optimizer/hyperparameter sensitivity are not fully specified; code availability not stated.
- Scope critique: conclusions about depth/pretraining are broader than supported by experiments limited to Gram-matching and a restricted texture set.
- Evaluation recommendations: provide aggregated VGG-loss statistics, pixel-domain baselines, SSIM, and a small human study (pairwise/2AFC) to validate the VGG proxy.
- Baseline recommendations: include scattering transform–based synthesis, modern Portilla–Simoncelli, and spectrum-constrained variants under matched optimization/runtime budgets.
- Reproducibility recommendations: specify exact multi-scale filter sizes, channel allocations, initialization ranges, normalization, ReLU placement, and sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters.
- Practical recommendations: report compute costs (time/memory) and compare to VGG synthesis and hierarchical random CNNs; analyze optimizer choices and gradient scaling sensitivity.
- Suggested caveats: temper claims about depth/pretraining, clarify failure modes (structured scenes, non-stationary textures, high-frequency microtextures), and consider independent metrics (LPIPS, DISTS).
- Questions for authors (summarized): exact multi-scale config and sensitivity; number of seeds and variability; evaluation under LPIPS/DISTS or human studies; quantitative comparison to scattering; aggregate statistics across 20 textures; importance of gradient scaling/LBFGS vs other optimizers; plan to release code.
