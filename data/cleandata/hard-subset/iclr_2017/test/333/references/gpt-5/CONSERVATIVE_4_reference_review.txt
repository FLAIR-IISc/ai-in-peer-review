1. Summary of the paper’s contributions
- The paper asks: which components are truly necessary to synthesize natural textures? It challenges the perceived necessity of depth and supervised pretraining by proposing a minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching of activations.
- It explores multiple single-layer filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA; 11×11 primarily) and a random multi-scale variant (up to 55×55 receptive fields).
- It uses optimization-based synthesis (L-BFGS-B) with tuned settings and evaluates texture quality primarily via a VGG-based Gram-loss computed across multiple layers (with some qualitative inspection).
- It argues that (i) random features can rival Gatys et al. (2015) on many textures, (ii) multi-scale is key to capturing both small- and long-range structure, (iii) ReLU nonlinearity is indispensable, and (iv) depth/pretraining are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.
- It discusses the MaxEnt perspective and the importance of allowing small mismatches in constraints for finite images, and raises the perceptual-similarity vs. variability trade-off.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical validation of the central claims: evaluation is dominated by a single, non-independent metric (VGG Gram-loss) and qualitative examples, with no human perceptual study and limited diversity assessment.
- The work’s novelty is incremental relative to established parametric texture models (e.g., Portilla–Simoncelli) and prior CNN Gram-statistics; the empirical evidence provided does not rigorously substantiate the strong claim that depth and pretraining are unnecessary.

3. Supporting arguments
- On the problem and motivation: The question—how minimal can texture synthesis be—fits well with the literature and is interesting. Relating findings to MaxEnt is appropriate. However, the paper positions itself strongly against prior conclusions about depth/pretraining without supplying the level of rigorous evaluation (especially perceptual) needed to overturn them.
- On methodology: The proposed model is a straightforward variant of Gatys et al.’s Gram-statistics approach with a single layer of random filters and multi-scale extensions. This is a reasonable baseline to test. The optimization relies on L-BFGS-B with a large loss scaling (1e7) and 2000 iterations; the claim that prior failures with random weights are due to “more elaborate tuning” is plausible but not convincingly ablated (e.g., sensitivity to scaling, iterations, solver choice).
- On evaluation: 
  - The primary quantitative measure is VGG Gram-loss. This is not an independent perceptual metric and is known to be imperfect; the paper itself notes mismatches (especially for highly regular textures). No human perceptual study (even small-scale 2AFC or Turing-style) is provided, despite the core claim being perceptual.
  - There is no principled assessment of variability/diversity. The discussion acknowledges the issue but offers no empirical quantification (e.g., multiple samples per texture, dispersion in LPIPS/VGG-space, or human discrimination of inpainted patches).
  - Dataset/coverage appears limited (≈20 textures showcased); selection criteria, statistical summaries (means/variances across textures), and significance analyses are absent.
  - Baselines are incomplete: there is no quantitative comparison to classic hand-crafted models (Portilla–Simoncelli) or to more recent fast parametric texture networks (e.g., feed-forward stylization methods), nor to power-spectrum-constrained methods, beyond a few qualitative remarks.
- On claims vs. evidence: The claim that random single-layer models “often rival” the state of the art is not backed by a robust, independent evaluation. Occasional lower VGG-loss than the VGG-synthesis itself suggests the metric can be gamed or misaligned, reinforcing the need for human evaluation.
- On practical aspects: The multi-scale random filters with large kernels are computationally heavy; the paper notes hierarchical alternatives but does not demonstrate a convincing efficiency–quality tradeoff. Reproducibility could be improved (no code link; sensitivity to random seeds not reported).

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add a small psychophysical study (e.g., 2AFC on “same texture or not?” or an inpainting test as you suggest) across a representative texture set. Even 20–30 participants with a few dozen trials would substantially strengthen claims.
- Quantify variability: generate multiple samples per texture; report dispersion in perceptual feature spaces (e.g., LPIPS, VGG-layer activations) and human discriminability. Show the trade-off as a function of optimization steps or relaxation of the constraint.
- Provide broader, aggregated metrics: report mean/variance VGG-loss/LPIPS over a larger texture dataset (e.g., DTD), with confidence intervals, for all variants (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA, multi-scale) and key baselines (Gatys, Portilla–Simoncelli).
- Include rigorous ablations: 
  - Number of filters vs. performance; sensitivity to random initialization seeds; effect of ReLU vs. other nonlinearities; filter size vs. multi-scale; padding/stride choices.
  - Optimization sensitivity (iterations, loss scaling, solver).
- Compare to Portilla–Simoncelli both qualitatively and quantitatively; include a modern perceptual metric (LPIPS) in addition to VGG Gram-loss; consider FID computed on texture patches with an appropriate feature extractor (acknowledging FID’s limitations for textures).
- Report efficiency: wall-clock time, memory, iterations to convergence, across resolutions; discuss feasibility and any acceleration (FFT conv, separable approximations).
- Release code and pretrained filters to improve reproducibility.
- Clarify the dataset and selection protocol for the showcased textures to mitigate concerns about cherry-picking.

Questions for the authors
- How many textures were evaluated in total, and how were they selected? Can you provide aggregated statistics (mean/variance) across the full set?
- How sensitive are results to random seeds and to the number of feature maps? Please provide plots of performance vs. N and vs. filter size for several textures.
- Can you provide a small-scale human study to validate that your multi-scale random model “often rivals” Gatys et al. perceptually?
- How diverse are the generated samples? For a fixed texture, if you run multiple syntheses, how much variation do you observe (quantified), and how does it compare to Gatys et al. and Portilla–Simoncelli?
- Can you detail the role of the loss scaling (1e7) and iterations? Does performance degrade substantially without this scaling, and is this the critical factor behind the discrepancy with prior reports on random weights?
- Do your conclusions hold when using alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) and/or when evaluating in human studies on highly regular textures where VGG-loss seems misaligned?
- Can you provide runtime/memory benchmarks and discuss feasible accelerations or hierarchical implementations without significant quality loss?
