Summary and contributions
The paper asks a crisp and valuable question: what minimal ingredients are required to synthesize natural textures? It argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is essential. The authors show that a single convolutional layer with random filters, matched via Gram-matrix statistics as in Gatys et al., can produce textures that often rival a VGG-based multi-layer model. Key contributions:
- A strong minimal baseline: single-layer, random-filter CNNs (and variants with Fourier/k-means/PCA filters), optimized via L-BFGS to match Gram statistics.
- A multi-scale random-filter variant that significantly improves over single-scale by capturing small-, mid-, and long-range correlations; ReLU nonlinearity is shown to be critical.
- A systematic ablation across filter families, filter counts, and scales; surprising finding that random filters can outperform unsupervisedly learned or analytic filters.
- Use of a VGG-based texture loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity; discussion of variability, and a thoughtful connection to the maximum entropy framework with finite-size effects.

Decision: Weak accept
Reason 1: The paper offers a simple, well-executed, and impactful baseline that challenges prevailing assumptions about the necessity of depth and pretraining for texture synthesis, supported by ablations (scales, filter count, nonlinearity).
Reason 2: The conceptual discussion linking Gram-based synthesis to MaxEnt under finite-sample uncertainties is insightful and timely, and the multi-scale finding is practically useful.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well placed in the literature of parametric texture models (Portilla & Simoncelli) and Gram-matrix-based synthesis (Gatys et al.). It focuses the scope to textures (not general image generation), making the “minimal ingredients” question concrete and answerable.
- Methodology correctness: The synthesis objective and gradient-based L-BFGS-B optimization are standard. The ablations are meaningful (scales, feature counts, linear vs. ReLU). The observation that multi-scale random filters outperform single-scale—even with the same total number of filters—substantively supports the “multi-scale is crucial” claim.
- Empirical support: Visual results are convincing for many textures, especially regular man-made patterns; the VGG-loss proxy provides some quantitative structure. The paper also observes and explains that single-layer optimization may get better (lower) VGG losses for some textures than directly optimizing in VGG space, due to different optimization landscapes.
- Novelty: The core empirical finding—that random, single-layer, multi-scale filters can yield competitive textures—goes against earlier reports and serves as an important baseline for the community. This is original and useful.

Main weaknesses
- Evaluation relies heavily on a VGG-based loss as a proxy for human perception, which introduces circularity when comparing to the VGG model, and the paper acknowledges limits (e.g., failure on some regular structures). There is no human study, and no diversity/variability metric beyond qualitative remarks.
- Missing rigor and detail in parts: the multi-scale architecture is not fully specified (#filters per scale; exact set of filter sizes; allocation strategy); variability across random seeds is not reported; there is no runtime/memory comparison despite practical claims about efficiency; no systematic study of the number of filters vs. quality.
- Related work: The connection to scattering transform-based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat for images, not only audio) could be deepened; other analytic filterbanks (e.g., Gabor, wavelets) might be more relevant baselines than PCA/k-means and Fourier alone.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report variability: For each method, generate multiple samples per texture and report the distribution of VGG-loss to the reference and the pairwise distances among samples to gauge diversity. Simple statistics (mean, std) would already strengthen claims about the similarity–variability trade-off.
- Clarify multi-scale details: List exact filter sizes, number of filters per scale, and normalization. Provide ablations on scale combinations to pinpoint marginal utility of each scale.
- Fairness and robustness: Include sensitivity to number of feature maps (e.g., 256/512/1024) and to random seeds. Consider random orthogonal filters vs. i.i.d. Gaussian to control conditioning.
- Extend baselines: Compare to scattering transforms on images and to recent spectrum-constrained Gram models (e.g., Liu et al., 2016) with identical optimization budgets.
- Efficiency: Provide wall-clock times and memory use for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. hierarchical multi-layer random nets; discuss FFT-based convolution or dilated convolutions as efficient alternatives to large spatial kernels.
- Perceptual evaluation: Even a small-scale two-alternative forced-choice user study (or an inpainting-based test as you suggest) would substantively reduce reliance on VGG-loss.
- Reproducibility: Release code and a script with all hyperparameters (including the 1e7 gradient scaling factor) and seeds. Specify whitening method for k-means (ZCA/PCA), and the exact data mean subtraction.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale architecture: What exact filter sizes did you use, and how many filters per scale? How were they normalized across scales to balance Gram contributions?
2) Seed sensitivity: How stable are results across different random initializations of filters and synthesis noise? Please report variance in VGG-loss and qualitative failures.
3) Diversity: Can you quantify variability among multiple samples from the same reference (e.g., distribution of pairwise VGG distances; spectrum statistics)? Do your models tend to collapse to near-deterministic reconstructions for some textures?
4) Optimization: You scale the loss/gradients by 1e7 to avoid early stopping. Can you explain why L-BFGS-B would early stop otherwise, and whether alternative line-search tolerances or using Adam/Adagrad would remove the need for global scaling?
5) Comparison fairness: Why do some multi-scale random samples achieve lower VGG-loss than the VGG-optimized images? Is this due to poor conditioning/initialization in the VGG optimization, and can VGG results be improved with your initialization consistently (beyond the anecdotal appendix figure)?
6) Relation to scattering: How do your findings relate to fixed analytic filterbanks (e.g., wavelet/Gabor/scattering)? Would random multi-scale orthogonalized filterbanks approximate their performance?
7) Efficiency: What are the runtimes for 2000 iterations per texture for single-scale and multi-scale models at common resolutions? Could dilated convolutions or FFTs approximate the receptive fields more cheaply?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, insightful empirical study that provides a strong baseline and raises important questions about what matters for texture synthesis. With stronger quantitative evaluation and clearer architectural/efficiency details, it would be a solid contribution.
