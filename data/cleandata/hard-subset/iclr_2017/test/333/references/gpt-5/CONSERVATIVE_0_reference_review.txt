Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: how much of the perceived success in modern texture synthesis is due to deep, pretrained architectures versus the statistics used at synthesis time? The authors propose a deliberately minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU and random filters, using Gram matrix matching as the sufficient statistics, optimized with L-BFGS-B. They explore several single-layer variants (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA-derived filters) and a random multi-scale version with large receptive fields. They evaluate primarily by a VGG-based Gram loss and visual inspection, and argue that depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary to obtain high-quality textures; in many cases their simple model “rivals” Gatys et al. They also provide a conceptual discussion relating practical synthesis to maximum entropy modeling and the role of allowing small mismatches in constraints.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical validation is not rigorous enough to support the central claims. The primary quantitative metric (VGG Gram loss) is a proxy that is not validated here as a perceptual metric and is arguably circular. No psychophysics or user studies are provided, and dataset-level quantitative summaries are limited.
2) Important experimental confounds and baselines are insufficiently controlled. The gains could be due to optimization/tuning rather than the architectural conclusion, and comparisons to several established texture models are missing.

Supporting arguments
- On evaluation rigor and metric choice: The paper relies heavily on a VGG-based Gram loss to quantify “perceptual similarity,” while also noting cases where the metric contradicts visual perception (e.g., multi-scale random sometimes attains lower VGG loss than VGG-synthesis). Without a perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC) or a cross-metric validation, it is difficult to trust the quantitative claims. The qualitative panels are interesting but anecdotal; the scope (20 textures) and selection procedure are not specified well enough to generalize.
- Baselines and literature placement: Beyond Gatys and a mention of Portilla–Simoncelli, several relevant baselines are not evaluated: scattering transform–based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat), feed-forward texture networks (Ulyanov et al.), or more recent spectrum-constrained methods. The paper argues random single-layer “rivals” state-of-the-art but does not provide a comprehensive comparison across established methods under consistent budgets.
- Optimization confounds: The paper attributes prior negative findings with random features to “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” and uses L-BFGS-B with a scaled loss/gradient (1e7). There is no ablation showing the sensitivity to optimizer choice, scaling, iteration budget, or initialization. The observation that initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer outputs improves results further underscores optimizer effects that are not disentangled from the main architectural claim.
- Scope and statistics: The exploration of filter types and scales is informative, and the finding that multi-scale random filters perform best is plausible. However, the study lacks dataset-level statistics (means/variances over many textures) and systematic sweeps over number of filters and kernel sizes to establish trends and diminishing returns. Claims like “often rivals” are subjective without aggregated metrics.
- Efficiency and practicality: The multi-scale model uses large kernels (up to 55×55) and is acknowledged to be inefficient; no runtime/memory comparisons are reported. The statement that hierarchical nets are faster but not better lacks quantitative support.
- Reproducibility and clarity: Implementation details leave gaps (e.g., not all hyperparameters are specified; no code link; heavy preprocessing for k-means over 1e7 patches). References contain numerous formatting issues. Some definitions (e.g., dp) are unused in the results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a proper evaluation protocol: a) a dataset of diverse textures with a clear selection procedure; b) human 2AFC/Turing tests for perceptual indistinguishability; c) multiple perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS, SSIM variants) alongside VGG Gram loss; d) report means/SDs over multiple random seeds and samples per texture; e) quantify sample diversity.
- Strengthen baselines: include scattering transform methods, feed-forward texture networks, spectrum-constrained synthesis, and a tuned Portilla–Simoncelli implementation. Ensure all methods are run with comparable iteration budgets and initialization.
- Disentangle optimization from architecture: ablate optimizer (L-BFGS-B vs Adam), loss/gradient scaling, iteration counts, stopping criteria, and initialization. Report convergence curves and wall-clock time.
- Systematic ablations: scale number of filters and kernel sizes, with and without ReLU, and report aggregate trends. Include a fairness check where parameter counts and receptive field sizes are matched across single-scale and multi-scale variants.
- Practicality: provide runtime/memory benchmarks and discuss how to approximate large receptive fields efficiently if the goal is a usable baseline.
- Clarity and reproducibility: release code and filter-generation scripts; clean up references and notation; make all evaluation textures and synthesized samples available.

Questions for the authors
1) What is the exact dataset (number and types of textures, selection criteria)? How many random seeds and samples per texture were used to compute each reported number?
2) How robust are the conclusions to optimizer and hyperparameters? What happens without the 1e7 scaling factor? How many iterations are actually needed, and what is the wall-clock cost?
3) Can you provide a user study (2AFC) to validate that the proposed model is perceptually on par with Gatys et al. across a representative texture set?
4) How does performance scale with the number of filters and kernel size? Are there diminishing returns? Please include aggregated plots.
5) What is the quantitative effect of ReLU versus linear activations across textures?
6) How do the random single-layer and multi-scale models compare to scattering-based and feed-forward texture networks under the same optimization budget?
7) Can you quantify variability/diversity across samples (e.g., pairwise distances in a perceptual space) and demonstrate that the method does not collapse to near-copies?
8) Please provide runtime/memory comparisons versus VGG-based synthesis and details on the hierarchical fast variant you mention.
