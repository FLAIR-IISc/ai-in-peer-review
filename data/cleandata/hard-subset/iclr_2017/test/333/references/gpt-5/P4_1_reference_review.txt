1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper investigates how minimal a parametric texture synthesis model can be while still producing high-quality natural textures. It revisits the Gram-matrix-based synthesis framework and argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are necessary. The core claims are:
- A single convolutional layer with random filters, when combined with Gram-matrix statistics and ReLU, can synthesize textures of competitive perceptual quality relative to the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. (2015).
- A multi-scale single-layer model (large receptive fields via large filters) notably improves over single-scale and often rivals the VGG-based method, especially for regular textures.
- The number of feature maps and the presence of nonlinearity are crucial; combining multiple filter sizes in one model outperforms a single large filter size.
- The paper proposes to use the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative evaluation metric, reducing reliance on purely qualitative human inspection.
- It offers a discussion generalizing maximum-entropy perspectives to account for finite-size images via soft constraints and an epsilon-environment around the target statistics.

The work includes broad ablations over filter types (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA learned, sampled-from-target) and model sizes, and reports that random filters are surprisingly strong baselines.

2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- The evaluation is predominantly tied to the VGG-based loss used in a competing method, creating a circular metric and lacking rigorous, independent perceptual assessment or aggregate quantitative analysis.
- Important architectural and experimental details (especially for the multi-scale model), figures, and references are incomplete or insufficiently specified, limiting clarity and reproducibility.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three core reviewing questions:

1) What is the specific problem? The paper targets natural texture synthesis via parametric models defined by spatial summary statistics, asking whether depth and supervised pretraining are indispensable and what minimal ingredients are required to achieve high-quality textures.

2) Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? The motivation is clear: ablate depth and pretraining in the Gram-based framework to establish a strong minimal baseline. The work is situated relative to Gatys et al. and classic texture models (e.g., Portilla & Simoncelli). The discussion connecting Gram statistics, stationarity, and maximum entropy is appropriate. However, the literature treatment and references are incomplete (placeholders in the bibliography) and some claims (e.g., prior failures with random weights) would benefit from more systematic contextualization.

3) Do the results support the claims with rigor?
- Positives: The ablation breadth (filter types and counts) is useful; findings that nonlinearity and multi-scale receptive fields matter are consistent with intuition. The observation that random filters can be as good as learned ones is a valuable and surprising negative result that could influence baselining.
- Limitations: The primary objective metric is the VGG-based synthesis loss (averaged across five layers), which favors images that match the same representation used by the competing method. This risks circularity and may not correlate with human perception in all cases (the authors themselves note failures on man-made textures). No human studies or independent perceptual metrics are provided. There are no aggregate statistics across datasets (e.g., mean and variance of losses across many textures, significance tests); results appear embedded in figures (not available here), with anecdotal commentary. Claims about robustness to local minima and optimization advantages are not supported by quantitative experiments (e.g., multiple seeds, convergence curves).
- Reproducibility: Essential details of the multi-scale model (exact filter sizes used, number of filters per scale, normalization of filters, initialization distributions) are not fully specified in the main text, and the figures referred to for many conclusions are missing. The reliance on gradient scaling (1e7) to prevent L-BFGS early stopping is implementation-specific and not ablated. References contain numerous placeholders and inconsistencies.

On novelty: While the Gram-matrix approach itself is known, the demonstration that single-layer random-filter CNNs (especially multi-scale) can rival VGG-based results is a meaningful and practically relevant negative result and baseline. However, without stronger evaluation and clearer specification, the novelty is undermined by limited rigor.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all items affect the accept/reject decision)
- Clarify and fully specify the multi-scale architecture: exact set of filter sizes, number of filters per scale, weight initialization ranges, whether filters are normalized (e.g., unit norm), and any per-scale weighting in the Gram loss.
- Provide aggregate quantitative results across a standardized texture set: for each method, report mean ± std of VGG-loss and other metrics. Include sensitivity to number of filters and iterations, and ablations for the optimization parameters (e.g., gradient scaling, box constraints).
- Address metric circularity: complement VGG-loss with additional measures. If human studies are not feasible, at least include a 2AFC-style pilot, or report additional perceptual proxies that are not directly aligned to VGG (e.g., alternative feature spaces, patch classification of real vs. synthesized using a model not trained on ImageNet). If available, report multiple seeds to quantify variability vs. similarity trade-offs.
- Report runtime and memory comparisons for single-layer multi-scale vs. multi-layer hierarchical variants; quantify the efficiency-accuracy trade-off rather than only qualitative statements.
- Improve presentation quality: fix references and placeholders; ensure figures are legible and tied to quantitative summaries; include code or sufficient pseudocode for reproducibility.
- The max-entropy/epsilon-environment discussion is interesting; consider tightening it with a concrete formalization or an empirical demonstration of how varying epsilon affects variability and perceptual similarity.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact specifications of the multi-scale model (filter sizes, filters per scale, initialization/normalization)? Are results sensitive to these choices?
- How many textures were evaluated quantitatively, and can you report aggregate statistics (means, variances) and significance analyses across that set?
- How consistent are results across random seeds (both initialization of filters and synthesis initial noise)? Please provide variability analyses and loss curves.
- Can you provide evidence to support the claim that the single-layer optimization is less prone to local minima (e.g., success rates, final losses, or convergence speed distributions)?
- How do your methods perform under an evaluation that is not based on VGG features (e.g., a classifier trained on unrelated data or a simple perceptual 2AFC study)?
- What is the computational cost (time/memory) for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. VGG-based synthesis at comparable quality levels?
- Are there classes of textures where the random multi-scale model consistently underperforms VGG (beyond anecdotal cases)? Can you characterize failure modes systematically?
