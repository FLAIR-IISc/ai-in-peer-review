1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates the minimal ingredients needed for high-quality parametric texture synthesis. It challenges the prevailing view that depth and pretraining on ImageNet are essential, showing that a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLUs, and Gram-matrix matching can synthesize textures that often rival the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. The authors:
- Propose a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random, hand-crafted (Fourier), or unsupervised filters, optimized to match Gram statistics of feature maps.
- Introduce a multi-scale single-layer variant (large receptive fields via large filters) that improves synthesis quality and captures long-range structure.
- Provide extensive qualitative comparisons and a quantitative evaluation using a VGG-based perceptual loss; present ablations on filter size, number of channels, and nonlinearity (ReLU vs. linear).
- Discuss the MaxEnt perspective and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, arguing for allowing small mismatches in constraints for finite-size images.

Overall, this is a clear, well-written exploration that establishes a surprisingly strong minimalist baseline and offers thought-provoking insights about what matters for texture synthesis.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty beyond demonstrating that random single-layer (multi-scale) features with Gram statistics work well; this is close in spirit to earlier wavelet/Portilla–Simoncelli and scattering-based approaches with fixed features, and to Gatys et al. with learned features.
- Insufficient quantitative/psychophysical evaluation to substantiate “rival state-of-the-art” claims; heavy reliance on a VGG-based loss metric without human studies or diversity metrics.

3. Supporting arguments
On the problem: The question—how minimal can a texture model be while preserving perceptual fidelity—is timely and important. The scope (stationary textures) is clear.

On motivation and placement: The paper is well situated amidst texture synthesis, Gram statistics, and MaxEnt. However, the relation to scattering transform-based texture synthesis (fixed filters, multi-layer) deserves deeper comparison; those works are highly relevant to the “no training needed” thesis. The relation to classical single-layer correlation-based models (Portilla–Simoncelli) is acknowledged but could be better quantified.

On correctness and rigor:
- Methods: The optimization setup (L-BFGS-B, box constraints, gradient scaling by 1e7) is described, but appears ad hoc. The claim that better optimization explains prior negative results with random weights is plausible but not rigorously supported (no systematic sensitivity analysis).
- Experimental coverage: Many filter banks and ablations are considered. The multi-scale vs single-scale analysis is helpful, and the role of ReLU is noted.
- Evaluation: The main quantitative metric is a VGG-based loss. This is reasonable as a proxy, but it is also the objective used by one of the baselines and is known to be imperfect, especially for man-made regular textures (as the authors note). There is no human evaluation (e.g., 2AFC), no diversity/entropy proxy, and limited statistical reporting (e.g., multiple seeds, variability across runs). Claims that the method “often rivals” VGG are thus not strongly substantiated.
- Reproducibility: Many implementation details are given, but key specifics are missing (exact multi-scale filter sizes and allocation, number of filters per scale, seeds, full optimizer settings). No code link is provided.

On significance: The main insight—that depth and pretraining are not strictly necessary when using Gram statistics over rich, multi-scale random features—is interesting and valuable as a baseline and for understanding the role of features vs. statistics. That said, the conceptual contributions (MaxEnt discussion, similarity-variability trade-off) remain at a high level without formalization or new methodology to measure variability.

4. Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Evaluation:
  - Include a small-scale psychophysical study (e.g., 2AFC discrimination between real and synthesized patches, or inpainting-based tests as you propose) to validate perceptual claims.
  - Quantify sample diversity: report intra-model variability across multiple seeds; consider LPIPS-based distances to the reference and among generated samples; report Gram-matching errors and their variance.
  - Compare against scattering transform-based texture synthesis and Portilla–Simoncelli with a fair, modern implementation; include runtime and parameter counts.
  - Report computational efficiency: time per synthesis, memory, and how multi-scale single-layer compares to multi-layer shallow hierarchies that you explored.

- Methodology:
  - Provide full details of the multi-scale design (filter sizes, number per scale), and a sensitivity study to optimizer settings (gradient scaling factor, iteration budget, initialization).
  - Analyze why ReLU helps: e.g., show how activation distributions and resulting Gram spectra differ with/without nonlinearity.
  - Consider connections to kernel two-sample testing/MMD: Gram of random features approximates second-order statistics in a random feature space; this might give a principled view of why many random filters suffice.

- Claims and presentation:
  - Temper “rival state-of-the-art” claims unless supported by human evaluation; highlight that the VGG-loss sometimes favors your outputs due to optimization ease and reduced variability.
  - Improve related work on fixed-feature texture models (scattering, random wavelets) and clarify what is genuinely new here (e.g., empirical demonstration that a single-layer, multi-scale random CNN with ReLU and Gram matching forms a strong baseline).
  - Clean up references (dates, venues) and ensure fairness in VGG comparisons (e.g., max vs average pooling choices).

Questions for the authors
- What exact filter sizes and number of filters per scale were used in the multi-scale model? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- How many random seeds per texture were evaluated? What is the spread in VGG-loss and in perceptual appearance across seeds?
- Can you provide results from a small human study (e.g., 2AFC) to corroborate VGG-loss as a perceptual proxy on your textures?
- How does your method compare to scattering-based texture synthesis (fixed wavelets, multi-layer) both qualitatively and quantitatively?
- How sensitive are your conclusions to the L-BFGS settings and the gradient scaling of 1e7? Does Adam or LBFGS without scaling perform similarly?
- Could you release code and the full set of synthesized images to facilitate reproducibility?

Despite my reject recommendation, I find the central empirical insight valuable. With stronger evaluation (especially psychophysics and diversity), clearer positioning relative to fixed-feature texture models, and fuller methodological detail, this work could make a solid contribution.
