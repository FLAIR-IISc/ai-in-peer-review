1. Summary of the paper’s claims and contributions
The paper asks what is truly necessary to synthesize high-quality natural textures. It argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is essential: a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching can produce textures that often rival the VGG-based model of Gatys et al. The authors:
- Propose a strong minimal baseline using single-layer CNNs with random, hand-crafted (Fourier), or unsupervised filters (k-means/PCA).
- Show that increasing the number of feature maps improves quality; multi-scale random filters (with large receptive fields) perform best and capture long-range regularities.
- Demonstrate the necessity of nonlinearity (ReLU) and of multi-scale combinations.
- Use a VGG-based Gram-matrix loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and discuss the trade-off between similarity and variability within a MaxEnt perspective.
- Provide qualitative comparisons to Gatys et al. and analyze optimization effects (e.g., VGG synthesis improves when initialized from single-layer outputs).

Overall, the message is clear and potentially provocative: much of the perceived power in modern texture synthesis may come from the choice of summary statistics (Gram of feature maps) rather than depth or supervised training.

2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- Insufficient empirical rigor to support the central claims, particularly the “rivals state of the art” conclusion: evaluation relies almost exclusively on a VGG-based proxy metric and qualitative figures without a human study, and the VGG baseline seems under-optimized.
- Limited novelty relative to existing fixed-feature texture models (e.g., scattering networks, wavelet/Portilla–Simoncelli): the new finding that random filters can work is interesting but not established with sufficiently strong evidence or analysis.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three reviewing questions:

- What problem is tackled? The paper studies minimal conditions for parametric texture synthesis and whether depth/pretraining are necessary.

- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? The paper engages with key references (Gatys et al.; Portilla & Simoncelli; Bruna & Mallat; GANs/PixelCNNs). However, the connection to scattering-based fixed feature models—which already showed strong texture synthesis with non-learned, multi-scale features—is underdeveloped; the novelty over “fixed filter banks + second-order statistics” is incremental.

- Do results support the claims with rigor?
  - Evaluation largely uses a VGG-based Gram loss as a perceptual proxy. This is an imperfect and potentially biased metric (e.g., it can reward images that exploit the metric). Claims that the proposed method “often rivals” VGG require either human perceptual studies or careful, multiple independent quantitative metrics. The paper acknowledges limitations but still makes strong comparative statements.
  - The reported paradox where the single-layer method achieves lower VGG loss than VGG optimization suggests the VGG baseline is not optimized strongly enough. The authors themselves note deeper models are “prone to local minima” and that initializing VGG with single-layer outputs helps. This undermines the fairness of comparisons.
  - Variability vs. similarity: the paper emphasizes that its procedure may lean toward low variability. No quantitative variability analysis (e.g., entropy proxies, diversity measures, LPIPS diversity, inpainting psychophysics) is provided, so one key desideratum of texture models is unassessed.
  - Experimental details are incomplete for reproducibility: exact multi-scale architecture (filter sizes per scale, number of maps per scale), normalization/initialization specifics, sensitivity to random seeds, and exact hyperparameters beyond a loss scaling factor are not fully specified. No code release is indicated.
  - Some references are incomplete/inconsistent, and figures are essential to judge quality but not accessible in this review context.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all part of the acceptance decision)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Include a small but principled human study (e.g., two-alternative forced-choice discriminability or inpainting-based psychophysics as you suggest) across multiple textures and multiple samples per method.
  - Report multiple quantitative metrics (VGG loss, LPIPS to the reference, power spectrum distance, SSIM as a negative control). Explicitly measure sample diversity/variability (e.g., pairwise LPIPS among samples, patch classification accuracy in an inpainting task).
  - Ensure a strong VGG baseline: multiple random restarts, improved initialization (including your single-layer output as a standard), tuned optimization schedules, and report convergence diagnostics.

- Clarify and expand experimental details:
  - Provide full specification of the multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, number of filters per scale, total parameter counts), padding scheme, and normalization. Analyze sensitivity to the number of feature maps and to the distribution of receptive field sizes.
  - Explain and justify the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling in L-BFGS-B, stopping criteria, and box constraints.
  - Report run-time and memory comparisons; quantify the efficiency benefit (or lack thereof) of hierarchical approximations to large receptive fields.

- Compare more directly to fixed-feature baselines:
  - Include scattering-network texture synthesis and spectrum/phase-based methods (e.g., Liu et al., random phase), in addition to Portilla–Simoncelli. This helps position the “random filters + Gram” model among non-learned, multiscale references.

- Analysis/theory:
  - Provide intuition or analysis for why random multi-scale filters plus ReLU and Gram constraints are sufficient: connections to translation-invariant kernels, second-order stationary processes, rank of Gram matrices vs. number of constraints, overlap of convolutions imposing long-range structure.
  - Examine the role of nonlinearity more thoroughly (e.g., leaky ReLU, squaring, modulus) and its interaction with the Gram statistics.

- Scope of claims:
  - Temper claims that “depth and pretraining are not necessary” by explicitly limiting to texture synthesis (not broader natural image generation). Discuss failure modes (e.g., near-regular man-made patterns where VGG loss disagrees with perception).

Questions for the authors
- How many textures were used, how were they chosen, and do results generalize across categories and resolutions? Please provide a list and quantitative summaries over the full set.
- What is the exact multi-scale configuration (filter sizes, counts per scale)? How sensitive are results to this allocation and to total number of feature maps?
- How sensitive are results to random initialization and to the L-BFGS-B hyperparameters (including the 1e7 scaling)? What is the variance across seeds?
- How strong is your VGG baseline? How many restarts and iterations did you run? Can you adopt your own improved initialization as the default baseline and re-run comparisons?
- Can you provide multiple independent samples per texture and quantify variability/diversity?
- How does your method compare to scattering-based synthesis and to spectrum-constraint methods on the same textures under the same evaluation?
- Do your synthesized textures generalize to larger canvas sizes than the reference? What happens to large-scale regularity?
- Will you release code and a standardized evaluation suite to establish the proposed minimal baseline?
