1. Summary (positive and generous)
The paper asks a clear and timely question: which elements of CNN-based texture synthesis are truly necessary? It challenges two common assumptions—that depth and supervised pretraining are crucial—by proposing a surprisingly strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random filters whose Gram matrix statistics are matched to a reference texture via L-BFGS. The authors conduct a broad set of ablations: random versus learned or hand-crafted filters (k-means, PCA, Fourier), number of feature maps (363 vs 3267), filter sizes, single-scale vs multi-scale, and the role of nonlinearity (ReLU). They introduce a quantitative evaluation based on a VGG-based perceptual loss and show that single-layer random multi-scale models frequently rival, and in some cases surpass, the VGG multi-layer model of Gatys et al. The paper further includes a thoughtful discussion connecting practical synthesis to maximum-entropy modeling under finite-sample uncertainty, and raises the important issue of balancing perceptual similarity and sample variability.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical support relies almost exclusively on a VGG-based loss that is not independent of the modeling framework, lacks a human perceptual study, and provides limited statistical aggregation; claims of matching or surpassing state of the art are therefore insufficiently substantiated.
- Important experimental details (especially for the multi-scale architecture) and comprehensive runtime/efficiency comparisons are missing, and several baselines and analyses that would shore up the central claims are absent.

3. Supporting arguments
- Evaluation rigor: The main metric is the VGG Gram-based loss over five layers. This is closely related to the very statistics the field optimizes for, raising concerns about circularity and metric bias. While the paper acknowledges limitations and briefly contrasts with SSIM, there is no human study (e.g., 2AFC, MUSHRA, or inpainting-based discrimination), no large-scale user evaluation, and no aggregate statistics (means, variances, significance) across the textures. Figures suggest some cases where VGG loss favors the proposed method even over VGG-based synthesis, which underscores the need for human validation.
- Scope and completeness: The multi-scale random model appears central to the strongest results, but its specification is not fully detailed (exact filter sizes, number per scale, normalization, relative weighting, and any per-scale preprocessing). Without a precise description, reproducibility and interpretability suffer.
- Baselines and controls: While the paper includes a commendable variety of filter banks, it lacks critical comparisons that help isolate what’s essential: e.g., spectrum-matching baselines, modern scattering-based models, or simpler Gram-free random-filter statistics. The comparison to Portilla–Simoncelli is mainly qualitative; more quantitative or controlled comparisons would strengthen the case.
- Variability: The paper correctly identifies the importance of sample diversity but does not quantify it (beyond anecdote) or propose a proxy (e.g., feature-space dispersion, FID-style measures adapted to textures, or psychophysical discrimination rates). The claim that the method leans toward similarity at the expense of variability needs measurement.
- Computational aspects: The authors note that large filters (up to 55x55) are inefficient and that hierarchical nets can speed up synthesis. However, there are no runtime/compute/memory measurements or fairness checks (e.g., equalized iteration budgets) to support the efficiency discussion or to verify that tuning choices do not unduly advantage the proposed method.
- Writing and presentation: Some references are incomplete, and formatting/citation placeholders remain. This reduces polish and makes it harder to assess related work positioning. The paper’s theoretical discussion of MaxEnt and epsilon-environments is interesting but remains high-level without concrete algorithmic consequences or experiments.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all decisive for the current recommendation)
- Add a human evaluation. Even a modest 2AFC test on a subset of textures would greatly strengthen claims about perceptual quality and help diagnose cases where VGG-loss diverges from human judgments.
- Precisely specify the multi-scale setup: the exact set of filter sizes, number of filters per scale, initialization ranges, normalization (per-filter and per-scale), and how Gram statistics from different scales are combined. Provide code.
- Quantify variability. Possible proxies: (a) distributional spread of VGG features across multiple samples; (b) classification accuracy in an inpainting discrimination task; (c) distance to nearest neighbor in reference texture patch space; (d) entropy estimates via kNN in feature space.
- Aggregate results across a larger, more diverse texture suite. Provide summary statistics, confidence intervals, and significance tests comparing your approach with VGG-based synthesis and classical baselines.
- Expand baselines: spectrum-only matching (phase randomization), scattering transform–based synthesis, and recent spectrum+CNN hybrids. Also report the effect of different nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, tanh), filter normalizations, and the number of feature maps at fixed compute.
- Provide runtime and memory usage for all methods under comparable stopping criteria, and ablate the effect of the 1e7 gradient scaling and iteration counts on convergence and quality.
- Clarify the role of “Kmeans-Sample” (filters learned from the target texture) in fair comparison; consider separating it clearly as an oracle-style variant.
- Consider a simple probabilistic interpretation (e.g., energy-based model with Gram features) to formalize the epsilon-environment idea and to connect the MaxEnt discussion to an implementable sampling or early-stopping scheme.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale details: What exact filter sizes are used in the “Random multi-scale” model? How many filters per scale, and how are filters normalized? Are Gram matrices computed jointly across all filters or per-scale and then aggregated?
- Optimization sensitivity: How critical are the L-BFGS-B settings (iteration count, gradient scaling 1e7, box constraints)? Do alternative optimizers (Adam/L-BFGS without scaling) yield comparable results?
- Reproducibility: How robust are results to random seeds for filter initialization? Please report mean and variance over multiple seeds for key textures.
- Fairness of VGG comparisons: When VGG-based synthesis underperforms on its own loss relative to your method, can you diagnose why? Did you try stronger optimization for the VGG method (e.g., more iterations, multi-resolution pyramids, better initialization)?
- Variability: Can you quantify diversity across 10–20 samples per texture using any of the proposed proxies, and contrast against VGG-based synthesis?
- Computational profile: Please report wall-clock time and memory for your single-scale and multi-scale variants vs the VGG model on standard image sizes.
- Failure modes: Beyond the few regular man-made cases, what are systematic failure textures (e.g., stochastic microtextures, globally anisotropic patterns)? Can you characterize when depth helps?
- Relation to scattering: How does your single-layer random model compare to scattering-based texture synthesis in quality and compute?

Overall, this paper presents a compelling and useful minimal baseline and an insightful negative result about the necessity of depth/pretraining. With stronger evaluation, clearer multi-scale specification, and more comprehensive comparisons, it could make a valuable contribution.
