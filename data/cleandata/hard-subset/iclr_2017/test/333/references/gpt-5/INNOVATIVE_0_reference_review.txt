1) Summary of contributions
The paper asks a crisp, provocative question: do we really need deep, pretrained feature hierarchies to synthesize convincing natural textures? The authors argue no, and present a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random filters and ReLU, whose Gram-matrix statistics are matched via optimization. They systematically compare random, hand-crafted (Fourier), and unsupervised (k-means/PCA) filters, and small vs large numbers of channels, and show that:
- Random filters perform on par with or better than learned/hand-crafted filters when sufficiently many channels are used.
- A multi-scale single-layer bank (3×3 to 55×55) is crucial: large filters alone capture long-range structure but miss fine detail; small filters do the opposite; combining scales captures both.
- Nonlinearities (ReLU) are necessary.
- Using VGG Gram losses as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, their single-layer multi-scale model often rivals and occasionally surpasses the VGG-based Gatys model, likely due to easier optimization.
They further offer a thoughtful discussion connecting practical synthesis to maximum entropy modeling, emphasizing the need to balance perceptual similarity and variability, and advocating explicit tolerance (epsilon-environments) in constraints.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and boldness: a compelling, minimalistic result that challenges the perceived necessity of depth and supervised pretraining for texture synthesis.
- Strong empirical evidence and ablations that elevate this from an anecdote to a baseline others should measure against.

3) Supporting arguments
- Breadth of comparisons: The paper explores multiple filter constructions (random, Fourier, k-means with/without whitening, PCA), two orders of magnitude in channel count, and single- vs multi-scale setups. The conclusion that random features suffice is robust across these choices.
- Clear insight on scale: The targeted experiments isolating filter-size effects convincingly show why multi-scale is the key ingredient for capturing both local and global regularities.
- Quantitative evaluation: While not perfect, using VGG Gram losses is a reasonable proxy over purely visual inspection and reveals the surprising finding that the multi-scale random model sometimes attains lower VGG loss than optimizing VGG loss directly—supporting the claim that optimization hurdles, not representational capacity, often limit performance.
- Practical significance: Establishing a strong minimal baseline reframes the design space for texture synthesis (and, by extension, some image generation tasks), and is likely to influence both algorithm design and evaluation practice.
- Thoughtful discussion: The MaxEnt perspective and explicit call-out of the similarity–variability trade-off are valuable, framing why “soft” constraint satisfaction is both inevitable and desirable in finite images.

Caveats I am willing to overlook given the novelty:
- Evaluation leans on VGG-based metrics; a human study is absent.
- The approach is optimization-based and computationally heavy for large filters; efficiency is discussed but not fully addressed.
- Some textual and reference formatting issues.

4) Suggestions to improve (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Reporting and reproducibility:
  - Release code, filter generators, and full hyperparameters (including the 1e7 loss scaling) to cement this as a community baseline.
  - Provide runtime and memory benchmarks for typical resolutions, with per-scale breakdowns and comparisons to VGG-based optimization.
- Ablations/analysis:
  - Provide curves showing synthesis quality vs number of feature maps for random filters, to show the sample complexity of the representation.
  - Detail the multi-scale composition (exact filter sizes, channel counts per scale) and show how redistributing channels across scales affects quality at fixed total budget.
  - Explore other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, tanh) and simple normalizations (instance/feature norm) to test how general the result is.
  - Analyze sensitivity to initialization and stopping criteria; quantify the similarity–variability trade-off by plotting distributions of VGG-loss across multiple seeds.
- Evaluation:
  - Include a small psychophysical 2AFC or inpainting-based test (as you suggest) on a subset to validate that VGG loss tracks human judgments here.
  - Compare more directly to scattering moments (Bruna & Mallat) and Portilla–Simoncelli under the same VGG-loss and visual protocol.
  - Report cases where the optimization exactly reconstructs the reference; characterize when/why this occurs.
- Theory/insight:
  - Elaborate on the connection between Gram matching with random features and second-order statistics of random projections of patches; a brief theoretical sketch would further demystify why random features work.
  - Formalize the epsilon-environment perspective with a concrete metric and threshold selection protocol; demonstrate how increasing epsilon trades similarity for variability in practice.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: What exact filter sizes and per-scale channel counts were used in the best multi-scale model? How sensitive is performance to reallocating channels across scales at fixed total channels?
- Nonlinearity: Beyond ReLU vs identity, did you try leaky ReLU or other pointwise nonlinearities? Do signed activations improve variability without hurting similarity?
- Feature normalization: Did normalizing feature maps (e.g., per-channel mean/variance) change synthesis quality or optimizer behavior?
- Variability: Can you quantify inter-sample diversity (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss between multiple samples matching the same target) and contrast it with Gatys-VGG?
- Optimization: The 1e7 loss scaling and use of LBFGS-B seems important. How do alternative optimizers (Adam) and stopping criteria affect outcomes? Any guidance on robust defaults?
- Efficiency: What are typical wall-clock times and memory footprints for 256×256 and 512×512 images for single- vs multi-scale? Any FFT-based convolution speedups considered?
- Exact reconstructions: Under what conditions did you observe exact reproductions of the reference? Do these correlate with highly regular, low-entropy textures?
- Generalization: How does the method fare on non-stationary or quasi-periodic textures, or when synthesizing larger outputs than the exemplar?
- Evaluation paradox: For cases where multi-scale achieves lower VGG-loss than directly optimizing VGG, can you provide aggregate statistics across textures to quantify how often and how much this occurs?

Overall, this paper is a refreshing and impactful rethinking of what is essential for texture synthesis. I recommend acceptance.
