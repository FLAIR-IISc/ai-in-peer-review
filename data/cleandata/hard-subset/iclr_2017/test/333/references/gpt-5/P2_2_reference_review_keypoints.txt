- Clear question and simple baseline: single conv layer with random filters + ReLU + Gram-matrix matching; explores single- and multi-scale variants and reports VGG-Gram loss and visual comparisons.  
- Main claim: depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary; number of features, multi-scale receptive fields, and nonlinearity are most important.  
- Practical contribution: optimization details (LBFGS, loss scaling) make random-feature synthesis work reliably.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline) because evaluation is mainly qualitative and uses the same VGG Gram loss as the target, and novelty is incremental with insufficient ablations.  
- Strength: Well-articulated problem and good positioning against classical and neural texture models.  
- Literature gap: Needs deeper discussion of scattering-based and earlier random-conv work (differences in invariances, nonlinearity, multi-scale design).  
- Evidence weakness: Central claim is plausible but supported mostly by visuals and a circular VGG-based metric rather than independent perceptual measures or user studies.  
- Ablation gaps: Some qualitative ablations exist (maps, scales, ReLU) but no systematic quantitative study across textures, seeds, or metrics.  
- Optimization claim unproven: No controlled ablation showing sensitivity to optimizer, loss scaling, iteration count, or initialization.  
- Variability vs similarity: Conceptual MaxEnt discussion is interesting but not backed by sampling/stopping-criterion experiments or diversity measurements.  
- Efficiency claim weak: Acknowledges large-filter cost but provides no runtime/memory benchmarks or concrete efficiency comparisons with deep nets.  
- Technical soundness: Method is simple and clearly defined, but evaluation protocol lacks modern rigor (no user study, no independent perceptual metric, no dataset-level stats, no seed variability).  
- Evaluation recommendations: Add psychophysical tests (e.g., 2AFC) or LPIPS, report results on standard datasets (e.g., DTD) with aggregate stats and multiple seeds, and quantify sample diversity.  
- Ablation recommendations: Systematically vary feature map counts and scales, detail multi-scale architecture, test alternative nonlinearities and normalizations, and run controlled optimizer/iteration ablations.  
- Comparison recommendations: Compare to scattering-transform synthesis and spectrum-constrained CNN methods, and include timing/memory benchmarks.  
- Reproducibility: Release code, exact hyperparameters, seeds, preprocessing steps, and ensure figures/references are clear.  
- Questions for authors: exact multi-scale configuration; sensitivity to feature map counts/minimal capacity; quantitative optimization ablation; sample variability metrics and examples; code release timeline; runtime/memory comparisons across variants.  
- Overall: Interesting and potentially influential baseline, but requires stronger independent evaluation and more rigorous ablations to be publishable.
