1) Summary and contributions
The paper asks a focused and important question: what elements are truly necessary to synthesize convincing natural textures using Gram-based statistics? The authors argue that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are indispensable. They propose a minimal baseline: a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching, optimized with L-BFGS-B. They show:
- Single-layer, single-scale random CNNs already produce competitive textures; multi-scale random CNNs (concatenating different filter sizes up to 55×55) often rival the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al.
- Unsupervised or handcrafted filters (k-means, PCA, Fourier) do not outperform random filters; the number of feature maps is more critical.
- Nonlinearity (ReLU) and multi-scale receptive fields are essential.
- They introduce a quantitative evaluation using the VGG texture loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, replacing anecdotal visual inspection, and discuss a principled view via a relaxed MaxEnt perspective that accounts for finite-size variability and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample diversity.

Overall, the work provides a strong, simple baseline and clarifies what matters in Gram-based texture synthesis.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual significance: convincingly challenges the assumption that depth or supervised pretraining are necessary for high-quality Gram-based texture synthesis, offering a strong minimal baseline that will influence future work and evaluation practices.
- Empirical support: broad comparisons across filter types, scales, and numbers of feature maps, with careful ablations (nonlinearity, single vs multi-scale), and a quantitative evaluation protocol.

3) Supporting arguments
- Clarity of the main finding: The multi-scale random single-layer model frequently matches or approaches the Gatys VGG model visually and sometimes achieves even lower VGG-loss, highlighting the primacy of the Gram statistics and the optimization setup over feature learning or depth.
- Thoroughness of ablations: The paper isolates key factors: number of channels (363 vs 3267), filter design (random vs trained vs Fourier), scale (single vs multi), and nonlinearity (ReLU vs linear). The conclusion that multi-scale + ReLU + many channels are the necessary ingredients is well motivated and demonstrated.
- Methodological soundness: The synthesis objective follows established Gram-matrix matching, and the authors provide an optimization setup that appears robust (L-BFGS-B with loss scaling and box constraints). The observation that initializing VGG-based synthesis from the shallow model improves results is compelling evidence that optimization, not representational capacity alone, limits the VGG pipeline.
- Broader implications: The discussion connects to the MaxEnt framework and underscores the practical need to allow constraint mismatch for finite-size textures, reframing texture synthesis as balancing perceptual similarity and variability. This conceptual contribution is valuable.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not central to the decision)
- Evaluation bias and perceptual validation: The VGG-based loss is a reasonable proxy but may favor Gram-like statistics and can miss perceptual failures (especially for man-made regular textures). A small-scale human 2AFC or inpainting psychophysics would materially strengthen claims. Alternatively, report LPIPS (with multiple backbones), and a simple nearest-neighbor analysis in the reference patch space to gauge overfitting/memorization.
- Diversity assessment: Include a quantitative diversity measure across multiple seeds (e.g., distribution of VGG-loss to reference and between samples, coverage of color/texture histograms) to substantiate the variability discussion beyond a single illustrative figure.
- Optimization analysis: The large loss/gradient scaling (1e7) and box constraints are likely material to performance; show sensitivity ablations (scaling factor, number of L-BFGS iterations, initialization schemes, Adam vs L-BFGS) and quantify convergence behavior. Demonstrate that lower VGG-loss for some random models reflects better optimization rather than metric quirks.
- Architectural details: Precisely specify the multi-scale layer (filter sizes used, number of filters per scale summing to 1024, channel mixing vs per-channel filters). Report runtime and memory vs VGG-based synthesis, and include throughput comparisons.
- Comparisons: Provide a direct quantitative/visual comparison with scattering-based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat), which also uses fixed multi-scale features. This situates your result among non-learned feature approaches. If feasible, add a feedforward baseline (e.g., TextureNet/Ulyanov) to contextualize the speed-quality trade-off.
- Color handling: Since some Fourier and k-means variants process channels separately vs jointly, report how color coupling affects performance and whether simple color histogram matching as a post-process changes results.
- Reproducibility: Release code and the exact filter initialization seeds; for k-means/PCA (1e7 patches is heavy), share trained filters or detail computational cost to reproduce.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale specification: What exact filter sizes and number of filters per scale were used? Are filters depthwise (per-channel) or full 3×3 RGB? How sensitive are results to the scale set and allocation?
2) Nonlinearity: Did you try leaky-ReLU, absolute value, or squaring (as in energy models) instead of ReLU? How do they affect performance and diversity?
3) Optimization sensitivity: How critical is the 1e7 loss scaling and the [0,1] box constraint? Can you provide convergence curves and success rates across textures and seeds?
4) Diversity: Across, say, 10 seeds per texture, what is the variance of VGG-loss and inter-sample distances? Do samples collapse to near-identical outputs for highly regular textures?
5) Initialization effects on VGG: If you initialize VGG-based synthesis with white noise but run more iterations or different optimizers, can you match the “paradoxically lower” VGG-loss you obtain from the random multi-scale model? In other words, is the gap purely due to suboptimal optimization?
6) Scattering comparison: How does your multi-scale random model compare against fixed wavelet/scattering features with Gram statistics under the same optimization procedure?
7) Exact reconstruction: You mention anecdotal exact reconstructions with deeper random CNNs. Can you provide a characterization of when this occurs (feature count, image size) and a principled estimate of the degrees-of-freedom vs constraints trade-off?

This paper delivers a compelling and useful result: a strong, minimal, single-layer, multi-scale random CNN baseline for Gram-based texture synthesis. With modest expansions in evaluation and clarity, it will be an influential reference.
