- Paper asks which ingredients of modern texture synthesis are truly necessary, finding a single-layer CNN with random filters + Gram matching suffices for high-quality textures.
- Main contribution: a minimal baseline using one conv layer + ReLU, no pooling, and Gram matrix matching of feature covariances.
- Key claim: supervised pretraining and deep hierarchies are not strictly necessary; multi-scale random filters provide most of the benefit.
- Extensive ablation over filter types, counts, scales, and nonlinearities shows random filters are competitive and ReLU is crucial.
- Introduces a quantitative proxy evaluation using VGG-based texture loss instead of purely qualitative judgements.
- Offers a thoughtful connection to MaxEnt reasoning and a discussion of the perceptual trade-off between similarity and variability.
- Decision: Weak accept — strong, surprising negative result and a useful minimal baseline despite evaluation weaknesses.
- Methodology is well-specified (model, optimization, loss) and ablations isolate number of maps, scale, and nonlinearity effectively.
- Situates work well in prior texture literature and notes novel empirical finding that random features outperform some learned filters.
- Qualitative results are convincing, especially for regular long-range patterns; VGG-loss sometimes favors the multi-scale random model.
- Paper is reasonably reproducible with many implementation details provided, though some hyperparameters and multi-scale specifics are missing.
- Main weakness: overreliance on VGG-loss introduces potential bias and lacks human perceptual validation.
- Variability is not rigorously quantified; risk of sample collapse to near-copies is not addressed quantitatively.
- Multi-scale model configuration is under-specified (scales, counts per scale, normalization/weighting).
- Limited quantitative baselines: Portilla & Simoncelli and scattering-based methods are not evaluated under the same metric.
- Efficiency trade-offs are not quantified; computational cost of large filters vs depth is unexplored.
- Suggested improvement: add a small human study (e.g., 2AFC) focusing on cases where VGG-loss and perception diverge.
- Suggested improvement: report variability with multiple samples per texture and quantitative dispersion (e.g., VGG-loss statistics).
- Suggested improvement: precisely specify multi-scale filter sizes, counts, normalization, and Gram aggregation method.
- Suggested improvement: include Portilla & Simoncelli baseline under the same evaluation and ablate filter counts (256–2048).
- Suggested improvement: discuss connection to kernel MMD/moment-matching to explain why random ReLU features work.
- Suggested improvement: release code, learned filters, and seeds for reproducibility.
- Questions for authors: exact multi-scale filter sizes, counts, and Gram aggregation method.
- Questions for authors: motivation for 1e7 loss/gradient scaling, sensitivity to optimization settings and iterations.
- Questions for authors: were filters normalized, were sine/cosine pairs and DC removal used for Fourier filters?
- Questions for authors: do independent seeds produce diverse samples; report inter-sample VGG-loss and sample grids.
- Questions for authors: were other nonlinearities (leaky-ReLU, etc.) tried and is ReLU specifically necessary?
- Questions for authors: how does the method handle non-stationary textures and could spatially varying statistics help?
- Questions for authors: can a shallow multi-layer network with small kernels match multi-scale large-filter performance at lower cost?
