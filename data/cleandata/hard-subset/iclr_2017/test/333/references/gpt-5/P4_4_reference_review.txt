1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper investigates what is minimally required to synthesize natural textures using parametric models based on deep features. It argues that neither depth nor supervised pre-training is essential. Instead, it proposes a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU nonlinearity and random (data-independent) filters, whose stationary summary statistics are Gram matrices of feature activations. The authors:
- Compare a range of single-layer architectures (random, k-means, PCA, Fourier; 11×11 filters; two capacities: 363 and 3267 channels) and a multi-scale random variant with larger receptive fields.
- Show visually and via a VGG-based Gram loss metric that single-layer random models, especially the multi-scale variant, can produce textures that often rival a VGG-19-based multi-layer model (Gatys et al. 2015).
- Provide ablations indicating the importance of ReLU and combining multiple spatial scales.
- Discuss a conceptual generalization of maximum entropy texture models to allow a tolerance around constraints, emphasizing the need to balance perceptual similarity and variability.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Evaluation lacks rigorous, independent quantitative validation; it relies primarily on the VGG-based loss as both the target of comparison and the evaluation metric, with limited perceptual/user studies or statistically grounded analyses.
- Novelty and significance are limited: the core mechanism (Gram matrices over conv feature maps) and the sufficiency of fixed filters for texture synthesis are close to existing lines (e.g., Gatys et al., scattering-based approaches), and the contribution is primarily an empirical baseline with modest theoretical advance.

3. Supporting arguments
- On the specific problem: The paper addresses whether complex, pre-trained, multi-layer CNNs are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis. This framing is clear and well-motivated as a stress-test of assumptions in the literature.
- Placement in literature: The work is positioned against Gatys et al. (2015) and connects to classic parametric models (Portilla-Simoncelli) and scattering networks (Bruna-Mallat). However, the relation to scattering (fixed wavelet filterbanks + second-order statistics) is not deeply analyzed; the result that random multi-scale features with ReLU and Gram statistics work well resonates with existing intuitions about random features and second-order stationarity.
- Technical soundness: The optimization setup (L-BFGS-B with loss scaling, box constraints) is standard and plausible; ablations on nonlinearity and scale are appropriate. However, several claims hinge on qualitative inspection and a VGG-based loss that is not an independent test metric; the authors note cases where their method achieves a lower VGG loss than the VGG model itself, indicating possible metric misalignment or optimization artifacts. The paper acknowledges the variability/perceptual similarity trade-off but does not quantify variability; no statistical assessment across many textures or seeds is provided.
- Evidence for claims: The core claim “random single-layer (multi-scale) suffices” is supported by side-by-side visuals and some ablations. Yet, rigorous support is missing:
  - No psychophysical/user study (e.g., 2AFC) to validate perceptual indistinguishability.
  - No objective diversity/entropy proxy; no multi-sample variability analysis.
  - No comprehensive speed/memory comparison; the multi-scale method is acknowledged as inefficient but not quantitatively contextualized.
  - Limited details on architecture (e.g., exact multi-scale filter sizes/allocations), and scarce quantitative summaries over a larger benchmark of textures.
- Novelty: The concept of using fixed filterbanks and second-order statistics for textures is well established; the main novelty is showing that a single random multi-scale layer can rival VGG-based models when carefully optimized. This is a useful baseline but incremental without stronger analysis or evaluation.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all required for acceptance)
- Evaluation:
  - Add human perceptual evaluations (e.g., 2AFC discrimination, inpainting tests) with appropriate sample sizes and statistics.
  - Report diversity metrics: multi-sample analyses, nearest-neighbor distances, coverage metrics, or classifier-based two-sample tests; at least provide qualitative grids of multiple samples per texture.
  - Include comparisons against scattering-based texture synthesis and Portilla-Simoncelli with both visuals and standardized metrics.
  - Provide timing and memory benchmarks vs. VGG-based synthesis, and vs. hierarchical multi-layer random nets, to ground the efficiency discussion.
- Analysis:
  - Offer a principled explanation for why random multi-scale features + ReLU + Gram statistics suffice: e.g., connections to random feature kernels, the effect of convolutional overlap, and how ReLU lifts higher-order pixel statistics into second-order feature correlations.
  - Expand ablations: number of channels, filter sizes and allocations per scale, stride/padding, ReLU vs. other nonlinearities, and the impact of optimization hyperparameters (including the 1e7 scaling and iteration budgets).
  - Quantify sensitivity to random seeds and provide confidence intervals over many textures/seeds.
- Reproducibility:
  - Precisely specify the multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, counts per scale), preprocessing, and all optimization settings. Release code and pretrained filter definitions.
- Writing and references:
  - Clean reference formatting and ensure proper citations; fix placeholders and missing years/pages.
  - Clarify the dataset and number of textures used in evaluation.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact multi-scale configurations (filter sizes, number of filters per scale), and how sensitive are results to this allocation?
- How many textures were evaluated in total, and how were they selected? Can you provide aggregate metrics over a larger benchmark?
- How robust are results across random seeds (filters and initialization)? Please report mean/variance of your VGG-loss metric and any other metrics across seeds.
- Can you provide user study results (e.g., 2AFC) to validate perceptual indistinguishability and quantify variability?
- How does your method compare quantitatively to scattering-based synthesis under both VGG-based and perceptual evaluations?
- What is the runtime and memory profile vs. VGG-based synthesis and vs. a hierarchical random multi-layer variant with equivalent receptive fields?
- Can you formalize the “epsilon-environment” generalization to MaxEnt and show how varying epsilon trades off similarity vs. variability in practice?
- You note optimization details were crucial; which specific elements (loss scaling, iterations, initialization) most affected success, and why did prior random-weight attempts fail?

Overall, the paper presents a valuable empirical baseline and thought-provoking observations, but stronger, independent evaluation and deeper analysis are needed to meet the bar for acceptance.
