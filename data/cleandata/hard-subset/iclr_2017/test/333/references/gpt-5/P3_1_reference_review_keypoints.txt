- Main claim: A single convolutional layer with random filters + ReLU and Gram-matrix matching can often synthesize textures rivaling multi-layer VGG-based models.
- Contribution: Proposes a strong minimal baseline—single-layer CNNs (including multi-scale filters) optimized with L-BFGS-B to match Gram statistics.
- Experiments: Systematically compares filter types (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means) and channel counts against VGG-based synthesis.
- Metric: Uses VGG Gram loss across layers as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and discusses its limitations.
- Key result: Random multi-scale single-layer models capture small-, mid-, and long-range structures and sometimes achieve lower VGG-loss than VGG-based synthesis.
- Interpretation: Attributes some superior VGG-loss to optimization effects and reduced variability; frames results in MaxEnt and finite-size constraint terms.
- Decision: Reviewer recommends Weak Accept due to the surprising, valuable negative result challenging the necessity of depth and supervised pretraining.
- Motivation & positioning: Well grounded in prior texture and deep synthesis literature (Julesz, Portilla & Simoncelli, Gatys et al.) and timely as a minimal baseline.
- Method clarity: Pipeline is clear—single conv + ReLU, compute Grams, optimize input—emphasizes multi-scale and nonlinearity importance.
- Empirical support: Qualitative comparisons are convincing; ablations show mix of filter sizes is crucial.
- Conceptual insight: Highlights Gram statistics’ generic usefulness for stationary feature spaces and discusses soft constraints/finite-size effects.
- Evaluation concern: Relying primarily on VGG Gram loss risks bias when methods are optimized against that representation.
- Suggestion: Report cross-metric evaluations (other networks, scattering), aggregate statistics over many textures, and consider small-scale human perceptual tests.
- Variability: The paper raises similarity–variability trade-offs but does not quantify variability; recommend diversity metrics across initializations or feature spaces.
- Reproducibility: Request release of code, exact multi-scale architecture details, normalization, random seeds, and full optimization hyperparameters.
- Fairness: Clarify parameter budgets and report performance as a function of number of features to contextualize “minimality.”
- Nonlinearity: ReLU shown necessary; suggest testing other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, ELU, tanh) for robustness.
- Color handling: Ask for analysis of color mixing vs per-channel processing and effect of removing per-channel DC components.
- Computational cost: Ask for concrete timing/memory comparisons and details on hierarchical architectures tried (depth, pooling, dilation) and why they failed to improve quality.
- Theoretical framing: Recommend explicit connection to kernel methods and scattering transforms to situate Gram matching with random features.
- Question—multi-scale details: What exact filter sizes and channel allocations are used, and how sensitive are results to allocation?
- Question—robustness: How robust are results across random seeds and are there failure cases for certain random draws?
- Question—normalization: Do you normalize feature maps before computing Grams, and how do scale differences affect results?
- Question—metrics: Can you report VGG-loss and alternative metrics averaged across a larger texture set with confidence intervals and across other feature spaces (AlexNet, scattering)?
- Question—variability quantification: How many independent samples can you generate before mode collapse, and can you quantify sample variability?
- Question—Fourier filters: How do results change with and without ReLU in Fourier filter experiments and is there a connection to spectral (power spectrum) constraints?
- Question—exact reconstruction: Clarify the “exact reconstruction” anecdote for deeper random CNNs—settings, frequency, and conditions under which it occurs.
