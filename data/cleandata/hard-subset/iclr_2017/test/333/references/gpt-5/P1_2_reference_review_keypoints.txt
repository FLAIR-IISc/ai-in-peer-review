- Paper proposes a minimal baseline: single-layer CNN with random filters + Gram-matrix statistics can synthesize convincing textures, sometimes rivaling VGG-based methods.
- Introduces a multi-scale random convolutional variant and systematically compares filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA) and channel counts.
- Provides ablations on number of features, filter size, multi-scale vs single-scale, and the role of ReLU.
- Uses VGG-based multi-layer loss as a surrogate perceptual metric and discusses the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability, relating to MaxEnt.
- Decision: Reject — primary reasons are limited empirical rigor and incomplete positioning versus related fixed-feature models.
- Main critique: evaluation is largely qualitative and depends on a VGG-biased metric rather than independent perceptual measures or human studies.
- No human perceptual study (e.g., 2AFC) to validate the VGG-loss conclusions.
- Lack of quantitative diversity/variability analysis across generated samples; claims about variability/similarity are unsubstantiated.
- Results may be anecdotal/cherry-picked; no statistical comparisons across a defined texture benchmark to support “rivals SOTA.”
- Missing comparisons to closely related fixed, multi-scale methods (e.g., scattering/wavelet-based models) and Portilla–Simoncelli, which are natural baselines.
- Method and multi-scale random conv idea are sensible and clearly described; ablations support importance of multi-scale and nonlinearity.
- Quality evaluation is biased toward VGG features; authors acknowledge this but need independent metrics or human data.
- No sensitivity analysis for crucial optimization choices (iterations, optimizer, gradient scaling like 1e7) or initialization robustness.
- Multi-scale architecture specifics (scales, channels per scale, normalization, Gram aggregation) are under-specified.
- Reproducibility issues: no code, seeds, full texture list, or detailed hyperparameters provided.
- Some references are incomplete or inconsistently reported.
- Recommended to include scattering and Portilla–Simoncelli baselines with quantitative results.
- Recommended to run a small human perceptual study (N≈10–20, 2AFC) to assess perceptual similarity and diversity.
- Recommended to quantify variability: distributions of VGG-loss, pairwise VGG distances, and independent metrics like LPIPS/DISTS.
- Recommended to report runtime and memory trade-offs for single-layer vs multi-layer/random nets.
- Recommended sensitivity analyses for number of maps, filter sizes per scale, nonlinearities, mean subtraction, and optimizer hyperparameters.
- Clarify whether Gram matrices are computed per-scale or jointly, and whether features/Grams are normalized across scales.
- Questions for authors: exact multi-scale details; robustness to optimizer/init/scaling/box constraints; quantitative diversity metrics; comparison to scattering-based synthesis; plan for human study; normalization strategy; code/data release plans.
