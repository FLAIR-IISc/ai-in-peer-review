1) Summary of claims/contributions (positive and generous)
- The paper asks what is minimally required to synthesize high-quality natural textures using parametric models. It argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are necessary: a single-layer CNN with random filters and ReLU, matched via Gram-matrix statistics, can often rival the VGG-based method of Gatys et al.
- It proposes a strong minimal baseline: single-layer models with various filter banks (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means; 11×11 filters unless otherwise noted), plus a random multi-scale variant with large receptive fields (up to 55×55).
- It uses VGG Gram-loss (averaged across five layers) as a quantitative surrogate for perceptual similarity, instead of relying solely on qualitative human inspection.
- It explores architectural factors (number of feature maps, filter size, multi-scale vs single-scale, presence of ReLU) and argues that multi-scale + nonlinearity are crucial, while training filters on natural images is not.
- It discusses MaxEnt interpretations and the importance of explicitly allowing small mismatches to balance similarity vs variability.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The experimental evaluation and presentation lack rigor and critical details (architecture specification, optimization protocol comparability, exact datasets, reproducibility), and the sole reliance on VGG Gram-loss as a perceptual metric is problematic and internally inconsistent with their own observations about optimization getting stuck.
- Numerous methodological ambiguities and formatting/citation issues undermine confidence in the scientific soundness and reproducibility of the results, despite interesting insights.

3) Supporting arguments
- Evaluation metric and fairness:
  - The paper evaluates perceptual quality almost exclusively with VGG Gram-loss, yet reports that their method sometimes achieves a lower VGG-loss than direct VGG-loss optimization (suggesting optimization issues). Without stronger optimization for the VGG baseline (longer runs, better initialization beyond a brief anecdote, alternative optimizers), the comparison is inconclusive.
  - No human study (2AFC/MOS) or alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS/scattering-based metrics) are provided; SSIM is mentioned only in an appendix figure. Given the central claim (“rivals perceptual quality”), subjective validation is needed.
- Missing or ambiguous experimental details:
  - Multi-scale model: exact filter sizes, number of filters per scale (only “1024 feature maps” total is given), and how features are concatenated are not specified. This makes ablations and reproduction difficult.
  - Filter normalization: Are filters L2-normalized? For k-means/PCA/Fourier/random bases? Are biases always zero? Are feature maps centered before Gram computation? These choices affect Gram statistics.
  - KMeans whitening: What whitening (PCA/ZCA)? On which dataset split? For Kmeans-Sample, filters are learned from the target texture itself—this is not a fair “general-purpose” model comparison and should be clearly separated.
  - Preprocessing: Subtracting ImageNet channel means rather than per-image means may bias color statistics for textures; justify or control.
  - Optimization: Scaling gradients/loss by 1e7 is ad hoc. Why 2000 L-BFGS-B iterations? Were stopping criteria tuned per model? Did you compare Adam/L-BFGS variants? These choices can strongly affect convergence and the fairness of comparisons.
  - VGG baseline: You switch to average pooling (not original max-pooling) without justification or analysis of how this affects the baseline and the quantitative metric used to evaluate all models.
- Confounds and incomplete ablations:
  - Number of feature maps varies widely (363 vs 3267 vs 1024), conflating gains from multi-scale with capacity. While a single-scale 1024-filter ablation is shown qualitatively, quantitative results across sizes and counts are missing.
  - Runtime/efficiency claims lack measurements (FLOPs, wall-clock, memory). Claims of hierarchical nets being faster but not better are unsupported by data.
  - Robustness: No variability analysis across random seeds, textures, or initialization is reported. The paper itself notes a tendency to overfit (exact reconstructions) in deeper random CNNs; this warrants quantitative analysis of variability vs similarity.
- Theoretical framing:
  - The MaxEnt discussion and epsilon-environment idea are interesting but remain purely conceptual; no concrete formulation or experiments demonstrate how this improves the trade-off between similarity and variability.
- Presentation/formatting issues:
  - References contain numerous placeholders and formatting errors (“? \Q2016\E”), missing years (e.g., Glorot & Bengio), and inconsistent citation styles. Figures are referenced but not available here; captions are minimal. The VGG code footnote is awkwardly embedded in text.
  - Notation inconsistencies: d(x,y) in Eq. (2) uses symmetric normalization; E(y) in Eq. (5) uses only ||G(x)|| normalization. This discrepancy is not explained.
  - Some models (e.g., Fourier-3267 weighting in {−1,0,1}) seem arbitrary; rationale and analysis are missing.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Provide full architectural specifications for all models, especially the multi-scale one: filter sizes, counts per scale, normalization, and exact Gram computation details.
- Strengthen baselines and evaluation:
  - Use the standard Gatys setup (including pooling choice) as a baseline; match optimization budgets; report multiple optimizers and initializations; include your “initialization with shallow outputs” baseline quantitatively.
  - Add a small 2AFC user study (10–20 subjects, 20 textures) to validate perceptual claims. Include LPIPS and scattering-based distances as additional metrics.
  - Report variability: For each texture, generate multiple samples, quantify within-texture dispersion in VGG/LPIPS space, and show trade-offs by early stopping or entropy proxies.
- Ablate capacity and scale systematically: sweep number of filters (e.g., 256–4096), single vs multi-scale, filter sizes, and ReLU vs linear across the same budget; report quantitative scores and runtimes.
- Provide runtime and memory measurements on a standard GPU/CPU for all methods.
- Clarify the theoretical part: formalize the epsilon-environment idea (choice of metric, thresholds) and show its effect in practice (e.g., early stopping thresholds that control variability).
- Clean up references, notation, and typos. Ensure equations are consistent (normalizations), and justify average-pooling choice for VGG.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale details: What are the exact filter sizes used, and how many filters per scale sum to 1024? Are filters L2-normalized per scale?
2) Gram computation: Are feature maps centered or normalized before forming Gram matrices? Does removing the DC component per channel affect filters like Fourier with a DC basis?
3) Optimization: Why scale gradients by 1e7? How sensitive are results to iteration count and optimizer choice? Did you try Adam or different L-BFGS-B settings for the VGG baseline?
4) VGG setup: Which exact layers contribute to the five Gram-loss terms? Why use average pooling instead of the original max pooling? Does this change your conclusions?
5) Fairness: For KMeans-Sample (filters learned from the target), do you consider this a generalizable model? Please separate its results from general-purpose models.
6) Variability: How variable are multiple samples from the same texture? Can you report dispersion metrics and show that your method does not collapse to near-copies?
7) Efficiency: Can you provide runtime/memory benchmarks for single-scale, multi-scale, and hierarchical variants?
8) Reproducibility: Will you release code and exact seeds/configurations to reproduce all figures?
