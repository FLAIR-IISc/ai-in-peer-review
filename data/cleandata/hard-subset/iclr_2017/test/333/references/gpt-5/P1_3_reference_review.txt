Summary and contributions
The paper asks: what minimal ingredients are needed to synthesize convincing natural textures? The authors argue that neither deep hierarchies nor supervisedly trained features are indispensable. They introduce a strong, simple baseline: a single convolutional layer with ReLU and a Gram-matrix statistic over feature maps, using random filters (and a multi-scale variant). They show qualitatively and via a VGG-based texture loss that these models often rival the VGG multi-layer Gram model of Gatys et al. They systematically compare different single-layer filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA-learned, per-channel vs mixed-channel, different numbers of maps), study the effect of filter size and multi-scale combinations, and emphasize the importance of nonlinearity. They also discuss the balance between perceptual similarity and variability, and relate practical synthesis to maximum-entropy ideas for finite images. Overall, the paper promotes a minimal, well-tuned baseline and provides insights into which components really matter.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Insufficient quantitative, unbiased evaluation to support “rivals state-of-the-art” claims; heavy reliance on a VGG-based loss as both a metric and a competing objective risks bias and does not measure variability or human perceptual quality.
- Limited novelty beyond demonstrating that random, single-layer (multi-scale) conv features with Gram statistics can work if optimization is tuned; the method is close in spirit to Gatys et al. and prior fixed-filter approaches, with modest theoretical or algorithmic advances.

Supporting arguments
- Evidence and metrics: The principal quantitative metric is the multi-layer VGG Gram loss, which is known to correlate with perceptual judgments but is imperfect and can paradoxically favor models that overfit to its statistics. The paper acknowledges this and shows qualitative wins, but does not provide human studies (e.g., 2AFC, inpainting tests) or diversity analyses. Claims that the method “often rivals” VGG are thus not rigorously substantiated.
- Novelty and positioning: The use of Gram matrices for texture synthesis is established. The new insight is that random, single-layer features (especially multi-scale) suffice with good optimization. This is interesting and useful as a baseline, but overlaps with existing fixed-filter paradigms (e.g., scattering transform-based synthesis) and with observations that many random feature models work well given enough features. A direct comparison to scattering or other fixed-basis approaches is missing.
- Scientific rigor: The study explores many filter banks and presents ablations (filters per scale, ReLU necessity). However, important factors are not systematically quantified: number of features vs quality trade-off, computational cost vs quality, robustness to initialization/optimization hyperparameters (the large loss/gradient scaling is critical but not analyzed), and sensitivity to image size/content.
- Variability vs similarity: The discussion correctly raises the need to balance perceptual similarity with sample diversity, noting that the proposed approach may collapse variability. Yet the paper does not measure diversity (e.g., inter-sample distances in VGG space, patch-level FID-like scores, or psychophysics), leaving a core generative criterion unassessed.
- Clarity and completeness: The paper is generally clear, but some architectural and experimental details are under-specified (exact multi-scale configuration, number of maps per scale, texture set, image sizes, runtime/memory), and several references are incomplete/malformed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add unbiased perceptual evaluation: a small-scale user study (2AFC real vs synthesized, or inpainting discrimination) would substantially strengthen claims. Alternatively, report quantitative psychophysical proxies or multiple metrics (e.g., LPIPS, DISTS), and analyze consistency across metrics.
- Measure diversity: for each texture, synthesize multiple samples and report inter-sample VGG-loss distances and coverage of patch statistics; visualize variability trade-offs by stopping criteria or explicit noise injection.
- Comparisons: include baselines with fixed wavelet/scattering networks (Bruna & Mallat) and spectrum-constrained methods (e.g., Liu et al. 2016), to contextualize random features vs principled fixed features.
- Ablations and scaling laws: quantify performance vs number of feature maps and vs largest receptive field; show curves instead of a small set of points. Analyze the effect of ReLU vs other activations (leaky ReLU, modulus), pooling, and color handling.
- Optimization analysis: report sensitivity to optimizer choice/parameters (e.g., Adam vs L-BFGS-B), the effect of the 1e7 scaling, and convergence/stability. Provide run times and memory needs for single-scale vs multi-scale vs hierarchical variants.
- Clarify multi-scale design: detail filter sizes used, allocation of maps per scale, and how features are concatenated and normalized. Consider normalizing Gram matrices per layer/scale to avoid domination by large-receptive-field features.
- Reproducibility: release code and texture set; fix reference formatting.
- Theory: the finite-size MaxEnt discussion is interesting; making it concrete (e.g., by specifying and testing an epsilon-tolerance scheme and showing how epsilon controls diversity/similarity) would enhance the contribution.

Questions for the authors
- What exact multi-scale configuration did you use (filter sizes, number of maps per scale, and how were features normalized across scales)?
- How sensitive are results to the gradient/loss scaling and to L-BFGS-B vs other optimizers? Do you observe mode collapse or exact reconstructions as you increase the number of features?
- How many textures were used in the evaluation, and what is their size/content diversity? Can you report quantitative results averaged over the set (with variance)?
- Can you provide inter-sample diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss distances) for multiple synthesized samples per texture, and compare to VGG-based synthesis?
- How do your random-feature models compare to scattering transform-based texture synthesis and to methods with explicit spectrum constraints?
- What is the computational cost (time/memory) per synthesis for single-scale, multi-scale, and hierarchical variants at common resolutions?
- Did you try alternative nonlinearities (e.g., absolute value/modulus, leaky ReLU) or pooling strategies, and how do they affect quality?
- How does performance scale with image size and with the number of feature maps? Is there an identifiable regime where random single-layer fails (e.g., strongly non-stationary or highly structured man-made patterns)?
