Summary and contributions
The paper asks what is minimally required to synthesize natural textures. It argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are necessary: a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching can yield texture samples that qualitatively rival Gatys et al. (2015). The authors:
- Propose several single-layer CNN variants (random, k-means, PCA, Fourier; 11×11 filters; 363 vs. 3267 feature maps), and a “multi-scale” single-layer variant with larger filters (up to 55×55).
- Use VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, in lieu of human inspection.
- Empirically compare the single-layer models against Gatys et al., and discuss the role of filter size, multi-scale composition, and nonlinearity.
- Provide a conceptual discussion linking practical synthesis to a generalized MaxEnt viewpoint, arguing for an explicit “epsilon-ball” around constraints to capture variability.

Decision: Reject (primary reasons: insufficient experimental rigor/reproducibility and over-claiming with incomplete methodological detail)
While the idea is interesting and potentially impactful as a minimal baseline, the work lacks essential details, ablations, and robust evaluation to support its strongest claims. Presentation/formatting issues are also pervasive.

Supporting arguments
- Insufficient quantitative evaluation and potential circularity. The sole quantitative metric is the VGG-based Gram loss (Eq. 6–7), which is very close to the optimization objective of the SOTA baseline it is used to evaluate. This risks circularity and does not convincingly demonstrate perceptual superiority or equivalence. Claims of surpassing Portilla & Simoncelli are not substantiated quantitatively nor with a controlled human study. Appendix references to SSIM are not integrated into a systematic analysis. No user study (even small-scale) or standardized perceptual metric (LPIPS, FID on texture distributions, etc.) is provided. Variability/diversity is discussed conceptually but not measured.
- Missing critical implementation details preclude reproducibility. The “multi-scale” model is not precisely specified (filter sizes per scale, number of filters per scale, how padding/normalization is handled across scales, exact initialization distributions). For the k-means/PCA models: “1e7 patches” is claimed, but whitening, clustering details, convergence criteria, compute resources, and patch source statistics are underspecified. Random seeds, image preprocessing, color space handling, and exact VGG layers used for evaluation are not fully enumerated. No code link is provided.
- Optimization sensitivities are under-explored. The paper suggests the key difference from prior negative results with random features (Gatys et al.) is “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” (Sec. 1), but the only concrete change is loss/gradient scaling by 1e7 and max iterations (2000). No ablation is given to show that this scaling, ReLU, box constraints, or initialization materially change outcomes. The claim that VGG-based synthesis improves when initialized with single-layer results (Appendix) is interesting, but quantitative evidence is missing.
- Over-claiming relative to evidence. Statements such as “rivals the current state-of-the-art,” “depth and pre-training are not indispensable,” and “surpasses Portilla & Simoncelli” are not convincingly supported across a wide texture set with robust metrics or user studies. The experiments appear limited to a small number of curated textures, with figures doing most of the work.
- Methodological ambiguities and likely errors. Equations 2–3 include normalizations that appear inconsistent (e.g., an extra factor N in Eq. 3; layer-wise N not defined in Eq. 6). The use of “average- instead of max-pooling1” has an orphaned footnote marker. The per-layer Gram normalization differs from Gatys et al. in unclear ways; implications on the evaluation should be discussed. The “multi-scale single-layer” architecture is not formalized.
- Computational claims not substantiated. The paper claims single-layer large filters are inefficient and that hierarchical nets are faster “without improving quality,” but no timing/memory benchmarks, architectural specs, or quantitative comparisons are reported.
- Literature positioning is incomplete. There is limited discussion contrasting to scattering-based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat), spectrum-constrained CNN methods (Liu et al.), or kernel/MMD perspectives that could explain why random features plus Gram statistics work when sufficiently wide. The “Variability” discussion is thoughtful but remains speculative.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Provide a complete, precise specification of the multi-scale model: list all filter sizes, counts per scale, initialization distributions, padding, normalization, and feature concatenation order. Include ablations: single-scale vs. multi-scale with equal parameter counts; effect of ReLU vs. linear; effect of loss scaling; different optimizers (Adam vs. L-BFGS-B) and iteration budgets.
- Expand quantitative evaluation. Add a small user study or two-alternative forced-choice on a standard texture set. Report LPIPS to reference images and between independent samples to probe the similarity–variability trade-off. Consider diversity metrics (e.g., pairwise Gram distances between independent samples) and show mode collapse or lack thereof. If claiming superiority to Portilla & Simoncelli, include that baseline with the same textures and metrics.
- Clarify the evaluation protocol with VGG: exact layers used; whether the preprocessing matches VGG’s training; whether average pooling was used at test for both methods; sensitivity to layer subset and normalization.
- Provide runtime and memory comparisons across methods and textures. Report number of parameters and FLOPs.
- Clean up equations, notation, and references. Fix Eq. 2–3 normalization, define all symbols, and ensure consistency. Remove orphaned footnotes, fix broken citations, and follow ICLR style.
- Discuss the relationship to kernel methods: Gram on random features approximates second-order statistics in an RKHS; this can contextualize why width matters and why multi-scale helps.
- Release code and trained filters (where applicable). If k-means/PCA used 1e7 patches, report cluster inertia, convergence criteria, and compute details.

Questions for the authors
1. Precisely define the multi-scale architecture: which filter sizes, how many filters per size (total 1024), initialization distributions, and padding? Are feature maps normalized before Gram computation?
2. What exact VGG layers are used for E(y)? Are weights per layer equal? Did you try alternative layer subsets and does your ranking remain?
3. Can you quantitatively show the effect of the 1e7 loss scaling, ReLU, and box constraints on synthesis quality and convergence?
4. How many textures are in your evaluation set? Please provide aggregate metrics (mean/std of VGG-loss, LPIPS if possible) across the set rather than per-image anecdotes.
5. How is variability measured? Can you report pairwise distances among multiple samples per texture for each method?
6. Provide runtimes and GPU/CPU specs for 2000 iterations for 11×11 vs. 55×55 filters, and compare to a shallow hierarchical alternative you mention.
7. Can you include Portilla & Simoncelli, scattering-based, and spectrum-constrained baselines with the same evaluation protocol?
8. Please correct Eq. 2–3 and clarify normalization; currently the factors appear inconsistent.
