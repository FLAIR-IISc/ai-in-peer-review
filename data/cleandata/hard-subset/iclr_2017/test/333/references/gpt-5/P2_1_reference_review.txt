Summary and contributions
- The paper asks what is minimally required to synthesize convincing natural textures and argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is essential. It proposes a single-layer CNN texture model using Gram matrices of feature activations, with random filters and ReLUs, and shows that a multi-scale version (multiple filter sizes in one layer) can often rival the VGG-based multi-layer approach of Gatys et al.
- It systematically compares a range of single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier basis, k-means/PCA-learned) and channel couplings, and with small vs large numbers of feature maps.
- It emphasizes two key findings: (i) multiscale filters and ReLU nonlinearity are crucial; (ii) random filters perform on par with or better than learned/hand-crafted banks, given enough channels.
- It proposes using a VGG-based texture loss (average Gram-matrix reconstruction error across several VGG layers) as a quantitative metric for texture quality to complement qualitative comparisons, and discusses the MaxEnt perspective and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The work provides a strong, surprising, and practically useful baseline that challenges a prevailing assumption (necessity of depth and supervised features) with clear ablations and broadly convincing empirical evidence.
- The insights (role of multiscale + nonlinearity; sufficiency of random features with Gram statistics; optimization details matter) are likely to influence future texture synthesis research and evaluation practice.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: Well motivated: stripping down the Gatys model to identify indispensable components is valuable. The work is well placed in the literature (Julesz, Portilla–Simoncelli, Gatys, scattering), and the connections to MaxEnt are thoughtful.
- Methodological clarity: The model and optimization are described precisely (single conv layer, ReLU, Gram stats, L-BFGS-B, normalization/scaling). The set of compared filter banks is diverse, allowing a fair test of the “random vs learned” claim. The multiscale vs single-scale ablation cleanly identifies why multiscale helps: it captures small-, mid-, and long-range statistics.
- Empirical support: The paper evaluates many textures and model variants, and the qualitative results (as described) align with the claims: more channels help; random ≈ learned; multiscale clearly improves; ReLU is necessary. The observation that initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer results improves VGG outcomes is also informative about optimization vs representation.
- Limitations: The primary quantitative metric is the VGG-based loss, which is not entirely independent of the baseline being compared against and may not correlate with human perception for highly regular man-made textures (the authors note this). There is no user study or diversity/entropy measure, and limited aggregate statistics (e.g., means/variances across many textures). Compute considerations for very large spatial filters are discussed but not rigorously benchmarked.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Evaluation:
  - Add aggregate quantitative results across the full texture set: report mean/median VGG-loss (and variability) per model variant, with confidence intervals, to support “often rivals” claims beyond selected examples.
  - Include a small-scale psychophysical evaluation (e.g., 2AFC real-vs-synth or inpainting discrimination) to validate the perceptual similarity claim, especially for the cases where VGG-loss disagrees with perception.
  - Report diversity across multiple random seeds per texture (e.g., VGG-loss distribution and visual montage) to document the similarity–variability trade-off.
  - Consider complementary perceptual metrics beyond SSIM and pixel L2 (even simple feature-space distances from other networks), acknowledging their limitations.

- Reproducibility and details:
  - Release code and exact configurations (filter shapes per scale, number of filters per scale in the multiscale model, padding type, channel normalization, Gram normalization constants, random seeds).
  - Provide runtime and memory usage comparisons for single-scale, multiscale, and deeper hierarchical implementations; detail the speed/quality trade-off.
  - Clarify whether filters were normalized (per-filter energy) and whether any per-feature-map normalization was used before Gram computation.
  - Expand ablations: number of channels vs quality; alternative nonlinearities (abs, leaky ReLU), color spaces, strides/pooling; effect of DC removal statistics.
  - Quantify the optimization sensitivity: iterations vs loss, effect of the 1e7 scaling on convergence, and whether early stopping meaningfully increases variability at acceptable quality.

- Analysis and positioning:
  - Tighten the discussion on why random multiscale filters + Gram suffice: e.g., arguments about overcomplete random features and overlapping receptive fields inducing strong constraints (links to random feature theory or JL-like intuitions).
  - Clarify the “paradox” where multiscale random sometimes beats VGG on the VGG loss: provide evidence of local minima in VGG optimization (loss curves, multiple restarts) to support the claim.
  - Revisit the “minimal” claim: while the architecture is simple, the parameter count and receptive fields can be large. Frame the contribution as “structurally simple and training-free” rather than strictly minimal in size.

Questions for the authors
- Multiscale architecture: What exact filter sizes and counts per scale were used? How sensitive is performance to this allocation?
- Filter normalization: Were filters normalized (e.g., unit L2) before use? Was any activation normalization (e.g., per-channel variance) applied before Gram computation?
- Nonlinearity: Did you test absolute value or other nonlinearities? Is ReLU’s sparsity the key factor?
- Optimization: How robust are results to the L-BFGS-B settings and to the 1e7 scaling? Could Adam/SGD with appropriate schedules match performance?
- Variability: How diverse are samples across seeds for a fixed texture at matched VGG-loss? Can you quantify or visualize this distribution?
- Generalization: How does the method handle non-stationary textures or images with global structure (beyond the stationary assumption)? Any failure modes to highlight?
- Efficiency: Can a shallow hierarchical multiscale (e.g., dilated convs) retain quality while reducing the cost of very large kernels?
- Metric dependence: If you evaluate with a different perceptual proxy (e.g., a different pretrained network or a scattering-based metric), do the relative conclusions (random ≥ learned; multiscale > single-scale) hold?

Overall, despite evaluation limitations, the paper makes a clear, well-argued empirical point, offers a strong training-free baseline, and raises useful conceptual questions about constraints, metrics, and optimization in texture synthesis.
