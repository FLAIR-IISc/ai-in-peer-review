1) Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear, important question: what are the minimal ingredients needed to synthesize convincing natural textures? Contrary to the prevailing view that depth and supervised pretraining are critical (as in Gatys et al.), the authors show that a single convolutional layer with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching can synthesize textures that often rival the VGG-based method. Key contributions:
- A strong minimalist baseline: single-layer CNNs with random, data-independent filters, optimized by L-BFGS-B to match Gram statistics.
- Systematic exploration across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), numbers of channels (363 vs 3267), and scales; identification of two crucial ingredients: nonlinearity and multi-scale filters.
- A quantitative evaluation proxy using the VGG-based texture loss and discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, relating practical texture synthesis to finite-sample MaxEnt considerations.

The ablations are carefully chosen, and the finding that random multi-scale features plus Gram matching suffice is both surprising and practically relevant as a new baseline for texture synthesis.

2) Decision: Weak accept
Primary reasons:
- Conceptual significance and clarity: the result that depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis with Gram statistics is a valuable and timely insight, supported by comprehensive ablations.
- A practically useful, strong baseline that the community can adopt; the paper also clarifies the role of nonlinearity and multi-scale receptive fields.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated among classic parametric models (Portilla & Simoncelli), scattering-based methods (Bruna & Mallat), and DNN-based Gram methods (Gatys et al.), and explicitly challenges the claimed necessity of depth/training. While the related work is cited, an empirical comparison to scattering would strengthen the context (see below).
- Methodological soundness: The formulation mirrors Gatys et al. but replaces learned features with random filters while maintaining Gram matching. The paper explores multiple filter banks (Fourier, PCA, k-means, random) and provides insightful ablations:
  - More channels generally improve quality.
  - Multi-scale random filters outperform single-scale filters with equivalent parameter counts, supporting the claim that combining scales, not simply large receptive fields, is key.
  - ReLUs are necessary; removing them breaks performance.
- Results and claims: Qualitative results are compelling across a diverse set of textures, especially for regular, large-scale structures, where the multi-scale random model sometimes even achieves lower VGG loss than the VGG model. The paper candidly discusses optimization issues (e.g., scaling the loss to avoid early stopping, and that initializing VGG synthesis with random-model outputs improves results). The theoretical discussion on finite-size effects and the need to allow small constraint mismatches is thoughtful and connects to MaxEnt.

4) Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Evaluation rigor and metrics:
  - The VGG-based loss is a reasonable proxy but not ground truth for perceptual similarity; it may bias evaluation toward VGG-like statistics and obscure differences in variability. Consider adding:
    - Human 2AFC or inpainting-based psychophysical evaluation (the paper sketches such a procedure).
    - Alternative perceptual metrics (e.g., LPIPS) and possibly scattering-distance as a non-VGG baseline.
    - Aggregate statistics (mean/variance of loss across many textures, not only per-image numbers).
  - Quantify variability explicitly (e.g., pairwise distances among multiple samples per texture under several metrics). This would substantiate the “similarity vs variability” trade-off.
- Comparisons:
  - Include a direct empirical comparison to a scattering-based texture synthesis method (Bruna & Mallat), which is philosophically closest to “fixed filters + moments” and an appropriate non-VGG baseline.
  - More systematic comparison to Portilla & Simoncelli (beyond a few visuals), possibly with the same evaluation metrics.
- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Detail the multi-scale architecture: exact filter sizes used, number of filters per scale, initialization ranges, padding, and how 1024 channels are allocated.
  - Report sensitivity to random seeds and the number of channels: how many random features are “enough” to reach a given VGG-loss threshold?
  - Provide runtime and memory costs for each model (single-scale vs multi-scale; large filters) and the potential speed gain from hierarchical multi-layer equivalents.
  - Clarify preprocessing (global Imagenet mean subtraction vs per-image mean for textures) and whether grayscale textures were tested.
  - Release code and configuration files to ensure reproducibility (including the 1e7 patch sampling for k-means/PCA).
- Analysis and discussion:
  - Offer intuition for why random features outperform k-means/PCA/Fourier in this Gram-matching regime. Is it coverage of diverse orientations/frequencies, or reduced bias that helps Gram constraints be more expressive?
  - Explore other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, absolute value, squaring) and their effect on Gram statistics and synthesis.
  - Investigate boundary conditions and tiling artifacts; discuss stationarity assumptions and limitations on non-stationary textures.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: Which exact filter sizes and channel counts per scale were used? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- Randomness and stability: Across seeds, how variable are the results? Is there a minimum number of feature maps required to consistently match VGG-loss thresholds across textures?
- Why do random filters beat k-means/PCA/Fourier in your setting? Any spectral or coverage analysis of the learned bases vs random?
- Optimization details: How crucial is the 1e7 gradient scaling? Did you try alternative optimizers (Adam/L-BFGS with different tolerances) and iteration budgets?
- Variability: Can you provide quantitative evidence (pairwise distances among multiple samples) that your method trades off similarity vs variability differently from VGG?
- Scattering baseline: Have you attempted a comparison to scattering-moment-based synthesis? If so, how does it fare on your VGG-loss and visually?
- Generalization: Does the approach handle near-regular but nonstationary textures (e.g., perspective foreshortening), or does it fail gracefully?

Overall, the paper delivers a clean, insightful take-home message with careful ablations. Strengthening the evaluation and adding a scattering baseline would make the case more compelling, but the core contribution is valuable to the community.
