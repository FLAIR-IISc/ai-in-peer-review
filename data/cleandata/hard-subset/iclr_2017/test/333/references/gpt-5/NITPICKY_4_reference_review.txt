1) Summary and contributions
The paper asks: what is actually necessary to synthesize convincing natural textures? It argues that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are essential; instead, a single convolutional layer with random filters, coupled with Gram-matrix matching and L-BFGS-B optimization, can produce textures that rival a VGG-based model. Contributions claimed:
- A minimal baseline: single-layer CNNs (and a multi-scale single-layer variant) with random, hand-crafted (Fourier), or unsupervised (k-means/PCA) filters, using Gram statistics.
- An empirical finding that random filters often match or surpass unsupervised or Fourier filters, and that multi-scale random filters are particularly strong.
- A VGG-based “perceptual” loss used as a quantitative proxy to evaluate synthesized textures.
- Discussion on the role of non-linearities, receptive-field size vs combining scales, and a perspective relating practical synthesis to a relaxed MaxEnt framework.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Evaluation is insufficiently rigorous: no human study, heavy reliance on a surrogate VGG-based metric (and qualitative figures), limited and underspecified dataset, and missing comparisons to closely-related fixed-filter baselines (e.g., scattering-based texture synthesis) undermine the central claims.
- Methodological and presentation issues (many missing details needed for reproducibility, unclear multi-scale architecture specification, incomplete ablations, and pervasive formatting/citation problems) detract from scientific clarity.

3) Supporting arguments
- On novelty and placement: The core idea (Gram statistics with fixed filters) is close to Portilla–Simoncelli and scattering-based models; several cited works (Bruna & Mallat, Portilla & Simoncelli) point to the viability of fixed transforms. Showing that random filters can work is interesting, but the paper does not convincingly isolate when and why they work beyond heuristic ablations. A direct and fair comparison to scattering-based synthesis is notably absent.
- Metric choice and validation: Using the VGG-based loss as both the evaluation metric and the target for the VGG competitor raises concerns; the paper itself notes paradoxes where the random multi-scale method achieves lower VGG-loss than VGG-optimized results, suggesting optimization artifacts or metric misalignment. Without a human study (e.g., 2AFC discrimination or inpainting-based tests proposed by the authors themselves), the claim that random single-layer models “often rival SOTA” is not substantiated.
- Experimental detail gaps:
  - The multi-scale architecture is under-specified (exact filter sizes used, allocation of the 1024 feature maps per scale, normalization per scale, initialization ranges).
  - Optimization details are crucial yet thin: the 10^7 scaling to avoid L-BFGS-B early stopping is ad hoc; no sensitivity analysis is given; fairness of identical optimization settings across methods is not clearly demonstrated.
  - Dataset is unclear: number and sources of textures, selection criteria, image sizes, pre-processing. The statement “20 textures” appears but with no list or protocol.
  - Reproducibility: no code link; randomness control/seeds unspecified; whitening method unspecified (ZCA/PCA?), patch sampling for Kmeans-Sample undefined.
- Comparisons and ablations: While there is an informative single-scale vs multi-scale and ReLU/no-ReLU ablation, other key axes are limited or qualitative: number of feature maps beyond two settings, effect of filter sizes and distributions across scales, comparison to spectrum-constrained methods (e.g., Liu et al., 2016) and to scattering models are absent; Portilla–Simoncelli comparison is asserted but not quantitatively demonstrated.
- Claims vs scope: The paper occasionally generalizes to “natural image generation,” while only addressing static texture synthesis. The efficiency discussion is anecdotal; no wall-clock/memory comparisons are provided.
- Presentation and formatting: Numerous citation formatting errors, typos, truncated references, and footnote/URL formatting issues harm clarity. Some definitions/notations are imprecise (e.g., normalization constants, dimensions, and variable names in equations).

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decisive)
- Provide a thorough, reproducible experimental section: list of textures, sizes, preprocessing, seeds; full architectural specification of the multi-scale model; exact optimization settings; release code.
- Add human perceptual evaluation (2AFC, discrimination, or the inpainting-based psychophysics you propose) to validate the VGG-loss proxy and establish “rival SOTA” claims.
- Include direct comparisons to scattering-based texture synthesis and to Portilla–Simoncelli with strong implementations; add spectrum-constrained baselines.
- Expand ablations: vary number of feature maps, per-scale allocation, filter-size distributions, normalization schemes, and quantify the effect of ReLU and mean subtraction. Report runtime/memory for fairness.
- Analyze why random filters work: e.g., show diversity/condition numbers of the Gram features, correlation coverage vs number of maps, and sensitivity to filter distribution. Consider simple linearizations to relate to classical filter banks.
- Clarify the discrepancy with prior negative results for random nets by explicitly re-running those settings and isolating which optimization tweaks matter (scaling, iterations, initialization, constraints).
- Temper claims to texture synthesis; avoid broad statements about “natural image generation.”
- Clean up references, typos, and formatting.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely specify the multi-scale model: list all filter sizes, counts per size, initialization ranges, and per-scale normalization. How are 1024 maps distributed?
- What textures, sizes, and pre/post-processing were used? Please provide the full list and a standardized evaluation protocol.
- How sensitive are results to the 10^7 loss/gradient scaling, iteration budget, and initialization? Did you retune VGG-based synthesis with the same scaling?
- Can you provide human evaluation results to validate the VGG-loss proxy?
- How does your method compare quantitatively to scattering-based synthesis and to spectrum-constrained methods on the same textures?
- What is the variability across different random seeds? Do some seeds fail?
- Can you report runtime/memory vs VGG-based synthesis and vs a shallow hierarchical multi-layer variant?
- What whitening method was used for k-means? How many patches for Kmeans-Sample? How are color channels normalized?
