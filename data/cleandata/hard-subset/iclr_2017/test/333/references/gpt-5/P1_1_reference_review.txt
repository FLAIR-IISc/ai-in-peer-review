Summary and contributions
This paper asks what is truly necessary to synthesize natural textures using parametric models based on CNN features. The key claim is that neither depth nor supervised pretraining are essential: a single convolutional layer with random filters, when coupled with Gram-matrix summary statistics and an appropriate optimization procedure, can synthesize textures that often rival the VGG-based multi-layer model of Gatys et al. The paper:
- Proposes a strong minimal baseline: single-layer CNNs (with ReLU), including a multi-scale variant with random filters, optimized to match Gram statistics.
- Systematically compares a broad set of single-layer feature banks (random, Fourier basis, PCA, k-means learned, with 363 vs 3267 maps and different filter sizes) and shows that increasing the number of feature maps and combining scales is more important than training features or network depth.
- Employs a quantitative evaluation via VGG-based Gram-loss (used as a proxy for perceptual similarity), in addition to visual comparisons on 20 textures.
- Discusses the balance between perceptual similarity and variability, limitations of MaxEnt modeling under finite image sizes, and argues for defining texture models via a distance metric and an ε-neighborhood of acceptable matches.

Decision: Accept (borderline)
Key reasons: (1) The work delivers a surprising and important baseline and insight—that random, single-layer, multi-scale models with Gram constraints can be highly competitive—challenging prevailing assumptions about the necessity of depth and pretraining. (2) The empirical study is broad and careful for this problem, with clear ablations (number of maps, scale, nonlinearity), and the conceptual discussion connecting practice to MaxEnt is thoughtful and valuable.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: While the Gram-based objective follows Gatys et al., demonstrating that random features in a single layer suffice—and in a multi-scale design can rival VGG—meaningfully advances understanding. This establishes a strong minimal baseline that future texture models should compare against.
- Experimental design: The paper evaluates multiple feature constructions (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means, with and without whitening, and per-texture sampling) and two capacities (363 vs 3267 maps), and shows that random filters are on par or better than learned feature banks. Ablations convincingly show ReLU is critical and that combining scales is essential (small filters capture color/statistics; large filters capture long-range structure; multi-scale captures both).
- Quantitative evaluation: Using VGG Gram-loss as a proxy gives a consistent—even if imperfect—quantitative measure, improving on pure visual inspection. The paper is frank about cases where this proxy fails (regular man-made textures) and discusses paradoxes (lower VGG loss for non-VGG optimization).
- Practical insights: Improved optimization details (L-BFGS-B with box constraints and loss scaling) help reconcile prior negative results with random networks. The observation that initializing VGG synthesis with single-layer results improves outcomes is useful.
- Conceptual clarity: The discussion on perceptual similarity vs variability and the limitations of MaxEnt under finite-size constraints is insightful and frames open problems well.

Limitations and concerns (not blocking)
- Evaluation proxy: Heavy reliance on VGG Gram-loss risks circularity and does not guarantee human perceptual equivalence. No psychophysical user study or alternative perceptual metrics are provided. The authors acknowledge this and outline a promising inpainting-based test, but evidence is deferred.
- Scope and reproducibility: While the texture set (20 images) is reasonable, a more systematic quantitative aggregation (e.g., averages, variance across seeds) would strengthen claims. Details of the multi-scale architecture (exact filter sizes/counts per scale, normalization) and optimization sensitivity (e.g., loss scaling, iteration counts) are limited. Code release is not clearly indicated.
- Efficiency: The single-layer multi-scale model with very large filters is computationally heavy. While the authors note that deeper architectures can replicate receptive field sizes more efficiently and that they do not improve quality, runtime/memory benchmarks are not presented.
- Theoretical grounding: The paper motivates but does not theoretically analyze why random features plus Gram suffice or how many features are needed for a given texture class.

Suggestions to improve (for the authors; not prerequisites for acceptance)
- Add a concise quantitative summary across textures: average VGG-loss ± std, and sensitivity to random seeds. Include a small set of failure cases.
- Provide full multi-scale design details (filter sizes, counts per scale, normalization of filters, treatment of DC components, per-channel handling) and report runtime/memory comparisons vs VGG synthesis.
- Include sensitivity analyses: effect of loss scaling, iteration count, initialization, and alternative nonlinearities (e.g., leaky ReLU), and normalization choices for Gram matrices.
- Augment evaluation with a small user study or an additional perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS if feasible) to validate that lower VGG-loss corresponds to better human judgments, at least on a subset.
- Report diversity across multiple synthesized samples per texture (qualitative panel, and a proxy diversity metric).
- Release code and pretrained filter definitions to enable reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale architecture: What exact set of filter sizes and number of filters per scale did you use to reach 1024 maps? How are filters normalized across scales to balance contributions?
- Feature preprocessing: Are filters normalized (e.g., unit norm) and is per-feature variance equalized before Gram computation? Do you include or remove DC components in filters?
- Channel handling: For random filters, are RGB channels mixed per filter or treated separately? How does cross-channel mixing affect results vs per-channel filters?
- Optimization: How sensitive are results to the 1e7 loss/gradient scaling, the iteration limit, and the initialization? Did you try Adam/LBFGS variants and observe differences?
- Nonlinearity: You show ReLU is important; did you test other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, absolute value, square) and how do they compare?
- Efficiency: What are the compute and memory costs (per 256x256 texture) for single-scale vs multi-scale vs VGG synthesis? Any approximations (e.g., low-rank Gram) explored?
- Variability: How variable are the samples across different noise seeds for the same target? Do you observe mode collapse or near-deterministic reconstructions in some settings?
- Generality: How does performance change with image size, non-stationary textures, or structured patterns (e.g., periodic grids), beyond the examples shown?

Overall, this is a clear, insightful, and empirically grounded paper that provides a valuable minimal baseline and reframes assumptions in texture synthesis.
