Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear and timely question: what are the minimal ingredients needed to synthesize natural textures that are perceptually convincing? It proposes a strong, simple baseline: a single convolutional layer with random (data-independent) filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching of feature maps. The authors explore multiple single-layer variants (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA-trained filters; 11×11 filters; 363 vs 3267 maps), and a “multi-scale” single-layer variant that mixes multiple filter sizes (up to 55×55). They argue that neither depth nor supervised pretraining is indispensable for texture synthesis quality; instead, number of features, multi-scale receptive fields, and nonlinearity matter most. They quantify quality primarily via the VGG Gram loss (used in Gatys et al.), report that their multi-scale random model often rivals the VGG texture model visually and in this metric, and discuss the tension between perceptual similarity and variability in a MaxEnt framing. They also note practical aspects (LBFGS with appropriate scaling) that make random-feature synthesis work reliably.

Decision
Reject (borderline), primarily because:
1) The empirical support relies almost entirely on qualitative inspection and a VGG-based loss that is not an independent perceptual metric; there is no perceptual user study or robust, independent quantitative evaluation.
2) The novelty is incremental relative to the known Gram-matching framework (Gatys et al.), and several key claims (e.g., random features rivaling VGG, optimization being the determining factor) are not supported by sufficiently rigorous ablations and measurements.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The question—what is essential for texture synthesis—is well articulated, and the paper is well positioned against classical models (Portilla & Simoncelli), Gram-based neural models (Gatys et al.), and alternatives (GANs, PixelCNN, scattering). The focus on minimality and baselines is valuable.

- Motivation and placement: The work is motivated as a challenge to the perceived necessity of depth and supervised training for texture synthesis. The literature positioning is reasonable, though the relationship to scattering-based texture synthesis (Bruna & Mallat) and earlier random-conv feature work could be discussed more systematically, including differences in invariances, nonlinearity, and multi-scale construction.

- Do results support the claims?
  • The central claim (single-layer random features + Gram matching can rival VGG textures) is plausible and interesting. However, support is primarily qualitative, and the single quantitative measure is the VGG loss itself—the same representation the paper argues is not necessary. This introduces circularity and weakens the conclusion about perceptual equivalence.
  • There is a welcome qualitative ablation: number of feature maps, multi-scale vs single-scale, and the necessity of ReLU. But there is no systematic, quantitative study across textures (e.g., average and variance of metrics over a dataset, multiple random seeds), nor any user study to validate perceptual judgments.
  • The claim that differences from prior “random weights fail” reports stem from better optimization is not rigorously established. The only explicit change described is loss scaling and iteration count; no ablation shows sensitivity to these hyperparameters or compares optimizers/initializations in a controlled manner.
  • The discussion on variability vs perceptual similarity is insightful, but the paper optimizes exclusively for similarity. Without sampling or stopping-criterion studies, the claim that the model can balance variability remains unsubstantiated.
  • Efficiency: the paper concedes that large filters are computationally expensive and suggests depth for efficiency, but provides no runtime/memory benchmarks or architectural details to substantiate the efficiency/quality trade-off.

- Technical soundness: The method is sound and simple, and the explicit definitions of Gram distances and the synthesis objective are clear. However, the evaluation protocol lacks rigor by modern standards (no user study, no independent learned perceptual metric, no dataset-level analysis, no variance across seeds). The MaxEnt discussion is thought-provoking but remains conceptual; no formalization or experiments demonstrate the proposed -environment framing.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Evaluation:
  • Add a small psychophysical study (2AFC) on a subset (10–20 textures) to validate that the random multi-scale model is perceptually on par with VGG-based synthesis. Alternatively, use an independent learned perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS) rather than the same VGG Gram loss used by Gatys.
  • Report results on a standard texture dataset (e.g., DTD) with aggregate statistics, including multiple random seeds to quantify variability and robustness.
  • Quantify sample diversity: generate multiple samples per texture and report pairwise distances (in feature space and pixel space). Discuss the trade-off via stopping criteria.

- Ablations:
  • Systematically vary the number of feature maps per scale and the set of scales; provide curves showing quality vs capacity.
  • Detail the multi-scale architecture: exact filter sizes and counts, normalization across scales, and how maps are concatenated.
  • Explore alternative nonlinearities (Leaky ReLU, ELU), and normalization schemes (instance or feature normalization before Gram).
  • Provide a controlled optimization ablation: optimizer choice (LBFGS vs Adam), iteration budget, loss scaling, initialization (noise vs reference), and their impact on quality and speed.

- Comparisons:
  • Compare to scattering transform-based synthesis and to spectrum-constrained CNN texture methods (e.g., Liu et al. 2016) with the same evaluation protocol.
  • Include timing and memory benchmarks to substantiate the efficiency discussion and the claim that deep hierarchical nets can match quality more efficiently.

- Reproducibility and clarity:
  • Release code and exact hyperparameters (seeds, normalization constants, gradient scaling), and specify all preprocessing steps (mean subtraction, whitening where applicable).
  • Clean up references and figures; ensure all figures referenced appear with captions sufficient for independent interpretation.

Questions for the authors
1) What is the exact configuration of the multi-scale single-layer model (filter sizes, number of filters per size, initialization, normalization across scales)?
2) How sensitive are results to the number of feature maps? What is the minimal capacity (per scale and total) to match VGG-level quality on your test set?
3) Can you provide a quantitative ablation of the optimization hyperparameters (loss scaling, iterations, optimizer) demonstrating that these, rather than feature learning, explain the success with random filters?
4) How variable are samples? Please report pairwise distances among multiple synthesized images per texture and show example failure/success cases.
5) Will you release code and scripts for reproducibility? If so, when?
6) Can you provide runtime and memory comparisons between single-scale, multi-scale, and a hierarchical multi-layer variant achieving similar quality?

Overall, this is an interesting and potentially influential baseline paper, but it needs stronger, independent evaluation and more rigorous ablations to meet the bar for acceptance.
