- Core claim: a shallow, random, multi-scale CNN with ReLU and Gram-matrix matching can synthesize high-quality textures; depth and supervised pretraining are not indispensable.  
- Main contribution: a strong, minimal baseline — single convolutional layer with random filters plus Gram loss, with a multi-scale variant.  
- Experimental scope: broad ablations across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA, hand-crafted), feature map counts, and receptive field sizes.  
- Evaluation proposal: use VGG-based multi-layer Gram loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity instead of purely qualitative assessment.  
- Conceptual insight: links findings to the MaxEnt view and articulates a trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.  
- Decision: Weak accept.  
- Key reason for acceptance: surprising and practically useful result that recalibrates assumptions about depth/pretraining and establishes a valuable baseline.  
- Clarity and support: paper is clear, well-motivated, and contains informative ablations supporting the main claim.  
- Approach strength: single-layer and multi-scale designs are straightforward, replicable, and principled for capturing spatial correlations.  
- Empirical evidence: qualitative results on ~20 textures show small- and mid-scale structures captured well; multi-scale improves long-range consistency.  
- Optimization finding: initializing VGG-based optimization from single-layer solutions suggests prior failures with random nets were due partly to optimization, not representation limits.  
- Notable observed behavior: multi-scale random models sometimes achieve lower VGG loss than direct VGG optimization, indicating local minima in deeper models.  
- Strengths summarized: broad filter/capacity comparisons, systematic receptive-field manipulation, clear loss/optimization setup, and thoughtful theoretical discussion.  
- Major weakness: heavy reliance on VGG loss and qualitative inspection; no human perceptual study or independent metric (e.g., LPIPS) provided.  
- Missing systematic reporting: no standardized texture-set averages/variances, no runtime/memory benchmarks, and no measured sample diversity.  
- Comparative gaps: lack of quantitative comparisons to scattering-based and Portilla–Simoncelli methods.  
- Scope limitation: conclusions about pretraining/depth are demonstrated only for stationary textures; generality beyond textures is unaddressed.  
- Suggested additions: provide quantitative summaries (avg/var VGG loss, LPIPS), pairwise human preference tests, and runtime/memory comparisons.  
- Additional experimental requests: precise multi-scale architecture details (scales, filters per scale, normalization), sensitivity to map counts, and optimization ablations.  
- Diversity analysis recommendation: measure intra-model VGG/LPIPS distances and consider simple behavioral tasks (2AFC/oddity) even with small N.  
- Request for fairness checks: match total number of Gram constraints across models (including VGG) to assess capacity fairness.  
- Efficiency clarification: report architectures, speedups, and quality comparisons for hierarchical multi-layer random CNNs.  
- Failure analysis: discuss which texture classes fail (e.g., non-stationary or strong perspective) and why.  
- Reproducibility: release code and texture list to facilitate adoption.  
- Specific questions for authors: exact multi-scale filter sizes/counts, map normalization before Gram, whether cross-scale Gram stats were tried, sensitivity to 1e7 loss scaling and optimizer settings, seeds/variability, and robustness across resolutions/color spaces and preprocessing choices.
