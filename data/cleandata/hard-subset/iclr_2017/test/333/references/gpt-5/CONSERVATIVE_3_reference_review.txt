Summary
The paper investigates what is minimally necessary to synthesize convincing natural textures. It argues that (i) depth and (ii) supervised/pretrained features are not essential: a single-layer CNN with random filters, ReLU, and Gram-matrix matching can produce textures that often rival Gatys et al. (2015). The authors explore a range of single-layer filter banks (Fourier, k-means, PCA, random), show that more feature maps help, and that multi-scale random filters (up to 55×55 with 1024 filters total) are particularly effective. They propose using the VGG-based Gram loss as an objective quantitative proxy for perceptual quality and include a discussion of maximum-entropy texture modeling and the role of allowing small mismatches in constraints.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Empirical evidence is not sufficiently rigorous: evaluation is largely circular (VGG-based loss to evaluate models that are closely related), limited texture set, and lacks human perceptual studies or robust quantitative alternatives; claims like “often rival” are not convincingly substantiated.
- The main claim contradicts prior reports (that random features fail) but the paper does not provide a thorough ablation isolating and validating the stated optimization changes as the cause.

Supporting arguments
- Evaluation design:
  - The primary quantitative metric is VGG Gram loss, which is closely tied to one of the baselines and to the general Gram-matching framework. Using VGG features as a judge introduces bias and can penalize methods that are perceptually good but not aligned with VGG. The paper itself notes cases where VGG loss does not match perceptual quality.
  - There is no human study (even small-scale) to validate perceptual parity, no psychophysical test, and no standardized dataset/metric protocol. Visuals on ~20 textures with single seeds risk cherry-picking; there is no statistical aggregation across textures or seeds.
  - Variability is only discussed conceptually; no empirical analysis (e.g., diversity metrics, patch discrimination tests, or entropy proxies) is presented.

- Comparisons and fairness:
  - The VGG baseline is optimized with the same L-BFGS-B iterations, but the paper acknowledges optimization in deeper models is more prone to local minima and that initializing VGG from the single-layer solution helps. This suggests the baseline comparison is not optimized fairly; the improved initialization should be incorporated for all VGG baselines.
  - Classical baselines (Portilla & Simoncelli; scattering-based models) are referenced but not quantitatively compared using any agreed-upon protocol (e.g., human 2AFC), and spectrum-constrained methods (e.g., Liu et al., 2016) are not thoroughly compared.
  - The proposed multi-scale model uses 1024 filters and large kernels—its capacity is nontrivial. Equal-capacity comparisons to multi-layer random CNNs, or to trained feedforward texture networks, are underexplored.

- Claims vs evidence:
  - The central claim that the discrepancy with prior negative results on random weights is due to “more elaborate tuning of the optimization” is not isolated via controlled ablations. For example, no experiment shows that simply changing the gradient scaling or box constraints flips failure to success for the prior setup.
  - The finding that random filters outperform k-means/PCA filters is potentially important but needs robust averaging across many textures, multiple seeds, and statistical tests. The current evidence is qualitative and limited.
  - The VGG-loss sometimes being lower for the proposed method than for VGG-based synthesis underscores the circularity/instability of the metric and weakens the central quantitative claim.

- Reproducibility and clarity:
  - Multi-scale architecture details are insufficient: exact filter sizes per scale, number of filters per scale, normalization (per-filter norm?), and implementation specifics are not fully specified. There is no code link.
  - Optimization setup is unusual (gradient scaling by 1e7); sensitivity analyses are missing. The normalization inconsistency between Eq. (2) and Eq. (5) warrants justification.

- Practicality:
  - The method is computationally heavy (55×55 kernels, L-BFGS-B). The paper mentions hierarchical nets for efficiency but provides no runtime, memory, or complexity analysis. Without efficiency or feedforward sampling, applicability is limited.

Additional feedback (to help improve the work; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide a balanced evaluation protocol:
  - A small-scale human 2AFC or patch discrimination user study for 15–30 textures, with multiple seeds per method, would substantially strengthen claims.
  - Report metrics across multiple random seeds and textures with confidence intervals. Include failure rates and representative failure cases.
  - For fairness, use improved initialization for all baselines (including VGG). Consider additional metrics not tied to VGG features (e.g., spectrum statistics, scattering distances), even if imperfect.

- Methodological ablations:
  - Isolate the effect of the alleged optimization advances by reproducing the “failed” random-weight setup from Gatys et al. and then changing one element at a time (loss scaling, box constraints, number of iterations, ReLU vs. linear).
  - Detail the multi-scale design (filter sizes, counts per scale, normalization), and test multi-layer random CNNs with small kernels to match effective receptive fields.
  - Study the impact of ReLU vs. other nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, tanh) and filter normalization.

- Variability:
  - Add a concrete variability assessment: e.g., entropy proxies over Gram distributions, nearest-neighbor patch diversity, or a psychophysical inpainting test as outlined.

- Practical aspects:
  - Provide runtime/memory comparisons versus VGG-based optimization and classical models.
  - Release code and exact hyperparameters to ensure reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact multi-scale details: which kernel sizes, how many filters per scale, and how are filters normalized? Are ReLUs applied after each scale’s convolution?
- Can you reproduce Gatys et al.’s negative result for random weights and then show a controlled ablation where only your optimization tweaks change the outcome?
- How sensitive are results to the gradient scaling factor (1e7), iteration count, and box constraints?
- Are your conclusions robust across multiple seeds per texture? Please report mean/variance of your VGG-loss and any alternative metrics across seeds.
- Did you try multi-layer random CNNs with small kernels to match the large receptive fields achieved by 55×55 single-layer filters? How do they compare in quality and runtime?
- Can you include a fair VGG baseline that is initialized from your single-layer solution, as you suggest improves results?
- How were the 20 textures selected? Do results generalize to standardized texture datasets?
