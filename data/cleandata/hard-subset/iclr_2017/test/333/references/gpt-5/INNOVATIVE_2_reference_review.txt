Summary and contributions
- The paper asks a sharp, important question: how much structure do we really need to generate natural textures? It challenges the prevailing view that depth and supervised pretraining are indispensable, and shows that a single convolutional layer with random filters, combined with Gram-matrix statistics and ReLUs, can synthesize textures that often rival VGG-based state of the art.
- Main contributions:
  - A strong minimal baseline: single-layer (and multi-scale) random CNNs with Gram statistics that achieve high perceptual quality, sometimes matching or exceeding Gatys et al.
  - A systematic comparison across filter families (random, Fourier, k-means/PCA, trained vs. untrained), number of feature maps, and filter sizes, showing that random filters often perform on par or better.
  - Evidence that multi-scale is crucial for capturing both local and long-range structure; nonlinearity (ReLU) is indispensable.
  - An evaluation protocol using VGG-gram loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity; a thoughtful discussion connecting Gram-based synthesis to the MaxEnt framework and the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Accept
- Key reasons:
  1) Novel and bold insight that depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis; establishes a simple, powerful baseline that reframes the problem.
  2) Comprehensive and convincing empirical exploration and clear conceptual analysis that will influence future work and evaluation practices in texture synthesis.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The necessity of depth and pretraining for texture synthesis, and what minimal ingredients suffice. This is timely and foundational.
- Is the approach well motivated and situated in the literature? Yes. The work builds on Gatys et al. (Gram statistics), contrasts with prior claims that random features fail, and positions itself against both earlier parametric models (Portilla & Simoncelli) and modern deep approaches (VGG, PixelCNNs, GANs, feed-forward texture nets). The link to MaxEnt and ergodic random fields provides a principled perspective.
- Do results support claims?
  - Qualitative comparisons across many textures show strong performance, especially for structured man-made textures, where the proposed multi-scale random model sometimes appears superior.
  - Ablations are compelling: varying the number of filters, scale, filter type, and presence of ReLUs. The multi-scale vs. single large filter study is particularly informative, demonstrating the need for multiscale composition rather than simply enlarging receptive fields.
  - The use of VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy is reasonable in this domain, and the observed “paradox” (multi-scale beating VGG on VGG-loss) is openly discussed, with a credible explanation tied to optimization and variability.
  - The paper is transparent about computational efficiency and shows that depth may improve efficiency but not quality, which is a valuable practical takeaway.
- Novelty and impact: High. Establishes a minimal, strong baseline and alters the narrative around what aspects of deep networks are essential for texture synthesis. The conceptual discussion on “epsilon-environments” around constraints and variability vs. similarity is insightful, and opens new avenues.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen evaluation:
  - Include a small-scale human 2AFC or inpainting-based psychophysics test to validate that perceived similarity aligns with the VGG-loss results, particularly on man-made periodic textures where the proxy may be unreliable.
  - Report diversity/variability across multiple samples per texture (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss among samples or a simple perceptual spread metric). This would empirically support the discussion on similarity–variability trade-offs.
- Clarify multi-scale architecture details: exact filter sizes, counts per scale, and how the 1024 feature maps are allocated. Confirm whether random filters are fixed across all textures or re-sampled per texture.
- Provide runtimes and memory costs for single-scale vs. multi-scale vs. hierarchical variants; add a brief note on practical settings (iterations, stopping criteria) that trade similarity for variability.
- Expand failure cases: show representative textures where the method underperforms, and analyze whether issues stem from periodicity, perspective effects, or non-stationarity.
- Consider reporting an additional perceptual metric (e.g., LPIPS) alongside VGG-loss to check robustness of conclusions. If not feasible, at least discuss expected behavior.
- Notation cleanup: equations (2)–(7) mix normalized distances and losses—briefly justify normalization choices. The gradient scaling by 1e7 is a critical optimization detail—explain sensitivity.
- Release code/models to solidify the paper as a community baseline.

Questions for the authors
- Are the random filters sampled once and reused for all textures, or re-sampled per texture? How sensitive are results to the specific random draw?
- How are filters allocated in the multi-scale model (sizes, counts per scale)? Did you try cross-scale Gram statistics or only within-scale?
- Have you tried alternative nonlinearities (e.g., leaky ReLU, absolute value) or normalization (e.g., instance/batch norm) and how do they affect synthesis?
- How does performance scale with the number of feature maps at fixed compute? Is there a point of diminishing returns?
- Can you quantify diversity across samples from the same reference texture under different random initializations and different early stopping thresholds?
- For the textures where multi-scale beats VGG in VGG-loss: if you initialize VGG synthesis from your multi-scale outputs, how often do you reach lower final loss than from noise, and does this systematically improve visual quality?

Overall, this is a bold, insightful paper that advances our understanding of what really matters for texture synthesis. Despite some evaluation limitations, the conceptual novelty and strong empirical message justify acceptance.
