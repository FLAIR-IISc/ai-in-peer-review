1) Summary of contributions
The paper asks a bold and timely question: what is truly necessary to synthesize natural textures? It proposes a strong minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with random (data-independent) filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching, optimized via L-BFGS-B. The core claims are:
- Depth and supervised pretraining are not necessary; a single layer with random filters can rival the Gatys et al. VGG-based multi-layer model for many textures.
- Multi-scale filters are crucial; combining multiple receptive field sizes in one layer substantially improves quality over any single scale.
- Nonlinearities are indispensable; removing ReLU collapses modeling power.
- Random filters often perform on par with or better than learned or hand-crafted filters (k-means, PCA, Fourier), especially with many feature maps.
- A VGG-based loss serves as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity, enabling more systematic evaluation than purely visual inspection.
- The paper discusses how practical synthesis departs from strict MaxEnt by allowing small mismatches in constraints, and frames the importance of a perceptual distance and ε-environments for variability.

The work includes qualitative comparisons across many textures, ablations over number/types of filters, scale, and nonlinearity, and an insightful discussion on variability vs perceptual similarity.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and importance: the result that single-layer, randomly-filtered CNNs coupled with Gram statistics can rival deep pretrained models challenges prevailing assumptions and establishes a simple, strong baseline.
- Broad, convincing empirical evidence and ablations that illuminate which components matter (multi-scale, ReLU) and which do not (pretraining), opening a new direction for texture synthesis and perceptual modeling.

3) Supporting arguments
- The paper is well-motivated, situating itself against state-of-the-art Gram-based methods and classic parametric texture models, and asking a sharp minimality question.
- The empirical exploration is broad: random vs learned (k-means, PCA) vs Fourier filters; small vs many features (363 vs 3267); single-scale vs multi-scale; with and without nonlinearity. The qualitative improvements from multi-scale and necessity of ReLU are robust and intuitive.
- The observation that initializing VGG-based synthesis with single-layer solutions yields better results suggests the proposed model finds favorable basins and is less prone to poor local minima.
- The fact that the random multi-scale model sometimes achieves lower VGG loss than directly optimizing VGG loss is intriguing; it reinforces that the optimization landscape in deep feature spaces can be tricky and that the minimal model is a valuable tool.
- The discussion connecting MaxEnt, finite-size effects, and the role of perceptual distances is thoughtful and valuable for the community.

Caveats (not deal-breakers given the novelty):
- Evaluation relies primarily on a VGG-based loss, which is close to the Gatys recipe and may introduce circularity. While qualitative visuals are strong, a small human study would strengthen claims about perceptual quality.
- Variability is acknowledged but not quantified; the method may bias toward low-entropy solutions due to the optimization design.
- Computational efficiency is a concern (large filters); while the paper notes hierarchical architectures can be more efficient without quality gains, runtimes and speedups are not reported.
- Some implementation details of the multi-scale model and optimization are under-specified (e.g., exact per-scale filter allocations, normalization, sensitivity to the 1e7 loss scaling).

Given the significance of the central insight and the breadth of ablations, these shortcomings are acceptable for ICLR.

4) Suggestions and questions for authors (to improve the paper; not all are required for acceptance)
Suggestions:
- Include a small psychophysical test (e.g., 2AFC discrimination or inpainting-based judgments) to validate VGG-loss as a perceptual proxy and assess variability.
- Quantify variability across random seeds and restarts: report distribution of VGG-loss and some diversity metric between generated samples; show how early stopping trades similarity for diversity.
- Report compute: wall-clock time, memory, and FLOPs for single-scale and multi-scale models; compare to a hierarchical untrained multi-layer model that matches receptive field size for efficiency.
- Provide full multi-scale architecture details: list filter sizes, number of filters per scale, filter normalization, and whether Gram is computed over concatenated features across all scales.
- Clarify optimizer settings and sensitivity: the role of the 1e7 scaling factor, number of iterations to convergence, and impact of box constraints and initialization.
- Consider additional metrics to complement VGG-loss (e.g., human judgments; if feasible, LPIPS or other feature-space distances) to reduce reliance on a single perceptual proxy.
- Release code and a textures list to facilitate reproducibility.

Questions:
- Multi-scale construction: What exact scales and counts per scale were used? Are filters normalized per scale to equalize contributions? Is the Gram computed on the concatenation of all scales or separately and then averaged?
- Sensitivity to random seeds: How consistent are results across seeds for filters and initialization? Can you provide aggregate statistics (mean/variance of VGG-loss over multiple runs)?
- Optimization details: How critical is the 1e7 scaling for L-BFGS-B? Would Adam or LBFGS without scaling achieve similar results? Any regularization (e.g., TV) tested?
- Texture set: How were the evaluation textures selected? Any failure cases on highly structured man-made textures beyond those shown? How does performance scale with image size?
- Kmeans-Sample: Using patches from the target texture risks leakage; how do results compare when using only external data (Imagenet) for unsupervised filters?
- Efficiency: Did you try FFT-based convolutions to mitigate the cost of large filters? For hierarchical untrained models that you say are faster, can you quantify the speed/quality trade-off?

Overall, this paper makes a clear, surprising, and impactful point, and I expect it to influence both the design of texture models and our understanding of where “deep” and “trained” matter in generative perception.
