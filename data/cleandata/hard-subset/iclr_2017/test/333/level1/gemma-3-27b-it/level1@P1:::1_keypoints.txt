- Decision: Accept — finds that single-layer CNNs with random filters can produce high-quality textures, a significant and counter-intuitive result.  
- Novelty & significance: Challenges the assumed necessity of depth and pre-training for texture synthesis; conceptually important.  
- Strong empirical evaluation: Comprehensive comparisons to Gatys et al., using both visual results and a VGG-based quantitative metric.  
- Extensive ablation study: Systematic testing of filter types, sizes, numbers, and multi-scale model components.  
- Rigorous methodology: Clear experimental setup, architectures, optimization details, and objective VGG-based evaluation.  
- Optimization details lacking: Need clearer description of optimization modifications (especially gradient scaling) versus Gatys et al. and why they help.  
- Theoretical underpinnings incomplete: Should further explore links to maximum-entropy models and limitations for finite image sizes.  
- Computational cost unclear: Paper notes inefficiency of the multi-scale model but lacks quantitative runtime/training/inference data.  
- Visualization suggestion: Include visualizations of the random filter weights to aid intuition about their effectiveness.  
- Question 1: Provide specific modifications to optimization (scaling factor) and rationale for improved results.  
- Question 2: Give examples where the VGG-based loss fails to match human perception of texture quality.  
- Question 3: Clarify how allowing a "small mismatch" in constraints influences the synthesized textures.  
- Question 4: Is there a measurable relationship between number of filters and entropy of generated textures?
