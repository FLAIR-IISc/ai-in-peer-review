## Review of “WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?”

This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in deep neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly good textures can be generated using a single-layer CNN with random filters, challenging the prevailing belief that hierarchical representations learned from large datasets are crucial for this task. They present a thorough empirical evaluation, comparing their baseline model against state-of-the-art methods, and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a counter-intuitive and significant finding – that complex, multi-layered networks are not *necessary* for producing high-quality textures. The experimental results are convincing, the analysis is thorough, and the paper is well-written.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Novelty & Significance:** The finding that a single-layer network with random filters can generate textures competitive with state-of-the-art methods is a notable contribution. It challenges the commonly held assumption regarding the importance of depth and pre-training in texture synthesis, prompting a re-evaluation of what aspects of deep learning are truly essential for this task. This is a conceptually important result.
2. **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, comparing numerous single-layer architectures (varying filter types, sizes, and numbers) against the established Gatys et al. (2015a) method. The inclusion of both qualitative visual comparisons and a quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss strengthens the validity of their claims. The extensive ablation study—testing different filter types and sizes—provides valuable insights into what contributes to performance.
3. **Rigorous Methodology:** The paper details the experimental setup clearly, including network architectures, optimization parameters, and evaluation metrics. The adoption of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric, replacing purely subjective human evaluation, increases the objectivity of the study. The analysis of the multi-scale model and its components (filter size importance, ReLU importance) is well-executed.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Optimization:** While the authors mention tuning the optimization procedure, a more detailed description of these modifications compared to the original Gatys et al. implementation would be beneficial. This is crucial, as the authors acknowledge this as a potential source of discrepancy with earlier findings. Specifically, what scaling factor was used and why was it effective?
* **Theoretical Underpinnings:** The paper touches on the connection to maximum entropy models but doesn’t fully explore it. Elaborating on how the random filters relate to maximizing entropy subject to constraints, or discussing the limitations of the MaxEnt framework in the context of finite image sizes (as hinted at in the Discussion section), would enrich the theoretical understanding.
* **Computational Cost:** The paper mentions the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model. Providing some quantitative data on the computational cost (e.g., training time, inference time) would be helpful for assessing its practical applicability.
* **Visualizations of Filter Weights:** Including visualizations of the random filters used in the best-performing models could provide some intuition into why they work surprisingly well.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detail about the specific modifications made to the optimization procedure (specifically the gradient scaling factor) compared to Gatys et al. (2015a)? Why did these changes improve the results?
2.  You mention that the VGG-based loss isn't a perfect perceptual metric. Could you provide examples where it demonstrably fails to accurately reflect the perceived quality of synthesized textures? 
3.  The paper suggests that the success isn’t solely due to capturing the statistical dependencies of the texture but also relates to the *way* the constraints are handled. Could you elaborate on this point? Specifically, how does allowing for a "small mismatch" in the constraints affect the generated textures?
4. The paper touches on the connection to maximum entropy principles. Is there a quantifiable relationship between the number of filters and the entropy of the generated textures?
