## Review for ICLR 2017 - WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in deep neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing belief that these are crucial, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be generated using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters. They present a minimal baseline for texture synthesis, compare it against state-of-the-art methods, and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based model from Gatys et al. (2015a). Furthermore, they discuss a generalization of maximum entropy models to account for natural variability in textures. The core finding is that the depth and pre-training aspects are *not* indispensable for achieving good texture synthesis results.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting observation and a compelling minimal baseline, the evidence presented doesn’t sufficiently convince me that the reported improvements are robust and generalize beyond the specific experimental setup. The quantitative evaluation, while an improvement over purely subjective assessments, still relies heavily on the VGG network’s features, potentially leading to circular reasoning.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Scope of Improvement:** The authors claim to rival state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance, but this is primarily shown through visual comparison in Figure 4. While some results *appear* competitive, a more rigorous statistical comparison is missing. The fact that the VGG loss is sometimes *lower* for the multi-scale model suggests it’s simply mimicking the VGG features, not creating truly novel and high-quality textures. The claim of rivaling SOTA requires stronger evidence.
*   **Dependence on Optimization Tuning:** The abstract mentions “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” as a key factor explaining why previous attempts with random filters failed. This is a crucial detail, but the paper doesn’t fully articulate what this tuning *is*. Without a clear explanation, it’s hard to assess whether the success is due to the network architecture itself or simply a lucky optimization setting. The specific scaling factor applied to the loss and gradients (10^7) seems unusually large and requires justification.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation Concerns:** The use of the VGG-based loss as a quality metric is problematic. Since the SOTA method they are comparing against *is* VGG-based, minimizing the distance to VGG features essentially means matching VGG’s representation. This could lead to a biased evaluation where the model is optimized to perform well on the VGG metric rather than genuinely creating textures that are perceived as natural by humans. The appendix notes that SSIM is a less useful metric, but does not present any human evaluation data to validate the VGG loss.
*   **Lack of Ablation Study Detail:** While multiple filter types are tested, the paper does not systematically ablate the contribution of each component (e.g., the multi-scale aspect, the ReLU non-linearity). The claim that the multi-scale aspect is crucial (section 6) is presented without extensive comparison. A more controlled ablation study would strengthen the conclusions.



**4. Additional Feedback:**

*   **Optimization Details:** Provide a much more detailed explanation of the optimization procedure, particularly the "elaborate tuning." What specific parameters were changed? How sensitive are the results to these parameter settings?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform a user study or a more robust quantitative evaluation (e.g., Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) score) to statistically demonstrate the improvements over existing methods. Simply presenting visual examples isn't enough.
*   **Generalization:** Test the model on a wider variety of textures and datasets. Is the performance consistent across different texture categories?
*   **Computational Cost:** The multi-scale model’s computational inefficiency is acknowledged, but a more thorough discussion of the trade-offs between performance and computational cost would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** The discussion of maximum entropy models is interesting but feels somewhat disconnected from the empirical results. Elaborate on how the results inform the theoretical framework, or conversely, how the theoretical framework motivates the experiments.
*   **Clarify the Role of Filter Size:** The relationship between filter size, receptive field, and generated texture quality seems critical. Provide a clearer explanation and potentially visualize the receptive fields of different layers/models.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the optimization tuning, including the specific parameters that were modified and their impact on the results?
2.  Have you conducted any user studies to assess the perceptual quality of the generated textures? If not, why not, and how do you justify relying solely on the VGG-based loss?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the scaling factor applied to the loss and gradients during optimization? What happens if this value is changed significantly?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed ablation study, systematically evaluating the contribution of each component (e.g., multi-scale, ReLU) to the overall performance?
5.  The paper claims the VGG loss approximates perceptual differences. How does the method handle textures that are *different* from those in the ImageNet dataset used for VGG pre-training? Do you observe performance degradation in such cases?
