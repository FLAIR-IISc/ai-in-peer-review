The paper investigates the question of what is essential for generating natural textures using deep neural networks. Challenging the prevailing belief that depth and pre-training are crucial, the authors demonstrate that high-quality textures can be synthesized from a single-layer network with random filters. This is a surprising and potentially significant finding in the field of texture synthesis.

**Reject.** While the results are intriguing, the paper lacks sufficient rigor in its evaluation and comparative analysis, hindering a confident assessment of its claims. The core argument – that a single-layer network with random filters can rival state-of-the-art – is not convincingly demonstrated despite the visually compelling examples.

The primary justification for rejection stems from concerns about the quantitative evaluation. The paper introduces a VGG-based loss as a proxy for perceptual quality, but the limitations of this metric are acknowledged even within the paper itself. Specifically, the authors note that it sometimes fails to capture perceptual advantages, particularly for textures with regular structures. Relying heavily on this flawed metric undermines the objective comparison with the Gatys et al. (2015a) method. A more robust and comprehensive evaluation, including rigorous human perceptual studies, is necessary to support the claim of comparable or superior performance.

Secondly, the ablation study, while extensive in its exploration of different filter types, lacks sufficient detail regarding hyperparameter optimization for each model. It’s unclear whether each model was tuned to its full potential, or if the observed performance differences are partially attributable to variations in optimization. The statement about “more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” contributing to the discrepancy with earlier work (Gatys et al., 2015a) needs further elaboration. What specifically was done to tune the optimization? 

Finally, while the discussion section acknowledges the limitations of the VGG-loss and introduces the concept of balancing perceptual similarity and variability, it remains largely theoretical. Concrete proposals for quantifying variability or improving the perceptual metric are missing.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Quantitative Analysis:** The authors should perform a more thorough statistical comparison. Instead of simply stating that the multi-scale model "often rivals" the state-of-the-art, provide quantitative data (e.g., average VGG loss across a diverse set of textures, statistical significance tests) to support this claim.
*   **Human Evaluation:** Conduct a user study where participants are asked to discriminate between textures synthesized by the proposed method and those generated by the Gatys et al. (2015a) approach. This would provide a more reliable assessment of perceptual quality.
*   **Hyperparameter Optimization:** Detail the optimization procedure for each model. Provide a table summarizing the hyperparameters used, and discuss any efforts made to tune them for optimal performance.
*   **Filter Size Investigation:** The investigation of filter sizes is interesting, but it’s not clear how the filter sizes were chosen and if the results generalize to other textures.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** While acknowledging the computational cost of the multi-scale model, the authors don’t explore potential optimizations or trade-offs.
*   **Clarification on Optimization Tuning**: Provide details on the "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" mentioned. What parameters were adjusted, and how did this impact the results?
*   **Ablation Study of Optimization Parameters:** Perform an ablation study on the optimization parameters (e.g., learning rate, number of iterations) to understand their influence on the synthesized texture quality for the single-layer network.
