- Paper investigates whether depth and pre-training are necessary for texture synthesis, showing single-layer random-filter networks can produce good textures.
- Main claim: a single-layer network with random filters can rival deep pre-trained methods for texture synthesis.
- Decision: Reject — contribution seen as incremental and not sufficient for a top-tier venue.
- Reviewer views core finding as a re-exploration of ideas previously touched on by Gatys et al. (2015a) rather than a paradigm shift.
- Lack of a clear theoretical explanation for why random filters work; results are viewed as empirical and attributed vaguely to “robust optimization.”
- Strength: Extensive and systematic experimentation across single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) and feature map counts.
- Strength: Fair comparison to the Gatys et al. baseline.
- Strength: Introduction of a VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative texture quality metric.
- Strength: Multi-scale model performs impressively, often outperforming single-scale models and sometimes rivaling VGG-based synthesis.
- Strength: Ablation studies effectively show the importance of combining scales and non-linearities.
- Weakness: Authors do not sufficiently analyze why their results differ from Gatys et al.; attribution to tuning is vague.
- Weakness: Discussion section is disjointed and introduces concepts (Minimax entropy, perceptual distance) without integration into the core argument.
- Weakness: Proposed theoretical construct (bipartition Px) reads as a direction rather than a concrete contribution.
- Weakness: Visual results do not consistently show a clear advantage over the VGG-based approach; improvements are often subtle.
- Weakness: Novelty is limited; single-layer approaches are not new and gains may stem mainly from tuning and multi-scale combination.
- Suggestion: Provide detailed optimization discussion and sensitivity analysis (scaling factor, L-BFGS-B parameters, convergence curves).
- Suggestion: Explore theoretical links (e.g., random Fourier features) to justify why random filters capture texture statistics.
- Suggestion: Add a small-scale human perceptual study to validate VGG-based quantitative metrics.
- Suggestion: Compare method to other recent texture synthesis approaches (GANs, normalizing flows).
- Suggestion: Directly replicate Gatys et al. (2015a) with their optimization to clarify the discrepancy.
- Suggestion: Streamline discussion to focus on key takeaways and avoid unrelated theoretical detours.
- Question: Provide a detailed breakdown of the optimization process and hyperparameter sensitivity.
- Question: Did you replicate Gatys et al. (2015a) exactly with random filters; what were the results?
- Question: Can you give an intuitive explanation for why random filters generate reasonable textures beyond “robust optimization”?
- Question: How does computational cost of the multi-scale model compare to the VGG-based approach (training and synthesis speed)?
- Question: Would results change significantly under a different perceptual metric given VGG loss limitations?
