This paper investigates the necessity of depth and pre-training in convolutional neural networks for natural texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that surprisingly good results can be achieved with a single-layer network employing random filters, challenging the prevailing wisdom that deep, pre-trained networks are essential for this task. They present a comprehensive evaluation of different single-layer architectures, comparing them to the state-of-the-art Gatys et al. (2015a) approach, and introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on VGG-based synthesis loss.

**Reject.** While the findings are intriguing and the experimentation thorough, the paper presents a somewhat incremental contribution that doesn’t fully justify publication at a top-tier conference like ICLR. The core finding – that a single layer with random filters *can* generate reasonable textures – feels like a re-exploration of territory previously touched upon (albeit dismissed) by Gatys et al. (2015a), and the improvements are not dramatic enough to fundamentally shift the understanding of texture synthesis. The paper lacks a clear theoretical explanation for *why* this works beyond “robust optimization,” making the results feel somewhat empirical and less impactful.

The paper's strengths lie in its extensive experimentation. The authors systematically explore various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) with different numbers of feature maps, providing a solid empirical foundation for their claims. The comparison against the Gatys et al. baseline is fair, and the introduction of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative evaluation metric is a positive step towards more objective texture quality assessment. The multi-scale model’s performance is indeed impressive, consistently outperforming single-scale models and, in some cases, rivaling the VGG-based approach.  The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the importance of combining different scales and non-linearities.

However, several weaknesses undermine the paper’s overall impact. First, while the authors acknowledge Gatys et al. (2015a) dismissed random filters, they don't delve deeply enough into *why* their results differ. They attribute it to "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure,” but this is vague. A more detailed analysis of the optimization landscape and sensitivity to hyperparameters would be crucial.  Second, the “discussion” section, while ambitious, feels somewhat disjointed and introduces concepts (Minimax entropy, perceptual distance measures) without fully integrating them into the core argument.  The proposed bipartition Px feels like a theoretical direction rather than a concrete contribution within the scope of this paper. Third, the visual examples, while illustrative, don't consistently demonstrate a clear advantage for the single-layer models over the VGG-based approach.  The improvements are often subtle, and in some cases, the VGG-based textures appear visually superior. Finally, the novelty of the approach is limited. Single-layer networks have been used for texture synthesis before, and this work largely demonstrates a successful parameter tuning and scale combination rather than a fundamentally new technique.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Optimization Details:** Provide a much more detailed discussion of the optimization procedure. How sensitive is the performance to the scaling factor (10^7)? What is the impact of different L-BFGS-B parameters? A learning curve showing the convergence of the optimization process would be helpful.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Explore potential theoretical explanations for the success of random filters. Is there a connection to the random Fourier feature literature? Can the authors provide insights into why a single layer is sufficient to capture the relevant statistical dependencies in textures?
*   **Perceptual Evaluation:** While the VGG-based loss is a useful metric, supplement it with a small-scale human evaluation study to provide more convincing evidence of perceptual quality.
*   **Comparison to Alternative Methods:** Briefly discuss how the proposed method compares to other recent texture synthesis approaches beyond Gatys et al. (2015a), such as those based on GANs or normalizing flows.
*   **Clarify the "Discrepancy":** Directly address the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a)'s findings. Repeat their experiment with the same optimization procedure and show a direct comparison.
*   **Streamline the Discussion:** Focus the discussion section on the key takeaways of the paper and avoid introducing unrelated theoretical concepts.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the optimization process used in this work, including a sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters (scaling factor, L-BFGS-B parameters)?
2.  Did you attempt to replicate the Gatys et al. (2015a) experiment with random filters using *exactly* their optimization procedure? If so, what were the results? If not, why not?
3.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* random filters are able to generate reasonable textures, beyond simply stating “robust optimization”?
4.  How does the computational cost of the multi-scale model compare to the VGG-based approach, both in terms of training time and synthesis speed?
5.  Given the limitations of the VGG-based loss, do you believe the results would change significantly with a different perceptual metric?
