## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper investigates the fundamental requirements for natural texture synthesis using deep neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are crucial, demonstrating that surprisingly high-quality textures can be generated using a single-layer network with random filters. They systematically compare this simple baseline against state-of-the-art methods, introducing a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG feature space reconstruction loss, and discuss the implications for maximum entropy modeling of textures. 

I **reject** this paper. While the findings are interesting and counterintuitive, the paper lacks sufficient justification for its experimental choices and fails to convincingly demonstrate the *superiority* of the proposed approach beyond showcasing parity with existing methods. The argument relies heavily on showing results *rival* existing methods, but doesn’t establish a clear advantage or novel capability. 

The core argument – that a single layer with random filters can achieve results comparable to deep, pre-trained networks – is intriguing. The experimental setup, comparing various single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, PCA, Random) against a state-of-the-art approach, appears comprehensive.  The introduction of a quantitative evaluation metric (VGG loss) is a positive step toward objective comparison. However, the justification for the chosen filter sizes (11x11) and the number of feature maps is weak. It's unclear why these specific parameters were selected, and a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of these choices would strengthen the claims. The paper also mentions a more elaborate optimization procedure as a key factor in their success, but provides limited detail about this tuning, hindering reproducibility. Further, the paper’s reliance on visual assessment alongside the VGG loss feels inconsistent; if the quantitative metric is reliable, why the continued emphasis on perceptual quality as judged visually?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The discussion of the MaxEnt framework and its limitations is valuable but feels somewhat disconnected from the main experimental results. A tighter integration would improve the paper's coherence.
*   The claim that the optimization is “less prone to local minima” for the single-layer network requires more evidence. Was this observed consistently? Are there any metrics to support this assertion?
*   It would be helpful to see a comparison of computational costs between the proposed method and existing approaches, given the simplicity of the model.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details about the “elaborate tuning” of the optimization procedure? Specifically, what parameters were adjusted and why?
2.  What is the rationale behind choosing an 11x11 filter size? Have you explored other filter sizes, and if so, how did they affect the results?
3.  The paper mentions that the VGG-based loss isn't *perfect*. Under what specific conditions or for what types of textures does it fail to accurately reflect perceptual quality?
