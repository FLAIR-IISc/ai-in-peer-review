**Summary of the paper's contributions**

This paper tackles the question of what aspects of deep neural network feature representations are crucial for natural image generation, specifically for the task of natural texture synthesis. The authors show that a single-layer network with random filters can generate high-quality textures, rivaling the state-of-the-art approach by Gatys et al. (2015a). They also demonstrate that the depth, pooling, and supervised training of features on natural images are not indispensable for natural texture synthesis. The paper presents a strong minimal baseline for parametric texture synthesis and evaluates the quality of synthesized textures using a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model.

**Decision and key reasons**

I decide to **accept** this paper, with two key reasons:

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear and well-motivated approach to investigating the essential elements of deep neural network feature representations for natural texture synthesis. They systematically evaluate the importance of various aspects, including the depth of the network, the use of pooling, and the training of features on natural images.
2. **Strong empirical evidence**: The paper presents strong empirical evidence to support its claims, including a comprehensive evaluation of various single-layer architectures with different filter sizes and types of filters. The results demonstrate that the proposed approach can generate high-quality textures that rival the state-of-the-art approach.

**Supporting arguments**

The paper's contributions are significant, as they challenge the conventional wisdom that deep hierarchical representations and supervised training of features on natural images are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The authors' approach is also well-grounded in the literature, building on the work of Gatys et al. (2015a) and other researchers in the field.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with a clear description of the methods and results. The use of a quantitative texture quality measure based on the synthesis loss in the VGG-based model is a welcome addition, as it provides a more objective evaluation of the synthesized textures.
