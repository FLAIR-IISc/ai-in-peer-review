## Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

This paper investigates the core requirements for generating natural textures using deep neural networks. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that depth and pre-training are essential, demonstrating that high-quality textures can be synthesized from a single-layer network with random filters. They present a strong baseline and a comprehensive comparison against state-of-the-art methods, introducing a VGG-based loss function for quantitative texture evaluation. This work offers a valuable contribution by simplifying the approach to texture synthesis and questioning established practices.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form, primarily due to concerns regarding the novelty and sufficient justification of the experimental setup, particularly the choice of hyperparameters and the optimization procedure. While the results are interesting, the paper doesn’t fully convince that the observed success is *specifically* due to the simplicity of the model, and not to careful tuning of the optimization process.

The authors convincingly show that a single-layer network *can* generate good textures, but the extent to which this is surprising, given the optimization details (scaling the loss, L-BFGS-B algorithm, 2000 iterations), is not adequately addressed. The claim that previous work failed due to inadequate optimization feels somewhat post-hoc. The extensive ablation study with different filter types is valuable, but the rationale behind the specific choices (e.g., the number of feature maps – 363 vs. 3267) isn’t fully explained. Furthermore, the discussion of the MaxEnt framework feels somewhat tacked on and doesn’t fully integrate with the empirical findings. The quantitative evaluation using the VGG-based loss is a positive step, but its limitations are acknowledged, and a more robust perceptual metric would strengthen the conclusions.

To improve the paper, I suggest: 
*   A more thorough investigation into the sensitivity of the results to the optimization parameters. A comparison to other optimization algorithms would be beneficial.
*   A clearer justification for the choices made in the experimental setup, particularly the number of feature maps and filter sizes.
*   A more detailed discussion of the limitations of the VGG-based loss and potential alternative evaluation metrics.
*   Clarify how the results relate to the theoretical framework of MaxEnt models. Is the observed success a consequence of finding a better balance between similarity and variability, or something else?
*   Could the authors provide examples of failed syntheses, and analyze why they failed?
