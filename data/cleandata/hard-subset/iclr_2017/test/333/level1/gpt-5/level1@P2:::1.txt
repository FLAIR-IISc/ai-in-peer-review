Summary and contributions
The paper asks a clear, focused question: which ingredients of recent deep-learning-based texture synthesis are truly necessary? It challenges the widespread belief that depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are essential by showing that high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single convolutional layer with random filters, no pooling, and simple ReLU nonlinearity—especially when multiple filter sizes are combined (multi-scale). The work:

- Establishes a strong minimal baseline: Gram-matrix matching from a single-layer CNN with random filters.
- Provides extensive comparisons across single-layer architectures (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means on ImageNet or target texture) and filter counts/sizes, including a multi-scale variant.
- Introduces a quantitative evaluation protocol using the VGG texture loss (average Gram-matrix discrepancy across 5 layers) instead of purely qualitative inspection, and documents when this surrogate metric aligns or misaligns with perception.
- Offers ablations highlighting indispensable elements: multi-scale filters and nonlinearity; shows that more feature maps generally improve quality; and that depth/pooling/pretraining are not necessary for good synthesis.
- Discusses the MaxEnt framing and advocates explicitly treating texture synthesis as operating within a small tolerance around constraints to balance perceptual similarity and variability.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Novel and impactful empirical finding: single-layer, randomly initialized CNNs with multi-scale filters and Gram statistics can rival or surpass VGG-based synthesis on many textures, overturning a common assumption and clarifying what “matters” in texture synthesis.
2) Careful, convincing ablations and broad comparisons that isolate the role of filter scale, count, and nonlinearity, plus a reasonable quantitative evaluation protocol.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The question is crisply stated and well positioned in the literature (Julesz, Heeger & Bergen, Portilla & Simoncelli; Gatys et al.; Ulyanov et al.; scattering transform). Testing the necessity of depth/pretraining is both timely and important for understanding generative models and for building lighter-weight alternatives.
- Approach correctness: The model follows the established Gram-matrix matching framework, differing only in the feature extractor (single-layer vs deep VGG). The optimization setup (L-BFGS-B with box constraints) is standard; the observation that proper scaling of the loss/gradients avoids premature termination is plausible and explains discrepancies with earlier negative results on random filters.
- Empirical support: Qualitative results across many textures are compelling; the multi-scale single-layer random model captures both small- and long-range structure better than single-scale counterparts and often rivals VGG-based synthesis. The ablation showing that very large single-scale filters capture long-range but miss fine details, whereas 11×11 capture local structure but not long-range, convincingly motivates multi-scale design. ReLU’s necessity is demonstrated. The VGG-based loss provides a reproducible quantitative proxy, and the paper is transparent about its limitations (notably on highly regular man-made textures).
- Rigor and caveats: The paper is appropriately cautious about variability vs similarity and about the surrogate nature of the VGG loss. The MaxEnt discussion, while partially conceptual, is coherent and situates the practical “epsilon-tolerance” needed in finite images.

Limitations (do not outweigh strengths, but worth noting)
- Evaluation relies primarily on the VGG texture loss and visual inspection; no psychophysical user study is conducted. The paper acknowledges misalignments with perception for certain textures.
- Some claims (e.g., “hierarchical networks do not improve quality but are faster”) are asserted without detailed experimental evidence in the main text.
- Computational efficiency of large spatial filters is a practical concern; runtimes and memory profiles are not reported.
- Variability is discussed but not quantified; the potential for overfitting/near-copying at finite sizes is noted but not systematically measured.
- Comparisons could better control for the number of constraints/feature maps to ensure fairness across models.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Include aggregate quantitative summaries (e.g., median and variance of VGG-loss across textures) and not only per-image examples.
- Report runtime, memory, and convergence behavior for single-scale vs multi-scale vs hierarchical variants; provide the quality–compute trade-off curves.
- Add a small psychophysical study (2AFC discriminability between real and synthesized patches; inpainting test) to validate the surrogate metric and assess variability.
- Provide more details on the multi-scale architecture (filter sizes, counts per scale, exact distribution across 3×3/11×11/55×55) and sensitivity analyses to these design choices.
- Clarify and illustrate the “VGG initialized from single-layer result” claim with quantitative improvements and examples in the main text.
- Explore alternative nonlinearities (absolute value, leaky ReLU, modulus à la scattering) and simple pooling to test robustness.
- Release code, texture set, and seeds for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale design: What are the exact filter sizes, counts per scale, and channel configurations used in the multi-scale model? How sensitive is performance to these allocations?
2) Effect of feature count: How does quality (VGG loss and visual) scale with the number of random filters per scale? Is there a saturation point?
3) Robustness: How sensitive are results to random initialization and noise seeds? Please report variance over multiple runs per texture.
4) Hierarchical vs single-layer: Could you provide quantitative comparisons (quality vs runtime) for hierarchical multi-layer random networks that achieve similar receptive field sizes?
5) Diversity/variability: Can you quantify sample diversity (e.g., pairwise VGG-loss between independently synthesized samples from the same reference) and compare it to diversity among real patches?
6) Overfitting risk: In the deeper random CNN anecdote where the reference is exactly reconstructed, how often does this happen? Under what conditions? How do you prevent trivial copying?
7) Evaluation bias: Given that VGG-based loss is also the optimization objective for one baseline, do you observe systematic biases favoring/penalizing specific models? Any metrics beyond VGG-loss (e.g., LPIPS, SSIM results) on a larger set?
8) Efficiency: What are typical per-image runtimes and GPU memory footprints for the single-layer multi-scale model vs VGG-based synthesis at the reported settings?
9) Nonlinearity: How do different nonlinearities (abs, leaky ReLU, tanh) affect results? Is ReLU special here, or is any rectifying/modulus nonlinearity sufficient?
10) Data dependence: For k-means/PCA filters trained on ImageNet, why do you think random filters often perform on par or better? Is whitening critical?

Overall, this paper makes a clear, well-motivated, and experimentally grounded contribution that will inform both theory and practice in texture synthesis. I recommend acceptance.
