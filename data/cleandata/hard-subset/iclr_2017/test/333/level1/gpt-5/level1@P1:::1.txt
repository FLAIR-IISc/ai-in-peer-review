1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper investigates which elements of deep feature representations are truly necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis. Contrary to prior assumptions that depth, pooling, and supervised training on natural images are crucial (e.g., Gatys et al. 2015), the authors demonstrate that textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality can be generated with a single convolutional layer, no pooling, and even completely random filters. The key contributions are:
- A strong minimalist baseline: single-layer CNNs with ReLU nonlinearity and Gram-matrix matching, using random filters (and multi-scale variants), can synthesize textures that often rival the VGG-based model.
- Systematic comparisons across filter types (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means), filter counts, and scales, showing that unsupervisedly learned filters do not consistently outperform random ones, and that multi-scale random filters are particularly effective.
- A quantitative evaluation using the VGG Gram-based loss as a proxy for perceptual similarity, supplementing qualitative results across 20 textures.
- Ablations indicating that nonlinearity (ReLU) and multi-scale receptive fields are critical, whereas depth and supervised pre-training are not.
- A thoughtful discussion placing the approach in the MaxEnt texture modeling framework and arguing for the importance of metrics and allowing small constraint mismatches to capture variability.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptually novel and impactful result: strong evidence that depth and supervised training are not necessary for high-quality texture synthesis when using Gram statistics, establishing a new minimalist baseline that informs our understanding of what matters in texture generation.
- Thorough empirical ablations (filter types, number of feature maps, scales, nonlinearity) and consistent qualitative evidence across many textures; multi-scale random features are especially effective.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper directly addresses an important open question raised by Gatys et al. and related follow-ups: which components of deep representations are necessary for texture synthesis? The work is well-situated within prior literature (Portilla & Simoncelli, scattering, VGG/Gram methods, feedforward texture nets) and takes a complementary stance by removing rather than adding complexity.
- Approach: The formulation is clean and transparent: Gram matrices over ReLU activations of a single conv layer (random or not), optimized with L-BFGS-B. The multi-scale variant compellingly shows that combining receptive fields at different sizes is more important than using very large single-scale filters.
- Evidence: Visual comparisons across 20 textures and a range of single-layer models convincingly show that random multi-scale filters can rival the VGG multi-layer model, sometimes even achieving lower VGG loss. Ablations demonstrate the necessity of the nonlinearity and the benefit of multi-scale. The observation that VGG-based synthesis can be improved by initializing from random single-layer outputs supports the claim that optimization issues confound deeper models.
- Rigor: While the VGG-based loss is not an ideal perceptual metric, it is a widely used and relevant proxy for this domain and favors the competing VGG model; the fact that the random model sometimes wins under this metric despite not optimizing it strengthens the claim.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decisive)
- Evaluation robustness and perceptual validity: The reliance on VGG loss as the primary quantitative metric risks circularity. A small-scale psychophysical study (e.g., two-alternative forced choice on discriminating real vs synthesized, or the proposed inpainting test) would substantially strengthen claims of perceptual quality and the discussion on variability.
- Variability vs. fidelity: Provide quantitative evidence on diversity by generating multiple samples per texture and reporting intra-set VGG distances, pixel-space diversity, and failure cases of near-copying. Even a simple analysis would improve the MaxEnt discussion.
- Reproducibility and detail: Specify the exact multi-scale configuration (filter sizes used, number of filters per scale, initialization ranges), optimization hyperparameters (learning rate if applicable, stopping criteria, gradient scaling rationale), and runtime/memory profiles vs. VGG and vs. hierarchical multi-layer variants.
- Comparison baselines: Include a Portilla & Simoncelli baseline and scattering-based synthesis for a few textures to triangulate the contribution of random features versus carefully designed filter banks. Also comment on color space choices (RGB vs. decorrelated color spaces) and whether channel-wise preprocessing affects results.
- Theory/links: Connect the findings to random features/kernel approximations: Gram matrices of random conv features with ReLU effectively induce a stationary second-order statistic over a rich nonlinear embedding. Relating to scattering transform’s stability/invariance would deepen the explanation of why random features suffice when combined with Gram pooling.
- Failure modes: Characterize textures where the approach underperforms (e.g., very long-range global structure, highly non-stationary or perspective distortions) and whether adding simple constraints (e.g., spectrum constraints) helps.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale design: What exact filter sizes and how many filters per scale were used to reach 1024 features? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- Optimization: How critical is the 1e7 scaling of the loss/gradients and the [0,1] box constraints? Did you try different optimizers (Adam, L-BFGS without scaling) and iteration budgets?
- Color handling: Did you experiment with different color spaces or per-channel Gram matrices versus cross-channel correlations? Does color decorrelation/whitening change outcomes?
- Diversity: How diverse are multiple samples from the same texture under different random seeds? Can you report intra-sample VGG losses and show examples where overfitting (near-copy) occurs?
- Regular structures: For cases where multi-scale random outperforms VGG visually but not in VGG loss, can you quantify this gap with an alternative metric (SSIM, LPIPS), or run a small user study?
- Efficiency: You mention hierarchical architectures speed up synthesis without quality gains. Could you provide concrete speed/memory numbers and whether dilated convolutions help?
- Constraints: Have you tried augmenting the Gram loss with simple spectrum or histogram constraints (as in Liu et al.) to address failures without adding depth or training?
- Generalization: Does the conclusion extend to style transfer, or does content preservation necessitate trained features? Any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper offers an insightful and well-executed study that clarifies the essential ingredients of Gram-based texture synthesis and establishes a valuable minimalist baseline.
