- Paper asks which aspects of deep feature representations are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis and shows a single-layer random-filter multi-scale network with Gram-matrix matching can rival VGG-based methods.

- Presents careful ablations across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), counts, sizes, and nonlinearities, and introduces a VGG-Gram-based quantitative evaluation.

- Situates findings in MaxEnt framework and highlights a perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-off.

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason 1: Strong, surprising empirical result that depth and supervised pretraining are not indispensable for texture synthesis.

- Key reason 2: Provides a simple, general baseline with practical value and clear insights (importance of multi-scale features and nonlinearity).

- Supports claims with clear problem formulation and literature placement (Julesz, Portilla–Simoncelli, Gatys, GAN/PixelCNN).

- Empirical evidence is extensive and robust: many single-layer variants, multi-scale vs. single-scale, and ReLU vs. linear comparisons.

- Optimization analysis (L-BFGS, gradient scaling) clarifies prior discrepancies and identifies optimization stability as a confound.

- VGG-based evaluation metric is more objective than visual inspection but may introduce circularity.

- Suggested improvements: add a psychophysical study to validate perceptual quality and variability.

- Suggested improvements: quantify diversity across seeds using LPIPS/VGG-loss dispersion and patchwise nearest-neighbor analyses.

- Suggested improvements: provide runtime/memory benchmarks and explore efficient multi-scale implementations (dilations, FFT, hierarchical nets).

- Suggested improvements: equalize parameter counts/feature-map budgets for fairer comparisons and report sensitivity to number of maps.

- Suggested improvements: discuss theoretical links to scattering transforms and clarify when “training is not necessary.”

- Questions for authors: total number of textures used and consistency across seeds (report mean±std of VGG-loss).

- Questions for authors: sensitivity to filter count/scale choices and existence of a compute–quality Pareto frontier.

- Questions for authors: potential bias of VGG-based metric and correlation with human judgments.

- Questions for authors: can dilated convolutions or FFT-based filtering reproduce multi-scale benefits more efficiently?

- Questions for authors: primary failure cases and whether they involve non-stationarity or global structure.
