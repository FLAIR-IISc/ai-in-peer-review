1. Summary and contributions
The paper tackles a precise and timely question: which aspects of deep feature representations are actually necessary for high-quality natural texture synthesis? The authors present a compelling minimal baseline showing that a single-layer convolutional network with random filters (particularly in a multi-scale configuration) and Gram-matrix matching can synthesize textures rivaling the VGG-based state of the art. They perform careful ablations across filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), counts, sizes, and the presence of nonlinearities, and introduce a quantitative evaluation based on VGG Gram losses to move beyond purely qualitative inspection. The discussion thoughtfully situates findings in the MaxEnt framework and highlights the perceptual similarity vs. variability trade-off.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) Strong, surprising empirical result that challenges prevailing assumptions (depth and supervised pretraining are not indispensable for texture synthesis), supported by broad ablations. (ii) A simple, general baseline with practical value and clear insights (importance of multi-scale features and nonlinearity), likely to influence future work and evaluation practices.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem formulation is clear and well placed in the literature (Julesz, Portilla–Simoncelli, Gatys et al., GAN/PixelCNN context). The paper advances understanding by disentangling architectural depth, training, and pooling from the core Gram-statistics mechanism.
- Empirical evidence is extensive: comparisons across many single-layer variants, multi-scale vs. single-scale, and with/without ReLU. The observation that multi-scale random filters can match or exceed VGG on several textures is robust and practically important.
- The optimization analysis (L-BFGS, gradient scaling) clarifies discrepancies with prior reports on random networks and identifies optimization stability as a confounder in deep setups.
- The VGG-based evaluation metric is reasonable and more objective than ad hoc visual inspection, though it does introduce potential circularity.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help, not central to the decision)
- Add a small psychophysical study (e.g., 2AFC/ABX or inpainting discrimination) to validate perceptual quality and assess variability. Report inter-rater reliability.
- Quantify diversity across multiple seeds per texture using LPIPS, VGG-loss dispersion, and patchwise nearest-neighbor analyses; discuss the similarity–variability trade-off more concretely.
- Provide runtime/memory benchmarks and explore efficient multi-scale implementations (dilations, FFT, or hierarchical nets) for practical use.
- Calibrate fairness in comparisons: equalize parameter counts/feature-map budgets and report sensitivity to number of maps.
- Discuss theoretical links to scattering transforms and random features; clarify conditions under which “training is not necessary.”

Questions for the authors
- How many textures were used in total, and how consistent are results across seeds? Please report mean±std of VGG-loss over multiple syntheses.
- How sensitive are outcomes to filter count and scale choices? Is there a compute–quality Pareto frontier?
- Does the VGG-based metric bias against/for certain regular textures? Any human study correlating with this metric?
- Could dilated convolutions or FFT-based filtering reproduce the multi-scale benefits more efficiently?
- What are the primary failure cases, and do they systematically involve non-stationarity or global structure?
