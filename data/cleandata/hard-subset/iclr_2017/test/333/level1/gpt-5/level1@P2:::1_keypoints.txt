- Paper asks whether depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are necessary for high-quality texture synthesis.
- Main finding: single-layer CNNs with random filters, no pooling, and ReLU can synthesize high-quality textures when using multi-scale filters.
- Establishes a strong minimal baseline: Gram-matrix matching on a single-layer random CNN.
- Extensive comparisons across single-layer variants (random, Fourier, PCA, k-means) and across filter counts/sizes, including multi-scale.
- Introduces a quantitative evaluation protocol using average VGG Gram-matrix discrepancy across 5 layers as a surrogate metric.
- Documents when the VGG-based surrogate aligns with human perception and when it misaligns (notably for highly regular man-made textures).
- Ablations show indispensable elements: multi-scale filters and nonlinearity; more feature maps generally improve quality.
- Demonstrates that depth, pooling, and supervised pretraining are not necessary for good texture synthesis in many cases.
- Frames texture synthesis in a MaxEnt perspective and advocates treating constraint satisfaction within a small tolerance to balance fidelity and variability.
- Decision: Accept — contribution is novel and impactful, overturning common assumptions about required ingredients.
- Strong experimental rigor: careful, convincing ablations isolating filter scale, count, and nonlinearity effects.
- Optimization and setup are appropriate and explain prior negative results on random filters via loss/gradient scaling.
- Empirical support: qualitative results across many textures show multi-scale single-layer models often rival VGG-based synthesis.
- Caveat: evaluation relies mainly on VGG loss and visual inspection; no psychophysical user study was conducted.
- Some claims (e.g., hierarchical nets not improving quality but being faster) lack detailed experimental evidence in the main text.
- Practical concerns: computational cost of large spatial filters, with runtimes and memory profiles not reported.
- Variability and overfitting risk are discussed but not quantitatively measured or systematically analyzed.
- Fairness issue: comparisons could better control for number of constraints/feature maps across models.
- Suggested improvements: add aggregate quantitative summaries (medians, variances), report runtime/memory, and provide quality–compute trade-off curves.
- Suggested improvements: run a small psychophysical study (2AFC, inpainting) to validate the surrogate metric and assess perceptual variability.
- Suggested improvements: provide detailed multi-scale architecture specifications and sensitivity analyses for filter sizes and allocations.
- Suggested improvements: clarify VGG initialized from single-layer results with quantitative examples, explore alternative nonlinearities and pooling, and release code/data/seeds.
- Key open questions for authors include exact multi-scale configurations, scaling with feature count, robustness to initialization, quantitative hierarchical comparisons, diversity metrics, frequency of overfitting/copying, evaluation bias from VGG-loss, runtimes/GPU footprints, effects of different nonlinearities, and why random filters often match learned ones.
