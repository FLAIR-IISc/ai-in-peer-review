1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates which aspects of CNN-based texture synthesis are actually necessary for generating perceptually convincing natural textures. Contrary to prevailing assumptions, it shows that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised training of features are indispensable. The authors propose a minimal baseline: a single-layer CNN with ReLU nonlinearity and random filters, combined with Gram matrix matching of feature correlations. They further introduce a multi-scale version (multiple filter sizes in one layer) and demonstrate that this model often rivals a VGG-based multi-layer model (Gatys et al., 2015a) in visual quality. Extensive comparisons are provided across filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), numbers of features (363 vs. 3267 vs. 1024 multi-scale), and the presence/absence of nonlinearities. For evaluation, the paper uses the VGG-based texture loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity and discusses the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability, arguing for a small-mismatch view beyond strict maximum entropy formulations. The work is clearly written, and the experiments are carefully described.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and impactful finding that a strong minimal baseline (single-layer, random filters, Gram statistics, multi-scale) can produce textures comparable to state-of-the-art methods, challenging the assumed necessity of deep, trained features.
- Careful ablations identify the critical ingredients (multi-scale filters, sufficient number of feature maps, nonlinearity), offering value as a baseline and as guidance for future models.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The study is well positioned relative to Gatys et al. (2015a), Portilla & Simoncelli (2000), and subsequent improvements, and explicitly addresses a community-relevant question: which parts of the deep feature stack truly matter for synthesis? It also relates to MaxEnt framing and classical filter bank models.
- Technical soundness: The methodology is straightforward and replicable: Gram-matching objective, L-BFGS-B optimization with bounded pixel constraints, clear normalization of Gram distances, explicit convolutional settings, and standardized input preprocessing. The ablations (filter counts, sizes, types, ReLU vs. linear) are appropriate for isolating factors. The observation that multi-scale filters outperform any single scale is consistent with known multiresolution requirements in texture modeling.
- Empirical support: Visual results are convincing across a variety of textures; the multi-scale random network often rivals VGG-based results, particularly for highly regular textures. The VGG-based loss is a reasonable proxy for perceptual similarity in this subfield, and the paper documents cases where the proxy and perceptual judgments diverge (e.g., highly regular man-made textures), lending credibility to a nuanced interpretation of the metric. The analysis of optimization robustness (initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer results) is a useful practical insight.
- Clarity: The paper is generally clear, with well-defined models and evaluation criteria. The discussion appropriately circumscribes claims (e.g., acknowledging inefficiency of large filters and the lack of a variability metric).

Caveats (not decision-critical but relevant):
- Evaluation depends heavily on the VGG loss and visual inspection; there is no human study or formal perceptual evaluation. While standard in this area historically, this limits the strength of claims about perceptual quality.
- The “generalization” beyond MaxEnt is mostly conceptual, not formalized as a new theoretical result.
- Runtime and computational cost are discussed qualitatively; quantitative timings are missing.
- Texture diversity/variability is acknowledged as an open issue; no measurement is provided.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Include a small-scale human perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC discrimination or inpainting-based tests) to corroborate the VGG-loss findings and quantify gaps for regular textures.
- Quantify variability: report inter-sample distances (in VGG-loss and simple spectral metrics) over multiple seeds; visualize coverage of patch distributions to assess mode collapse vs. diversity.
- Provide runtime metrics and iteration counts for single- vs multi-scale models and for the VGG-based baseline, including GPU/CPU details.
- Expand ablations: curves of performance vs. number of features; comparisons across more filter sizes; power spectrum matching as a controlled add-on; robustness to input size and boundary conditions.
- Clarify multi-scale architecture details (exact sizes and counts of filters per scale, allocation of the 1024 feature maps).
- Strengthen connections to scattering transforms and classical multiscale filter banks; possibly include a scattering-based baseline for context.
- Improve citation formatting and clean up fragmented references.

Questions for the authors
- Multi-scale details: What exact filter sizes and per-scale feature counts are used in the multi-scale model? How sensitive are results to this allocation?
- Robustness: How consistent are results across random seeds (filters and initial noise)? Provide statistics over multiple runs per texture.
- Diversity: Can you quantify sample variability (e.g., distribution of VGG-loss among pairs of samples for the same texture, or spectral diversity)?
- Generalization: How do results scale with image size, and do very large textures reduce the tendency to “overfit” the constraints?
- Optimization: How many L-BFGS iterations are typically needed for convergence? What are typical runtimes relative to VGG-based synthesis?
- Baseline filters: How do scattering filters or steerable pyramids compare to the random multi-scale model when used with Gram matching?
- Failure modes: Beyond the shown regular man-made textures, are there specific categories (e.g., stochastic microtextures vs. structured macrotextures) where the approach consistently underperforms?
- ReLU necessity: Have you tested alternative nonlinearities (e.g., leaky ReLU, tanh, abs)? Do rectification or phase-sensitive features matter more?
- Availability: Will you release code and preconfigured models to facilitate adoption as a baseline?

Overall, the paper offers a clear, well-executed, and practically valuable minimal baseline that challenges common assumptions and will likely influence how future work evaluates the necessity of depth and training in texture synthesis.
