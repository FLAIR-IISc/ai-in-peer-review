- Main claim: High-quality texture synthesis can be achieved with a single convolutional layer of random filters plus Gram-matrix statistics when multi-scale filters and tuned optimization are used.
- Strong baseline: Single-layer CNNs with random, Fourier, k-means, or PCA filters are established, with random filters often performing best.
- Multi-scale necessity: Combining multiple spatial scales (3×3 to 55×55) is crucial; large receptive fields alone are insufficient.
- Nonlinearity importance: ReLU nonlinearity materially improves results and is implicated as a necessary ingredient.
- Empirical parity: A random multi-scale single-layer model often rivals or exceeds VGG-based (Gatys et al.) synthesis, especially on globally regular textures.
- Optimization role: Careful optimization (including gradient scaling to avoid L-BFGS-B early stopping) is critical and explains prior negative results with random networks.
- Quantitative proxy: VGG-19 Gram losses are proposed as a reasonable proxy for perceptual similarity, with acknowledgement of limitations.
- Variability trade-off: The paper discusses similarity vs. variability trade-offs and frames them via a MaxEnt perspective.
- Conceptual novelty: The finding challenges assumptions about the necessity of depth, pooling, and supervised training for texture synthesis.
- Ablation rigor: Careful ablations examine scale, number of maps, and nonlinearity, providing substantive community insight.
- Placement: Work is well-situated relative to parametric, deep, and fixed-filter approaches (Portilla–Simoncelli, Gatys et al., scattering transform).
- Honesty about paradox: Authors transparently investigate why random multi-scale sometimes yields lower VGG loss than VGG-optimized outputs.
- Evaluation caveat: Reliance on VGG-based loss risks circularity and may not reflect human preference; no user study was performed.
- Breadth limitation: Claims about non-necessity of depth/pooling/supervision are restricted to textures; broader texture coverage and failure-case analysis are limited.
- Variability not quantified: Sample diversity under fixed loss is not quantitatively assessed.
- Missing comparisons: Scattering transform and Portilla–Simoncelli baselines are only qualitatively referenced and need quantitative/visual comparisons.
- Efficiency concern: Multi-scale single-layer with large kernels is computationally heavy; concrete runtimes and memory use are not reported.
- Reproducibility sensitivity: Results may depend on maps count, filter normalization, and optimization hyperparameters; more seeds and sensitivity analysis are needed.
- Suggested additions: Provide explicit multi-scale architecture details, runtimes, memory, and filter normalization practices.
- Suggested experiments: Quantify variability (multiple samples per texture), add scattering and Portilla–Simoncelli baselines, and run a simple psychophysical pilot if feasible.
- Metric fairness: Clarify VGG synthesis hyperparameters/initialization and show effects of seeding VGG with random multi-scale outputs to disentangle optimization vs representation.
- Further ablations: Report effects of filter normalization, alternative nonlinearities, mean subtraction, and VGG metric variants (layers, pooling).
- Failure analysis: Curate and discuss textures where the method fails (nonstationary, perspective, semantic cues) and analyze missing scales/features.
- Reproducibility commitment: Release code and fixed random filters/seeds to facilitate replication.
- Key open questions for authors: exact multi-scale architecture and normalization; stability across random filter seeds; alternative optimizers and their effects; wall-clock time/memory comparisons to VGG; metric robustness across nets/layers; comparison to scattering-based synthesis; evidence about sample diversity and stopping-criteria trade-offs.
