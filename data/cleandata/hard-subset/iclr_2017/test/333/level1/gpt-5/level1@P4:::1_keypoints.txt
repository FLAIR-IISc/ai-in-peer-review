- Main claim: A single-layer CNN with random filters, ReLU nonlinearity, and Gram-matrix matching can synthesize perceptually convincing textures.  
- Key extension: A multi-scale single-layer variant (multiple filter sizes) often rivals the multi-layer VGG-based model.  
- Major finding: Depth, pooling, and supervised training are not strictly necessary for strong texture synthesis.  
- Proposed minimal baseline: Single-layer, random filters, Gram statistics, L-BFGS-B optimization, bounded pixels.  
- Ablations: Experiments vary filter banks (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), feature counts, filter sizes, and nonlinearities to isolate important factors.  
- Critical ingredients identified: Multi-scale filters, sufficient number of feature maps, and nonlinearity (ReLU).  
- Evaluation metric: Uses VGG-based texture loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual similarity.  
- Trade-off analysis: Argues for a small-mismatch view focusing on perceptual similarity vs. variability rather than strict MaxEnt approaches.  
- Empirical evidence: Visual results show multi-scale random networks often match VGG results, especially on regular textures.  
- Robustness insight: Initializing VGG synthesis from single-layer outputs can improve optimization robustness.  
- Positioning: Well situated relative to Gatys et al., Portilla & Simoncelli, classical filter banks, and MaxEnt framing.  
- Reproducibility: Methodology and preprocessing are clearly described and straightforward to replicate.  
- Clarity: Paper is clearly written with well-defined models and evaluation criteria.  
- Decision: Reviewer recommends accept due to novelty and impact of the minimal baseline finding.  
- Limitation: No human perceptual study or formal perceptual evaluation—reliance on VGG loss and visual inspection.  
- Theoretical caveat: Generalization beyond MaxEnt is conceptual and not formalized theoretically.  
- Missing metrics: Runtime and computational cost are discussed qualitatively but lack quantitative timings.  
- Variability gap: Texture diversity/variability is noted as an open issue with no quantitative measurement.  
- Suggested experiment: Add a small human perceptual evaluation (e.g., 2AFC) to validate VGG-loss conclusions.  
- Suggested analysis: Quantify sample variability (inter-sample VGG distances, spectral metrics) and show coverage/patch distribution.  
- Suggested reporting: Provide runtime metrics, iteration counts, and hardware details for single- vs multi-scale and VGG baselines.  
- Additional ablations: Performance vs. number of features curves, more filter sizes, power-spectrum matching, robustness to input size/boundaries.  
- Clarify architecture: Give exact filter sizes and per-scale feature allocations for the multi-scale model.  
- Comparative baseline: Strengthen links to scattering transforms and include scattering or steerable pyramid baselines.  
- Presentation fixes: Improve citation formatting and clean fragmented references.  
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to per-scale allocations, consistency across random seeds, quantified diversity, scaling with image size, typical L-BFGS iterations and runtimes.  
- More questions: How do scattering/steerable filters compare; which texture categories fail; have alternative nonlinearities been tested; will code/models be released?
