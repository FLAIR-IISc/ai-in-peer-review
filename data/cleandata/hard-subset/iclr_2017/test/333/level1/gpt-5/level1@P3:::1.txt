Summary and contributions
The paper investigates which aspects of deep feature representations matter for natural texture synthesis. It makes the striking claim that neither depth, pooling, nor supervised training are indispensable: high-quality textures can be synthesized using a single convolutional layer with random filters and Gram-matrix (second-order) statistics, provided optimization is tuned and (critically) multiple spatial scales are combined. The authors:
- Establish a strong, minimal baseline using single-layer CNNs with random, Fourier, k-means, or PCA filters, and demonstrate that random filters often perform best.
- Show that a random multi-scale single-layer architecture (3×3 to 55×55) with ReLUs produces textures often rivaling Gatys et al. (2015a).
- Provide ablations implicating multi-scale and nonlinearity as crucial, while large receptive fields alone are insufficient.
- Propose a quantitative evaluation via VGG-19 Gram losses as a proxy for perceptual similarity, and discuss the trade-off between similarity and variability through a MaxEnt perspective.
This is a clear, provocative, and well-executed empirical study that challenges common beliefs about the necessity of depth and supervised features for texture synthesis.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Conceptual novelty and empirical evidence that a simple, random, single-layer multi-scale model with Gram statistics can rival SoTA is both surprising and impactful; (2) Careful ablations (scale, number of maps, nonlinearity) and a thoughtful discussion of evaluation/variability offer substantive insight for the community.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-situated within parametric (Portilla & Simoncelli; Heeger & Bergen) and deep learning–based approaches (Gatys et al., Johnson et al., Ulyanov et al.), and connects to fixed-filter approaches (scattering transform). It directly addresses an important open question: which ingredients actually matter for texture synthesis?
- Methodology: The architecture definition is clear; the Gram-based loss is standard and well-justified for stationary texture statistics. The multi-scale design is convincingly motivated by ablations showing small scales model color/marginals, mid scales capture local structures, and large scales capture long-range organization; combining them is necessary. The importance of ReLU is demonstrated.
- Empirical support: Visual quality is strong across diverse textures, and the multi-scale random model often matches or exceeds VGG-based synthesis in perceived structure, especially for globally regular textures. The paper acknowledges and explores the “paradox” that multi-scale random can achieve lower VGG loss than the VGG-optimized output, attributing it to optimization and reduced variability—this is honest and informative.
- Rigor and caveats: The quantitative evaluation via VGG losses is reasonable given prevailing practice (and probably better than SSIM or pixel distances), but it is not definitive. The authors are transparent about its limitations and discuss the need for variability metrics. The optimization details (e.g., gradient scaling to avoid L-BFGS-B early stopping) are important and plausibly explain prior negative results with random networks.

Limitations and concerns (do not outweigh strengths)
- Evaluation: Reliance on VGG-based loss risks circularity and may not capture human preference for certain regular textures; no user study. The claim “none of depth, pooling, or supervised training are indispensable” is supported for textures, but breadth of textures and systematic failure-case analysis could be expanded. Variability is not quantitatively assessed (e.g., sample diversity under fixed loss).
- Comparisons: Scattering transform–based synthesis (Bruna & Mallat) and classical Portilla–Simoncelli are only qualitatively referenced; a more thorough quantitative/visual comparison would strengthen the case, given those are single/fixed-filter paradigms too.
- Efficiency: The proposed multi-scale single-layer with large kernels is computationally heavy; while the authors note hierarchical efficiency gains without quality improvement, concrete timings/complexity would be useful.
- Reproducibility and sensitivity: Conclusions may depend on number of feature maps, filter normalization, and optimization hyperparameters; more systematic sensitivity analysis and reporting multiple random seeds per texture would improve confidence.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Provide explicit multi-scale architecture details: filter sizes used, maps per scale, normalization of filters, and how feature map counts relate to quality. Include runtimes and memory for representative resolutions.
- Quantify variability: For each texture, generate multiple samples and report distribution of VGG losses and pairwise distances in VGG space; if feasible, include a simple psychophysical pilot (2AFC on inpainting or discrimination).
- Baselines: Add scattering transform–based synthesis results and a more systematic comparison to Portilla–Simoncelli on the same set.
- Fairness of VGG comparison: Clarify whether the VGG synthesis used standard hyperparameters, stopping criteria, and initialization; show that seeding VGG with your random multi-scale outputs consistently improves results to disentangle optimization from representational capacity.
- Normalization and nonlinearity: Report ablations on filter normalization, alternative nonlinearities (leaky ReLU, ELU), and the effect of removing mean subtraction. Include an experiment with max vs average pooling in the VGG evaluator to test metric robustness.
- Failure analysis: Curate and discuss textures where the method fails (e.g., nonstationary, perspective cues, strong semantics), and analyze which scales/features are lacking.
- Code and seeds: Release code and fixed random filters to facilitate replication.

Questions for the authors
1) Multi-scale design: What exact filter sizes and numbers of feature maps per scale were used in the best-performing model? Were filters per scale normalized (e.g., unit norm)? How sensitive is quality to these choices?
2) Randomness and stability: How much do results vary across different random initializations of filters? Can you report mean/variance of the VGG-loss across, say, 5–10 different filter samplings and synthesis runs per texture?
3) Optimization details: The 1e7 loss/gradient scaling to prevent L-BFGS-B early stopping is unusual. Did you try alternative optimizers (Adam/L-BFGS without scaling) and different iteration budgets? How do these choices affect the perceived gap between random and trained filters?
4) Efficiency: What are the wall-clock times and memory footprints for single-scale (11×11) and multi-scale (up to 55×55) models at typical resolutions, and how do they compare to VGG-based synthesis?
5) Metric robustness: If you evaluate using variants of the VGG metric (different layers, max pooling, other pretrained nets), do the relative conclusions hold?
6) Scattering/wavelet baselines: How does your random multi-scale compare to scattering transform–based synthesis when both are optimized with Gram constraints? Could your strong performance be attributed to random features approximating a sufficiently rich multiscale directional bank?
7) Variability: Can you provide evidence that your method’s sample diversity is not collapsing (e.g., nearest-neighbor distance to the reference in VGG space versus VGG-based synthesis), and discuss stopping criteria that trade off similarity vs variability?

Overall, this paper delivers a clear and thought-provoking message with solid empirical support and valuable discussion. With minor additions to evaluation and clarity, it would make a strong contribution.
