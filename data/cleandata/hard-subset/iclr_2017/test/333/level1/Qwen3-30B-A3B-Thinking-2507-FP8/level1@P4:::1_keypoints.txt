- Challenges the need for deep, pre-trained hierarchical representations for texture synthesis by proposing a single-layer CNN with random, data-independent filters.
- Systematically evaluates various single-layer architectures (different filter sizes and types: random, hand-crafted, unsupervised) against the VGG-based Gatys et al. baseline.
- Demonstrates that high-perceptual-quality natural textures can be synthesized without depth, pooling, or supervised feature training.
- Introduces a quantitative texture-quality measure based on the VGG synthesis loss to complement human evaluations.
- Decision: accept with minor revisions.
- Experimental design is rigorous with multiple ablations isolating key factors.
- Empirical results show single-layer random-filter networks often match or surpass the VGG-based model; number of feature maps is more important than filter training.
- The multi-scale model (combining different filter sizes) better captures short-, mid-, and long-range correlations and outperforms single-scale models.
- Both qualitative human evaluation and quantitative VGG-loss metrics support the claims, though the VGG loss has some perceptual limitations.
- Suggest adding quantitative comparisons of computational efficiency and runtime between single-layer and deep models.
- Recommend a deeper theoretical or empirical exploration of why random filters perform comparably to trained filters.
- Advise comparison to more recent texture-synthesis methods (2016–2017) to better position the contribution.
- Recommend a more detailed analysis of variability versus perceptual similarity in synthesized textures.
- Questions for authors: explain why random filters work, clarify limitations and possible improvements of the VGG-based perceptual metric, and discuss whether the multi-scale approach generalizes beyond texture synthesis.
