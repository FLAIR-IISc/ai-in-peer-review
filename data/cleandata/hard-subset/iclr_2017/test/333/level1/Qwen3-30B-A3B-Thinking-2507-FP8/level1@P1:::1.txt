

# Peer Review for "What Does It Take To Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of deep learning for natural image generation by challenging fundamental assumptions about the necessity of deep hierarchical representations for texture synthesis. The authors convincingly demonstrate that high-quality natural textures can be generated using a single-layer convolutional neural network with random filters, without pooling or supervised training. Their main contribution lies in providing a strong minimal baseline that shows depth, pooling, and pre-training are not indispensable for natural texture synthesis. The paper systematically evaluates various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA) and demonstrates that random filters can rival or even surpass the quality of state-of-the-art multi-layer approaches. The authors also introduce a quantitative evaluation metric based on VGG-based loss and provide insightful analysis of the importance of filter scale, number of feature maps, and non-linearities. This work represents an important step toward understanding what aspects of deep networks are truly essential for natural image generation.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

Key reasons: (1) The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging a fundamental assumption in the field (that deep hierarchical representations are necessary for natural texture synthesis), and (2) the empirical evidence provided strongly supports their claims through comprehensive experiments and a thoughtful quantitative evaluation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main claim is well-supported by its experimental results. The visual comparisons (Figure 1) and quantitative evaluation (Figure 4) clearly demonstrate that single-layer networks with random filters can generate textures of comparable or superior perceptual quality to the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach. The authors provide a thorough ablation study showing that the number of feature maps, filter scale, and non-linearities are crucial components, while depth and pre-training are not.

The paper's contribution is particularly valuable because it addresses a fundamental question about the role of deep networks in natural image generation. Previous work (Gatys et al., 2015) suggested that multi-layer representations were necessary, but this paper shows that the core mechanism (Gram matrices of feature activations) works even with random filters in a single layer. This insight has broader implications for understanding what aspects of deep learning truly matter for natural image generation, beyond just texture synthesis.

The quantitative evaluation using VGG-based loss is a notable methodological contribution that provides an objective metric to supplement the traditional human evaluation. While the authors acknowledge this metric is imperfect, it provides a useful proxy for perceptual quality and enables systematic comparison across different models.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, building directly on Gatys et al. (2015) while challenging its foundational assumptions. The authors carefully acknowledge related work and position their contribution as a critical test of the assumptions underlying previous approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance its quality:

1. The paper mentions that "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" explains the discrepancy with Gatys et al.'s claim that random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images. Please provide specific details about this tuning (e.g., number of iterations, L-BFGS-B parameters, scaling factors) to help readers reproduce your results.

2. Include a human evaluation study to complement the quantitative VGG-based loss metric. While the VGG-based loss is useful, human perception remains the ultimate measure for texture synthesis. A small-scale human study comparing the different models would strengthen the perceptual quality claims.

3. The paper mentions that multi-scale models outperform single-scale models, but doesn't fully explain why the combination of filter sizes is important. Consider adding a visualization showing how different filter sizes capture different spatial structures in the texture (e.g., small stones vs. large patterns).

4. The paper acknowledges that the VGG-based loss sometimes fails for highly regular textures (like walls and grids). Discuss this limitation more thoroughly and consider providing a small set of examples where the VGG-based loss doesn't correlate with human perception.

5. The paper mentions that "the combination of different scales in the multi-scale network is important" but doesn't test whether a multi-layer network with random filters would also benefit from multi-scale filtering. This would provide an even stronger test of the authors' claims.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." However, the training procedure for the k-means filters is not directly comparable to the random initialization used in the VGG network. Could you clarify how the training procedures differ and why this might explain the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015)?

2. The paper mentions that "the multi-scale model clearly outperforms the single-scale models on all textures." However, Figure 4 shows some textures where the VGG-based model appears to produce better samples than the multi-scale random model. Could you elaborate on these specific cases and explain why the multi-scale model might not be superior in these instances?

3. The paper states that "the naive synthesis performed here favors images that are perceptually similar to the reference texture and thus looses variability." How do you reconcile this with the fact that the multi-scale model often produces more visually appealing results? Is there a specific aspect of the optimization that better balances perceptual similarity and variability?

4. The paper claims that "the number of feature maps generally increases the perceptual quality of the generated textures." However, Figure 1 shows that the Random-363 model sometimes produces better textures than Random-3267. Could you explain this apparent contradiction and whether there's an optimal number of feature maps for different textures?
