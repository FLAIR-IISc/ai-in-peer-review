- Claim: single-layer convolutional networks with random filters can synthesize high-quality natural textures, challenging Gatys et al.  
- Contributions: comprehensive comparison of single-layer filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA), a multi-scale model, and a VGG-based quantitative evaluation metric.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary critique: evidence does not sufficiently support the main claim due to flawed evaluation.  
- Reliance solely on VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric without human evaluation; questionable correlation with perceptual quality.  
- Authors admit VGG-loss fails to capture perceptual advantages for some textures (e.g., wall, grids), directly contradicting conclusions.  
- No human perceptual study provided, so claims of “high perceptual quality” are unvalidated.  
- Insufficient details on the “elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure” claimed to enable random networks.  
- Failure to reconcile or adequately compare results with Gatys et al. (2015a), which reported random networks fail to produce perceptually interesting images.  
- Lack of balanced analysis of where the method succeeds versus fails and missing examples of failure cases.  
- Computational efficiency concerns are noted but not thoroughly addressed; contradictory statements about hierarchical nets.  
- Suggested improvements: conduct human evaluations, detail optimization/tuning steps, include failure analysis, and strengthen theoretical links to the Maximum Entropy framework.  
- Key questions for authors: exact optimization tuning differences from Gatys et al., concrete failure examples and explanations, why results differ from Gatys et al., and justification that VGG-loss reflects human perception.
