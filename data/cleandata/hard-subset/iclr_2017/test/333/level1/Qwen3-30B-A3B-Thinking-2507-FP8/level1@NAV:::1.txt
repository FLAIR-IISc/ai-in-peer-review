

This paper makes a compelling contribution by challenging fundamental assumptions in deep learning-based texture synthesis. The authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, no pooling, and without supervised training on natural images—contrary to prevailing beliefs that hierarchical representations and pre-trained features are essential. Their key insight is that the depth and training of multi-layer networks are not indispensable, as a minimal single-layer architecture with random filters can rival state-of-the-art results. The paper provides a thorough empirical comparison across multiple single-layer architectures (including Fourier, K-means, and random filters) and introduces a quantitative evaluation method using the VGG-based loss, moving beyond subjective human inspection.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's central claim is well-supported by rigorous experiments: (1) random single-layer networks achieve perceptual quality comparable to multi-layer VGG-based approaches, and (2) the multi-scale variant specifically outperforms single-scale models by capturing diverse spatial correlations. The findings fundamentally challenge assumptions in the field and provide valuable insights into what aspects of deep networks are truly necessary for texture generation.

The paper's strength lies in its clear experimental design and thoughtful analysis of filter size, network depth, and optimization procedures. The authors successfully demonstrate that the critical factors are the number of feature maps and the multi-scale approach, not network depth or filter training. The quantitative evaluation method is a notable improvement over typical qualitative assessments.

For improvement, I suggest: (1) providing more detail on the optimization procedure's sensitivity to hyperparameters, as the paper mentions "elaborate tuning" but doesn't specify which parameters were adjusted; (2) including ablation studies on the impact of different filter types for varying texture complexities. 

I'd appreciate clarification on whether the success of random filters generalizes to other texture synthesis tasks beyond the specific examples shown, and whether the multi-scale approach could be implemented more efficiently (e.g., using dilated convolutions) to address the computational inefficiency concern raised in the discussion.
