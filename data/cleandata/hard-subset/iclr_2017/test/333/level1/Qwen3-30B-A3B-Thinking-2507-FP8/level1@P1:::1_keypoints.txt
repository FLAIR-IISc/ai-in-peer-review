- Main claim: single-layer CNNs with random filters (no pooling or pre-training) can generate high-quality natural textures, challenging the need for deep hierarchies.  
- Contribution: provides a minimal baseline showing depth, pooling, and pre-training are not essential for texture synthesis.  
- Evaluation: systematically compares single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier, k-means, PCA).  
- Key result: random filters can rival or surpass state-of-the-art multi-layer approaches for many textures.  
- Metric: introduces a VGG-based quantitative evaluation as a proxy for perceptual quality.  
- Analysis: examines the importance of filter scale, number of feature maps, and non-linearities.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is significant for challenging a core assumption and is strongly supported by experiments.  
- Evidence: visual comparisons and quantitative results clearly support the main claim; thorough ablation studies back up component importance.  
- Positioning: effectively builds on and challenges Gatys et al. (2015) and situates the work within related literature.  
- Caveat on metric: VGG-based loss is useful but imperfect and can fail for highly regular textures.  
- Reproducibility request: provide specific optimization/tuning details (iterations, L-BFGS-B params, scaling factors) to explain discrepancies with prior work.  
- Human evaluation: recommend adding a human perceptual study to complement the VGG-based metric.  
- Multi-scale explanation: ask for visualization/analysis showing why combining filter sizes captures different spatial structures.  
- Failure cases: request examples and discussion where VGG-based loss does not correlate with human perception (e.g., walls, grids).  
- Additional experiment: suggest testing whether multi-layer networks with random filters also benefit from multi-scale filtering.  
- Questions for authors: clarify k-means vs random filter training procedures; explain textures where VGG-based model outperforms the multi-scale random model; reconcile naive synthesis’s reduced variability with multi-scale visual appeal; explain why fewer feature maps sometimes outperform many and whether optimal counts vary by texture.
