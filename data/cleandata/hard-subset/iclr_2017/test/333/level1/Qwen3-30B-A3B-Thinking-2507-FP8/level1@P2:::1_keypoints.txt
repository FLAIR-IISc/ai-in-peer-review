- Contribution: Single-layer CNN with random, data-independent filters can generate high-quality natural textures, challenging the need for deep supervised networks.  
- Empirical finding: Comprehensive experiments show random filters often outperform trained filters, and a multi-scale model rivals state-of-the-art methods.  
- Filter/architecture study: Systematic comparisons of Fourier, K-means, PCA, and random filters plus single- vs. multi-scale architectures with ablations on size, feature maps, and non-linearities.  
- Quantitative metric: Introduces a VGG-based loss as an objective measure of texture quality complementary to human inspection.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends acceptance due to the novel, counterintuitive result and rigorous empirical support.  
- Interpretation: Results prompt rethinking of which aspects of deep representations are crucial for natural image generation.  
- Limitations noted: Multi-scale model is computationally inefficient; trade-offs exist between perceptual similarity and variability.  
- Writing and presentation: Paper is well-written, logically organized, and figures effectively illustrate findings.  
- Request for theory: Reviewer asks for a deeper theoretical explanation of why random filters work and links to natural texture statistics.  
- Request for analysis: Wants more detailed analysis of when and why random filters outperform trained ones across texture types.  
- Comparative context: Suggests adding comparisons with more recent texture synthesis methods beyond Gatys et al.  
- Clarification needed: Ask authors for specific examples where random filters beat trained filters and explanations.  
- Architecture question: Request elaboration on how the multi-scale design improves quality versus single-scale models with larger receptive fields.  
- Theory question: Seeks the theoretical basis for Gram matrix effectiveness with random filters and connection to the MaxEnt framework.  
- Evaluation question: Asks how VGG-based loss correlates with human perception and whether human studies were conducted to validate it.
