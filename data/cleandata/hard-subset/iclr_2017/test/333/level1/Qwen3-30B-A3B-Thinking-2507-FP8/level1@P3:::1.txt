

# Peer Review: "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a bold claim: natural texture synthesis does not require deep hierarchical representations or supervisedly trained features. The authors demonstrate that high-quality natural textures can be synthesized using a single-layer convolutional network with random filters, challenging the conventional wisdom established by Gatys et al. (2015a). They present a comprehensive experimental comparison of various single-layer architectures with different filter types (random, Fourier-based, k-means, PCA) against the state-of-the-art VGG-based model, and introduce a multi-scale model that combines filters of different sizes. The paper's main contributions include establishing a minimal baseline for texture synthesis, showing that depth and pre-training are not indispensable, and proposing a quantitative evaluation metric using VGG-based loss.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

Key reasons: The paper's main claim is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the evaluation methodology has significant limitations that undermine the validity of their conclusions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim—that "natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated from networks with only a single layer, no pooling and random filters"—is not adequately supported by the evidence presented. The primary issue lies in the evaluation methodology. The authors rely exclusively on the VGG-based loss (equation 7) as a quantitative measure of texture quality, claiming it "is an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" between reference and synthesized textures. However, they acknowledge this metric "fails to capture the perceptual advantage of the multi-scale synthesis" for certain textures (e.g., wall, grids), which directly contradicts their main claim.

The paper fails to provide any human evaluation to validate that lower VGG-based loss actually corresponds to higher perceptual quality. Without human assessment, the authors cannot claim their synthesized textures have "high perceptual quality," as the standard for texture synthesis is human perception. The paper's own Figure 4 shows perceptual differences between samples, yet they don't quantify these differences through human evaluation.

Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address the discrepancy with Gatys et al. (2015a), who reported that networks with random weights fail to generate perceptually interesting images. The authors suggest this difference "originates from a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" but provide no specific details about what this tuning entails or how it differs from standard practices. Without this crucial information, the paper cannot convincingly demonstrate that their approach is fundamentally different from the one previously tested.

The paper also fails to provide a balanced analysis of when their approach works well versus when it fails, and doesn't adequately address computational efficiency concerns (noting that "a more efficient way to achieve receptive fields of similar size would be to use a hierarchical multi-layer net" but then stating that hierarchical architectures don't improve quality).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Human evaluation**: The most critical improvement would be to conduct a small-scale human evaluation comparing the perceptual quality of textures generated by the proposed method versus the state-of-the-art. This would validate whether the VGG-based loss actually correlates with human perception.

- **Optimization details**: Provide specific details about the "elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" that enables random networks to work. What parameters were changed from standard practice, and why?

- **Failure analysis**: Include a detailed analysis of cases where the single-layer random filter approach fails to produce good results, along with explanations for these failures.

- **Theoretical connection**: Strengthen the connection between the empirical results and the Maximum Entropy framework discussed in the paper. How do these findings advance our understanding of texture modeling in this theoretical framework?

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How exactly does your "elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" differ from the standard approach used in Gatys et al. (2015a)? What specific parameters were adjusted and why?

2. Can you provide examples of textures where your method fails to produce good results, and explain why these textures are particularly challenging for your approach?

3. How do you explain the discrepancy between your results and those reported in Gatys et al. (2015a) regarding the failure of random networks? Is this a fundamental difference in the approach or simply due to optimization details?

4. Given that you acknowledge the VGG-based loss "fails to capture the perceptual advantage" for some textures, how can you be confident that your method truly produces "high perceptual quality" textures for the majority of cases?
