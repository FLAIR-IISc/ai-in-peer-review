

# Review of "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of texture synthesis by challenging a fundamental assumption about deep neural networks in natural image generation. The authors demonstrate that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated from networks with only a single layer, no pooling, and random filters—contradicting the prevailing belief that hierarchical multi-layer representations and supervised training are necessary for natural texture synthesis. The paper presents a strong minimal baseline that relies solely on a single-layer network with random, data-independent filters. Through comprehensive experiments comparing various filter types (Fourier, K-means, PCA, random) and architectures (single-scale vs. multi-scale), the authors show that random filters often outperform trained filters, and that the multi-scale model frequently rivals state-of-the-art approaches. The paper also introduces a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based loss, providing a valuable alternative to purely qualitative human evaluation.

## 2. Decision
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper addresses an important question with a novel and counterintuitive finding that challenges established assumptions in the field, and (2) the empirical evidence is scientifically rigorous, well-designed, and thoroughly supported by comprehensive experiments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's central contribution—demonstrating that random filters in a single-layer CNN can generate high-quality textures—is compelling and well-supported. This challenges the common belief that deep networks with supervised training are essential for natural image generation, a claim that has been widely accepted since Gatys et al. (2015a). The authors provide a thorough experimental evaluation that systematically tests different filter types and architectures, including ablation studies on filter size, number of feature maps, and the importance of non-linearities.

The methodology is sound and well-justified. The use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure of texture quality is particularly valuable, as it provides a more objective evaluation metric than human inspection alone. The paper's comparison between the single-layer random filter model and the state-of-the-art VGG-based model is thorough and convincing, with multiple examples across different texture types. The finding that the multi-scale model outperforms single-scale models is well-supported by the ablation study in Figure 2, which demonstrates that combining different filter scales is crucial for capturing both small- and large-scale structures.

The paper is well-written and logically organized, with clear explanations of the methodology and results. The figures effectively illustrate the key findings, and the discussion of the implications for understanding what aspects of deep representations are crucial for natural image generation is insightful. The authors also thoughtfully address the limitations of their approach, including the computational inefficiency of the multi-scale model and the balance between perceptual similarity and variability.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, a few additions would further enhance its contribution:
- A more detailed theoretical explanation for why random filters work so well would strengthen the paper. Is there a connection between the statistical properties of random filters and the statistics of natural textures?
- The paper mentions that random filters sometimes outperform trained filters, but a more detailed analysis of when and why this occurs would be valuable. For example, do certain texture types benefit more from random filters than others?
- Including a comparison with other recent texture synthesis approaches (beyond Gatys et al.) would provide better context for the paper's contribution within the current landscape of texture synthesis methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In Figure 1, the textures generated from random filters often appear to be of high quality. Can you provide more specific examples where random filters outperform trained filters, and explain why this might be the case?
2. The paper mentions that the multi-scale model's success is due to the combination of different scales, but could you elaborate on how the multi-scale architecture specifically contributes to improved quality compared to single-scale models with larger receptive fields?
3. The authors suggest that the Gram matrix approach works well with random filters, but what is the theoretical basis for this? Is there a connection to the MaxEnt framework that the authors discuss in Section 7?
4. The paper uses VGG-based loss as a quantitative measure, but how does this correlate with human perception? Have you conducted any human evaluation studies to validate this metric beyond the qualitative comparisons presented?
