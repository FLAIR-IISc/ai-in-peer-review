

# Review for "What Does It Take to Generate Natural Textures?"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper makes a significant contribution by challenging the prevailing assumption that deep hierarchical representations with pre-trained features are essential for natural texture synthesis. The authors present a minimal baseline for texture synthesis that relies solely on a single-layer convolutional network with random, data-independent filters. They systematically test various single-layer architectures with different filter sizes and types (random, hand-crafted, unsupervisedly learned) against the state-of-the-art VGG-based model by Gatys et al. (2015a). The paper demonstrates that natural textures of high perceptual quality can be generated without depth, pooling, or supervised training of features. The authors further introduce a quantitative texture quality measure based on the VGG-based synthesis loss, providing an objective complement to traditional human inspection evaluations.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a clear and important question in the field of deep learning for image generation, making a strong case for reevaluating the necessity of deep hierarchical representations in texture synthesis. The experimental design is scientifically rigorous, with multiple ablation studies to isolate key factors. The authors conduct comprehensive comparisons across a wide range of single-layer architectures (including Fourier-based, K-means, and random filters with varying numbers of feature maps) against the state-of-the-art VGG model.

The empirical results are compelling: textures synthesized from single-layer networks with random filters often rival or exceed the perceptual quality of the VGG-based model, despite the latter's reliance on deep, pre-trained features. The paper provides a thorough analysis of why random filters work well, demonstrating that the number of feature maps (rather than their training) is critical for high-quality synthesis. The multi-scale model, which combines filters of different sizes, further demonstrates superior performance by capturing small-, mid-, and long-range correlations simultaneously.

The paper's contribution is well-supported by both qualitative human evaluation and quantitative metrics (VGG-based loss), with the latter providing a valuable complement to traditional qualitative assessment. The authors clearly demonstrate that neither depth nor pre-training is indispensable for natural texture synthesis, which represents a fundamental shift in understanding what aspects of deep networks are truly necessary for natural image generation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could provide more detailed discussion of the computational efficiency trade-offs between single-layer and multi-layer models. While the authors mention that deeper networks are faster but don't improve quality, a quantitative comparison of runtime would strengthen this point.
- The authors might benefit from a more thorough exploration of why random filters perform as well as trained filters, which could provide deeper insights into visual representation learning.
- The paper could be strengthened by comparing against more recent texture synthesis methods (2016-2017) beyond the VGG baseline to better position their contribution within the current literature.
- The discussion of variability in synthesized textures (Section 7) is intriguing but somewhat superficial; a more detailed exploration of how to balance perceptual similarity and variability would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "random filters perform on par or better than filters that have been trained on the statistics of natural images." Could the authors provide more insight into why this might be the case, given that the filters are data-independent? Is there a theoretical explanation for this counterintuitive finding?

2. The authors note that "the VGG-based loss appears to generally be an acceptable approximation of the perceptual differences" but fails for some regular textures like walls and grids. Could the authors elaborate on the limitations of this metric as a perceptual measure and suggest potential improvements?

3. The paper demonstrates that the multi-scale model outperforms single-scale models across all textures. How do the authors explain the specific improvement from combining filters of different sizes, and would this approach generalize to other image generation tasks beyond texture synthesis?
