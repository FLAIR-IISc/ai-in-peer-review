- Challenges prevailing assumption: high-quality natural textures can be generated with a single-layer convolutional network using random filters, no pooling, and no supervised training.  
- Key insight: network depth and supervised filter training are not essential; a minimal single-layer architecture can rival state-of-the-art methods.  
- Thorough empirical comparison across single-layer architectures (Fourier, K-means, random) supports the claim.  
- Introduces a quantitative evaluation using a VGG-based loss, improving over purely qualitative assessments.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Experimental support: (1) random single-layer networks match perceptual quality of multi-layer VGG approaches; (2) multi-scale variants outperform single-scale by capturing diverse spatial correlations.  
- Contributions: challenges field assumptions and clarifies that number of feature maps and multi-scale design, not depth or filter training, are the critical factors.  
- Strengths: clear experimental design and careful analysis of filter size, depth, and optimization.  
- Improvement request: provide more detail on optimization procedure sensitivity to hyperparameters and specify which parameters were tuned.  
- Improvement request: include ablation studies on how different filter types affect textures of varying complexity.  
- Clarification sought: does the success of random filters generalize to other texture synthesis tasks beyond presented examples?  
- Efficiency question: could the multi-scale approach be implemented more efficiently (e.g., dilated convolutions) to mitigate computational inefficiency?
