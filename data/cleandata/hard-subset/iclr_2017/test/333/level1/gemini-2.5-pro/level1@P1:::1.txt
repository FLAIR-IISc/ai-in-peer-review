### **Review**

**1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions**

This paper investigates the minimal architectural requirements for high-quality parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. The work challenges the prevailing assumption, established by the state-of-the-art method of Gatys et al. (2015a), that deep, multi-layer architectures with features learned from large-scale supervised training (e.g., VGG on ImageNet) are necessary for this task. The authors' central contribution is to demonstrate, compellingly, that a very simple model—a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters—can generate textures of surprisingly high perceptual quality.

The key claims are:
*   Neither network depth, pooling layers, nor supervisedly pre-trained filters are indispensable for synthesizing natural textures.
*   A "minimal baseline" model consisting of a single layer of random multi-scale filters and a ReLU non-linearity can generate textures that often rival, and in some cases exceed, the quality of those produced by the 19-layer VGG network.
*   The paper systematically explores a range of single-layer architectures, comparing random filters against hand-crafted (Fourier) and unsupervisedly learned (K-means, PCA) filters, concluding that random filters are highly effective.
*   It introduces a novel quantitative metric for texture quality—the reconstruction loss of the VGG-based model—as a more objective alternative to purely subjective human evaluation.
*   The paper provides an insightful discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability in texture synthesis, framing it within a generalized maximum entropy framework for finite-sized images.

**2. Decision and key reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant and Surprising Finding:** The paper's core result—that a shallow network with random weights can generate high-quality textures—is a significant and counter-intuitive finding. It fundamentally reframes our understanding of what components are essential for neural texture synthesis, providing a much simpler and more elegant baseline that challenges the community's assumptions about the necessity of deep, pre-trained representations for this specific task.
2.  **Rigorous and Thorough Experimentation:** The authors support their claims with a comprehensive set of experiments. They systematically ablate key architectural components (depth, pooling, filter type), compare a wide variety of single-layer models, and cleverly isolate the importance of multi-scale filters and non-linearities. The use of a VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric, coupled with a candid discussion of its limitations, adds a layer of scientific rigor that strengthens the paper's conclusions.

**3. Supporting arguments for the decision**

The strength of this paper lies in its focused and effective deconstruction of a state-of-the-art model. By asking "what is truly necessary?", the authors provide a valuable insight into the nature of texture modeling.

The prior work by Gatys et al. (2015a) was groundbreaking, but it conflated several factors: a deep hierarchical architecture, ReLU non-linearities, pooling operations, and features learned on ImageNet. This paper successfully disentangles these factors for texture synthesis. The visual results presented in Figures 1 and 4 are compelling. The "Random multi-scale" model, in particular, produces textures (e.g., the brick wall, the grids) that are perceptually as good or even better than the VGG-based model, which directly supports the main claim. The authors' explanation for why previous attempts with random networks may have failed (sub-optimal optimization procedure, which they fixed by scaling the loss) is a crucial and credible piece of the puzzle.

The experimental design is very strong. The comparison in Figure 2, which tests single-scale random models with varying filter sizes, provides a clean and convincing demonstration that combining multiple scales is key to capturing both fine-grained detail and long-range structure. This is a much stronger argument than simply showing the multi-scale model works well. Similarly, the inclusion of the "no ReLU" control case cleanly demonstrates the necessity of the non-linearity.

Finally, the discussion in Section 7 elevates the paper from a simple empirical study to a more thoughtful contribution. The authors correctly identify the crucial trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability and discuss the limitations of the maximum entropy framework for finite-size images. Proposing a more general framework based on a perceptual distance metric and an epsilon-environment is a forward-looking contribution that will likely stimulate further research.

**4. Additional feedback and questions**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Clarity on the Multi-Scale Model:** The description of the "Random multi-scale" model is brief. It would be helpful to specify how the 1024 filters are constructed and distributed across the different scales (e.g., 3x3, 11x11, up to 55x55). Are the outputs of different filter scales simply concatenated before computing the Gram matrix? A more precise definition would improve reproducibility.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** The authors correctly note that a deep architecture is a more efficient way to achieve large receptive fields than using large convolutional filters. It would strengthen this point to provide some quantitative evidence. For instance, a simple comparison of the synthesis time (or FLOPS) for the 55x55 single-layer model versus a multi-layer equivalent that achieves a similar receptive field would make the trade-off between model simplicity and computational efficiency concrete.
*   **Highlighting Failure Cases:** The paper shows many impressive successes. However, a scientific paper is often strengthened by also showing the limitations of its proposed method. Are there specific types of textures (perhaps those with more semantic, non-repeating structures) where the random multi-scale model clearly fails while the VGG-based model succeeds? Showing such a comparative failure case would provide a more nuanced understanding of where the learned features of VGG are still indispensable.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  The scaling of the loss and gradients by 10^7 is presented as a key factor for successful optimization. Is the synthesis result sensitive to this specific value? Could you elaborate on how this hyperparameter was chosen and what happens if it is significantly smaller or larger?
2.  In the discussion, you mention finding anecdotal evidence of deeper multi-layer random CNNs exactly reconstructing the reference texture. This is a fascinating observation about overfitting in the summary statistics space. Could you provide an example of this in the appendix? It would be a powerful illustration of the point you make about the balance between similarity and variability.
3.  Have you considered the effect of the number of random filters? The paper contrasts 363 with 3267, showing that more is generally better. Is there a point of diminishing returns? How does the performance of the single-scale 11x11 model change as the number of random filters approaches that of the layers in the VGG model?
