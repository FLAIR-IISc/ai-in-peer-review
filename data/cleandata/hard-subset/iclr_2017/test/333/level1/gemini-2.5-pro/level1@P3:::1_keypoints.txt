- Paper shows a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters can generate high-quality natural textures, often rivaling or exceeding VGG-based models.  
- Challenges the necessity of deep, pooled, and supervised features for texture synthesis, isolating rich random filter banks and second-order statistics as core mechanisms.  
- Systematic experiments compare filter types (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and show filter diversity and count matter more than specific learned filters.  
- Combining multiple filter scales is critical for best performance; receptive field size and multi-scale integration are disentangled via ablations.  
- Proposes using the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual texture quality.  
- Frames texture synthesis as a trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability and connects results to Maximum Entropy theory.  
- Decision: Accept — the result is surprising, foundational, and forces re-evaluation of why deep models work for textures.  
- Decision justification: rigorous, well-designed ablation studies and convincing qualitative and quantitative evidence.  
- Paper overturns prior belief that random networks are ineffective, attributing past failures to optimization/tuning issues.  
- Comparative analyses (Fig.1) and multi-scale ablations (Fig.2) provide clear evidence for the authors' claims.  
- Section 7 discussion is strong, offering conceptual insights beyond empirical findings.  
- Request to clarify optimization details, specifically the effect and robustness of scaling loss/gradients by 10^7.  
- Suggest quantifying computational trade-offs (e.g., wall-clock synthesis time) between the multi-scale model and VGG-based model.  
- Recommend including an example of over-constrained exact reconstruction by deeper random networks in the appendix.  
- Questions for authors: does initializing VGG synthesis with the multi-scale model consistently yield equal or lower final VGG loss?  
- Question for authors: how does sample variability of the multi-scale model compare side-by-side to the VGG-based model for the same texture?
