### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the fundamental components necessary for parametric natural texture synthesis using neural network feature statistics. It critically re-evaluates the state-of-the-art method of Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on a deep, multi-layer convolutional network pre-trained for object recognition (VGG). The authors question whether network depth, pooling, and supervised feature training are indispensable for this task. The primary contribution is the demonstration that a strikingly simple model, consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, untrained filters, is sufficient to generate textures of high perceptual quality. These results often rival, and in some cases exceed, the quality of those produced by the much more complex VGG-based model. The paper supports this claim with a comprehensive empirical study comparing a wide range of single-layer architectures, varying filter types (random, Fourier, learned via K-means/PCA), number of feature maps, and filter scales. Furthermore, the work introduces a quantitative metric for texture quality based on the synthesis loss of the VGG model and provides a thoughtful discussion that connects the empirical findings to the theoretical Maximum Entropy framework for texture modeling.

### **Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear Accept. The two key reasons for this decision are:
1.  The work presents a surprising and significant finding that challenges established assumptions in the field of generative image modeling. It convincingly demonstrates that the complex machinery of deep, pre-trained networks is not essential for high-quality texture synthesis, thereby providing crucial insight into the underlying principles of these models.
2.  The claims are supported by rigorous and extensive empirical evidence. The ablation-style analysis is methodologically sound, and the results are presented with high clarity, making a compelling case for the authors' conclusions.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Novelty and Significance:** The central result—that a shallow network with a single layer of random filters can generate high-quality textures—is highly novel and counter-intuitive. It directly challenges the prevailing narrative that the power of models like Gatys et al. (2015a) stems from the hierarchical features learned for high-level semantic tasks. This finding forces a re-evaluation of why deep feature statistics are effective and suggests that a sufficiently diverse, overcomplete set of random projections, when combined with a correlation-matching objective, can capture the necessary statistics for defining a texture. This is a fundamental contribution with implications beyond texture synthesis, touching upon the nature of learned versus random representations in generative models.

**Technical Soundness and Rigor:** The paper's methodology is strong. The authors systematically explore the design space of simple models, comparing handcrafted (Fourier), unsupervisedly learned (K-means, PCA), and random filters. The experiments carefully control for factors like the number of filters and their spatial scale. The identification of a key optimization detail (scaling the loss) that may explain the discrepancy with prior reports of failure with random networks adds to the paper's technical credibility. The results are presented through extensive qualitative examples (Figs. 1, 2, 4) that provide clear and convincing visual evidence.

**Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is exceptionally well-written. The research question is posed directly and motivated clearly in the introduction. The methods are described with sufficient detail to be understandable, and the results are presented logically. The figures are well-designed and effectively communicate the key findings. The discussion in Section 7 is particularly valuable, as it elevates the paper from a purely empirical study to a more conceptual piece by linking the results to the limitations of the Maximum Entropy framework for finite-sized images and proposing a more general definition of texture models.

**Evaluation:** While acknowledging that human perception is the gold standard, the authors' use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality is a pragmatic and useful step towards more objective evaluation. Critically, they also discuss the limitations of this metric, demonstrating a nuanced and scientifically rigorous approach to their evaluation.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from the recommendation to accept.
*   The "Random multi-scale" model is the best-performing proposed architecture, but its precise definition is somewhat scattered. It would improve clarity to formally define this model (e.g., number of filters per scale, filter sizes used) in Section 2 alongside the other architectures.
*   The paper mentions that hierarchical random networks were tested and did not improve quality, though they were more efficient. Including a representative qualitative result from such an experiment, perhaps in the appendix, would further strengthen the claim that depth itself (independent of receptive field size) is not a critical factor for quality in this random-filter regime.
*   The theoretical discussion in Section 7 regarding a metric `d(x,y)` and an epsilon-environment is insightful. This could be more tightly integrated with the empirical results by discussing how the success of the random multi-scale model (which combines diverse feature extractors) might inform the design of a robust perceptual metric `d(x,y)`.

### **Questions for the Authors**
*   You suggest that a crucial factor for success, differing from prior work, was scaling the loss function during optimization. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the synthesis quality to this scaling factor? Does this imply a particular structure in the optimization landscape (e.g., vast flat regions or sharp minima) that the L-BFGS-B algorithm struggles with at its default settings?
*   Your best model combines filters of multiple scales. The comparison in Figure 2 against single-scale models (e.g., 55x55 filters) is compelling. Could you specify how the 1024 filters in the multi-scale model are distributed across the different scales? Is it possible that the single-scale models are simply under-resourced at certain scales (e.g., the 55x55 model lacks enough filters to also capture fine detail), rather than combination being the only crucial factor?
*   For some regular textures, you report that the VGG loss is lower for your model's outputs than for outputs from the VGG-based synthesis, which directly optimizes this loss. You hypothesize this is due to the VGG optimization getting trapped in a poor local minimum. The evidence in Appendix A is strong support. Did you experiment with alternative optimization strategies or multiple random initializations for the VGG synthesis to further verify that this is an optimization artifact rather than a property of the loss surface itself?
