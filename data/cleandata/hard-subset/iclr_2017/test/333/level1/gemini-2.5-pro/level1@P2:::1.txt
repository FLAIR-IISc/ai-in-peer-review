### **Summary**

This paper investigates the fundamental components required for high-quality parametric texture synthesis using neural networks. It directly challenges the prevailing assumption in the literature, stemming from the work of Gatys et al. (2015a), that a deep, hierarchical network pre-trained on a large-scale object recognition task is essential for this task. The authors systematically deconstruct this complex model and make the surprising discovery that neither depth, pooling, nor learned features are indispensable. Their primary contribution is a minimal baseline model consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters. The authors demonstrate that by using a sufficient number of random filters across multiple spatial scales, this simple model can generate textures of exceptionally high perceptual quality, often rivaling or even surpassing the state-of-the-art VGG-based approach. The paper supports its claims with extensive qualitative comparisons across a variety of filter types (random, hand-crafted, and unsupervisedly learned) and introduces the use of the VGG-based synthesis loss as a quantitative, albeit imperfect, metric for texture quality.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a clear accept. The paper presents a simple, elegant, and highly surprising result that provides significant insight into the mechanisms of neural texture synthesis. The key reasons for this decision are:

1.  **Impactful Core Finding:** The paper decisively shows that much of the perceived complexity of the state-of-the-art model (depth, pre-training) is not necessary for excellent performance. This finding fundamentally clarifies our understanding of the problem and establishes a powerful and simple baseline for future research.
2.  **Scientific Rigor:** The claims are substantiated by a series of well-designed and systematic experiments that carefully ablate the core components of the model. The inclusion of both extensive visual evidence and a quantitative metric makes the results convincing.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The central strength of this paper lies in its "less is more" approach. The work of Gatys et al. was groundbreaking, but it was unclear whether its success was due to the specific hierarchical features learned by VGG for object recognition or a more general property of the Gram matrix matching procedure in a high-dimensional feature space. This paper provides a clear answer: the latter is far more important.

The experimental setup is excellent. By comparing a wide range of single-layer models—using Fourier basis filters, K-means/PCA filters from natural images, and purely random filters—the authors convincingly demonstrate that the specific origin of the filters is less important than their quantity and scale. The finding that random filters perform on par with or better than filters learned from natural image statistics is a particularly strong and counter-intuitive result. This suggests that a sufficiently overcomplete, random projection provides a feature space rich enough to capture the necessary second-order statistics for texture modeling.

The analysis of filter scale is another key contribution. The experiments in Figure 2 clearly illustrate that a combination of multiple filter sizes is crucial for capturing texture statistics across different spatial frequencies. Small filters capture fine detail, large filters capture coarse structure, and only their combination yields perceptually satisfying results. This demonstrates that while network *depth* may not be necessary, a *multi-scale representation* is.

Finally, the paper’s discussion is thoughtful and goes beyond a simple presentation of results. It contextualizes the findings within the maximum entropy framework, astutely discussing the practical implications of finite-size images and the inherent trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability. The proposal to frame texture synthesis in terms of a perceptual distance metric and an epsilon-environment around a reference texture is a valuable conceptual contribution.

### **Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Deeper Random Networks:** The paper mentions in the discussion that deeper hierarchical networks with random filters were explored but did not improve quality. This is a very important negative result that strengthens the paper's main claim. I would strongly encourage the authors to include a brief summary of these experiments and a few visual examples in the appendix.
*   **Optimization Robustness:** The authors suggest that the single-layer model benefits from a more robust optimization procedure that is less prone to local minima than the deep VGG model. This is a plausible and interesting hypothesis. While a full analysis of the loss landscape may be out of scope, showing a few plots of the loss curve over iterations for both the shallow and deep models on the same texture could provide some evidence for this claim.
*   **Variability vs. Similarity:** The discussion on the trade-off between perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. This could be further strengthened visually by showing more diverse samples (e.g., 4-5 samples instead of 2 in Figure 3) generated from the same source texture for the main models (Random multi-scale and VGG). This would give readers a better intuition for the "variability" of each model.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   You show convincingly that the multi-scale random model is very effective. How sensitive is the model to the specific choice of scales (3x3, 11x11, 55x55)? Is it the specific combination that matters, or would any sufficiently diverse set of scales (e.g., log-spaced from 3x3 to 61x61) perform similarly?
*   You make the fascinating observation that for some textures, the VGG-based loss is lower for samples from your multi-scale model than for samples from the VGG model itself. You also mention in the text that initializing the VGG synthesis with your model's output yields superior results. Could you elaborate on how consistently this initialization strategy helps? Does it consistently lead to a lower final VGG-loss and/or better perceptual quality across many textures? This seems like a powerful practical technique that merits more emphasis.
*   Your results demonstrate a clear benefit when moving from 363 to 3267 feature maps. Have you investigated the effect of even larger numbers of filters? Is there a point of diminishing returns where adding more random filters ceases to improve texture quality? Understanding this scaling property would be very informative.
