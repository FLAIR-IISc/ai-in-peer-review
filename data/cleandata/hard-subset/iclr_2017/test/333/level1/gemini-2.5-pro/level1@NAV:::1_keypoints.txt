- Demonstrates a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters can synthesize high-quality natural textures.
- Uses a multi-scale bank of random filters and matches their Gram matrix statistics to generate textures.
- Shows results often comparable to state-of-the-art VGG-19 based texture synthesis.
- Challenges the assumption that deep, learned hierarchical features are necessary for texture synthesis.
- Core finding: multi-scale representation is critical rather than specific learned features or depth.
- Provides thorough empirical evaluation comparing single-layer random/hand-crafted/unsupervised filters to VGG-based models.
- Ablation studies on filter sizes support the conclusion about the importance of multi-scale filters.
- Uses VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric but acknowledges it is imperfect, especially for regular textures.
- Decision: Accept.
- Strengths: a strong, counter-intuitive result simplifying understanding of texture synthesis; convincing empirical support.
- Suggestion: add a small-scale human perceptual study to better validate perceptual quality for regular textures.
- Suggestion: include a quantitative comparison of computational cost between the single-layer model and the VGG-based model.
- Question 1: request clarification on optimization tuning, particularly the role of scaling the loss by 10^7 and other critical choices.
- Question 2: ask whether the simpler single-layer optimization landscape inherently favors perceptual similarity over variability, explaining superior performance on highly regular textures.
