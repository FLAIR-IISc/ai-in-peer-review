### Review of "WHAT DOES IT TAKE TO GENERATE NATURAL TEXTURES?"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the fundamental components necessary for high-quality parametric texture synthesis, a problem for which deep neural networks have recently defined the state of the art. The authors critically re-evaluate the highly successful model of Gatys et al. (2015a), which relies on matching feature statistics from a deep, 19-layer VGG network pre-trained on object recognition. The central question is whether the key architectural choices of that model—namely, its depth, use of pooling, and supervisedly learned features—are truly indispensable.

The paper's main contribution is the surprising and powerful demonstration that they are not. The authors show that a remarkably simple model, consisting of a single convolutional layer with no pooling and random, untrained filters, is sufficient to generate natural textures of high perceptual quality. These results often rival, and in some cases for highly regular textures, even surpass those from the much more complex VGG-based model.

Through a comprehensive set of experiments, the paper establishes this simple architecture as a strong new baseline. It systematically compares various filter banks (random, Fourier, K-means, PCA) and demonstrates the critical importance of using a large number of filters and combining multiple filter scales. Furthermore, the paper contributes to a more rigorous evaluation methodology by proposing the use of the synthesis loss from the VGG-based model as a quantitative proxy for perceptual quality. Finally, it provides a thoughtful discussion that connects these empirical findings to the theoretical underpinnings of texture modeling, such as the Maximum Entropy framework, and astutely frames the core challenge as a trade-off between perceptual similarity and sample variability.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong and insightful paper that makes a significant contribution to our understanding of generative models for images. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Surprising and Foundational Result:** The paper's core finding—that a single layer of random filters is sufficient for high-quality texture synthesis—is both counter-intuitive and profound. It challenges the prevailing wisdom that deep, hierarchical, and learned features are essential for capturing natural image statistics. This result forces a re-evaluation of *why* deep models work for this task and elegantly isolates the core mechanisms: a sufficiently rich non-linear filter bank and second-order feature statistics.

2.  **Rigorous and Systematic Investigation:** The authors support their claims with a thorough and well-designed set of experiments. The ablation studies on filter type, filter count, filter scale, and the role of non-linearity are clear and convincing. By providing both strong qualitative results across a wide range of textures and introducing a quantitative metric, the paper sets a high standard for scientific rigor in the field.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in its scientific approach. Instead of merely presenting a new method, it uses its method as an investigative tool to probe a successful but complex existing model. The prior belief, reinforced by earlier work, was that random networks were ineffective for this task. This paper not only overturns that belief but also provides a plausible explanation for the discrepancy: careful tuning of the optimization process. This highlights that seemingly minor implementation details can have a major impact on outcomes, a valuable lesson for the community.

The comparative analysis in Figure 1 is particularly effective. By testing everything from principled filter banks (Fourier) to unsupervisedly learned filters (K-means) and finding that simple random filters perform just as well or better, the authors make a compelling case that the specific nature of the filters is far less important than their diversity and quantity. The subsequent analysis in Figure 2, which demonstrates that combining multiple scales is key to the success of their best model, is a textbook example of a well-executed ablation study. It cleanly separates the effect of receptive field size from the effect of multi-scale feature integration.

Furthermore, the discussion in Section 7 is a major strength. The authors move beyond their empirical results to thoughtfully consider the broader implications. The framing of texture synthesis as a balance between "perceptual similarity" and "variability," and the discussion of how finite image sizes complicate the classic Maximum Entropy view, show a deep understanding of the problem. This elevates the paper from a simple empirical report to a more conceptual piece that can guide future research.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Clarification on Optimization:** The claim that a "more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure" is key to the success of random filters is crucial. However, the only detail provided is scaling the loss and gradients by 10^7. It would be highly beneficial to expand on this. For instance, an ablation showing the synthesis quality with and without this scaling would directly and powerfully support this claim. Is this scaling factor robust?

*   **Computational Trade-offs:** The paper rightly notes that while a single-layer model with large filters can be effective, a hierarchical model is more computationally efficient for achieving large receptive fields. Quantifying this would be helpful. A simple comparison of the wall-clock time required to synthesize a single texture using the proposed multi-scale model versus the VGG-based model would provide a concrete sense of this trade-off.

*   **The Over-Constrained Regime:** The anecdotal evidence mentioned—that some deeper multi-layer random networks exactly reconstructed the source texture—is fascinating. This is a powerful illustration of the risk of over-constraining the model and losing variability. If possible, including an example of this phenomenon in the appendix would make the discussion in Section 7 even more concrete.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   You observe a fascinating paradox where your multi-scale model sometimes achieves a lower VGG-loss than the VGG-based model, which directly minimizes that same loss. You rightly attribute this to the simpler optimization landscape of the shallow model. Your appendix shows that initializing the VGG synthesis with your model's output improves VGG's result. Does this initialization strategy allow the VGG model to consistently achieve a *final* loss value that is lower than or equal to the loss of the initialization image from your model?

*   In your excellent discussion on similarity vs. variability, you propose methods for future evaluation. Qualitatively, how does the sample variability of your multi-scale model (when initialized with different noise seeds) compare to the variability of the VGG-based model? Figure 3 shows variability for your model, but a direct side-by-side comparison for a single texture would be very illustrative.
