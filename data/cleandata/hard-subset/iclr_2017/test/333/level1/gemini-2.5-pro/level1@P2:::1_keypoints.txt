- Paper challenges the assumption that depth and pre-training are required for high-quality neural texture synthesis, proposing a minimal single-layer random-filter model.
- Decision: Accept — the result is surprising, elegant, and has significant impact as a simple baseline.
- Core finding: depth, pooling, and learned features are not necessary; many random filters across multiple scales suffice for excellent textures.
- Scientific rigor: claims supported by systematic ablation experiments, extensive visual comparisons, and a quantitative VGG-based synthesis loss metric.
- Experimental result: random, hand-crafted, and unsupervised filters perform similarly; random filters often match or outperform learned filters.
- Overcompleteness matters: sufficiently many random feature maps provide a rich feature space capturing necessary second-order statistics.
- Multi-scale representation is crucial: combining small and large filters captures fine detail and coarse structure needed for perceptual quality.
- Conceptual contribution: situates findings within a maximum-entropy framework and proposes framing synthesis via a perceptual distance/epsilon-environment.
- Recommendation: include experiments/visuals of deeper random networks (negative result) in the appendix.
- Recommendation: provide loss-curve plots to support the claim that the shallow model has more robust optimization than deep VGG.
- Recommendation: show more sample variability (4–5 samples) to better illustrate the variability vs. similarity trade-off.
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the specific choice of filter scales versus any sufficiently diverse (e.g., log-spaced) set?
- Question: How consistently does initializing VGG synthesis with the multi-scale model improve final VGG-loss and perceptual quality?
- Question: How does texture quality scale with even larger numbers of filters, and is there a point of diminishing returns?
