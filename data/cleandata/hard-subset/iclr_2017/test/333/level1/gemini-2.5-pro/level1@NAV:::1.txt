This paper presents a compelling and surprising investigation into the essential components of parametric texture synthesis. The authors challenge the prevailing assumption that deep, hierarchical networks with features learned from large-scale image datasets are necessary for this task. The core contribution is the demonstration that a strikingly simple model, consisting of a single convolutional layer with random, data-independent filters, can generate natural textures of high perceptual quality. The work systematically shows that by using a multi-scale bank of random filters and matching their Gram matrix statistics, the resulting textures are often on par with those from the state-of-the-art VGG-19 based model.

Decision: **Accept**.
My decision is based on two key strengths: 1) The paper presents a strong, counter-intuitive result that significantly simplifies our understanding of texture synthesis. 2) The claims are supported by a thorough and convincing empirical evaluation that systematically deconstructs the problem.

The primary argument for acceptance is the paper's impactful finding that neither depth nor learned features are indispensable for generating high-quality textures. This is a significant result that establishes a powerful minimal baseline and forces the community to reconsider the role of complex architectures in this specific domain. The authors support this claim rigorously by comparing a wide range of single-layer models (with random, hand-crafted, and unsupervised filters) against the deep VGG-based model. The visual results are impressive, and the use of the VGG-based loss as a quantitative metric, while imperfect, adds a layer of objectivity to the evaluation. The conclusion that a multi-scale representation is critical, rather than specific learned features, is well-supported by ablation studies on filter sizes.

The following suggestions are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision. While the VGG-based loss is a clever evaluation metric, a small-scale human study could offer more direct evidence of perceptual quality, especially for the regular textures where the VGG loss was shown to be inconsistent. Furthermore, a brief quantitative comparison of the computational cost between the proposed single-layer model (with its large filters) and the VGG-based model would provide a more complete picture of the practical trade-offs.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1. You mention that previous attempts with random weights failed, and you attribute your success partly to "a more elaborate tuning of the optimization procedure." Could you elaborate on the specific importance of scaling the loss by 10^7 and any other critical optimization choices?
2. The discussion on balancing perceptual similarity and variability is excellent. Does your simpler optimization landscape (in a single-layer network) inherently favor similarity over variability compared to the deeper VGG model, and could this explain its superior performance on highly regular textures?
