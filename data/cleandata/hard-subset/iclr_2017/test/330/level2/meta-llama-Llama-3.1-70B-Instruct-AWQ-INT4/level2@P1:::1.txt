The authors propose a novel document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The framework includes a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while suppressing common and non-discriminative ones. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this choice are: (1) the authors propose a novel and efficient document representation learning framework that outperforms existing methods on several tasks, and (2) the framework is well-motivated and grounded in the literature.

The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of the proposed framework, including its architecture, training objective, and regularization mechanism. The corruption model is a key innovation that allows Doc2VecC to capture the global semantics of a document while suppressing non-discriminative words. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the regularization mechanism, showing that it favors informative or rare words while suppressing common and non-discriminative ones.

The experimental results are impressive, with Doc2VecC outperforming several state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The authors also provide a detailed comparison of the performance of Doc2VecC with other methods, including Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed framework. While they mention that Doc2VecC can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine, they do not provide a detailed breakdown of the computational resources required to train the model.

To improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the proposed framework, including the time and memory requirements for training and testing. Additionally, the authors could provide more insights into the interpretability of the learned document representations, such as visualizations or examples of documents that are well-represented by the model.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address are:

* Can the authors provide more details on the corruption model, such as the probability distribution used to sample the corrupted documents?
* How does the choice of hyperparameters, such as the corruption rate and the dimensionality of the word embeddings, affect the performance of the model?
* Can the authors provide more insights into the interpretability of the learned document representations, such as visualizations or examples of documents that are well-represented by the model?

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed framework is novel and efficient. With some additional analysis and insights, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
