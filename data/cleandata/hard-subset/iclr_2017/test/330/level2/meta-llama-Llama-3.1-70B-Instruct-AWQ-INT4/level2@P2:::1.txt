This paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The model includes a corruption mechanism that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative ones to be close to zero. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec and outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

I decide to accept this paper. The main reasons for this choice are: (1) the proposed model is well-motivated and has a clear connection to the literature on word embeddings and document representation learning; and (2) the paper provides a thorough evaluation of the proposed model on several tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness and efficiency.

The authors provide a clear explanation of the proposed model and its components, including the corruption mechanism and the data-dependent regularization. The paper also includes a thorough review of related work, which helps to situate the proposed model within the broader context of document representation learning. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering several tasks and comparing the proposed model to several state-of-the-art baselines.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the authors do not provide a detailed analysis of the corruption mechanism and its impact on the learned word embeddings. While the authors provide some intuition about the effects of the corruption mechanism, a more thorough analysis would be helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of the proposed model.

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Provide a more detailed analysis of the corruption mechanism and its impact on the learned word embeddings. This could include an examination of the types of words that are most affected by the corruption mechanism and an analysis of how the corruption mechanism influences the semantic relationships between words.
* Consider including additional experiments to evaluate the robustness of the proposed model to different types of noise or corruption in the input data.
* Provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process, including the range of values considered for each hyperparameter and the criteria used to select the optimal values.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details about the corruption mechanism and how it is implemented in practice? For example, how do you select the words to be corrupted, and what is the probability of corruption for each word?
* How do you determine the optimal value of the corruption rate (q) in the proposed model? Is this value task-dependent or dataset-dependent?
* Have you considered applying the proposed model to other tasks or datasets, such as question answering or machine translation? If so, what were the results, and how did they compare to the results reported in the paper?
