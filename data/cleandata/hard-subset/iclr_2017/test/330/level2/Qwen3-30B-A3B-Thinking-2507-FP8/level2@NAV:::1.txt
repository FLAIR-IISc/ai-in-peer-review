

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption model that introduces data-dependent regularization. The authors claim that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while maintaining exceptional training and inference efficiency. The model's key innovations include decoupling model complexity from corpus size, naturally incorporating a regularization that suppresses common non-discriminative words, and enabling efficient test-time representation generation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a compelling case for a simple yet effective document representation approach that addresses key limitations of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors. The experimental results across multiple datasets demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines, with Doc2VecC achieving 15% better performance than BoW on sentiment analysis while being significantly faster to train and test. The theoretical analysis of the corruption model as a data-dependent regularizer is well-executed and provides valuable insight into why the approach works.

The main limitation is the lack of detailed exploration of how the corruption rate q affects performance. While the paper states that results are "not sensitive to the corruption rate q," it would strengthen the argument to include a table or figure showing performance across different q values. Additionally, the paper could better clarify the trade-offs between Doc2VecC and LSTM-based approaches, as it notes Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought vectors on IMDB (longer documents) but performs slightly worse on SICK (shorter sentences).

The paper is well-motivated, well-placed in the literature, and provides sufficient details for reproducibility. The claims are well-supported by both empirical results and theoretical analysis, and the model's simplicity is a significant practical advantage. I suggest the authors add a brief analysis of how the corruption rate impacts performance and clarify the relative strengths of Doc2VecC versus sequence-based methods for different document lengths.

To help clarify the approach, I would ask: How does the corruption model interact with the subsampling technique for frequent words (mentioned in section 4.2), and does the model's inherent regularization reduce the need for this technique?
