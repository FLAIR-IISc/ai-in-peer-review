

Review for "Document Vector through Corruption"

Summary: The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning framework that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism during training to introduce data-dependent regularization. The authors claim this approach leads to better word embeddings than Word2Vec, produces competitive document representations across multiple tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness), and achieves significant efficiency gains in both training and inference. The approach is well-motivated by observations that document semantics can be captured through averaging word embeddings, and the corruption mechanism provides a natural way to suppress common but uninformative words.

Decision: Accept with minor revisions.

Key reasons for acceptance: The paper presents a simple yet effective approach that successfully addresses key limitations of existing document representation methods. The corruption mechanism is well-justified both theoretically and empirically, and the experimental results consistently demonstrate improvements over strong baselines across multiple tasks. The efficiency claims are well-supported with comprehensive timing measurements.

Supporting arguments: The paper makes a compelling case for Doc2VecC's advantages over existing methods. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 provides a solid foundation for why the corruption mechanism serves as effective data-dependent regularization, particularly for suppressing embeddings of common but uninformative words. The experimental results across sentiment analysis (Table 1), document classification (Table 5), and semantic relatedness (Table 6) consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, and Skip-thought Vectors. The efficiency claims are particularly convincing, with the paper providing detailed timing measurements (Table 2) showing Doc2VecC trains faster than Paragraph Vectors and generates document representations more efficiently than methods requiring additional inference steps. The comparison with Word2Vec+IDF is especially valuable, as it demonstrates that Doc2VecC's regularization mechanism naturally incorporates what would otherwise require post-processing.

Additional feedback: While the paper is well-written and the experiments are thorough, there are a few areas where the presentation could be strengthened to maximize impact. First, the paper refers to "Doc2Vec" in the title but uses "Doc2VecC" throughout the paper - this inconsistency could confuse readers and should be clarified. Second, the theoretical derivation of the regularization effect (Section 3.1) is mathematically sound but could benefit from a more intuitive explanation to help readers grasp why the corruption mechanism leads to better document representations without relying on the complex equations. Third, the comparison to Paragraph Vectors could be expanded to include more recent variants (e.g., PV-DBOW) to better position Doc2VecC within the current literature.

Questions for authors: 
1. In Figure 3, Doc2VecC produces clearly separated document clusters while Doc2Vec does not. Could you elaborate on how the corruption mechanism specifically contributes to this improved clustering behavior, beyond the theoretical regularization effect?
2. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Could you provide more intuition about why this is the case, particularly why the corruption mechanism specifically leads to better document representations compared to simply averaging word embeddings without corruption?
3. The paper mentions that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed from the paradigm shown in Figure 1." Could you clarify the relationship between Doc2VecC and DEA, and why including the local context words leads to better performance?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning with a simple yet effective approach. The combination of theoretical justification, comprehensive experimental evaluation, and strong efficiency claims makes this a compelling submission. The minor issues noted above are easily addressable and would further strengthen an already strong paper.
