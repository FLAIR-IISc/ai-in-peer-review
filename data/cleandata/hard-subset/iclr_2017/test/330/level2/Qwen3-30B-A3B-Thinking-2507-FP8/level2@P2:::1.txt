

# Review of "Document Vector through Corruption"

## Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple document representation learning framework that represents documents as averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is a corruption model that randomly removes words during training, introducing data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while favoring rare, informative ones. The authors claim Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec, matches or outperforms state-of-the-art document representation methods across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, and is highly efficient in both training and test-time inference.

## Decision
I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a simple and efficient approach, the evidence for the core claims is insufficient, and the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism is weak.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper makes several strong claims that are not adequately substantiated:

1. **Theoretical justification for corruption mechanism is inadequate**: The paper's derivation in Section 3.1 is mathematically complex but fails to convincingly demonstrate how the corruption mechanism specifically improves document representation. The key equation (7) shows that Doc2VecC minimizes a regularized loss, but the paper does not clearly explain why this regularization leads to better document representations compared to simply using Word2Vec embeddings with standard techniques (like subsampling frequent words).

2. **Comparison to Paragraph Vectors is misleading**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "matches or outperforms" Paragraph Vectors across tasks. However, the paper itself shows Paragraph Vectors performed poorly on the word analogy task (Section 4.3), which the authors attribute to Paragraph Vectors' "large capacity" causing it to rely on document vectors rather than word embeddings. This undermines the claim that Doc2VecC's approach is fundamentally better, as it may simply be addressing a specific weakness of Paragraph Vectors' implementation rather than providing a general improvement.

3. **The "data-dependent regularization" claim is overstated**: Table 3 shows Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words, but the paper does not demonstrate that this suppression leads to better document representations. The effect appears similar to what's achieved by subsampling frequent words in Word2Vec, which the paper acknowledges is necessary for Word2Vec+AVG (Section 4.2). The paper claims Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick," but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that this is a meaningful advantage.

4. **Experimental design limitations**: The paper compares Doc2VecC to several baselines but doesn't compare it to other simple averaging approaches (like Word2Vec+AVG) that use the same document representation method. The efficiency claim is valid, but the paper doesn't demonstrate that the efficiency gain is substantial enough to justify the new model over simpler alternatives.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would be strengthened by a more rigorous comparison between Doc2VecC and standard Word2Vec+AVG with subsampling. The paper claims Doc2VecC naturally handles the regularization, but doesn't show that this leads to better performance without additional tuning.

2. The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 should be simplified and focused on the key insight: how the corruption mechanism specifically improves document representation beyond what's achievable with standard Word2Vec techniques.

3. The claim that "Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec" is not fully supported. The word analogy results (Table 5) show improvement, but the paper doesn't address whether this improvement is meaningful for the document representation tasks.

4. The paper should clarify the relationship between the corruption mechanism and denoising autoencoders (DEA), as the paper states "Doc2VecC reduces to DEA if local context words are removed," but DEA is presented as a separate baseline. This creates confusion about the novelty of the approach.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the corruption rate q affect performance, and why did you choose q=0.9 specifically? The paper claims the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate," but provides no analysis of this claim.

2. Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task, and how does this relate to the document representation task? The paper attributes this to "large capacity," but this doesn't directly explain why Doc2VecC would be better for document representation.

3. How does the data-dependent regularization in Doc2VecC compare to other regularization techniques (like subsampling frequent words in Word2Vec) in terms of performance and computational cost? The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally" handles this without needing subsampling, but doesn't provide a direct comparison.

4. Why does Doc2VecC perform better than Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB but worse on SICK? The paper attributes this to paragraph length, but this is a significant limitation of the approach that needs to be acknowledged.
