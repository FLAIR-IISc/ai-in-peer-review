

The paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel approach to document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training, which serves as a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. This approach addresses limitations of existing methods: Paragraph Vectors have high parameter complexity and expensive test-time inference, while simpler approaches like bag-of-words lack semantic understanding. Doc2VecC claims to match or outperform state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly more efficient in training and test-time representation generation.

The paper should be accepted. The core contribution is well-motivated, and the empirical results demonstrate consistent improvements over baselines across multiple tasks. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is compelling and well-supported by the experimental analysis.

The paper effectively addresses a meaningful problem in document representation learning. The corruption mechanism is well-justified through theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. The experimental section provides comprehensive comparisons across multiple datasets and tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC consistently outperforms existing methods while being significantly more efficient. The claim that Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training, rather than requiring post-processing as with Word2Vec+IDF, is well-supported by the results. The efficiency claims are also well-justified with training and inference time comparisons.

To improve the paper, the authors should clarify the relationship between the corruption mechanism and standard dropout techniques. While the paper draws parallels to dropout, it would be helpful to explicitly state how the corruption mechanism differs from standard dropout, particularly regarding the unbiased corruption process described in equation (1). A more detailed explanation of why q=0.9 was chosen as the optimal corruption rate would also strengthen the paper, especially since the paper notes the algorithms are not sensitive to q.

The paper should explicitly address the claim that Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) when local context words are removed. While mentioned in the sentiment analysis section, a more detailed explanation of how the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC differs from the corruption used in DEA would help clarify the methodological distinction.

The paper could provide more analysis of the word embeddings generated by Doc2VecC. While the word analogy results (Table 5) show clear improvements over Word2Vec, a deeper exploration of how the corruption mechanism specifically affects semantic properties of the word vectors would strengthen the theoretical contribution. The paper mentions that "Paragraph Vectors on the other hand performs surprisingly bad on this dataset," but does not provide sufficient explanation for this phenomenon beyond the model capacity hypothesis.

The claim that Doc2VecC "produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec" is supported by the word analogy results, but the paper could provide more context about how these better word embeddings translate to improved document representations. The paper notes that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but it would be helpful to have more concrete evidence about how the corruption mechanism specifically contributes to this property.

The paper makes a clear contribution to document representation learning by providing an efficient model that achieves strong performance with a simple architecture. The corruption mechanism is a novel and well-justified approach to regularization that addresses a real limitation in current document representation methods, and the empirical results demonstrate significant improvements over existing techniques.
