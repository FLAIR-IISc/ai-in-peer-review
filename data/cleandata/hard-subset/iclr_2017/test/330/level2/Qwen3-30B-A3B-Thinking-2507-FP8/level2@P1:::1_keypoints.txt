- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism for data-dependent regularization.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Main claim: Corruption yields better word embeddings than Word2Vec and competitive document representations across tasks with large efficiency gains.  
- The corruption mechanism is theoretically justified as suppressing common/uninformative words via data-dependent regularization.  
- Empirical results: Doc2VecC outperforms strong baselines (Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, Skip-thought) on sentiment, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Efficiency: Detailed timing (Table 2) shows faster training than Paragraph Vectors and faster inference than methods requiring extra inference steps.  
- Comparison to Word2Vec+IDF shows Doc2VecC’s regularization naturally incorporates what would otherwise be post-processing.  
- Presentation issues: Inconsistent naming ("Doc2Vec" vs "Doc2VecC") needs clarification.  
- Exposition suggestion: Theoretical derivation (Section 3.1) should include a more intuitive explanation of the regularization effect.  
- Literature suggestion: Expand comparison to more recent Paragraph Vector variants (e.g., PV-DBOW).  
- Question 1: How exactly does corruption improve clustering in Figure 3 beyond the theoretical regularization argument?  
- Question 2: Provide more intuition why corruption enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged embeddings versus simple averaging.  
- Question 3: Clarify the relationship between Doc2VecC and Denoising Autoencoders and why including local context words improves performance.  
- Overall: Valuable, simple, and effective contribution supported by theory, thorough experiments, and convincing efficiency claims; minor fixes recommended.
