- Introduces Doc2VecC: averages word embeddings with a corruption model to produce efficient semantic document embeddings.
- Corruption mechanism acts as data-dependent regularization, up-weighting rare/informative words and down-weighting frequent/non-discriminative ones.
- Authors claim Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness while being computationally efficient.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Significant improvement in both efficiency and performance over existing methods with strong empirical results across tasks.
- Key reason 2: Simple model architecture combined with the corruption mechanism is a novel, practical contribution.
- Empirical validation: Rigorous experiments on IMDB, Wikipedia, and SICK show consistent outperformance of baselines (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, Denoising AEs) and faster inference.
- Corruption mechanism is justified as regularization and its impact is quantitatively demonstrated.
- Novelty/practicality: Simplicity and avoidance of expensive inference for unseen documents (unlike Paragraph Vectors) make Doc2VecC practical for real-world use.
- Efficiency: Reported training and inference times show strong scalability, especially vs. Skip-thought and LSTM-based methods.
- Field knowledge/completeness: Strong literature situating, comprehensive comparisons, and reproducibility via public code.
- Suggestion 1: Include detailed ablation studies isolating the corruption mechanism (e.g., vary corruption rates).
- Suggestion 2: Provide qualitative error analysis to understand failure cases, especially where LSTM-based methods outperform.
- Suggestion 3: Broaden evaluation with additional datasets/tasks (e.g., machine translation, question answering) to test generalizability.
- Questions: How performance varies with corruption rate q and its optimal range; whether corruption can incorporate semantic/syntactic info instead of random removal; how OOV words are handled in low-resource or specialized domains.
- Overall: Compelling case for Doc2VecC as a practical, effective document representation method with potential for significant impact after minor improvements.
