This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The method represents a document as a simple average of its word embeddings, a concept elegantly enforced during training through a unique corruption mechanism. The authors provide a strong theoretical motivation, showing that this random word-dropping acts as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, non-discriminative words while favoring rare, informative ones. The primary contributions are the model's efficiency, the principled regularization technique, and its claimed state-of-the-art or competitive performance on a diverse set of downstream tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. The decision is based on two key strengths: (1) The core idea of using corruption as a principled, data-dependent regularizer is both novel and well-justified through a Taylor expansion analysis. (2) The claims are robustly supported by extensive experiments across multiple benchmarks against a strong set of state-of-the-art baselines.

The paper's main strength lies in its simplicity and the clear theoretical grounding for its main component—the corruption model. Unlike heuristic approaches, Section 3.1 demonstrates how this mechanism is approximately equivalent to adding a specific penalty term to the loss, providing a clear explanation for *why* it works. This elevates the work beyond a simple engineering trick. Furthermore, the experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC not only achieves competitive or superior accuracy but also significantly outperforms methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors in terms of training and inference efficiency (Table 2). The qualitative analysis of the learned embeddings (Table 3) and the quantitative results on the word analogy task further validate the claims about the regularization effect.

For future revisions, I offer the following suggestions to strengthen the paper:
*   The title appears to have a typo ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION") and should be corrected.
*   The corruption rate `q` is set to 0.9 throughout, with the authors noting the model is not sensitive to this choice. While understandable for brevity, a small sensitivity analysis (e.g., a plot of performance vs. `q` on one dataset) would strengthen this claim and provide guidance for future practitioners.
*   The reference formatting appears inconsistent and could be cleaned up.

Finally, I have a couple of clarifying questions for the authors:
1.  In Equation 7, the regularization strength is proportional to `q/(1-q)`. With `q=0.9`, this factor is 9, which seems quite high. Could you comment on how this high regularization factor impacts training stability and the final embedding norms?
2.  The paper notes that Doc2VecC does not rely on the frequent word subsampling trick common in Word2Vec. Was this trick disabled for the Doc2VecC experiments, or was it used in addition to the corruption model? Clarifying this would help isolate the effect of the proposed regularization.
