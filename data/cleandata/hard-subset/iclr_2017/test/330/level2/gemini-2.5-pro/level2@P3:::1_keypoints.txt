- Introduces Doc2VecC: trains document vectors as averages of word embeddings using a corruption mechanism that enforces compositionality and acts as a data-dependent regularizer.  
- Corruption is theoretically analyzed (Section 3.1) as a regularizer that penalizes embeddings of frequent, non-informative words.  
- Empirical results show Doc2VecC is much faster to train and infer than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought, while achieving competitive or superior performance across tasks.  
- Word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are higher quality than standard Word2Vec (evidenced by word analogy and semantic tasks).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Exceptional combination of simplicity, scalability, and strong empirical performance with orders-of-magnitude speed gains.  
- Key reason 2: Well-motivated corruption mechanism with theoretical justification and empirical support for down-weighting stop words.  
- Strength: Builds on CBoW with a minor modification, making the model easy to implement and scale.  
- Strength: Efficiency does not sacrifice accuracy—outperforms strong baselines on IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia classification.  
- Strength: Theoretical derivation (approximate regularizer) explains why model shrinks norms of uninformative word vectors; qualitative and quantitative evidence supports this.  
- Suggestion: Acknowledge limitations of the second-order Taylor approximation and discuss higher-order effects or validity conditions.  
- Suggestion: Show how Doc2VecC performance scales with increased dimensionality to compare model capacity with Skip-thought.  
- Suggestion: Report quantitative results with and without fine-tuning on the SICK dataset to clarify the improvement.  
- Suggestion: Verify that the Paragraph Vectors t-SNE visualization is representative and not an artifact of t-SNE hyperparameters or initialization.  
- Clarifying question: Is the target word wt ever included in the corrupted global context x̃ for that training update, or is it excluded?  
- Clarifying question: How does the high variance from sampling a single x̃ per stochastic update affect learning dynamics beyond the expected regularization effect?
