- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings trained to predict target words from local context plus a corrupted global document context.  
- Corruption (random word dropout) is theoretically shown to act as a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words.  
- Empirically, Doc2VecC is much faster to train/infer than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought, while producing competitive or superior document and word representations.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Method is simple, well-motivated, highly efficient, and removes the need for per-document vectors, enabling fast inference.  
- Reason 2: Corruption mechanism is theoretically justified, turning a heuristic into a principled regularization approach.  
- Reason 3: Comprehensive experiments show strong performance across sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness.  
- Strength: Clever synthesis of CBoW, Paragraph Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders retaining CBoW efficiency with global context benefits.  
- Strength: Theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) links dropout-style corruption to a regularizer that penalizes common non-discriminative words; qualitative results support this.  
- Strength: Experiments show state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art results (notably ~20% improvement in word analogy) and better-separated document clusters.  
- Suggestion: Expand discussion of the connection to Denoising Autoencoders and frame Doc2VecC as a DAE regularized by local CBoW prediction.  
- Suggestion: Provide an ablation on corruption rates (e.g., q=0.5, 0.7, 0.9) to justify the chosen q=0.9 and the claim of insensitivity.  
- Suggestion: Analyze performance versus document length to substantiate claims about Skip-thought’s weaknesses and Doc2VecC’s robustness.  
- Suggestion: Soften abstract/conclusion language claiming universal state-of-the-art to acknowledge weaker SICK results versus some LSTM/Skip-thought methods.  
- Question: How accurate is the Taylor expansion approximation in Section 3.1 in practice, especially at high corruption rates like q=0.9?  
- Question: How would a Skip-gram version of Doc2VecC be formulated, and would the same data-dependent regularization effect be expected?
