**Summary of the paper's contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. To make this simple composition effective, the model is trained with a specific objective: to predict a target word from both its local context (as in Word2Vec CBoW) and a global document context. Critically, this global context is formed by averaging word embeddings from a *corrupted* version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words, pushing them towards zero. The paper presents a strong set of experiments across sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC is not only significantly faster to train and test than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, but also produces document and word representations that are competitive with or superior to the state-of-the-art.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  The proposed method is simple, well-motivated, and highly efficient, directly addressing major drawbacks (scalability, inference speed) of a key baseline, Paragraph Vectors.
2.  The corruption mechanism is not just an ad-hoc trick; it is elegantly justified through a theoretical analysis that links it to a principled, data-dependent regularization scheme, providing a strong foundation for the approach.
3.  The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and demonstrates strong performance across a variety of tasks, validating the effectiveness of the learned representations for both documents and individual words.

**Supporting arguments**

The main strength of this work is its elegant simplicity backed by both strong empirical results and a solid theoretical motivation.

First, the method is a clever synthesis of ideas from Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders. It retains the highly efficient CBoW architecture of Word2Vec while successfully incorporating a global document context, a goal shared with Paragraph Vectors. However, unlike Paragraph Vectors, it avoids learning a unique vector per document, which keeps the model size independent of the training corpus size and makes inference for unseen documents trivial and fast (a simple average). The speed comparison in Table 2 makes this advantage strikingly clear: inference takes seconds for Doc2VecC versus minutes for Paragraph Vectors, a critical difference for practical applications.

Second, the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a key contribution. By showing that the dropout-style corruption approximates a specific regularizer on the word embeddings (Equation 7), the paper elevates the method from a heuristic to a principled approach. The analysis that this regularizer penalizes frequent yet non-discriminative words provides a satisfying explanation for *why* the method works well and why it does not require heuristics like subsampling of frequent words. The qualitative results in Table 3, where Doc2VecC pushes stop words towards zero while preserving embeddings for rare but meaningful words, provide excellent empirical support for this theoretical claim.

Third, the experimental results are convincing. Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB) and document classification (Wikipedia). The visualization in Figure 3 also provides strong qualitative evidence that Doc2VecC learns better-separated document clusters than Doc2Vec. A particularly impressive result is the significant improvement in the quality of the word embeddings themselves, as shown by the ~20% uplift over Word2Vec on the word analogy task (Table 4). This indicates that the document-level training objective successfully injects semantic constraints that lead to better individual word vectors, a non-obvious and valuable outcome.

**Additional feedback for improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   The connection to Denoising Autoencoders (DAE) is mentioned briefly but could be discussed more explicitly. The objective in Equation 2, which predicts a word `wt` from a corrupted document representation `x̃`, seems very close to a DAE objective. The paper states that Doc2VecC reduces to a DAE if the local context `ct` is removed. It would be insightful to elaborate on this, perhaps by framing Doc2VecC as a DAE that is simultaneously regularized by a local CBoW-style prediction task.
*   The choice of the corruption rate `q = 0.9` seems high. The paper states the model is "not sensitive" to this value, but providing a small ablation study showing performance and/or training time across a few values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) would strengthen this claim and make the choice less arbitrary.
*   The hypothesis that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on IMDB due to document length is plausible. This could be strengthened by showing how Doc2VecC's performance changes with document length. Does it also degrade, but more gracefully? This would provide a more direct comparison and add nuance to the analysis.
*   The paper's abstract claims the model "matches or out-performs the state-of-the-art". While this is true for several tasks, the results on the SICK dataset are slightly below LSTM-based methods and Skip-thought vectors. The discussion provides a reasonable explanation (short vs. long text). It might be slightly more accurate to nuance this claim in the abstract and conclusion to reflect that the model's strength is particularly pronounced for longer documents.

**Questions for the authors**
*   Could you comment on the Taylor expansion approximation in Section 3.1? How accurate is this approximation in practice, especially for a high corruption rate like `q=0.9` where the deviation `(x̃ - µx)` can be large?
*   You focus on the CBoW architecture. You mention that extending to Skipgram is "straightforward". Could you briefly elaborate on what a Skipgram version of Doc2VecC would look like? Would the objective be to predict context words from the corrupted document vector, and would you expect the same data-dependent regularization effect to hold?
