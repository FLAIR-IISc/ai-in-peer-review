**Summary**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The model learns these embeddings using an architecture similar to Word2Vec's Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) model. The key contribution is the inclusion of a "global context" vector, which is an average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted (i.e., subsampled) version of the document. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis, showing that this corruption process is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that this simple and efficient model matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks. Furthermore, it is shown to produce higher quality word embeddings than Word2Vec on the word analogy task.

**Decision: Accept**

Key Reasons:
1.  The proposed method is simple, highly efficient, and effective. It provides a practical and scalable solution for learning document representations, which is a significant contribution.
2.  The paper provides a strong theoretical motivation for the corruption mechanism, connecting it to a principled data-dependent regularizer. This elevates the method from a heuristic to a well-grounded approach and provides valuable insight into why it works.
3.  The empirical evaluation is comprehensive, comparing against strong baselines across a diverse set of tasks (sentiment analysis, classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness). The results convincingly support the paper's claims about both the quality of the representations and the model's efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is the elegance and effectiveness of its proposed method. Doc2VecC cleverly extends the well-understood Word2Vec framework. By representing the document context as a corrupted average of word vectors, it avoids the major drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors: the number of parameters does not grow with the corpus size, and inference on unseen documents is extremely fast (a simple averaging operation). The reported training and test times in Table 2 clearly highlight this significant practical advantage.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is another major strength. By framing the corruption as a form of regularization through a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss, the authors provide a clear explanation for the model's effectiveness. The insight that this regularization naturally penalizes common and uninformative words, while being modulated by their predictive power, is compelling. This analysis provides a satisfying answer to why simply averaging word embeddings can work so well within this framework, without requiring post-hoc weighting schemes like TF-IDF.

The experimental results are strong and well-presented.
-   On the IMDB sentiment analysis task, Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance while being significantly more efficient than Paragraph Vectors at test time.
-   On the Wikipedia document classification task, it consistently outperforms all baselines across different embedding dimensions. The t-SNE visualizations provide good qualitative support for the superiority of the learned document spaces.
-   Perhaps most impressively, the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC significantly outperform those from Word2Vec on the word analogy task. This suggests that incorporating the document-level objective improves the quality of the word-level representations themselves, which is a valuable finding.
-   While not outperforming specialized sequential models like LSTMs on the SICK sentence relatedness task, it remains competitive and offers a much more efficient alternative, reinforcing the authors' reasonable hypothesis about the model's suitability for longer documents.

**Additional Feedback and Questions**

The paper is well-written and the claims are well-supported. The following points are intended to help further improve the paper and clarify some details.

-   **Impact of Frequent Word Subsampling:** In Section 4.2, you mention that for other models, subsampling frequent words is critical, but that Doc2VecC's regularization makes this trick less necessary. However, you also state that subsampling was applied for all reported results. To fully validate the claim about the regularizer's effect, it would be very informative to see the performance of Doc2VecC on the IMDB task *without* this subsampling step. Does the performance remain high, as the theory would suggest?

-   **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper states the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and fixes it at 0.9. While this is acceptable, a small ablation study showing performance on a validation set for a few different values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) would strengthen this claim and provide useful guidance for practitioners.

-   **Connection to Denoising Autoencoders:** The link made to Denoising Autoencoders (DAE) is interesting. The paper could benefit from a slightly more detailed explanation. When the local context is removed, is the objective function of Doc2VecC mathematically equivalent to a particular variant of a DAE? A brief discussion could help place the work more firmly in relation to that line of research.

-   **Taylor Expansion Approximation:** The theoretical analysis relies on a second-order Taylor approximation of the log-likelihood. This is a standard and reasonable technique. However, given the high corruption rate (q=0.9), the perturbation from the mean could be quite large. Do you have any intuition on how tight this approximation is in practice, and how the interpretation might change for very high corruption rates?

-   **Fine-tuning on SICK dataset:** For the semantic relatedness task, you mention fine-tuning the model on the SICK dataset (unsupervised). Could you please report the performance of the model trained only on the book corpus, without this task-specific fine-tuning step? This would provide a clearer comparison against the generic, off-the-shelf Skip-thought model.
