- Introduces Doc2VecC: representing a document as the average of its word embeddings with a corrupted global context vector, using a CBoW-like architecture.  
- Core contribution: corruption of the document yields a global context that acts as a data-dependent regularizer.  
- Theoretical result: corruption is equivalent to a regularizer that penalizes frequent, non-discriminative words.  
- Empirically matches or outperforms Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought on sentiment analysis and document classification.  
- Produces higher-quality word embeddings than Word2Vec on the word analogy task.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Method is simple, highly efficient, and scalable with fast inference and no parameter growth with corpus size.  
- Key reason 2: Strong theoretical motivation turns the corruption trick into a principled approach rather than a heuristic.  
- Key reason 3: Comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse tasks validates representation quality and efficiency.  
- Advantage vs Paragraph Vectors: avoids per-document parameters and enables very fast inference (simple averaging).  
- Practical advantage supported by reported training and test time improvements (Table 2).  
- Theory explains why averaging works without post-hoc weighting (e.g., TF-IDF) by penalizing uninformative words.  
- IMDB: achieves state-of-the-art sentiment performance while being more efficient than Paragraph Vectors.  
- Wikipedia classification: consistently outperforms baselines across embedding sizes; t-SNE visualizations support qualitative separation.  
- SICK semantic relatedness: competitive with sequential models but does not beat specialized LSTMs, remaining a more efficient option for longer documents.  
- Requested clarifications: effect of removing frequent-word subsampling on performance (IMDB).  
- Requested clarifications: sensitivity analysis for corruption rate q (e.g., q=0.5, 0.7, 0.9).  
- Requested clarifications: clearer connection/equivalence to Denoising Autoencoders.  
- Requested clarifications: validity/tightness of the second-order Taylor approximation at high corruption rates.  
- Requested clarifications: performance on SICK when trained only on the book corpus without task-specific fine-tuning.
