Summary
The paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. Each document is represented as the average of its word embeddings, while training augments a CBOW-style predictor with a corrupted global document context obtained by unbiased dropout on the bag-of-words. A second-order Taylor expansion shows the corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, non-informative words and preserves informative ones. The approach retains Word2Vec-like training efficiency and yields very fast inference for unseen documents. Empirically, Doc2VecC achieves strong or state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), large-scale document classification (Wikipedia), and competitive results on semantic relatedness (SICK), while also producing better word embeddings than Word2Vec on word analogy tasks. The paper includes code and demonstrates substantial training/inference speed advantages.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A compelling combination of conceptual simplicity, clear motivation, and practical efficiency, with a theoretically grounded regularization effect.
- Strong empirical results across multiple tasks and scales, alongside clear benefits in training and test-time efficiency relative to established baselines.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The work is well placed between Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, and explicitly contrasts with prior global-context averaging (Huang et al., 2012) by introducing corruption. The analysis links to dropout/marginalized autoencoder literature (Wager et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014), providing a principled view of the induced regularizer.
- Methodological clarity: The model is straightforward to implement. The unbiased corruption and scaling by 1/(1–q) are clearly specified; the model objective is detailed and trained with negative sampling.
- Theoretical insight: The second-order expansion yields an interpretable, data-dependent regularizer that naturally downweights frequent words unless predictive, aligning with observed improvements and reducing reliance on heuristic IDF weighting.
- Empirical validation:
  - IMDB sentiment: Doc2VecC matches or outperforms Paragraph Vectors and substantially outperforms BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and DAE under both transductive and inductive representation-learning regimes. The runtime analysis shows significant training and test-time gains (notably vs PV inference).
  - Wikipedia document classification (100-way): Consistent gains across representation sizes; t-SNE plots qualitatively support better clustering.
  - SICK relatedness: Competitive with LSTM and skip-thought despite far simpler architecture and much lower compute; clearly faster to train and test.
  - Word analogy: Doc2VecC embeddings surpass Word2Vec across most subtasks, with gains growing with data/embedding size; a plausible explanation is provided for PV underperformance on this metric.
- Reproducibility: Code is provided; key hyperparameters (e.g., q=0.9) and setups are described.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all essential for acceptance)
Essential clarifications
- Baselines:
  - Paragraph Vectors: Specify PV variants used (DM, DBOW, DM+DBOW), concatenation vs averaging, window sizes, negative sampling, and inference details. PV has several variants with different performance/runtime trade-offs; clarity will strengthen fairness.
  - Word2Vec: Clarify negative sampling distribution (unigram^0.75 vs uniform); the paper states uniform Pv for negatives, which differs from common practice and may affect results.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Report sensitivity to corruption rate q and window size k (a small table/plot). The text states insensitivity to q, but quantitative evidence would help.
  - Include a “no-corruption” variant to isolate the gain from corruption/regularization vs simple averaging trained end-to-end.
  - Analyze the effect of the 1/T scaling of the global context on documents of highly variable length; consider an alternative normalization by number of surviving tokens or show robustness.
- Experimental fairness:
  - SICK: Clarify which models are trained only on SICK vs pretrained on large corpora. The paper mirrors skip-thought’s setup by pretraining on a book corpus, which is appropriate; still, explicitly partition comparisons into “with external pretraining” vs “SICK-only” for transparency.
  - IMDB/Skip-thought: Acknowledge limitations of using the off-the-shelf skip-thought encoder on very long reviews; if possible, include a sentence-chunking + pooling baseline for skip-thought to mitigate sequence length issues.

Nice-to-have improvements
- Provide wall-clock training details for Doc2VecC vs Word2Vec across datasets (cores, threads, negative samples per step, learning rate), and report memory footprint.
- Include additional datasets (e.g., 20 Newsgroups, Yelp, AG News) to demonstrate robustness across domains.
- Explore light-weight weighting at test time (e.g., learned token-wise scaling from the regularizer) to see if small gains are possible without harming efficiency.
- Clean up the references for consistency and correct citations.

Questions
- What is the exact negative sampling distribution and number of negatives used across methods? Was the same setting used for Word2Vec and Doc2VecC?
- Which Paragraph Vector variants and hyperparameters were used, and how was PV tuned? Were both PV-DM and PV-DBOW evaluated for classification, and PV-DM for word analogies?
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and window size k? Are there failure modes for very short or extremely long documents?
- Does removing subsampling of frequent words (Mikolov & Dean, 2013) degrade Doc2VecC performance substantially, or does the induced regularizer largely replace its effect?
- For SICK, how much gain comes from the book-corpus pretraining vs SICK-only training? Could Doc2VecC trained from scratch on SICK approach the reported performance?

Overall, the work presents a well-motivated, principled, and practical advancement for document representation learning, with compelling evidence and strong efficiency benefits.
