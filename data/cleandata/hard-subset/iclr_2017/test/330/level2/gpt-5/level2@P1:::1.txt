Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. The key idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings and to train those embeddings jointly with a corruption process that randomly drops words from the document during learning. The corruption yields a data-dependent regularization term (derived via a second-order Taylor expansion) that suppresses embeddings for frequent, non-informative words and preserves/boosts embeddings for informative or rare words. The approach keeps model size independent of corpus size (depending only on vocabulary), trains as efficiently as Word2Vec with negative sampling, and yields fast test-time inference (a single average). Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or exceeds state-of-the-art document representation methods on sentiment analysis and document classification, produces stronger word embeddings on analogy tasks, and is competitive on semantic relatedness, while being substantially faster than many baselines. The paper is clearly written, well-motivated, and provides code.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is elegant and practical: it combines a compelling theoretical insight (data-dependent regularization from corruption) with a minimal architecture that scales and yields strong empirical results. (2) The empirical evaluation is broad and generally favorable, with clear efficiency benefits at both train and test time.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper identifies pain points of Paragraph Vector methods (parameter growth, slow test inference) and proposes a principled alternative that preserves Word2Vec’s simplicity while enforcing document-level compositionality during training.
- Theory: The corruption-as-regularization derivation is clear, connects to established analyses of dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders, and explains the empirical behavior (e.g., suppression of stop-word embeddings).
- Simplicity and scalability: The model keeps only U and V (no per-document parameters), uses negative sampling, and supports very fast test-time averaging; timing comparisons concretely demonstrate the advantages.
- Empirical breadth: Tasks include IMDB sentiment, a 100-class Wikipedia classification dataset, SICK semantic relatedness, and word analogy; Doc2VecC consistently performs strongly, often outperforming Word2Vec+IDF and Paragraph Vectors, and produces notably better word analogies than Word2Vec.
- Analysis: The paper provides qualitative and quantitative evidence that frequent but uninformative words are suppressed by the learned regularizer, aligning with the theoretical claims.
- Reproducibility: Code is provided; datasets and settings are described.

Additional feedback and questions to improve the paper
These points are offered to help strengthen the work and are not all critical to the accept decision.

- Fairness and detail of baselines:
  - Negative sampling distribution: You state Pv is uniform. Word2Vec typically uses a unigram^0.75 distribution. Please clarify what you used in practice for Doc2VecC and for the Word2Vec baseline; report sensitivity if different. This choice can materially affect both speed and quality.
  - Paragraph Vectors: Specify which PV variant(s) were used (DM, DBOW, or both) and their hyperparameters (context window, negative samples, vector size). PV-DBOW is a strong baseline and sometimes more competitive; please ensure both are included or discuss.
  - Skip-thought: Since you did not train it on the same data, make explicit the domain mismatch and length mismatch. The IMDB comparison is still informative but readers would benefit from a discussion of fairness and possibly a shorter-sequence dataset where Skip-thought is expected to excel.
  - Additional strong baselines: Consider including or discussing weighting schemes beyond IDF, e.g., SIF/Arora-style weighting and removal of the first principal component, which are known to make averaging-based representations competitive.

- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Corruption rate q: You report using q=0.9 and note limited sensitivity. Please provide a plot/table showing performance vs q and training time vs q. This will help practitioners choose q.
  - Role of context vs document vector: Include an ablation removing local context (document-only prediction) and/or removing the document vector (context-only) to quantify their contributions on each task.
  - Subsampling of frequent words: You note using Mikolov subsampling. Given the paper’s central claim that Doc2VecC intrinsically suppresses frequent words, please show results with and without subsampling for Doc2VecC, Word2Vec+AVG, and Word2Vec+IDF to isolate the effect of the proposed regularizer.
  - Document length effects: Since the method averages and scales by 1/T, analyze performance vs document length; extremely short texts may be more sensitive to corruption and averaging.

- Theoretical clarifications:
  - The Taylor expansion approximation is taken at the mean of the corruption; please discuss the approximation’s accuracy and the conditions under which higher-order terms might matter. An empirical check (e.g., showing the regularizer correlates with observed norm suppression) would help.
  - The variance Σx is diagonal due to independent corruption per word. If you consider non-binary x (counts) or alternative corruptions (e.g., word dropout proportional to frequency), how would the regularizer change?

- Implementation details:
  - Provide exact training hyperparameters (window size, negatives per step, learning rate schedule, vector sizes across tasks) and report variance across multiple runs to assess stability.
  - Memory footprint and throughput: Since speed is a key claim, include GPU/CPU configurations, tokens/sec, and memory usage for Doc2VecC vs baselines.

- Scope and limitations:
  - Order information is largely ignored by document averaging. It would be useful to acknowledge cases where order-sensitive models (RNN/CNN) may be preferred, and to outline potential extensions (e.g., n-gram embeddings or positional averaging) that could retain some word order while keeping efficiency.
  - Discuss OOV handling at test time and the impact of extending the vocabulary in-domain vs keeping it fixed.

Questions for the authors
1) What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use for each method? How sensitive is Doc2VecC to these choices?
2) How does performance change with q in the range [0.5, 0.95]? Is there a sweet spot balancing training speed and accuracy?
3) Which Paragraph Vector variants were included (DM/DBOW), and what were their embedding sizes? Did you tune PV’s inference steps at test time for best quality?
4) Can you report results for Doc2VecC without subsampling of frequent words to isolate the effect of the corruption-induced regularizer?
5) Have you tried frequency-proportional corruption (higher drop for frequent words) or count-based x instead of binary x? Does the induced regularizer improve further?
6) On SICK, could you report results without fine-tuning to separate pretraining vs fine-tuning effects?

Overall, the paper is well-motivated, insightful, and practical, with strong evidence and clear contributions. I recommend acceptance.
