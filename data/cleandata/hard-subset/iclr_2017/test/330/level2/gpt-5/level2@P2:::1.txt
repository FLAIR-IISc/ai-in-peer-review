Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a very simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to learn word embeddings such that the average of the embeddings over a document serves as a strong global representation. During training, the model predicts a target word from its local context (as in CBOW) augmented with a global document vector formed by averaging a corrupted subset of words from the document. The corruption (masking) scheme is unbiased (rescaling by 1/(1−q)) and enables both computational speedups (fewer active tokens per update) and an analytic, data-dependent regularization: via a second-order Taylor expansion, the authors show that corruption penalizes embeddings of frequent, non-informative words more than rare/informative ones. Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia-100), and is competitive on semantic relatedness (SICK), while also producing stronger word embeddings (word analogy) and substantial training/inference speedups. The approach is simple, well-motivated, and practical.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical performance with a very simple, scalable architecture that enables fast training and test-time encoding by averaging word embeddings.
- Clear and insightful analysis showing corruption induces a beneficial data-dependent regularizer that suppresses common, non-discriminative words, with supporting qualitative and quantitative evidence.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: While averaging word embeddings for document representations is known, the paper’s key novelty is to enforce during training that simple averaging suffices by injecting a corrupted global context into the prediction objective, together with an analysis connecting corruption to an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer. This fills a gap between post-hoc averaging heuristics (e.g., IDF weighting) and more complex models that learn document vectors (PV), and is well situated relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, SDA/DAE, and Skip-Thought.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion around the unbiased corruption mean yields a tractable approximation that highlights how the regularizer scales with word frequency and prediction confidence, aligning with intuition from dropout/logistic regression. Although approximate, it is carefully derived and plausibly explains the observed behavior (e.g., suppression of stop words).
- Empirical rigor:
  - IMDB: Doc2VecC is on par with or better than PV and clearly ahead of BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, DAE, Skip-Thought, and RNNLM with a linear classifier. The authors fairly report two settings (with and without using test data for unsupervised representation learning).
  - Wikipedia classification: Consistently lower error across embedding sizes; strong qualitative t-SNE visualizations corroborate better clustering structure.
  - SICK: Competitive with task-specific or heavy models; reasonable given focus on longer documents rather than short sentences.
  - Word analogy: Substantial improvements over Word2Vec across most subtasks, suggesting the regularization improves the quality of the learned word space.
- Efficiency: The model has complexity independent of the number of documents (unlike PV) and yields extremely fast test-time encoding. The training-speed gains from corruption are attractive, and the CPU-only timings suggest a favorable tradeoff.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The method is clearly described; code is provided.

Weaknesses and concerns
- The corruption-as-regularization analysis relies on a second-order approximation and a particular negative sampling distribution; the empirical section would benefit from stronger ablations to solidify the causal claims (e.g., removing corruption, varying q, and isolating the contribution of the global context vs local context).
- Fairness and completeness of baselines:
  - PV has multiple variants (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW); details and tuning could affect results. The surprisingly poor PV word analogy performance may stem from capacity focusing on document vectors; a more thorough PV configuration sweep would strengthen the comparison.
  - Skip-Thought results on IMDB use a generic model with no domain adaptation, which may disadvantage it. This is acknowledged but still makes cross-method comparisons less clean.
  - fastText-style baselines (supervised) are not strictly comparable, but an unsupervised bag-of-ngrams + IDF baseline would be informative.
- Subsampling of frequent words: The paper states Doc2VecC “does not rely on this trick,” yet subsampling is applied across methods in reported numbers. A direct comparison with and without subsampling for Doc2VecC would better validate this claim.
- Some implementation details are under-specified (negative sampling distribution, number of negatives, window sizes), and the reported CPU timings could be complemented by normalized throughput metrics or GPU comparisons.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the decision)
- Add ablations:
  - No-corruption variant; different q values; corruption applied/not applied to local vs global contexts.
  - Replace corruption with explicit IDF weighting during training to compare against the learned regularizer.
  - Show Doc2VecC without subsampling frequent words to substantiate that it is less reliant on subsampling.
- Clarify negative sampling: specify the sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75) and number of negatives; report if choices differ across baselines.
- Provide PV details (DM vs DBOW, concatenation vs sum, inference steps at test, learning rates) to ensure fairness.
- Extend comparisons: include a recent unsupervised baseline using smoothed inverse frequency (SIF) weighting of averages if feasible, and/or a bag-of-ngrams + IDF baseline.
- Report throughput: tokens/sec and updates/sec, and a breakdown of speed gains attributable to corruption (vs CBOW baseline).
- Minor: Fix typos/formatting in title and references; ensure all tables/figures are numbered and referenced consistently.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used in Doc2VecC, and were they matched across Word2Vec/PV baselines? Your derivation assumes uniform negatives—does performance change with the commonly used unigram^0.75?
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q? Please provide a plot of accuracy vs q and training speed vs q, and an ablation with q=0 (no corruption).
- Can you report Doc2VecC results with and without subsampling frequent words to validate the claim that the model does not rely on subsampling?
- How much does the local context contribute relative to the global document vector? An ablation removing local context (or global context) would help.
- For PV, which variants and hyperparameters were used? Did you try PV-DBOW and combinations, and ensure sufficient inference steps at test time?
- For SICK and IMDB, did you fine-tune word embeddings for all methods equally where applicable? On SICK, Doc2VecC is fine-tuned on the target data (unsupervised); was a similar vocabulary expansion/fine-tuning attempted for Skip-Thought to balance the comparison?
- At test time, do you average over token occurrences or unique types? Does weighting by term frequency help or harm relative to pure averaging?
