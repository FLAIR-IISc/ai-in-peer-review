- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn word embeddings so simple averaging of corrupted-word embeddings yields strong document vectors used to predict target words.

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason: Strong empirical performance with a very simple, scalable model enabling fast training and test-time encoding by averaging embeddings.

- Key reason: Clear analysis showing corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses frequent, non-discriminative words.

- Novelty: Enforces during training that averaging suffices by using a corrupted global context, bridging post-hoc averaging heuristics and learned document-vector models.

- Theory: Uses a second-order Taylor expansion to show corruption yields an analytic regularizer that scales with word frequency and prediction confidence.

- Empirical (IMDB): Matches or outperforms Paragraph Vector and several baselines on sentiment, with fair reporting of two training settings.

- Empirical (Wikipedia): Consistently lower classification error across embedding sizes with supportive t-SNE visualizations.

- Empirical (SICK): Competitive on semantic relatedness given focus on longer documents rather than short sentences.

- Word embeddings: Substantial improvements on word analogy tasks, indicating the regularizer improves word-space quality.

- Efficiency: Complexity independent of number of documents, very fast test-time encoding, and training-speed gains from corruption with CPU timings shown.

- Clarity/Reproducibility: Method clearly described and code provided.

- Weakness: Corruption-as-regularization relies on a second-order approximation and particular negative-sampling assumption; causal claims need stronger ablations.

- Weakness: Baseline fairness concerns—PV variants, tuning, and Skip-Thought domain adaptation could affect comparisons.

- Weakness: Lack of unsupervised bag-of-ngrams + IDF or SIF baselines to contextualize gains.

- Weakness: Claim that Doc2VecC “does not rely on subsampling” is undermined by using subsampling across methods; direct comparisons with/without subsampling are missing.

- Weakness: Some implementation details under-specified (negative sampling distribution, number of negatives, window sizes) and CPU timings lack normalized throughput or GPU comparisons.

- Suggested ablations: No-corruption variant, varying q, corruption on local vs global contexts, and replacing corruption with explicit IDF weighting during training.

- Suggested clarifications: Specify negative sampling distribution and number of negatives, and ensure baseline configurations (PV DM/DBOW, inference steps, learning rates) are detailed.

- Suggested additional comparisons: Include SIF-weighted averages and unsupervised bag-of-ngrams + IDF baselines.

- Suggested measurements: Report tokens/sec and updates/sec, and breakdown speed gains attributable to corruption vs CBOW.

- Minor fixes: Correct typos/formatting, ensure tables/figures numbered and referenced, and tidy references.

- Question: What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used, and were they matched across baselines; does performance change with unigram^0.75?

- Question: How sensitive is performance to corruption rate q; provide accuracy vs q, speed vs q, and q=0 ablation.

- Question: Provide results with and without subsampling frequent words to validate claim of reduced reliance on subsampling.

- Question: How much does local context contribute relative to the global document vector; provide ablation removing each.

- Question: Which PV variants and hyperparameters were used; were DBOW/DM and sufficient inference steps tried?

- Question: Were embeddings fine-tuned equally across methods for SICK and IMDB; was Skip-Thought fine-tuned or vocabulary-expanded similarly?

- Question: At test time, are averages over token occurrences or unique types; does TF weighting help vs pure averaging?
