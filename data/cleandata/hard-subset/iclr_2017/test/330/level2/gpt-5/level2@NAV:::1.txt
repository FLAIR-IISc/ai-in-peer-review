Summary and contributions: The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for document representation that averages word embeddings and introduces a corruption mechanism during training. The key claims are: (1) the corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while preserving informative ones; (2) the model decouples complexity from corpus size and yields fast training and test-time inference; (3) despite its simplicity, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods across sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and produces stronger word embeddings than Word2Vec on analogy tasks. The paper provides an intuitive derivation via a second-order Taylor expansion, compelling empirical results, and publicly available code.

Decision: Accept. Primary reasons: (1) Strong empirical performance with a highly practical, scalable method; (2) clear, well-motivated idea with a principled analysis linking corruption to adaptive regularization, and careful positioning vs. Word2Vec and Paragraph Vector baselines.

Supporting arguments: The problem is timely—learning document vectors that are both expressive and efficient. The approach is well motivated by prior observations about linear compositionality of word embeddings and ties to dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders. The literature coverage is appropriate and the baseline set is reasonable for the tasks considered; comparisons include BoW, SDA/DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought, and LSTM-based methods where applicable. Results are consistent and convincing: large gains on IMDB and Wikipedia classification with notable efficiency; a thorough word analogy evaluation supports the claim that the corruption improves word embeddings. The theoretical approximation is standard and plausibly explains the observed regularization effects. Reproducibility is enhanced by releasing code.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not central to my decision):
- Clarify and justify the choice of negative sampling distribution Pv; a uniform sampler differs from common unigram^0.75 practice and may affect results.
- Provide an ablation on the corruption rate q and on removing local context to isolate contributions of each component.
- Report more training details (epochs, learning rates, optimizer, context window, subsampling thresholds) to strengthen reproducibility; consider releasing the Wikipedia split.
- Discuss limitations of averaging (loss of order/compositionality) and when sequence models are preferable; briefly consider hybrid weighting (e.g., learned IDF-like weights).
- References contain formatting inconsistencies; please clean for camera-ready.

Questions for the authors:
- How sensitive are results to the negative sampling distribution and number of negatives?
- What is the effect of q across tasks, and does an adaptive q help?
- Do gains persist when using subword embeddings or modern baselines like fastText-style averaging?
- On SICK, were any supervised signals used during fine-tuning beyond unsupervised sentence modeling?
- Can you quantify the regularization effect: e.g., distribution of embedding norms vs. frequency before/after training?
