- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings trained jointly with random-word corruption.  
- Corruption yields a data-dependent regularizer (via second-order Taylor expansion) that suppresses frequent uninformative words and preserves informative/rare words.  
- Model size depends only on vocabulary (no per-document parameters), trains as efficiently as Word2Vec with negative sampling, and enables very fast test-time inference (single average).  
- Empirically matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on sentiment, document classification, semantic relatedness, and word analogy tasks while being substantially faster.  
- Paper is clearly written, well-motivated, provides code, and demonstrates qualitative/quantitative evidence supporting the theory.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Elegant, practical approach combining a principled corruption-based regularizer with a minimal, scalable architecture.  
- Key reason 2: Broad and favorable empirical evaluation with clear efficiency benefits at train and test time.  
- Motivation/positioning: Addresses Paragraph Vector pain points (parameter growth and slow inference) while preserving Word2Vec simplicity and enforcing document-level compositionality.  
- Theory: Clear corruption-as-regularization derivation connected to dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders, explaining empirical behavior.  
- Simplicity & scalability: Keeps only U and V, uses negative sampling, and demonstrates concrete timing advantages.  
- Empirical breadth: Strong results across IMDB, 100-class Wikipedia, SICK, and word analogy, often outperforming baselines.  
- Analysis: Provides evidence that frequent uninformative words are suppressed by the learned regularizer.  
- Reproducibility: Code and dataset/settings described.  
- Baseline fairness/details needed: clarify negative sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75), PV variant(s) and hyperparameters, and the fairness of Skip-thought comparisons.  
- Suggest including/Discussing additional baselines: weighting schemes like SIF/Arora and first-PC removal for averaging methods.  
- Ablations & sensitivity requested: performance vs corruption rate q, role of context vs document vector, effect of Mikolov subsampling vs no subsampling, and performance vs document length.  
- Theoretical clarifications requested: accuracy of Taylor approximation and effects of non-binary counts or alternative corruption schemes on the regularizer.  
- Implementation details requested: exact hyperparameters, variance across runs, and hardware/throughput/memory measurements for speed claims.  
- Scope & limitations: acknowledge loss of word order and suggest extensions (n-grams/positional averaging); discuss OOV handling and vocabulary updates.  
- Questions to authors: negative sampling distribution and negatives used; sensitivity of Doc2VecC to these; performance vs q in [0.5,0.95]; which PV variants and tuning details; results without subsampling; experiments with frequency-proportional corruption or count-based x; SICK results without fine-tuning.
