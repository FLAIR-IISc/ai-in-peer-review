- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaged word embeddings with a corruption mechanism during training.
- Claims corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while preserving informative ones.
- Claims the model decouples complexity from corpus size, enabling fast training and test-time inference.
- Claims Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and improves word embeddings on analogy tasks.
- Provides an intuitive derivation via a second-order Taylor expansion, strong empirical results, and publicly released code.
- Decision: Accept.
- Primary reason: strong empirical performance with a practical, scalable method.
- Primary reason: clear, well-motivated idea with principled analysis linking corruption to adaptive regularization and careful baseline comparisons.
- Approach is timely and ties to observations about linear compositionality and to dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders.
- Literature coverage is appropriate and baselines (BoW, SDA/DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought, LSTM methods) are reasonable.
- Results show large gains on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, notable efficiency, and improved word analogy performance.
- Theoretical approximation is standard and plausibly explains observed regularization effects.
- Releasing code enhances reproducibility.
- Ask authors to clarify and justify the negative sampling distribution Pv choice (uniform vs unigram^0.75).
- Request ablation studies on corruption rate q and removal of local context to isolate component contributions.
- Ask for more training details (epochs, learning rates, optimizer, context window, subsampling thresholds) and consider releasing the Wikipedia split.
- Suggest discussing limitations of averaging (loss of order/compositionality) and when sequence models are preferable; consider hybrid/learned weighting (IDF-like).
- Note references contain formatting inconsistencies to be fixed for camera-ready.
- Questions: sensitivity to negative sampling distribution and number of negatives?
- Questions: effect of q across tasks and whether an adaptive q helps?
- Questions: do gains persist with subword embeddings or fastText-style averaging?
- Questions: on SICK, were any supervised signals used during fine-tuning beyond unsupervised sentence modeling?
- Questions: can the authors quantify the regularization effect (e.g., embedding norm distribution vs. frequency before/after training)?
