This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, enhanced with a corruption mechanism acting as a regularizer. The approach aims to address limitations of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors by decoupling model complexity from corpus size and improving test efficiency. The work demonstrates strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, often matching or exceeding state-of-the-art results.

The decision is to **accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and effective approach to document representation learning with a compelling combination of simplicity and performance. The empirical results are convincing and demonstrate the advantages of Doc2VecC over existing methods.

The strengths of the paper lie in its clear motivation, sound methodology, and thorough experimental evaluation. The corruption mechanism is a clever idea, functioning as a data-dependent regularizer that improves the quality of learned embeddings, particularly by suppressing less informative words. The reported efficiency gains in both training and testing are also significant. The comparisons to a wide range of baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, strengthen the claim of improved performance. The ablation study related to the data-dependent regularization demonstrates the effectiveness of the corruption mechanism.

However, there are areas where the paper could be improved. Firstly, while the paper mentions the use of a cutoff during vocabulary creation for the Wikipedia dataset, it doesn’t detail the rationale behind this specific cutoff (10). Justifying this choice would be beneficial. Secondly, the paper could benefit from a discussion of the limitations of the approach. Are there specific types of documents or datasets where Doc2VecC might underperform compared to other methods? Addressing this would demonstrate a more nuanced understanding of the model's capabilities. Thirdly, the explanation of why Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task could be elaborated. Is it solely due to the high capacity, or are there other contributing factors? Lastly, although the code is available, the specific hyperparameter settings used in each experiment could be more clearly stated in the main text or supplementary material to facilitate reproducibility.

Specific questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the rationale behind choosing a vocabulary cutoff of 10 for the Wikipedia dataset, and how sensitive the results are to this parameter?
*   Are there specific characteristics of documents or datasets where you anticipate Doc2VecC might not perform as well as other methods, and if so, why?
*   Can you provide more insight into why Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task? Is it solely due to its high capacity, or are other aspects of the model contributing to this result?
*   Could you provide a more detailed list of the hyperparameters used for each experiment, either in the main text or supplementary material, to improve reproducibility?
