## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient document representation learning framework. The core idea – representing documents as the average of word embeddings and incorporating a corruption model to regularize learning – is well-motivated and builds upon existing work in word and document embeddings like Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The authors convincingly argue that their approach addresses limitations of previous methods, specifically the scaling issues of Paragraph Vectors and the need for post-processing in other averaging-based approaches. The claimed benefits of simplicity, training speed, and competitive performance on several downstream tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness) are promising.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and well-justified, and the experimental results demonstrate its effectiveness, matching or outperforming state-of-the-art methods. The scalability highlighted in the abstract is a significant advantage.

The support for the claims is strong. The paper provides comprehensive experimental results across diverse tasks, comparing Doc2VecC against established baselines. The ablation studies, particularly the analysis of the corruption model’s impact and comparison with denoising autoencoders, are particularly valuable. The inclusion of training time comparisons further strengthens the claim of efficiency. The results consistently show Doc2VecC performing competitively, and in some cases, surpassing existing methods. The analysis demonstrating the regularization effect of the corruption model is also well-presented.

The paper’s simplicity is a key strength. It allows for efficient training and representation generation, which is crucial for large-scale applications. The connection to data-dependent regularization is a nice theoretical insight that explains the observed performance gains. 

However, the paper could be strengthened with further clarification. It would be helpful to understand *why* Doc2VecC performs better on longer documents compared to Skip-thought Vectors, beyond simply stating the limitation of RNNs with long sequences. Additionally, while the ablation studies are good, more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process (e.g., the range of values explored for the corruption rate ‘q’) would be valuable.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the specific characteristics of the IMDB movie review dataset that might contribute to the superior performance of Doc2VecC compared to Skip-thought Vectors?
*   What was the range of values explored for the corruption rate (q) during hyperparameter tuning, and what justification was used for the selected value of 0.9?
*   While the paper mentions initializing word embeddings with pre-trained GloVe vectors, does the performance significantly change if the embeddings are randomly initialized?
