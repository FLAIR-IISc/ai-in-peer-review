This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings, incorporating a corruption model to regularize the learning process. The core idea – representing documents as the average of their word embeddings, enforced through learning – is simple and well-motivated by observations of compositional semantics in Word2Vec. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being computationally efficient.

**Decision:** Accept. The simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results of Doc2VecC make it a valuable contribution. The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation and methodology.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The approach of enforcing a meaningful document representation via averaging during learning, coupled with the corruption mechanism, is a novel and sensible extension of Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The connection to data-dependent regularization provides a strong theoretical justification.
* **Empirical Performance:** The extensive experimental results demonstrate that Doc2VecC performs comparably to, or better than, state-of-the-art methods across diverse tasks. The consistent outperformance, especially considering the model’s simplicity, is compelling. The results on the Wikipedia dataset are particularly impressive.
* **Efficiency:** The authors clearly articulate and demonstrate the computational efficiency of Doc2VecC, both in training and, critically, in generating representations for unseen documents. This is a significant advantage over methods like Paragraph Vectors that require expensive inference at test time.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and the concepts are explained in a clear and accessible manner. The inclusion of the theoretical justification (the Taylor expansion and regularization analysis) enhances the paper’s credibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Corruption Rate Analysis:** While the authors mention that Doc2VecC is not sensitive to the corruption rate 'q', it would be beneficial to include a small ablation study in the experiments section demonstrating this claim more formally. A plot showing performance variation with different ‘q’ values would be informative.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper would benefit from a brief discussion about the sensitivity of the model to other hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension, learning rate). This would provide additional guidance for practitioners.
* **Comparison to FastText:** The authors compare to a variety of baselines, but do not include FastText, which also uses subword information and can be very efficient. Including a comparison would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Qualitative Examples:** While quantitative results are strong, including a few qualitative examples of document representations (e.g., visualizing the vectors with t-SNE and highlighting differences between Doc2VecC and other methods) would provide further intuition.
* **Clarify "Unbiased Corruption":** While the equation (1) defines the corruption process, a more intuitive explanation of *why* setting uncorrupted dimensions to 1/(1-q) makes the corruption “unbiased” would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The regularization analysis in section 3.1 is based on a Taylor expansion. What are the limitations of this approximation, and could it potentially affect the observed performance?
* In the word analogy experiments, Paragraph Vectors performs poorly. Is this behavior consistent across different datasets, and can you offer a more detailed explanation for this result beyond the model capacity issue?
* Could you elaborate on the computational cost of Doc2VecC compared to Word2Vec for very large vocabularies (e.g., > 1 million words)? Does the scaling behavior remain favorable?
* The authors mention that Doc2VecC does not rely on the frequency subsampling technique. Is this entirely true or does some benefit accrue from its inclusion, even if less significant than with Word2Vec+AVG?
