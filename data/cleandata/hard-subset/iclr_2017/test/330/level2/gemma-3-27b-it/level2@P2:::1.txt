This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings with a corruption model to regularize the learned representations. The approach aims to be efficient and competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the paper demonstrates strong empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a simple yet effective approach to document representation learning, backed by solid experimental results. The efficiency gains and competitive performance, alongside a clear explanation of the data-dependent regularization, make this a valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The paper consistently demonstrates that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods across diverse tasks. The results in Tables 1, 5, and 6 are particularly compelling, showing improvements in accuracy and efficiency.
* **Efficiency:** The authors explicitly address the computational cost of document representation learning, highlighting the efficiency of Doc2VecC, especially at test time. This is a significant advantage for large-scale applications, as detailed in Table 2.
* **Clear Motivation and Intuition:** The motivation for the approach – leveraging the compositionality of word embeddings and introducing a targeted regularization – is well-explained. The corruption model is intuitively presented as a way to focus on informative words.
* **Data-Dependent Regularization:** The detailed explanation of how the corruption model acts as a data-dependent regularization (Section 3.1) is a key strength of the paper. The derivation connects the corruption process to the Hessian of the log-likelihood, providing a theoretical justification for the observed benefits.
* **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors conduct a thorough set of experiments, comparing Doc2VecC to a range of baselines. The inclusion of various datasets and tasks strengthens the validity of the results.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Related Work:** While the related work section covers relevant topics, it could be strengthened by a more detailed discussion of recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., ELMo, BERT). How does Doc2VecC compare to these more complex models? A brief comparison could position the work more effectively.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that Doc2VecC is not sensitive to the corruption rate 'q'. Providing a small ablation study showing performance variations with different 'q' values would further support this claim.
* **Vocabulary Cutoff:** The choice of vocabulary cutoff (10 for Wikipedia, 100 for IMDB) seems somewhat arbitrary. Briefly justifying these choices or exploring their impact on performance would be beneficial.
* **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis (e.g., examining misclassified examples) could provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of Doc2VecC.
* **Code Availability:** The authors mention code availability, which is excellent. Ensuring the code is well-documented and easily reproducible would further enhance the impact of the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you elaborate on the computational cost of the Hessian calculation mentioned in Section 3.1? Is this a significant bottleneck in practice, and how is it addressed?
* How does Doc2VecC perform on datasets with long documents, beyond the datasets tested in this paper?
* In the semantic relatedness task, you mention that Doc2VecC is slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Do you have any insights into why this is the case?
* Could you provide more details on the hardware used for the experiments, particularly the size of the vocabulary and the training corpus?
