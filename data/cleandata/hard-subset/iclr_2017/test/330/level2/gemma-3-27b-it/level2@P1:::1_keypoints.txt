- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings with a corruption model to regularize learning.  
- Simple, well-motivated extension of Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors leveraging compositional semantics.  
- Empirically competitive or superior on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Highly efficient in training and especially for generating representations of unseen documents.  
- Decision: Accept — simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results make it valuable.  
- Novelty: enforcing averaging during learning plus corruption is novel and justified by data-dependent regularization.  
- Empirical strength: extensive experiments with particularly strong results on the Wikipedia dataset.  
- Efficiency advantage: faster and cheaper inference compared to Paragraph Vectors.  
- Clarity: well-written paper with theoretical justification via Taylor expansion and regularization analysis.  
- Suggestion: include an ablation study/plot for sensitivity to the corruption rate q.  
- Suggestion: discuss sensitivity to other hyperparameters (embedding dimension, learning rate).  
- Suggestion: add comparison to FastText (subword modeling and efficiency).  
- Suggestion: provide qualitative examples/visualizations (e.g., t-SNE) of document vectors.  
- Suggestion: clarify intuitively why scaling uncorrupted dimensions by 1/(1-q) yields an “unbiased” corruption.  
- Question: limitations of the Taylor expansion approximation and its potential impact on performance?  
- Question: is poor Paragraph Vectors performance on word analogy consistent across datasets and why beyond capacity issues?  
- Question: how does Doc2VecC’s computational cost scale versus Word2Vec for very large vocabularies (e.g., >1M)?  
- Question: is Doc2VecC truly independent of frequency subsampling, or does subsampling still provide some benefit?
