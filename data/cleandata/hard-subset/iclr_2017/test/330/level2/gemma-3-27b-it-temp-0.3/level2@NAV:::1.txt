## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient document representation learning framework. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” model that regularizes the embeddings, favoring informative words and suppressing common ones. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance to state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while also being computationally efficient.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is well-motivated, and the experimental results convincingly demonstrate its effectiveness. The simplicity of the model and its efficiency are particularly appealing, addressing limitations of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors regarding parameter count and inference time. 

The paper clearly articulates the problem of document representation learning and positions Doc2VecC within the existing literature. The motivation for the corruption model, framed as a data-dependent regularization, is well-explained and theoretically sound. The experimental setup is comprehensive, comparing Doc2VecC against a strong set of baselines across diverse tasks. The results consistently show Doc2VecC performing favorably, and the ablation study highlighting the benefit of the corruption mechanism is insightful. The authors also acknowledge the limitations of their approach, such as potential issues with very long documents, which is commendable.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification in a few areas. While the authors mention the use of a cutoff for vocabulary size, the specific criteria for selecting this cutoff (e.g., frequency threshold) could be detailed. Additionally, the impact of the corruption rate 'q' on performance could be explored more thoroughly. While they state it’s not sensitive, a small sensitivity analysis would be beneficial. Finally, the discussion of why Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB but not on semantic relatedness could be expanded. 

I would appreciate it if the authors could elaborate on the following: 
*   What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism itself during training?
*   How does the performance of Doc2VecC change with different embedding dimensions beyond those already explored?
*   Could the authors provide examples of words that are effectively suppressed by the corruption model, illustrating its impact on the learned embeddings?
