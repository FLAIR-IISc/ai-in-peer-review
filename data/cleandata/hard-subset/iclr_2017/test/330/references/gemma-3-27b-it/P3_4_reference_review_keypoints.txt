- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings enforced during training via random word corruption for efficiency and regularization.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions—conceptually simple, effective, and computationally efficient.
- Novelty: Enforcing compositional averaging during training with corruption differentiates it from post-hoc composition methods.
- Theoretical soundness: Taylor-expansion derivation provides a data-dependent regularization explanation for the corruption mechanism.
- Empirical results: Competitive or state-of-the-art on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness tasks with notable training/inference speed advantages.
- Presentation: Paper is well-written with helpful figures and clear notation.
- Ablation study needed: More detailed analysis of corruption rate q and a performance vs. q plot recommended.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Report sensitivity to embedding dimension and window size and their impact on performance and cost.
- Baseline comparison: Include a simple average of pre-trained Word2Vec (no fine-tuning/corruption) as a clearer baseline.
- Qualitative analysis: Provide broader visualization (e.g., t-SNE/UMAP) of embeddings beyond the Wikipedia example.
- Limitations: Explicitly discuss potential loss of word order and document types where averaging may be ineffective.
- Corruption mechanism question: Justify choice of uniform corruption and whether alternative strategies were explored.
- Theoretical robustness question: Clarify robustness of the derived regularization when Taylor assumptions are violated.
- Scalability question: Explain practical scaling to very large datasets (e.g., billions of documents) and any memory/training limits.
- Pre-trained embeddings question: Assess dependence on pre-trained embedding quality and whether joint training of word/document vectors was explored.
- Conclusion: Strong, valuable contribution; addressing the above points would further strengthen clarity and impact.
