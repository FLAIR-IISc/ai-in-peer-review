- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as averaged word embeddings trained with a corruption (word-dropout) mechanism to regularize and favor informative words.
- Claims advantages: decouples model complexity from corpus size, trains as efficiently as Word2Vec, and yields fast test-time inference.
- Decision: Accept — method is compelling, well-motivated, and shows clear improvements on multiple benchmarks.
- Novelty & motivation: Combining averaging with corruption-based training addresses scalability and post-processing limits of prior methods.
- Strong empirical results: Consistent improvements on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness versus strong baselines.
- Theoretical justification: Corruption interpreted as data-dependent regularization with a Taylor-expansion derivation supporting empirical gains.
- Efficiency: Training and inference times are attractive, making the approach practical for large-scale use.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Request more detailed analysis on sensitivity to corruption rate q, embedding size h, and context window.
- Dataset details: Ask for fuller preprocessing details (stemming, stop-word removal) to improve reproducibility.
- Visualization clarity: Figure 1 is cluttered; recommend clearer depiction of the corruption process.
- Skip-Thought discussion: Suggest expanding reasons for Skip-Thought’s poor performance (e.g., paragraph length, pre-training data differences).
- Question on corruption: Ask whether optimal corruption rate q relates to dataset traits (average length, vocab size, noise) and guidance for choosing q.
- Scalability question: Ask about memory limits with extremely large vocabularies and whether the embedding matrix becomes a bottleneck.
- Ablation clarification: Request clearer ablation separating contributions of local context and document corruption, with results for both.
- Comparison to newer methods: Ask how Doc2VecC compares to more recent document embedding approaches (e.g., Sentence Transformers).
- Overall verdict: Strong, novel, theoretically grounded, empirically validated paper that warrants acceptance.
