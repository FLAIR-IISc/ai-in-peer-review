This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vector learning method based on averaging word embeddings, combined with a corruption mechanism for regularization and efficiency. The core idea, leveraging the compositionality of word embeddings and introducing a corruption step during training, is well-motivated by prior work (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) and observed linguistic properties. The method boasts advantages in training and inference speed, parameter efficiency, and competitive performance on several benchmark tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness).

**Decision: Reject**

While the approach is conceptually sound and demonstrates promising results, the paper lacks sufficient detail and rigorous analysis to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The primary reason for rejection is the superficiality of the experimental evaluation. The improvements over baselines, while reported, are often not statistically significant or convincingly demonstrated, and several crucial experimental details are missing, hindering reproducibility and thorough assessment.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** Tables 1, 5, and 6 present performance improvements, but without any statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests, p-values). It is unclear whether the observed differences are genuine improvements or simply due to random variations in training. This is particularly concerning given the relatively small performance gains reported in some cases.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. For example, the hyperparameter tuning process isn’t clearly explained. What validation set was used? What search space was explored? Specific settings for the baselines are also lacking, making it difficult to assess a fair comparison. The choice of the cutoff value (10) for vocabulary size in the document classification task is arbitrary and requires justification.

3. **Limited Ablation Study:** The paper mentions that the corruption mechanism acts as a regularizer, but a dedicated ablation study to isolate the impact of the corruption is missing. Showing results with and without corruption (and potentially with different corruption rates) would significantly strengthen the argument for its importance.

4. **Over-Reliance on Existing Datasets:** The experiments rely heavily on standard datasets. While benchmarking is necessary, there is no attempt to demonstrate the effectiveness of Doc2VecC in a more novel or challenging scenario. A more targeted evaluation on a domain-specific dataset or a task that specifically benefits from the method’s properties would be valuable.

5. **Discussion of Limitations is Missing:** The paper does not acknowledge potential limitations of the approach. For instance, does Doc2VecC perform poorly on documents with highly complex semantic structures? Are there specific types of text where the simple averaging approach breaks down?



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Statistical Testing:** Add statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to all tables reporting performance comparisons.
*   **Hyperparameter Details:**  Clearly specify the hyperparameter settings used for all algorithms, including search spaces and validation strategies.
*   **Ablation Study:** Include an ablation study to evaluate the impact of the corruption mechanism.
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform an error analysis to identify common failure cases of Doc2VecC and gain insights into its limitations.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide qualitative examples of document vectors learned by Doc2VecC and discuss their interpretability.
*   **Clarify the Benefit of Corruption:** The paper states that the corruption model introduces a data-dependent regularization.  A more intuitive explanation of *why* this regularization is beneficial (beyond the mathematical derivation) would be helpful.  What aspects of the word embeddings are being improved by this regularization?
* **Reproducibility:** Make the code and datasets readily available, and provide detailed instructions for reproducing the results. This will significantly increase the credibility and impact of the work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the corruption rate (q=0.9), how sensitive are the results to changes in this parameter? Have you explored a wider range of values, and if so, what were the observed effects on performance?
2.  The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative." Can you provide examples of specific words whose embeddings are significantly altered by the corruption mechanism compared to Word2Vec?
3.  What is the computational cost of the corruption step during training? Does it significantly impact the overall training time, or is it relatively negligible?
4. Can you provide a discussion on the sensitivity of the method to the choice of word embeddings used for initialization? Would using different pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe, FastText) significantly alter the results?
5.  Why was the skip-thought vector model not retrained for this task and instead, embeddings trained on a different corpus were used? Would retraining improve the performance?
