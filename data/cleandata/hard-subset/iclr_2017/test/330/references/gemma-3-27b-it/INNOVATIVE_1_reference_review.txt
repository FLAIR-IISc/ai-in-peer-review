This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to document vector representation learning. The core idea – representing documents as the average of word embeddings *during* training, coupled with a corruption mechanism – is refreshingly simple yet potentially powerful. The authors motivate this approach by observing the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and aim to address the scalability issues of Paragraph Vectors. They claim Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on several text understanding tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) while being computationally efficient.

**Reject.** While the idea is intriguing and the results promising, the paper feels somewhat underdeveloped and lacks sufficient depth of analysis to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The novelty is the primary driver here, but it's not fully exploited or convincingly demonstrated to be a significant leap forward.

Here’s a detailed breakdown of my concerns:

1. **Limited Novelty Justification:** The authors highlight the efficiency gains and regularization effect of the corruption mechanism. However, the connection between the corruption and the regularization is presented as a post-hoc justification via a Taylor expansion, rather than a core design principle. Why does *this specific* form of corruption lead to desirable regularization? Is it truly data-dependent in a meaningful way, or is it simply a form of dropout? A more thorough exploration of the regularization properties would strengthen the paper.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Depth:** The experiments are reasonably broad, covering several tasks. However, the ablation studies are weak. While they demonstrate the impact of the corruption, they don’t fully explore the sensitivity to the corruption rate *q*. The effect of different window sizes in the Word2Vec-like training process isn’t explored.  The comparison to baselines feels somewhat superficial – for example, simply stating Skip-thought Vectors performs poorly on the IMDB dataset without a deeper analysis of *why* is unsatisfying. The experiments on the word analogy task, while interesting, feel tacked on. How does Doc2VecC’s performance on analogy tasks relate to its performance on downstream tasks?

3. **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:** While the Taylor expansion provides some intuition, the paper lacks a more rigorous theoretical justification for why averaging word embeddings, even with corruption, should produce meaningful document representations. Exploring connections to other representation learning theories (e.g., information bottleneck) could strengthen the work.

4. **Overstated Claims:** Phrases like “Doc2VecC intrinsically makes sure document representation...capture semantics” are overly strong. The model *learns* representations, it doesn’t guarantee semantic capture. A more cautious tone is warranted.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Expand the related work section:** Discuss recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (ELMo, BERT, etc.) and how Doc2VecC relates to these approaches.
*   **Provide more details on the implementation:** What libraries were used? What were the hyperparameters (learning rate, embedding dimension, window size, etc.) for each experiment?
*   **Analyze the learned embeddings:** Visualize the document embeddings using t-SNE or UMAP for different datasets. Provide examples of documents that are close to each other in embedding space and explain why.
*   **Investigate the impact of vocabulary size:** How does Doc2VecC perform with different vocabulary sizes?
*   **Consider alternative corruption strategies:** Explore different ways to corrupt the document (e.g., masking entire phrases instead of individual words).



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The Taylor expansion seems primarily used to *justify* the regularization, rather than directly inform the model design. Was there any attempt to incorporate this regularization directly into the loss function, rather than relying on the corruption mechanism?
2.  You mention that Doc2VecC doesn’t rely on subsampling frequent words. Is this always true? Or is there a point where subsampling would still be beneficial, even with the data-dependent regularization?
3.  Given the simplicity of Doc2VecC, are there any known failure cases or types of documents where it performs poorly?
4.   How does Doc2VecC’s performance change as the document length increases? Is there a point where averaging word embeddings becomes less effective?
