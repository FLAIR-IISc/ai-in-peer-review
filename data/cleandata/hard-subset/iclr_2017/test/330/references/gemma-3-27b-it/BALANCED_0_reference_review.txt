This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vector learning approach that averages word embeddings, incorporating a corruption mechanism for efficiency and regularization. The central idea – that meaningful document representations can emerge from averaging embeddings learned with a corruption strategy – is compelling and well-motivated by observations about compositional semantics in Word2Vec.

**Decision: Reject**

While the idea is interesting and the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous evaluation to warrant acceptance at ICLR. Specifically, the core contribution (averaging word embeddings with corruption) feels incremental, and the empirical validation, while extensive, doesn’t fully demonstrate superiority over well-established baselines in a statistically convincing manner, particularly given the relatively small performance gains reported. Furthermore, the lack of ablation studies to specifically isolate the impact of the corruption mechanism weakens the argument for its importance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The concept of using averaged word embeddings for document representation is not new (references are provided in the paper acknowledging this). While the *combination* with corruption is presented as novel, the paper doesn't sufficiently highlight what distinguishes this approach beyond a regularization technique. The argument that this method *enforces* compositionality seems overstated; compositionality can emerge from other methods as well.

2. **Incremental Performance Gains:** The performance improvements reported across the datasets are modest. While Doc2VecC consistently outperforms some baselines, the margins are often small (e.g., a few percentage points in accuracy). Considering the complexity of the implementation and the existing strong baselines, the improvement doesn’t convincingly justify the method as a significant advancement. A more detailed statistical analysis to confirm the significance of these gains is required.

3. **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper claims the corruption mechanism is crucial for performance and speed. However, there’s no ablation study systematically varying the corruption rate (q) or removing it entirely to demonstrate its impact. Without this, it’s difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are directly attributable to the corruption or other factors.

4. **Lack of Theoretical Depth:** The derivation of the regularization term, while present, is somewhat hand-wavy and lacks a deeper theoretical justification. The connection between the corruption mechanism and the resulting regularization is asserted but not rigorously proven. Further theoretical exploration could strengthen the contribution.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Clarify Novelty:** The introduction and related work sections should explicitly address how Doc2VecC differs from and improves upon existing averaged-embedding approaches. What specifically makes this approach *unique* beyond the corruption mechanism?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate that the performance differences between Doc2VecC and the baselines are statistically significant.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct thorough ablation studies varying the corruption rate (q) and completely removing the corruption mechanism to quantify its impact on performance and training speed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, such as embedding dimensionality, window size, and learning rate.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide examples of documents where Doc2VecC generates particularly good or poor representations to gain a better understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.
* **Code Quality**: Ensure the code released is well-documented and easily reproducible.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost overhead introduced by the corruption mechanism during training? Is there a trade-off between corruption rate and training time?
2.  How does the performance of Doc2VecC vary with different corpus sizes and domain-specific datasets?
3.  The paper claims Doc2VecC naturally regularizes embeddings. Can you demonstrate this by visualizing the embedding space and comparing the distribution of embeddings for frequent vs. rare words with and without corruption?
4. The approximation using negative sampling is described in Eq. 3. How sensitive is the performance to the number of negative samples?
5. What is the impact of initializing the word embeddings with pre-trained embeddings like GloVe, rather than learning them from scratch?
