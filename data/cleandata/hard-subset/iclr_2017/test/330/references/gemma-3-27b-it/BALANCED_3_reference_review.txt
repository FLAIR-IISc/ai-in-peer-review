This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning method that averages word embeddings with a corruption mechanism for efficiency and performance. The core idea—that averaging word embeddings can produce meaningful document representations when learned appropriately—is simple yet potentially powerful, especially given the efficiency gains over methods like Paragraph Vectors.

**Reject.** While the idea is compelling and the experiments demonstrate competitive performance, the paper lacks sufficient depth in explaining *why* Doc2VecC works as well as it does, beyond the hand-wavy explanation of data-dependent regularization. The connection between the regularization and the improved performance isn’t convincingly established, and the experimental results, while positive, don't offer a clear advantage over existing methods to warrant acceptance at a conference like ICLR. The extensive comparison to baselines is appreciated, but the analysis feels somewhat superficial.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of compelling justification for the observed performance. The theoretical derivation of the regularization effect feels contrived and doesn't intuitively explain the gains. The empirical results show Doc2VecC matches or beats state-of-the-art, but the gains are often marginal, and the paper doesn't provide ablation studies to convincingly demonstrate the contribution of the corruption mechanism. The argument that the regularization “favors rare or informative words” is a common trope in machine learning and requires more rigorous evidence. Without a better understanding of *how* and *why* Doc2VecC achieves its results, it's difficult to assess its true contribution.

Secondly, the paper is overly reliant on referencing existing work without fully explaining its relevance to the presented approach. For example, the connection to Denoising Autoencoders is mentioned but not deeply explored in terms of the advantages of Doc2VecC over that method.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Deepen the Theoretical Analysis:** The derivation of the regularization effect needs to be more clearly explained and motivated. What specifically does this regularization achieve in terms of the learned embedding space? Can you provide a more intuitive understanding of why penalizing frequent words is beneficial in this context?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Crucially, conduct ablation studies. What happens if you remove the corruption mechanism? Does the performance degrade significantly? What if you vary the corruption rate (q)? These experiments are essential to demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.
*   **Visualization of Embeddings:** Provide more insightful visualizations of the learned word and document embeddings. t-SNE visualizations are a good start (as provided for the Wikipedia dataset), but consider using other techniques to understand the structure of the embedding space.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters (embedding dimension, corruption rate, learning rate, etc.).
*   **Explain Choice of Baselines:** Justify the choice of baselines more thoroughly. Why were these particular methods selected for comparison?
*   **Clarify Terminology:** The notation is dense. While reasonably standard, more explanation and consistent use of terms would improve readability.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide some qualitative error analysis. Look at examples where Doc2VecC fails and try to understand why.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer, beyond the Taylor expansion approximation?
*   What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism, and how does it impact the overall training time?
*   Have you experimented with different corruption strategies (e.g., masking entire phrases instead of individual words)?
*   What are the limitations of Doc2VecC? Are there specific types of datasets or tasks where it is likely to perform poorly?
*   How does the performance of Doc2VecC scale with the size of the vocabulary and the training corpus?
*   You mention in the conclusion "averaging word embeddings can produce meaningful document representations when learned appropriately." What, specifically, constitutes "learned appropriately" and how does Doc2VecC achieve this?
