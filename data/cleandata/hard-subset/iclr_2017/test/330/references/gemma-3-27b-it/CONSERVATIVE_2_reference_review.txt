This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism involving random word removal during training. The authors motivate this by observing the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and aim to create a computationally efficient and effective document vectorization technique. They claim Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being more efficient than methods like Paragraph Vectors.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging the compositional properties of word embeddings via averaging is reasonable, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous evaluation to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The presented approach feels incremental and doesn’t demonstrate a significant leap forward compared to established methods. The empirical validation, despite claiming SOTA results, is often incremental and lacks a convincing demonstration of superiority given the effort required to achieve it.

The core issue lies in the lack of substantial justification for the "corruption" mechanism. While presented as data-dependent regularization, the explanation feels hand-wavy and the benefit isn’t clearly demonstrated beyond stating it speeds up training. The analysis regarding the regularization effect (Section 3.1) relies on approximations and lacks a clear, intuitive explanation of *why* this particular form of regularization is beneficial. Simply stating it favors rare/informative words isn't enough; empirical evidence demonstrating this effect is needed. The comparisons to baselines, while extensive, often show only marginal improvements, which are not substantial enough to justify a new method, especially considering the simplicity of the core idea.

Furthermore, the experimental setup feels somewhat imbalanced. The constant claim of beating "state-of-the-art" is weakened by comparing against a diverse set of methods with varying complexities and training procedures. For example, Skip-thought Vectors is criticized for its length handling, but it was trained on a different corpus and wasn’t retrained for a fair comparison. Claiming Doc2VecC is faster is helpful, but the speed comparisons lack context (e.g., hardware specifications, implementation details). The authors also rely heavily on comparisons to Paragraph Vectors, but this comparison is somewhat predictable given the well-known scalability issues of the latter. A more compelling comparison would be against more recent and efficient alternatives that address similar problems.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a thorough ablation study to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism. How much does it contribute to performance compared to simply averaging word embeddings *without* corruption?
*   **Regularization Analysis:** Provide more concrete evidence of the data-dependent regularization effect. Show examples of how the embeddings of frequent vs. rare words change with and without corruption. Visualize the embedding space to demonstrate the impact of regularization.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the model to the corruption rate (q).
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Strengthen the theoretical motivation for the corruption mechanism. Is there a connection to existing regularization techniques?
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include more recent and competitive baselines, such as newer transformer-based sentence/document embedding methods.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.
*   **Code Quality:** Ensure the publicly available code is well-documented and easy to reproduce the results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the specific form of corruption used in Doc2VecC is beneficial?
*   What is the computational cost of the corruption process itself, and how does it compare to the savings gained from faster training?
*   Did you explore other forms of regularization, and if so, how did they compare to the corruption mechanism?
*   Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the hardware and software used for the experiments to ensure reproducibility?
*   Is the performance gain consistent across different datasets and domains?
