The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The authors argue this approach is computationally efficient, introduces a data-dependent regularization, and achieves state-of-the-art performance on several text understanding tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging averaging and corruption for document representation is interesting, the paper suffers from a concerning lack of rigor in both presentation and experimental evaluation. The numerous minor flaws accumulate to create a sense of carelessness that undermines confidence in the results.

The most significant issue is the sheer volume of minor errors and inconsistencies throughout the manuscript. For instance, the notation is not consistently applied (e.g., different uses of subscripts and superscripts), and there are several instances of poorly defined variables. The description of the corruption mechanism, while intuitively understandable, lacks precise mathematical formalization beyond Equation (1), making it difficult to fully grasp its implications. The connection between the Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 and the resulting regularization is not clearly articulated and feels hand-wavy. 

The experimental section, while extensive, is also problematic. The baseline comparisons are not always fair. For example, the Skip-thought Vectors model is used without re-training, potentially disadvantaging it. Furthermore, the justification for choosing specific hyperparameter settings is often lacking. The claims of improved test efficiency (e.g., Paragraph Vectors taking 4 minutes vs. 7 seconds) are presented without sufficient detail about the hardware and software environment. The discussion of results frequently relies on qualitative observations ("Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics...") rather than quantitative analysis. The figures (especially Figure 3) lack proper labeling and clear interpretations.  The authors claim Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders under specific conditions, but do not offer a direct comparison to a similarly configured DEA model, hindering a proper assessment. The choice of vocabulary cutoff (10) for the Wikipedia dataset is arbitrary and not justified. 

Furthermore, the literature review, while broad, is not critically engaged with. The authors mention many related works but fail to adequately position their contribution within the existing landscape, offering little more than a laundry list of approaches. The references are also inconsistently formatted.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Notation:** Please standardize the notation throughout the paper. Define all symbols clearly and consistently.
*   **Mathematical Rigor:** Provide more detailed mathematical derivations and explanations, especially for the corruption mechanism and the regularization term.
*   **Experimental Details:**  Provide complete details about the experimental setup, including hardware specifications, software versions, and hyperparameter tuning procedures. Report confidence intervals or standard deviations for all results. Ensure fair comparisons with baselines.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of individual components of Doc2VecC, such as the corruption mechanism.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a qualitative error analysis to identify the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   **Formatting:** Carefully proofread the manuscript for typos, grammatical errors, and inconsistencies in formatting. Ensure all references are correctly formatted.
*   **Explain the choice of q=0.9**. What happens with different values?
*   **How sensitive is the model to the vocabulary size?**
*   **Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the unbiased mask-out/drop-out corruption?** What are the advantages of this specific approach compared to other corruption techniques?
*   **What is the computational cost of generating the document representation for a document of length T?** How does this compare to other methods?
