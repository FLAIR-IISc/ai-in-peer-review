This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vectorization method that averages word embeddings, incorporating a “corruption” mechanism – random word removal – during training. The authors claim this approach is efficient, scalable, introduces a data-dependent regularization, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

Reject. While the idea is straightforward and potentially interesting, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance. The performance improvements reported are incremental and not substantial enough to justify a new method, especially given the established baselines. The empirical evaluation, while extensive, feels more like a collection of results rather than a convincing demonstration of superiority.

The core idea of averaging word embeddings isn’t novel; it’s a common baseline. The “corruption” mechanism is presented as a key innovation, but its benefit feels largely anecdotal and insufficiently explored. The authors claim it acts as a data-dependent regularizer, and provide some derivation to support this, but the practical impact of this regularization isn’t thoroughly investigated. The experiments demonstrate performance gains, but these are often marginal and sometimes dependent on specific parameter settings. The comparison to strong baselines, such as Skip-thought Vectors and LSTM-based models, is not always favorable, and the authors’ explanations for these discrepancies feel somewhat dismissive. The paper relies heavily on claims of efficiency, but lacks a detailed computational analysis that definitively shows Doc2VecC’s advantages in terms of training or inference time, beyond superficial statements in Table 2. The ablation studies are not extensive enough to understand the true contribution of each component of the model. For example, a more thorough comparison with a simple average of word embeddings *without* the corruption mechanism is needed.

Additional feedback:

*   **Novelty:** The paper needs to better articulate the specific contribution beyond simply combining existing ideas. What makes Doc2VecC fundamentally different from, and superior to, other averaging-based approaches, especially considering the limited gains observed?
*   **Regularization Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the data-dependent regularization. Visualize the regularization effect on word embeddings for different frequencies. Demonstrate how the corruption rate (q) affects performance and regularization strength.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Perform a more fair and comprehensive comparison to stronger baselines. Properly tune the hyperparameters of the baselines. Provide error bars or statistical significance tests to demonstrate the robustness of the results.
*   **Efficiency Analysis:** A more detailed timing analysis is needed. Quantify the training and inference time for different dataset sizes and vocabulary sizes. Compare the memory footprint of Doc2VecC to other methods.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more ablation studies to assess the impact of each component of the model. Specifically, test the performance of the model without the corruption mechanism.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the derivation of the data-dependent regularization is appreciated, it feels somewhat disconnected from the practical implementation and results. Clarify how this theoretical framework translates into observable benefits.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of Doc2VecC compared to other methods, particularly Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, for both training and inference?
*   How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of corruption rate (q)? Can you provide a learning curve showing the performance as a function of q?
*   What is the effect of the corruption mechanism on the learned word embeddings? Does it lead to more robust or generalizable embeddings?
*   Why do you believe Doc2VecC underperforms compared to Skip-thought Vectors on the semantic relatedness task, and what steps could be taken to address this?
*   Could you perform an experiment demonstrating the usefulness of the regularization term? For example, are embeddings from Doc2VecC more robust to noise in the input document?
