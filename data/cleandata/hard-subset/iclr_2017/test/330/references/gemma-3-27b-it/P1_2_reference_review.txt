## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary**

This paper proposes a novel and efficient document vector learning method called Doc2VecC. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism where words are randomly removed during training. The authors argue this approach is motivated by the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and introduces a data-dependent regularization, leading to improved performance and efficiency compared to existing methods like Paragraph Vectors. The paper demonstrates strong empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach to document vectorization that is both theoretically motivated and empirically strong. The efficiency gains, particularly in test time, are significant and the data-dependent regularization is a clever contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of leveraging the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and combining it with a corruption mechanism is insightful. The authors provide clear justification for their approach, drawing connections to existing work and highlighting the limitations of current methods. The rationale behind the corruption as a regularizer is well explained.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance across three diverse tasks, surpassing several strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors in some cases. The results presented in the tables and figures are convincing.
* **Efficiency:** The efficiency gains, especially the reduced inference time at test time, are a significant advantage. The authors clearly articulate how Doc2VecC decouples model complexity from corpus size, making it scalable to large datasets.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the data-dependent regularization using a Taylor expansion is a valuable addition. It provides a deeper understanding of why the corruption mechanism is effective.

**4. Additional Feedback**

* **Clarify Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention that the model isn’t highly sensitive to the corruption rate, it would be beneficial to include a small ablation study showing the performance variations with different corruption rates (q values).
* **Discussion of Negative Sampling:** The paper mentions using negative sampling, but doesn't provide details regarding the number of negative samples used.  Providing this information would enhance reproducibility.
* **Comparison to More Recent Methods:** While the baselines chosen are strong, the field of document representation has evolved since the publication of some of them.  Adding a comparison to more recent approaches (e.g., sentence transformers like BERT or Sentence-BERT) – even if only briefly – would strengthen the paper’s positioning.  Discussing *why* Doc2VecC might be advantageous even compared to these more complex models (e.g., simplicity, efficiency) would be helpful.
* **Visualizations of Embeddings:** While the t-SNE visualization for document vectors is helpful, including a similar visualization of the *word* embeddings learned by Doc2VecC and comparing them to Word2Vec word embeddings would be beneficial for understanding the impact of the document context on word representations.
* **Further Elaboration on Regularization:** The explanation of the data-dependent regularization is good, but could benefit from a slightly more intuitive explanation of *how* the regularization specifically helps to focus on informative words.  A concrete example would be helpful.

**5. Questions for Authors**

* The Taylor expansion used to derive the regularization relies on expanding around the mean.  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of different choices for the expansion point?
* In the semantic relatedness task, you mention fine-tuning the model on the SICK dataset. How does the performance change if you *don't* perform this fine-tuning?
* Could you provide more details on the computational resources (e.g., GPU, RAM) used for training the models, particularly for the larger datasets?  This would further help with reproducibility.
* You mention a significant improvement on the word analogy task.  Can you provide specific examples of analogies that Doc2VecC solves correctly while Word2Vec fails?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The proposed method is innovative, efficient, and empirically successful. Addressing the suggested improvements will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
