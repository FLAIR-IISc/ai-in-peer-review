This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vector learning method based on averaging word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism for improved performance and efficiency. The core idea is that by corrupting documents during training, the model learns to represent documents effectively using averaged word embeddings, reducing computational costs compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of convincing novelty and a disconnect between the presented results and the claims made. While the idea of averaging word embeddings is not new, the claimed improvements due to the “corruption” mechanism aren't thoroughly demonstrated as stemming *specifically* from this mechanism. The paper presents competitive results, but fails to adequately demonstrate *why* Doc2VecC performs well, beyond stating it’s due to the regularization effect of corruption.  The experimental setup doesn't sufficiently isolate the impact of the corruption technique.

The paper's claims are supported with experimental results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. However, the comparisons are often against a broad range of baselines, making it difficult to pinpoint the contribution of Doc2VecC specifically. The ablation studies are limited. While the authors mention that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on subsampling frequent words (unlike Word2Vec+AVG), this is presented as a benefit without a corresponding experiment demonstrating it. The analysis regarding the data-dependent regularization is descriptive rather than rigorously quantified. The authors claim that the corruption dampens frequent but uninformative words, but the evidence provided in Table 3 is anecdotal and doesn’t establish a clear connection between the corruption and the learned embeddings. The discussion of performance on the word analogy task also lacks depth. While Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec, the explanation focuses on Paragraph Vectors’ shortcomings, rather than the specific strengths of Doc2VecC. The visualization in Figure 3 is helpful but doesn't provide quantitative evidence.

Additional feedback:

*   **Clarify the novelty:** The paper should more clearly articulate what distinguishes Doc2VecC from existing approaches that also use averaged word embeddings. What specific aspect of the corruption mechanism is crucial and how does it overcome limitations of simpler averaging methods?
*   **Ablation Study:** A critical experiment is needed to directly assess the impact of the corruption mechanism. Train a Doc2VecC model *without* corruption and compare its performance to the model with corruption.
*   **Regularization Analysis:** Provide a more quantitative analysis of the data-dependent regularization. For example, analyze the distribution of embedding norms before and after corruption. Show how the regularization strength varies with word frequency and informativeness.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the model's performance to the corruption rate 'q'. Provide a plot showing how performance changes as 'q' is varied.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** Though efficiency is mentioned, a more detailed analysis of training and inference time compared to other models (beyond Table 2) would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical justification:** The derivation of the regularization effect in Section 3.1 feels somewhat hand-wavy. A more rigorous mathematical treatment would strengthen the argument.
*   **Regarding the datasets:** Explain the choice of dataset sizes, and the impact of these choices on the results.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide a detailed explanation of why you chose to use the specific corruption mechanism (randomly removing words) over other possible corruption strategies (e.g., masking words with a special token)?
*   What is the computational cost of approximating the probability in Equation (3) with negative sampling, and how does this contribute to the overall efficiency of the model?
*   Can you provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned word embeddings, showing how the corruption mechanism affects the representation of different types of words (e.g., frequent vs. rare, informative vs. uninformative)?
*   Are there specific types of documents or tasks where Doc2VecC is expected to perform particularly well or poorly? Why?
