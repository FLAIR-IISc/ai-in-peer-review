This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vectorization method that averages word embeddings, incorporating a corruption mechanism for efficiency and regularization. The authors claim several advantages: decoupling model complexity from corpus size, efficient training, data-dependent regularization, fast test-time vector generation, and state-of-the-art or competitive performance on several text understanding tasks.

Reject. While the idea is reasonably straightforward and builds on established concepts like Word2Vec, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous analysis that collectively diminish its contribution and raise serious concerns about the validity of the reported results. The presentation is also notably sloppy.

The paper is riddled with inconsistencies and imprecise language. For example, the statement “Doc2VecC has several desirable properties” is followed by a numbered list, yet the numbering is not consistently applied throughout the subsequent sections. Similarly, the notation section lacks clarity; the definitions of ‘x’ and ‘ct’ as BoW representations are confusing as they are presented without context regarding dimensionality or application within the model. The use of ‘v’ for both vocabulary size and a vector is highly problematic. The mathematical formulation, while attempting to be thorough, is prone to errors and lacks sufficient explanation. Equation (1) defines a corruption mechanism but doesn’t explicitly state whether it’s applied element-wise to the entire vector 'x' or dimensionally. Further, the explanation of how this relates to a Taylor expansion in section 3.1 feels rushed and incomplete; the connection between the expansion and the regularization term isn’t clearly established.

The experimental setup also contains several issues. While baselines are included, their implementation details are often vague. The choice of hyper-parameters is not justified. The statement "We did not re-train the Skip-thought vector models on this dataset" in section 4.5 raises a concern as the comparison isn't on an even playing field, and should be acknowledged in the limitations of the comparison. The authors mention using pre-trained GloVe vectors, but don't specify which version (e.g., dimensions, training data). The claim of “significantly outperforming” the competition in semantic relatedness (section 4.5) needs more quantitative justification beyond simply stating the results are better; what is the statistical significance? The time measurements (Table 2) are presented without specifying the hardware configuration beyond "a desktop with Intel i7 2.2Ghz cpu", which hinders reproducibility. The inclusion of figures (Figure 3 and 4) is good, but they are not adequately discussed within the text – what specific conclusions can be drawn from the visualizations? The tables are poorly formatted, lacking consistent decimal places or clarity in the headings.

The authors claim a data-dependent regularization, but the explanation feels hand-wavy. The connection between the regularization term and the benefit of suppressing common words requires more detailed justification. The authors note the importance of subsampling frequent words for Word2Vec+AVG but downplay its impact on Doc2VecC, which feels unjustified. The description of how the corruption model affects performance is also superficial.

Additional feedback:

*   **Clarify Notation:** Consistently define and use all symbols. Avoid reusing 'v' for different concepts.
*   **Improve Mathematical Rigor:** Provide more detailed derivations and explanations for the mathematical formulations. Ensure equations are correct and clearly explained.
*   **Elaborate on Experimental Details:** Provide full details of the experimental setup, including hyperparameter tuning, hardware configuration, and specific versions of pre-trained models.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all comparative results.
*   **Discussion of Visualizations:** Thoroughly discuss the insights gained from the visualizations.
*   **Address Limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the study, such as the uneven comparison with Skip-thought vectors and the lack of sensitivity analysis for the corruption rate.

Questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 and how it leads to the described regularization?
*   What specific strategies were used to tune the hyperparameters for each model and dataset?
*   Why is the corruption rate 'q' fixed at 0.9? Was any sensitivity analysis performed with different values of 'q'?
*   How does the performance of Doc2VecC vary with different embedding dimensions?
*   Can you elaborate on the implementation details of the baselines, specifically regarding the DEA model and the Word2Vec+IDF approach?
