This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach to document vector representation learning. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism during training to enhance performance and efficiency. The authors claim several advantages, including decoupling model complexity from corpus size, efficient training, implicit regularization, and competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings via averaging is intuitive, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet significant, flaws in presentation, execution, and justification. The sheer number of these issues collectively undermine confidence in the validity of the reported results and the novelty of the contribution.

The most glaring issue is the pervasive lack of rigor in the experimental setup. The comparisons with baselines are often incomplete or lack crucial details (e.g., hyperparameter settings, specific configurations used for publicly available methods). Table 1, for instance, presents accuracy results with little context regarding the SVM implementation or parameter tuning process. Furthermore, the claim of “reproducibility” via a GitHub link is undermined by the fact that the results presented in the paper aren’t directly obtainable from the provided code without substantial effort (and potentially, further modifications). 

The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization, while mathematically presented, feels overly convoluted and ultimately lacks a clear, intuitive explanation of *why* this regularization is beneficial in practice. The derivation in Section 3.1, while attempting to be rigorous, relies on approximations and assumptions that are not thoroughly discussed or justified. The assertion that it “naturally” dampens frequent but uninformative words requires stronger evidence beyond the qualitative examples in Table 3. 

The paper also contains numerous stylistic and formatting issues: inconsistent notation (e.g., different uses of boldface), grammatical errors, and a general lack of polish. The related work section, while extensive, feels like a laundry list of references rather than a critical analysis of the existing literature. The repeated use of phrases like "surprisingly" and "noticeably" weakens the objective tone of a scientific paper.  Even simple things, like not consistently defining abbreviations (e.g., CBoW) or failing to provide units for time measurements (Table 2), contribute to a sense of carelessness. The claim that Doc2VecC "significantly outperforms" methods in Section 4.5 is subjective and should be quantified with statistical significance tests, which are absent throughout. The visualization in Figure 3 is not properly explained. What axes are being displayed? What is the overall interpretation?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reproducibility:** The authors *must* provide a complete, runnable codebase with instructions to reproduce all reported results. This is non-negotiable.
*   **Statistical Significance:**  Include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support all claims of performance improvement.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Provide a detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process for all methods, including search spaces and validation procedures.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of the corruption mechanism. Does removing it significantly degrade performance?
*   **Clarify Regularization:** Provide a more intuitive explanation of how the corruption mechanism acts as a regularizer.
*   **Notation:** Ensure consistent and clear notation throughout the paper.
*   **Writing Quality:** Proofread the paper carefully for grammatical errors and stylistic issues.
*   **Baselines:** Include more recent and relevant baselines.
*   **Figure Captions:** Provide detailed and informative captions for all figures.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a clear and concise explanation of *why* the corruption mechanism improves performance, beyond the mathematical derivation?
2.  What specific hyperparameter tuning strategies were used for each baseline method?
3.  What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism during training? Does it significantly increase training time?
4.  The paper refers to reproducing the experiments with the code on GitHub. Can you confirm that all results in the paper can be readily reproduced by running the provided code without any modifications? If not, what steps are required?
5. Why is the performance on the IMDB dataset comparatively better than other datasets, like the SemEval dataset?
