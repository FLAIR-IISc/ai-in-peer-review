This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to document vector representation learning. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, enforced during training through a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words. The authors argue this implicitly introduces regularization, favoring informative words and boosting test efficiency. They demonstrate competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea is intriguing and conceptually simple, the paper feels underdeveloped and relies heavily on claims without sufficient justification or ablation studies. The results are presented as improvements, but the marginal gains aren’t compelling enough to outweigh the lack of detailed analysis, and the presented evidence is not convincing enough.

The primary weakness lies in the insufficient exploration of the “corruption” mechanism. The authors claim it acts as a data-dependent regularizer, but the justification feels hand-wavy. The analysis around *why* this corruption works is shallow. Is the improvement solely due to the regularization, or does the averaging benefit from the forced focus on remaining words? A critical ablation study systematically varying the corruption rate (q) and analyzing its impact on performance is missing. Additionally, the claim that Doc2VecC doesn’t rely on frequent word subsampling (unlike Word2Vec+AVG) is undercut by the fact that they *do* use subsampling – the benefit comes from the corruption being *additional* to subsampling, and this isn’t clearly demonstrated. Furthermore, the claim of speed-up seems to be based on the relative simplicity of the approach, but doesn't quantify the actual training time improvement against baselines beyond a single table.

The experimental evaluation, while broad, lacks depth. While the authors compare against several baselines, the parameter tuning details aren't sufficiently reported. Were baselines tuned optimally? The results on word analogy, while positive, are not thoroughly discussed in relation to the method’s design. The observation that Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec in this task, but Paragraph Vectors does poorly, isn't explained. The explanation regarding Skip-thought vectors and longer documents is plausible, but also requires more rigorous validation – perhaps shortening the documents in the IMDB dataset and observing the performance change. Finally, the visualization using t-SNE is informative but lacks quantitative evaluation of cluster separation.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **More detailed explanation of the regularization:**  The theoretical justification for the regularization effect of the corruption mechanism needs to be expanded and made more rigorous.  What specific properties of the Hessian matrix contribute to this regularization?
*   **Ablation Studies:** A comprehensive ablation study is crucial.  Specifically, compare Doc2VecC *with and without* the corruption mechanism, *with and without* word subsampling, and with different values of *q*.
*   **Parameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters, including the embedding dimension, learning rate, and corruption rate.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide some examples of how the learned document vectors capture semantic information.  Perhaps show nearest neighbors in the embedding space.
*   **Clarify Training Details:**  Provide more details on the training procedure, including the optimizer used, batch size, and early stopping criteria.
*   **Error Analysis:**  A brief error analysis on the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks could provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the approach.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed mathematical justification for why the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer, and specifically, how it differs from standard L1 or L2 regularization?
*   What is the computational cost of the corruption step during training, and how does it compare to the cost of training other baselines?
*   How does the performance of Doc2VecC change when using different types of corruption mechanisms (e.g., randomly replacing words with a mask token)?
*   You state that Doc2VecC is not sensitive to the corruption rate *q*.  Can you show results that corroborate this claim with a systematic variation of *q*?
*   How were the hyperparameters for the baseline models tuned? Was a consistent validation set used for all models?
