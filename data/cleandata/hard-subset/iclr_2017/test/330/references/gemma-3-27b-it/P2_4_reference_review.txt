## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” mechanism – randomly removing words during training. The authors argue this encourages the model to learn more robust and informative word embeddings, leading to better document representations, and offers benefits in terms of computational efficiency and regularization.  The paper demonstrates competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks compared to existing methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper tackles a relevant problem in text representation learning, presents a well-motivated and conceptually simple approach, and backs up its claims with thorough experimentation. The efficiency gains are also a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of explicitly enforcing compositionality through averaging word embeddings *during* training, combined with the corruption mechanism, is a reasonable and interesting approach. The authors clearly connect their work to existing literature (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) and highlight the limitations they address.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper presents a comprehensive set of experiments across diverse tasks. Doc2VecC consistently performs well, often outperforming established baselines like Paragraph Vectors and competitive alternatives like Skip-thought Vectors. The ablation study regarding the corruption mechanism (demonstrating its role as regularization) is particularly convincing.
* **Efficiency:**  The authors clearly articulate the computational benefits of their approach – decoupling model complexity from corpus size and faster inference times – which are important practical considerations. The results in Table 2 support this claim.
* **Clear Explanation of Data-Dependent Regularization:**  The mathematical derivation explaining how the corruption acts as a data-dependent regularization is well-presented and insightful. It provides a strong theoretical justification for the observed performance improvements.



**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the model being "not sensitive" to the corruption rate *q*.  It would be beneficial to include a small ablation study showing performance variation with different *q* values, even if the impact is minimal. A plot demonstrating this would be helpful.
* **Word Analogy Task Detail:** While the results on the word analogy task are positive, the description is somewhat brief. A table detailing performance on individual analogy types (e.g., semantic, syntactic) would provide a more nuanced understanding of the strengths of Doc2VecC embeddings.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** A brief discussion of potential limitations of Doc2VecC would strengthen the paper. For example, does the averaging approach inherently struggle with documents where word order is crucial (e.g., legal contracts)?
* **Code Availability:** The mention of code availability is great! Ensuring the code is well-documented and easily reproducible is crucial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Impact of Vocabulary Size:**  How does the performance of Doc2VecC scale with vocabulary size?  Are there any diminishing returns or practical limitations associated with very large vocabularies?
* **Corruption Mechanism Intuition:** The paper effectively *shows* that corruption works, but is there a more intuitive explanation for *why* it’s effective beyond the formal derivation? Does it encourage the model to focus on more discriminative words, and if so, how?
* **Alternative Corruption Schemes:** Were any other corruption schemes explored beyond the unbiased mask-out/drop-out approach? If so, what were the results, and why was this particular scheme chosen?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a clear contribution. The proposed Doc2VecC method is both theoretically sound and empirically validated.  Addressing the minor suggestions above will further enhance the paper's quality and impact.
