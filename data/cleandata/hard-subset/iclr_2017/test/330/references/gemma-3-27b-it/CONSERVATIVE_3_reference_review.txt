This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, combined with a “corruption” mechanism involving randomly removing words during training. The authors motivate this approach by the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning, and aim to create a computationally efficient alternative to methods like Paragraph Vectors. They claim their method achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being faster to train and test.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging the compositional properties of word embeddings is reasonable, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance. The core concept – averaging word embeddings – is not new, and the “corruption” mechanism feels largely ad-hoc without a strong theoretical justification beyond a somewhat hand-wavy Taylor expansion argument. The empirical results, while promising, are not presented convincingly enough to demonstrate a clear and substantial advantage over well-established baselines.

The primary concern is the lack of a compelling demonstration of novelty. The authors position Doc2VecC as distinct from simply averaging word embeddings, attributing the performance gain to the corruption mechanism. However, the benefits attributed to this corruption are not clearly isolated or demonstrated. The paper mentions a data-dependent regularization effect, but the explanation feels incomplete and lacks a thorough analysis of *how* this regularization contributes to the observed performance gains. Simply stating that it “favors rare or informative words” is insufficient; the authors need to show, perhaps through ablation studies, the specific impact of this regularization. Furthermore, comparing against a simple average of word embeddings *with* similar regularization techniques (e.g., weighting by IDF, or applying dropout directly to the word embeddings during averaging) would be crucial to isolate the unique contribution of their corruption scheme.

The experimental setup also raises concerns. While a reasonable set of baselines are included, the presentation of results feels somewhat selective. The claims of “state-of-the-art” performance require stronger evidence, particularly considering the maturity of the tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification). The performance gains aren't overwhelmingly significant and, in some cases, are reported alongside ambiguous statements like "Doc2VecC matches or beats…". More precise comparisons with error bars or statistical significance testing are needed. The choice of hyperparameters is not fully explained, and sensitivity analyses are missing. The large vocabulary size in the document classification task (107,691) is concerning, and it's unclear if this impacts the comparability of results. The comparison to Skip-thought vectors feels particularly weak, as the authors acknowledge the models were not retrained and the datasets have differing paragraph lengths, rendering the comparison unfair.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Theoretical Foundation:** Strengthen the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism. Is the Taylor expansion a crucial component, or is it merely a post-hoc rationalization? Explore alternative theoretical frameworks that might explain the observed benefits.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct rigorous ablation studies to isolate the impact of the corruption mechanism. Compare Doc2VecC to a simple average of word embeddings *with* comparable regularization techniques applied.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Perform a thorough sensitivity analysis of the key hyperparameters, especially the corruption rate *q*.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate the robustness of the performance gains.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide an error analysis to understand the types of errors made by Doc2VecC and identify areas for improvement.
*   **Reproducibility:** Ensure the code is fully reproducible and provide clear instructions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the Taylor expansion is critical to the performance of Doc2VecC? Is there a specific property of the Hessian matrix that contributes to the regularization effect?
2.  Have you experimented with alternative regularization techniques applied directly to a simple average of word embeddings (e.g., IDF weighting, dropout)? How does Doc2VecC compare to these methods?
3.  What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism during training? Does it significantly increase training time compared to a simple average of word embeddings?
4.  How does the choice of the corruption rate *q* impact performance, and is there an optimal value that depends on the dataset or task?
5.  Regarding the document classification task, how was the vocabulary cutoff of 10 chosen? Could this have impacted the performance comparison?
