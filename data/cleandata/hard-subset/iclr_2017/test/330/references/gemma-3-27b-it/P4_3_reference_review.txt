This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document vectorization method that averages word embeddings with a corruption mechanism. The core idea is to enforce meaningful document representations through averaging during training, contrasting with post-processing approaches. The authors motivate their work by observing the preservation of syntactic and semantic relationships within linear combinations of Word2Vec embeddings. They demonstrate improved performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject** with a primary reason being insufficient novelty and limited experimental justification of key claims. While the idea of averaging word embeddings is not new, the presented corruption mechanism doesn’t demonstrate enough practical advantage over existing techniques to warrant significant attention.

The paper's approach builds on existing work (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) and introduces a corruption step. However, the novelty of this corruption mechanism is not clearly established. The authors claim it acts as a data-dependent regularization, but the explanation, while mathematically detailed, doesn’t convincingly show *why* this regularization is superior to standard techniques like dropout or L1/L2 regularization already employed in similar models. The provided experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, but often only marginally better than baselines. The claims of significant improvements, particularly regarding test efficiency, are not strong enough to justify the added complexity. The paper heavily relies on the observation of regularities in word embeddings (e.g., "king" - "man" + "woman" = "queen") as motivation, a well-established phenomenon that doesn’t uniquely support the proposed method. Furthermore, the ablation study regarding the corruption rate (q=0.9) and its impact on training time is mentioned but not thoroughly presented with comparative timings.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the Corruption's Role:** The authors should provide a more intuitive explanation of *how* the corruption mechanism leads to better representations. A more focused analysis, perhaps comparing the learned word embeddings with and without corruption, would be beneficial.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Consider including more recent and stronger baselines, such as those based on transformers (e.g., Sentence-BERT).
*   **Ablation Studies:** A more comprehensive ablation study is needed to isolate the contribution of each component (averaging, corruption, data-dependent regularization). What happens when the corruption is removed? What happens with a different corruption strategy?
*   **Explain Parameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the model to the corruption rate (q). Is 0.9 optimal? How does performance change with different values of q?
*   **Computational Cost:** While test efficiency is mentioned, a more detailed computational analysis comparing training and inference times with other methods would be valuable.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Strengthen the theoretical motivation behind the data-dependent regularization. Is there a connection to other regularization techniques?
*   **Detailed Results:** Provide detailed results, including standard deviations, for all experiments.
*   **Question:** The paper mentions the benefit of the corruption model introducing data-dependent regularization. Could you elaborate on how the specific form of this regularization (penalizing frequent but uninformative words) is different from or superior to other regularization methods like L1 or L2 regularization applied to the word embeddings directly?
*   **Question:** The paper states that Doc2VecC reduces to a Denoising Autoencoder if context words are removed. However, the authors don't present a comparison to a standard Denoising Autoencoder implementation with a similar architecture. What were the results of such a comparison, and why was it not included in the main results?
