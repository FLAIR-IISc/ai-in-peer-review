## Review for "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" (Doc2VecC)

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, *enforced during training* through a corruption mechanism (randomly removing words).  The authors argue that this approach is computationally efficient, introduces a beneficial regularization effect, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on several text understanding tasks – sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. They provide a theoretical justification for the regularization and demonstrate empirical results supporting their claims.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions). The paper presents a conceptually simple yet effective method for document representation learning. The proposed Doc2VecC is well-motivated, performs competitively with existing approaches, and offers significant advantages in terms of training and inference efficiency. The inclusion of theoretical justification regarding the regularization is a strong point.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The idea of explicitly *enforcing* compositionality through averaging during training, coupled with the corruption mechanism, is a compelling approach. It differentiates itself from methods that perform composition as a post-processing step. The motivation drawing on the success of linear relationships in Word2Vec is logical.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The derivation of the data-dependent regularization through the Taylor expansion is a significant contribution. It provides a clear understanding of why the corruption mechanism is beneficial.  The explanation is relatively easy to follow, even for readers unfamiliar with the detailed mathematical derivations.
* **Empirical Results:** The comprehensive experimental evaluation across multiple tasks and datasets demonstrates the effectiveness of Doc2VecC. The consistent performance improvement over strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, is convincing.  The speed advantages, particularly at test time, are a major benefit.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to understand. The figure illustrating the model architecture is helpful, and the notation is clearly defined.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Study:** While the authors mention the influence of the corruption rate (q), a more detailed ablation study exploring the effect of different values of ‘q’ on performance would strengthen the analysis.  A figure showing performance vs. 'q' would be helpful.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a discussion of the sensitivity of the model to other hyperparameters, such as the embedding dimension (h) and the window size. How do these parameters influence performance and computational cost?
* **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:**  While the comparisons to complex models are valuable, including results against a very simple average of pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings *without* any fine-tuning or corruption would provide a clearer baseline for understanding the contribution of Doc2VecC’s training process.
* **Qualitative Analysis:**  A qualitative analysis of the learned document embeddings (e.g., visualizing them using t-SNE or UMAP) could provide further insights into the model's behavior and its ability to capture semantic relationships. The provided visualization is limited to the Wikipedia dataset.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper could be strengthened by explicitly acknowledging the limitations of the approach. For instance, does the averaging process inherently lose some information about word order?  Are there specific types of documents where Doc2VecC might perform less effectively?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the corruption mechanism:**  Why was a uniform corruption distribution chosen over other distributions (e.g., a distribution that favors removing less informative words)?  Were other corruption strategies explored?
* **Regarding the theoretical justification:**  The Taylor expansion relies on several assumptions.  How robust is the derived regularization in practice if those assumptions are slightly violated?
* **Regarding scaling to very large datasets:** While the paper highlights the computational efficiency, how does Doc2VecC scale to truly massive datasets (e.g., billions of documents)? Are there any practical limitations in terms of memory usage or training time?
* **Regarding the impact of pre-trained word embeddings:**  Does the performance of Doc2VecC significantly depend on the quality of the pre-trained word embeddings used for initialization?  Have you explored training the word embeddings jointly with the document representation learning process?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The proposed Doc2VecC method is promising, well-supported by empirical evidence, and offers practical advantages in terms of efficiency.  Addressing the suggestions above would further enhance the paper's clarity and impact.
