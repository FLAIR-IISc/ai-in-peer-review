- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings with random-word “corruption” during training, claiming efficiency and competitive performance.
- Decision: Reject — idea is promising but lacks sufficient analysis and justification.
- Missing ablation studies: no rigorous comparison to simple averaging without corruption or other denoising baselines.
- Limited novelty: averaging embeddings is incremental and corruption step not convincingly distinguished as a major contribution.
- Hyperparameter choice unclear: corruption rate q = 0.9 appears arbitrary and lacks sensitivity analysis.
- Inconsistent results: only marginal improvement on SICK semantic relatedness despite broader performance claims.
- Insufficient empirical support for claimed data-dependent regularization and its effect on embeddings (e.g., frequent vs. rare word magnitudes).
- Recommendation: include baseline of averaging without corruption and comparison to dropout applied before averaging.
- Recommendation: analyze which words are dropped during corruption (stopwords vs. informative tokens).
- Recommendation: visualize learned embeddings (t-SNE/UMAP) and compare to Word2Vec/GloVe for qualitative insight.
- Recommendation: provide formal computational complexity, training time, and memory comparisons to other methods.
- Recommendation: make code available and well-documented for reproducibility.
- Recommendation: clarify relationship to denoising autoencoders and how Doc2VecC differs.
- Questions for authors: provide empirical evidence of regularization, justify q=0.9 and report other values, explain SICK performance, show sensitivity to embedding dimension, and report resource requirements vs Paragraph Vectors.
- Overall conclusion: interesting direction but requires substantial revisions, more experiments, and stronger justification to warrant acceptance.
