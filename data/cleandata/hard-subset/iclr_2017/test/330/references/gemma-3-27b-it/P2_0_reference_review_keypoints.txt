- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents a document as the average of word embeddings enforced during training via random word corruption.  
- Claimed advantages: training efficiency decoupled from corpus size, implicit regularization, fast test-time representation, and competitive task performance.  
- Decision: Reject — idea interesting and well-written but experimental evidence insufficient for ICLR acceptance.  
- Primary critique: Performance gains over strong baselines are marginal (≈1–2%) and not consistent across tasks.  
- Experimental detail lacking: key hyperparameters and setup choices (learning rate, window, embedding size, corruption rate) are not fully reported or justified.  
- Ablation missing: no clear comparison between Doc2VecC with and without corruption to isolate the corruption mechanism’s effect.  
- Overstated claims: test-time efficiency and “data-dependent regularization” are not unique to Doc2VecC and are not convincingly demonstrated.  
- No statistical significance: reported performance differences lack significance testing or confidence intervals.  
- Baselines: more recent and stronger baselines should be included for a robust comparison.  
- Qualitative analysis absent: no examples or analyses showing where Doc2VecC captures semantics better than competitors.  
- Motivation unclear: the link between word analogy motivation and averaging-with-corruption approach needs clarification.  
- Suggested experiments: thorough ablation studies, hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (especially corruption rate q), and statistical tests.  
- Questions for authors: computational cost of the Taylor expansion, full hyperparameter breakdown and rationale, alternative corruption strategies tried, evidence of damping on frequent words, and variance/confidence intervals for results.  
- Overall: reasonable approach but insufficient empirical evidence and analysis to justify acceptance; addressing the above would strengthen the paper.
