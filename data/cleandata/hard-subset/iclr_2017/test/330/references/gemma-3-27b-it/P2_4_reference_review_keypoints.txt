- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of word embeddings trained with random word “corruption” to encourage robust, informative embeddings.  
- Demonstrates competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions due to solid motivation, experiments, and efficiency gains.  
- Novelty: Enforces compositionality by averaging during training and combines this with corruption, addressing limitations of prior methods.  
- Strong empirical results: Comprehensive experiments show Doc2VecC often outperforms Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors; ablation confirms corruption’s regularization role.  
- Efficiency: Method decouples complexity from corpus size and yields faster inference, supported by reported timings (Table 2).  
- Theoretical insight: Clear derivation showing corruption acts as data-dependent regularization, explaining performance improvements.  
- Suggestion: Include an ablation plot showing sensitivity to corruption rate q, even if impact is small.  
- Suggestion: Expand word analogy results with a breakdown by semantic vs. syntactic analogy types.  
- Suggestion: Briefly discuss limitations, e.g., potential issues with tasks where word order is crucial.  
- Suggestion: Ensure released code is well-documented and easily reproducible.  
- Question: How does performance scale with vocabulary size and are there practical limits or diminishing returns?  
- Question: Beyond the formal derivation, can the authors provide more intuitive justification for why corruption improves embeddings (e.g., emphasizing discriminative words)?  
- Question: Were alternative corruption schemes tested, and why was the unbiased mask-out/drop-out approach chosen?
