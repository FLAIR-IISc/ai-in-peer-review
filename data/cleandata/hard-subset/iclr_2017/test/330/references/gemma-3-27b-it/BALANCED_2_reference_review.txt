This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning method based on averaging word embeddings with a corruption mechanism. The core idea – that averaging word embeddings, when learned appropriately, can produce effective document vectors – is intuitively appealing and well-motivated by prior work on vector arithmetic with Word2Vec. The authors clearly articulate the benefits of their approach, including decoupling model complexity from corpus size, efficient training, and a data-dependent regularization effect. The experimental results demonstrate competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a solid contribution, the experimental validation, while extensive, lacks sufficient depth to convince me of the significance of the improvements over strong baselines. The performance gains are often marginal, and the analysis of *why* Doc2VecC performs well is somewhat superficial. The lack of ablation studies specifically examining the impact of the corruption mechanism is a key weakness.

Here’s a more detailed breakdown:

**Strengths:**

*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper clearly explains the motivation behind Doc2VecC, drawing connections to successful concepts in word embedding literature (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors).
*   **Efficient Approach:** The method is computationally efficient, a significant advantage for large-scale document analysis. The decoupling of model complexity from corpus size is a practical benefit.
*   **Theoretical Insight:** The derivation relating the corruption mechanism to data-dependent regularization is insightful and provides a solid theoretical justification for the approach.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The authors evaluate Doc2VecC on a variety of tasks and datasets, demonstrating its generalizability.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The paper compares against a relevant set of baseline methods, including established techniques like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Marginal Improvements:** The performance gains over strong baselines are often small and not always statistically significant. It’s difficult to ascertain whether the observed improvements are consistently reproducible.
*   **Insufficient Ablation Studies:** The paper lacks a thorough analysis of the corruption mechanism. Specifically, how does performance change with different corruption rates (q)? What happens when the corruption mechanism is removed entirely?  An ablation study is crucial to understand the contribution of each component.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:**  The paper presents performance numbers but lacks deeper analysis of *where* Doc2VecC succeeds or fails. Are there specific types of documents or tasks where it consistently outperforms other methods?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of Doc2VecC to hyperparameter settings. Are there specific parameters that require careful tuning?
* **Lack of Statistical Significance**: The Tables do not include statistical significance tests to show that the improvements are not due to chance.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **More detailed analysis of the regularization effect:** Expand on the discussion of how the data-dependent regularization affects the learned word embeddings.  Visualize the embeddings of common vs. informative words to demonstrate this effect.
*   **Comparison to simpler baselines:** Include a comparison to a simple average of pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe) *without* any fine-tuning. This would help establish a stronger baseline and highlight the benefits of learning document-specific embeddings.
*   **Explore different corruption strategies:** Investigate alternative corruption mechanisms beyond random word removal.  Could masking entire phrases or using a more sophisticated corruption pattern improve performance?
*   **Qualitative Examples:** Provide qualitative examples of how Doc2VecC represents different types of documents. This would help build intuition and demonstrate the model's ability to capture semantic information.
*   **Discussion of limitations:**  Acknowledging the limitations of the approach (e.g., potential sensitivity to vocabulary size, difficulty handling very long documents) would strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed ablation study focusing on the impact of the corruption rate (q)? What happens to performance as q varies from 0 to 1?
2.  What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism during training? Does it significantly impact training time, and how does it scale with corpus size?
3.  How sensitive is Doc2VecC to the choice of word embedding model (e.g., Word2Vec vs. GloVe)?
4.  Have you explored any techniques for handling out-of-vocabulary words during the averaging process?
5. Can you provide statistical significance tests for your results to demonstrate the reliability of the improvements?
