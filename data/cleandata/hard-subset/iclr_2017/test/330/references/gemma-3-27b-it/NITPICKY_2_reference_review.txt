The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vectorization method leveraging the inherent properties of Word2Vec embeddings and introducing a corruption mechanism. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of their word embeddings, enforced during training, and to improve learning through random word removal (corruption). The authors claim several benefits: decoupling model complexity from corpus size, efficient training, implicit regularization, and competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea is conceptually sound and builds upon existing work (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors), the paper suffers from a multitude of minor yet significant flaws in presentation, experimentation, and analysis. The sheer number of these imperfections overshadows the potential contribution, making a positive assessment difficult.

The primary issues stem from a lack of rigor and attention to detail. Firstly, the experimental setup, while comparing against a range of baselines, lacks crucial details. For example, hyperparameter tuning is mentioned but not explained—how were hyperparameters selected for Doc2VecC and the baselines? The claim of reproducing results via a GitHub link is insufficient; the specific commit hash or version used during evaluation should be stated. Secondly, the reported results are often presented without sufficient statistical significance testing. Reporting accuracy percentages without error bars or p-values is insufficient to claim superiority. The “significant” performance gains on various tasks are not convincingly demonstrated. Third, the data-dependent regularization explanation in section 3.1 relies on a Taylor expansion and simplification which, while mathematically presented, lacks intuitive justification regarding its practical impact and isn’t thoroughly validated empirically. Fourth, there are inconsistencies in notation (e.g., x is defined both as a BoW vector and something else), and several instances of awkward or imprecise language throughout the paper. Finally, the visualization in Figure 3, while suggestive, doesn't provide quantitative evidence for the claimed better clustering of Doc2VecC. 

Here's a breakdown of specific concerns:

*   **Section 2: Related Works:** The citation of “Blei et al.,? \Q2003\E” appears repeatedly at the end of sentences, suggesting a copy-paste error. This demonstrates a lack of meticulous proofreading.
*   **Section 3:** Equation (1) describes the corruption process. It is unclear why setting the uncorrupted dimensions to `1/(1-q)` times the original value is necessary. What is the justification for this scaling? The explanation feels arbitrary.
*   **Section 4.1: Baselines:**  The description of Skip-thought Vectors mentions using “model1”. What exactly *is* model1? The lack of clarity hinders reproducibility.
*   **Section 4.2: Sentiment Analysis:** The statement “Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed” is misleading. DEA typically involves *reconstructing* the input, while Doc2VecC simply averages embeddings. This isn’t a direct reduction.
*   **Section 4.4: Word Analogy:** The claim of “20% uplift” requires quantification. What was the baseline performance and what is the performance of Doc2VecC, specifically?
*   **Throughout:** The consistent formatting of references ("Author et al.,? \Qyear\E") is unconventional and unprofessional. 
*   **Tables:** Tables 1, 2, 5 and 6 lacks error metrics or statistical significance tests. This makes it hard to assess the validity of the reported improvements.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   Provide a more intuitive explanation of the benefits of the corruption mechanism beyond the mathematical derivation. Why does randomly removing words lead to better document representations?
*   Conduct ablation studies. For instance, evaluate the performance of Doc2VecC *without* the corruption mechanism to quantify its impact.
*   Include a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of Doc2VecC compared to other methods.
*   Thoroughly proofread the paper for grammatical errors, inconsistencies in notation, and formatting issues.
*   Consider adding a discussion of potential limitations of Doc2VecC.
*   Provide an analysis of the types of words that are most affected by the data-dependent regularization.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide the specific hyperparameters used for Doc2VecC and the baseline models in each experiment?
2.  What statistical tests were used to determine the significance of the performance differences between Doc2VecC and the baselines? Please provide p-values or confidence intervals.
3.  What is the rationale behind scaling the uncorrupted dimensions by `1/(1-q)` in the corruption process? How does this affect the learning dynamics?
4.  Can you elaborate on the specific details of the "model1" used for Skip-thought Vectors?
5.  Can you explain why you have used a specific citation format ("Author et al.,? \Qyear\E") throughout the paper?
