The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document vector learning method leveraging the observation that linear operations on word embeddings preserve semantic meaning. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. This approach aims to be more efficient than previous methods like Paragraph Vectors, which scale with corpus size, and improve upon simpler averaging methods by introducing a data-dependent regularization effect.

**Reject.** While the idea is intuitive and the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail and analysis to convincingly demonstrate the claimed benefits, especially regarding the theoretical grounding of the "data-dependent regularization." The empirical results, while positive, do not strongly differentiate Doc2VecC from other methods, and the presentation could be more rigorous.

The paper's primary strength lies in its motivation. The observation that linear operations on word embeddings can be meaningful is well-established, and applying this to document representation is a logical step. The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches (Paragraph Vectors’ scalability, BoW's lack of semantic understanding) and position Doc2VecC as a potential solution. The claims of efficiency (decoupled complexity, faster test inference) are also significant.

However, the theoretical justification for the "data-dependent regularization" is weak. The Taylor expansion used to derive this regularization feels hand-wavy and lacks a clear explanation of *why* it leads to the observed benefits (suppressing common words, favoring informative ones). The derivation is complex, and a more intuitive explanation would be beneficial. The connection between the corruption rate 'q' and the performance isn't thoroughly explored. Furthermore, while the experiments are extensive, the improvements over strong baselines are often marginal, and the ablation studies are limited (e.g., is the corruption mechanism truly essential, or does a simple weighted average suffice?). The comparisons to Skip-thought Vectors are somewhat dismissive, focusing solely on paragraph length without acknowledging potential parameter tuning issues. The presentation of the results in tables could be improved for clarity (e.g., adding standard deviations or confidence intervals). The code availability is a positive aspect.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the corruption mechanism:** The rationale behind randomly *removing* words (rather than, say, masking them) isn't fully explained. Does this specifically target less informative words, and if so, how?
*   **More rigorous regularization analysis:** Provide a more compelling explanation of how the derived regularization term impacts the learned word embeddings. Visualize the embeddings before and after regularization.
*   **Ablation studies:** Include experiments *without* the corruption mechanism to isolate its effect. Explore different corruption rates.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the model to key hyperparameters (embedding dimension, corruption rate, learning rate).
*   **Statistical significance:** Report statistical significance tests for the reported performance gains.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** Discuss the potential limitations of Doc2VecC (e.g., its performance on very long documents).
*  **Skip-thought Vectors:** A fairer comparison with Skip-thought Vectors would involve re-training them on the target datasets or providing more detailed information on the pre-trained model used.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why the corruption mechanism leads to a data-dependent regularization effect, beyond the Taylor expansion?
2.  How does the choice of corruption rate 'q' impact the performance of Doc2VecC, and what is a principled way to choose this value?
3.  What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism during training, and is it a significant bottleneck for large datasets?
4.  What are the key differences between the pre-trained Skip-thought Vectors model used and the one described in the original paper, and how might these differences explain the observed performance?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of words whose embeddings are most affected by the regularization term?
