This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning method based on averaging word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism during training. The core idea is to learn document representations where averaging word embeddings is meaningful, facilitated by randomly removing words during the learning process. The authors claim Doc2VecC is efficient, introduces data-dependent regularization, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of directly learning representations amenable to averaging is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The empirical results, while generally positive, aren't convincing enough to overcome the relatively straightforward nature of the proposed method. The claims of state-of-the-art performance are often marginal and not consistently demonstrated across all tasks.

The primary concern is the lack of substantial novelty. Averaging word embeddings is a common and intuitive approach, and the addition of a random word removal (corruption) mechanism feels more like a regularization technique than a fundamental innovation. The connection to data-dependent regularization, while mathematically derived, doesn't intuitively explain the observed performance gains and feels overly complex for the simplicity of the core idea. The paper doesn’t sufficiently differentiate itself from existing regularization techniques commonly employed in word embedding training (e.g., dropout, subsampling), and the presented regularization analysis feels post-hoc rather than a driving force behind the method's design.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while comprehensive in terms of the number of baselines, lacks depth. The improvements over strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors are often small, and the error bars or statistical significance testing are absent. This makes it difficult to assess whether the observed improvements are statistically significant or simply due to random variation. The choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity analysis are not sufficiently detailed. The ablation studies focusing on the corruption rate (q) are limited. The analysis in Section 4.3 is particularly weak, offering only a marginal improvement over existing methods without significant statistical backing. The assertion that Doc2VecC is "much faster" than Skip-thought vectors is qualified ("less than 2 hours" vs. "2 weeks"), but without a more standardized comparison (e.g., training time per epoch, resources used), this claim remains unsubstantiated. The conclusion overstates the contribution, framing the method as a substantial advancement rather than a refinement of existing techniques.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not impacting the rejection decision):**

*   **Novelty:** Clearly articulate the specific contribution of Doc2VecC beyond simply encouraging averaging of word embeddings. What makes this approach fundamentally different from other regularization methods or averaging techniques?
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation of the data-dependent regularization is complex. Provide a more intuitive explanation of how this regularization impacts the learned representations and contributes to performance.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** Include statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate the significance of the performance improvements. Provide error bars on reported results. Perform a more extensive hyperparameter search and analyze the sensitivity of the method to different parameters.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of the corruption mechanism. For example, compare Doc2VecC to a version without corruption but with other regularization techniques (e.g., dropout).
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more standardized comparison of training and inference times, including details about the hardware and software used.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the approach and discuss potential areas for future research.

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on why the corruption mechanism is more effective than standard regularization techniques like dropout when used directly on the word embeddings?
*   What is the computational overhead of the corruption process during training?
*   Have you explored other forms of corruption beyond random word removal, and if so, what were the results?
*   How does the performance of Doc2VecC change with different vocabulary sizes and corpus sizes?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned word embeddings, highlighting how the corruption mechanism influences the semantic relationships between words?
