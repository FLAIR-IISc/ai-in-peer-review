This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document vector learning method that aims to efficiently represent documents by averaging word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism for regularization. The authors claim their method outperforms existing approaches in terms of training efficiency, test efficiency, and performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging the compositional properties of word embeddings and introducing corruption for regularization is interesting, the paper suffers from a significant number of minor flaws – inconsistencies in notation, lack of clarity in experimental setup, and a somewhat superficial discussion of results – which collectively undermine confidence in the validity and significance of the reported findings. The paper reads as a collection of ideas rather than a cohesive, rigorously evaluated contribution.

My primary concerns are with the meticulousness of the experiments and the clarity of presentation. 

1. **Notational Inconsistencies & Lack of Rigor:** The notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For instance, `x` is used to represent both a BoW vector *and* the original document vector. This ambiguity is confusing and makes understanding the mathematical formulations difficult. The explanation of the corruption mechanism (eq. 1) is unclear regarding the impact of scaling by `1/(1-q)`—why is this crucial, and what specifically does it achieve? The derivation of the regularization term in section 3.1 feels rushed and lacks sufficient explanation for readers unfamiliar with Taylor expansion techniques. Furthermore, the expansion assumes a second-order Taylor expansion, but the justification for this choice is missing.
2. **Experimental Details & Baselines:** The experimental setup lacks sufficient detail.  The hyperparameter tuning process is mentioned, but not described.  What validation set was used? What search strategy was employed?  For the baselines, the specific implementations used are not always clear (e.g., “DEA” – what specific parameters were used?). The comparison to Skip-thought vectors is problematic given the difference in training datasets and the substantial computational cost of training that model – a more fair comparison would involve fine-tuning Skip-thought vectors on the same datasets used for Doc2VecC. Additionally, the reported performance gains on some tasks (e.g., semantic relatedness) appear substantial, yet the discussion lacks a critical analysis of *why* Doc2VecC performs so well – is it due to a genuinely superior representation, or simply to a more effective hyperparameter search?
3. **Result Discussion & Analysis:** The authors claim Doc2VecC “naturally exerts a stronger regularization,” but the empirical evidence presented (Table 3) is limited. Simply showing that Doc2VecC down-weights common words isn’t enough to demonstrate a robust regularization effect. A more comprehensive ablation study, varying the corruption rate (q) and observing its impact on performance, would be necessary. The figures (3 & 4) provide limited insight. Figure 3 lacks axis labels, making it difficult to interpret the visualization of document vectors. Figure 4 is only visual; there’s no quantitative analysis to support the claims about clustering.
4. **Overstated Claims & Redundancy:** Some claims appear overstated (e.g., “Doc2VecC significantly outperforms the winning solutions…” – the margin isn’t always statistically significant). There’s considerable redundancy throughout the paper, particularly in the descriptions of related work.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   Provide a detailed appendix outlining all hyperparameters used for Doc2VecC and all baselines.
*   Conduct a thorough ablation study, varying the corruption rate and analyzing its impact on performance.
*   Include statistical significance tests to support all performance claims.
*   Address the notational inconsistencies and provide clearer explanations of the mathematical formulations.
*   Elaborate on the implementation details of the baselines.
*   Provide a more critical discussion of the results, acknowledging potential limitations and areas for future work.
*   Reduce redundancy and streamline the writing.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the scaling factor `1/(1-q)` is necessary in the corruption mechanism? What would happen if you omitted it?
*   What specific validation strategy was used for hyperparameter tuning?
*   How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of the corruption rate (q)? Can you present results of an ablation study?
*   Why was the Skip-thought vector model *not* fine-tuned on the datasets used for Doc2VecC? How would that impact your comparison?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the regularization effect, beyond simply observing that common words are down-weighted?
