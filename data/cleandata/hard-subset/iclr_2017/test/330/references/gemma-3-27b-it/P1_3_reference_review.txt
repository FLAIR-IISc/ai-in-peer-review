## ICLR 2024 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION (Doc2VecC)

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The authors propose representing documents as the average of their word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” mechanism – randomly removing words during training. This approach contrasts with methods requiring learning a unique document vector for each corpus entry and offers benefits in terms of scalability, training speed, and regularization. The paper claims Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being computationally efficient.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the idea is interesting and the presented results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient depth in analysis and justification for several critical design choices. The experimental setup, while comprehensive, doesn't fully isolate the benefits of the corruption mechanism and struggles to convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over simpler baselines given the computational cost.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper claims the corruption mechanism is crucial, but doesn’t provide sufficient ablation studies to rigorously demonstrate *why* it's effective.  A comparison against a simple averaging of word embeddings *without* corruption would be vital. The authors mention DEA as a point of comparison but don’t clearly articulate the benefit of corruption over similar denoising approaches.
* **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of averaging word embeddings is not entirely new. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently distinguish Doc2VecC beyond the corruption step. The contribution feels incremental, and the paper needs to strongly justify why this incremental contribution is significant enough for publication at ICLR.
* **Justification of Hyperparameters:** The choice of the corruption rate *q* = 0.9 feels somewhat arbitrary. The paper states it isn't sensitive to this parameter, but doesn't present evidence (e.g., a small sensitivity analysis).  A clearer rationale for this choice would strengthen the paper.
* **Inconsistent Performance:**  While the paper shows good results on many tasks, the performance on the Semantic Relatedness task (SICK dataset) is only slightly better than some simpler baselines, despite the claims of outperforming the competition. This discrepancy warrants further investigation.
* **Insufficient discussion of Regularization:** The authors relate the corruption to a data-dependent regularization, and explain the theoretical underpinnings. However, they don’t provide empirical evidence demonstrating the effect of this regularization on the learned embeddings themselves (e.g. examining the magnitude of the embeddings for frequent vs. infrequent words).



**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Expand Ablation Studies:** Crucially, include a baseline of averaging word embeddings *without* corruption.  Also, compare against simply applying dropout to the word embeddings before averaging.
* **Detailed Analysis of Corruption:**  Provide a more detailed analysis of *which* words are typically dropped during corruption. Does the algorithm tend to drop stop words, or more informative words? This could offer insights into the mechanism’s behavior.
* **Visualization of Embeddings:** Visualizing the learned word embeddings (e.g., using t-SNE or UMAP) and comparing them to Word2Vec or GloVe embeddings could provide valuable qualitative insights.
* **Computational Complexity Analysis:** While the paper mentions efficiency, a more formal analysis of the computational complexity (in terms of training time and memory usage) compared to other methods would be beneficial.
* **Code Availability:** Ensuring the code is readily accessible and well-documented is crucial for reproducibility.
* **Clarify Relationship to Denoising Autoencoders:** Better discuss how Doc2VecC relates to and differs from Denoising Autoencoders.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  You state that Doc2VecC introduces a data-dependent regularization. Can you provide empirical evidence (beyond the theoretical analysis) that shows this regularization is indeed happening and is beneficial? For example, are embeddings for frequent words generally smaller than those for rare words when trained with Doc2VecC?
*  What is the rationale behind choosing a corruption rate of *q* = 0.9? Have you experimented with other values, and if so, what were the results?
*  The performance on the SICK dataset seems less convincing than on the other tasks. Can you elaborate on potential reasons for this, and perform a more detailed error analysis?
*  How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the size of the word embedding dimension?  A plot of performance vs. embedding dimension would be helpful.
*  Could you elaborate on the computational resources required to train Doc2VecC on datasets of varying sizes, and compare this to the requirements for training Paragraph Vectors?

In conclusion, while the Doc2VecC approach presents an interesting direction for document representation learning, the paper requires substantial revisions to fully justify its claims and demonstrate its significance to the ICLR community. The lack of thorough ablation studies and the limited novelty necessitate a rejection at this stage.
