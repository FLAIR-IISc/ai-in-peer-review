## ICLR 2017 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION (Doc2VecC)

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism (randomly removing words during training). This approach aims to address limitations of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors (high parameter count, expensive inference) while leveraging the compositional properties of word embeddings. The authors claim Doc2VecC is computationally efficient, introduces a data-dependent regularization, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (Weak Accept)**. The paper presents a compelling approach with solid empirical results. While the novelty is incremental (building on Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors), the efficiency gains and strong performance are noteworthy. However, the paper could benefit from more in-depth analysis and discussion of the regularization effect.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Well-Motivated:** The authors clearly articulate the shortcomings of existing document representation methods and position Doc2VecC as a solution. The motivation based on the compositional properties of word embeddings is sound and aligns with prior work.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates competitive or superior performance across a diverse set of tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) compared to several strong baselines. The consistent performance improvement suggests the proposed approach is effective. The inclusion of runtime comparisons is particularly valuable, highlighting the efficiency of Doc2VecC.
* **Novelty (Incremental):** While Doc2VecC builds on existing techniques, the combination of averaging word embeddings *with* the corruption mechanism is a novel contribution. The theoretical justification for the corruption acting as a data-dependent regularizer is a significant addition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the method and experimental setup. The inclusion of code availability is a plus.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Deeper Dive into Regularization:** The analysis of the data-dependent regularization is somewhat limited.  While the authors explain *how* it works theoretically, more empirical evidence demonstrating *what* it's regularizing in practice would be beneficial.  For example, visualizing the word embeddings before and after training with and without corruption could provide insights.
* **Ablation Study:** A more comprehensive ablation study would strengthen the paper. Specifically, what is the impact of *removing* the corruption mechanism?  How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate (q)?  This would better isolate the contribution of each component of the model.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of performance to hyperparameters, such as embedding dimension and learning rate.  Providing a small table or figure illustrating this could improve reproducibility.
* **Error Analysis:** Providing some qualitative examples of where Doc2VecC succeeds and fails would be valuable. This could offer insights into the model’s limitations and potential areas for improvement.
* **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While the authors compare to many strong baselines, including a comparison to *just* averaging word embeddings *without* the corruption mechanism would further highlight the benefits of the proposed approach.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Regularization Analysis:**  Can you provide more empirical evidence demonstrating the impact of the data-dependent regularization?  Specifically, how does the corruption mechanism affect the distribution of word embeddings (e.g., visualization, statistics on embedding norms)?
2.  **Corruption Rate (q):** What is the optimal corruption rate (q) across the different datasets?  Is it sensitive to the characteristics of the data (e.g., document length, vocabulary size)?
3. **Scaling:** How does Doc2VecC scale to truly massive datasets (e.g., billions of documents)? Are there any bottlenecks that might limit its applicability in such scenarios?
4. **Computational Cost of Corruption:** While you highlight the efficiency of Doc2VecC, could you quantify the computational overhead introduced by the corruption mechanism itself (e.g., the time required to generate the corrupted representations)?
