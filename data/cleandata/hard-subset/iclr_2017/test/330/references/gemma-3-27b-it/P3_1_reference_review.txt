## ICLR 2024 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION (Doc2VecC)

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” mechanism that randomly removes words during training.  The authors argue this approach is advantageous due to its decoupling of model complexity from corpus size, efficiency, implicit regularization, and strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. They present a theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism acting as a data-dependent regularizer and demonstrate competitive or superior results compared to several state-of-the-art methods.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to document vectorization. The method is conceptually simple, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated with a thorough set of experiments. The authors effectively demonstrate the advantages of Doc2VecC over existing techniques, particularly regarding efficiency and performance.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** While averaging word embeddings isn’t entirely new, the crucial addition of the corruption mechanism and its theoretical justification as a regularizer are novel and insightful. The motivation stemming from observations in Word2Vec’s compositional properties is clear and convincing.
* **Theoretical Soundness:** The derivation of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3.1) is a significant strength. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the observed performance improvements. The connection to Taylor expansion and the analysis of the Hessian are well-explained.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments are comprehensive, covering a range of tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) and comparing Doc2VecC against a strong set of baselines, including Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec, and more recent approaches like Skip-thought Vectors.  The consistently strong performance, particularly the advantage in training and testing efficiency, is convincing.
* **Efficiency:** The authors repeatedly emphasize and demonstrate the efficiency of their approach, both in terms of training time and inference speed.  This is a significant practical benefit, especially for large-scale applications.  The comparison to Paragraph Vectors’ inference time is particularly notable.
* **Ablation Study:** The discussion about the effect of the corruption mechanism and the data-dependent regularization in section 4.3 is well conducted, and provides further insight into the benefits of the proposed method.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Clarify the Role of Hyperparameters:** The paper mentions tuning hyperparameters on a validation set, but doesn’t provide details about *which* hyperparameters were tuned or the specific tuning procedure. This information would enhance reproducibility.
* **Discuss Limitations:**  While the method performs well, a discussion of potential limitations (e.g., performance on very long documents, sensitivity to the corruption rate 'q' in specific scenarios) would strengthen the paper.
* **Vocabulary Handling:**  The text mentions extending the vocabulary during semantic relatedness experiments. Elaborate on this procedure.  How are OOV words handled specifically?
* **Code Availability:** While the authors mention code availability, a direct link to the repository would be helpful.
* **Figure 1 Enhancement:** Improve the clarity of Figure 1.  Highlighting the "corrupted" words visually within the document might make the concept more intuitive.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the corruption rate 'q':**  You mention the model is not sensitive to 'q'. Could you provide more details on the range of 'q' values you experimented with and the observed performance variation?  Are there scenarios where 'q' might be more critical (e.g., smaller datasets, different document lengths)?
* **Complexity Analysis:**  While you state the complexity is decoupled from corpus size, could you provide a more formal complexity analysis, particularly concerning the scaling with vocabulary size 'v'?
* **Relation to Denoising Autoencoders:** You mention Doc2VecC reduces to a Denoising Autoencoder when context words are removed.  However, Denoising Autoencoders often use different noise functions (e.g., masking individual word *positions* rather than random word *removal*).  How does your approach differ fundamentally from standard DAEs for document representation?
* **Semantic Relatedness - Data Preprocessing:**  Were any preprocessing steps (e.g., stemming, lemmatization) applied to the SICK dataset before creating the word embeddings?




In conclusion, this is a strong paper that proposes a novel, efficient, and theoretically grounded method for document vectorization.  The empirical results are compelling, and the authors have convincingly demonstrated the benefits of Doc2VecC. Addressing the minor feedback points will further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
