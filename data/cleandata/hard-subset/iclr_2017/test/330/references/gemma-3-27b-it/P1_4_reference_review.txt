## ICLR 2017 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION

**1. Summary**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism that randomly removes words during training. This approach cleverly incorporates a data-dependent regularization and boasts several advantages: decoupling model complexity from corpus size, efficient training akin to Word2Vec, improved performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, and faster test-time inference. The authors provide a clear motivation, grounding their work in existing literature, and present comprehensive experimental results.

**2. Decision**

**Accept**. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported contribution to document representation learning. The proposed method is innovative, addresses practical limitations of existing approaches (specifically scalability and inference speed), and demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple tasks.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The approach of directly averaging word embeddings, enforced through corruption during training, is a conceptually simple yet powerful idea. The authors effectively connect this to prior work on vector arithmetic and compositional semantics.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The extensive experimental evaluation across three different tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) consistently demonstrates that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods.  The improvements are statistically significant and not limited to a single task.
* **Efficiency:** A key strength of Doc2VecC is its efficiency.  The authors clearly demonstrate that training and inference times are significantly faster compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors, making it suitable for large-scale datasets.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The explanation of the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is insightful and provides a theoretical grounding for the observed performance gains. The detailed derivation of the regularization term strengthens the paper’s contribution.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the proposed method, related work, and experimental setup.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Ablation Study on Corruption:** While the authors mention the importance of the corruption mechanism, a more detailed ablation study specifically isolating the impact of different corruption rates (q) on performance would be beneficial. This would further solidify the understanding of this key component.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions hyperparameter tuning, elaborating on the sensitivity of the results to specific hyperparameter settings (e.g., embedding dimensionality, window size) would provide a more complete picture.
* **Analysis of Regularization:**  A more detailed analysis of the learned embeddings with and without the corruption regularization could provide further insights. Visualizing the embedding space or examining the norms of the embeddings for frequent vs. rare words could be revealing.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** A more explicit discussion of the limitations of the method, perhaps regarding its performance on very short documents or in scenarios with heavily specialized vocabulary, would enhance the paper’s credibility.
* **Code Release:** The authors mention code availability, which is excellent. Ensuring the code is well-documented and easily reproducible is crucial.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding the corruption rate (q):**  You mention that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate.  However, in Section 3.1, you state that you used q=0.9 throughout the experiments. Was there any experimentation with values significantly different from 0.9, and if so, what were the results?
* **Regarding the choice of averaging:**  You chose simple averaging of word embeddings. Were other composition functions (e.g., weighted averaging, max-pooling) explored, and if so, why was averaging ultimately selected?
* **Regarding Paragraph Vectors performance:** The paper points out that Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task.  Do you have any hypotheses as to *why* this might be the case beyond the stated reason of relying mostly on the document vector? Is there a potential interaction with the training data used for these analogies?
* **Regarding the SICK dataset:** For the semantic relatedness task, you fine-tune the embeddings on the target dataset. Does the performance significantly change if this fine-tuning step is omitted?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The proposed Doc2VecC is a practical and effective method with clear advantages over existing approaches.  I recommend it for acceptance to ICLR.
