The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning method based on averaging word embeddings with a corruption mechanism. The core idea is to learn representations where the average of word embeddings already captures document semantics, enforced by randomly removing words during training. This approach aims to be efficient, scalable, and performant compared to existing methods like Paragraph Vectors.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging averaging of word embeddings is intuitive and the potential for efficiency is appealing, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and empirical justification to warrant acceptance. The improvement over baselines, while present, isn’t substantial enough to justify a new method, and the presented evidence feels incremental rather than groundbreaking.

The motivation is reasonably well-placed within the literature, acknowledging the limitations of BoW, the success of Word2Vec, and the shortcomings of Paragraph Vectors. The authors correctly identify the parameter growth and inference cost issues with Paragraph Vectors. The proposed approach, building on the observation that linear operations on word embeddings can preserve meaning, is a logical extension.

However, the paper falls short in convincingly demonstrating the superiority of Doc2VecC. The experiments are comprehensive, covering sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, with comparisons to a range of baselines. The results *do* show that Doc2VecC performs competitively, often matching or exceeding the performance of existing methods. But the gains are often marginal, and the authors don’t fully dissect *why* Doc2VecC works better. For instance, the data-dependent regularization explanation, while theoretically interesting, isn’t strongly tied to the empirical results. The visualizations (Figure 3) are helpful but don’t provide a definitive explanation for the performance differences. The claim that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on subsampling frequent words while others do doesn’t equate to a significant advantage without demonstrating a clear performance difference when subsampling is disabled for all methods. Additionally, the reliance on pre-trained word embeddings (Glove) throughout the experiments introduces a potential confounding factor. While common practice, the extent to which the results are attributable to Doc2VecC versus the quality of the pre-trained embeddings isn't adequately discussed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of averaging word embeddings is not entirely new (mentioned in related work, Huang et al., 2012). The contribution lies in the corruption mechanism and the theoretical justification linking it to data-dependent regularization. However, this connection could be strengthened.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from ablation studies to specifically assess the impact of the corruption mechanism. What happens if you remove it? What if you vary the corruption rate (q)?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings (embedding dimensionality, corruption rate, window size, etc.) would be valuable.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The performance differences reported in the tables should be accompanied by statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to establish whether the improvements are statistically meaningful.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper mentions efficiency, but a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of Doc2VecC compared to other methods (training time, memory usage) would be helpful.
*   **Error Analysis**: A deeper dive into the types of errors Doc2VecC makes could provide valuable insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of pre-trained word embeddings? Have you experimented with training the word embeddings from scratch?
2.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation for *why* the corruption mechanism improves performance? Is it primarily due to the regularization effect, or are there other factors at play?
3.  What is the computational overhead of the corruption mechanism during training? Does it significantly impact training time?
4.  The paper states that Doc2VecC doesn’t rely on subsampling frequent words. However, is it possible that Doc2VecC implicitly addresses the issue of frequent words through other mechanisms?
5.  Can you elaborate on the specific benefits of Doc2VecC for handling longer documents, as hinted at in the Skip-thought Vectors comparison?
