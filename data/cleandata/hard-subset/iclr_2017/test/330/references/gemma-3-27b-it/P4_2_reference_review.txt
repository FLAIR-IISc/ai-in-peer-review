This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” mechanism – randomly removing words during training. This approach aims to efficiently learn document representations, decoupling model complexity from corpus size and offering faster test-time inference compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors. The authors motivate their work by observing the semantic properties preserved by linear operations on Word2Vec embeddings and demonstrate improvements over several baselines across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea is intuitively appealing and the experimental results demonstrate competitive performance, the paper lacks sufficient clarity in explaining *why* the proposed corruption mechanism leads to the observed benefits and regularization effects. The theoretical justification, while attempting to connect to Taylor expansion and regularization, feels hand-wavy and lacks crucial details that would convince a reader of its effectiveness. The empirical evidence, while positive, doesn’t clearly isolate the impact of the corruption itself beyond showing it works well within the overall framework.

The central argument hinges on the corruption mechanism acting as a data-dependent regularizer. The derivation presented in Section 3.1 attempts to demonstrate this but relies on several simplifying assumptions and a Taylor expansion that isn't fully justified. The connection between the derived regularization term and the observed performance gains remains tenuous. Specifically, the authors claim the regularization favors rare words and suppresses common ones; however, the quantitative impact of this specific regularization effect isn't explicitly shown through ablation studies or analysis of learned word embeddings. Furthermore, the paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of performance to the corruption rate *q*. While they state the model isn’t sensitive, supporting data on the robustness of *q* would be helpful. 

The experimental setup is thorough, with a good range of baselines and datasets. However, the discussion of the results could be more nuanced. While the authors highlight improvements over baselines, they don't delve deeply into *where* Doc2VecC excels and *why*. For instance, the observation that Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought vectors on IMDB but underperforms on semantic relatedness suggests dataset-specific strengths, but this isn’t explored in detail.  The claim that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on word subsampling, unlike Word2Vec+AVG, is weakened by the fact that Word2Vec itself *already* employs subsampling. The improvement isn't then necessarily attributable to Doc2VecC but could be a side effect of avoiding double-subsampling.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the motivation:** Re-emphasize the problems with existing methods and how Doc2VecC directly addresses those limitations.
*   **Strengthen the theoretical justification:** Provide a more rigorous derivation of the regularization effect. Explain the intuition behind the Taylor expansion and its limitations in this context. What are the assumptions made, and how do they affect the validity of the results?
*   **Ablation studies:** Include ablation studies to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism. Compare performance with and without corruption, and with varying levels of corruption.
*   **Analyze learned embeddings:** Provide more insights into the learned word embeddings. Do they indeed exhibit the properties described (e.g., dampened representations for common words)? Show examples of word analogies.
*   **Error analysis:** Conduct error analysis to understand where Doc2VecC fails and why.
*   **Discussion of hyperparameters:** Include a discussion on the sensitivity of performance to other hyperparameters, not just *q*.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  The derivation of the regularization term relies on a Taylor expansion. How does the accuracy of this approximation depend on the document length and the magnitude of the word embeddings?
2.  What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism during training? Does it significantly impact training time compared to a standard Word2Vec/Doc2Vec approach?
3.  The paper claims Doc2VecC isn’t sensitive to *q*. Can you provide a plot or table showing performance as a function of *q*?
4.  What is the effect of using different averaging strategies for generating document vectors (e.g., TF-IDF weighted averaging)? Does Doc2VecC perform better or worse with more sophisticated averaging schemes?
5.  Could you comment on the relationship between Doc2VecC and other regularization techniques applied to word embeddings (e.g., dropout)?
