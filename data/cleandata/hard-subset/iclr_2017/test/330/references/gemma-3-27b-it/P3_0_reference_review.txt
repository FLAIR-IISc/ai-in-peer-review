## ICLR 2017 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION (Doc2VecC)

**1. Summary**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism that randomly removes words during training.  The authors argue this approach is computationally efficient (scaling with vocabulary size rather than corpus size), introduces a data-dependent regularization, and achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The paper provides a solid theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a regularizer and demonstrates its effectiveness through extensive experimentation.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  The paper presents a compelling and well-supported approach to document representation learning.  The theoretical justification is strong, the experimental results are convincing, and the method offers significant advantages in terms of efficiency. The clarity of the writing and the thoroughness of the experiments significantly strengthen the contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Motivation:**  The idea of leveraging the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings through simple averaging, combined with corruption as a regularization technique, is a valuable contribution. It offers a practical and efficient alternative to more complex document embedding methods like Paragraph Vectors. The motivation rooted in the observed linear relationships between word embeddings is well articulated.
* **Theoretical Soundness:**  The derivation of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer is a significant strength.  This section provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method and clearly explains *why* it works. The explanation of how it favors informative words and suppresses common ones is particularly insightful.
* **Empirical Validation:** The paper demonstrates consistently strong performance across a diverse range of tasks. The comparison against strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors, Denoising Autoencoders, and Skip-thought Vectors, provides compelling evidence of Doc2VecC’s effectiveness.  The ablation study highlighting the benefits of the corruption mechanism is also valuable. The results show Doc2VecC is competitive with more complex models while being significantly more efficient.
* **Efficiency:** The authors clearly highlight the efficiency benefits of their approach, particularly the reduced computational cost at test time compared to methods requiring inference for unseen documents. This makes Doc2VecC a particularly attractive option for real-world applications.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement)**

* **Clarify Corruption Rate Selection:** The paper states a corruption rate of q=0.9 was used throughout experiments. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss the sensitivity of performance to this parameter.  A small ablation study varying 'q' could strengthen this claim.
* **Discussion of Negative Sampling:**  The paper mentions negative sampling but doesn’t provide much detail about the number of negative samples used.  Including this detail would increase reproducibility.
* **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** While the baselines are strong, it might be helpful to include a comparison to a very simple average of pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings *without* any corruption or fine-tuning. This could provide a clearer baseline for assessing the impact of the corruption mechanism and the learned document representation.
* **Qualitative Analysis:**  While the quantitative results are impressive, including a qualitative analysis of the learned document vectors (e.g., visualizing the nearest neighbors in vector space for a few example documents) could provide further insights into the model's behavior.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Regarding the theoretical derivation:** Could you elaborate on the assumptions made during the Taylor expansion and the potential impact of these assumptions on the validity of the regularization term?
* **Regarding the semantic relatedness task:**  Skip-thought Vectors showed significantly poorer performance on the IMDB dataset but performed well on SICK. Is there a specific characteristic of SICK (e.g., sentence length, vocabulary) that explains this difference?
* **Regarding computational cost:** Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the training time for each algorithm, including the hardware specifications used? This would further highlight the efficiency advantages of Doc2VecC.
* **Regarding scalability:** How does the performance of Doc2VecC degrade with extremely large vocabularies (e.g., >1 million words)? Are there any techniques that could be used to mitigate this potential issue?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning.  The authors have clearly articulated their approach, provided a solid theoretical justification, and demonstrated its effectiveness through comprehensive experimentation.  I am confident that with the minor revisions suggested above, this paper will be a valuable addition to the ICLR conference.
