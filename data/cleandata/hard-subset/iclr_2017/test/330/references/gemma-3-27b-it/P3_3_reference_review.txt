## Review for "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, combined with a “corruption” mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The authors argue this approach is computationally efficient, introduces a data-dependent regularization effect, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The work is well-motivated by the observed compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and addresses limitations of previous document vector methods like Paragraph Vectors regarding scalability and test-time efficiency.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a conceptually simple yet surprisingly effective method for document representation learning. The results are consistently strong across diverse tasks, and the theoretical justification connecting corruption to regularization is compelling. The clear writing and thorough experimentation further strengthen the contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Performance:** The authors demonstrate competitive or superior performance compared to established baselines (BoW, DEA, Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors) on three distinct tasks. The gains are particularly notable in document classification, where Doc2VecC significantly outperforms other methods.
* **Efficiency:** The model’s efficiency is a key advantage. Unlike Paragraph Vectors, the complexity doesn’t scale with corpus size, making it practical for large datasets. The fast test-time computation (simple averaging) is also a significant benefit.  The reported training times demonstrate this efficiency.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The connection between the corruption mechanism and a data-dependent regularization term is a valuable theoretical contribution. The derivation shows how the corruption process penalizes frequent but uninformative words, which aligns with intuition and explains the observed performance improvements.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors conduct a comprehensive set of experiments, including analysis of word analogies and ablation studies. This demonstrates a robust understanding of the method's strengths and weaknesses.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and logically organized. The motivation is clear, the method is explained in detail, and the results are presented in a concise and informative manner.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention that Doc2VecC is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q`, a more detailed analysis of the impact of various hyperparameters (embedding dimension, window size, learning rate) would be beneficial. Showing a sensitivity analysis would build confidence in the robustness of the results.
* **Comparison to More Recent Methods:** While the baselines are strong, the field of document representation learning has evolved since some of the cited works. Adding comparisons to more recent methods like BERT or Sentence-BERT (even as brief, negative results) would further contextualize the contributions of Doc2VecC.
* **Qualitative Examples:**  Providing a few qualitative examples of how Doc2VecC represents documents, perhaps through visualization of the embedding space or by identifying the most salient words contributing to a document’s vector, could provide more intuitive insight into the method’s behavior.
* **Clarify “corruption” context:** It's implied that the corruption happens *during* training, but it's not explicitly stated how this is integrated with the backpropagation process. A slightly more detailed explanation of the training loop and how the gradients are handled with the corrupted input would be helpful.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Scalability to Extremely Large Datasets:** While the model complexity doesn't scale with corpus size, are there any practical limitations on the size of datasets Doc2VecC can handle efficiently in terms of memory usage during training?
* **Impact of Pre-trained Word Embeddings:**  The experiments use pre-trained word embeddings. How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the quality of these initial embeddings? Would training the word embeddings jointly with the document representation improve performance?
* **Choice of Negative Sampling:** The authors use negative sampling. Was the number of negative samples tuned, and if so, how did it affect performance?
* **Beyond Averaging:**  Have the authors explored alternative methods for combining word embeddings to form document representations besides simple averaging (e.g., weighted averaging based on TF-IDF or attention mechanisms)? If so, why was averaging chosen over these alternatives?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The proposed method is efficient, effective, and theoretically well-grounded. Addressing the minor suggestions above would further strengthen the paper and solidify its impact.
