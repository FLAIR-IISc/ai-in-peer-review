This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to document vector representation learning. The core idea – representing documents as the average of word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism during training – is simple yet surprisingly effective. The authors motivate their approach by observing the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and aim to directly enforce this compositionality during document vector learning. They demonstrate strong performance on several text understanding tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results while maintaining computational efficiency.

**Reject.** While the idea is appealing and the results promising, the paper feels somewhat under-developed and lacks sufficient depth in analyzing *why* Doc2VecC works so well. The empirical evaluation, while broad, doesn't fully justify the claims of significant improvement, and the theoretical justification (the data-dependent regularization argument) feels a bit hand-wavy and not thoroughly explored.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The core idea of directly averaging embeddings with corruption is a fresh take on document vectorization. It’s a bold move to eschew more complex architectures like Paragraph Vectors.
*   **Efficiency:** The claimed efficiency benefits (training and inference speed) are significant and address a practical limitation of existing methods.
*   **Performance:** The reported performance is competitive, and in some cases surpasses state-of-the-art approaches.
*   **Clear Writing:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Analysis of Corruption:** The 'corruption as data-dependent regularization' section feels incomplete. The derivation is complex but the connection to improved performance isn’t fully elucidated. What is the optimal corruption rate `q` and why? What’s the effect of different corruption strategies? A more detailed ablation study varying `q` would be beneficial.
*   **Insufficient Comparison:** While several baselines are included, the comparison lacks depth. For example, the Skip-thought Vectors results are dismissed as being due to paragraph length, but the authors don’t explore adapting Skip-thought Vectors to shorter document segments or incorporating length-aware mechanisms. A more thorough comparison with relevant state-of-the-art methods is needed.
*   **Limited Error Analysis:** The paper presents overall performance metrics but lacks detailed error analysis. What types of documents or examples does Doc2VecC struggle with? Understanding failure modes would provide valuable insights.
*   **Theoretical Underpinnings:** While the authors attempt a theoretical justification, the second-order Taylor expansion and subsequent simplification feel contrived and may not fully capture the dynamics of the learning process. The claim of data-dependent regularization needs stronger support.
* **Vocabulary size**: While the paper mentions removing words that appear less than 10 times for the IMDB dataset, no information is provided regarding the vocabulary size on the other tasks.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Explore different corruption strategies:** Beyond random removal, investigate more sophisticated corruption mechanisms, like masking based on part-of-speech tags or importance scores.
*   **Visualize embeddings:** Provide visualizations (e.g., t-SNE plots) of the learned document embeddings to demonstrate the semantic relationships captured by Doc2VecC. The provided visualization is limited to a coarse categorization.
*   **Investigate the effect of pre-trained word embeddings:** Does Doc2VecC benefit from using pre-trained word embeddings like GloVe or fastText?
*   **Ablation studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each component of Doc2VecC (averaging vs. corruption, different corruption rates).
* **Hyperparameter sensitivity**: Discuss the sensitivity of the performance to hyperparameter settings, and provide justification for the chosen hyperparameters.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer?  What specific properties of the regularization are beneficial?
*   What is the computational cost of calculating the Hessian matrix in the corruption regularization derivation, and how does this affect the scalability of the approach?
*   Have you explored alternative methods for approximating the log likelihood beyond negative sampling?
*   What were the vocabulary sizes for the Wikipedia and SemEval datasets? How does the vocabulary size affect performance and scalability?
*   Can you elaborate on the choice of `q = 0.9` and justify its robustness across different datasets?



In summary, Doc2VecC presents a promising new direction for document representation learning. However, the paper requires further development and a more rigorous analysis to fully support its claims and convince me of its significant impact. I recommend rejection in its current form, but encourage the authors to address the concerns outlined above and resubmit a revised version.
