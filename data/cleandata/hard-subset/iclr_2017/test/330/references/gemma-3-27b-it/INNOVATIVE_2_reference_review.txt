This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to learning document vector representations. The core idea—representing documents as the average of word embeddings *during* training, coupled with a corruption mechanism—is a refreshingly simple yet potentially powerful concept. The authors motivate this approach by observing the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and aim to address limitations of existing document vector methods, particularly the scalability issues of Paragraph Vectors. The paper claims state-of-the-art or competitive performance on several text understanding tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, while boasting improved training and inference efficiency.

**Reject.** While the core idea is intriguing and the paper demonstrates promising empirical results, I find the presentation and validation somewhat lacking for an acceptance at ICLR. The novelty lies in a relatively simple modification to existing methods, and the paper doesn’t fully convince me that the observed improvements are robust and well-understood, especially given the limitations acknowledged in the experimental section.

My primary concern revolves around the lack of thorough ablation studies. The authors introduce a "corruption" mechanism, claiming it acts as a data-dependent regularization. However, they don't provide a clear comparison of performance *without* corruption. Is the averaging of word embeddings itself sufficient? How sensitive are the results to the corruption rate *q*?  Furthermore, the explanation of *why* the corruption acts as a regularization, while mathematically presented, feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks intuitive clarity. The connection between the Taylor expansion and the resulting regularization effect isn't fully elucidated.  The empirical results, while positive, are presented without statistical significance tests, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the improvements.  The comparisons to baselines are good, but a more focused analysis of the components contributing to Doc2VecC's success is needed.

Additionally, the paper relies heavily on pre-trained word embeddings (Glove). While common, it obscures the extent to which the performance gains are due to the document representation learning technique versus the quality of the initial word embeddings. A comparison starting from randomly initialized word embeddings would provide a stronger assessment of Doc2VecC’s ability to learn meaningful representations from scratch.  The experiment involving word analogy, while interesting, feels somewhat disconnected from the core task of document representation. A more direct analysis of the learned document embeddings—perhaps through visualization or probing tasks specifically designed for document-level semantics—would be more compelling.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of Notation:** The notation section is quite dense. Consider simplifying it or moving some definitions to the relevant sections where they are first used.
*   **Related Work:** The related work section is extensive but could benefit from a more critical discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches.
*   **Experimental Details:** Provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process. What search space was used for learning rate, embedding dimension, etc.?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to support the claims of improved performance.
*   **Ablation Study:** Crucially, add a thorough ablation study varying the corruption rate and even removing the corruption step entirely.
*   **Zero-Shot Learning:** Explore the zero-shot learning capabilities of Doc2VecC. Can it generalize to new categories or tasks without retraining?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the performance of Doc2VecC *without* the corruption mechanism?  Is the corruption rate particularly sensitive?
2.  Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of how the corruption acts as a form of data-dependent regularization, beyond the Taylor expansion?
3.  How do the learned document embeddings compare to those from Paragraph Vectors or Doc2Vec in terms of their ability to capture specific semantic relationships between documents (e.g., using a document similarity task)?
4.  Have you experimented with starting from randomly initialized word embeddings, instead of pre-trained Glove embeddings? If so, what were the results?
5.  Can you discuss the computational cost of the Hessian calculation in the Taylor expansion, and how it scales with vocabulary and embedding size? Is this a practical bottleneck for very large datasets?
