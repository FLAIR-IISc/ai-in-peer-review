- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of word embeddings with random word corruption during training to learn robust document vectors.  
- Key contribution is the corruption mechanism, motivated by dropout and denoising autoencoders, with a theoretical regularization justification.  
- Claims model complexity decouples from corpus size and that corruption provides data-dependent regularization.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, often outperforming baselines.  
- Emphasizes efficiency: reduced training and inference time (Table 2) as a major advantage for large corpora.  
- Paper is well-written with clear equations and helpful figures explaining the method.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions due to simplicity, effectiveness, and solid theoretical/empirical support.  
- Suggest adding an ablation study to show sensitivity (or insensitivity) to the corruption rate and other hyperparameters (embedding size, window).  
- Recommend a more detailed discussion of limitations, e.g., potential issues on very short sentences and other failure cases.  
- Ask for comparison to a simple average of pre-trained Word2Vec (no training) to isolate benefits of corruption and training.  
- Request earlier clarification of dataset size used for the word analogy experiments (mentioned only in Table 5 caption).  
- Advise expanding practical guidance on vocabulary size choices and handling out-of-vocabulary words.  
- Questions: provide intuition on the magnitude of regularization induced by corruption and its relationship to the corruption rate (q).  
- Questions: report sensitivity to negative sampling rate and its effect on performance.  
- Questions: discuss applicability to languages other than English and potential modifications for different linguistic structures.  
- Questions: clarify practical scaling limits when applied to extremely large datasets (memory/computation bottlenecks).
