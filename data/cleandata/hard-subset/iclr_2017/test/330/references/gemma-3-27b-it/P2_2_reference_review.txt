## ICLR 2024 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The authors argue this approach is advantageous due to its simplicity, efficiency (decoupled model complexity from corpus size), implicit regularization, and strong empirical performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, matching or exceeding state-of-the-art results. The paper provides a clear theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach to document representation learning, offering a good balance of theoretical justification and strong empirical validation. The simplicity and efficiency of the proposed method are particularly attractive.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The idea of leveraging the compositionality of word embeddings through averaging, combined with the corruption mechanism, is a sensible and well-motivated approach. The paper effectively builds upon previous work in Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, identifying their limitations and proposing a solution.
* **Theoretical Foundation:** The analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer is a significant strength. Deriving the regularization term and explaining its effect on the embeddings provides valuable insight into the method's behavior. The explanation is clear and convincing.
* **Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates strong performance across a diverse set of tasks. The results consistently show Doc2VecC performing competitively with or outperforming existing methods. The comparison to Paragraph Vectors, particularly highlighting the efficiency gains during testing, is noteworthy. The extensive experimental evaluation, including the ablation study on the effect of corruption, lends credibility to the claims.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The notation is well-defined, and the model architecture is easy to understand.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions that Doc2VecC isn't overly sensitive to the corruption rate *q*, a more detailed exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity (learning rate, embedding dimension, window size) would strengthen the paper. Providing guidance on selecting appropriate hyperparameters for different datasets would be helpful.
* **Detailed Analysis of Error Cases:** While the quantitative results are strong, a qualitative analysis of error cases could provide deeper insight into the strengths and weaknesses of Doc2VecC.  Are there specific types of documents or sentences where the model struggles?
* **Connection to Related Regularization Techniques:** Briefly discussing connections to other regularization techniques, such as dropout, beyond the mention in Section 3.1, might add further context and highlight the unique benefits of the proposed approach.
* **Code Availability:** While a GitHub link is provided, ensuring that the code is well-documented and readily reproducible is crucial for fostering adoption and further research.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the Corruption Mechanism:**  The paper explains that the corruption mechanism introduces a data-dependent regularization. Could you elaborate on how the choice of the corruption probability *q* influences the type of regularization applied? Are there theoretical guidelines for selecting an optimal *q* value based on dataset characteristics?
* **Regarding Scaling:** While the paper highlights the efficiency gains of Doc2VecC, how does its performance scale with extremely large datasets (e.g., billions of documents)? Are there potential bottlenecks or limitations that might emerge at scale?
* **Regarding the Semantic Relatedness Task:** The results on the SemEval 2014 Task 1 are impressive.  Did you observe any trade-offs between performance on this task and performance on the other tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification)?  If so, how were these trade-offs addressed?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The proposed method is both theoretically sound and empirically effective, and the clear presentation makes it accessible to a wide audience. The minor revisions suggested above would further enhance the paper's impact.
