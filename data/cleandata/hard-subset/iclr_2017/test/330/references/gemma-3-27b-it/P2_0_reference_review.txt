## ICLR 2024 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, *enforced during training* through a corruption mechanism (random word removal). This differs from previous approaches that either learn a separate document vector or post-process existing word embeddings. The authors claim Doc2VecC offers several advantages: decoupled complexity from corpus size, training efficiency (similar to Word2Vec), implicit regularization, efficient test-time representation generation, and competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Reject.** While the idea is conceptually interesting and the paper is well-written, the experimental results, as presented, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advancement over existing methods to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive conference like ICLR. Specifically, the performance gains are often marginal, and the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for robust evaluation. The paper relies heavily on comparisons to baselines without clearly demonstrating a substantial improvement justifying the novelty of the proposed approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Limited Performance Gains:** While Doc2VecC achieves competitive results, the improvements over strong baselines (like Paragraph Vectors) are often small. For example, in the document classification task, the improvement is a few percentage points (around 1-2%), which, while positive, doesn’t seem substantial enough given the introduction of a new method. The gains are not consistently observed across all tasks either.
* **Insufficient Experimental Detail:** The experimental setup lacks key details. For example, the hyperparameters (learning rate, window size, embedding dimension, corruption rate) for Doc2VecC and baselines are not consistently reported or clearly justified. A more thorough ablation study would be beneficial to understand the contribution of the corruption mechanism specifically. The choice of datasets is also not justified beyond being standard benchmarks.
* **Overstated Claims:** The claim of “significant boost” in test efficiency is misleading.  All methods utilizing averaged word embeddings (Word2Vec+IDF, DEA, Doc2VecC) have similar test efficiency. The benefit isn’t unique to Doc2VecC. Similarly, the claim of “data-dependent regularization” feels somewhat contrived based on the second-order Taylor expansion and doesn't translate into clearly demonstrable benefits beyond performance.
* **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The paper doesn’t provide any statistical significance tests to support the observed performance differences.  It’s unclear if the observed improvements are statistically significant or simply due to random chance.



**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Ablation Study:** Conduct a thorough ablation study to isolate the impact of the corruption mechanism. Compare Doc2VecC *without* corruption to the baseline of simply averaging word embeddings. This will help quantify the actual benefit of the corruption strategy.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of Doc2VecC's performance to different hyperparameters, particularly the corruption rate (q).  Provide a clear rationale for the chosen hyperparameters.
* **Statistical Significance Testing:** Include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests, Wilcoxon signed-rank test) to demonstrate that the observed performance differences are statistically significant.
* **More Robust Baselines:** Include more recent and stronger baseline models where available.  The landscape of document representation learning is constantly evolving.
* **Qualitative Analysis:**  While quantitative results are important, some qualitative analysis of the learned word embeddings and document representations could provide valuable insights.  Are there examples where Doc2VecC captures semantic relationships better than other methods?
* **Clarify the Motivation:** The connection between the initial motivating examples (e.g., "king - man + woman = queen") and the overall approach of averaging embeddings with corruption isn't entirely clear.  A stronger connection should be made.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational cost of the second-order Taylor expansion used to derive the regularization term?  Does this computational overhead affect the training time significantly?
2.  Could you provide a detailed breakdown of the hyperparameters used for Doc2VecC and each baseline method, along with the rationale for their selection?
3.  Have you experimented with different corruption strategies beyond the random removal of words (e.g., replacing words with a mask token)?  If so, how did they compare?
4.  Regarding the claim of implicit regularization, can you provide examples of learned word embeddings where you observe a clear damping effect of frequent, uninformative words as predicted by your analysis?
5.  What is the variance of the performance results reported? Providing confidence intervals would give more confidence in the results.



In conclusion, while Doc2VecC presents a reasonable approach to document representation learning, the lack of substantial performance gains and insufficient experimental details prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time.  Addressing the feedback above could significantly strengthen the paper.
