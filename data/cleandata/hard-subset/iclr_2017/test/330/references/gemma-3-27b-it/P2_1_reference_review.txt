## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of their word embeddings, and crucially, to incorporate a “corruption” mechanism during training which randomly removes words from the document. The authors argue this corruption acts as a form of regularization, favoring informative words and improving performance.  The method boasts several advantages: decoupling model complexity from corpus size, training efficiency similar to Word2Vec, and strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results in some cases.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept.** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated approach to document vectorization. The results demonstrate clear improvements over existing methods on multiple benchmarks, and the proposed corruption mechanism offers a theoretically sound and practically effective regularization technique. The efficiency gains are also a significant benefit.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty & Motivation:** The idea of directly averaging word embeddings *and* enforcing this through a corruption-based learning process is a valuable contribution.  The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing methods (Paragraph Vectors' scalability, post-processing reliance in other approaches) and position Doc2VecC as a solution. The connection to compositional semantics (citing Mikolov et al.’s vector arithmetic) is well-established.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements across diverse tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness). The comparisons to strong baselines (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors) are comprehensive and generally favor Doc2VecC. The ablation studies (e.g., impact of the corruption mechanism) are convincing.
* **Theoretical Justification:** The explanation of the corruption as a data-dependent regularization is well-developed and insightful. The Taylor expansion and derivation showing the regularization term are a significant strength, providing a solid theoretical grounding for the empirical observations.
* **Efficiency:** The reported training and inference times are appealing, particularly the fast test-time vector generation compared to methods requiring inference.  This makes Doc2VecC practical for large-scale applications.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  While the authors mention the model isn’t very sensitive to the corruption rate (q), a more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity would be beneficial.  Specifically, how sensitive is performance to the embedding dimensionality (h) and the window size used in the CBoW model?
* **Dataset Details:** While the datasets used are standard, providing more detail regarding preprocessing steps (e.g., stemming, stop word removal) would be helpful for reproducibility.
* **Visualization Clarity:**  Figure 1 is a little cluttered. A clearer visual representation of the corruption process might improve understanding.
* **Skip-Thought Vectors Discussion:** The authors briefly mention the poor performance of Skip-thought vectors, attributing it to paragraph length. Expanding this discussion with possible reasons (e.g., differences in pre-training data) would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the Corruption Mechanism:** You state the corruption acts as a data-dependent regularization.  Is there a relationship between the optimal corruption rate *q* and the characteristics of the dataset (e.g., average document length, vocabulary size, noise levels)? Could you provide a guide on how to choose an appropriate *q* for new datasets?
* **Regarding Scalability:** While the model complexity is decoupled from corpus size, what are the practical memory limitations when dealing with extremely large vocabularies (e.g., millions of words)? Does the embedding matrix become a bottleneck?
* **Ablation Study Clarification:** You mention that removing the local context words from Doc2VecC reduces it to a denoising autoencoder.  However, the corruption mechanism still exists in that case.  Could you clarify the specific comparison you are making and provide results showing the contribution of *both* the local context *and* the document corruption?
* **Comparison to Newer Methods:** Since the submission of this paper, several new document embedding techniques have emerged (e.g., Sentence Transformers). How does Doc2VecC compare to these more recent approaches?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning.  The proposed method is novel, well-motivated, empirically validated, and theoretically grounded. I believe it warrants acceptance to ICLR.
