## Review for "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, while incorporating a "corruption" mechanism – randomly removing words during training – which acts as a regularizer and speeds up learning. The authors demonstrate through experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, while being significantly more efficient, especially during test time. The paper is well-motivated, building on the success of Word2Vec and addressing limitations of Paragraph Vectors.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling approach with solid empirical results, addressing a relevant problem in text representation learning. The efficiency gains and the insightful connection between corruption and regularization are particularly noteworthy.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Strong Empirical Performance:** The results across all three tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness) consistently demonstrate that Doc2VecC performs competitively, often outperforming existing methods. The performance on the Wikipedia document classification task is particularly convincing, showcasing a clear advantage over baselines.
*   **Efficiency:**  The paper clearly highlights the computational advantages of Doc2VecC, particularly in test time, avoiding the costly inference step required by Paragraph Vectors. This makes it much more practical for real-world applications.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The connection between the corruption mechanism and data-dependent regularization is well-explained and theoretically grounded. The Taylor expansion argument provides a solid rationale for why this approach works.
*   **Clear Writing & Motivation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the motivation behind the proposed method, effectively building upon existing work in the field.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions the corruption rate *q* doesn't significantly impact performance, it would be beneficial to provide a more detailed analysis of the sensitivity of Doc2VecC to other hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension, window size) and how these parameters were chosen.
*   **Ablation Study:** A dedicated ablation study isolating the contribution of the corruption mechanism would further strengthen the argument for its importance. For example, comparing Doc2VecC to a version *without* corruption (simply averaging word embeddings) would clearly demonstrate the benefits.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** While the quantitative results are strong, a qualitative analysis of the learned word embeddings and document vectors could provide further insights into what the model is capturing. Visualizing the word embeddings using t-SNE or PCA could reveal interesting patterns.
*   **Related Work Expansion:** The related work section is thorough, but could briefly mention more recent advances in contextualized word embeddings (e.g., ELMo, BERT), and discuss how Doc2VecC complements or differs from these approaches.
*  **Code Quality**: While the code is available, some clarification on the data pre-processing steps in the GitHub repository would be beneficial for reproducibility.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Corruption and Out-of-Vocabulary Words:** How does the corruption mechanism interact with out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words? Are OOV words always removed during corruption, or are they treated differently?
*   **Scaling to Larger Datasets:** While the experiments demonstrate good scalability, what are the anticipated limitations of Doc2VecC when applied to extremely large datasets (e.g., those with billions of documents)? 
*   **Impact of Window Size:** You mention the model resembles Word2Vec.  Did you experiment with different window sizes and how did this affect the performance of Doc2VecC?
*   **Computational Cost of Hessian Approximation:** The paper mentions approximating the Hessian matrix. Could you provide more details on the computational cost of this approximation, and how it affects the overall training time?
*   **Reproducibility:** Please clarify the exact steps for data pre-processing and setup, including specific versions of libraries used, to ensure complete reproducibility of the results.



Overall, this is a strong paper with a well-motivated and effective approach. The proposed Doc2VecC model offers a compelling solution for efficient and accurate document representation learning. Addressing the minor feedback points would further enhance the quality and impact of the work.
