This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism involving random word removal during training. The authors motivate this approach by observing that linear combinations of Word2Vec embeddings can preserve semantic meaning and aim to learn embeddings where averaging is a meaningful operation. They claim Doc2VecC is efficient, introduces data-dependent regularization, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on several text classification and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of directly learning for average-based composition is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and, critically, lacks convincing evidence to support its claims of state-of-the-art performance. The presentation feels somewhat superficial and relies heavily on assertions without providing the necessary depth of analysis.

My primary concerns stem from the experimental evaluation. The results presented, while showing some improvement over baselines, are not overwhelmingly convincing. The comparisons are often with older or less sophisticated baselines (e.g., basic BoW) and the claims of “matching or beating state-of-the-art” require more robust justification. The performance gains are modest and frequently lack statistical significance testing. Furthermore, the extensive comparisons to Skip-thought Vectors seem unfair, as the skip-thought vector model wasn’t re-trained on the specific datasets used for evaluation, while Doc2VecC was. This makes a direct comparison difficult to interpret. The ablation study regarding the corruption model is also insufficient; merely stating it's a form of regularization isn’t enough – the impact of varying the corruption rate *quantitatively* needs to be demonstrated. The data-dependent regularization explanation, while theoretically appealing, is presented without sufficient empirical validation. Is this regularization *actually* suppressing common, uninformative words, or is the observed performance improvement due to other factors?

Additionally, the paper’s reliance on established methods (Word2Vec) as a foundation doesn’t inherently justify the proposed addition. The core idea – averaging embeddings – is not new (mentioned themselves), and the contribution of the "corruption" mechanism needs to be more clearly articulated and justified through rigorous experimentation. The discussion of related work, while extensive, doesn’t adequately contextualize the paper’s contribution within the broader landscape of document representation learning. The introduction briefly mentions limitations of Paragraph Vectors, but doesn’t fully engage with how Doc2VecC specifically addresses these, aside from efficiency. The explanations regarding the mathematical derivation of the regularization term are dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for all performance comparisons to demonstrate the reliability of the results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform a thorough ablation study on the corruption mechanism, varying the corruption rate (q) and analyzing its impact on performance.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Include more recent and competitive baselines, such as transformers-based models (e.g., BERT, Sentence-BERT) or more advanced averaging techniques with learned weights.
*   **Regularization Validation:** Provide empirical evidence (e.g., analyzing the norms of word embeddings before and after training) to support the claim that the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of Doc2VecC’s performance to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Offer qualitative examples of how Doc2VecC captures document semantics.
*   **Clarify the Novelty:** Clearly articulate what distinguishes Doc2VecC from existing averaging-based approaches.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of the corruption mechanism during training, and how does it scale with document length and corpus size?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why averaging word embeddings works well in this framework, and what properties of the learned embeddings enable this?
*   How does the choice of Word2Vec hyperparameters (e.g., window size, negative sampling rate) affect the performance of Doc2VecC?
*   Have you explored alternative corruption mechanisms, such as replacing words with masked tokens or randomly shuffling words? If so, how did they compare to the proposed approach?
*   Could you share the exact command line arguments and configurations used to run the experiments, to ensure reproducibility?
