## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, a technique coupled with a “corruption” mechanism – randomly removing words during training.  The authors argue this approach is more efficient than existing methods like Paragraph Vectors (which scale with corpus size) and allows for fast inference.  They demonstrate, through experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods while being computationally more efficient. A key contribution is the framing of the corruption process as a form of data-dependent regularization, favoring informative words.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. This paper presents a simple yet effective method for document vectorization. The theoretical justification, experimental results, and clear writing make a compelling case for Doc2VecC's utility. While the core idea of averaging word embeddings isn’t entirely novel, the addition of the corruption mechanism and its subsequent theoretical analysis significantly elevates the contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Strong Motivation & Novelty:** The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing document representation methods (BoW, Paragraph Vectors) and position Doc2VecC as a solution to those problems, particularly scalability and inference speed. The corruption mechanism introduces a novel element and the connection to data-dependent regularization is insightful.
*   **Solid Empirical Results:**  The results across three diverse tasks are convincing. Doc2VecC consistently performs well, often surpassing strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors and achieving comparable results to more complex models like LSTM-based approaches. The inclusion of both accuracy and timing results is commendable.
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  The derivation of the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization is well-explained and provides a strong theoretical foundation for the method. The explanation of how the regularization affects word embeddings is particularly valuable.
*   **Clear and Concise Writing:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The notation is clearly defined, and the figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Ablation Study on Corruption:** While the paper demonstrates the effectiveness of Doc2VecC, a more targeted ablation study on the corruption rate (q) could further strengthen the analysis. Showing performance variations with different q values would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:**  While many strong baselines are included, a simple baseline like averaging pre-trained GloVe or fastText embeddings (without any corruption or training) could highlight the benefit of the training process in Doc2VecC.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., embedding dimension, window size) would be helpful.
*   **Computational Resources:** Providing more details about the computational resources used (e.g., GPU type, memory) would enhance reproducibility.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A short discussion on the limitations of Doc2VecC, such as its potential weakness in capturing long-range dependencies compared to RNNs, would provide a more balanced view.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Intuition for Corruption:** The authors mention the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization. Could you elaborate on the intuition behind *why* randomly removing words leads to more robust and informative word embeddings? Is there a connection to ideas from dropout in neural networks beyond the regularization aspect?
*   **Impact of Corpus Size:** The paper highlights the scalability advantage over Paragraph Vectors. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how performance scales with increasing corpus size for Doc2VecC and Paragraph Vectors, perhaps with a graph showing training time versus corpus size?
*   **Negative Sampling:** The choice of uniform distribution for negative sampling (Pv) seems straightforward. Have you experimented with other negative sampling distributions (e.g., based on word frequency) and observed any significant performance differences?
*   **Word Order:**  Since Doc2VecC relies on averaging word embeddings, it inherently loses word order information.  Could you discuss how this impacts performance on tasks where word order is crucial, and whether there are potential modifications to mitigate this issue (e.g., incorporating positional embeddings)?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The authors present a novel and efficient method with strong theoretical justification and empirical validation. Addressing the minor feedback points and answering the questions above would further strengthen the paper.
