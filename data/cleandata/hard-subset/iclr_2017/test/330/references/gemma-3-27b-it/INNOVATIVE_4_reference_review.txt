This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach to document vector representation learning. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of their word embeddings, enforced during training via a corruption mechanism – randomly removing words during the learning process. The authors motivate this approach by highlighting the compositional properties of Word2Vec embeddings and aim to address the scalability and inference costs associated with existing document vector models like Paragraph Vectors. They demonstrate competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, alongside significantly improved training and inference efficiency.

**Reject.** While the idea is intriguing and addresses a practical limitation of existing methods, the paper feels somewhat underdeveloped and lacks the depth of analysis expected for ICLR. The gains demonstrated aren’t substantial enough to justify a potentially paradigm-shifting approach, and the empirical validation, while present, isn't convincing enough to offset this.

The core strength lies in the novelty of the approach. Directly learning document representations through averaged word embeddings *during* training, coupled with the corruption mechanism, is a clever idea. It elegantly addresses the scalability issue of Paragraph Vectors and offers a compelling alternative to post-processing word embeddings. The theoretical justification, specifically the connection to data-dependent regularization through the Taylor expansion, is also a strong point, demonstrating a thoughtful consideration of the method's properties. 

However, the experiments, while comprehensive in terms of dataset coverage, lack sufficient detail and comparative analysis. The performance improvements over baselines are often marginal, and the paper doesn’t adequately investigate *why* Doc2VecC works.  For example, the ablation study regarding the corruption rate is mentioned but not thoroughly presented in the results. The visualization using t-SNE is suggestive but lacks quantitative evaluation to support the claim of improved clustering. The authors state Doc2VecC doesn’t rely on subsampling frequent words, but don’t analyze the impact of *removing* this step from the baselines to demonstrate the inherent regularization effect.  The discussion of Skip-thought Vectors' performance feels somewhat dismissive, attributing it solely to paragraph length without a more detailed analysis. The advantage in speed, while significant, feels secondary to the core contribution of a novel representation learning method. A deeper investigation into the qualitative differences in the learned embeddings would strengthen the paper.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Elaborate on the corruption mechanism:** While the rationale is explained, a more detailed discussion of the impact of different corruption rates (q) on the learned representations and performance would be valuable. Perhaps a sensitivity analysis?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Systematically remove components (e.g., the corruption mechanism) to isolate their contribution to performance.
*   **Embedding Analysis:**  Visualize the embeddings learned by Doc2VecC and compare them to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. Are there discernible differences in the clustering of semantically related words?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Provide a more thorough analysis of the hyperparameter settings and their influence on the results.
*   **Theoretical Discussion:** The Taylor expansion is interesting, but could benefit from more accessible explanation for a broader audience.
*   **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis, looking at examples where Doc2VecC fails, could provide valuable insights.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of the corruption rate (q) on performance and the learned embeddings?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of Word2Vec parameters (e.g., window size, negative sampling rate)?
3.  Can you provide examples of words where the regularization effect of the corruption mechanism is most pronounced, and explain how this impacts downstream tasks?
4.  You mention the limitations of Skip-thought Vectors on longer documents. Have you considered any techniques to mitigate this limitation, such as hierarchical Skip-thought Vectors or truncation?
5.  Beyond the tasks presented, have you explored the performance of Doc2VecC on other document-level tasks, such as topic modeling or document summarization?
