## ICLR 2017 Review: MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION (Doc2VecC)

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, and crucially, to introduce a corruption mechanism – randomly removing words during training – to encourage the learning of robust and informative representations.  The authors argue that this approach decouples model complexity from corpus size, improves training efficiency, provides a form of data-dependent regularization, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions.  The paper presents a simple yet effective approach to document vectorization. The theoretical motivation behind the corruption mechanism as a regularizer is compelling, and the empirical results demonstrate strong performance across a variety of tasks.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** While the idea of averaging word embeddings isn’t entirely new, the introduction of the corruption mechanism is a key contribution. The authors effectively motivate this mechanism by drawing parallels to successful techniques like dropout and denoising autoencoders, and provide a theoretical justification based on Taylor expansion and regularization.
* **Strong Empirical Performance:**  The paper presents a comprehensive evaluation across diverse tasks. Doc2VecC consistently achieves competitive or superior results compared to established baselines (BoW, DEA, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors). The results on the Wikipedia document classification task and the semantic relatedness task are particularly convincing.
* **Efficiency:** The authors highlight the efficiency of their approach, which is a significant advantage, especially when dealing with large corpora. The reduced computational cost during both training and inference is a strong selling point.  The comparison of training and inference times in Table 2 clearly demonstrates this benefit.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. The equations are well-defined, and the figures are helpful in illustrating the model architecture.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Elaborate on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate (q). It would be beneficial to include a small ablation study demonstrating this insensitivity and perhaps explore the impact of other hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, window size) on performance.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the results are strong, it's important to acknowledge any limitations. For example, the authors briefly mention potential issues with the approach for very short sentences, but a more detailed discussion of scenarios where Doc2VecC might underperform would strengthen the paper.
* **Comparison with Simpler Baselines:**  Including a comparison to a simple average of pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings (without any training) as a baseline could further highlight the benefits of the proposed corruption mechanism and training procedure.
* **Clarify the Dataset Size used for Word Analogy:** The text mentions training on the English news dataset, but the size isn't explicitly stated until the caption for Table 5.  Adding this information earlier would improve readability.
* **Expand on Practical Considerations:**  Discussing the practical implications of choosing appropriate vocabulary size and handling out-of-vocabulary words could be helpful for potential users of the method.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Regularization Strength:** Could you provide more intuition regarding the magnitude of the regularization effect induced by the corruption mechanism? Is there a relationship between the corruption rate (q) and the strength of regularization?
*   **Impact of Negative Sampling:**  How sensitive is the performance to the choice of the negative sampling rate?
* **Generalizability to Different Languages:** Have you experimented with applying Doc2VecC to languages other than English? Do you anticipate any challenges or modifications would be necessary for different linguistic structures?
* **Scaling to Extremely Large Datasets:**  While you claim the model complexity is decoupled from corpus size, what are the practical limitations of scaling Doc2VecC to datasets containing trillions of words?  Are there any memory or computational bottlenecks that might arise?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling approach and promising results. Addressing the suggested improvements will further enhance the quality and impact of the work.
