This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. This approach aims to be efficient, scalable, and implicitly regularized, leading to competitive performance on several text understanding tasks.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of convincing novelty and significant empirical advantage over existing methods, given the substantial body of prior work already exploring averaging word embeddings for document representation. While the corruption mechanism is a reasonable addition, its impact doesn’t appear substantial enough to warrant a dedicated publication, especially considering the established benefits of similar regularization techniques (like dropout) already employed in related work. Furthermore, the presentation of results is somewhat scattered and lacks a clear comparative analysis highlighting *why* Doc2VecC outperforms other methods beyond simply stating that it does.

Here's a more detailed breakdown of my concerns:

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of averaging word embeddings for document representation is not new (as acknowledged in the Related Work). The authors frame the corruption mechanism as key, but its connection to existing regularization techniques (like dropout) is not fully explored. The authors state it introduces a data-dependent regularization, however, this is somewhat hand-wavy and not rigorously justified. It isn't clear *how* the corruption differs meaningfully from simply applying dropout to the word embeddings during document vector creation.

2. **Insufficient Empirical Evidence:** The results are presented across multiple tasks, but the improvements are often marginal. For example, on the Wikipedia dataset, the improvement is 1-2% which, while positive, might not be statistically significant or practically meaningful. More importantly, the authors don’t provide sufficient analysis to understand *why* Doc2VecC performs better. Is it the corruption mechanism? The implicit regularization? The averaging process itself?  The paper would benefit from ablation studies specifically isolating the contribution of the corruption component.

3. **Presentation of Results:** The paper jumps between tasks and datasets without a unifying narrative. The experiments feel somewhat disconnected. While the Github link is provided, the reproducibility is hindered by the reliance on external datasets and potentially complex training procedures.  The discussion of the word analogy task feels particularly detached from the main contribution. The authors claim significant improvements, but don't thoroughly explain why the method excels at this particular task, or how it relates to the document representation goal.

4. **Theoretical Justification:** The theoretical analysis of the corruption as a regularization technique (Section 3.1) is complex and not entirely convincing. The Taylor expansion and resulting equations are difficult to follow, and the practical implications of the derived regularization term aren't clearly explained.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify Novelty:**  Explicitly position Doc2VecC relative to existing methods that utilize averaged word embeddings. What specific problem does it solve that others don't, and how?  A more in-depth comparison with related approaches like SIF (Sentence-BERT’s predecessor) or simpler weighted averaging techniques would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct rigorous ablation studies to assess the contribution of the corruption mechanism. Compare performance with and without corruption, and explore different corruption rates.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all results to demonstrate that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
*   **Error Analysis:** Include a qualitative error analysis. Examine examples where Doc2VecC fails and try to understand the underlying reasons.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters (e.g., corruption rate, embedding dimension).
*   **Computational Cost:** While the paper mentions efficiency, a more detailed analysis of the computational cost (training and inference time) compared to other methods would be valuable.
*   **Simplify Theoretical Section:** The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is overly complex. Consider streamlining the explanation and focusing on the key intuition.
* **Consider a more streamlined presentation:** I recommend consolidating the results. Perhaps focus on 2-3 key experiments and provide a more thorough analysis of those.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the corruption mechanism differs from standard dropout techniques applied to word embeddings?
2.  What is the computational overhead of the corruption mechanism during training? Does it significantly increase training time?
3.  Have you experimented with different corruption strategies (e.g., masking entire phrases instead of individual words)? If so, what were the results?
4.  Can you provide more insight into *why* Doc2VecC outperforms other methods on the Wikipedia dataset? What specific characteristics of the Wikipedia data make it well-suited for this approach?
5.  Could you clarify the role of the pre-trained Glove vectors in your experiments? Does performance vary if you train the word embeddings from scratch?
6.  How does the performance of Doc2VecC compare to more recent and advanced sentence/document embedding techniques like Sentence-BERT?
