### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed vector representations for documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. To ensure this compositional representation is semantically meaningful, the model is trained in a framework similar to CBoW Word2Vec, where the goal is to predict a target word given its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. The key innovation is that this global context is derived from a "corrupted" version of the document, created by randomly dropping out a large fraction of its words.

The authors make several key claims:
1.  The model is highly efficient and scalable, as its complexity depends on the vocabulary size, not the corpus size, unlike Paragraph Vectors.
2.  Inference for new documents is extremely fast, requiring only an averaging of word vectors.
3.  The corruption mechanism is not just a computational trick but is shown, through a Taylor expansion analysis, to be equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words, thereby promoting more meaningful representations.
4.  Empirically, the method matches or exceeds the performance of strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, on tasks like sentiment analysis, document classification, and word analogy, while being significantly faster to train and use.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective method for an important problem. The decision to accept is based on three primary reasons:

1.  **Well-Motivated and Novel Approach:** The method cleverly combines ideas from Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders. The use of a corrupted average as a global context is a novel way to enforce meaningful compositionality in the learned word embeddings. The theoretical justification connecting this corruption to a data-dependent regularizer provides significant insight into why the method works and is a standout contribution.
2.  **Excellent Empirical Validation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation across four different tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness) against a comprehensive set of strong baselines. Doc2VecC demonstrates consistently strong, often state-of-the-art, performance. The inclusion of results where the model is not the absolute best (SICK dataset) and the candid discussion of why this might be the case adds to the credibility of the evaluation.
3.  **Significant Practical Advantages:** The paper effectively addresses major practical drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors, namely the slow inference time for unseen documents and the model size dependency on the training corpus. The reported speed-ups in both training and testing are substantial, making Doc2VecC a highly practical and attractive alternative for real-world applications.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strengths are evident in its clear motivation, technical depth, and rigorous experiments.

**Motivation and Method:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of prior work, particularly Paragraph Vectors, and proposes a solution that directly targets these weaknesses. The model architecture (Figure 1) is intuitive and easy to grasp. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a major highlight. It elevates the "corruption" mechanism from a simple heuristic to a principled form of regularization. This analysis explains the empirical observation that the model learns to suppress the embeddings of common stop words (Table 3) without relying on heuristic subsampling techniques.

**Experimental Rigor:**
*   **Sentiment Analysis (IMDB):** Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art results (Table 1), outperforming Paragraph Vectors and significantly beating Word2Vec+IDF and the surprisingly poor Skip-thought vectors. The efficiency comparison in Table 2 is particularly compelling, showing orders of magnitude faster inference time than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought.
*   **Document Classification (Wikipedia):** The method again shows a clear advantage over all baselines across different embedding dimensions (Table 5). The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) provide strong qualitative evidence that the learned representations produce better-separated clusters than those from Doc2Vec.
*   **Word Analogy Task:** The results demonstrating that Doc2VecC produces higher-quality word embeddings than both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors are very impressive. A 20% uplift over Word2Vec is substantial and suggests that forcing the embeddings to be compositionally meaningful improves their intrinsic quality.
*   **Semantic Relatedness (SICK):** While LSTM-based methods and Skip-thought vectors perform better here, the authors provide a plausible hypothesis related to document length. The fact that Doc2VecC is still competitive with feature-engineered systems and is orders of magnitude faster to train is a strong result.

The combination of these results strongly supports the paper's claims about the quality and efficiency of the proposed method.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened for the final version. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the decision to accept.

1.  **Hyperparameter Details:** The paper mentions using a corruption rate of `q=0.9` throughout. However, details on other important hyperparameters (e.g., window size for local context, number of negative samples, learning rate) are missing. Including these in an appendix would improve reproducibility.
2.  **Ablation on Corruption Rate:** The paper claims the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q". While this may be true, the claim would be much stronger if supported by a small ablation study showing performance for a few different values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9).
3.  **Clarity on Normalization:** In Equation 2, the global context term is `1/T U x̃`, where `T` is the length of the original document. This implies the sum of the surviving word vectors is normalized by the original document length, not the number of surviving words. This seems unusual and could potentially down-weight the contribution of the global context significantly, especially with `q=0.9`. Clarification on this design choice and its effect would be welcome.
4.  **Minor Formatting:** Several tables and figures are missing numbers or have incorrect references in the text (e.g., there are two "Table 5"s, the word analogy table is unnumbered, Figure 4 is not referenced in the text). This should be corrected in the final version.

### Questions for Authors

1.  In Section 4.2, you state, "Note that for all the numbers reported, we applied the trick of subsampling of frequent words...". However, you also argue that Doc2VecC's data-dependent regularization makes this trick unnecessary. Could you clarify if the reported Doc2VecC results were achieved with or without frequent-word subsampling? If it was used, how does the performance change if it is removed, to better isolate the effect of the corruption-based regularization?
2.  Following up on the normalization point above: Can you confirm that the global context vector is indeed normalized by the original document length `T`? Have you experimented with normalizing by the number of non-zero elements in the corrupted vector `x̃`, i.e., `1/|{j|x̃_j > 0}|`, and how does that affect performance?
