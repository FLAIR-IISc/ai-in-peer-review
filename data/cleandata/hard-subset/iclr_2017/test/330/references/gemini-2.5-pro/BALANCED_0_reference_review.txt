### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. To ensure this simple compositional vector is semantically meaningful, the model is trained to predict a target word using both its local context (similar to Word2Vec) and a global document context. Critically, this global context is derived from a *corrupted* version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving the magnitude of rare or discriminative words. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC is not only significantly more efficient than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors but also achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful modification to the standard CBoW architecture. The use of corruption to enforce that a simple average of word vectors captures document-level semantics is an elegant and novel idea. It directly addresses the primary drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth with corpus size and slow inference) while retaining high performance.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The contribution is not merely a heuristic. The authors provide a principled justification for the corruption scheme by linking it to an explicit data-dependent regularizer via a Taylor expansion. This theoretical insight is complemented by extensive and convincing empirical results across a diverse set of tasks and datasets, which robustly demonstrate the method's effectiveness and efficiency.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

**Methodological Strength:** The primary novelty lies in the corruption mechanism. While using an average of word embeddings as a document vector is not new, previous methods often do this as a post-processing step. Doc2VecC integrates this compositional assumption directly into the training objective. The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects this stochastic corruption to a deterministic regularizer, is a significant contribution. It provides a clear explanation for why the method works: it learns word vectors that are not only good for local context prediction but are also regularized to be meaningfully composable. This regularization effect, which suppresses common words and elevates informative ones, is qualitatively validated in Table 3 and is a key factor in the model's success.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed.
*   **Efficiency:** Table 2 clearly demonstrates the practical advantages of Doc2VecC. It is orders of magnitude faster at inference for unseen documents than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, a crucial advantage for real-world applications.
*   **Performance:** Doc2VecC achieves excellent results across multiple domains. It outperforms Paragraph Vectors on document classification (Table 5) and is competitive on sentiment analysis (Table 1). The discussion regarding the trade-offs with sequence-based models like Skip-thought vectors (strong on short sentences like SICK, weaker on long documents like IMDB) is balanced and insightful.
*   **Representation Quality:** The evaluation is not limited to downstream task performance. The strong results on the word analogy task (Table 4) are particularly impressive, showing that Doc2VecC learns higher-quality word embeddings than both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. This suggests the global context training improves the word-level representations themselves, which is a valuable finding. The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) also provide strong qualitative evidence for the superior separability of the learned document representations.

**Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, and the method is explained clearly. The authors' decision to make the code available further strengthens the paper and its potential impact.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Analysis of Corruption Rate:** The paper mentions that a high corruption rate of `q=0.9` was used and that the model is "not sensitive" to this parameter. This is a strong and somewhat surprising claim. An ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9) on one of the tasks would make this claim more concrete and provide readers with better guidance for applying the method.
*   **Validity of Taylor Approximation:** The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is based on a second-order Taylor approximation. It would be beneficial to briefly discuss the validity of this approximation. For instance, are there reasons to believe the higher-order terms are negligible in this context?
*   **Comparison to Denoising Autoencoders:** The paper states that Doc2VecC reduces to a Denoising Autoencoder (DAE) if local context is removed. This comparison could be clarified. The objective of a DAE is typically reconstruction of the input, whereas Doc2VecC's objective is predicting a held-out word. This seems like a fundamental difference in training signals, and a more nuanced comparison would be valuable.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  **Normalization in Global Context:** In Equation 2, the global context term `(1/T) * U * x_tilde` is normalized by `T`, the length of the original document. Did you consider normalizing by the number of non-zero elements in the corrupted vector `x_tilde` instead? Could you clarify the rationale for this choice and how it might affect the model's learning and the derivation in Section 3.1?
2.  **Interaction with Subsampling:** In Section 4.2, you state that you "applied the trick of subsampling of frequent words" but also that Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick". To help disentangle the benefits of your proposed corruption-as-regularization from the standard subsampling heuristic, could you report the performance of Doc2VecC on the IMDB task *without* frequent-word subsampling?
3.  **Performance of Paragraph Vectors on Word Analogy:** The performance of Paragraph Vectors on the word analogy task is surprisingly poor (Table 4). Your hypothesis is that the model relies more on the unique document vectors than the word vectors. Is it possible that this is an artifact of the specific Paragraph Vector variant (Distributed Memory) used? Have you experimented with the DBOW variant, and does it exhibit a similar effect on the learned word embeddings?
