### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed vector representations for documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. Unlike previous methods that use averaging as a post-processing step, Doc2VecC enforces this compositional structure during training. This is achieved by modifying the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture to predict a target word from both its local context and a global context vector. The global context is formed by averaging the embeddings of a randomly sampled subset of words from the document, a process the authors term "corruption."

The paper makes several key claims. First, it provides a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer naturally penalizes the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, providing a principled alternative to heuristics like stop-word removal or frequent word subsampling. Second, the proposed model is highly efficient, as its complexity depends on the vocabulary size rather than the corpus size, and inference for new documents is a simple and fast averaging operation. Finally, through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, the paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, while being significantly faster to train and apply.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper introduces a simple yet powerful modification to the standard word embedding framework. The key novelty lies in using input corruption to enforce a compositional document representation during training. This approach is well-motivated and supported by a clear theoretical analysis that connects the corruption process to a data-dependent regularizer, providing a strong justification for the design.

2.  **Empirical Strength and Practical Significance:** The method is thoroughly validated against a strong suite of baselines across multiple diverse benchmarks. The results compellingly show that Doc2VecC is not only highly efficient in both training and inference but also produces high-quality document and word representations that match or exceed the performance of much more complex and computationally expensive models. This combination of simplicity, efficiency, and effectiveness makes the proposed method a significant practical contribution.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The decision to accept is based on the following objective assessments:

*   **Clarity:** The paper is well-written and clearly structured. The motivation is well-established by contrasting the proposed method with the limitations of Paragraph Vectors (scalability, slow inference). The method itself is described precisely in Section 3, and the architecture is clearly illustrated in Figure 1. The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1, while technical, is presented in a step-by-step manner that makes the connection between corruption and regularization understandable.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The core technical claim—that the corruption process acts as a specific form of regularization—is well-supported by the Taylor expansion analysis in Section 3.1. This provides a principled explanation for the model's effectiveness and its ability to down-weight uninformative words, a claim further substantiated by the qualitative analysis in Table 3. The overall model architecture is a logical extension of the successful Word2Vec framework, making its implementation straightforward and its behavior predictable.

*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   **Baselines:** The paper compares against relevant and strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), Skip-thought Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders.
    *   **Tasks:** The evaluation spans four different tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness), demonstrating the general applicability of the learned representations.
    *   **Analysis:** The results are compelling. Doc2VecC consistently outperforms Paragraph Vectors and other averaging-based methods. Crucially, Table 2 highlights the dramatic efficiency gains over both Paragraph Vectors (at inference time) and Skip-thought Vectors (at both training and inference time). The superior performance on the word analogy task (Section 4.3) is a strong indicator that the training process yields higher-quality compositional word embeddings, which is a significant finding. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide qualitative evidence that supports the quantitative classification results.

In summary, the paper successfully identifies key weaknesses in a widely used method (Paragraph Vectors) and proposes a simple, well-motivated, and theoretically grounded solution that is shown to be superior in both efficiency and performance through rigorous experimentation.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and uses `q=0.9` throughout. This is a very high corruption rate, meaning only 10% of words are used for the global context on average. An ablation study or a plot showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) on a validation set would make this claim more robust and provide practical guidance for users.
*   **Missing Table:** The text in Section 4.3 references Table 4 to show performance with increasing embedding dimensionality and training data size, but this table appears to be missing from the document provided. Including this table is important for completeness.
*   **Approximation Limitations:** The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is based on a second-order Taylor approximation. A brief comment on the potential limitations of this approximation or the conditions under which it holds best would add to the technical depth.
*   **Clarification on Averaging:** The notation in Equation 2, `(1/T) * Ux̃`, could be interpreted as dividing by the original document length `T`. It would be helpful to explicitly state whether the normalization is by `T` or by the number of non-zero elements in the corrupted vector `x̃`.

### 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the global context term in Equation 2, `(1/T) * Ux̃`, is `T` the length of the original document or the number of words remaining after corruption? The latter seems more intuitive for a true average, but the former might have a different regularizing effect.
2.  Your results show that Doc2VecC outperforms recurrent models like Skip-thought Vectors on long documents (IMDB) but is slightly worse on short sentences (SICK). This is a very interesting finding. Do you have additional insights into why the simple averaging approach scales better to longer texts, and at what approximate document length you observe this crossover in performance?
3.  Have you considered alternative corruption strategies? For example, instead of uniform random sampling, one could use a non-uniform strategy, such as one based on IDF, to preferentially keep more informative words in the global context vector. Would you expect such a strategy to offer further improvements?
