### Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

#### 1. Summary of the Paper
This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed document representations. The core idea is to extend the Word2Vec CBOW model by incorporating a global document context, similar to Paragraph Vectors. However, unlike Paragraph Vectors which learns a unique vector for each document, Doc2VecC represents the global context as a simple average of word embeddings. Crucially, during training, this average is computed over a "corrupted" version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism is equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the training objective. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, thereby encouraging the model to learn more meaningful representations. The resulting model is highly efficient: its complexity depends only on the vocabulary size (not the corpus size), and generating a vector for an unseen document is a fast operation of averaging its word embeddings. The paper presents strong empirical results across sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC often matches or exceeds the performance of state-of-the-art methods while being significantly faster to train and use.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant and Efficient Method:** The paper proposes a simple, well-motivated, and highly efficient method that directly addresses the primary drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors—namely, its poor scalability with corpus size and slow inference time for new documents. Doc2VecC achieves this while retaining the core intuition of combining local and global contexts.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The work is supported by a compelling theoretical analysis that elegantly links the corruption process to a data-dependent regularizer, providing clear insight into why the method is effective. This theoretical justification is backed by comprehensive and convincing empirical results across a diverse set of benchmarks, where Doc2VecC is shown to be highly competitive with, and often superior to, strong baselines.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty and Insight:** The primary contribution is not just the model architecture, but the insight that a good document representation (a simple average of word vectors) can be enforced during the word embedding learning process itself. The connection drawn between the dropout-style corruption and a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3.1) is a significant highlight. This analysis moves beyond heuristic motivation and provides a principled reason for the design choice, explaining its ability to suppress common, uninformative words (as shown in Table 3).
*   **Efficiency and Scalability:** The practical benefits of Doc2VecC are substantial. By avoiding per-document parameters, the model's size is decoupled from the training corpus size, making it applicable to massive datasets. Furthermore, inference is reduced to a simple, non-iterative averaging operation, which is orders of magnitude faster than the inference procedure required for Paragraph Vectors, as convincingly demonstrated in Table 2.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough empirical evaluation against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders. The experiments cover a wide range of tasks: long-document classification (IMDB, Wikipedia), short-sentence semantic similarity (SICK), and an intrinsic evaluation of the learned word vectors (Word Analogy). The consistent strong performance across these varied tasks robustly supports the paper's claims about the quality and versatility of the learned representations.
*   **High-Quality Word Embeddings:** A surprising and impressive result is the performance on the word analogy task (Section 4.3). The fact that Doc2VecC produces word embeddings that are significantly better than those from the standard Word2Vec model suggests that the global document context signal improves not just the document vectors, but the word vectors themselves. This is a valuable secondary contribution.

**Weaknesses:**

While the paper is strong, there are minor areas that could be improved. The choice of the corruption rate `q=0.9` seems high and is justified only by a brief statement that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate". A more detailed analysis or ablation study would strengthen this claim and provide better guidance for practitioners. Additionally, the performance of the Paragraph Vectors baseline on the Wikipedia dataset appears unusually low, which raises questions about the experimental setup for that specific comparison. However, these are minor points that do not detract from the paper's core contributions.

#### 4. Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Analysis of the Corruption Rate `q`:** The corruption rate is a key hyperparameter. Including a small ablation study (e.g., a plot of performance vs. `q` on the IMDB validation set) would be very valuable. A rate of `q=0.9` implies that the global context is formed from just 10% of the document's words, which is an interesting finding worth exploring further.
*   **Performance vs. Document Length:** Your hypothesis about the relative strengths of Doc2VecC on long documents and RNN-based models (like Skip-thought) on short sentences is insightful. This could be substantiated with a brief analysis. For instance, on the IMDB dataset, you could plot classification accuracy for Doc2VecC and Skip-thought Vectors on documents binned by length. This would provide direct evidence for your hypothesis.
*   **Clarity on Fine-tuning:** In Section 4.5, you mention fine-tuning on the SICK dataset. It would be helpful to explicitly state that this fine-tuning uses the same unsupervised Doc2VecC objective, just applied to the SICK corpus, to avoid any potential confusion that supervised information from the task labels was used.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate clarification on the following points during the author response period:

1.  The performance of Paragraph Vectors on the document classification task (Table 5) is surprisingly poor, underperforming even BoW in some cases. This is inconsistent with results on the IMDB task and in the original Paragraph Vectors paper. Could you comment on potential reasons for this? Were hyperparameters for baselines extensively tuned for this specific dataset?
2.  Your hypothesis for the poor word analogy performance of Paragraph Vectors is that it learns to rely on the unique document vectors rather than the word embeddings. This is a very interesting observation. Does this suggest that the learned word embeddings `U` in the Paragraph Vector model are themselves of low quality, and could this be why the PV-DBOW variant (which discards these context word embeddings) often performs as well as the full PV-DM model?
