### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel method for learning distributed representations of documents. The approach is an extension of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture from Word2Vec. It represents a document's global context as the simple average of the embeddings of words within that document. The key innovation is a corruption process, where a random subset of words is dropped from the document before its vector representation is computed and used to predict a target word. The authors provide a theoretical argument, based on a Taylor expansion of the loss function, that this corruption mechanism acts as an implicit data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer is claimed to penalize the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words. The paper presents empirical results across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, arguing that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms established baselines like Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec) and Skip-thought Vectors, while being significantly more efficient, particularly at inference time.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Incremental Novelty:** The proposed method is a relatively minor modification of the well-established Paragraph Vector (PV-DM) model. The core idea of combining local word context with a global document context is identical. The only change is how the document vector is formed: instead of a unique learned parameter, it is an average of its constituent (and corrupted) word embeddings. While this has efficiency benefits, the conceptual leap is limited.
2.  **Insufficient Justification for the Core Contribution:** The central claim is that the corruption mechanism provides a crucial form of regularization that explains the model's success. This claim is not sufficiently supported. The theoretical analysis is an approximation and does not fully capture the dynamics of stochastic training. Critically, the paper lacks a direct ablation study to isolate and quantify the effect of this corruption. Without comparing to a non-corrupted version of the same model, it is difficult to attribute the performance gains to this specific mechanism.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper presents a method that is simple and efficient, which are laudable goals. However, the work falls short of the rigorous validation required to establish it as a significant advance over existing methods.

First, the novelty of the approach is limited. The architecture is nearly identical to that of Paragraph Vectors (PV-DM). The idea of representing documents by averaging word embeddings is also a long-standing baseline. The contribution therefore rests almost entirely on the introduction of a dropout-like corruption on the words that constitute the document vector. While this is a reasonable heuristic, its presentation as a principled regularization scheme requires stronger evidence than provided. The Taylor expansion analysis provides some intuition but is not a proof; it analyzes the *expected* loss, whereas training proceeds via SGD on single, stochastic samples.

Second, and more importantly, the empirical evidence does not adequately support the central claims. The primary new component is the corruption. To validate its importance, a direct ablation study is essential. The paper should compare Doc2VecC against an identical model trained *without* corruption (i.e., `q=0`). The current "Word2Vec + AVG" baseline is insufficient, as its word vectors are not trained with the same document-level objective. Without this crucial experiment, the performance gains cannot be confidently attributed to the proposed regularization effect.

Furthermore, the strength of the empirical results against established baselines is questionable.
*   On the IMDB sentiment analysis task (Table 1), the error rate of Doc2VecC (11.70%) is statistically indistinguishable from that of Paragraph Vectors (11.77%). This does not support the claim of outperforming the state-of-the-art.
*   The strongest result is on the Wikipedia classification task (Table 5), where Doc2VecC shows a clear margin over Paragraph Vectors. However, the reported performance for the Paragraph Vector baseline seems potentially weak, which raises concerns about whether the baseline was implemented and tuned to its full potential.
*   On the SICK semantic relatedness task, the paper acknowledges that Doc2VecC is outperformed by LSTM-based methods and Skip-thought vectors, pivoting the argument to efficiency. While efficiency is a valid contribution, it weakens the primary claim of producing superior representations.

Finally, the effect of the corruption rate `q` is not explored. The paper states that the model is "not sensitive" and fixes `q=0.9`—a very high rate of corruption—without providing any sensitivity analysis. This lack of analysis on a key hyperparameter of the novel component of the model is a significant weakness.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper but are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   The efficiency of the model, especially its fast inference time compared to Paragraph Vectors, is a significant practical advantage. This aspect could be emphasized more clearly as a primary contribution.
*   The name "Doc2VecC" is very similar to "Doc2Vec," which may cause confusion.
*   The analysis in Table 3, which shows that Doc2VecC learns smaller norms for frequent, uninformative words, is interesting and provides good qualitative support for the regularization claim. This is a strong point of the paper.

I would like the authors to address the following questions to clarify my understanding and strengthen their claims:

1.  Could you provide results from an ablation experiment where Doc2VecC is trained without the corruption step (i.e., setting the corruption probability `q=0`)? This would provide a direct measure of the utility of the proposed corruption mechanism.
2.  The choice of `q=0.9` seems extreme, implying that the global context is derived from only 10% of a document's words on average. Could you provide a sensitivity analysis showing how performance on a key task varies with different values of `q`?
3.  Could you provide more details regarding the implementation and hyperparameter tuning of the Paragraph Vector baseline, particularly for the Wikipedia task where its performance seems unexpectedly low? This would help ensure the comparison is on equal footing.
