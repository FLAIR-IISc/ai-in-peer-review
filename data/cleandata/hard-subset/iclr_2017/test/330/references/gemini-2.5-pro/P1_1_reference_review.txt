### Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. Critically, unlike prior work that uses averaging as a post-processing step, Doc2VecC enforces this compositional structure during training. The model architecture resembles Word2Vec's CBoW model but incorporates a "global context" vector, formed by averaging the embeddings of a randomly sampled subset of words from the document. This "corruption" step (randomly dropping words) is shown to be key to the model's success. The authors provide a strong theoretical justification, demonstrating through a Taylor expansion that this corruption mechanism is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC is not only significantly faster to train and use for inference than methods like Paragraph Vectors, but also achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant, Efficient, and Effective Method:** The proposed method is simple, well-motivated, and directly addresses major practical limitations of its closest predecessor, Paragraph Vectors, namely scalability with corpus size and slow inference for new documents. The empirical results convincingly show that Doc2VecC achieves these efficiency gains while matching or exceeding the performance of state-of-the-art baselines on multiple benchmarks.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Justification:** The paper provides a principled explanation for its core mechanism—corruption. By showing its equivalence to a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3.1), the authors move beyond a simple heuristic and offer a clear insight into *why* the method works. This analysis explains the model's ability to naturally down-weight uninformative words, a crucial aspect for creating good document representations from simple averages.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's primary strength lies in its successful combination of simplicity, efficiency, and performance, all backed by a solid theoretical argument.

**Efficiency and Scalability:** The paper clearly identifies the weaknesses of Paragraph Vectors (PV), where parameters scale with the number of documents and inference is an expensive optimization process. Doc2VecC's design cleverly circumvents these issues. Model complexity depends only on vocabulary size, and generating a vector for an unseen document is a trivial and fast averaging operation. The experimental results in Table 2 provide striking evidence: inference for 25,000 test documents takes 7 seconds for Doc2VecC versus over 4 minutes for PV. This is a significant practical advantage.

**Performance:** Despite its simplicity, Doc2VecC performs exceptionally well. It achieves top results on the IMDB sentiment analysis task (Table 1) and the Wikipedia document classification task (Table 5), outperforming PV and other strong baselines. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide a compelling qualitative illustration of the superiority of Doc2VecC's learned representations over PV's on the Wikipedia task. The strong performance on the word analogy task is also notable, suggesting that by forcing the compositionality into the word vectors themselves (rather than offloading it to document-specific vectors as PV does), Doc2VecC learns higher-quality word embeddings.

**Theoretical Grounding:** The analysis in Section 3.1 is a standout feature of this work. It connects the seemingly ad-hoc choice of corrupting the input to the well-understood concept of regularization. The derivation shows how this "dropout" on words in the global context leads to a regularizer that penalizes vectors of common words, unless those words are critical for making confident predictions. This provides a satisfying explanation for the qualitative results in Table 3, where Doc2VecC learns small-norm embeddings for frequent stop words, while PV and Word2Vec zero out rare (but potentially useful) words instead. This analysis elevates the paper from a purely empirical contribution to one that provides deeper understanding.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is very well-written and the experiments are thorough. The following points are suggestions for minor improvements and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Title:** The title in the provided PDF is incomplete ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). This should be corrected.
*   **Clarity on `x`:** In Section 3, the notation `x` is defined as a BoW vector where `xj = 1` if word `j` appears. However, the subsequent analysis (e.g., in Section 3.1, `xij^2` term) seems to treat `x` as a standard BoW count vector. Clarifying this notation would be helpful.
*   **Baseline Details:** The description for the "RNNLM (supervised)" baseline in the sentiment analysis experiment is brief. It would be useful to clarify if this model was trained end-to-end on the classification task or if features were extracted from a language model trained on the labeled data.
*   **Quantifying "Small Improvement":** In Section 4.5, the authors mention that fine-tuning on the SICK dataset "brings small improvement". It would be more informative to quantify this improvement (e.g., report Pearson's r with and without fine-tuning) to help the reader understand its impact.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate `q`" and uses `q=0.9` throughout. Could you provide results for a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) on one of the main tasks, such as IMDB sentiment analysis? This would strengthen the claim of robustness and provide valuable guidance for practitioners.
2.  **Accuracy of Taylor Approximation:** The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is based on a second-order Taylor approximation. How well do you believe this approximation holds in practice for this objective function? For instance, does the observed impact of changing `q` align with what the derived regularizer term `q/(1-q)` would predict?
3.  **Normalization in Global Context:** The global context term is formulated as `(1/T) * U*x̃`. Is the `1/T` scaling factor applied during training important for learning stability or performance? Have you experimented with simply using `U*x̃` and only applying the `1/T` averaging at test time to form the final document vector?
