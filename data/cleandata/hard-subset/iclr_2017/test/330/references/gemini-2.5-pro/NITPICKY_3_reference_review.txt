### Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary**

The paper proposes a novel and efficient method, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The model is trained in a similar fashion to Word2Vec's CBOW architecture, predicting a target word from its local context and a global document context. The key contribution is the construction of this global context: it is derived from an average of word embeddings from a *corrupted* version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The authors provide a theoretical analysis via a Taylor expansion, arguing that this corruption scheme is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings. This regularizer is claimed to suppress the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words while preserving those of rare, discriminative words. The paper presents empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, claiming that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, while being significantly more efficient to train and use for inference.

**2. Decision**

**Reject.**

While the paper presents an interesting idea with a plausible theoretical motivation and promising empirical results, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of flaws, ranging from critical methodological ambiguities to severe sloppiness in presentation. The lack of rigor and attention to detail undermines confidence in the work's correctness and reproducibility, making it unsuitable for publication at ICLR in its current form.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on two primary concerns: a lack of methodological clarity and an exceptionally poor standard of presentation.

**A. Methodological and Technical Flaws/Ambiguities:**

1.  **Negative Sampling Distribution:** In Equation (3), the paper states that negative samples `w'` are drawn from `Pv`, which "stands for a uniform distribution over the terms in the vocabulary." This is a significant and unjustified departure from the standard practice in Word2Vec and related models, which use a unigram distribution raised to the power of 3/4. This non-standard choice can have a substantial impact on the quality of the learned embeddings, yet the authors provide no rationale for this deviation. This detail is critical for reproducibility and for trusting the reported results.

2.  **Ambiguous Notation:** The notation is inconsistent and unconventional. The output projection matrix is denoted `V>`, which is non-standard; `V^T` would be conventional. In the sentiment analysis features for Section 4.5, the authors use `u1 · u2` to denote the "component-wise product," whereas `·` typically denotes a dot product. The Hadamard product symbol (`⊙`) would be appropriate here. These ambiguities create unnecessary confusion.

3.  **Unclear Experimental Details:** The experimental setup lacks crucial details. For the document classification task in Section 4.4, the data is described as "Body texts (the second paragraph) were extracted for each page". This is a highly arbitrary and unusual choice that is not justified. Why the second paragraph, specifically? This choice could significantly bias the results, and without a strong reason, it calls the generalizability of the findings into question.

4.  **Corruption Process:** The text states, "we describe the stochastic process we used to generate a global context at each update," and the loss in Equation (4) includes `x̃_t^i`. This notation implies that a new corrupted document vector is generated for *every single target word* `t` in the corpus. This would be a computationally intensive process and is a key architectural detail that needs to be stated and justified explicitly. Is this what was done, or was the corruption applied once per document per epoch?

**B. Severe Lack of Professionalism in Presentation:**

1.  **Formatting and Typos:** The paper is littered with errors that suggest a profound lack of care. The title itself appears to be truncated ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). The famous word analogy example is incorrect: `vec(“women”)` should be `vec(“woman”)`. There are numerous typos such as "well-know" (Sec 2), "DEA" for DAE (Sec 4.1), and "quantatively" (Sec 4.3). Footnotes are not formatted correctly and appear as inline text. Table numbering is incorrect (Section 4.3 refers to Table 5, which belongs to Section 4.4).

2.  **References:** The reference section is completely unusable. The formatting is inconsistent, contains extraneous characters (e.g., `\Q2003\E`), and appears to be an unedited export from a bibliography tool. This is not a minor issue; proper citation is a cornerstone of academic integrity. A reference section in this state is unacceptable for a top-tier conference submission and shows disrespect for both the cited authors and the reviewers.

3.  **Missing Figures/Tables:** The provided text references several tables (1, 2, 3, 4, 6) and figures (1, 3, 4) which are not included. While this may be a formatting issue with the review system, combined with the other errors, it adds to the overall impression of a rushed and incomplete manuscript. The claims made in the text are difficult to verify without this evidence.

While the core idea of using corruption as an implicit regularizer is compelling, the execution and presentation of this work are far below the standards expected for ICLR. The accumulation of these seemingly "minor" flaws creates a major barrier to trusting the paper's central claims.

**4. Additional Feedback**

Despite my recommendation, I believe the core contribution is valuable and could form the basis of a strong paper with substantial revision.

*   The analysis in Section 3.1 is the strongest part of the paper. This connection between a simple corruption process and a data-dependent regularizer is elegant. This should be the central focus of a revised manuscript.
*   The efficiency of the model is a very strong selling point over Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors. The empirical timing comparisons, if accurate, are an important result.
*   I strongly advise a complete and thorough overhaul of the manuscript. Every sentence should be proofread. All notations must be clarified and made consistent. The reference list needs to be rebuilt from scratch with a standard format (e.g., BibTeX).
*   All non-standard methodological choices (e.g., uniform negative sampling, using only the second paragraph of articles) must be explicitly justified, or replaced with standard procedures. If a non-standard choice leads to better performance, this should be presented as a finding itself, supported by an ablation study.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please confirm the distribution used for negative sampling? If it was indeed uniform, can you provide a justification for this choice over the standard smoothed unigram distribution? Did you experiment with the standard approach, and if so, how did it affect performance?
2.  Could you clarify the precise implementation of the corruption process? Is a new corrupted document `x̃` sampled for every training instance (i.e., for each target word `t`), or once per document in each pass (epoch)?
3.  What was the rationale for using only the second paragraph of Wikipedia articles for the document classification task? How sensitive are the results to this choice (e.g., compared to using the first paragraph or the full text)?
