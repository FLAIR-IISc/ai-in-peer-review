### Review of "Learning Document Representations through Corruption"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and effective method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to extend the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture of Word2Vec. In addition to predicting a target word from its local context, the model also uses a "global context" vector. This global context is formed by averaging the word embeddings of a randomly corrupted subset of words from the entire document.

The authors make several key claims:
1.  **Efficiency:** The model's complexity depends only on the vocabulary size, not the corpus size, making it much more scalable than methods like Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec). Inference for new documents is extremely fast, requiring only a simple averaging of word vectors.
2.  **Principled Regularization:** The paper provides a theoretical justification, using a second-order Taylor expansion, to show that the proposed corruption mechanism is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The method is evaluated on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. It achieves results that are competitive with or superior to strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, especially for longer documents.
4.  **Improved Word Embeddings:** The learned word embeddings themselves are shown to be of high quality, significantly outperforming standard Word2Vec embeddings on the word analogy task.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant and Effective Method:** The paper proposes a simple yet powerful modification to the standard CBoW framework. It elegantly solves the main scalability and inference-time issues of Paragraph Vectors while maintaining high performance. The idea of using a corrupted average as the global context is intuitive and well-motivated.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by a thorough set of experiments across multiple diverse tasks. The method not only achieves strong results on document-level tasks but also demonstrates a significant improvement in the quality of the underlying word embeddings, which is a compelling and somewhat surprising result. The inclusion of efficiency comparisons (Table 2) provides a strong practical argument for the method's utility.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary strength of this paper lies in its well-rounded contribution. It presents a method that is not only empirically successful but also computationally efficient and theoretically motivated.

**Motivation and Simplicity:** The method directly addresses the two main weaknesses of Paragraph Vectors: the linear growth of parameters with corpus size and the expensive inference step for unseen documents. By representing the document vector as a composition of existing word vectors, Doc2VecC avoids both problems. The inference step becomes a trivial averaging operation, making it orders of magnitude faster. This is a significant practical advantage.

**Theoretical Justification:** The analysis in Section 3.1 is a major strength. Rather than presenting the corruption as a mere heuristic, the authors show that it approximates a specific form of regularization. This regularization term insightfully explains *why* the method works: it forces the model to learn meaningful embeddings for informative words while suppressing the influence of common, uninformative words (e.g., stop words), a problem that simple averaging of word embeddings often suffers from. The qualitative analysis in Table 3 provides strong empirical evidence supporting this theoretical claim.

**Experimental Rigor:** The experimental setup is comprehensive.
*   **Baselines:** The authors compare against a strong and relevant set of baselines, from simple BoW to complex models like Skip-thought Vectors.
*   **Task Diversity:** The evaluation spans document classification (IMDB, Wikipedia) and sentence-level semantic relatedness (SICK), demonstrating the model's versatility.
*   **Word-Level Evaluation:** The word analogy task results are particularly impressive. The nearly 20% improvement over standard Word2Vec suggests that forcing word embeddings to form coherent document representations via averaging also improves their individual semantic quality. This is a powerful secondary contribution.
*   **Analysis:** The paper goes beyond reporting final numbers. The timing comparison in Table 2, the qualitative analysis of word vector norms in Table 3, and the t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide a much deeper understanding of the method's behavior and advantages. The discussion on why Skip-thought vectors underperform on the long documents of the IMDB dataset is thoughtful and adds credibility to the analysis.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to further improve its impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not change my decision to accept.

*   **Ablation Study on Corruption:** The paper claims that the corruption mechanism is "critical to the performance." This claim would be significantly strengthened by an ablation study. Specifically, what is the performance of the exact same model architecture trained with no corruption (i.e., corruption rate `q=0`)? This would directly isolate and quantify the benefit of the corruption, separating it from the benefit of simply including a global context.
*   **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and that `q=0.9` was used throughout. A small plot showing performance on a validation set as `q` varies (e.g., from 0.1 to 0.9) would make this claim more convincing and provide useful guidance for practitioners.
*   **Clarity on "Unbiased" Corruption:** The term "unbiased" in Section 3 refers to the fact that the *expected value* of the corrupted vector equals the original vector, E[x̃] = x. This is a standard technique when using dropout, but it might be worth a brief explanatory sentence, as it's crucial for the Taylor expansion analysis that follows.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you provide the results of an ablation study where the model is trained without corruption (`q=0`) on one of the key tasks, like the IMDB sentiment analysis? This would help disentangle the effect of adding the global context from the effect of corrupting it.
2.  In the analysis of the data-dependent regularization (Section 3.1), the regularization term penalizes the norm of the word vectors `u_j`. How does this implicit regularization interact with any explicit weight decay (e.g., L2 regularization) that might have been used during training? Was standard weight decay also applied?
3.  The performance on the word analogy task is substantially better than Word2Vec. This is a fantastic result. Do you have any further intuition as to why forcing embeddings to be compositionally effective for document representation leads to such a marked improvement on word-level syntactic and semantic regularities?
