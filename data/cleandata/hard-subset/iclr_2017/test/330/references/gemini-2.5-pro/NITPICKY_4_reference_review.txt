### Summary of Claims

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. During training, this document representation is constructed from a "corrupted" version of the document, where words are randomly dropped. The model, similar in architecture to Word2Vec's CBOW, learns to predict a target word from its local context (neighboring words) and this corrupted global context (the averaged document vector). The authors claim this corruption mechanism serves as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes frequent, uninformative words and promotes rare, discriminative ones. Key claimed contributions include: (1) a model whose complexity is independent of the training corpus size, unlike Paragraph Vectors; (2) high training and inference efficiency; (3) an implicit regularization scheme; and (4) state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Reject**

My recommendation is to reject this paper. While the core idea of using corruption as a data-dependent regularizer for learning compositional document vectors is interesting and potentially valuable, the manuscript is marred by a substantial number of critical flaws in its presentation, methodological description, and empirical validation. The sheer volume of errors, inconsistencies, and missing information prevents a rigorous assessment of the paper's claims and undermines its scientific credibility.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Critical Missing Information:** The paper is fundamentally incomplete. The text refers to Tables 1, 2, 3, 4, 6, and Figure 1, none of which are present in the provided manuscript. These missing elements contain the primary results for sentiment analysis (accuracy and timing), the qualitative analysis of the regularizer, word analogy results, and semantic relatedness scores. Without this data, it is impossible to verify the central performance claims of the paper.
2.  **Sloppy Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a severe lack of proofreading. The title itself is truncated ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). There are numerous grammatical errors, typos ("well celebrated," "on the other hands," "quantatively"), and awkward phrasings throughout the text. The reference section is completely broken, with inconsistent formatting, strange character sequences (e.g., `\Q2003\E`), and incorrect citation styles that make it difficult to identify the cited works. Such carelessness suggests a rushed submission and a lack of respect for the review process.

### Supporting Arguments

**Methodological Ambiguity and Lack of Rigor:**

*   **Notation:** The choice of notation is often unconventional and confusing. Using `V>` for the output projection matrix is non-standard and not explained. The definition of `x` as a BoW vector where `xj = 1 iff word j does appear` is ambiguous; it is unclear if this is a binary vector or a count vector, which has implications for the averaging in Eq (5).
*   **Corruption Model:** The description of the corruption process in Eq (1) and the accompanying text is slightly imprecise. While the scaling factor `1/(1-q)` is standard for unbiased dropout, the explanation could be clearer. More importantly, crucial hyperparameters like the context window size `k` and the number of negative samples are never mentioned, which is a major omission for reproducibility.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The derivation in Section 3.1, which frames corruption as regularization, is the theoretical core of the paper. However, it relies on a second-order Taylor approximation. The paper does not discuss the limitations or validity of this approximation, nor does it provide any empirical analysis to show that the model's behavior in practice actually aligns with the derived regularizer `R(uj)`. The claim that the model "intrinsically minimizes" the regularized loss is an overstatement based on an approximation.

**Flawed Empirical Evaluation:**

*   **Missing Results:** As stated above, this is the most critical flaw. Claims like "Doc2VecC achieved similar or even better performance than Paragraph Vectors" and "Doc2VecC significantly outperforms the word embeddings produced by Word2Vec" are completely unsubstantiated without the corresponding tables.
*   **Questionable Protocols:** In the sentiment analysis experiment (Section 4.2), one of the evaluation settings involves learning the representation "using all the available data, including the test set." This transductive setup can lead to overly optimistic performance estimates and is not a standard evaluation for generalization. While the authors also test an inductive setting, presenting the transductive results prominently is questionable.
*   **Unfair Comparisons:** The comparison to Skip-thought vectors on the IMDB dataset feels like a strawman argument. The authors themselves hypothesize that its poor performance is due to the long document length, a known limitation of sequential RNNs. A more insightful comparison would have been against other models designed for document-level tasks or a more thorough analysis on tasks with shorter texts where Skip-thought is known to perform well. The claim that Doc2VecC is "better at handling longer paragraphs" is not rigorously demonstrated through controlled experiments.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   **Complete the Paper:** The first and most obvious step is to include all missing tables and figures. Captions should be descriptive and self-contained.
*   **Thorough Proofreading:** The manuscript requires a complete and thorough proofreading pass to fix the title, typos, grammatical errors, and awkward phrasing.
*   **Fix the References:** The reference list must be regenerated using a standard bibliographic tool (e.g., BibTeX) and a consistent, accepted format (e.g., the ICLR style file).
*   **Clarify Method and Notation:** Define all variables and notation clearly and conventionally. Provide a dedicated subsection or table listing all hyperparameters used for the experiments (learning rate, embedding dimensions, corruption rate `q`, window size `k`, negative samples, etc.) to ensure reproducibility.
*   **Strengthen Evaluation:** The primary evaluation should be strictly inductive (test set held out completely). If transductive results are included, they must be clearly marked and justified. The analysis would be stronger if it included a sensitivity analysis on key hyperparameters like the corruption rate `q`.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please provide the missing Tables 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 and Figure 1? Without these, your central empirical claims cannot be evaluated.
2.  Can you confirm that the title "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" is an error and provide the intended title?
3.  In Section 4.2, when learning representations using the test set, were the test set labels used in any way? Could you further justify why this transductive setting is a meaningful benchmark for a general-purpose document representation method?
4.  What were the context window size `k` and the number of negative samples used in your experiments? These are critical details for Word2Vec-style models and are currently missing.
