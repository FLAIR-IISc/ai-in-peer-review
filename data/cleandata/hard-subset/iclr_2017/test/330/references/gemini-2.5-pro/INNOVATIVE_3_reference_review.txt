### Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and highly efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The novelty lies in the training procedure: instead of just averaging pre-trained word vectors, Doc2VecC learns word embeddings specifically *such that* their average forms a meaningful document representation. This is achieved by modifying the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture to predict a target word from both its local context and a global context vector. Crucially, this global context is formed by averaging the embeddings of a randomly corrupted (i.e., subsampled) version of the full document. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument, via a second-order Taylor expansion, that this corruption scheme is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings. This regularizer naturally penalizes the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that Doc2VecC is not only orders of magnitude faster to train and use for inference than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, but also achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. It presents a simple, elegant, and bold idea that challenges the prevailing trend of increasing model complexity for representation learning. My decision is based on two key factors:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The introduction of input corruption as a mechanism to enforce compositional semantics in word vectors is a powerful and original contribution. The theoretical link between this simple procedural trick and a data-dependent regularization scheme is a beautiful insight that provides a strong foundation for the method. This is precisely the kind of creative, high-impact idea that moves the field forward.
2.  **Impressive Practicality and Performance:** The proposed method, Doc2VecC, directly addresses major practical limitations (scalability, inference speed) of prior work like Paragraph Vectors. The fact that this much simpler and faster model not only competes with but often outperforms more complex state-of-the-art methods across a range of tasks is a testament to the strength of the core idea.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main strength lies in its novel approach. While using an average of word embeddings to represent a document is not new, previous attempts have often treated this as a post-processing step. Doc2VecC's key innovation is to integrate this averaging directly into the learning objective. The corruption mechanism is the brilliant stroke that makes this work. By forcing the model to reconstruct a target word from a partial, noisy representation of the document, the model learns word embeddings that are robust and encode semantics in a way that is amenable to simple linear composition.

The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is a major highlight. Deriving the implicit regularizer provides a satisfying explanation for *why* the corruption helps. It shows that the model is not just a heuristic but is instead optimizing a well-principled objective that learns to down-weight common, non-discriminative words. This is supported empirically by the analysis in Table 3, which clearly shows Doc2VecC successfully shrinking the norms of stop words, unlike Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The efficiency gains reported in Table 2 are dramatic and of immense practical importance. An inference time of 7 seconds versus over 4 minutes for Paragraph Vectors on the IMDB test set makes the proposed method far more suitable for real-world applications. Furthermore, the performance is consistently strong. Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors on document classification (Table 5) and sentiment analysis (Table 1), while also producing far superior word embeddings for analogy tasks. The fact that the word embeddings themselves are improved is a significant and perhaps unexpected benefit of the approach. While it underperforms Skip-thought vectors on the short-sentence SICK dataset, the authors provide a plausible explanation (order-invariance of averaging vs. sequence-modeling of RNNs), and its dominant performance on the longer-document IMDB task supports this view. Given its massive efficiency advantage, this is a very reasonable trade-off.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Impact of Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and uses `q=0.9` throughout. While I don't doubt this, a small ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.1, 0.5, 0.9, 0.99) would make this claim more robust and provide valuable insight for practitioners.
*   **Approximation Quality:** The Taylor expansion is a key piece of the theoretical motivation. A brief comment on the validity of this second-order approximation would be welcome. Are there reasons to believe higher-order terms are negligible, or does the empirical success simply suggest it's a useful-enough approximation?
*   **Order Invariance:** The paper could benefit from a slightly more explicit discussion of the trade-offs of using an order-invariant composition function (averaging). This seems to be the key factor explaining the performance difference between Doc2VecC and RNN-based models on short vs. long texts. Framing this as an architectural choice rather than a pure weakness would strengthen the paper's positioning.

I would also like to ask the authors the following questions to clarify my understanding:

1.  Your hypothesis for the poor performance of Paragraph Vectors on the word analogy task is that the model leans too heavily on the unique document vectors. This implies a potential design flaw in the original Paragraph Vector model. Does your work suggest that simultaneously learning high-quality word *and* document vectors is best achieved by architecturally coupling them, as you have done, rather than treating them as separate parameter groups?
2.  The derived regularizer in Eq. 7 depends on the model's own predictions (the `σ` terms). This is fascinating, as it suggests the regularization is adaptive. Could you elaborate on the intuition here? Does this mean that as the model becomes more confident about certain predictions, it effectively "turns off" the regularization for the words involved?
3.  Have you considered alternative corruption strategies? For example, instead of uniform random dropout, one could drop words based on their IDF scores. Would your theoretical framework provide any insight into what kind of implicit regularizer such a scheme might produce?
