### 1. Summary

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. To ensure that the learned word embeddings compose meaningfully, the model is trained in a CBoW-like framework to predict a target word from both its local context (neighboring words) and a global context (the document vector). The key innovation is that this global context vector is formed by averaging embeddings from a "corrupted" version of the document, where a random subset of words has been dropped. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words, encouraging the model to learn more meaningful representations. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance while being significantly more efficient to train and use for inference than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Effective and Efficient Method:** The paper proposes a method that is simple, elegant, and highly efficient. It directly addresses significant practical limitations of prior work like Paragraph Vectors, namely the model size dependency on the corpus and the slow inference time for new documents.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The central idea of using corruption is well-motivated and supported by both a theoretical analysis connecting it to data-dependent regularization and a thorough set of experiments. The empirical results are consistently strong across multiple diverse benchmarks, validating the effectiveness of the approach.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation, technical novelty, and rigorous evaluation.

**Motivation and Novelty:** The approach is very well-motivated. While averaging word embeddings is a simple baseline, it often underperforms. This paper's central claim is that one can explicitly train word embeddings *to be averaged*. The mechanism for achieving this—corrupting the document context—is a clever synthesis of ideas from denoising autoencoders and Word2Vec. The theoretical derivation in Section 3.1 is a significant contribution, providing a compelling explanation for *why* corruption helps. By showing its equivalence to a regularizer that penalizes common, non-discriminative words, the authors provide valuable insight into the learning dynamics. This elevates the method from a simple heuristic to a principled approach.

**Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental setup is comprehensive and convincing.
*   **Breadth of Tasks:** The method is tested on a diverse set of tasks: sentiment analysis (IMDB), multi-class classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK), in addition to an intrinsic word-level evaluation (word analogy). This demonstrates the general applicability of the learned representations.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The paper compares against a strong and relevant set of baselines, including BoW, Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), Word2Vec+averaging, and the powerful Skip-thought vectors.
*   **Impressive Results:** Doc2VecC consistently performs at or near the top across tasks. It notably outperforms Paragraph Vectors on the Wikipedia classification task and Skip-thought vectors on the long-document IMDB task. Its performance on the word analogy task suggests it learns higher-quality word vectors than both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors.
*   **Efficiency Analysis:** A key claim of the paper is efficiency, and this is well-supported by the timing experiments in Table 2. The massive speedup in inference time compared to Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors is a crucial practical advantage.

**Clarity:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained clearly with a helpful diagram, and the connection to prior work is well-articulated.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Clarification of the Corruption Process:** The corruption is defined in Eq. (1) as acting on the BoW vector `x`. This suggests corruption happens at the level of word *types* in the vocabulary for a given document. However, the text also describes it as "randomly remove words from a document," which could imply sampling word *tokens*. A brief clarification of the exact sampling process would be beneficial. For example, if the word "movie" appears five times, are all five instances dropped together, or is each token dropped independently?
*   **On the Taylor Approximation:** The theoretical analysis hinges on a second-order Taylor expansion. This approximation is most accurate when the corrupted input `x̃` is close to its mean `x`. However, the paper reports using a high corruption rate of `q = 0.9`. It would strengthen the paper to briefly discuss the validity of this approximation under such a high corruption rate, or to acknowledge it as a potential limitation of the analysis.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q`, which is a desirable property. This claim could be made more concrete by including a small plot in the appendix showing performance on a validation set as `q` is varied (e.g., from 0.1 to 0.9).
*   **Connection to Dropout:** The corruption mechanism, particularly the `1/(1-q)` rescaling, is formally identical to dropout applied to the input BoW vector for the global context. It would be valuable to explicitly state this connection to help readers place the work within the broader context of regularization techniques.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Could you confirm the precise implementation of the corruption mechanism? Is the random mask applied to the vocabulary (word types) for a given document, or are individual word tokens within the document dropped with probability `q` before their embeddings are averaged?
*   In the SICK dataset experiments, you mention fine-tuning the model on the SICK training corpus after pre-training on the book corpus. Could you quantify the performance gain from this fine-tuning step? This would help understand how adaptable the pre-trained representations are.
*   The results on the word analogy task (referenced as Table 4, but not provided in the excerpt) are very interesting, particularly the claim that Doc2VecC word vectors outperform Word2Vec's. Do you have a hypothesis for why forcing the embeddings to be compositionally meaningful for documents also improves their ability to capture these finer-grained linear relationships?
