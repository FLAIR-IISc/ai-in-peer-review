### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient model for learning distributed vector representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The model is trained, in a manner similar to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, to predict a target word from its local context (neighboring words) and a global context (the document vector). The key contribution is the introduction of a corruption mechanism where the global context vector is constructed from a randomly sampled subset of the document's words during each training step.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the training objective. This regularizer is shown to penalize the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words, thereby encouraging the model to learn more meaningful word representations. The resulting model has several desirable properties: its complexity is independent of the training corpus size, it is very fast to train, and inference for unseen documents is trivial (simple averaging), overcoming major limitations of prior work like Paragraph Vectors. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds the performance of several state-of-the-art baselines.

### 2. Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper for the following key reasons:

1.  **Effective and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful method that directly addresses significant scalability and efficiency drawbacks of the influential Paragraph Vector model. The approach is elegantly motivated by the compositional properties of word vectors and the principles of denoising autoencoders.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The central claims are substantiated by both a sound theoretical analysis, which links the corruption mechanism to a data-dependent regularizer, and a comprehensive set of experiments across diverse tasks. The empirical results consistently demonstrate the superiority or competitiveness of Doc2VecC against strong baselines.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and technically sound. The proposed method, Doc2VecC, makes a solid contribution to the field of document representation learning.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The main technical novelty lies in the specific application of a corruption process to the formation of the global document context within a predictive training framework. While averaging word vectors and using corruption (e.g., in Denoising Autoencoders) are not new concepts in themselves, their combination in this framework is novel and highly effective. The theoretical justification provided in Section 3.1 is a major strength of the paper. By using a Taylor expansion, the authors demonstrate that the stochastic corruption is not just an ad-hoc trick but approximates a principled regularizer. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the norms of frequent words unless their embeddings are critical for making confident predictions, which is empirically validated in the analysis of word embedding norms (Section 4.2, "Data dependent regularization").

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
*   **Efficiency:** The paper clearly demonstrates the practical advantages of Doc2VecC. Table 2 shows that it is orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors at inference time and significantly faster to train than Paragraph Vectors, making it a much more practical solution for large-scale applications.
*   **Performance:** The method is evaluated on four distinct tasks, showcasing its versatility. It achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and word analogy tasks. The performance on the word analogy task is particularly impressive, suggesting that the data-dependent regularization helps learn higher-quality word embeddings compared to both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors.
*   **Honest Analysis:** The authors provide a balanced discussion of the results on the semantic relatedness (SICK) task. They acknowledge that LSTM-based methods perform better on these short sentence pairs and offer a plausible hypothesis related to sequence length, which strengthens the credibility of their analysis.

In summary, the paper successfully identifies critical limitations in a widely-used baseline (Paragraph Vectors), proposes a simple and well-justified solution, and provides compelling evidence of its effectiveness and efficiency.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Clarity of Figures/Tables:** There appear to be some inconsistencies in the numbering of tables. For instance, the text in Section 4.2 refers to "Table 3" for the word norm analysis, but the classification results in Section 4.4 are in a table labeled "Table 5". Similarly, there are two separate tables labeled "Table 5". Please review and correct the numbering of all figures and tables for consistency.
*   **Ablation on Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and that `q=0.9` was used throughout. While this is a practical choice, the claim would be strengthened by a small ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9) on a validation set. This would provide readers with a better understanding of this key hyperparameter.
*   **Clarification of Baselines:** In Section 4.1, the Denoising Autoencoder (DAE) baseline is described as using negative sampling. This is an unusual choice for a reconstructive model. A brief justification or a citation for this approach would be helpful for clarity.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

Your answers to these questions would help clarify some details of the work.

1.  The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 relies on a second-order Taylor expansion to approximate the training objective. How tight do you believe this approximation is in practice? Does the empirically observed regularizing effect (e.g., on the L2 norms of frequent vs. rare word embeddings) closely match the behavior predicted by the derived regularizer `R(uj)`?
2.  For the semantic relatedness task on the SICK dataset, you mention fine-tuning the model on the target dataset without using the relatedness scores. Could you please clarify the exact training objective used for this fine-tuning step? Was the standard Doc2VecC loss function applied to the sentences in the SICK corpus?
3.  The corruption model in Equation 1 is an unbiased dropout mechanism. Did you experiment with any alternative corruption strategies, such as replacing words with random words from the vocabulary or applying a non-uniform dropout probability (e.g., one that is inversely proportional to word frequency)?
