### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and highly efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core contribution is an elegant model that learns word embeddings specifically so that a simple, unweighted average of them forms a high-quality document vector. This is achieved by extending the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture to predict a target word from both its local context and a global context. The key innovation is how this global context is formed: it is the average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted version of the full document, where a significant fraction of words are dropped out.

The authors provide a strong theoretical motivation for this corruption scheme, showing via a Taylor expansion that it is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the training objective. This regularizer intuitively penalizes the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, while preserving the magnitude of embeddings for words that are critical for making confident predictions. The paper supports its claims with a comprehensive set of experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks. The results show that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds the performance of state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors on several benchmarks, while being orders of magnitude more efficient for both training and, critically, for generating representations of unseen documents at test time.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant and Effective Simplicity:** The proposed method addresses significant drawbacks of prior work (e.g., the scalability and slow inference of Paragraph Vectors) with a simple and well-motivated modification to the Word2Vec framework. The ability to generate high-quality document vectors by simply averaging word embeddings is a highly desirable property, and this work provides a principled way to achieve it.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The paper does not just present a new architecture but also provides a compelling theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) that explains *why* the corruption mechanism is effective. This is backed by thorough empirical validation across multiple diverse tasks and against a strong suite of baselines, which robustly demonstrates the method's effectiveness and efficiency.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

First, the method's **efficiency** is a major practical contribution. By design, the model's complexity depends only on the vocabulary size, not the corpus size, unlike Paragraph Vectors. The inference process—a simple averaging of word vectors—is extremely fast, as shown in Table 2 (7 seconds vs. >4 minutes for Paragraph Vectors on the IMDB test set). This makes Doc2VecC a much more practical choice for real-world applications involving large corpora or low-latency requirements.

Second, the **theoretical justification** for the corruption process is a standout feature. The derivation in Section 3.1 connects the dropout-like mechanism to a concrete data-dependent regularizer. The analysis that this regularizer penalizes frequent, non-discriminative words provides a clear, principled explanation for the model's strong performance and its reduced reliance on heuristics like frequent word subsampling. The qualitative results in Table 3, which show Doc2VecC shrinking the norms of stop words while Paragraph Vectors shrinks the norms of rare words, provide excellent empirical evidence for this theoretical claim.

Third, the **empirical evaluation** is comprehensive and convincing. The authors compare Doc2VecC against a wide range of relevant baselines, including Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders, across four different tasks. Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on document-level tasks (IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia classification) where its design is most relevant. The authors also provide a nuanced discussion of the results, correctly identifying that sequence-based models like LSTMs may have an edge on shorter, structured sentence-pair tasks (SICK dataset), while their model excels on longer, bag-of-words-style documents. The t-SNE visualizations further provide a clear qualitative picture of the superior separability of the learned representations.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The paper is excellent, but the following points could help further improve its clarity and impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

1.  **Clarity on Implementation:** The description of how the global context vector is computed could be slightly more precise. Specifically, in Equation (2), is the document length `T` the length of the original document or the number of words remaining after corruption? A clarification on the exact computation of `1/T * Ux̃` in practice (e.g., is it `(1/T_original) * (1/(1-q)) * sum_{w in corrupted_doc} u_w`?) would be helpful for reproducibility.

2.  **Relationship to Marginalized DAEs:** The theoretical analysis is strongly reminiscent of the work on Marginalized Denoising Autoencoders (mDAE), which is cited. A brief discussion explicitly stating the connection or differentiation would be valuable. For instance, could Doc2VecC be viewed as applying the principles of mDAE to a CBoW prediction objective rather than a reconstruction objective? This would help place the theoretical contribution more firmly in context.

3.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that `q=0.9` is used and that the model is not sensitive to this choice. While this is plausible, adding a small ablation study (e.g., a plot of classification error vs. `q` on a validation set) would more robustly substantiate this claim and provide readers with practical guidance.

4.  **Ablation on Subsampling:** The analysis suggests the data-dependent regularization serves a similar purpose to the frequent word subsampling heuristic in Word2Vec. An interesting experiment would be to train Doc2VecC *without* this heuristic subsampling step. If the performance remains high, it would provide even stronger evidence that the corruption mechanism is a more principled replacement for this heuristic.

**Questions for Authors:**
*   In the SICK dataset experiments, you mention fine-tuning the model for "sentence representation learning only" on the SICK corpus. Could you please clarify what the training objective was during this fine-tuning phase? Was it the same masked word prediction task as the initial training?
*   In the word analogy task, Paragraph Vectors performs very poorly. The hypothesis is that the model relies heavily on the per-document vectors. Did you evaluate the PV-DBOW variant, and if so, did it show a similar degradation in word vector quality? This could further support your hypothesis.
