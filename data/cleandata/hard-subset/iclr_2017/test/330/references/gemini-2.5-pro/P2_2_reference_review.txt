### **ICLR Review**

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed vector representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document's global context as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. Critically, during training, this global context vector is formed from a "corrupted" version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The model is trained in a Word2Vec CBoW-style framework to predict a target word from its local context (neighboring words) and this corrupted global context.

The paper makes several key claims:
1.  The model is highly efficient, as its complexity depends on vocabulary size, not corpus size, and it avoids the expensive inference step required by Paragraph Vectors for unseen documents.
2.  The corruption mechanism acts as an implicit, data-dependent regularizer. The authors provide a theoretical justification via a Taylor expansion, showing that this regularization penalizes the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words.
3.  The resulting document representations, formed by a simple average of the learned word embeddings at test time, achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.
4.  The process also yields higher-quality word embeddings compared to the standard Word2Vec model, as demonstrated on the word analogy task.

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective method for document representation learning. The primary reasons for this positive assessment are:
1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Formulation:** The paper clearly identifies and convincingly addresses major practical limitations of a highly influential prior method, Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec)—namely, the parameter scaling with corpus size and the slow inference for new documents. The proposed solution is intuitive and builds cleverly upon the well-understood Word2Vec framework.
2.  **Strong Empirical and Theoretical Support:** The claims are backed by a compelling combination of theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The derivation of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer provides a solid theoretical underpinning for the method's success. This is supported by strong quantitative results across multiple benchmarks and insightful qualitative analyses.

**Supporting Arguments**

The proposed Doc2VecC model is a valuable contribution to the field of representation learning. Its main strengths are its simplicity, efficiency, and performance.

*   **Efficiency and Scalability:** By representing the document vector as a composition of existing word vectors rather than a unique learned parameter per document, the model decouples its size from the training corpus size. This is a significant practical advantage over Paragraph Vectors, especially for web-scale datasets. Furthermore, the inference at test time is extremely fast—a simple averaging of word vectors—in stark contrast to the iterative optimization process required by Paragraph Vectors. The timing results in Table 2 provide clear and compelling evidence for this practical advantage.

*   **The Role of Corruption:** The central innovation is the use of corruption. The authors do an excellent job not only of proposing this mechanism but also of explaining *why* it works. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as an implicit regularizer that penalizes common, non-discriminative words, is a key strength of the paper. This is not just a heuristic trick; it is a well-justified technique. This claim is further substantiated by the qualitative results in Table 3, which clearly show Doc2VecC learning smaller-norm embeddings for stop words, unlike Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors which penalize rare (but potentially informative) words.

*   **Comprehensive Experimental Validation:** The authors evaluate their model across a diverse set of tasks and datasets, comparing it against a strong and relevant set of baselines.
    *   On the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks, Doc2VecC consistently matches or outperforms all baselines, including Paragraph Vectors. The performance gains over simple averaging (Word2Vec+AVG/IDF) demonstrate that explicitly training for compositionality is effective. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide a clear visual confirmation of the superior separability of the learned document clusters.
    *   The results on the word analogy task are particularly surprising and impressive. The fact that adding the corrupted global context objective during training leads to *better* word embeddings than Word2Vec is a significant finding. It suggests that learning to compose document meaning simultaneously improves the semantic quality of the component word vectors.
    *   The authors are commendably transparent about the model's limitations. On the SICK dataset, Doc2VecC is outperformed by LSTM-based models. Their hypothesis—that sequence models like LSTMs excel at shorter, structured sentences while their averaging-based approach is more robust for longer documents—is plausible and shows a mature understanding of their method's place in the broader literature.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a very good paper, the following points could further strengthen it:

1.  **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper states that `q=0.9` was used throughout the experiments and that the model was not sensitive to this parameter. While this simplifies reproducibility, a small ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) on a validation set would add significant value and bolster this claim.
2.  **Interaction with Subsampling:** The paper notes that standard frequent word subsampling is critical for Word2Vec+AVG but less so for Doc2VecC due to its intrinsic regularization. An explicit ablation study comparing Doc2VecC with and without this subsampling trick would be a powerful demonstration of the regularization effect and would make the paper's claims even more concrete.
3.  **Elaboration on Unbiased Corruption:** The term "unbiased" is used in relation to the corruption process (Eq. 1). It would be helpful to explicitly state that this means the expected value of the corrupted vector is the original vector, i.e., E[x̃ | x] = x, which is a key assumption for the Taylor expansion that follows. This is a minor clarification but would improve readability.

**Questions for Authors**

1.  In the formulation of the global context term (Eq. 2), the sum of word embeddings is scaled by `1/T`. This is a standard averaging operation. Did you experiment with other pooling functions, such as unscaled summation or max-pooling, for creating the global context vector? If so, how did they perform?
2.  For the SICK dataset experiments, you mention fine-tuning the model on the target dataset. Could you quantify the performance gain from this fine-tuning step? This would help clarify its importance for domain adaptation.
3.  The superior performance on the word analogy task is a very interesting result. Do you have any intuition as to why forcing word embeddings to average into a meaningful document context helps them better capture fine-grained syntactic and semantic regularities like `king - man + woman ≈ queen`?
