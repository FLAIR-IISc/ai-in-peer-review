### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and highly efficient method for learning unsupervised document representations. The core idea is to modify the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture by representing the document's global context not as a unique learned vector (as in Paragraph Vectors), but as a simple average of word embeddings from the document. Critically, during training, this global context is formed from a *corrupted* version of the document, where a random subset of words is dropped out. The authors provide an elegant theoretical argument, via a Taylor expansion, that this corruption scheme is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings. This regularizer implicitly penalizes frequent, uninformative words, forcing the model to learn more meaningful word vectors such that their simple average can effectively represent the entire document. The resulting model is computationally efficient, scalable, and produces document vectors that achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that I am happy to champion. It presents a simple, elegant, and bold idea that challenges more complex and computationally expensive approaches to document representation. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The primary contribution—using random corruption of the document's words to implicitly learn a regularizer—is a significant and novel concept. Instead of adding complexity (e.g., per-document parameters or recurrent architectures), the authors achieve superior results by introducing a principled noise process. The theoretical justification linking this corruption to a data-dependent regularizer that down-weights uninformative words is a powerful insight that elevates the work beyond a mere empirical trick.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results Paired with Major Efficiency Gains:** The proposed method is not just a theoretical curiosity. It demonstrates impressive performance, outperforming strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors and even the much more complex Skip-thought Vectors on tasks involving longer documents. The fact that this performance is achieved with a model that is orders of magnitude faster to train and use for inference is a major practical contribution. This work successfully trades unnecessary model complexity for a clever training strategy, with compelling results.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its "less is more" philosophy. While Paragraph Vectors introduced dedicated parameters for each document—a solution that is powerful but scales poorly and complicates inference—this work shows that such parameters are unnecessary. By forcing the model to predict words from a corrupted, partial view of the document, Doc2VecC learns word embeddings that are inherently more compositional. The simple average of these embeddings at test time is not a post-hoc heuristic (like Word2Vec+AVG); it is the very objective the model was trained to succeed at.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a standout feature. It provides a satisfying explanation for *why* the corruption helps. The derived regularizer penalizes words that are frequent but not critical for making confident predictions, which is precisely the behavior one desires. The empirical validation in Table 3, showing that Doc2VecC assigns low-magnitude vectors to stop words while preserving indicative rare words, provides strong support for this theoretical claim.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness across a diverse set of tasks:
*   On the IMDB sentiment analysis task, Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors and trounces Skip-thought Vectors, highlighting its strength on long-document classification.
*   On the Wikipedia classification task, the performance gap is significant, and the t-SNE visualization in Figure 3 clearly illustrates the superior class separation achieved by Doc2VecC's representations.
*   Perhaps most surprisingly, the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are substantially better on the word analogy task than those from Word2Vec. This suggests the corruption-based training objective is a better regularizer for learning word semantics than the standard Word2Vec objective, which is a significant finding in its own right.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision.

*   **Further Exploration of Corruption:** The analysis assumes a simple uniform dropout. It would be fascinating to know if the authors experimented with other corruption schemes. For instance, would a process that preferentially drops high-frequency words (a form of inverse document frequency sampling) yield further improvements or simplify the learned regularization?
*   **Approximation Quality:** The theoretical argument relies on a second-order Taylor expansion. While this provides a beautiful intuition, it is still an approximation. It might strengthen the paper to briefly discuss the conditions under which this is a tight approximation or to provide some empirical validation of its effects.
*   **Comparison to Marginalized Denoising Autoencoders (mDAE):** The theoretical analysis is very reminiscent of the analysis for mDAE. The paper cites the mDAE work but could benefit from a more direct comparison in the text, perhaps framing Doc2VecC as a predictive variant of mDAE applied to text, which could help position the theoretical contribution more clearly.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:

1.  The improved performance on the word analogy task is a fantastic result. Do you have an intuition for why forcing compositionality from a corrupted bag of words would lead to word vectors that better capture linear syntactic/semantic relationships, compared to the local context window used by Word2Vec?
2.  In your analysis, the regularization term `R(uj)` depends on `x_ij`, which is 1 if word `j` is in document `i`. How does this interact with the common practice of subsampling frequent words during training? The paper notes that Doc2VecC does not rely on this trick, but were the experiments in Table 5 run with or without frequent word subsampling for Doc2VecC? Clarifying this would be helpful.
