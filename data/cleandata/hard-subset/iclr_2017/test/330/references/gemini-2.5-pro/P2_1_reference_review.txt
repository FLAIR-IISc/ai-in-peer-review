### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The model builds upon the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture of Word2Vec. Its key innovation is to represent the global document context not as a unique learned vector (as in Paragraph Vectors), but as the average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted version of the document itself. The authors claim this approach has several advantages: 1) it is highly efficient for both training and inference, as model complexity depends only on vocabulary size, not corpus size; 2) the corruption mechanism acts as an implicit, data-dependent regularizer that down-weights common, uninformative words; and 3) the resulting document vectors, formed by a simple average of word embeddings, achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant and Effective Simplification:** The proposed method is a simple, well-motivated, and effective modification of existing powerful models (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors). It directly addresses major practical drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors, namely the linear growth of parameters with corpus size and the expensive inference step for unseen documents, without sacrificing performance.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical justification for its core mechanism—the corruption process—by linking it to a form of data-dependent regularization via a Taylor expansion analysis. This theoretical insight is strongly supported by a comprehensive set of experiments across diverse tasks, demonstrating that the model not only performs well but that its underlying mechanisms (e.g., down-weighting uninformative words) behave as predicted.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of prior work. While Paragraph Vectors are powerful, their scalability is a significant concern for very large corpora, and their inference time is a practical bottleneck. Simple averaging of pre-trained Word2Vec vectors is fast but can be suboptimal. Doc2VecC presents a principled way to bridge this gap by integrating the document representation directly into the word embedding learning process, enforcing that the average of these embeddings becomes a meaningful representation.

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The core idea of using a corrupted document average as context is novel in this specific formulation. The analysis in Section 3.1 is a major strength of the paper. By showing that the stochastic corruption is approximately equivalent to adding a specific regularizer to the loss function, the authors provide a clear intuition for *why* their method works. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of words that are frequent but not predictive, which is a desirable property that other methods achieve through heuristics like IDF weighting or frequent word subsampling.

**Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental validation is thorough and convincing.
*   **Breadth of Tasks:** The model is tested on sentiment analysis (IMDB), topic classification (Wikipedia), semantic relatedness (SICK), and even the quality of its word vectors (word analogy task). This demonstrates the general applicability of the learned representations.
*   **Strong Baselines:** The comparisons are made against relevant and strong baselines, including Bag-of-Words, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), and the powerful Skip-thought Vectors.
*   **Performance:** Doc2VecC consistently matches or outperforms its most direct competitor, Paragraph Vectors, across sentiment and document classification tasks. The efficiency gains are substantial: Table 2 shows inference for Doc2VecC is orders of magnitude faster than for Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.
*   **Insightful Analysis:** The paper goes beyond reporting accuracy scores. The analysis in Table 3, showing which words receive the smallest L2 norm, provides concrete evidence for the claimed regularization effect. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 effectively illustrate the superior class separability of Doc2VecC's representations compared to Doc2Vec's on the Wikipedia dataset. The honest assessment of where the model underperforms (SICK dataset vs. LSTMs for short sentences) adds to the credibility of the work.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

1.  **Clarity on Frequent Word Subsampling:** In Section 4.2, you state that Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick" (subsampling of frequent words) because of its inherent regularization. However, you immediately follow by saying, "for all the numbers reported, we applied the trick." This is confusing. The claim that the model's regularization makes subsampling unnecessary would be much more powerful if supported by an ablation study showing results for Doc2VecC *without* subsampling. How does its performance change? If it is indeed robust, this would be a significant practical advantage.

2.  **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** You mention that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and use `q=0.9` throughout. While this simplifies the experiments, a small plot or table showing performance on a validation set for a few different values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) would substantiate this claim more rigorously and provide better guidance for practitioners.

3.  **Connection to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA):** The link to DEAs is an interesting one. You note that the model reduces to a DEA if the local context is removed. It would be illuminating to see an empirical comparison to this variant. How much performance is gained by including the local Word2Vec-style context (`Uct`) in addition to the global corrupted document context? This would help disentangle the contributions of the two context types.

4.  **Final Document Representation:** In Equation 3, the global context is `1/T * Ux̃`, where `x̃` is scaled by `1/(1-q)`. The final document representation in Equation 5 is a simple average, `1/T * Σ uw`. Could you clarify that at test time, you are effectively setting `q=0`, making the unbiased estimator `x̃` equal to `x`, thus reconciling the training objective with the final representation? A brief sentence clarifying this would be helpful for the reader.
