### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The key innovation lies in the training process: the model, similar in architecture to Word2Vec's CBoW, learns to predict a target word from its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. This global context is derived from an average of word embeddings from the document, but critically, this set of words is "corrupted" by randomly dropping out a large fraction of them for each training step.

The authors provide a strong theoretical motivation for this corruption, showing via a Taylor expansion that this procedure is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularization term to the standard objective function. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of words that are frequent but not predictive, effectively learning to down-weight uninformative words.

The paper makes the following key claims:
1.  **Efficiency:** Doc2VecC is significantly more efficient than Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), as its parameter count is independent of the corpus size and inference for new documents is a trivial averaging operation.
2.  **Performance:** The resulting document representations achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and word analogy tasks.
3.  **Regularization:** The corruption mechanism provides a principled way to learn robust embeddings without heuristic post-processing like IDF weighting or frequent word subsampling.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective method for learning document embeddings. The core idea is well-motivated, and the contributions are significant.

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Excellent Motivation and Problem Framing:** The paper clearly identifies the primary weaknesses of Paragraph Vectors (PV) — poor scalability in parameters and slow inference time for unseen documents. Doc2VecC directly addresses both issues with a method that is both conceptually simple and practically efficient.
2.  **Principled Methodology:** The "corruption" mechanism is not just an ad-hoc application of dropout. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which connects the stochastic corruption to a data-dependent regularizer, is a major strength. It provides a compelling explanation for *why* the method works, transforming a heuristic into a principled technique. This analysis also elegantly explains the empirical observation that the model learns to suppress uninformative words (Table 3).
3.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Evaluation:** The authors validate their claims across a diverse set of tasks (sentiment, classification, word analogy, semantic relatedness) against a strong suite of baselines, including PV and Skip-thought Vectors. The consistent outperformance on document-level tasks (IMDB, Wikipedia) and the surprisingly strong results on the word analogy task provide convincing evidence for the method's effectiveness. The reported training and inference times further solidify the practical advantages of the proposed model.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in the tight coupling between its simple premise, theoretical justification, and empirical results. The central idea of forcing a good document representation to emerge from an average of a *random subset* of its word embeddings is powerful. It elegantly bridges the gap between simple post-hoc averaging of Word2Vec embeddings (which doesn't enforce compositional learning) and the expensive per-document parameterization of Paragraph Vectors.

The results on the word analogy task are particularly insightful. The fact that Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than the standard Word2Vec model suggests that incorporating the global (document) context, even in this corrupted form, helps the model learn richer word-level semantics. This is a strong, and perhaps unexpected, positive side effect of the architecture.

The efficiency gains are a significant practical contribution. The slow inference of Paragraph Vectors is a major barrier to their deployment in many real-world systems. By producing document vectors via a simple averaging operation, Doc2VecC is orders of magnitude faster at test time, making it far more practical.

While the model underperforms Skip-thought vectors on the SICK dataset, the authors provide a plausible hypothesis related to document length. Given its superior performance on longer documents (IMDB reviews) and its vastly superior efficiency, this trade-off is well-justified and does not detract from the overall contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Clarity on Input Representation:** The notation is slightly ambiguous. The global context is formed by `(1/T) * U*x̃`, where `x̃` is a corrupted version of `x`, the document's BoW. However, `T` is the total number of words (length) in the document. This seems to conflate a set-based BoW representation with a sequence-based length. Is the averaging actually over the number of unique words in the document, or is `x` intended to be a multi-set representation? Clarifying this would be helpful.
*   **Ablation Study on Corruption Rate:** The paper states that `q=0.9` was used throughout and that performance was not sensitive to this hyperparameter. While this simplifies experiments, a small ablation study (e.g., a table or graph showing performance on a dev set for `q` in {0.1, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.99}) would make this claim more robust and provide readers with better practical guidance.
*   **Comparison with Denoising Autoencoders:** The corruption mechanism is conceptually similar to the input noise in Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs). The paper mentions DAEs as a baseline but could benefit from a more direct comparison in the methodology section. The key difference is the objective function (predicting context words vs. reconstructing the input). A brief discussion of how this difference in objective leads to different representational properties would be insightful.
*   **Visualization:** The t-SNE plots in Figure 3 are effective but would be even clearer with a legend indicating which colors correspond to which categories, even if just for a representative subset of the 100 classes.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify the precise definition of the document vector `d` used during training? Specifically, is the term `(1/T) * U*x̃` an average over the *total* number of words `T` in the original document, or is it an average over the number of non-zero elements in the corrupted BoW vector `x̃`?
2.  Your hypothesis that RNN-based models like Skip-thought are better for short sentences while Doc2VecC excels at longer documents is very interesting. Have you considered a controlled experiment to verify this, for instance, by training and evaluating on the IMDB dataset but truncating all reviews to the average length of sentences in the SICK dataset?
3.  The improvement on the word analogy task over standard Word2Vec is substantial. Do you have an intuition as to why forcing the embeddings to also form a coherent document-level average improves their performance on these fine-grained linguistic regularity tasks?
