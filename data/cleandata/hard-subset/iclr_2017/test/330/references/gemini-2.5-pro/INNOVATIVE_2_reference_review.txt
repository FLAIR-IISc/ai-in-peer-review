### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed document representations. The core idea is to extend the Word2Vec (CBoW) architecture by incorporating a global document context. Critically, unlike Paragraph Vectors which learns a unique vector for each document, Doc2VecC represents this global context as a simple average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted subset of the words in the document itself. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis, showing that this corruption mechanism is approximately equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common but uninformative words. This encourages the model to learn meaningful, compositional word embeddings that can be simply averaged at test time to produce high-quality document vectors. The method is evaluated on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, where it is shown to be highly competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods, while being significantly more efficient to train and, especially, to apply to unseen documents.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **High Novelty and Elegance:** The central contribution—using a corrupted average of word embeddings as a stochastic document context during training—is a simple, elegant, and highly novel idea. It cleverly sidesteps the major drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth with corpus size and slow inference) while proposing a more principled way to achieve compositional document representations than post-hoc averaging of Word2Vec embeddings.
2.  **Strong Theoretical and Empirical Support:** The paper provides both a compelling theoretical motivation for the corruption scheme, linking it to data-dependent regularization, and a comprehensive set of experiments. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating that this simple approach not only matches or outperforms more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors on several key benchmarks but does so with vastly superior computational efficiency.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper lies in its bold yet simple proposal. Instead of adding more parameters to model documents (as in Paragraph Vectors), it re-purposes the existing word embeddings in a novel, stochastic training scheme. This is exactly the kind of creative and efficient modeling that the field should encourage.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a standout feature. By using a second-order Taylor expansion, the authors demonstrate that the seemingly ad-hoc corruption process is not just a computational trick but approximates a well-defined regularization penalty. This regularizer has the desirable property of down-weighting the embeddings of words that are frequent but not predictive, providing a more principled alternative to heuristics like subsampling frequent words. This analysis adds significant depth to the paper and justifies the design choices.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The performance on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks is particularly impressive, showing clear improvements over strong baselines with a much smaller and more efficient model. The result on the word analogy task is also very insightful; the fact that Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than the original Word2Vec suggests that forcing the model to reconstruct context from a document-level signal leads to superior word-level semantics. This supports the authors' claim that the model truly learns compositional representations, rather than offloading document meaning into separate, non-compositional vectors as Paragraph Vectors might do. The efficiency gains reported in Table 2 are dramatic and constitute a major practical advantage, making the method far more scalable and usable than its predecessors.

While the performance on the SICK dataset is slightly below that of large RNN-based models, the authors' hypothesis regarding document length is plausible and does not detract from the overall contribution. Given the model's simplicity and speed, its strong performance is remarkable.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen a future version of the paper and did not factor negatively into my decision.

*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper mentions that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and that `q=0.9` was used throughout. While I appreciate the focus on the main results, a small ablation study in an appendix showing performance across a range of `q` values would strengthen this claim and provide useful guidance for practitioners.
*   **Analysis of the Taylor Approximation:** The link to regularization via the Taylor expansion is elegant. It would be beneficial to add a brief sentence acknowledging that this is an approximation and discussing, even at a high level, under what conditions it is expected to hold well.
*   **Corruption Strategy:** The current work uses a simple, unbiased dropout-style corruption. It might be interesting to briefly speculate on whether other corruption strategies (e.g., corrupting contiguous spans of text, or a TF-IDF-weighted corruption) could yield further benefits.

### Questions for the Authors

*   In Section 4.2, you state that frequent word subsampling (as introduced in Word2Vec) is critical for `Word2Vec+AVG` but that Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick" due to its intrinsic regularization. For the experiments presented, was this frequent word subsampling technique disabled for Doc2VecC, or was it used for all Word2Vec-based models, including your own? Clarifying this would ensure the comparison is perfectly apples-to-apples.
*   Your hypothesis that Paragraph Vectors stores most of the document-level information in the unique document vectors (d) rather than the word embeddings (U) is supported by the poor word analogy results. Have you tried visualizing the norms of the learned document vectors vs. the word vectors in a trained PV model to see if this provides further evidence?
*   The paper hypothesizes that Doc2VecC excels at longer documents while RNN-based methods are better for short sentences. This is a very interesting point. Would it be possible to provide a more direct analysis by, for example, splitting the IMDB test set into buckets based on review length and reporting the error rate for each bucket for both Doc2VecC and an RNN baseline? This could provide strong, direct evidence for this claim.
