### Summary

The paper proposes a new method, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), for learning distributed document representations. The core idea is to extend the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture from Word2Vec. While standard CBoW predicts a target word from its local context, Doc2VecC adds a global document context. Critically, this document context is not a separately learned parameter (as in Paragraph Vectors), but is formed by averaging the embeddings of a randomly sampled subset of words from the document itself. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process is equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, effectively learning to create meaningful document representations via simple averaging. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that Doc2VecC is highly efficient and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and word analogy tasks, outperforming several strong baselines.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** Doc2VecC presents an elegant and effective solution to a known problem. It successfully addresses the primary drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors—namely, the model size scaling with the training corpus and the expensive inference step for new documents—by instead tying the document representation directly to the learned word embeddings. This approach is simple, scalable, and highly efficient.

2.  **Strong Theoretical Justification:** The paper goes beyond presenting an empirical "trick." The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as a form of data-dependent regularization via a second-order Taylor expansion, provides a principled explanation for *why* the method works. This theoretical insight, which connects the stochastic training process to a deterministic objective that down-weights uninformative words, is a significant contribution and elevates the quality of the work.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The claims made in the paper are well-supported by both theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical evaluation.

*   **Addressing Key Limitations of Prior Work:** The primary motivation is to overcome the scalability and inference-time issues of Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014). The proposed architecture directly achieves this. Model parameters are independent of the number of documents, and generating a vector for an unseen document is a fast operation (averaging word vectors), as shown in the timing comparison in Table 2. This is a crucial practical advantage.

*   **Principled Approach to Averaging:** Simply averaging word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec+AVG) is a common but often suboptimal baseline. Doc2VecC’s novelty lies in making this averaging operation a central part of the learning objective. The model is explicitly trained to produce word embeddings that, when averaged, form a meaningful document representation capable of predicting held-out words. The corruption ensures that the model cannot rely on any single word and must learn a more distributed, robust representation.

*   **Empirical Validation of the Regularization Effect:** The theoretical claim that the corruption regularizes the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words is convincingly demonstrated in Section 4.2 (Table 3). Unlike Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors, where infrequent words have near-zero embeddings, Doc2VecC successfully learns to shrink the norms of common stop words and symbols, while preserving the norms of rare but semantically meaningful words. This is strong evidence that the theoretical model accurately describes the model's behavior.

*   **Strong and Broad Experimental Results:** The method is evaluated on a diverse set of tasks and consistently performs well.
    *   On sentiment analysis (IMDB) and document classification (Wikipedia), it outperforms strong baselines including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide a clear qualitative illustration of the superior separability of its learned representations.
    *   The word analogy task results (Section 4.3) are particularly impressive. The fact that Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than the original Word2Vec suggests that forcing the embeddings to be compositionally meaningful for documents also improves the quality of the word-level semantics they capture.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to further improve it.

*   **Clarity on Training vs. Test Representation:** During training, the global context is an average of a *corrupted* subset of word vectors (Eq. 2). At test time, the representation is an average of *all* word vectors in the document (Eq. 5). The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 justifies this by showing the training objective is an approximation of an objective on the uncorrupted document vector `x`. However, the paper could be more explicit in connecting these pieces. A short sentence clarifying that training on the corrupted version optimizes embeddings for composition via the full average at test time would improve readability.

*   **Deeper Comparison with Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs):** The paper notes that the model reduces to a DAE if the local context is removed. This is an excellent connection. It would be beneficial to elaborate slightly on the conceptual difference. For instance, DAEs aim for reconstruction, while Doc2VecC has a predictive objective (predicting a word). Does this predictive objective, coupled with the local context, inherently push the model to learn more semantic rather than just statistical regularities? A brief discussion here could further highlight the model's novelty.

*   **Performance on Short Texts (SICK dataset):** The authors rightly note that Doc2VecC is outperformed by LSTM-based models on the SICK dataset and hypothesize this is due to document length. This is a plausible explanation. To make it more concrete, it would be interesting to see if this performance gap shrinks if the model is evaluated on longer texts for semantic relatedness, or conversely, if the performance of Skip-thought vectors degrades more sharply than Doc2VecC on the IMDB task if documents are truncated to sentence length. This is not essential for acceptance but would add valuable nuance.

### Questions for Authors

1.  In Section 4.2, you state that Doc2VecC "does not rely on" the subsampling of frequent words trick used by Mikolov et al. (2013a), whereas it is "critical" for a baseline like Word2Vec+AVG. For the experiments reported, was this subsampling procedure enabled or disabled for Doc2VecC? Clarifying this is important for ensuring a perfectly apples-to-apples comparison of the implicit regularization from corruption versus the explicit subsampling heuristic.

2.  The corruption rate `q` is set to 0.9. While you mention the model is not sensitive to it, this is a very high rate of corruption. Could you provide some intuition on why such an aggressive corruption works so well? Does performance degrade significantly if `q` is lowered to, say, 0.5 or 0.25? An ablation study, even a small one, would strengthen this claim.

3.  The theoretical analysis shows the regularizer `R(uj)` depends on `σ(1-σ)`, which is small for confident predictions. Does this mean the model effectively "stops" regularizing words once they become part of a context that is highly predictive? Could this lead to a situation where common words in highly idiomatic phrases are not regularized as heavily as expected?
