### Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and highly efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The model builds upon the Word2Vec CBoW architecture by incorporating a global document context to help predict a target word. The key innovation is how this global context is constructed: instead of learning a unique vector for each document (as in Paragraph Vectors), Doc2VecC represents the document context as the average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted version of the document, where a large fraction of words are dropped out. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption scheme is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-informative words. The method is evaluated on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, where it demonstrates strong performance, often outperforming more complex and computationally expensive baselines like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, particularly on tasks involving longer documents.

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. It presents a simple, elegant, and powerful idea that offers a new direction for learning document representations. The core contribution—using document corruption as a training mechanism and a form of implicit regularization—is highly novel and well-motivated. This work is a prime example of how a bold, conceptually simple approach can outperform more complex and heavily parameterized models, aligning perfectly with the values of the ICLR community.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Elegance:** The central idea of Doc2VecC is compelling. It cleverly combines concepts from denoising autoencoders (corruption), dropout, and distributed representation learning (Word2Vec) into a unified and simple framework. Instead of adding complexity, like the per-document parameters in Paragraph Vectors, it introduces a stochastic process that not only simplifies the model but also improves it. The idea that you can learn a better document representation by forcing the model to reconstruct meaning from a heavily damaged version of the document is both counter-intuitive and brilliant.

2.  **Strong Theoretical and Intuitive Grounding:** The paper doesn't just present a new heuristic; it provides a solid justification for *why* it should work. The Taylor expansion analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer, is a significant strength. It provides a clear and principled explanation for the model's ability to down-weight common, non-discriminative words (like stop words) and focus on more informative ones. This analysis elevates the contribution from a clever trick to a well-understood technique.

3.  **Impressive Empirical Results and Efficiency:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and convincing.
    *   **Performance:** Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across a range of tasks. Its outstanding performance on the IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia classification tasks demonstrates its strength in capturing topic and sentiment from long documents.
    *   **Efficiency:** The practical benefits of this model are substantial. By decoupling model size from the corpus size, it is vastly more scalable than Paragraph Vectors. The reported training and inference times in Table 2 are a testament to this; an inference time of 7 seconds versus over 4 minutes for Paragraph Vectors on the IMDB test set is a massive practical advantage.
    *   **Word Embedding Quality:** A surprising and powerful result is the significant improvement in the quality of the learned *word* embeddings, as shown in the word analogy task (Table 4). That the document-level training objective simultaneously produces superior word vectors is a strong indicator of the model's effectiveness and elegance.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While these points do not change my decision, they could help strengthen the final version of the paper:

*   **Ablation on Subsampling:** The paper states that Doc2VecC's implicit regularization makes it less reliant on the frequent word subsampling heuristic used in Word2Vec. However, the experiments still employ this heuristic. To fully demonstrate the power of the corruption-as-regularization mechanism, it would be highly compelling to include results for Doc2VecC *without* any explicit subsampling and compare it to both the standard Word2Vec+AVG baseline (which you show suffers without it) and the full Doc2VecC model.
*   **Analysis of Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper mentions that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and sets it to 0.9. While this is acceptable, a small paragraph or a plot in the appendix showing the effect of varying `q` (e.g., from 0.5 to 0.99) on a validation set would add further confidence in this claim and provide guidance for future users of the model.
*   **Document Length Analysis:** The hypothesis that Doc2VecC excels on long documents while recurrent models excel on short sentences is plausible. This could be strengthened with a brief quantitative analysis. For instance, on the IMDB dataset, you could bucket the test documents by length and plot the accuracy for Doc2VecC and a baseline like Skip-thought Vectors to visually confirm this trend.

**Questions for the Authors**

*   In your analysis, the regularization term `R(uj)` appears to be a function of the model's confidence (`σw,c,x`). Does this mean that as the model becomes more confident during training, the effective regularization on a word `j` diminishes? If so, this suggests a very interesting dynamic where the regularization is strongest early in training, which seems like a desirable property. Could you comment on this interpretation?
*   Have you considered applying the corruption idea to the local context as well? That is, randomly dropping words from the context window (`ct`) in addition to the global document context. It seems like a natural extension, and I am curious if you explored this and what the effects were.
