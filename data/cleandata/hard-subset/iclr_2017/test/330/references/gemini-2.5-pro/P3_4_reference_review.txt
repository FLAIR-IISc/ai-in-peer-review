### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Doc2VecC (Document Vector through Corruption), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to extend the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture of Word2Vec. In addition to the standard local word context used to predict a target word, Doc2VecC incorporates a global document context. This global context is cleverly constructed by taking a simple average of the word embeddings from a "corrupted" version of the document, where a random subset of words has been dropped out.

The main contributions are:
1.  A simple and highly efficient model architecture that decouples model size from the number of documents in the training corpus, addressing a key limitation of Paragraph Vectors.
2.  A theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism, showing through a Taylor expansion that it is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings. This regularizer penalizes frequent, non-discriminative words, effectively learning to perform a weighted average without explicit weighting schemes like IDF.
3.  A comprehensive set of experiments demonstrating that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly faster to train and, especially, to use for inference on unseen documents compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method is elegant, simple, and very well-motivated. It provides a principled way to improve upon the common but often mediocre "average of word embeddings" baseline by integrating the compositional objective directly into the training process.
2.  The claims are strongly supported by both a compelling theoretical analysis (linking corruption to data-dependent regularization) and a thorough empirical evaluation against a strong suite of modern baselines. The paper clearly demonstrates significant improvements in both performance and computational efficiency.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**Methodology and Motivation:** The paper is built on a very solid foundation. The authors correctly identify the major drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (PV-DM), namely the parameter scaling with corpus size and the expensive inference step for new documents. The proposed solution of using an average of the document's own word embeddings as the document vector is a natural and efficient alternative. The key innovation—and the paper's main strength—is the introduction of the corruption process during training.

The derivation in Section 3.1 is the highlight of the paper. By showing that the stochastic corruption approximates a deterministic objective with an additional regularization term, the authors provide a clear and insightful explanation for *why* their method works. This regularizer's properties—penalizing frequent words unless their embeddings are crucial for making confident predictions—is an intuitive and powerful mechanism. It essentially learns an implicit, data-driven down-weighting of stop words and other common but uninformative terms, a task often handled by heuristics like IDF weighting or stop word removal. This provides a much more elegant and integrated solution.

**Experimental Rigor and Results:** The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing.
*   **Baselines:** The choice of baselines is excellent, including classic methods (BoW), modern unsupervised standards (DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors), and a powerful sequential model (Skip-thought Vectors). This allows for a robust assessment of Doc2VecC's place in the current landscape.
*   **Tasks:** The evaluation spans multiple core NLP tasks: sentiment analysis (long documents), document classification (multi-class), and sentence-level semantic relatedness. This demonstrates the general applicability of the learned representations.
*   **Performance:** Doc2VecC's performance is impressive. It consistently matches or outperforms Paragraph Vectors while being orders of magnitude faster at inference (Table 2: 7s vs 4m17s is a huge practical difference). The poor performance of Skip-thought vectors on the long IMDB documents, and the authors' correct hypothesis for why, further strengthens the case for Doc2VecC's suitability for document-level tasks.
*   **Analysis:** The paper goes beyond just reporting accuracy. The qualitative analysis in Table 3, showing which words receive the smallest L2 norm, provides compelling direct evidence for the claimed regularization effect. Furthermore, the strong results on the word analogy task (Table 4) are a surprising and significant finding, suggesting that the document-level objective in Doc2VecC not only doesn't harm but actually *improves* the quality of the underlying word embeddings compared to the standard Word2Vec objective. The t-SNE visualizations also provide good qualitative support for the superiority of the learned document space.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help further improve its clarity and impact:
1.  **Title:** The title "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" appears to have a word missing. I assume it should be "DOCUMENT REPRESENTATIONS THROUGH CORRUPTION" or similar. This should be corrected.
2.  **Clarification on Corruption Input:** The vector `x` is defined as a BoW vector (implying binary presence/absence), but the subsequent formulas and the corruption model (scaling by `1/(1-q)`) suggest it might be a multi-hot vector or that counts are implicitly handled. A slightly more precise definition of how `x` and `x̃` are constructed and used in `1/T * Ux̃` would be beneficial. For example, is `T` the original document length or the length after corruption?
3.  **Ablation Study on `q`:** The authors state that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and use `q=0.9` for efficiency. While this is a reasonable choice, a small table or graph showing the performance on a validation set for a few values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) would make this claim more concrete and provide useful guidance for practitioners.
4.  **Comparison to Denoising Autoencoders:** The paper correctly notes a conceptual link to DAEs. However, the objective here is predictive (predicting a held-out word) rather than reconstructive (reconstructing the original input). This is an important distinction. A brief sentence clarifying this difference might be helpful, as the current formulation could be misinterpreted as a standard DAE variant.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you clarify the interaction between the document length normalization `1/T` and the unbiased corruption scaling `1/(1-q)`? Is the average in Eq. 2 taken over the original number of words `T`, or the number of words remaining after corruption, which would be `T * (1-q)` in expectation?
2.  The superior performance on the word analogy task over standard Word2Vec is a very interesting result. Do you have any further intuition as to why forcing word embeddings to also compose into a meaningful document average improves their ability to capture fine-grained analogical relationships? Does this suggest that the global context provides a richer training signal than the local context alone?
3.  Have you considered applying the corruption mechanism to the local context `ct` as well? It seems like a similar principle could apply, potentially forcing more robustness on the local context representation.
