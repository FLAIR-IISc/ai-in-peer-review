### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and highly efficient architecture for learning distributed representations of documents. The central idea is to extend the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) model from Word2Vec. In addition to the local context window, the model predicts a target word using a global context vector representing the entire document. Critically, instead of learning a unique vector for each document (as in Paragraph Vectors), Doc2VecC represents this global context as a simple average of word embeddings from the document. The key innovation is a corruption process applied during training, where this average is computed over a small, random subset of the document's words. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism acts as an implicit, data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer naturally suppresses the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words while promoting those of rare, informative words. Empirically, the paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient to train and, especially, to use for inference on unseen documents compared to major baselines like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. It presents a simple, elegant, and bold idea that challenges more complex and computationally expensive approaches to document representation learning. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Insight:** The paper's core contribution—framing input corruption as a data-dependent regularizer for learning compositional document vectors—is a significant conceptual leap. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which connects the stochastic corruption to a specific regularization term via a Taylor expansion, provides a powerful explanation for *why* such a simple mechanism works so well. This is far more than an incremental improvement; it offers a new perspective on how to enforce meaningful compositionality during the learning process itself, rather than relying on post-hoc averaging or expensive explicit document parameters.

2.  **Excellent Practicality and Strong Empirical Support:** The proposed method addresses a major bottleneck of Paragraph Vectors: the high cost of training and inference due to per-document parameters. Doc2VecC is not only much faster but also achieves superior or comparable performance across a range of diverse and standard benchmarks. This combination of simplicity, speed, and strong performance makes the work highly impactful and likely to be widely adopted. The paper makes a compelling case that complex machinery is not always necessary and that a well-motivated, simple approach can be superior.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The strength of this work lies in its blend of a novel theoretical insight with impressive practical results. The idea of simply averaging word embeddings to get a document vector is not new, but such methods have often been seen as weak baselines. This paper demonstrates that by integrating this averaging process into the learning objective under a corruption scheme, the resulting word embeddings become optimized for this very purpose. The resulting regularization effect, as shown qualitatively in Table 3 where Doc2VecC correctly learns small-norm vectors for stop words rather than for rare words, is a powerful and intuitive validation of the method's mechanism.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The method's strong performance on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks, particularly its ability to outperform Paragraph Vectors while being orders of magnitude faster at inference, is a standout result. While it doesn't beat sequence-based models like LSTMs on the short-sentence SICK dataset, the authors provide a reasonable hypothesis for this discrepancy (model architecture suitability for sequence length), and the performance is still highly competitive. More importantly, its dramatic outperformance of Skip-thought vectors on the longer-form IMDB dataset highlights the robustness and applicability of the proposed approach for document-level tasks. The significant improvement on the word analogy task over both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors further suggests that the learning process in Doc2VecC yields higher-quality, more semantically coherent word embeddings as a byproduct, which is an impressive and valuable feature.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Further Exploration of Corruption:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and sets it to 0.9 for efficiency. While practical, this is a very high corruption rate, meaning the global context is derived from only 10% of the words on average. It would be valuable to include a small ablation study (perhaps in an appendix) showing performance on a validation set across a range of `q` values. This would provide a more robust characterization of the model's behavior and solidify the claim of insensitivity.
*   **Comparison to Dropout:** The corruption mechanism is functionally similar to dropout on the input layer. It would be interesting to briefly discuss the connection more explicitly. Is the unbiased masking scheme (`xd/(1-q)`) critical, or would standard dropout (just setting to 0) work similarly? The theoretical analysis relies on the unbiased nature of the corruption, so this might be an important detail to highlight.
*   **Ablation on Subsampling:** The paper notes that Doc2VecC does not rely on the frequent-word subsampling trick used in Word2Vec, as the implicit regularization handles this effect. This is a strong claim. The experiments reported, however, seem to use this trick. An ablation study showing Doc2VecC's performance with and without subsampling would be a powerful addition to empirically validate this claim and further distinguish the method from prior work.

I would be interested in the authors' thoughts on the following:

*   The Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 provides a compelling intuition, but it is an approximation. Have you considered how the higher-order terms might affect the regularization? Or, from an empirical standpoint, does the observed regularization effect (i.e., the L2 norms of embeddings) closely mirror what the derived regularizer `R(uj)` would predict?
