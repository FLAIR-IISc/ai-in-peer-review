### Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The learning process is based on the CBoW architecture, where the model predicts a target word from its local context (neighboring words) and a global context derived from the entire document. The main contribution lies in the "corruption" mechanism: the global context is formed by averaging embeddings from a randomly sampled subset of words from the document. The authors provide a theoretical analysis framing this corruption as a form of data-dependent regularization, which implicitly penalizes frequent, non-discriminative words. The paper claims that this approach is highly efficient for both training and inference, and produces representations that are competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper is critically incomplete and therefore unreviewable. A majority of the tables (1, 2, 3, 4, 6) and all figures (1, 3, 4) referenced in the text are missing. These elements are essential for understanding the model's architecture and verifying the empirical claims that form the core of the paper's contribution.
2.  The manuscript exhibits an extreme lack of care in its preparation, to a degree that is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier conference. This includes a typo in the title, a completely corrupted reference section, numerous typographical and grammatical errors, and inconsistent notation. This pervasive carelessness undermines the credibility of the research itself.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The decision for rejection is based on a substantial number of severe flaws that prevent a fair and thorough assessment of the work.

*   **Unverifiable Empirical Claims:** The experimental evaluation is the primary evidence for the proposed method's efficacy. However, the manuscript is missing the key results:
    *   **Table 1 & 2 (Sentiment Analysis):** All accuracy and timing results for the IMDB task are absent, making it impossible to evaluate claims of outperforming baselines or being more efficient.
    *   **Table 3 (Regularization Effect):** The qualitative evidence for the data-dependent regularization is missing.
    *   **Table 4 (Word Analogy):** While the text discusses results from a Table 4, it is not present. The text then incorrectly references Table 5 in its place.
    *   **Table 6 (Semantic Relatedness):** The results on the SICK dataset are absent.
    *   **Figure 1 (Model Architecture):** This figure is crucial for understanding the proposed model, yet it is missing. The description in Section 3 relies entirely on this non-existent figure.
    *   **Figure 3 & 4 (Visualizations):** The t-SNE visualizations meant to demonstrate the quality of the learned document vectors are also missing.
    The single table that is present (Table 5) shows a marginal improvement over baselines, which makes the claim of outperforming existing algorithms "by a significant margin" seem overstated. Furthermore, the reported poor performance of the Paragraph Vectors baseline in this table is surprising and raises questions about the experimental setup.

*   **Severe Formatting and Presentation Issues:** The manuscript is riddled with errors that demonstrate a profound lack of proofreading.
    *   **Title:** The title itself appears to be truncated or contain a typo: "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION".
    *   **References:** The reference section is completely malformed and unreadable (e.g., `Blei et al\.,? \Q2003\E, Blei et al\.,`). This is a critical failure that prevents verification of citations and situating the work in the literature.
    *   **Notation:** The use of `V>` for the output projection matrix and `v>w` for a row vector is highly non-standard and confusing. The standard notation is `V^T` for transpose.
    *   **Typos and Grammar:** There are numerous errors throughout the text, such as "well-know" (Sec 2), "quantatively" (Sec 4.3), "on the other hands" (Sec 2), "Denoising Autoencoders (DEA)" instead of DAE (Sec 4.1), and inconsistent use of "DEA" and "SDA".
    *   **Broken Footnotes and Links:** Footnote markers are not rendered correctly, and the provided GitHub link for reproducing experiments is dead, which severely hinders reproducibility.

*   **Minor Methodological Imprecisions:**
    *   In the famous "king - man + woman = queen" example, the paper uses "women" instead of "woman". While minor, this is an error in a widely known example.
    *   The description of negative sampling states it uses a "uniform distribution" (Sec 3), which is a simplification of the standard approach that uses a smoothed unigram distribution. This is an important implementation detail that is misstated.

While the core idea of using document-level corruption as a regularizer is interesting and the Taylor expansion analysis provides a plausible theoretical grounding, the execution and presentation of this work are far below the standards for publication at ICLR.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The strongest part of this paper is the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which connects the corruption scheme to a data-dependent regularizer. If the empirical results are indeed strong, this connection would be a valuable contribution. I would advise the authors to highlight this analysis more.
*   The entire paper requires a thorough revision for clarity, consistency, and correctness. This includes fixing all typos, grammatical errors, and formatting issues. The reference list must be completely rebuilt using a standard format (e.g., BibTeX).
*   All missing figures and tables must be included. Every claim in the text must be directly supported by data presented in these elements.
*   Standard mathematical notation should be adopted (`V^T`).
*   The description of all baselines and implementation details (e.g., negative sampling distribution, hyperparameter tuning) should be more precise. The surprisingly poor performance of some strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors needs to be investigated and explained.
*   The claim that extending the model to the Skip-gram architecture is "straightforward" should be either briefly justified or removed.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide all the missing tables and figures so that your empirical claims can be properly evaluated?
2.  Could you clarify the poor performance of the Paragraph Vectors baseline on the document classification task (Table 5)? What hyperparameters were used, and was a similar level of tuning performed for it as for your proposed model?
3.  The paper states that negative sampling uses a uniform distribution. Is this an intentional deviation from the standard power-of-3/4 unigram distribution used in Word2Vec, and if so, what was the motivation?
4.  Can you provide a working link to the code for the sake of reproducibility?
