### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes "Document Vector through Corruption" (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning document representations. The proposed model extends the CBoW architecture of Word2Vec by incorporating a global document context alongside the local word context to predict a target word. The key innovation is how this global context is formed: it is the average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted version of the document, where words are dropped out. The authors provide a theoretical analysis, based on a Taylor expansion of the loss function, arguing that this corruption scheme is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words more heavily. The paper empirically evaluates Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks. The results suggest that Doc2VecC is highly efficient and achieves performance that is competitive with, or superior to, several state-of-the-art methods, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Severe Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper is in a critically incomplete and unpolished state. The title itself appears to be truncated ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). More importantly, multiple figures (1, 3, 4) and all tables (1-6) referenced in the text are completely missing. This makes it impossible to verify any of the quantitative or qualitative claims that form the core of the paper's contribution. Furthermore, the entire reference section is malformed with BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `\Q2003\E`), inconsistent formatting, and capitalization errors, indicating a profound lack of care in the preparation of the manuscript.
2.  **Methodological and Notational Ambiguities:** The paper contains several points of confusion and non-standard choices that are not adequately justified. The mathematical notation is unconventional (e.g., `V>` for transpose) and confusingly defined. Key methodological details, such as the use of a uniform distribution for negative sampling, deviate from standard practice without explanation. These issues undermine the clarity and reproducibility of the proposed method.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The decision to reject is based on the manuscript's current state, which falls far short of the standards expected for publication at ICLR.

*   **Incomplete Manuscript:** The absence of all figures and tables is a fatal flaw. For instance, Section 4.2 discusses the accuracy results in "Table 1" and training times in "Table 2", but neither is present. The word analogy improvements are described with reference to missing tables. The t-SNE visualizations in "Figure 3" are central to the argument in Section 4.4 but are not included. Without this evidence, the empirical section is reduced to a set of unverifiable assertions.

*   **Lack of Professionalism and Care:** Beyond the missing content, the paper is riddled with errors. The title is incomplete. Typographical errors such as "well-know" (Section 2) are present. The reference list is unusable in its current state. A manuscript submitted with such fundamental errors raises serious concerns about the rigor and diligence applied to the research itself.

*   **Technical Obscurities:**
    *   **Notation:** The notation `V>` for the transpose of the output projection matrix is non-standard and should be `V^T`. The description of `uw` and `vw` as columns of `U` and `V` respectively, combined with the matrix dimensions provided, is confusing. Clear, standard notation is essential for reproducibility.
    *   **Negative Sampling:** The paper states that negative samples are drawn from a uniform distribution `Pv` (Section 3). The standard and empirically validated practice in Word2Vec and related models is to sample from a smoothed unigram distribution (e.g., `U(w)^(3/4)`). This deviation is significant and could impact performance, yet it is mentioned without justification.
    *   **Unsubstantiated Claims:** In Section 4.2, the paper claims that Doc2VecC "does not rely on" the trick of subsampling frequent words. However, this claim is supported only by showing that Word2Vec+AVG's performance degrades without it. To be convincing, the authors should have presented an ablation study showing the performance of Doc2VecC with and without subsampling.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite the severe flaws in the current manuscript, the core idea presented is interesting and potentially valuable. The connection between corruption and data-dependent regularization is well-motivated, and the goal of creating a simple, efficient, and high-performing document embedding model is worthwhile. Should the authors choose to revise and resubmit, I would strongly recommend the following:

*   **Complete Overhaul of Presentation:** Perform a thorough proofreading of the entire manuscript. Correct all typos, grammatical errors, and formatting issues. Re-format the entire reference list using a standard citation style. Ensure all figures and tables are correctly included, numbered, and captioned.
*   **Clarify Notation and Methodology:** Adopt standard mathematical notation (`V^T`, clear indexing). Provide a precise and unambiguous definition of all variables and matrices in Section 2. Justify any deviations from standard practices, such as the choice of negative sampling distribution, or revert to the standard method.
*   **Strengthen Experimental Rigor:**
    *   When comparing against models like Skip-thought Vectors, acknowledge the vast difference in dimensionality (100 vs 4800) and discuss its implications. While efficiency is a key advantage, the performance comparison should be framed carefully.
    *   Provide the necessary ablation study to support the claim about subsampling.
    *   Clarify arbitrary data processing choices, such as using only "the second paragraph" of Wikipedia articles, and consider their impact on the results.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the missing Figures 1, 3, and 4, and Tables 1 through 6? They are essential for assessing the validity of your claims.
2.  What was the rationale for using a uniform distribution for negative sampling instead of the standard smoothed unigram distribution? How does this choice affect the model's performance and the resulting word embeddings?
3.  Regarding the claim that Doc2VecC does not rely on frequent word subsampling, could you provide the empirical result (i.e., classification error on IMDB) for Doc2VecC trained *without* this subsampling step?
