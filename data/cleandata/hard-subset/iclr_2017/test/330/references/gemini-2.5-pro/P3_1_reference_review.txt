### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to extend the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture from Word2Vec by incorporating a global document context. Unlike Paragraph Vectors, which learns a unique vector for each document, Doc2VecC represents this global context as the simple average of word embeddings within the document. The key innovation is the application of a corruption process during training, where a random subset of words from the document is used to form this average context vector.

The authors make several key contributions:
1.  They propose a simple and efficient architecture that decouples model size from the training corpus size, addressing a major limitation of Paragraph Vectors.
2.  They provide a compelling theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism, showing through a Taylor expansion that it is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words.
3.  They demonstrate that this approach leads to a very fast inference process, as document vectors can be computed by simply averaging the learned word embeddings, eliminating the need for an expensive optimization step for unseen documents.
4.  Through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, they show that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance compared to strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The proposed method is a simple yet powerful synthesis of ideas from Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders. It directly addresses the scalability and inference-time limitations of Paragraph Vectors with a clever and well-justified corruption technique.
2.  **Strong Empirical and Theoretical Support:** The paper's claims are backed by both a solid theoretical analysis (the regularization argument in Section 3.1) and comprehensive empirical evaluations. The experiments are well-designed, compare against relevant and strong baselines, and demonstrate the method's effectiveness and efficiency across a diverse set of tasks. The authors are also commendably transparent about the model's limitations on short-text tasks.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

**Strengths:**
*   **Novelty and Simplicity:** The central idea of using a corrupted average of word embeddings as a global context is both novel and elegant. It avoids the parameter explosion of Paragraph Vectors while still enabling the model to learn document-level semantics. The resulting test-time procedure—simple averaging—is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:** The derivation in Section 3.1 is a major strength of the paper. It moves the corruption mechanism from a heuristic trick to a principled technique by showing its equivalence to a data-dependent regularizer. This analysis provides a clear explanation for why the model can effectively suppress uninformative words and why simple averaging works so well at test time. This is a level of analysis often missing in related work.
*   **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.
    *   On the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks, Doc2VecC demonstrates superior or competitive performance against strong baselines, including the much more complex Skip-thought model. The efficiency comparison in Table 2 clearly highlights the practical advantages of the proposed approach.
    *   The word analogy task results are particularly interesting. The finding that Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than both standard Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors suggests that the document-level objective provides a beneficial training signal that improves the quality of the word representations themselves. The hypothesis for why Paragraph Vectors fails on this task is insightful.
    *   The qualitative analyses, such as the examination of embedding norms in Table 3 and the t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3, provide strong intuitive support for the quantitative results and the theoretical claims.
*   **Clarity and Writing:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the method is described precisely, and the experiments are presented logically.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further improve its quality and impact. These are suggestions for the authors and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Ablation Study on Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and sets it to 0.9. While plausible, this claim would be much stronger if supported by an ablation study. A small table or plot showing performance on a validation set for different values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) would provide valuable insight into the model's robustness and help guide future users.
*   **Local vs. Global Context:** The model uses both a local CBoW context and a global document context. An ablation study that trains models with only the local context (i.e., standard CBoW) and only the global context (i.e., a Denoising Autoencoder-style model, as noted by the authors) would help disentangle their respective contributions and further justify the combined architecture.
*   **Comparison with Siamesed CBOW (S-CBOW):** The proposed method bears some resemblance to the S-CBOW model from Kenter et al. (2016), which also learns sentence embeddings by predicting a target word from a context representation. A brief discussion of the similarities and differences would better position the work in the literature.
*   **Title of the Paper:** The title in the provided text, "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION," appears to be truncated. Assuming the full title is "DOCUMENT REPRESENTATIONS THROUGH CORRUPTION" or similar, this should be corrected.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Your hypothesis that Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task because it relies heavily on the unique document vectors is very compelling. Have you performed any analysis to support this? For instance, do the learned document vectors in a PV model have significantly larger L2 norms on average compared to the word vectors, suggesting they capture most of the predictive power?
2.  In Section 3.1, you derive the regularization term based on a Taylor expansion. This approximation holds best when the corruption noise is small. Given that you use a very high corruption rate (`q=0.9`), how confident are you that this approximation accurately characterizes the model's learning dynamics? Does the empirical evidence of down-weighting frequent words suggest the approximation is a good guide even at high `q`?
3.  The performance drop on the SICK dataset relative to LSTMs is attributed to document length. Could your model be adapted for shorter texts, perhaps by using a smaller corruption rate `q` to ensure the global context vector is formed from a sufficient number of words, or by using a different composition function than simple averaging?
