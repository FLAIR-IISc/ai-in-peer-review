### 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel and efficient model, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), for learning distributed representations of documents. The method builds upon the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture of Word2Vec. It represents a document's global context as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The core contribution is the introduction of a corruption mechanism during training, where a random subset of words from the document is used to form this global context vector for predicting a target word.

The authors claim several benefits for this approach. First, it is computationally efficient for both training and inference, as the model size depends only on the vocabulary size (not the corpus size), and generating a vector for a new document is a simple averaging operation. This directly addresses major efficiency drawbacks of methods like Paragraph Vectors. Second, the paper provides a theoretical analysis showing that the corruption process is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the training objective. This regularizer is claimed to penalize the embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving the magnitude of embeddings for rare or discriminative words. Finally, the authors support their claims with extensive empirical evaluations across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art baselines.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method is a simple, effective, and well-motivated approach that directly addresses significant limitations of prior work, namely the high computational cost of inference in Paragraph Vectors. The model's efficiency and simplicity are major strengths.
2.  The paper provides a strong theoretical justification for its core mechanism (corruption) by showing its equivalence to a data-dependent regularizer. This analysis provides valuable insight into *why* the method works and elevates the contribution beyond a purely empirical result.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in its clarity, technical soundness, and empirical rigor.

**Clarity and Motivation:** The paper is clearly written and situates the proposed method effectively within the existing literature. It identifies a clear and practical problem with an influential prior method, Paragraph Vectors (i.e., slow inference and model size scaling with the corpus). The proposed solution, Doc2VecC, is a logical and elegant extension of the Word2Vec framework. The motivation for using corruption is twofold and convincing: it provides a practical speedup during training and, more importantly, induces a desirable regularization effect.

**Technical Soundness:** The model architecture is a straightforward and sound modification of the CBoW model. The most compelling technical aspect is the derivation in Section 3.1, where a second-order Taylor expansion is used to analyze the effect of the corruption process. This analysis demonstrates that minimizing the expected loss under corruption is approximately equivalent to minimizing the standard loss plus a regularization term that penalizes word embeddings based on their frequency and predictive utility. This provides a principled explanation for the model's effectiveness.

**Empirical Support:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and provides strong evidence for the paper's claims.
*   **Performance:** Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across three distinct tasks: sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK). It consistently outperforms baselines like BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors, and is shown to be more effective than Skip-thought vectors on tasks with longer documents (IMDB).
*   **Efficiency:** Table 2 clearly demonstrates the significant advantage of Doc2VecC in both training and testing time compared to Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors. The inference time for new documents is orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors, confirming a key motivation for the work.
*   **Analysis of Regularization:** The claims about the data-dependent regularization are supported by both qualitative (Table 3, showing low-norm embeddings for stop words) and quantitative evidence (superior performance on the word analogy task), suggesting that Doc2VecC learns higher-quality word embeddings than its predecessors.

The combination of a simple, efficient model, a solid theoretical justification, and comprehensive empirical validation makes this a strong contribution to the field of representation learning.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   The connection to Denoising Autoencoders (DAE) mentioned in Section 4.2 is interesting but could be made more precise. A standard DAE objective involves reconstructing the entire input, whereas Doc2VecC predicts a single target word. Clarifying this distinction would strengthen the comparison.
*   The visualization in Figure 4 is helpful, but the coarse categories ("television", "albums", etc.) are created manually. A brief description of how the 100 fine-grained categories were mapped to these 7 coarse ones would aid interpretability.
*   The paper reports fine-tuning the model on the SICK dataset. While it is stated that this did not use the relatedness scores, the exact training objective used for this fine-tuning step is not specified. Elaboration on this process would improve reproducibility.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
1.  Could you please clarify the exact objective function and procedure used for fine-tuning the pre-trained model on the SICK dataset? For instance, was the standard Doc2VecC objective used on the SICK sentences, and how were out-of-vocabulary word embeddings initialized?
2.  The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is based on a second-order approximation. Have you performed any empirical analysis to investigate how closely this approximation models the actual training dynamics?
3.  Table 3 provides a compelling qualitative look at the regularization effect. Have you considered a more quantitative analysis, such as plotting the L2 norm of word embeddings against their corpus frequency (or IDF) to show a clearer trend for Doc2VecC compared to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors?
4.  The paper focuses on the CBoW architecture. Did you experiment with applying the corruption mechanism to the Skip-gram architecture? If so, were the results and observed effects on the embeddings comparable?
