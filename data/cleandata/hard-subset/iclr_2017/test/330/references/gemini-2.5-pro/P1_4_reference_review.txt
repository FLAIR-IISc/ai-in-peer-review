### **Summary**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and effective method for learning distributed document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its constituent word embeddings. Unlike previous methods that compute this average as a post-processing step on pre-trained word vectors, Doc2VecC enforces this compositional structure directly during the learning process. The model extends the popular CBoW architecture by adding a global document context, which is formed by averaging embeddings from a randomly corrupted subset of the document's words. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis, framing this corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization that penalizes the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds the performance of state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, while being significantly faster to train and, critically, to use for inference on unseen documents.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed method is simple, elegant, and addresses key practical limitations of prior work. The decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Strong Motivation and Practicality:** Doc2VecC provides a compelling solution to the scalability and inference-time challenges of Paragraph Vectors. By avoiding document-specific parameters, it decouples model size from corpus size and enables extremely fast generation of embeddings for new documents, which is a significant practical advantage.
2.  **Thorough and Convincing Evaluation:** The paper supports its claims with a strong combination of theoretical justification (linking corruption to regularization) and extensive empirical results. The method is shown to be highly effective across a diverse set of benchmarks, outperforming strong baselines in multiple settings and providing insightful analysis into its performance characteristics.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous:

*   **Novelty and Significance:** While building on the well-established Word2Vec framework, the core contribution—using a corrupted document average as a global context during training—is novel and effective. It elegantly forces the word vectors themselves to encode information in a way that is amenable to simple averaging for document-level semantics. This provides a principled alternative to heuristic weighting schemes (like IDF) or computationally expensive models.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The model architecture is a sensible extension of CBoW. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a major highlight. By using a Taylor expansion, the authors demonstrate that the stochastic corruption process is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer has the desirable property of shrinking the norms of words that are frequent but not predictive, providing a formal justification for the method's effectiveness and connecting it to established concepts like dropout and denoising autoencoders.

*   **Empirical Rigor:** The experimental setup is thorough and convincing.
    *   **Baselines:** The authors compare against a comprehensive set of relevant and strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), Skip-thought vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders.
    *   **Tasks:** The evaluation spans multiple NLP tasks, from document-level classification (IMDB, Wikipedia) to word-level semantics (word analogy) and sentence-level similarity (SICK), demonstrating the versatility of the learned representations.
    *   **Analysis:** The results are well-analyzed. The authors not only report accuracy but also provide crucial timing comparisons (Table 2) that underscore the efficiency benefits of their approach. The qualitative analyses, such as the examination of word vector norms (Table 3) and the t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3), provide strong intuitive support for the paper's claims about the model's behavior. The discussion on how performance varies with document length (IMDB vs. SICK) is also insightful.

### **Additional Feedback**

The paper is very well-written and clear, but a few points could be further clarified or strengthened. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my overall positive assessment.

*   **Model Naming:** The name "Doc2VecC" is very close to "Doc2Vec," the common name for Paragraph Vectors. While descriptive, this could lead to confusion in the literature.
*   **Corruption Process Clarification:** The description of the corruption process in Section 3 could be more precise. The variable `x` is defined as a BoW vector (implying binary values), but Equation (1) seems to operate on a general vector `xd`. Clarifying whether `x` is a binary BoW or a term-frequency vector would be helpful.
*   **Connection to Denoising Autoencoders (DAE):** The paper claims that Doc2VecC reduces to a DAE if the local context is removed. This connection is not immediately obvious, as DAEs typically aim to reconstruct the entire input from a corrupted version, whereas this model predicts a single held-out word from the corrupted context. A brief elaboration on this analogy would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and uses `q=0.9` throughout. This is a very high corruption rate, meaning only 10% of the words contribute to the global context on average. An ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values would make this claim more robust and provide valuable practical guidance.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I have a few questions that would help clarify my understanding of the work:

1.  In the global context term `(1/T) * Ux̃` (Equation 2), the normalization is by `T`, the length of the original document. Did you experiment with normalizing by the number of non-zero elements in the corrupted vector `x̃` instead? It seems this might provide a more stable estimate of the mean embedding.
2.  Your hypothesis that Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on word analogy because the model relies on document vectors is very interesting. Is there any quantitative evidence from the trained PV model that might support this? For instance, do the document vectors have significantly larger norms or gradients compared to the word vectors during training?
3.  For the sentiment analysis task, the training protocol includes learning the representation on the test set, which is a form of transductive learning. While this is noted and follows a prior protocol, have you measured the performance drop when the test set is held out completely during representation learning for all models? This would give a clearer picture of the models' inductive capabilities.
