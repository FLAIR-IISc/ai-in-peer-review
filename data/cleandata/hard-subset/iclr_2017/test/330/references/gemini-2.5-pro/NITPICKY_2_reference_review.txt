### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel and efficient method, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), for learning distributed document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. Unlike prior work that post-processes pre-trained word embeddings, Doc2VecC is trained specifically to produce word embeddings that are effective under this averaging composition. The main technical innovation is a corruption process, where a random subset of words from the document is used to form a "global context" vector to aid in predicting a target word, alongside its local context. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption scheme is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings. This regularizer is shown to penalize frequent, uninformative words, thereby promoting more meaningful representations. The paper claims that the resulting model is highly efficient to train and test, and that its representations match or exceed the performance of state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors on tasks including sentiment analysis, document classification, and word analogy, particularly for longer documents.

### **Recommendation: Reject**

While the core idea of learning word embeddings specifically for averaging is appealing and the theoretical connection to regularization is insightful, I must recommend rejection. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **The paper is incomplete and not ready for review.** The manuscript is riddled with severe formatting and presentational issues, most critically the absence of nearly all figures (1, 3, 4) and tables (1, 2, 3, 4, 6). These missing elements contain the core empirical evidence needed to substantiate the paper's claims. Without them, it is impossible to verify the performance of the proposed method, its efficiency, or the effects of the proposed regularization.
2.  **The manuscript suffers from a lack of scientific rigor and meticulousness.** Beyond the missing components, the paper contains a multitude of minor but collectively significant flaws, including a typo in the title, inconsistent and non-standard mathematical notation, numerous grammatical errors, and a completely unformatted bibliography. These issues undermine confidence in the care with which the research was conducted and reported.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper in its current state is far below the quality standard expected for an ICLR submission. The number of minor flaws is substantial and, in aggregate, constitutes a major obstacle to a fair evaluation.

**Major Presentational Flaws:**
*   **Title:** The title itself appears to be a typo ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"), which is a serious oversight.
*   **Missing Figures and Tables:** The paper refers to Figures 1, 3, and 4, and Tables 1, 2, 3, 4, and 6, none of which are present in the document. These are not peripheral but central to the paper's argument. For instance, Table 1 is meant to show the main sentiment analysis results, Figure 3 is supposed to visualize the quality of document clusters, and Table 2 should substantiate the efficiency claims. Reviewing the paper without this information is an exercise in speculation.
*   **Bibliography:** The reference section is improperly formatted, containing raw BibTeX-like artifacts (e.g., "Blei et al\.,? \Q2003\E, Blei et al\.,"). This is unacceptable for a formal submission and makes it difficult to parse the cited literature.

**Methodological and Clarity Issues:**
*   **Notation:** The mathematical notation is confusing and non-standard. The use of `V>` for the output matrix and `v>w` to denote a row vector is highly unconventional; `V^T` and `v_w^T` are standard. This makes the equations in Sections 2 and 3 difficult to read.
*   **Negative Sampling:** In Equation (3), the negative sampling distribution `P_v` is described as "a uniform distribution over the terms in the vocabulary." The standard and empirically critical practice in word2vec is to use a unigram distribution raised to the 3/4 power. This deviation is not justified or even acknowledged, leaving the reader to wonder if it is a methodological choice or an error in the description. This detail is crucial for reproducibility and for understanding the model's behavior.
*   **Frequent Word Subsampling:** In Section 4.2, the authors state that their model "does not rely on this trick [subsampling]" because its regularizer naturally handles frequent words. However, they then state, "for all the numbers reported, we applied the trick." This is a direct contradiction that significantly weakens the claim about the practical benefit of the proposed regularizer. A rigorous experiment would compare Doc2VecC with and without subsampling to prove its robustness.

**Minor Errors and Typos:**
The paper is replete with minor errors that collectively indicate a lack of careful proofreading.
*   "well-know" (Sec 2) -> "well-known"
*   `vec(“women”)` (Sec 1) -> `vec(“woman”)`
*   "Denoising Autoencoders (DEA)" (Sec 4.1) -> The standard acronym is DAE.
*   Table numbering is inconsistent. Section 4.3 refers to Table 3 for regularization and then to Table 5 for word analogy results, but Table 5 is used for document classification in Section 4.4.
*   Numerous grammatical slips, such as "in together with" (Sec 1) and "randomly remove words" (Sec 1).

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The core concept of this paper is promising. Should the authors choose to revise and resubmit, I would strongly recommend the following:

1.  **Complete the Paper:** First and foremost, embed all figures and tables and ensure they are correctly numbered and referenced.
2.  **Thoroughly Proofread:** The entire manuscript needs to be edited for grammar, spelling, and clarity. The title must be corrected.
3.  **Standardize Notation:** Adopt standard mathematical notation for vectors, matrices, and transposes to improve readability.
4.  **Format the Bibliography:** Use a standard citation style and ensure the bibliography is correctly generated.
5.  **Clarify Methodological Details:** Explicitly state and justify any deviation from standard practices, particularly regarding the negative sampling distribution.
6.  **Strengthen Experimental Claims:** To support the claim about the data-dependent regularization making subsampling unnecessary, provide an ablation study showing results for your method and key baselines (e.g., Word2Vec+AVG) with and without frequent-word subsampling.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please clarify if you intentionally used a uniform distribution for negative sampling, or if this is a misstatement in the paper? If intentional, what was the motivation and how did it affect performance compared to the standard unigram-based distribution?
2.  Given your claim that the corruption-based regularization obviates the need for frequent word subsampling, why was this technique still applied in your experiments? Could you provide results for Doc2VecC without subsampling to demonstrate the effectiveness of your regularizer in isolation?
