### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel and efficient method for learning document vector representations, named Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The key contribution is a training procedure that makes this simple composition effective. Specifically, the model extends the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture from Word2Vec by incorporating a global document context, which is formed by averaging word embeddings. During training, this global context is "corrupted" by randomly dropping out a significant fraction of words from the document.

The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This implicit regularization penalizes the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words, forcing the model to learn more meaningful representations. The paper claims several advantages for Doc2VecC: 1) model complexity is independent of the training corpus size; 2) it is highly efficient for both training and inference; 3) it produces high-quality word and document embeddings that achieve state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful modification to the standard CBoW framework for learning document representations. The introduction of the corruption mechanism is well-motivated, and the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which connects this stochastic process to a data-dependent regularizer, provides a solid and convincing justification for the method's design and effectiveness.
2.  **Strong Empirical Evidence and Efficiency:** The method is comprehensively evaluated against a strong set of baselines across multiple diverse NLP tasks. The experimental results consistently demonstrate that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds the performance of more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, while being significantly more efficient, particularly for generating embeddings for unseen documents at test time. This combination of high performance and practical efficiency is a significant contribution.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant solution to the problem of scalable document representation learning. It successfully addresses the main drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec)—namely, parameter growth linear with corpus size and slow inference for new documents—by building upon the simple compositional principle of averaging word vectors. While averaging word vectors is a common baseline, this work is novel in that it integrates this composition directly into the learning objective through a principled mechanism.

The technical analysis in Section 3.1 is a core strength. By using a Taylor expansion, the authors demonstrate that the stochastic corruption is not merely a heuristic but approximates a specific form of regularization. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the norms of word vectors that are common across the corpus unless they are highly predictive for the local context prediction task. This provides a clear explanation for why the model learns to suppress uninformative words and produce robust document representations from a simple average. This analysis elevates the work from a simple empirical finding to a well-grounded method.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing.
*   On the **IMDB sentiment analysis** and **Wikipedia classification** tasks, Doc2VecC outperforms or performs on par with all baselines, including the significantly more complex and computationally expensive Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors. The efficiency gains highlighted in Table 2 are substantial; inference for 25,000 documents takes seconds for Doc2VecC versus several minutes for Paragraph Vectors.
*   The results on the **word analogy task** are particularly compelling. The finding that Doc2VecC produces word embeddings that are superior to those from the original Word2Vec model suggests that the global document context provided during training helps to learn richer word-level semantics. This demonstrates that the benefits of the model extend beyond just the final document vectors.
*   The discussion of performance on the **SICK dataset** is balanced. The authors acknowledge that LSTM-based models perform better on these short sentences and provide a plausible hypothesis for this difference, strengthening the overall analysis.

The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the experiments are well-designed to support the central claims of the paper.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions for improving the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   The derivation of the regularizer in Section 3.1 is insightful but could be made slightly clearer. For instance, explicitly writing out the full form of `R(uj)` by substituting the definition of `σw,c,x` would help readers grasp the dependencies more directly.
*   An ablation study could further strengthen the paper's claims. The model combines a local context (`Uct`) and a global, corrupted context (`Ux̃`). It would be interesting to see an experiment where the local context is removed, which the authors suggest would make the model similar to a Denoising Autoencoder. Quantifying the performance drop from removing the local context would precisely measure its contribution.
*   The hypothesis that Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on word analogy tasks because the model "relies mostly on the unique document vectors" is interesting. While plausible, this could be supported by analyzing the relative norms or update magnitudes of the word embeddings versus the document vectors during training in a PV model.
*   The chosen corruption rate of `q = 0.9` seems very high. While the paper states the model is not sensitive to this hyperparameter, a small plot showing performance on a validation set across a range of `q` values (e.g., from 0.1 to 0.9) would make this claim more robust and provide useful guidance for practitioners.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The theoretical analysis links the stochastic corruption to an explicit regularization term (Equation 7). Have you experimented with directly optimizing the regularized objective deterministically instead of using the stochastic approximation? It would be interesting to compare the final performance and training dynamics of these two approaches.
*   The analysis in Section 3.1 is based on a second-order approximation. Do you have any intuition or empirical evidence regarding the impact of higher-order terms? For example, does the high corruption rate (`q=0.9`) push the model into a regime where this approximation is more or less accurate?
*   You hypothesize that RNN-based models like Skip-thought vectors struggle with the long documents in the IMDB dataset. Do you think the corruption principle could be adapted to sequence models? For instance, could one train an LSTM encoder to reconstruct a target sentence from not only adjacent sentences but also a corrupted bag-of-words representation of the wider document context?
