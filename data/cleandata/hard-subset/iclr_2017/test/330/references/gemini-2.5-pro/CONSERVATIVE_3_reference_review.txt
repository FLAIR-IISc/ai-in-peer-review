### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a new model for learning distributed document representations. The method builds upon the CBOW architecture of Word2Vec by representing a document's global context as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The core contribution is the introduction of a corruption mechanism during training, where words are randomly dropped from the document context. The authors provide a theoretical analysis suggesting this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, uninformative words. The paper claims this approach is highly efficient in both training and inference compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), and that it achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Insufficient Ablation and Validation:** The central claim of the paper is that the proposed corruption mechanism is "critical to the performance." However, this claim is not substantiated with empirical evidence. The paper lacks a crucial ablation study comparing the proposed Doc2VecC model against an identical model trained without corruption (i.e., with a corruption rate `q=0`). Without this baseline, it is impossible to determine whether the performance gains come from the novel corruption scheme or simply from the base architecture of averaging word embeddings within the learning objective.
2.  **Limited Novelty and Inconsistent SOTA Claims:** The proposed method is a straightforward combination of two well-established ideas: representing documents by averaging word embeddings and using dropout/corruption as a regularizer. While the application is novel, the conceptual leap is modest. Furthermore, the claim of matching or beating the state-of-the-art is not consistently supported; while the model performs well on long-document classification, it underperforms strong baselines like LSTMs and Skip-thought vectors on the sentence-level semantic relatedness task (SICK dataset).

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary weakness of this work lies in its failure to rigorously validate its core contribution. The entire narrative is built around the benefits of the corruption model, which is theoretically justified as a data-dependent regularizer. While the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is interesting, it is based on a second-order Taylor approximation and is not a substitute for empirical validation. The most direct and necessary experiment would be to train a variant of Doc2VecC where the global context is the average of *all* word embeddings in the document (no corruption). This would isolate the effect of the corruption and demonstrate its actual impact. The paper instead compares its full model to external baselines, leaving the reader to speculate on the true source of the performance gains.

Secondly, the claim of state-of-the-art performance is overstated. In Section 4.5, on the SICK dataset, Doc2VecC is clearly outperformed by Skip-thought vectors and LSTM-based models. The authors hypothesize that this is due to document length, suggesting their model is better for long documents while LSTMs are better for short ones. This is a plausible explanation, but it undermines the claim of general-purpose, state-of-the-art performance. A state-of-the-art method should ideally be robust across different task types and data characteristics. The presented results suggest that Doc2VecC is a strong method for topic-centric tasks on long documents (like IMDB and Wikipedia classification) but is less effective for tasks requiring a nuanced understanding of sentence structure and short-term dependencies.

Finally, while the efficiency improvements over Paragraph Vectors are a significant practical advantage, the comparison to this key baseline could be more thorough. The Paragraph Vectors framework includes two architectures, PV-DM and PV-DBOW, which are often combined for best performance. The paper does not specify which variant was used for comparison, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the results. The surprisingly poor performance of Paragraph Vectors on the word analogy task is also concerning and could potentially be due to suboptimal implementation or hyperparameter choices rather than a fundamental flaw in the method itself.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

1.  **Ablation Study:** The most critical addition would be to include results for a "no-corruption" version of your model on all tasks. This would provide direct evidence for the utility of the corruption mechanism.
2.  **Sensitivity Analysis:** The paper states the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and then uses `q=0.9` throughout. A plot showing performance as a function of `q` on a validation set would be much more convincing and would help justify the choice of this high value.
3.  **Baseline Details:** For reproducibility and fairness, please provide more explicit details about the baseline implementations. Specifically for Paragraph Vectors, clarify which model (PV-DM, PV-DBOW, or combined) and what hyperparameters were used.
4.  **Refine Claims:** The claims in the introduction and conclusion should be moderated to more accurately reflect the empirical results. For example, instead of claiming to match or beat SOTA generally, it would be more accurate to state that the method is highly competitive or state-of-the-art on long-document classification tasks while being computationally efficient.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you provide the performance of a model variant trained without the corruption mechanism (`q=0`)? This seems essential to validate the central contribution of the paper.
2.  The performance of Paragraph Vectors on the word analogy task is substantially lower than even Word2Vec, which is surprising since its formulation (PV-DM) is a superset of the CBOW architecture. Can you elaborate on why this might be the case? Could this be an artifact of implementation or hyperparameter tuning?
3.  Your hypothesis regarding document length is compelling. Have you considered an analysis that might support this, for example, by stratifying the performance on the IMDB dataset by document length to see if Doc2VecC's advantage over baselines increases for longer reviews?
