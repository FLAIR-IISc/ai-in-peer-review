### Review of "Document Vector Representations through Corruption"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The novelty lies in the training procedure: inspired by the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) model, Doc2VecC predicts a target word using both its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. This global context is derived from an aggressively corrupted version of the full document, where a large fraction of words are randomly dropped.

The authors make several key claims:
1.  **Efficiency:** The model's complexity depends only on vocabulary size, not corpus size, and its architecture allows for very fast training and inference, significantly outperforming methods like Paragraph Vectors that require a separate inference step for unseen documents.
2.  **Principled Regularization:** The corruption mechanism is shown, via a second-order Taylor approximation, to be equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-informative words, forcing the model to learn more meaningful representations for rarer, more discriminative words.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Performance:** The resulting document vectors, formed by simple averaging, achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and word analogy tasks, while also performing reasonably on semantic relatedness (SICK).

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, elegant, and surprisingly effective method for document representation learning. The key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Significant Practical Advantages:** Doc2VecC directly addresses two major drawbacks of the widely used Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec): the linear growth of parameters with corpus size and the computationally expensive inference step for new documents. The proposed method is not only faster but also conceptually simpler, making it a highly attractive practical alternative.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support and Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors provide a comprehensive evaluation across a diverse set of tasks. The method shows clear improvements over strong baselines on multiple benchmarks. Crucially, the paper also honestly reports where the model underperforms (on the SICK dataset compared to LSTMs) and provides a plausible hypothesis, which strengthens the overall credibility of the work. The improvement on the word analogy task is particularly impressive, suggesting the learned word embeddings are of higher quality than those from standard Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's primary strength is the connection it draws between a simple heuristic (randomly dropping words) and a principled regularization effect. The derivation in Section 3.1 is compelling; it provides a solid theoretical grounding for why the corruption is not just a computational shortcut but an integral part of the learning objective that improves representation quality. This analysis elevates the work from a simple engineering trick to a well-motivated algorithmic contribution. The qualitative results in Table 3, which show that Doc2VecC successfully dampens the norms of uninformative stopwords while preserving those of rare, meaningful words, provide excellent empirical validation for this theoretical claim.

The experimental setup is thorough and convincing. The comparison against a wide range of baselines—from classic BoW to powerful sequential models like Skip-thought Vectors—is well-executed. The performance on the IMDB and Wikipedia datasets is particularly strong, demonstrating the model's ability to capture semantic content relevant for classification in both long and medium-length documents. The fact that Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors on the Wikipedia classification task (Figure 3, Table 5) is a significant result, as it challenges a popular and powerful baseline on its own turf while offering massive gains in efficiency.

Furthermore, the authors astutely diagnose the potential failure modes of competing models. For instance, the hypothesis that Skip-thought vectors and LSTMs struggle with the very long documents in the IMDB dataset is plausible and highlights a key advantage of Doc2VecC's order-agnostic averaging approach for such tasks. Conversely, acknowledging that LSTMs are superior on the SICK dataset, where word order and syntactic structure are more critical, shows a nuanced understanding of the model's limitations and its place within the broader landscape of NLP models.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or strengthened to further improve its impact. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

*   **Ablation Study on Corruption Rate (q):** The authors state they use `q=0.9` throughout the experiments and that the model is not sensitive to this hyperparameter. However, this is a central component of the method. An ablation study showing performance as `q` varies (e.g., `q` = 0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9) would be highly valuable. In particular, the `q=0` case would directly correspond to the model of Huang et al. (2012) and would explicitly quantify the performance gain attributable to the corruption scheme.
*   **Comparison with SIF:** A very strong baseline for document representation via weighted averaging is Smooth Inverse Frequency (SIF) by Arora et al. (2017). While this might be concurrent work, a comparison or at least a discussion of how Doc2VecC's learned implicit weighting compares to SIF's explicit weighting scheme would place the work in a more complete context.
*   **Interaction with Subsampling:** The paper notes that frequent word subsampling (from Mikolov et al.) is critical for Word2Vec+AVG but less so for Doc2VecC. This is a strong point. It would be compelling to see a direct comparison in a table: the performance of Word2Vec+AVG and Doc2VecC with and without this subsampling trick. This would further underscore the benefit of the intrinsic regularization.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  The core of the model is the interaction between the local context (`Uct`) and the global context (`1/T * Ux̃`). Could you elaborate on the intuition behind why combining these two signals is more effective than using either alone? Does the local context learn to capture syntax while the global context captures topic, or is the distinction more nuanced?
2.  The Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 is performed around the mean of the corruption distribution (`µx = x`). This is valid, but the corruption is very aggressive (`q=0.9`), meaning the corrupted vector `x̃` is very far from `x`. Does the high variance of the corruption distribution affect the validity or tightness of this second-order approximation?
3.  How does the model perform on extremely short texts, such as tweets or single sentences? The averaging approach seems robust for paragraphs, but for very short texts, the corruption might remove all or nearly all words, potentially making the global context signal vacuous. Have you explored this domain?
