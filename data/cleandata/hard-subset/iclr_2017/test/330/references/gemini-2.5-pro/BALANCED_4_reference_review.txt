### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and highly efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The approach builds upon the successful Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture of Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The key innovation is to represent the global document context not with a unique, learned document vector, but as a simple average of word embeddings from the document itself. Crucially, during training, this average is computed over a "corrupted" version of the document, where a large fraction of words are randomly dropped. The authors provide a compelling theoretical argument that this corruption scheme acts as an implicit, data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words. The paper claims that this method resolves major drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors, namely that its parameter count is independent of the corpus size and that inference for unseen documents is extremely fast. These claims are substantiated with extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, where Doc2VecC is shown to achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive results while being significantly more efficient than its predecessors.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Improvement in Efficiency and Scalability:** The proposed method, Doc2VecC, provides an elegant and practical solution to the key limitations of Paragraph Vectors. By eliminating the need for per-document parameters, it decouples model complexity from corpus size and dramatically speeds up inference for new documents, making high-quality document embeddings feasible for much larger-scale applications.
2.  **Strong and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The paper’s claims are exceptionally well-supported by a comprehensive and convincing set of experiments. The method is benchmarked against strong, relevant baselines across a diverse suite of NLP tasks. The results consistently demonstrate that Doc2VecC not only matches but often exceeds the performance of prior state-of-the-art methods, all while offering substantial efficiency gains.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strengths are numerous and justify a clear recommendation for acceptance.

*   **Novelty and Simplicity:** The core idea is both simple and powerful. Using a corrupted average of word embeddings to represent the global context is a clever way to enforce that the learned word embeddings are suitable for simple composition into meaningful document vectors. This avoids the overhead of learning explicit document vectors while still capturing document-level semantics.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The analysis in Section 3.1 is a significant contribution. By framing the corruption process as an approximate data-dependent regularizer via a Taylor expansion, the authors provide a principled explanation for *why* their method works. This analysis insightfully shows that the model learns to down-weight the embeddings of common but uninformative words, a claim further supported by the qualitative analysis in Table 3.
*   **Comprehensive Experimental Evaluation:** The empirical evaluation is thorough and rigorous.
    *   **Task Diversity:** The method is tested on sentiment analysis (IMDB), multi-class classification (Wikipedia), semantic relatedness (SICK), and even the quality of the underlying word vectors (word analogy task). This demonstrates the general applicability and robustness of the learned representations.
    *   **Strong Baselines:** The comparisons are made against the most relevant and powerful methods of the time, including Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors, and Denoising Autoencoders.
    *   **Convincing Results:** Doc2VecC consistently performs at or near the top across all tasks. The significant outperformance on the Wikipedia classification task and the improved word analogy scores are particularly impressive, suggesting the method learns both better document *and* word representations.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is very well-written and easy to follow. The motivation is clear, the method is explained precisely, and the experiments are well-documented. The authors also provide a link to the code, which is commendable and strengthens the contribution.

In short, this paper presents a complete piece of research: it identifies a clear problem, proposes an elegant solution, provides theoretical backing, and validates it with extensive and strong empirical evidence.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Analysis of Corruption Rate:** The paper fixes the corruption probability `q = 0.9` throughout the experiments, stating the model is not sensitive to it. While this may be true, an ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) on a validation set would have strengthened this claim and offered more practical guidance to researchers wishing to apply the method.
*   **Limitations of Theoretical Analysis:** The Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 is an approximation. It would be helpful to briefly discuss the potential limitations of this analysis. For instance, how does the quality of the approximation depend on the model's confidence (`σ`) or the dimensionality of the embeddings?
*   **Comparison to TF-IDF:** The regularizer's effect of penalizing frequent words is conceptually similar to IDF weighting. A more direct discussion or comparison of the learned implicit weights versus an explicit IDF weighting scheme could be very insightful.
*   **Visualizations:** The t-SNE plot in Figure 3 is effective. To make the comparison more direct, it would be even more compelling to show a side-by-side visualization including other strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors on the same dataset.

### Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  The regularization analysis in Table 3 is compelling. Have you investigated whether this effect (suppressing the norms of stop words) is the primary driver of the performance gains, or does the joint training of local and global contexts contribute equally? In essence, is Doc2VecC primarily a better way to learn word vectors for averaging, or is the prediction task itself crucial?
2.  You hypothesize that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on IMDB due to document length. Does Doc2VecC's performance show any degradation as document length increases significantly (e.g., to thousands of words)? Is there a point where the simple averaging of a corrupted sample fails to capture the global context?
3.  The theoretical analysis is performed on the log-likelihood objective `f(w, c, x̃)`. However, the model is trained in practice using negative sampling. How does the negative sampling approximation interact with the Taylor expansion analysis? Does the conclusion that corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer still hold in the same form?
4.  The paper focuses exclusively on the CBoW architecture. Have you experimented with a Skip-gram-style objective, where the computed document vector (and perhaps a target word) is used to predict surrounding context words? If so, how did its performance and learned representations compare to the CBoW-based Doc2VecC?
