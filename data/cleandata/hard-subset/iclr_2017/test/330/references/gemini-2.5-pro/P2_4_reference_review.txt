### ICLR Review

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The key innovation lies in the training process: to predict a target word, the model uses not only the local context (similar to Word2Vec's CBoW) but also a global context derived from a "corrupted" version of the full document, where words are randomly dropped out.

The authors provide a strong theoretical motivation for this corruption mechanism. By analyzing a second-order Taylor expansion of the loss function, they show that this stochastic corruption is equivalent to introducing a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, while preserving the embeddings of words that are rare or critical for making confident predictions. The paper supports its claims with a comprehensive set of experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC is not only computationally efficient but also achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to several state-of-the-art methods.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  **Elegant and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful method that effectively combines ideas from word embedding models (Word2Vec) and denoising autoencoders. The theoretical justification connecting the corruption process to a principled data-dependent regularizer is a significant contribution that elevates the method beyond a mere heuristic.
2.  **Strong and Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The authors have conducted a thorough empirical study across a diverse range of tasks and datasets. The comparison against a strong set of baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, convincingly demonstrates the effectiveness and, crucially, the efficiency of the proposed approach.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its clear motivation and rigorous execution. It identifies two key weaknesses in the highly related Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec) model: the linear growth of parameters with corpus size and the expensive inference required for unseen documents. Doc2VecC elegantly solves both problems. By representing the document as an average of word embeddings, the model complexity is tied only to the vocabulary size, and inference for a new document is a fast and simple averaging operation.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is the paper's cornerstone. It provides a clear and insightful explanation for *why* the corruption mechanism works. The derivation showing that corruption approximates a regularizer that penalizes common but uninformative words (e.g., stop words) is compelling. This provides a formal basis for a phenomenon that other models address with heuristics like frequent word subsampling. The subsequent experiment in Section 4.2 (Table 3), which shows that Doc2VecC successfully learns small-norm embeddings for stop words while preserving indicative rare words, provides excellent qualitative support for this theoretical claim.

The experimental results are strong across the board.
*   **Sentiment Analysis (IMDB):** Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance, matching Paragraph Vectors while being significantly faster at both training and, especially, inference time (Table 2). Its superiority over Word2Vec+IDF shows the benefit of integrating the compositionality objective directly into the training process.
*   **Document Classification (Wikipedia):** The method again outperforms all baselines by a clear margin across different embedding dimensions (Table 5). The t-SNE visualization (Figure 3) provides a compelling visual confirmation of the quality of the learned representations compared to Paragraph Vectors.
*   **Word Analogy Task:** A particularly interesting result is that the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are significantly better than those from both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. This suggests that forcing the model to reconstruct meaning from a corrupted global context encourages the learning of more robust and meaningful individual word vectors.
*   **Efficiency:** The practical benefits of the model's efficiency cannot be overstated. The dramatic difference in inference time compared to Paragraph Vectors and training time compared to Skip-thought Vectors makes Doc2VecC a highly practical tool for real-world applications.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are suggestions and do not detract from my recommendation to accept.

1.  **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and uses `q=0.9` throughout. This is a strong claim that would be more convincing with a small ablation study. Including a table or graph showing performance on a validation set for different values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9, 0.95) would add significant value.
2.  **Clarity on Global Context Normalization:** In Equation 2, the global context term is `1/T * U*x̃`, where `T` is the length of the *original* document. However, `x̃` is a corrupted vector with approximately `T*(1-q)` non-zero entries. This means the magnitude of the global context vector is dependent on the corruption rate `q`. Was this intentional? It seems more natural to normalize by the number of non-corrupted words, `T' = ||x̃||_0`. A brief clarification on this design choice would be helpful.
3.  **Comparison to Denoising Autoencoders:** The paper positions the method as an improvement over Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) by including local context. The experimental comparison shows Doc2VecC outperforming DEA. However, the DEA baseline is also a custom implementation adapted for large vocabularies. It would be useful to elaborate slightly more on this baseline to ensure the comparison is on equal footing (e.g., were the hyperparameters for DEA tuned as carefully as for Doc2VecC?).

**Questions for Authors**

To clarify my understanding and potentially strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following:

1.  The theoretical derivation of the regularizer in Section 3.1 is based on a second-order approximation. Have you considered directly optimizing the regularized objective in Equation 7 without using the stochastic corruption process during training? How might this deterministic approach compare in terms of performance and computational cost?
2.  The paper focuses exclusively on the CBoW architecture. Have you experimented with a Skipgram-style variant, where the combined document and context vectors are used to predict multiple words in a window? If so, does the corruption-as-regularization argument still apply, and how does the performance compare?
3.  In the semantic relatedness task, you mention fine-tuning the model on the SICK dataset without using the relatedness scores. Could you elaborate on this fine-tuning step? Was the model trained with the same corruption objective on the SICK sentences, and how much did this step contribute to the final performance?
