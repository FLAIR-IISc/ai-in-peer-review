### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a novel and efficient method, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The key contribution is a training procedure that explicitly enforces this property. During training, the model predicts a target word based on its local context (similar to Word2Vec's CBoW) and a global context vector. Crucially, this global context vector is derived from the average of word embeddings from a "corrupted" version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis, framing this corruption process as a form of data-dependent regularization that penalizes frequent, uninformative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks that Doc2VecC is not only significantly more efficient to train and use for inference than strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors, but also achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong paper that presents a simple, elegant, and effective solution to a well-known problem. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant Practical Improvements:** The proposed method directly addresses major scalability and efficiency limitations of the influential Paragraph Vector model. By avoiding per-document parameters, the model complexity is decoupled from the corpus size, and the inference time for new documents is reduced by orders of magnitude, making it far more practical for real-world applications.
2.  **Well-Supported Novelty:** The central idea of using corruption is not just a heuristic; it is supported by a solid theoretical analysis that interprets it as a data-dependent regularizer. This theoretical insight is complemented by a comprehensive and convincing set of empirical results across four different tasks, demonstrating that the method improves not only efficiency but also the quality of both document and word representations.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

*   **Methodological Clarity and Simplicity:** The proposed Doc2VecC model is an elegant extension of the well-understood Word2Vec architecture. This simplicity is a major advantage, making the method easy to understand, implement, and likely to be widely adopted. It successfully removes the main bottlenecks of Paragraph Vectors (the per-document vector matrix and the expensive inference step) while retaining the core idea of learning from both local and global context.

*   **Theoretical Grounding:** Section 3.1 provides a principled justification for the corruption mechanism. By using a second-order Taylor expansion, the authors demonstrate that the training objective implicitly optimizes a loss function that includes a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, an effect that is qualitatively validated in Table 3. This analysis elevates the method from a clever engineering trick to a theoretically motivated technique.

*   **Comprehensive and Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The authors evaluate their model thoroughly on a diverse set of tasks, which strengthens the paper's claims of generality.
    *   On document-level tasks (IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia classification), Doc2VecC consistently outperforms strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Word2Vec+IDF averaging schemes. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide a clear qualitative illustration of the learned representations' superiority.
    *   Perhaps most surprisingly, the experiments on the word analogy task (Section 4.3) show that the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are substantially better than those from the standard Word2Vec model. This is a significant result, suggesting that the global context signal forces the model to learn richer, more robust word semantics.
    *   The results on the SICK dataset are presented with nuance. While the model is slightly outperformed by LSTM-based methods, the authors provide a reasonable hypothesis regarding document length and correctly highlight the massive efficiency advantage of their method over competitors like Skip-thought vectors.

*   **Efficiency:** The practical benefits of the model are clearly demonstrated in Table 2. The training is much faster than Paragraph Vectors, and the inference time for unseen documents is drastically reduced from minutes to seconds, which is a critical advantage for many applications.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help further improve its clarity and impact. These are suggestions for the authors and did not negatively influence my decision.

*   **Title:** The title in the provided PDF, "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION", appears to be a typo or an incomplete phrase. I assume the intended title is something like "DOCUMENT EMBEDDINGS THROUGH CORRUPTION" or similar. This should be corrected for the final version.
*   **Ablation on Corruption Rate:** The paper states that a corruption rate of `q = 0.9` was used throughout and that the model is "not sensitive" to this hyperparameter. The paper would be strengthened by including a small ablation study or a plot showing performance across a range of `q` values. This would provide concrete evidence for the claim of insensitivity and give practitioners more confidence in applying the method.
*   **Limitations of Theoretical Analysis:** The theoretical justification relies on a second-order Taylor approximation. While elegant, it would be beneficial to briefly discuss the potential limitations of this approximation, especially given the high-dimensional and sparse nature of the document vectors `x`.
*   **Comparison to Dropout:** The corruption mechanism is related to dropout. A brief discussion comparing and contrasting the proposed "mask-out/drop-out" corruption with applying standard dropout directly to the averaged word embedding layer could provide additional insight.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions in their rebuttal:

1.  The improvement on the word analogy task over the standard Word2Vec model is impressive. Do you have further intuition as to why adding a corrupted global context signal helps learn qualitatively better *individual* word vectors? Does the global signal force the model to learn word representations that are less ambiguous or more context-independent?
2.  The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q". Could you provide some empirical data (e.g., a table or plot of performance vs. q on a validation set) to support this claim?
3.  The theoretical analysis is a key strength of the paper. Could you comment on how tight you believe the second-order approximation is in practice for this problem? Does the empirical performance suggest the approximation holds well, or are there likely other factors at play that the analysis does not capture?
