- Introduces Doc2VecC: document vectors as averages of word embeddings with random-word corruption during training.
- Claims benefits: model complexity depends only on vocabulary, efficient training/testing, implicit data-dependent regularization, and simple generation of document representations.
- Empirical claim: matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness while being much faster.
- Decision: Accept — method is simple, theoretically grounded, and empirically strong.
- Theoretical support: corruption analyzed via Taylor expansion shows it acts as data-dependent regularization suppressing common/uninformative words.
- Strong empirical validation: thorough experiments across three tasks, ablation on corruption effect, and a word analogy evaluation.
- Practical impact: much smaller vector sizes and large test-time speedups (e.g., 7s vs 4min for Paragraph Vectors on IMDB).
- Clear positioning: effectively contrasted with Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and other document representation methods.
- Improvement — novelty: paper should more explicitly contrast the corruption mechanism with standard dropout in NLP.
- Improvement — limitations: paper should discuss scenarios where Doc2VecC might underperform (e.g., very short documents).
- Improvement — visualization: t-SNE plot needs clearer labels and more detailed cluster interpretation.
- Improvement — hyperparameter choice: clarify why corruption rate q=0.9 was used and how performance varies with q.
- Question: clarify the relationship to Denoising Autoencoders and how Doc2VecC's corruption differs from DEA corruption.
- Question: explain how the regularization term R(uj) specifically targets frequent/common words as shown in Table 3.
- Question: why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task despite capturing document-level semantics?
