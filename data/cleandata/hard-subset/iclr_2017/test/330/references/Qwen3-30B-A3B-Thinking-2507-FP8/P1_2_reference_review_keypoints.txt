- Proposes Doc2VecC: document representations as averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training.
- Corruption acts as data-dependent regularization, favoring informative words and suppressing common, non-discriminative words.
- Provides theoretical justification showing corruption introduces a regularization term that downweights frequent/uninformative word embeddings.
- Empirically matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia classification, and SICK semantic relatedness tasks.
- Demonstrates significant computational advantages in training and testing efficiency over Paragraph Vectors and other methods.
- Recommends Accept due to a well-motivated, simple, and effective approach addressing a meaningful problem.
- Corruption serves a dual purpose: improves performance via regularization and enables efficient average-based document encoding.
- Theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) convincingly explains why the corruption regularization favors rare informative words.
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive with appropriate baselines and informative ablation studies (Tables 2–3).
- Time measurements (e.g., 25k test docs in 7s vs 4m17s for Paragraph Vectors) strongly support efficiency claims.
- Paper is well-situated in related work and is clearly written and organized.
- Suggests clarifying the relationship and differences between Doc2VecC and Denoising Autoencoders (DEA).
- Requests clearer explanation of the claim that parameter updates increase with surviving words despite corruption.
- Asks to address the apparent contradiction regarding subsampling: claim of not relying on it vs using it in experiments.
- Recommends adding a formal time/space complexity analysis to strengthen efficiency claims.
- Proposes including experiments or analysis on document-length sensitivity compared to other methods.
- Asks for intuition on why the regularization term depends on prediction confidence (σ(1−σ)) and why it is small for confident predictions.
- Requests more detailed analysis of sensitivity to corruption rate q, especially at extreme values (e.g., 0.1 and 0.99).
- Seeks clarification of the exact training-objective relationship between Doc2VecC (without context) and DEA.
- Asks whether differences in paragraph length between datasets (IMDB vs SICK) might explain relative performance versus LSTM-based methods.
