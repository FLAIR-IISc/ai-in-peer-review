

# Review of "Doc2VecC: Document Representation through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel approach for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is a corruption mechanism during training that randomly removes words from documents, which the authors theoretically justify as a form of data-dependent regularization. This regularization suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words while favoring rare or informative ones. The paper claims Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while offering significant efficiency advantages in both training and inference compared to existing methods like Paragraph Vectors. The authors provide theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as regularization, along with extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to document representation learning with a theoretically grounded and empirically validated approach that addresses limitations of existing methods. The key reasons for acceptance are: 1) The corruption mechanism provides a theoretically sound justification for using simple averaging of word embeddings, which is a significant conceptual contribution; 2) The empirical results consistently demonstrate superior performance across multiple document representation tasks while offering substantial efficiency advantages.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper clearly identifies a well-established problem in document representation learning: Paragraph Vectors (PV) require a document-specific vector for each document, resulting in model complexity that scales with corpus size, making them inefficient for large datasets and requiring expensive inference for unseen documents. The authors propose a simple yet effective solution that uses a corruption mechanism during training to implicitly learn document representations through averaging word embeddings.

The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as data-dependent regularization is rigorous. The authors derive the relationship between the corruption process and the regularization term, showing how it naturally suppresses embeddings of common words while preserving those of informative ones. This theoretical foundation is a significant contribution that goes beyond the empirical results.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The paper evaluates Doc2VecC across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) using multiple datasets. The results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming strong baselines including Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, and Skip-thought Vectors. The efficiency claims are well-supported by detailed training and inference time measurements, showing Doc2VecC significantly outperforms Paragraph Vectors in both training and test time.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is well-written and makes a strong contribution, but could be improved with:

1. More detailed ablation studies on the corruption rate parameter (q). The paper mentions that Doc2VecC is not sensitive to q, but provides no evidence of how performance varies across different corruption rates.

2. Clarification on the relationship between Doc2VecC and Denoising Autoencoders (DEA). The paper states that Doc2VecC "reduces to DEA if the local context words are removed," but the connection isn't clearly explained.

3. Additional analysis of why Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors on the word analogy task, particularly since Paragraph Vectors were designed to capture document-level semantics.

4. Comparison with more recent document representation methods, such as those based on BERT or other contextualized embeddings, to better establish the relevance of the work in the current landscape.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Can you provide additional evidence beyond the empirical results to support this claim, such as visualizations or analysis of how the corruption mechanism affects word embedding quality?

2. In the corruption mechanism, the authors use a uniform corruption rate q across all words. How would the performance change if the corruption rate were made word-type dependent (e.g., higher for stop words, lower for content words)?

3. The paper states that Doc2VecC "significantly outperforms the winning solutions of the competition" on the SemEval task. Could you clarify whether the winning solutions used the same training protocol as Doc2VecC (e.g., training on the full book corpus followed by fine-tuning on SICK), or if there were differences in the experimental setup that might affect the comparison?
