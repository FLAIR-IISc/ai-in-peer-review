

# Review for "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

## 1. Summary of Claims/Contributions

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel approach for document representation learning that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, with a key innovation being the incorporation of a corruption mechanism during training. The authors claim this approach offers several advantages over existing methods: (1) model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (unlike Paragraph Vectors), (2) it's computationally efficient to train and test, (3) it implicitly introduces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words, and (4) it achieves competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The core insight is that document-level semantics can be effectively captured through averaging word embeddings when combined with a corruption mechanism that provides meaningful regularization.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated approach that is theoretically sound and empirically validated. The key reasons for acceptance are the strong theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization and the consistent empirical performance across multiple tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing the three key review questions. The problem tackled is clearly identified: the inefficiency of existing document representation methods like Paragraph Vectors, which require a parameter per document and have expensive inference. The motivation is well-placed in the literature, with careful comparison to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and related work on denoising autoencoders.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is particularly strong. The authors rigorously demonstrate that the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization, showing how it suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words. This theoretical justification is well-supported by the experimental results in Section 4.2, where the authors show that Doc2VecC successfully dampens representations of stop words while preserving meaningful embeddings for sentiment-indicative words.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, with experiments across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) on standard datasets. The results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors, while being significantly more efficient. The ablation studies (e.g., Table 3 on word embeddings, Table 5 on document classification) provide convincing evidence for the claims. The paper also carefully addresses computational efficiency, with detailed timing comparisons showing Doc2VecC's advantages over Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The title "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" appears to be a typo that should be corrected to "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" throughout the paper.
- While the paper mentions the connection to denoising autoencoders, a more explicit discussion of how Doc2VecC relates to this established paradigm would strengthen the theoretical foundation.
- The paper could benefit from more detailed analysis of the corruption rate parameter q. The current experiments use q=0.9 without exploring how performance varies across different corruption rates.
- The explanation for why Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on IMDB compared to SICK could be more nuanced, particularly regarding the relationship between sentence length and model architecture.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Table 1, Doc2VecC achieves similar performance to Paragraph Vectors but with much better efficiency. Given that Paragraph Vectors have a dedicated document vector while Doc2VecC uses simple averaging, could you elaborate on why the corruption mechanism doesn't harm performance while improving efficiency?

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Could you provide more specific examples or analysis showing how the corruption mechanism specifically improves the semantic quality of the averaged representations compared to standard averaging?

3. In the semantic relatedness task (Table 6), Doc2VecC performs slightly worse than LSTM-based methods on SICK but better on IMDB. The authors hypothesize this is due to sentence length differences. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of sentence length distributions across these datasets and how they might specifically impact the performance of different architectures?
