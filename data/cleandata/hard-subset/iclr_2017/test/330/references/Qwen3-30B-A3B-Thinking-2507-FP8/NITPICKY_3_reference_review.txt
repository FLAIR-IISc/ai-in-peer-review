

# Review for "Doc2VecC: Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a new method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings with a corruption mechanism during training. The authors claim that their approach achieves better performance than existing methods (including Paragraph Vectors and Word2Vec+IDF) on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being more efficient to train and test. They argue that their corruption mechanism introduces a data-dependent regularization that favors informative words and suppresses common, non-discriminative words.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple methodological inconsistencies, unclear theoretical claims, and insufficient empirical validation that undermine its core contributions.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Critical Methodological Inconsistency**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" (Section 4.2). However, DEA reconstructs the original document from corrupted versions, while Doc2VecC uses the corrupted document to predict target words. The authors provide no evidence of this equivalence, which is a fundamental claim about their method's relationship to prior work. This undermines their theoretical foundation.

2. **Mathematical Errors in Theoretical Justification**: In Section 3.1, the authors derive that Doc2VecC minimizes a loss function with a data-dependent regularization. However, their derivation contains a critical error: they state "the linear term disappears as Ep(x̃|x)[x̃ − µx] = 0" but fail to demonstrate this for their specific corruption model (equation 1). The unbiased corruption they define (x̃d = 0 with probability q, xd/(1-q) otherwise) does not satisfy Ep(x̃|x)[x̃] = x, making their derivation mathematically unsound.

3. **Inadequate Experimental Validation**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "significantly outperforms" Word2Vec+IDF by 15% on sentiment analysis (Table 1), but the table is missing the actual numbers. Without these numbers, it's impossible to verify their claims. Additionally, the paper uses a cutoff of 10 for vocabulary size without justification, which could artificially boost results for Doc2VecC (which supposedly handles frequent words better).

4. **Problematic Baseline Comparisons**: The Skip-thought vectors comparison is unfair because the authors used a pre-trained model on a different dataset (book corpus) rather than training on the IMDB data, while other methods were trained on the same data. This undermines their claim that Doc2VecC "outperforms" Skip-thought vectors.

5. **Title and Terminology Errors**: The title "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" is clearly a typo for "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" or "DOC2VEC THROUGH CORRUPTION." This basic error in the title and inconsistent use of "Doc2VecC" vs. "Doc2Vec" throughout the paper suggests carelessness.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the Corruption Mechanism**: The paper should provide a clear mathematical justification for why their corruption model is unbiased and how it differs from standard dropout or denoising autoencoders. The claim that it's "unbiased" needs to be rigorously demonstrated.

2. **Provide Complete Results**: Include all numerical results in tables, not just qualitative descriptions. The missing numbers in Table 1 prevent proper evaluation of the main claim.

3. **Justify Vocabulary Cutoff**: Explain why a cutoff of 10 was chosen for vocabulary size and how this affects the comparison between methods.

4. **Fix Terminology**: Correct the title and ensure consistent use of "Doc2VecC" throughout the paper.

5. **Re-evaluate Baseline Comparisons**: Train all methods on the same dataset and protocol to ensure fair comparisons, particularly for Skip-thought vectors.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide the exact numerical results for the sentiment analysis task (Table 1) to verify the claimed 15% improvement over BoW?

2. Please clarify the mathematical basis for the claim that Ep(x̃|x)[x̃] = x for your corruption model. Your equation (1) does not satisfy this property.

3. Why did you use a vocabulary cutoff of 10 for the word analogy task (Section 4.3), and how does this affect the comparison between methods?

4. Can you provide a theoretical justification for why Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders when local context is removed, as claimed in Section 4.2?

5. How does your corruption mechanism differ from standard dropout training, and why is your specific formulation necessary for the observed improvements?

The paper presents an interesting idea but is undermined by methodological errors, incomplete results, and unclear claims. Without addressing these fundamental issues, the paper cannot be accepted.
