

# Review for "Document Vector through Corruption"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning method that represents documents as the average of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption mechanism during training. The authors claim this approach is more efficient than existing methods like Paragraph Vectors, naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings, and introduces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses common but uninformative words. The method is evaluated on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, with claims of state-of-the-art performance.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains fundamental technical flaws in the theoretical derivation of the regularization mechanism, and the experimental results are not properly presented, making it impossible to verify the claims.

## Supporting Arguments
1. **Fundamental flaw in the theoretical derivation**: The paper's central claim that the corruption mechanism introduces "data-dependent regularization" (Section 3.1) is based on a flawed Taylor expansion argument. The authors approximate f(w,c,x̃) with a Taylor expansion around the mean of the corruption distribution, but then incorrectly compute the expectation over x̃. Specifically, the derivation in equation (6) claims that Ep(x̃|x)[f(w,c,x̃)] ≈ f(w,c,x) + 1/2 tr(Σx∇²x̃f), but this is mathematically incorrect. The Taylor expansion should be around x̃ = µx, not x, and the subsequent substitution of x for µx (the mean of the corrupting distribution) is inconsistent with the definition of unbiased corruption. This fundamental error invalidates the paper's main theoretical contribution.

2. **Incorrect corruption mechanism**: The corruption mechanism defined in equation (1) is mathematically problematic. For BoW vectors (where xj ∈ {0,1}), x̃j = xj/(1-q) when not corrupted, which means x̃j can exceed 1 (e.g., when q=0.9, x̃j=10 when xj=1). This violates the definition of a BoW vector and makes the global context term (1/T)Ux̃ meaningless, as it would scale word embeddings by a factor larger than 1. The paper claims "unbiased corruption" but fails to properly maintain the expected value of x̃ = x, which is a basic requirement for dropout.

3. **Inconsistent and incomplete empirical presentation**: The paper claims results in "Table 1" and "Table 2" but does not include these tables in the text. Without seeing the actual numbers, it's impossible to verify claims that Doc2VecC "achieves similar or even better performance than Paragraph Vectors" on IMDB sentiment analysis or that "Doc2VecC significantly outperforms the winning solutions of the competition" on SemEval 2014. The lack of presented results undermines the paper's empirical claims.

4. **Inconsistent citation and formatting**: The paper has numerous citation inconsistencies (e.g., "Mikolov & Dean, 2013" vs. "Mikolov et al., 2013a"), inconsistent capitalization of the model name "Document Vector through Corruption" (Doc2VecC), and grammatical errors. These suggest poor attention to detail, which is particularly concerning for a paper that claims to be "meticulous" in its approach.

## Additional Feedback
1. The claim that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" is unsupported by the equations. The DEA formulation in the paper is inconsistent with standard DEA, which reconstructs the original document from the corrupted version using a decoder, not a neural network with a global context term.

2. The paper's assertion that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training" is contradicted by the corruption mechanism, which uses a corrupted document to form the global context rather than the original document.

3. The claim about "data-dependent regularization" is not substantiated by proper analysis of how the regularization term behaves in practice. The paper shows some words with small l2 norms (Table 3) but doesn't demonstrate how this affects model performance.

4. The corruption rate q=0.9 (90% of words removed) is extremely high and likely detrimental to performance, yet the paper claims this doesn't affect performance. The paper should provide evidence for this claim with results across different corruption rates.

## Clarifying Questions
1. Could the authors provide the actual numbers from Tables 1-5 to verify the performance claims?
2. How does the corruption mechanism in equation (1) maintain the expected value of x̃ = x, given that x̃j = xj/(1-q) when not corrupted?
3. Why does the paper claim Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders" when DEA reconstructs the original document from the corrupted version, while Doc2VecC uses the corrupted version as input to a prediction task?
4. How does the global context term (1/T)Ux̃ in equation (2) make sense when x̃ is a corrupted BoW vector (with entries potentially >1) and T is the original document length?

The paper's fundamental theoretical flaws and incomplete empirical presentation prevent me from recommending acceptance. The authors should address these issues before considering resubmission.
