

# Peer Review for "Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel model for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a key innovation being a corruption mechanism during training. The authors observe that linear combinations of word embeddings can capture document semantics (citing Mikolov et al., 2013b), and propose to enforce this property during learning through a corruption process that randomly removes words from documents. This corruption mechanism is theoretically shown to provide a data-dependent regularization that favors rare, informative words while suppressing embeddings of common, uninformative words. The paper claims Doc2VecC has several advantages: model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (depending only on vocabulary size), it's efficient to train and test, and it achieves competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks compared to existing approaches.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in document representation learning with a simple yet effective approach. The core idea of using a corruption mechanism to implicitly regularize word embeddings during document representation learning is both novel and well-motivated. The authors clearly position their work in the literature by building upon Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, and they provide a strong theoretical justification for their corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization (Section 3.1).

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed across three different tasks: sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK). The results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming or matching existing baselines. The paper's claim that Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training" is well-supported by the results (Table 1), particularly when compared to Word2Vec+IDF which requires post-processing.

The paper also provides valuable insights into why certain methods perform poorly on specific tasks (e.g., Skip-thought vectors' poor performance on IMDB due to paragraph length), demonstrating a deep understanding of the problem space. The efficiency gains are substantial - Doc2VecC trains significantly faster than Paragraph Vectors and achieves comparable or better performance with much simpler test-time inference.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following:

1. **Title and Terminology**: The title "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" appears to be a typo (likely "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). This should be corrected immediately. Additionally, the "C" in Doc2VecC should be explicitly defined early in the paper.

2. **Corruption Rate Justification**: The paper uses q = 0.9 throughout without justification. An ablation study showing how different corruption rates affect performance would strengthen the paper and provide readers with practical guidance.

3. **Clarification on DEA Connection**: The claim that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" needs clearer explanation. The paper should explicitly state how the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC relates to the reconstruction objective in DEA.

4. **Quantitative Comparison**: When stating that Doc2VecC "beats the BOW representation by 15%," it would be helpful to include the exact error rates to make the comparison more concrete.

5. **Sub-sampling Comparison**: The paper claims Doc2VecC doesn't rely on subsampling of frequent words like Word2Vec+AVG does. A more detailed comparison of how the corruption mechanism achieves similar effects to subsampling would strengthen this claim.

## Questions for Authors

1. In Section 3.1, the paper states that "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃." This seems counterintuitive - wouldn't removing words reduce the number of parameters to update? Could you clarify this point?

2. In Table 5, the paper claims Doc2VecC significantly outperforms Word2Vec across all subtasks, but the table only shows results for the Wikipedia document classification task. Could you clarify which table shows the word analogy results mentioned in Section 4.3?

3. The paper states that "Doc2VecC naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative." How does this differ from the standard subsampling technique used in Word2Vec training? A more detailed comparison would help readers understand the novelty of this regularization effect.

4. In the sentiment analysis experiments, the paper reports that "Doc2VecC achieved similar or even better performance than Paragraph Vectors." However, Table 1 shows a 0.2% improvement for Doc2VecC over Paragraph Vectors. Could you provide more context about whether this difference is statistically significant?

The paper makes a strong contribution to document representation learning with a simple yet effective approach. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism, combined with strong empirical results across multiple tasks, makes this a valuable contribution to the field. With the minor clarifications suggested above, the paper would be suitable for publication at ICLR.
