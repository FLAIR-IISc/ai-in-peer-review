

# Review

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes "Doc2VecC" (Document Vector through Corruption), a method for document representation learning that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings learned through a corrupted version of the document. The authors claim this approach: (1) has model complexity decoupled from corpus size, (2) can be trained efficiently like Word2Vec, (3) introduces a data-dependent regularization favoring rare/informative words, and (4) outperforms established methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection

1. **Lack of Novelty and Insufficient Justification for Core Innovation**: The core idea of representing documents as averages of word embeddings has been previously proposed by Huang et al. (2012). The paper's main contribution—introducing a corruption mechanism—appears to be primarily a mathematical device to derive a regularization term rather than a meaningful data augmentation technique. The authors do not convincingly demonstrate why this specific corruption mechanism (removing 90% of words) is necessary for the observed performance improvements. The paper fails to show that simply averaging word embeddings without corruption would not yield comparable results, which would be the most straightforward baseline.

2. **Insufficient Evidence for Claims**: The empirical results show modest performance gains over baselines (typically 1-2% on sentiment analysis, 1-3% on document classification), but the paper does not provide sufficient evidence that the corruption mechanism is the true source of these gains. Crucially, the paper does not include a comparison to the simple average of word embeddings (without corruption) as a baseline. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the corruption mechanism is actually beneficial or whether the performance gains are simply due to the aggressive corruption rate (q=0.9) causing the model to focus on the most informative words. The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is mathematically complex but fails to clearly connect to the empirical results.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's main claim—that Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training—lacks supporting evidence. The authors present results showing Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, but they don't demonstrate that Doc2VecC's training process actually leads to better document representations than Word2Vec+IDF. The comparison is presented as evidence for their claim, but it's circular reasoning: they claim Doc2VecC enforces document semantics during training, then show it outperforms a method that doesn't enforce this, but don't prove the enforcement actually occurs.

The paper also fails to properly benchmark against more recent state-of-the-art methods. For example, it compares to Paragraph Vectors (2014) but not to more recent methods like BERT, which would be the natural comparison point for document representation in 2017. The paper claims Doc2VecC "outperforms the state-of-the-art," but the baselines are outdated.

The corruption rate (q=0.9) is extremely high—removing 90% of words—and the paper doesn't justify why this specific value is optimal or how it would generalize to other datasets. The paper states "we found that both models are not sensitive to the corruption rate q in the noise model," but this claim is not substantiated with ablation studies across different corruption rates.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Critical Missing Baseline**: The most important missing comparison is to the simple average of word embeddings (without corruption). This would demonstrate whether the corruption mechanism is truly necessary for the performance gains or if the high corruption rate is simply causing the model to focus on more informative words.

2. **Clarify the Role of Corruption**: The paper should more clearly explain why randomly removing 90% of words is a meaningful data augmentation technique rather than just a mathematical trick. What is the intuition behind this specific form of corruption?

3. **Compare to Modern Methods**: The paper should compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods for document representation, such as those using recurrent or transformer architectures, which were becoming popular in 2017.

4. **Ablation Studies**: Include ablation studies showing the effect of different corruption rates (q) on performance, which would help determine whether the specific value of q=0.9 is critical or if the performance gains are simply due to the high level of corruption.

## Questions for Authors

1. Why did you choose q=0.9 as the corruption rate? What would happen if you used a lower corruption rate (e.g., q=0.5 or q=0.7)? How sensitive is the performance to this parameter?

2. Can you provide results for the simple average of word embeddings (without corruption) as a baseline? How does this compare to Doc2VecC?

3. How does Doc2VecC compare to more recent methods like fastText or GloVe for document representation, which were becoming widely adopted around 2017?

4. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings. What evidence do you have that the document representations learned by Doc2VecC are actually better at capturing document semantics than those learned by other methods?
