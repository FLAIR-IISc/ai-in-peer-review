

1. Summary: The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning method that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training, which the authors theoretically analyze as introducing a data-dependent regularization. This regularization suppresses embeddings of common but non-discriminative words while preserving those of rare or informative words. The approach addresses limitations of Paragraph Vectors (model complexity growing with corpus size, expensive inference for unseen documents) by decoupling model complexity from corpus size and enabling efficient test-time representation generation. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance to state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated approach with solid empirical results that clearly demonstrate the advantages of the corruption mechanism. The method provides a clear improvement over existing document representation techniques with significant efficiency benefits.

3. Supporting arguments:
The paper correctly identifies the limitations of Paragraph Vectors (model complexity growing with corpus size, expensive inference for unseen documents) and proposes a solution that addresses these issues. The theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularization is sound and well-explained, with clear mathematical derivation showing how the corruption process naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings. The empirical evaluation across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) consistently shows Doc2VecC outperforming or matching baselines while being more efficient. The results are statistically significant and the experimental setup is appropriate, using standard datasets and evaluation protocols. The paper also provides valuable insights into the regularization effect through experiments comparing word embedding norms across methods.

4. Additional feedback:
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the corruption rate q and the regularization effect, including how the optimal q varies across different datasets.
- While the theoretical analysis is strong, additional empirical evidence supporting the claim that the corruption mechanism specifically benefits document representation (beyond the regularization effect) would strengthen the argument.
- The visualization in Figure 3 is helpful but could be supplemented with quantitative metrics measuring clustering quality to better support the claim that Doc2VecC produces more meaningful document representations.

5. Questions for authors:
- How does the performance of Doc2VecC vary with different corruption rates q across the different datasets used in the experiments? What is the optimal q for each task?
- The paper shows that Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, but how does it compare to other methods that use averaging of word embeddings with different weighting schemes (e.g., TF-IDF, attention-based weighting)?
- The paper states that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but the experimental results show that Doc2VecC outperforms DEA. Could you clarify the relationship between these two approaches and why the inclusion of local context leads to better performance?
