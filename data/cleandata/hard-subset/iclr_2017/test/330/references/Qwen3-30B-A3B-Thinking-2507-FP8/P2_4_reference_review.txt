

# ICLR Review: "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel method for learning document representations that addresses key limitations of existing approaches. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. This approach offers several advantages over prior work: (1) model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (only dependent on vocabulary size), (2) it's efficient to train and test, (3) it implicitly introduces data-dependent regularization that favors informative words, and (4) document representations can be generated by simply averaging word embeddings. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance to state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly faster to train and test.

## 2. Decision

**Accept.** The paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of document representation learning through a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically validated approach. The key reasons for acceptance are the method's simplicity combined with strong empirical results, and the insightful theoretical explanation of the corruption mechanism as data-dependent regularization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in several critical areas:

**Strong Theoretical Foundation:** The authors provide a rigorous theoretical justification for their corruption mechanism, showing it acts as a data-dependent regularization that naturally suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words. This is a significant contribution beyond just proposing a new method. The Taylor expansion analysis (Section 3.1) is particularly convincing, demonstrating how the corruption mechanism leads to a specific regularization term that aligns with the goal of capturing document semantics through averaging word embeddings.

**Comprehensive Empirical Validation:** The experiments are thorough and well-designed across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) with multiple strong baselines. The results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming or matching state-of-the-art methods while being significantly faster to train and test. The ablation study on the effect of corruption (Section 4.2) and the word analogy task (Section 4.3) provide strong evidence for the proposed mechanism.

**Practical Impact:** The paper's focus on efficiency (training time, test time, memory usage) is highly relevant for real-world applications. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves comparable or better performance with a much smaller vector representation size than alternatives (Table 5), and the test-time efficiency (7 seconds vs. 4 minutes for Paragraph Vectors on IMDB) is a major practical advantage.

**Clear Positioning in Literature:** The paper effectively situates itself within the literature by clearly contrasting with Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and other document representation methods, highlighting the specific limitations that Doc2VecC addresses.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the novelty:** The connection to dropout is noted, but the paper could more explicitly contrast Doc2VecC with existing dropout applications in NLP. How does this specific corruption mechanism differ from standard dropout applied to document representations?

2. **Expand the discussion of limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss scenarios where Doc2VecC might underperform (e.g., very short documents, specific document types). A brief discussion of limitations would strengthen the paper.

3. **Improve visualization:** The t-SNE visualization (Figure 3) is good but could be enhanced with clearer labels or a more detailed description of the clustering patterns.

4. **Clarify the corruption rate selection:** The paper states they used q=0.9 throughout experiments but doesn't explain why this particular value was chosen over others (e.g., q=0.8 or q=0.95). A brief analysis of how performance varies with q would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To further assess the paper's contributions, I'd like to ask:

1. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could you provide more detail on this relationship, especially how the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC differs from the corruption used in DEA?

2. In Table 3, you show that Doc2VecC clamps down on embeddings of frequent words. Could you elaborate on how this relates to the data-dependent regularization term R(uj) in Equation 7? Specifically, how does the regularization term specifically target common words?

3. The paper mentions that Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, especially given that Paragraph Vectors is designed to capture document-level semantics rather than word-level semantics?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to document representation learning with a simple yet effective approach. The theoretical insights combined with strong empirical results make it a valuable addition to the literature, and I believe it would be a valuable contribution to ICLR.
