

# Peer Review for "Document Vectors through Corruption" (ICLR)

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient model for learning document representations. The authors propose a simple yet effective approach to document representation learning that builds on the observation that linear operations on word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning. Unlike existing approaches like Paragraph Vectors, Doc2VecC represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, but with a key innovation: it uses a corruption mechanism during training where words are randomly removed from documents. This corruption serves as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words while favoring rare or discriminative ones.

The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC has several advantages: (1) model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (depending only on vocabulary size), (2) it can be trained efficiently with a Word2Vec-like architecture, (3) it provides implicit data-dependent regularization, (4) test-time inference is extremely fast (just averaging word embeddings), and (5) it achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance across multiple tasks including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.

The paper provides thorough theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization, along with extensive empirical validation across multiple datasets and tasks. The authors demonstrate that their approach outperforms strong baselines while being significantly more efficient in both training and inference.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper makes a clear, novel contribution to document representation learning with strong empirical validation.
2. The approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the existing literature.
3. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as regularization is sound and well-supported by empirical results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLP: efficient and effective document representation learning. Document representations are fundamental to many text understanding tasks, and existing approaches have limitations in terms of scalability, efficiency, and representation quality.

The motivation for Doc2VecC is well-founded. The authors correctly identify the two main drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014): 1) parameter growth with corpus size, and 2) expensive inference for unseen documents. Their solution—using a simple average of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism—directly addresses these limitations while maintaining or improving performance.

The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is particularly strong. The authors derive how the corruption process leads to a specific regularization term that favors rare words and suppresses common but uninformative ones. This is not just a heuristic but has a clear theoretical basis, as shown in Section 3.1.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors test Doc2VecC across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness) with multiple baselines. The results consistently show that Doc2VecC outperforms existing methods while being significantly more efficient in training and inference. The ablation study on the corruption rate (Table 2) and the analysis of word embeddings (Table 3) provide valuable insights into why the approach works.

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of regularization in document representation learning. The observation that Doc2VecC naturally suppresses embeddings of common words without requiring additional techniques (like subsampling, which Word2Vec+AVG relies on) is an important insight.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **Clarity of the corruption mechanism**: The description of the corruption mechanism (Equation 1) could be clarified. The authors state that "x̃d = 0 with probability q, and xd/(1-q) otherwise," but it's not immediately clear how this preserves the expectation (i.e., why the 1/(1-q) scaling is needed). A brief sentence explaining that this scaling ensures E[x̃] = x would help readers understand the unbiased nature of the corruption.

2. **Comparison with other recent work**: The paper compares to Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, but it would be valuable to include a comparison with more recent approaches like Universal Sentence Encoder (Cer et al., 2018) or BERT-based document representations. While these might be outside the scope of the paper's focus on efficient document representations, a brief mention of how Doc2VecC compares to these more recent methods would strengthen the paper's positioning.

3. **Implementation details**: The paper states that "we used q = 0.9 throughout the experiments" (Table 2), but it would be helpful to know if they tested other values of q and how the performance varied. A small ablation study on the corruption rate would provide more insight into this hyperparameter.

4. **Visualization improvement**: The t-SNE visualization in Figure 3 is useful, but it would be more informative to include a more detailed analysis of the clusters, such as which words or features contribute most to the separation between categories.

5. **Limitations discussion**: The paper would benefit from a brief discussion of limitations. For example, how does Doc2VecC perform on very short documents (e.g., tweets) compared to longer documents? What about documents with highly unusual structures or non-standard language?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better understand and assess the paper, I would like clarification on the following points:

1. In Section 3.1, the authors state that "the regularization is modulated by σw,c,x(1-σw,c,x), which is small if σw,c,x → 1 or 0." Could the authors provide a concrete example of a word that would be heavily regularized versus one that would not be? This would help clarify how the data-dependent regularization works in practice.

2. The paper claims that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed from the paradigm shown in Figure 1." Could the authors elaborate on how this reduction occurs? Specifically, how does the document vector representation in DEA differ from the one used in Doc2VecC, and why is the corruption mechanism critical for the performance improvement?

3. In the semantic relatedness task (Section 4.5), the authors note that "Doc2VecC is much faster to train and test comparing to skip-thought vectors." Given that skip-thought vectors require training on a large book corpus, could the authors provide more details about the computational resources used for training Doc2VecC (e.g., CPU vs. GPU, memory requirements) compared to skip-thought vectors?

4. The authors state that "Doc2VecC naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, therefore does not rely on this trick [subsample frequent words]." Could the authors provide a quantitative comparison of Doc2VecC's performance with and without the subsampling trick (as applied in Word2Vec+AVG), to better illustrate this advantage?

These questions are intended to help me better understand the paper's contributions and ensure my assessment is accurate. Thank you for your thoughtful work on this important topic in document representation learning.
