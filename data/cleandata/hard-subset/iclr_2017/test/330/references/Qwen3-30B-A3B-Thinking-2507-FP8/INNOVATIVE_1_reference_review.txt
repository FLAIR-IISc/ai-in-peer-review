

# Review for "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach to document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is a corruption mechanism applied during training, which the authors theoretically justify as introducing data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but non-discriminative words. The approach challenges the prevailing wisdom that document representation requires complex, document-specific vectors (as in Paragraph Vectors) and offers a simpler, more efficient alternative. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while significantly reducing training and inference time compared to existing methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper introduces a bold, novel direction in document representation learning that challenges the need for complex document-specific vectors. The corruption mechanism during training is a theoretically grounded and practically effective innovation that enables simple averaging of word embeddings to work well.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's core contribution—using a corruption mechanism to enable simple averaging of word embeddings for document representation—is genuinely novel and impactful. While the idea of averaging word embeddings for document representation isn't new (Huang et al., 2012), the authors' key innovation is the corruption mechanism during training, which introduces a data-dependent regularization that makes this simple approach work well.

The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is particularly strong, showing how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization that penalizes embeddings of common words more heavily. This provides a clear explanation for why the approach works better than simply averaging word embeddings without corruption.

Empirically, the results across multiple tasks are compelling. Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks while being significantly more efficient (Table 2 shows Doc2VecC takes 7 seconds to generate test representations vs. 257 seconds for Paragraph Vectors). The authors also demonstrate that Doc2VecC's word embeddings outperform Word2Vec on the word analogy task (Table 5), which is a strong indicator of better semantic representation.

Most importantly, this paper challenges the prevailing paradigm in document representation learning. The authors argue that "a document vector generated by averaging word embeddings can capture the global semantics of the document," which is a bold claim that the results support. This is not an incremental improvement but a fundamentally different approach to document representation.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could provide a clearer explanation of why the corruption mechanism is necessary and better than simply using subsampling of frequent words (which is already used in Word2Vec). The claim that Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization" needs more theoretical justification.

- While the comparison to Skip-thought Vectors is interesting, it might not be entirely fair since Skip-thought was trained on a different corpus. A more direct comparison where both models are trained on the same corpus would strengthen the claims.

- The paper could explore the sensitivity of the corruption rate q to different datasets. The paper uses q=0.9 throughout, but it's unclear if this is optimal for all tasks or datasets.

- The visualization in Figure 3 (t-SNE) is compelling but could be more informative if it showed how Doc2VecC's representations cluster by category compared to other methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization" than Word2Vec+IDF, which relies on subsampling. Could you provide more theoretical justification for why the corruption mechanism provides stronger regularization than simply subsampling frequent words?

2. How does the corruption mechanism compare to other regularization techniques (like dropout) when applied to document representation learning? Would the same benefits be achieved with a different regularization approach?

3. The paper shows Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks. However, Paragraph Vectors were originally designed for document representation, while Doc2VecC is built on top of Word2Vec. How do you think the authors would perform if Paragraph Vectors were trained with the same corruption mechanism?

4. Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task (Table 5)? Is this a general issue with the Paragraph Vectors architecture, or is it specific to this implementation?

This paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that a simple averaging approach to document representation can be highly effective with the right training mechanism. The novelty and boldness of the approach, combined with strong empirical results, make it a worthy contribution to the field.
