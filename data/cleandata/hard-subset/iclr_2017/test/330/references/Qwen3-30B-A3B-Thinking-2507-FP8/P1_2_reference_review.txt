

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

The paper "Document Vector through Corruption" (Doc2VecC) proposes a novel and efficient approach for document representation learning. The authors address the limitations of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014), which suffer from high model complexity (growing with training corpus size) and expensive inference for unseen documents. Doc2VecC represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a key innovation being a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. This corruption serves as a data-dependent regularization that favors informative words while suppressing common, non-discriminative words.

The authors provide a compelling theoretical justification for their corruption mechanism, showing it introduces a regularization term that naturally suppresses embeddings of frequent but uninformative words. Empirically, the paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on three major document representation tasks: sentiment analysis (IMDB dataset), document classification (Wikipedia subset), and semantic relatedness (SICK dataset). The method also offers significant computational advantages in training and testing efficiency compared to existing approaches.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The main reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated, simple yet effective approach to document representation that addresses a meaningful problem in the field.
2. The corruption mechanism is theoretically well-justified and provides a clear advantage in both performance and computational efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong case for Doc2VecC. The corruption mechanism is particularly innovative as it serves a dual purpose: improving performance through a data-dependent regularization while simultaneously enabling the simple average-based document representation that makes the method computationally efficient. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 provides a solid foundation for the approach, showing how the corruption process naturally leads to a regularization term that favors rare but informative words.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, covering multiple tasks with appropriate baselines. Doc2VecC consistently outperforms or matches state-of-the-art methods across all three tasks. The ablation studies (Tables 2-3) provide valuable insights into why the approach works well, particularly regarding the regularization effect on word embeddings. The authors also provide a thoughtful explanation for why certain methods (like Skip-thought vectors) underperform on specific datasets (e.g., IMDB due to long paragraph lengths).

The computational efficiency claims are well-supported by the time measurements in Table 2. The fact that Doc2VecC can process 25,000 test documents in 7 seconds compared to 4 minutes 17 seconds for Paragraph Vectors is a significant practical advantage.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with thorough discussion of related work and clear positioning of Doc2VecC relative to existing methods. The writing is clear and well-organized, making the technical contributions accessible to readers.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved to make it even more impactful:

1. **Clarify the relationship with Denoising Autoencoders**: The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't provide a clear comparison of the training objectives or implementation details. A brief table or paragraph contrasting the two methods would help readers understand the novelty of the approach.

2. **Clarify the parameter update claim**: The paper states that "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃." This seems counterintuitive since corruption should reduce the number of parameters to update. A clearer explanation of this point would strengthen the paper.

3. **Address the subsampling issue**: The paper claims that Doc2VecC "does not rely on [subsampleing of frequent words]" but still uses subsampling in the experiments (Section 4.2). It would be helpful to clarify whether Doc2VecC truly eliminates the need for subsampling or simply reduces its necessity, and why the authors still use it.

4. **Provide more detailed complexity analysis**: While the paper mentions computational efficiency, a formal analysis of time and space complexity compared to other methods would strengthen the claims about efficiency.

5. **Include a comparison of document length sensitivity**: The paper notes that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on IMDB due to long paragraph lengths. It would be interesting to see how Doc2VecC's performance varies with document length compared to other methods.

## Questions for the Authors

1. In Section 3.1, the paper states that "the jth dimension of the Hessian's diagonal evaluated at the mean x is given by..." Could you provide more intuition about why the regularization term depends on the confidence of predictions (σw,c,x(1-σw,c,x))? Why would this term be small when predictions are confident?

2. The paper mentions that "Doc2VecC is not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and used q = 0.9 throughout. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how different values of q impact performance? Specifically, what happens when q is very low (e.g., q = 0.1) or very high (e.g., q = 0.99)?

3. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could you clarify the exact relationship between the two methods? Specifically, how does the training objective differ between Doc2VecC (with no context) and DEA?

4. In the semantic relatedness task (Section 4.5), Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought vectors (11.70% error rate vs 17.42% on IMDB), but is slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Given that the authors hypothesize Doc2VecC is better for longer paragraphs, could you elaborate on how the paragraph length in the SICK dataset compares to IMDB and whether this might explain the performance difference?

These questions would help clarify some aspects of the paper and provide deeper insights into the method's behavior, which would strengthen the overall contribution.
