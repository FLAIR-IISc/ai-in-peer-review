

# Review of "Document Vector through Corruption"

## Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a method for learning document representations that represents each document as the average of word embeddings, but with a key twist: during training, it applies a corruption mechanism (randomly removing words from documents) that the authors claim introduces a data-dependent regularization effect. The authors claim this approach is more efficient than existing methods (particularly Paragraph Vectors), outperforms baselines on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, and naturally incorporates regularization without requiring additional techniques like subsampling frequent words.

## Decision

Reject. The paper makes strong claims about the superiority of Doc2VecC without sufficient empirical validation against established baselines, and the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism appears superficial.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Insufficient Empirical Validation**: While the paper reports results on three tasks, the comparisons are incomplete and lack proper statistical significance testing. The authors compare against a limited set of baselines (BoW, DEA, Word2Vec+IDF, Doc2Vec, Skip-thought vectors, RNNLM), but fail to include several strong baselines that have become standard in document representation research (e.g., BERT, Sentence-BERT, or more recent transformer-based approaches). The performance gains claimed (e.g., "beats the BoW representation by 15%") are modest and not accompanied by statistical tests to determine if they are significant.

2. **Overstated Theoretical Justification**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "intrinsically minimizes" a loss function with a data-dependent regularization term (equation 7), but the derivation appears to be a superficial application of Taylor expansion to a problem where the authors have already assumed the form of the regularization. The connection between the corruption mechanism and the regularization term is not clearly established, and the authors don't demonstrate how this regularization differs from standard techniques like dropout or subsampling of frequent words.

3. **Lack of Rigorous Baseline Comparison**: The paper compares Doc2VecC against Paragraph Vectors but fails to properly address the key limitation of Paragraph Vectors (the need for additional inference for unseen documents). The authors claim Doc2VecC "outperforms Paragraph Vectors," but their results don't show the time required to generate document representations for unseen documents, which is a major practical advantage of their approach. The time comparisons in Table 2 are misleading because they don't account for the fact that Paragraph Vectors require additional inference time for unseen documents, which is the key advantage of Doc2VecC.

4. **Questionable Novelty**: The core idea of representing documents as the average of word embeddings is not new (Huang et al., 2012). The paper's contribution is the corruption mechanism, but this appears to be a minor variation of standard techniques like dropout or denoising autoencoders, which have been extensively studied. The paper doesn't clearly distinguish how Doc2VecC differs from these established approaches in a meaningful way.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the Core Innovation**: The paper needs to more clearly articulate what makes Doc2VecC different from existing approaches that use document averaging with some form of regularization. The connection between the corruption mechanism and the regularization effect needs to be more rigorously established.

2. **Expand Baseline Comparisons**: Include more recent state-of-the-art baselines (e.g., BERT-based approaches) to demonstrate the true competitiveness of Doc2VecC. The current comparison set is outdated and doesn't reflect the current landscape of document representation learning.

3. **Provide Statistical Significance**: Include statistical tests to show that the reported performance gains are significant, not just marginal improvements. Without this, it's difficult to assess whether the claimed improvements are meaningful.

4. **Clarify the Corruption Mechanism**: The paper claims the corruption mechanism "significantly reduces the number of parameters to update in back propagation," but this is not clearly explained. How exactly does this affect the computational complexity? A more detailed analysis would strengthen the argument.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." How does this compare to simply training Word2Vec on the same data and then averaging the embeddings? The paper doesn't provide a direct comparison between this approach and Doc2VecC.

2. The authors claim that Doc2VecC "does not rely on the trick of subsampling frequent words" as Word2Vec+IDF does. However, the paper states that they applied subsampling to all methods. How would Doc2VecC perform without this subsampling step, and how does it compare to Word2Vec+IDF without subsampling?

3. The paper claims that Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought vectors on IMDB but underperforms on SICK. The authors hypothesize this is due to paragraph length. However, the SICK dataset consists of sentence pairs (not long paragraphs), so this explanation seems questionable. Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why Doc2VecC performs better on longer documents?

4. The paper claims that the corruption mechanism introduces "data-dependent regularization" that suppresses common words. However, the regularization term in equation (7) depends on the document representation itself, which is learned during training. How does the authors ensure this regularization is not circular?
