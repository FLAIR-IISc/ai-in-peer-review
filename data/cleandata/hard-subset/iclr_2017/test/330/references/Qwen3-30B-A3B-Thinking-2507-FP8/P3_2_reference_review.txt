

# Peer Review: "Document Vector through Corruption" (Doc2VecC)

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel approach for document representation learning that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The key innovation is the introduction of a corruption mechanism during training, where words are randomly removed from documents with probability q, which the authors claim acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words while favoring rare or informative words. The authors claim several advantages for their approach: (1) model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size), (2) efficient training similar to Word2Vec, (3) test-time efficiency (no separate inference for unseen documents), and (4) superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The paper makes a strong case that their corruption mechanism is theoretically justified as a form of data-dependent regularization, and the empirical results across multiple tasks support their claims. The approach is conceptually simple yet effective, building on the well-established intuition that word embeddings can be compositionally combined to form document representations.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper presents a genuinely novel and well-motivated approach to document representation that addresses a clear limitation of existing methods (specifically, the high complexity of Paragraph Vectors).
2. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer is compelling, even if complex, and is supported by empirical results.
3. The empirical results across multiple tasks are strong and demonstrate clear advantages over baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Theoretical Contribution and Motivation
The paper's theoretical contribution is its derivation of how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer, which is a significant insight. The authors correctly identify a key limitation of existing approaches: that methods like Paragraph Vectors require learning a unique vector for each document, making them computationally expensive for large corpora. The observation that simple averaging of word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning (e.g., "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen") is well-established in the literature, but the authors' insight that incorporating corruption during training can enforce this averaging to be meaningful is novel.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is complex but valid. The authors correctly derive that the corruption mechanism leads to a regularization term that penalizes embeddings of frequent words more heavily. This is a valuable contribution to the understanding of how noise injection can serve as regularization in representation learning.

### Empirical Results
The empirical results are strong and comprehensive across multiple tasks:

1. **Sentiment Analysis (IMDB):** Doc2VecC achieves the best results among the baselines, significantly outperforming BoW (15% better) and Word2Vec+IDF. The authors provide a convincing explanation for why Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on this dataset (long paragraphs), which is a thoughtful analysis of dataset characteristics.

2. **Word Analogy Task:** Doc2VecC produces word embeddings that significantly outperform Word2Vec (close to 20% uplift), while Paragraph Vectors perform surprisingly poorly. This is a strong validation of the quality of the learned word embeddings.

3. **Document Classification (Wikipedia):** Doc2VecC outperforms all baselines, including Paragraph Vectors, by a significant margin (e.g., 31.92% error rate vs. 35.78% for Paragraph Vectors with h=100). The t-SNE visualization in Figure 3 compellingly demonstrates better cluster separation with Doc2VecC.

4. **Semantic Relatedness (SICK):** Doc2VecC significantly outperforms the competition winners and baseline methods, despite its simplicity. The authors correctly note that while LSTM-based methods perform better on short sentences (as in SICK), Doc2VecC is much faster to train and test.

The ablation studies, particularly in Table 3 showing the effect of the corruption mechanism on word embeddings, provide strong support for the authors' claims about the regularization effect.

### Efficiency Advantages
The paper convincingly demonstrates the efficiency advantages of Doc2VecC. The training time is comparable to Word2Vec (only slightly slower than Word2Vec, faster than Paragraph Vectors), and test-time efficiency is significantly better (7 seconds vs. 4 minutes 17 seconds for Paragraph Vectors on IMDB). This is a crucial practical advantage for real-world applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

### Major Concerns for Revision
1. **Comparison to Recent Work:** The paper doesn't compare against more recent methods like BERT or other transformer-based approaches, which were emerging at the time of submission. While the paper focuses on more traditional methods, a brief discussion of how Doc2VecC might compare to these newer approaches would strengthen the paper's relevance.

2. **Ablation Study for Corruption Mechanism:** The paper doesn't provide an ablation study showing the contribution of the corruption mechanism itself. It would be valuable to see how performance changes when the corruption is removed (i.e., when Doc2VecC reduces to simply averaging word embeddings without corruption). This would clarify whether the performance gains are due to the averaging mechanism itself or the specific corruption regularization.

3. **Clarity of Corruption Mechanism:** The paper uses an unbiased corruption mechanism (setting uncorrupted dimensions to 1/(1-q) times their original value), but the theoretical justification for this specific scaling is not clearly explained. A more intuitive explanation or comparison with other corruption schemes would improve the paper.

4. **Comparison to Huang et al. (2012):** The paper mentions Huang et al. (2012) who proposed using average of word embeddings, but doesn't clearly differentiate their work from Doc2VecC. The authors should explicitly state how their corruption mechanism differentiates their approach from this earlier work.

### Minor Concerns
1. **Implementation Details:** The paper mentions they use q=0.9 throughout experiments, but doesn't discuss how sensitive the results are to this choice. A brief sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.

2. **Theoretical Justification Clarity:** The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is mathematically sound but quite dense. A more intuitive explanation of why the corruption leads to the observed regularization would help readers better understand this key insight.

3. **Data Preprocessing:** The paper mentions they apply subsampling of frequent words, but doesn't clarify whether this is applied to all methods or only to Word2Vec+IDF. This could affect the fairness of the comparison.

## Questions for Authors

1. In Section 3.1, you claim the corruption mechanism leads to "data-dependent regularization." Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of how this regularization specifically benefits document representation learning, beyond the mathematical derivation?

2. You state that Doc2VecC "naturally enforces a meaningful document representation can be formed by averaging the word embeddings during learning." How does this differ from simply using a weighted average of word embeddings (as in Word2Vec+IDF), where the weights are learned during training?

3. In Table 3, you show that Doc2VecC clamps down on embeddings of common words. Could you provide a concrete example of how this leads to better document representation in practice (e.g., how does this help distinguish between positive and negative movie reviews)?

4. The paper claims that Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors across all tasks. However, Paragraph Vectors have a more expressive model architecture. How do you reconcile this with the fact that a more expressive model should generally be able to achieve better performance, and what specific limitation of Paragraph Vectors does Doc2VecC overcome?

## Final Assessment

This paper presents a strong contribution to document representation learning with a novel approach that is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective. The corruption mechanism is a clever and well-justified extension to existing word embedding techniques, and the results across multiple tasks are compelling. The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the methodology and thorough experimental evaluation. While there are some areas for improvement in terms of comparison to recent work and clarity of certain theoretical points, these are not sufficient to warrant rejection. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field and is worthy of acceptance at ICLR.
