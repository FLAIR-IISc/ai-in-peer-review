

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper proposes "Document Vector through Corruption" (Doc2VecC), a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings during training, with an added corruption mechanism that randomly removes words from documents. The authors claim that this approach:
- Has model complexity decoupled from corpus size
- Enables efficient test-time document representation (just averaging word embeddings)
- Introduces a data-dependent regularization that favors informative words
- Outperforms established methods (BoW, Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF) on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes strong claims about a new method that supposedly outperforms established baselines, but the evidence provided is insufficiently rigorous to justify the novelty and claimed advantages.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper suffers from several critical issues that undermine its contribution:

First, the motivation for the corruption mechanism is weak. The authors cite Mikolov et al.'s work on word vector arithmetic as justification for averaging word embeddings, but they don't address why previous approaches (e.g., Huang et al., 2012) that simply averaged word embeddings didn't work well. The corruption mechanism is presented as a necessary innovation, but the paper fails to demonstrate that it's actually required for the observed performance gains. The Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 is presented as theoretical justification, but it's not properly validated or explained in terms of how it translates to practical performance improvements.

Second, the empirical validation is inadequate. The paper compares to several baselines, but misses key recent approaches (e.g., transformer-based methods for document representation that have become state-of-the-art). The comparison to Paragraph Vectors is particularly problematic because Paragraph Vectors explicitly learns a unique vector per document, while Doc2VecC uses a fundamentally different approach (averaging word embeddings). The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training, but provides no evidence beyond the performance numbers. The authors claim that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but don't show why this is an advantage or how the corruption mechanism specifically improves performance beyond standard averaging.

Third, the paper makes several unsupported claims. For example, the claim that "Doc2VecC outperforms the state-of-the-art" is not properly supported, as the comparison is limited to older methods. The paper also claims that "Doc2VecC does not rely on subsampling frequent words" as a benefit, but the experiments in Table 3 actually show that their method still requires this preprocessing step, contradicting their claim.

The most concerning issue is the lack of ablation studies to demonstrate the necessity of the corruption mechanism. The paper doesn't show that removing the corruption leads to significantly worse performance, which would be essential to validate the core innovation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct ablation studies showing that the corruption mechanism is essential for the observed performance gains. Specifically, compare Doc2VecC with and without corruption on the same tasks.
2. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods for document representation, not just older approaches like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.
3. Provide a clearer explanation of why their approach differs from previous work that simply averages word embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). What specific problem does the corruption mechanism solve that the previous approaches missed?
4. Re-examine the claim about not needing subsampling of frequent words. The evidence in Table 3 suggests that this claim is not fully supported.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. How do you explain the performance gains of Doc2VecC compared to simple averaging of Word2Vec embeddings (without corruption), given that your method is essentially averaging embeddings with an added corruption step that shouldn't fundamentally change the representation?
2. Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task according to your results, when it's supposed to learn word embeddings as part of the model? This seems counterintuitive and requires stronger explanation.
3. Can you provide a direct comparison of Doc2VecC with more recent document representation methods like BERT-based approaches, which have become the standard for many NLP tasks? The current comparison to Skip-thought Vectors seems outdated.

The paper's core idea—using corrupted word embeddings to learn document representations—is interesting, but the current evidence does not sufficiently support the claims made. Without stronger validation of the necessity and effectiveness of the corruption mechanism, the contribution remains unconvincing. The paper would benefit from a more thorough empirical validation of the proposed mechanism rather than simply presenting performance numbers.
