

# Review of "Doc2VecC: Document Representation through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel model for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with the key innovation being a corruption mechanism during training where words are randomly removed from documents. The authors claim this approach addresses limitations of existing methods, particularly Paragraph Vectors, by decoupling model complexity from corpus size, enabling efficient training and testing, and introducing data-dependent regularization that favors informative words. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly faster to train and test than competing methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution with well-justified claims, and the empirical results robustly support the authors' claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper tackles a well-defined problem in document representation learning with clear motivation. The authors effectively situate their work in the literature, properly citing and contrasting with Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and other relevant approaches. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is sound and well-supported by the empirical results.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous, covering three distinct text understanding tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) across multiple datasets. The results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming strong baselines including Bag-of-Words, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors. The paper provides clear evidence of the regularization effect through experiments showing how Doc2VecC handles frequent but uninformative words differently than other methods.

The paper also demonstrates significant practical advantages: Doc2VecC is faster to train than Paragraph Vectors (which scales with corpus size) and significantly faster to test than Paragraph Vectors (which requires inference for unseen documents). The time efficiency claims are well-supported by the reported training and inference times.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper would benefit from a more precise distinction between Doc2VecC and previous approaches that use word embedding averaging (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). While the paper mentions this, it doesn't sufficiently clarify the novelty of the corruption mechanism in the context of existing averaging approaches.

- The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism is interesting but could be strengthened with more intuitive explanation about why this specific corruption scheme works better than alternatives.

- The claim that Doc2VecC "matches or beats the state-of-the-art" could be strengthened by a more comprehensive comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., transformer-based approaches), even if only as a brief discussion.

- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of how the corruption rate q affects performance, beyond the statement that the model is not sensitive to it.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does Doc2VecC compare to recent transformer-based document representation methods (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) that have achieved state-of-the-art results on many text understanding tasks? While the paper acknowledges these are different approaches, a brief comparison would help situate Doc2VecC in the current landscape.

2. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could you clarify how this reduction works, and why including the local context words leads to better performance?

3. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative." Could you provide more detailed analysis of how the corruption mechanism specifically leads to this stronger regularization compared to the sub-sampling technique used in Word2Vec?

4. The paper hypothesizes that Skip-thought Vectors performed poorly on IMDB due to paragraph length. Could you provide additional empirical evidence supporting this hypothesis (e.g., by testing Skip-thought Vectors on shorter paragraphs or comparing with other long-text datasets)?
