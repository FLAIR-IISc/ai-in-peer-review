- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism (random word removal + scaling) that acts as data-dependent regularization.  
- Claim: Corruption suppresses embeddings of common/uninformative words, improving over simple averaging and Paragraph Vectors while being more efficient.  
- Recommendation: Weak accept — method is well-motivated and theoretically novel, but experimental issues must be addressed.  
- Theoretical strength: Taylor expansion analysis shows corruption introduces a regularizer that penalizes common-word embeddings, explaining empirical gains.  
- Empirical strength: Strong results on tasks (e.g., Wikipedia classification 30.24% vs Paragraph Vectors 33.02%) and visualizations showing meaningful representations.  
- Concern — Skip-thought comparison: Comparing to Skip-thought trained on short sentences for IMDB (long reviews) is invalid without retraining on longer texts.  
- Concern — missing ablation: No direct comparison of Doc2VecC with vs. without corruption to demonstrate the corruption’s critical role.  
- Concern — unverified “natural enforcement”: Claim that averaging naturally captures document semantics lacks empirical evidence beyond the regularization argument.  
- Concern — DEA comparison overstated: Theoretical reduction to Denoising Autoencoders is claimed but not empirically validated.  
- Requested experiment: Provide direct empirical comparison of Doc2VecC without local context vs. DEA to confirm equivalence.  
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Include ablation over corruption rates (e.g., q=0.5, 0.7, 0.9) to justify chosen q=0.9.  
- Reevaluate baselines: Retrain or use Skip-thought on longer-text data (IMDB) to make fair comparisons.  
- Qualitative analysis: Show how corruption alters specific word embeddings (e.g., norms for common vs rare words).  
- Prior work relation: Clarify how Doc2VecC differs from/ improves upon prior averaging methods (e.g., Huang et al. 2012).  
- Question — q choice: Why q=0.9 and how does performance vary with other q values?  
- Question — ablation result: Can you provide performance with and without the corruption mechanism?  
- Question — novelty vs dropout: Is the corruption mechanism essentially dropout on document representations?  
- Question — evidence for semantics claim: What concrete evidence beyond regularization supports the “natural enforcement” of semantics?  
- Question — Paragraph Vectors weakness: Why do Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task — architecture or data-specific issue?
