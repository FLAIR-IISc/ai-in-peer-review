- Decision: recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Core contribution: Doc2VecC represents documents as averages of word embeddings and trains with a random-word-corruption mechanism.  
- Novelty/theory: Corruption is theoretically framed as data-dependent regularization that favors rare informative words and suppresses common uninformative ones.  
- Complexity/efficiency: Model complexity depends only on vocabulary size, and training/testing are substantially faster than Paragraph Vectors.  
- Empirical results: Comprehensive evaluation (IMDB, Wikipedia, SICK) shows Doc2VecC matches or outperforms baselines like Word2Vec+IDF and Paragraph Vectors.  
- Claim supported: Averaged embeddings capture document semantics during training, unlike methods that require post-processing.  
- Analysis insight: Paper explains why some methods (e.g., Skip-thought) fail on certain tasks (long paragraphs) and gives sensible task-specific interpretation.  
- Typo/terminology: Title appears to have a typo ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"); the meaning of the "C" in Doc2VecC should be defined early.  
- Corruption-rate concern: Uses q = 0.9 with no justification—an ablation study over q is recommended.  
- DEA connection: The claim that Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders needs clearer explanation and linkage to reconstruction objectives.  
- Quantitative detail: When claiming e.g. "beats BOW by 15%," report exact error rates for concreteness.  
- Sub-sampling comparison: Provide a more detailed comparison showing how corruption replicates or differs from Word2Vec subsampling effects.  
- Clarify backprop statement: The claim that surviving words increase parameters to backpropagate seems counterintuitive and needs clarification.  
- Table clarification: Which table shows the word analogy results referenced in Section 4.3? Table 5 appears to show only Wikipedia classification.  
- Regularization vs subsampling: Explain precisely how Doc2VecC’s regularization differs from standard Word2Vec subsampling.  
- Statistical significance: Clarify whether small improvements (e.g., 0.2% over Paragraph Vectors on IMDB) are statistically significant.  
- Overall verdict: Strong, simple, well-justified contribution with solid empirical support; minor clarifications would strengthen the paper.
