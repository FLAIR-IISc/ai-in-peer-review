

# Peer Review for "Document Vector through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient approach for learning document representations. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, but with a key innovation: during training, they apply a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words from the document. This corruption acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while favoring informative or rare words.

The paper makes several important claims:
- Doc2VecC has model complexity that's decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size)
- It's highly efficient to train and test (no expensive inference needed at test time)
- The corruption mechanism intrinsically enforces that document representations can be formed by averaging word embeddings
- Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks
- The approach outperforms existing methods like Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, and Skip-thought vectors

The paper is well-structured, provides a solid theoretical foundation for the corruption mechanism, and demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple benchmark datasets. The approach is elegant in its simplicity while effectively addressing key limitations of existing document representation methods.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning with a simple yet effective approach. The key reasons for this decision are:
1. The corruption mechanism provides a theoretically sound and practically effective solution to the problem of document representation learning, with strong empirical validation across multiple tasks.
2. The paper clearly demonstrates the advantages of Doc2VecC over existing methods in terms of both performance and computational efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper provides strong evidence for its claims through both theoretical analysis and comprehensive empirical evaluation. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularization is well-developed, showing how the corruption process naturally leads to a regularization term that favors informative words. This is a significant contribution that goes beyond simply proposing a new model architecture.

The empirical results are robust and compelling across multiple tasks:
- On IMDB sentiment analysis, Doc2VecC achieves 84.3% accuracy, outperforming Paragraph Vectors (83.1%) and other baselines by 15%.
- On Wikipedia document classification, Doc2VecC achieves 30.24% error rate with h=1000, significantly outperforming all other methods.
- On the SICK semantic relatedness task, Doc2VecC achieves 11.70% error rate, outperforming Skip-thought vectors (17.42%) and other baselines.

The paper also provides valuable insights into the nature of the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC, demonstrating that it effectively suppresses embeddings of common words (Table 3) and produces better word embeddings for analogy tasks (Table 5).

The computational efficiency claims are well-supported by the timing results (Table 2), showing Doc2VecC is significantly faster to train and test than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper makes a strong contribution, I have a few suggestions to enhance its quality and impact:

1. **Ablation study on the corruption mechanism**: The paper claims that the corruption mechanism is critical to performance, but it would be valuable to include an ablation study comparing Doc2VecC with the same averaging approach but without corruption. This would help isolate the specific contribution of the corruption mechanism versus the averaging approach itself.

2. **Comparison to more recent methods**: Although the paper was written in 2017, it might be helpful to briefly acknowledge how Doc2VecC compares to more recent approaches (like BERT) in the discussion, even if a full comparison isn't included. This would help situate the work in the broader evolution of document representation learning.

3. **Justification for corruption rate q**: The paper states that "both models are not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and uses q=0.9 throughout experiments. A brief discussion on why this specific value was chosen and how performance varies with different q values would strengthen the paper.

4. **Clarification on the relationship to Denoising Autoencoders**: The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but this isn't fully explained. A more detailed explanation of this relationship would help readers understand the conceptual connections between these approaches.

5. **More discussion on limitations**: While the paper demonstrates strong performance, a brief discussion of potential limitations (e.g., how the approach might perform on extremely short documents or very specialized domains) would provide a more balanced perspective.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. Could you provide more details about the ablation study comparing Doc2VecC to a version without the corruption mechanism? How much of the performance gain is due to the corruption versus the averaging approach?

2. The paper states that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces a meaningful document representation can be formed by averaging the word embeddings during learning." How does this differ from the approach of simply averaging word embeddings (without any corruption) that has been used in prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012)? What specific aspects of the corruption mechanism enable this "enforcement"?

3. In the word analogy task (Section 4.3), the paper shows that Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec. Could you elaborate on why the corruption mechanism improves word embeddings for analogy tasks, when the corruption is applied to document-level representations rather than directly to word embeddings?

4. The paper mentions that "the model complexity of Doc2VecC is decoupled from the size of the training corpus, depending only on the size of the vocabulary." How would this change if the vocabulary size grows during training (e.g., with a dynamic vocabulary)? Would the model still maintain its computational advantages?

5. The paper states that "Doc2VecC is much faster to train and test comparing to skip-thought vectors." Given that skip-thought vectors are also based on a sequence model, could you elaborate on why Doc2VecC is significantly faster despite the similar task of learning document representations?
