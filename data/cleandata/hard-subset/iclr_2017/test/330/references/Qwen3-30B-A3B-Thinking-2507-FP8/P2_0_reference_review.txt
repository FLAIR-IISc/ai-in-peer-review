

# ICLR Review: "Doc2VecC: Document Vector through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a new model for document representation learning that addresses key limitations of existing approaches. The authors identify two main problems with Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014): (1) model complexity grows with corpus size, and (2) generating representations for unseen documents is computationally expensive at test time. 

The key contribution is a simple yet effective model that represents documents as the average of word embeddings, with a novel corruption mechanism during training that introduces a data-dependent regularization. This approach ensures that document representation can be naturally formed by averaging word embeddings during learning, without requiring additional post-processing. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or better performance than state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

## 2. Decision: Accept

**Accept** with the following key reasons:
1. The paper identifies a clear and important problem in document representation learning that has practical implications for efficiency and scalability.
2. The proposed approach is well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and supported by thorough empirical evaluation across multiple tasks and datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

### Problem Statement and Motivation (Strong)
The paper clearly identifies a real limitation of Paragraph Vectors (model complexity growing with corpus size, expensive test-time inference) that has practical implications for large-scale document processing. The motivation is well-placed in the literature, with excellent references to prior work (Mikolov et al., 2013b; Le & Mikolov, 2014) that demonstrates the compositionality of word embeddings. The authors correctly note that while averaging word embeddings has been explored before (Huang et al., 2012), their corruption mechanism provides a novel way to make this approach work well.

### Theoretical Contribution (Strong)
The theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularization is a significant contribution. The paper provides a clear derivation showing that the corruption process introduces a regularization term that favors rare or informative words. This is a well-executed theoretical contribution that explains why the approach works better than simple averaging of word embeddings.

### Empirical Evaluation (Strong)
The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed:
- The paper evaluates on three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) with multiple datasets
- The results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforms baselines while being significantly faster to train and test
- The t-SNE visualizations provide strong qualitative evidence of better document representation
- The ablation study on the corruption mechanism (Table 3) provides compelling evidence of the regularization effect
- The comparison to Word2Vec+IDF shows that Doc2VecC's integrated approach is superior to post-processing techniques

The paper's claim that "Doc2VecC matches or beats the state-of-the-art" is well-supported by the empirical results, particularly on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia document classification tasks.

### Implementation and Practical Value (Strong)
The paper emphasizes practical benefits that are crucial for real-world applications: the model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size), training is efficient, and test-time representation generation is trivial (just averaging word embeddings). The time comparison in Table 2 clearly demonstrates the efficiency advantage over Paragraph Vectors.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, these suggestions would further improve its impact:

1. **Comparison to more recent state-of-the-art methods**: The paper compares to Paragraph Vectors and Word2Vec+IDF but doesn't compare to more recent methods like BERT or other transformer-based approaches that have become the state-of-the-art in many NLP tasks. Including these comparisons would strengthen the paper's position in the current literature.

2. **More detail on corruption rate selection**: The paper states "we found that both models are not sensitive to the corruption rate q" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. A small ablation study showing performance across different q values would strengthen this claim.

3. **Clarify the relationship with bag-of-words approaches**: The paper mentions that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" but doesn't provide a clear comparison to DEA. A more detailed comparison would help position Doc2VecC within the literature.

4. **Scalability analysis**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally scales to large datasets" but doesn't provide a scalability analysis showing how training time or memory requirements scale with corpus size. A small experiment showing this would be valuable.

5. **More detail on the regularization effect**: While the paper provides the theoretical analysis of the regularization, a more concrete example showing how the regularization affects specific word embeddings would strengthen the argument.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, but the improvement seems marginal in some cases (e.g., 12.1% vs 12.0% error rate on IMDB). Could you provide more detailed analysis of why Doc2VecC is better, beyond just the numerical results?

2. How does Doc2VecC compare to more recent methods like BERT or other transformer-based approaches for document representation? Given that these have become the state-of-the-art for many NLP tasks, it would be valuable to see how Doc2VecC performs relative to them.

3. The paper mentions "the corruption introduced in Doc2VecC acts as a data-dependent regularization" but doesn't provide clear evidence of why this is better than standard regularization techniques (like L2 regularization). Could you elaborate on this?

4. In the semantic relatedness task (SICK), Doc2VecC performs slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Given the authors' hypothesis that Doc2VecC is better for longer paragraphs, could you provide more analysis of how sentence length affects the performance of different methods?

5. The paper states that "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃" but doesn't explain how this affects training time. Could you clarify the relationship between corruption rate q and training efficiency?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear and valuable contribution to document representation learning. The approach is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. With the suggested improvements, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
