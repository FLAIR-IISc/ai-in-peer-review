

# Review for "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings learned through a corruption-based training process. The authors motivate their approach by noting that linear operations on word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning, and they extend this to document representation. The key innovation is the incorporation of a corruption mechanism during training (randomly removing words from documents), which they theoretically analyze as a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of common, uninformative words while favoring rare, informative ones. The paper claims Doc2VecC offers several advantages over existing methods: (1) model complexity decoupled from training corpus size; (2) efficient training and inference; (3) implicit data-dependent regularization; and (4) competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a technically sound approach with strong empirical validation. The key reasons for acceptance are the well-executed theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer and the consistent empirical performance across multiple document representation tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution lies in the theoretical analysis of how the corruption mechanism functions as a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3.1), which is novel and well-supported. The derivation shows that the corruption process introduces a regularization term that naturally suppresses embeddings of common words while preserving those of informative words, without requiring additional hyperparameters or post-processing. This theoretical insight is a significant contribution to the understanding of document representation learning.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. The authors compare against multiple relevant baselines across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) with proper evaluation protocols. The results consistently demonstrate Doc2VecC's advantages in both performance (outperforming baselines by 1-5% on most tasks) and efficiency (significantly faster training and inference than Paragraph Vectors). The ablation study in Table 3 provides strong evidence for the regularization effect, showing Doc2VecC effectively dampens embeddings of common stop words.

The paper is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature, clearly differentiating Doc2VecC from both Word2Vec-based approaches and Paragraph Vectors. The explanation of how Doc2VecC avoids the scalability limitations of Paragraph Vectors (which requires learning a unique vector per document) is particularly strong.

## 4. Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of novelty. While the corruption mechanism is presented as novel, the core idea of averaging word embeddings for document representation is not new (Huang et al., 2012). The paper should more explicitly state how the corruption mechanism fundamentally changes the approach and why it's necessary, rather than just presenting it as an additional component.

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but doesn't provide a direct comparison between Doc2VecC and a baseline that simply averages standard Word2Vec embeddings. Including this comparison would strengthen the claim about the necessity of the corruption mechanism.

3. The paper states "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't explain why this is beneficial or how the inclusion of local context words improves performance. A brief analysis of this design choice would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims the corruption mechanism is "critical to the performance and learning speed." Could you provide a comparison between Doc2VecC with and without the corruption mechanism (i.e., using standard Word2Vec embeddings averaged for documents)? This would clarify the necessity of the corruption component.

2. In Table 3, Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words, but the paper doesn't explain how this aligns with the observation that Word2Vec+IDF (which uses a similar averaging approach with IDF weighting) still requires the IDF weighting to handle frequent words. How does the corruption mechanism's regularization achieve what IDF weighting accomplishes?

3. The paper mentions "subsample frequent words" as a trick used with Word2Vec+AVG, but states Doc2VecC doesn't require this. However, the corruption mechanism itself is a form of subsampling. Could you elaborate on how the corruption mechanism's effect differs from standard subsampling of frequent words in Word2Vec training?
