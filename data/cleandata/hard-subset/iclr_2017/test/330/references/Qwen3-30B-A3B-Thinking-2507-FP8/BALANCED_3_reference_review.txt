

# Review for "Doc2VecC: Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings learned through a corruption-based training process. The authors claim their approach has several advantages: (1) model complexity decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size), (2) efficient training and inference, (3) implicit data-dependent regularization that favors informative words, and (4) competitive performance across multiple document representation tasks. The core idea builds on Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, introducing a corruption mechanism during training that randomly removes words from documents, which the authors argue provides regularization and improves performance.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents a reasonable approach with some interesting insights, the core contribution lacks sufficient novelty and the empirical evidence does not fully support the claimed advantages over existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Lack of Clear Novelty:** The fundamental idea of representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings was already proposed by Huang et al. (2012), which the authors cite but do not sufficiently differentiate from their approach. The key novelty claim is the corruption mechanism, but the paper does not convincingly demonstrate that this mechanism provides significant benefits beyond what could be achieved with standard Word2Vec embeddings and simple averaging. The authors claim Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but this is not meaningfully different from simply using Word2Vec embeddings with averaging (with or without IDF weighting).

**Inadequate Ablation Studies:** The paper does not provide a clear ablation study showing how much the corruption mechanism contributes to performance gains. For example, they don't compare Doc2VecC to a baseline that simply uses Word2Vec embeddings averaged with IDF weighting (which is already a standard approach). The results show Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, but it's unclear whether the improvement comes from the corruption mechanism or simply from the averaging approach itself. The paper states "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't provide a fair comparison between these two approaches.

**Misleading Comparison to Paragraph Vectors:** The comparison to Paragraph Vectors (PV) is problematic because PV explicitly learns a document vector while Doc2VecC uses a different training objective. PV's higher parameter count is expected because it learns a unique vector per document, while Doc2VecC uses a fixed-size vocabulary. The paper doesn't properly control for the different model architectures when comparing performance.

**Overstated Claims:** The paper claims Doc2VecC "matches or beats the state-of-the-art" but doesn't demonstrate this against more recent, strong baselines like BERT or other transformer-based approaches that have become standard for document representation. The comparison to Skip-thought Vectors is limited to a single dataset where Doc2VecC performs better, but the paper doesn't address why Doc2VecC would be preferred in settings where Skip-thoughts perform better.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- **Clarify the Novel Contribution:** The paper needs to more clearly distinguish Doc2VecC from existing averaging approaches. A thorough ablation study comparing (a) simple Word2Vec+IDF averaging, (b) Word2Vec+IDF with corruption, and (c) Doc2VecC would strengthen the claims.

- **Improve Baseline Comparisons:** Include more recent state-of-the-art document representation methods as baselines, particularly transformer-based approaches that have largely superseded the methods used in the paper.

- **Clarify the Regularization Mechanism:** The theoretical analysis of corruption as regularization is interesting but needs to be connected more clearly to the empirical results. How much does the regularization actually contribute to the performance gains? The paper states that Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization" but doesn't provide evidence beyond the word list in Table 3.

- **Address the Word2Vec+IDF Comparison:** The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics" while Word2Vec+IDF requires "post-processing," but this isn't clearly demonstrated. The paper should explain why their approach is better than standard IDF weighting, which is a well-established method for this purpose.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide a more direct comparison between Doc2VecC and a simple Word2Vec+IDF baseline? The paper claims Doc2VecC outperforms this baseline, but doesn't show whether this is due to the corruption mechanism or simply the averaging approach.

2. The paper states that "Doc2VecC naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, therefore does not rely on [subsampleing]." However, the Word2Vec+IDF baseline also uses subsampling as a standard practice. How does Doc2VecC's regularization compare to the standard subsampling approach used in Word2Vec?

3. The paper claims Doc2VecC "outperforms not only in testing efficiency, but also in the expressiveness of the generated representations." What specific aspects of expressiveness are improved beyond what could be achieved with standard averaging approaches?

4. How does Doc2VecC compare to more recent document representation approaches like BERT or other transformer-based models, which have become the standard in the field? The paper's evaluation is limited to older methods, which doesn't provide a fair comparison to current state-of-the-art.
