

# Review for "Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The authors claim this method offers several advantages over existing approaches: (1) model complexity is decoupled from corpus size, (2) efficient training and inference, (3) implicit data-dependent regularization that suppresses common but uninformative words, and (4) competitive or superior performance on multiple text understanding tasks. The key innovation is the corruption mechanism (randomly removing words during training), which the authors theoretically justify as a form of data-dependent regularization that improves performance while maintaining efficiency.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The primary reason for rejection is that the experimental evaluation lacks sufficient rigor to support the paper's claims, particularly in the comparison to Skip-thought vectors, which are trained on different datasets and not fairly compared. Additionally, the paper uses the test set for training in some experiments without clear labeling, which misleads readers about standard evaluation protocols.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim is that Doc2VecC outperforms existing methods while being more efficient. However, the evidence provided is insufficient for several reasons:

First, the comparison to Skip-thought vectors is fundamentally unfair. The paper states: "We used the generic model trained on a much bigger book corpus to encode the documents" for Skip-thought, but doesn't provide comparable results for Skip-thought trained on the IMDB dataset. The authors acknowledge Skip-thought performs poorly on IMDB due to paragraph length differences, yet they don't train Skip-thought on IMDB for fair comparison. This creates a misleading impression of Doc2VecC's relative performance.

Second, the paper uses the test set for training in some experiments (Table 1), which is common practice but should be clearly labeled as "representation learning using test set" rather than implying it's standard evaluation protocol. This obscures the true performance of the methods.

Third, the corruption rate q=0.9 is unusually high, and the paper doesn't explore how sensitive the results are to this parameter. The authors state "we found that both models are not sensitive to the corruption rate q," but provide no evidence for this claim through ablation studies.

Fourth, the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism is interesting but disconnected from the empirical results. The paper claims Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but provides no experimental evidence for this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a fair comparison with Skip-thought vectors by training them on the IMDB dataset (or at least acknowledge the unfairness of the comparison). The paper states Skip-thought vectors "perform surprisingly poor on this dataset," but without a fair comparison, this claim is unsubstantiated.

2. Clearly label all experiments where the test set is used for training, and provide results with proper train-test separation for standard evaluation.

3. Include an ablation study on the corruption rate q (e.g., q=0.5, 0.7, 0.9) to demonstrate how performance varies with this critical hyperparameter.

4. Provide experimental evidence supporting the claim that Doc2VecC reduces to DEA when local context is removed.

5. Clarify the distinction between Doc2VecC and Word2Vec+IDF. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but doesn't sufficiently explain why this is different from Word2Vec+IDF with post-processing.

## Questions for the Authors
1. How sensitive is Doc2VecC to the corruption rate q? Can you provide results for q=0.5 and q=0.7 to demonstrate the trade-off between speed and performance?

2. Why did you choose to use the test set for training in some experiments (Table 1)? Is this standard practice for document representation learning, or should it be clearly labeled as a specific experimental protocol?

3. Can you provide a fair comparison to Skip-thought vectors by training them on the IMDB dataset, or at least acknowledge the unfairness of the comparison in your analysis?

4. Can you provide evidence that Doc2VecC reduces to DEA when local context words are removed, as claimed in the paper?

5. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Can you provide more detailed analysis of how this differs from Word2Vec+IDF, which also uses averaged word embeddings but with post-processing?

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in document representation learning with a simple and elegant approach, and the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism is interesting. However, the experimental evaluation lacks the rigor needed to confidently support the paper's claims, particularly regarding the comparison to Skip-thought vectors. With careful revision to address these concerns, the paper could make a valuable contribution to the field.
