

# Review

## Summary of Claims
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach to document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism during training that acts as a data-dependent regularization. The authors argue that this approach is more efficient than existing methods (like Paragraph Vectors) because it decouples model complexity from corpus size, enables fast test-time inference, and naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings. They provide theoretical justification for their corruption mechanism as a form of regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words, and demonstrate competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons
1. The core idea of representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism is novel and bold, challenging the prevailing assumption that document representations require unique document vectors. This simple yet powerful approach addresses a fundamental inefficiency in existing methods.
2. The paper successfully demonstrates that this simple approach can outperform or match more complex state-of-the-art methods across multiple document representation tasks, providing strong empirical validation for the novelty.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's central contribution is genuinely novel and addresses a meaningful inefficiency in document representation learning. While averaging word embeddings has been explored before (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), the authors make a crucial innovation by introducing the corruption mechanism during training, which enables the model to learn meaningful document representations through averaging. This is not just a minor tweak but a fundamental rethinking of how document representations can be learned.

The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is compelling, showing how the corruption mechanism naturally introduces a data-dependent regularization that favors informative words. The authors' explanation of how this regularization differs from standard dropout or subsampling (which is commonly used in Word2Vec) is insightful and adds theoretical depth to the approach.

The empirical results are robust across multiple tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness), with clear evidence of both performance gains and efficiency improvements. The ablation studies showing that Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF (which requires post-processing) and matches or exceeds Paragraph Vectors (which is more complex) are particularly convincing.

While the paper could have more clearly positioned itself against related work on averaging word embeddings, the novelty of the corruption mechanism during training is sufficiently distinct to justify the contribution. The authors have demonstrated that their approach is not just a theoretical curiosity but has practical advantages in both training and inference efficiency.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how Doc2VecC differs from standard Word2Vec applied to documents (e.g., using the average of word embeddings as document representation without the corruption mechanism). The current explanation in Section 3.1 is somewhat technical and could be made more accessible.

2. The authors should clarify the relationship between their corruption mechanism and standard dropout or data augmentation techniques. While they mention similarities, a more direct comparison would strengthen their theoretical contribution.

3. The paper mentions that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't provide evidence of this equivalence in the experimental results. Including a comparison with DEA would help validate this theoretical claim.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that Doc2VecC "outperforms Word2Vec+IDF by a significant margin," but the results in Table 1 show only a 1-2% improvement. Could you provide more specific evidence of the significance of this improvement beyond just the numbers?

2. In Section 4.2, you mention that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to paragraph length, but you don't compare with other sentence encoding methods that handle longer sequences. How would Doc2VecC compare to recent methods designed for longer text sequences (e.g., Transformer-based approaches)?

3. The corruption rate q=0.9 is used throughout the experiments, but the paper doesn't explore the sensitivity of results to different values of q. Could you provide some analysis of how performance changes with different corruption rates?
