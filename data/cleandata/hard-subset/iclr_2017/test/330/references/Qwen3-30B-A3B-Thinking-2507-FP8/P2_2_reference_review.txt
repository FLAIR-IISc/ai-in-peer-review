

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a new model for document representation learning. The authors address the limitations of existing approaches like Paragraph Vectors (which have parameters that scale with corpus size and require expensive inference for unseen documents) by proposing a model that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The key contributions are:

1. A model architecture where document representation is simply the average of word embeddings, with the corruption mechanism ensuring that this averaging process captures meaningful document semantics during training.
2. A theoretical justification showing that the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words.
3. Empirical results demonstrating that Doc2VecC outperforms several baselines (including BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors) on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and makes a reasonable contribution to the document representation literature by providing a simple yet effective alternative to more complex models.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a solid contribution to document representation learning. The key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper identifies a clear problem (the drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors) and proposes a well-motivated solution that is both theoretically grounded and empirically effective.
2. The results demonstrate clear improvements over multiple baselines across multiple tasks, with significant advantages in training and inference efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Strengths

- **Clear Problem Statement and Motivation**: The paper effectively identifies the limitations of Paragraph Vectors (parameters scaling with corpus size, expensive inference for unseen documents) and provides a strong theoretical motivation for their approach based on the observation that linear operations on word embeddings preserve semantic meaning.

- **Theoretical Justification**: Section 3.1 provides a compelling theoretical analysis showing how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization. The derivation of the regularization term (equation 7) is rigorous and well-explained, making a strong case for why the approach works.

- **Strong Empirical Results**: The paper demonstrates consistent improvements over multiple baselines across three different tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness). The results are presented clearly with appropriate baselines and ablation studies (e.g., Table 3 showing the effect of regularization on word embeddings).

- **Efficiency Advantage**: The paper makes a strong case for the efficiency of Doc2VecC, with training and inference time comparisons (Table 2) showing significant advantages over Paragraph Vectors and other methods. This is particularly important for practical applications.

- **Appropriate Baselines**: The authors compare against a comprehensive set of relevant baselines, including BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors, and others that are commonly used in the field.

### Minor Concerns (not sufficient to reject)

- The paper doesn't directly compare to some of the more recent transformer-based document representation methods (like BERT), but this is understandable given the paper's focus on simpler, more efficient models that build on the Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors line of work.

- The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally scales to large datasets" like Word2Vec, but doesn't provide actual training times for very large datasets (beyond the IMDB dataset). However, the theoretical justification and the efficiency claims are reasonable.

- The corruption rate (q) is set to 0.9 throughout, and while the paper notes it's not sensitive to this parameter, a more thorough analysis of how different q values affect performance would be valuable.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify the Corruption Mechanism**: The paper describes the corruption as "randomly removing words from a document," but doesn't fully explain how this works for very short documents. For example, if a document has only 5 words and q=0.9, the expected number of remaining words is 0.5. How does the model handle this? A sentence or two explaining how the model deals with extremely short documents would be helpful.

2. **Compare to Recent Work**: While the paper is focused on the Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors line of work, it would be valuable to briefly mention how Doc2VecC compares to more recent methods like BERT or other transformer-based approaches, even if only to acknowledge the different design space.

3. **Statistical Significance**: The paper reports performance improvements but doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these improvements. Adding a brief analysis of statistical significance (e.g., confidence intervals or p-values) would strengthen the empirical claims.

4. **More Detail on Implementation**: The paper mentions using negative sampling, but doesn't specify the negative sample size or other hyperparameters. Including a brief note about these implementation details would help with reproducibility.

5. **Discussion of Limitations**: The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of limitations, particularly regarding short documents or documents with highly domain-specific vocabulary where the averaging approach might be less effective.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed from the paradigm shown in Figure 1." Can you provide a more detailed explanation of this relationship and perhaps include a brief comparison of the two approaches?

2. For the semantic relatedness task, the paper states that "Doc2VecC significantly outperforms the winning solutions of the competition" but is slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Given that the authors note "LSTM-based methods are superior for relatively short sentences (of length in the order of 10s)," could you elaborate on why Doc2VecC performs better on the IMDB dataset (which has longer paragraphs) but worse on the SICK dataset (which has shorter sentences)?

3. The paper mentions that "the vector representation of a document can be generated by simply averaging the learned word embeddings of all the words in the document." However, the corruption mechanism during training only uses a subset of words. How does this affect the document representation at test time, where all words are used? Is there a theoretical guarantee that the averaging process will be consistent between training and test?

4. The paper uses a cutoff of 10 for vocabulary in the sentiment analysis task. How sensitive is the performance to this cutoff parameter, and how does it compare to the effect of the corruption rate (q) on performance?

This paper makes a solid contribution to document representation learning with a simple yet effective approach. With the minor clarifications mentioned above, it would be a strong addition to the ICLR proceedings.
