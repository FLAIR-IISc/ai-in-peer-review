

# Review for "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning model that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism during training. The authors claim this approach offers several advantages: (1) model complexity decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size), (2) efficient training and testing (no document-specific vectors), (3) implicit data-dependent regularization favoring informative/rare words, and (4) competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's core idea is not sufficiently novel, and the empirical claims are not adequately supported against the most relevant baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The main issue is that the core idea of using word embedding averages for document representation has been explored before (Huang et al., 2012), and the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate that the proposed corruption mechanism provides significant advantages over simpler approaches. The authors mention Huang et al. but don't clearly differentiate their approach, making it difficult to assess the novelty.

The empirical results are limited in scope. The paper compares to some older baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors) but omits the most direct comparison: using simple word embedding averages without any corruption (which is essentially what Huang et al. did). The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings, but provides no evidence that this enforcement is necessary or superior to simply using averages.

The theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as a "data-dependent regularization" is complex and not well-connected to the empirical results. The paper doesn't show that this regularization effect is actually causing the performance improvements, or that it's different from standard techniques like subsampling frequent words (which the authors acknowledge is used in Word2Vec).

Furthermore, the paper's claim that Doc2VecC "outperforms the state-of-the-art" is misleading. The baselines include some weaker approaches (like BoW), but the paper doesn't compare to more recent strong methods (e.g., transformer-based models, BERT, etc.) that have since become the standard for text understanding tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Clearly distinguish their approach from Huang et al. (2012) and other similar work, explicitly stating what makes their corruption mechanism necessary and how it leads to demonstrably better results.

2. Add a direct comparison to the simplest baseline: using word embedding averages without any corruption. This is crucial to establish whether the corruption mechanism actually provides benefits.

3. Provide a more thorough comparison to the current state-of-the-art document representation methods. The paper should acknowledge that since its submission, methods like BERT have become the standard for many NLP tasks.

4. Clarify the relationship between the corruption mechanism and the regularization effect. Show that the regularization is indeed causing the performance improvements, rather than just being a side effect.

5. Explain why the corruption mechanism is needed when standard techniques like subsampling frequent words already exist and are used in Word2Vec.

## Questions for Authors
1. How does your corruption mechanism differ from standard subsampling of frequent words (used in Word2Vec), and why is your approach necessary when subsampling already exists?

2. Why did you not compare to the simplest baseline of using word embedding averages (without corruption) as your document representation? This would be the most direct test of your core claim.

3. The paper claims Doc2VecC "outperforms the state-of-the-art" but doesn't compare to many recent strong baselines. How does Doc2VecC compare to more recent document representation methods like BERT or other transformer-based approaches?

4. In the theoretical analysis, the paper claims the corruption mechanism introduces "data-dependent regularization," but doesn't provide evidence that this is the primary reason for performance improvements. Can you provide ablation studies isolating the effect of this regularization?

5. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't explain how this relationship helps justify the approach. How does this connection inform your method's design?
