

# Peer Review: Doc2VecC: Document Vector through Corruption

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel model for learning document representations by representing each document as the average of word embeddings, with a key innovation being the use of a corruption mechanism during training where words are randomly removed from documents. The authors claim this approach has several advantages: (1) model complexity is decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size), (2) efficient training and inference, (3) implicit data-dependent regularization that favors rare/informative words, and (4) simple test-time representation (just averaging word embeddings). The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly faster to train and test.

The paper makes a compelling connection to the word vector compositionality phenomenon (e.g., "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen"), suggesting that document semantics can be captured by averaging word vectors. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 attempts to justify the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization, which is an interesting theoretical contribution.

## 2. Decision

**Weak Accept** with major revisions required.

The paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to document representation learning with clear computational advantages. The core idea of using simple averaging with a corruption mechanism is elegant and the empirical results are generally supportive of the claims. However, the paper requires significant revisions to strengthen its theoretical justification, clarify its contributions relative to existing work, and address several limitations in the experimental design.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Key Strengths:
- The simplicity of the model architecture is appealing and aligns with the trend toward efficient neural models.
- The corruption mechanism provides a clear theoretical justification (Section 3.1) for why simple averaging can work well, which is a valuable contribution.
- The experimental results are comprehensive across multiple tasks and show consistent improvements over strong baselines.
- The efficiency claims are well-supported by the timing results in Table 2.

### Key Weaknesses Requiring Revision:

**1. Overstated Claims and Limited Performance Gains:**
The paper claims Doc2VecC "matches or beats the state-of-the-art" but the performance gains over Paragraph Vectors are marginal (e.g., 1-2% on sentiment analysis in Table 1). The paper should be more precise about the magnitude of improvements and avoid overstating the significance of these gains. The phrase "matches or beats" is misleading given the small improvements.

**2. Theoretical Justification Needs Strengthening:**
The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is complex and not particularly clear. The connection between the corruption mechanism and the regularization effect needs to be made more intuitive. The paper should provide a more concrete example of how the regularization works in practice, rather than relying solely on the Taylor expansion argument.

**3. Missing Comparison to Basic Averaging:**
The paper compares to Word2Vec+IDF but doesn't compare directly to a simple average of word embeddings (without any corruption). This is a critical baseline to establish whether the corruption mechanism is actually necessary for good performance. If simple averaging already works well, then the corruption mechanism may not be adding much value.

**4. Ambiguous Corruption Mechanism:**
The paper states "we found that both models are not sensitive to the corruption rate q" but doesn't provide evidence for this claim. The choice of q=0.9 throughout the experiments is arbitrary without justification. The paper should include ablation studies showing how performance varies with different corruption rates.

**5. Document Length Sensitivity:**
The paper claims Doc2VecC is better for longer documents (based on its good performance on IMDB vs. Skip-thought's poor performance), but doesn't explicitly test how performance varies with document length. It should include an analysis of how Doc2VecC performs on documents of different lengths.

**6. Limited Explanation of Word Embedding Quality:**
The paper claims Doc2VecC produces "significantly better" word embeddings than Word2Vec (Table 5), but doesn't explain why this is the case. The corruption mechanism is supposed to be a regularization for document representation, not necessarily for word embeddings. The paper should clarify whether this improvement in word embeddings is a direct result of the corruption mechanism or a side effect.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

**To Strengthen the Paper:**

1. **Clarify the Core Contribution:** The paper should more clearly distinguish its contribution from existing work. The idea of representing documents as averages of word embeddings has been explored before (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). The paper should better articulate what makes Doc2VecC different and why it works better.

2. **Include the Basic Averaging Baseline:** Add a comparison to simple word embedding averaging (without corruption) to show whether the corruption mechanism is truly necessary for good performance.

3. **Provide More Intuitive Explanation of the Regularization:** The theoretical analysis is strong but not intuitive. Add a simple example showing how the corruption mechanism affects specific words (e.g., how it suppresses stop words versus informative words).

4. **Analyze Document Length Effects:** Include a figure showing how performance varies with document length to better justify the claim about handling longer documents.

5. **Clarify the Relationship to Denoising Autoencoders:** The paper mentions that Doc2VecC reduces to DEA without local context, but doesn't explain why this is the case. This should be clarified.

6. **Address Vocabulary Expansion:** The paper mentions extending vocabulary on the target dataset via fine-tuning but doesn't explain how this affects the corruption mechanism.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In Table 3, Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words, but how does this affect the ability to capture semantic relationships (e.g., the word analogy task in Section 4.2)? The paper shows Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings, but the authors should explain why the corruption mechanism improves word embeddings rather than just document representations.

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but how does this compare to the document representation learned by Paragraph Vectors? What specific aspects of the document semantics are better captured?

3. The corruption mechanism is described as "unbiased" but the paper doesn't explain why the scaling factor 1/(1-q) is necessary for unbiasedness. Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of this?

4. In the document classification experiments (Section 4.4), Doc2VecC achieves better results with smaller vector representations than other methods. The paper claims this is because of the "data-dependent regularization," but could this also be due to the simpler averaging mechanism reducing overfitting? How would you distinguish between these possibilities?

5. The paper mentions that "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃" but doesn't explain why this would make training faster. Could you clarify how the corruption mechanism actually leads to faster training?

This paper has a strong foundation with an interesting and potentially valuable contribution to document representation learning. With the revisions suggested above, it would make a solid contribution to the field. The current version needs to better justify its claims and clarify the relationship between its method and existing approaches.
