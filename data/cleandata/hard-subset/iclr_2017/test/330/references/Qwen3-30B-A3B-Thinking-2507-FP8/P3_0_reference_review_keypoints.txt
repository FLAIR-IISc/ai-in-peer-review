- Proposes Doc2VecC: document vectors formed by averaging word embeddings of a randomly corrupted (word-removed) document during training.  
- Claims the corruption acts as data-dependent regularization that downweights common/uninformative words and favors informative/rare words.  
- Claims model complexity depends only on vocabulary size (not corpus size) and requires no expensive inference for unseen documents.  
- Claims high training/testing efficiency and matching or outperforming state-of-the-art on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Provides comprehensive experiments and timing comparisons supporting empirical claims.  
- Situates method clearly with prior work (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) and acknowledges related approaches.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions due to strong empirical contribution despite some issues.  
- Strength: Strong empirical results across multiple benchmarks with statistical comparisons.  
- Strength: Clear and significant efficiency advantages over Paragraph Vectors and similar methods.  
- Strength: Simple, elegant, and easy-to-implement approach building on existing ideas.  
- Strength: Well-positioned in the literature, addressing Paragraph Vectors’ complexity limitations.  
- Strength: Provides a Taylor expansion-based theoretical argument that corruption induces regularization (though can be improved).  
- Weakness: Inconsistency about subsampling—paper claims subsampling unnecessary yet experiments appear to use it.  
- Weakness: No ablation study to isolate effect of corruption versus plain averaging or standard dropout.  
- Weakness: Theoretical justification via Taylor expansion is reasonable but doesn't fully explain why performance improves.  
- Weakness: Missing direct comparison to earlier simple-averaging methods (e.g., Huang et al., 2012).  
- Feedback: Clarify whether subsampling is required or why it was used in experiments despite claims otherwise.  
- Feedback: Add ablation study comparing (a) plain averaging, (b) Doc2VecC corruption, and (c) averaging with standard dropout.  
- Feedback: Strengthen theoretical link between the induced regularization term and observed performance gains.  
- Feedback: Directly compare Doc2VecC to Huang et al. (2012) and similar averaging baselines.  
- Feedback: Clarify that the corruption is scaled dropout (1/(1-q) scaling) rather than simple word removal.  
- Question: Does Doc2VecC truly obviate subsampling, or does it still benefit from subsampling of frequent words?  
- Question: Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on word analogy tasks—architecture limitation or training data issue?  
- Question: Could suppression of common words be due to smaller effective vocabulary from corruption rather than the corruption mechanism itself?  
- Question: Clarify the relationship between Doc2VecC and Denoising Autoencoders given different reconstruction/prediction objectives.  
- Question: How does Doc2VecC training time scale with corpus size and document length compared to Word2Vec’s sliding-window approach?
