

# Review of "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning method that represents documents as averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism during training. The authors claim this approach introduces data-dependent regularization that favors rare/informative words, avoids the need for expensive inference on unseen documents, and outperforms existing methods on multiple text understanding tasks. The core claim is that Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple methodological inconsistencies and technical inaccuracies that undermine its core claims and make the experimental results unreliable.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Major Methodological Inconsistencies:**
- The paper confuses Paragraph Vectors (PV) variants. PV-DBOW (distributed bag-of-words) does NOT require per-document parameters, so its model complexity does NOT grow with corpus size. Only PV-DM (distributed memory) has this property. The paper incorrectly states "the number of parameters grows with the size of the training corpus" for Paragraph Vectors without distinguishing between the two variants, which is a fundamental error in the literature review.
  
- The claim that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" is incorrect. DEA is a reconstruction-based model, while Doc2VecC is a prediction-based model. They are fundamentally different approaches, and the paper fails to properly characterize DEA.

- Equation (7) contains a critical mathematical error: the term "x2ij" should be "xij" (since xij is binary, x2ij = xij). This error affects the interpretation of the regularization term.

- The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative" without providing clear evidence that this is different from standard Word2Vec training. The experiment in Table 3 comparing embedding norms doesn't demonstrate that Doc2VecC's regularization is fundamentally different from standard Word2Vec.

**Experimental Methodology Issues:**
- The comparison between Doc2VecC and Word2Vec+IDF is unfair. Word2Vec+IDF uses a post-processing step (IDF weighting) that Doc2VecC doesn't need. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics, but this is exactly what Word2Vec+IDF does with IDF weighting.

- The paper claims "Doc2VecC significantly out-performs the winning solutions of the competition" on SICK dataset, but doesn't provide sufficient detail about the winning solutions to make a fair comparison. The paper doesn't specify which winning solutions were compared to.

- In Section 4.2, they state "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃." This is factually incorrect. The number of parameters remains fixed (size of vocabulary), while the corruption affects the number of words used in the forward pass, not the number of parameters to update.

- The paper fails to provide a clear ablation study showing that Doc2VecC without subsampling performs better than Word2Vec+AVG without subsampling, which is necessary to support the claim that Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick" (subsampling frequent words).

**Formatting and Presentation Issues:**
- The paper contains inconsistent references (e.g., "Mikolov et al., 2013a" vs. "Mikolov et al., 2013a)").
- Section 3.1 contains a missing coefficient 1/2 in the Taylor expansion.
- The paper claims "Doc2VecC has several desirable properties" but Property 4 (simple document representation) is exactly what Word2Vec+AVG does, so it's not a novel advantage.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the fundamental confusion between PV-DM and PV-DBOW, and properly characterize Paragraph Vectors.
2. Revisit the mathematical formulations, particularly the corruption mechanism (Equation 1) and the regularization derivation (Equation 7), to fix the errors.
3. Conduct a proper ablation study to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism from other factors.
4. Clarify the comparison with Word2Vec+IDF by either including IDF weighting in Doc2VecC or removing it from Word2Vec+IDF for a fair comparison.
5. Provide more details about the winning solutions compared in the SICK dataset experiment.
6. Fix the formatting inconsistencies in references and equations.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify which Paragraph Vectors variant (PV-DM or PV-DBOW) you're comparing to in Section 2, and why you consider both to have complexity growing with corpus size?
2. How does Doc2VecC's regularization mechanism differ from standard dropout or L2 regularization, and why does it specifically favor rare words?
3. In the experiment comparing word embeddings on the analogy task (Section 4.3), did you train Word2Vec using the same hyperparameters as Doc2VecC (e.g., negative sampling parameters, learning rate, etc.) for a fair comparison?
4. In Table 3, how do you statistically validate that the differences in embedding norms between methods are meaningful, rather than due to random variation?
