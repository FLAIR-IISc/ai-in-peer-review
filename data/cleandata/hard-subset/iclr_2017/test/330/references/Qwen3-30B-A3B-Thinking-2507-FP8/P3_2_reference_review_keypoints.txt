- Proposes Doc2VecC: document vectors computed as simple averages of word embeddings with a random word-removal corruption during training.  
- Claims corruption acts as data-dependent regularization that downweights frequent/uninformative words and favors rare/informative words.  
- Advantages claimed: model complexity depends only on vocabulary size, fast training like Word2Vec, test-time efficiency (no per-document inference), and superior task performance.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Major reason 1: Novel, well-motivated approach addressing Paragraph Vectors' scalability limitation.  
- Major reason 2: Theoretical derivation showing corruption induces data-dependent regularization is compelling.  
- Major reason 3: Strong empirical results across multiple tasks supporting claims.  
- Theoretical contribution: Corruption mechanism derivation shows heavier penalty on frequent-word embeddings, justifying noise-as-regularizer.  
- Sentiment (IMDB): Doc2VecC outperforms baselines significantly and explains Skip-Thoughts' weakness on long texts.  
- Word analogy: Doc2VecC yields substantially better word embeddings than Word2Vec; Paragraph Vectors perform poorly.  
- Document classification (Wikipedia): Outperforms baselines including Paragraph Vectors with clearer cluster separation (t-SNE).  
- Semantic relatedness (SICK): Outperforms competition winners and baselines; LSTMs still better on short sentences but Doc2VecC is much faster.  
- Ablation: Table 3 supports that corruption suppresses common-word embeddings, validating the regularization claim.  
- Efficiency: Training time comparable to Word2Vec and much faster test-time inference than Paragraph Vectors.  
- Major revision concerns: missing comparisons to transformers (e.g., BERT), lack of explicit ablation removing corruption, unclear intuition/justification for unbiased scaling factor, and unclear differentiation from Huang et al. (2012).  
- Minor concerns: sensitivity to q=0.9 not analyzed, theoretical section is dense and needs more intuition, and unclear preprocessing/subsampling details across methods.  
- Questions for authors: request intuitive explanation of data-dependent regularization, distinction from weighted/learned averages like Word2Vec+IDF, concrete example of corrupted embeddings improving downstream discrimination, and reconciliation of Doc2VecC outperforming more expressive Paragraph Vectors.  
- Final assessment: Strong, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective contribution worthy of acceptance, with some suggested clarifications and additional comparisons.
