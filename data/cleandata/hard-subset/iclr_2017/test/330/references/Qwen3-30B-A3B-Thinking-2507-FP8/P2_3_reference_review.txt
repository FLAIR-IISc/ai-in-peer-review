

# Review of "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective model for document representation learning. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings (rather than learning unique document vectors as in Paragraph Vectors), with a key innovation being a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The paper claims Doc2VecC achieves several desirable properties: (1) model complexity independent of corpus size, (2) efficient training similar to Word2Vec, (3) implicit data-dependent regularization that favors informative words, (4) simple test-time inference via averaging, and (5) competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The authors provide theoretical justification for their corruption mechanism as a form of regularization and present strong empirical results across multiple benchmarks.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning by offering a simple, efficient approach that outperforms existing methods while addressing practical limitations of current approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**

1. **Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper effectively identifies two key limitations of Paragraph Vectors (model complexity scaling with corpus size and expensive test-time inference) and provides a compelling motivation for their approach based on the compositional properties of word embeddings (citing Mikolov et al., 2013b). The connection to Word2Vec and the observation that simple averaging can capture document semantics is well-placed in the literature.

2. **Strong empirical results:** The paper demonstrates consistent performance improvements across multiple tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) compared to multiple strong baselines. The results are well-presented with appropriate statistical significance (e.g., Table 1 shows Doc2VecC outperforms BoW by 15% on IMDB sentiment analysis). The efficiency gains are particularly compelling (Table 2 shows Doc2VecC is 36x faster than Paragraph Vectors at test time).

3. **Novelty of the corruption mechanism:** While the idea of averaging word embeddings for document representation has been explored before (Huang et al., 2012), the authors' introduction of the corruption mechanism during training as a form of data-dependent regularization is novel and well-justified through experiments (Table 3 shows Doc2VecC better suppresses embeddings of common, uninformative words).

4. **Practical value:** The simplicity of the approach (no need for document-specific vectors, no expensive inference for unseen documents) makes it highly appealing for real-world applications, which is a significant contribution to the field.

**Weaknesses:**

1. **Theoretical justification needs strengthening:** The derivation in Section 3.1 that connects the corruption mechanism to the regularization term is somewhat hand-wavy. The Taylor expansion approach is not clearly explained, and it's not entirely clear how the corruption process directly leads to the specific regularization term they derive. A more rigorous theoretical treatment would strengthen the paper.

2. **Comparison to similar approaches:** While the paper acknowledges prior work using average word embeddings (Huang et al., 2012), it doesn't sufficiently contrast Doc2VecC with this approach or explain why their corruption mechanism leads to significantly better results. The paper claims "heuristic weighting at test time is not required" but doesn't demonstrate how their corruption mechanism specifically overcomes limitations of previous averaging approaches.

3. **Skip-thought Vector explanation:** The paper hypothesizes that Skip-thought Vectors perform poorly on IMDB due to paragraph length, but this is not thoroughly tested or explained. A more detailed analysis of why Skip-thought Vectors struggle with longer paragraphs would strengthen their argument.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the theoretical contribution:** The paper should provide a more rigorous derivation of how the corruption process leads to the specific regularization term. A clearer explanation of how the corruption mechanism differs from standard dropout or denoising autoencoders would be valuable.

2. **Better contrast with prior averaging approaches:** The paper should explicitly compare Doc2VecC to the simplest possible averaging approach (without corruption) to demonstrate the specific contribution of their corruption mechanism. This would strengthen the claim that the corruption is the key innovation.

3. **Expand on the regularization explanation:** The paper could include a visualization or additional analysis showing how the corruption mechanism affects word embedding quality, particularly for common words versus rare words.

4. **Include ablation studies:** A simple ablation study showing performance with and without the corruption mechanism would provide stronger evidence for its importance.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more details on the connection between your corruption mechanism and standard dropout techniques? How is your approach different from simply applying dropout to the word embeddings during training?

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Could you elaborate on why this is different from simply training word embeddings on the document-level task (e.g., using a document-level loss function)?

3. In Table 5, Doc2VecC achieves better performance than Paragraph Vectors across all vector sizes. However, Paragraph Vectors have a more sophisticated model architecture. Could you provide more insight into why the simpler Doc2VecC approach outperforms the more complex Paragraph Vectors?

4. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could you clarify this relationship? How does the absence of local context in the document representation affect the relationship between Doc2VecC and DEA?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to document representation learning. The empirical results are convincing, the approach is simple and efficient, and the paper is well-written. With minor revisions to strengthen the theoretical justification and better contextualize the contribution relative to prior work, this paper would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
