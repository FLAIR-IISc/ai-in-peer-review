- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings trained with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training to provide data-dependent regularization.  
- Corruption regularization suppresses common/uninformative word embeddings and favors rare/discriminative ones, with theoretical derivation in Section 3.1.  
- Advantages claimed: model size depends only on vocabulary, Word2Vec-like efficient training, implicit regularization, extremely fast test-time inference, and competitive or state-of-the-art performance.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, showing strong performance and efficiency; includes ablation on corruption rate and embedding analysis.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Key strengths: clear, novel contribution; well-motivated relative to Paragraph Vectors; solid theoretical justification and strong empirical support.  
- Clarify corruption mechanism notation/intuition (explain the 1/(1-q) scaling to show E[x̃] = x).  
- Compare or discuss relation to more recent methods (e.g., Universal Sentence Encoder, BERT-based representations) to better position the work.  
- Provide more implementation details and ablation on different corruption rates q beyond the reported q = 0.9.  
- Improve t-SNE visualization analysis by identifying which words/features drive cluster separation.  
- Add a limitations section discussing performance on very short documents (e.g., tweets) and on documents with unusual structure or non-standard language.  
- Ask for concrete examples of words that are heavily vs. lightly regularized to illustrate the data-dependent regularization.  
- Request elaboration on how Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders and how document vectors differ between the two.  
- Ask for computational resource details (CPU/GPU, memory, training time) when comparing Doc2VecC to skip-thought vectors.  
- Request a quantitative comparison of Doc2VecC with and without the subsampling trick used in Word2Vec+AVG to demonstrate the claimed advantage.
