## Summary of the Paper's Claims
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach to document representation learning that fundamentally challenges the prevailing paradigm of using document-specific vectors. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a key innovation being the use of a corruption mechanism during training (randomly removing words from documents). This simple yet powerful approach enables the model to implicitly learn meaningful document representations through the averaging process, while simultaneously introducing a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC outperforms existing methods on multiple tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

## Decision
Accept. The paper presents a bold, novel approach to document representation learning that challenges conventional wisdom. The core idea—using document representations as simple averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism—is both simple and powerful, offering significant efficiency gains without sacrificing performance.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in its novelty and conceptual boldness. While the idea of using word embeddings to represent documents is not entirely new (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), the authors' key innovation is the corruption mechanism that enables the simple averaging approach to work effectively. This is a significant conceptual leap that transforms a naive approach into a powerful method.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is particularly strong, demonstrating how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization that naturally suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words. This provides a principled justification for why the simple averaging approach works well, which is often missing in similar proposals.

The empirical results are compelling across multiple tasks and datasets. Doc2VecC consistently outperforms baselines while being significantly faster to train and test. The ablation studies (Tables 3 and 4) provide clear evidence of the regularization effect, showing how Doc2VecC handles common words differently than existing methods. The efficiency gains are substantial—Doc2VecC is orders of magnitude faster to generate document representations than Paragraph Vectors, which requires expensive inference for unseen documents.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly situating its contribution relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and other document representation methods. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches (particularly the high model complexity of Paragraph Vectors) and show how their approach addresses these limitations.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of why the specific corruption mechanism (with the scaling factor 1/(1-q)) was chosen over other forms of corruption. This is critical to understanding why the method works.

2. The sensitivity analysis of the corruption rate q is minimal. A brief exploration of how performance changes with different q values would strengthen the argument for the chosen value (q=0.9).

3. While the paper compares against several baselines, it would be valuable to include a comparison with more recent document representation methods (e.g., those using transformers) to demonstrate how Doc2VecC stands against the state-of-the-art as of today.

4. The connection to dropout and denoising autoencoders in Section 3.1 is interesting but could be developed further to show how Doc2VecC fits into the broader landscape of regularization techniques.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings. Could you elaborate on how this differs from other approaches that use weighted averages (e.g., Word2Vec+IDF), which also attempt to form document representations from word embeddings?

2. In Table 3, Doc2VecC shows better performance on common words, but what happens when the corruption rate q is set to 0 (no corruption)? Would the method degrade to a simple average of word embeddings, and how would performance compare to Word2Vec+AVG?

3. The paper notes that Skip-thought Vectors perform poorly on IMDB due to paragraph length. Given that Doc2VecC is designed for longer documents, could you elaborate on how the corruption mechanism specifically helps with longer documents compared to other methods?
