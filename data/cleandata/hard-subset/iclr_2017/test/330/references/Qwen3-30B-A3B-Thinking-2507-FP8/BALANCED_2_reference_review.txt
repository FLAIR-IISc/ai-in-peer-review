

# Review of "Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel approach to document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The authors claim this method is more efficient than existing approaches like Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014) because it decouples model complexity from corpus size, avoids expensive inference for unseen documents, and implicitly introduces a data-dependent regularization through a corruption mechanism. The paper presents empirical results across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, claiming Doc2VecC matches or exceeds state-of-the-art performance while being significantly more efficient.

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the paper presents a reasonable approach, the core contribution lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical grounding to warrant publication at ICLR. The main idea (using average word embeddings for document representation) has been previously proposed by Huang et al. (2012), and the proposed corruption mechanism does not provide a compelling advancement over standard techniques like subsampling frequent words.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The key reasons for rejection are:

1. **Lack of Novelty**: The core idea of representing documents as averages of word embeddings is not new (Huang et al., 2012). The paper's main contribution is the corruption mechanism during training, but this is not sufficiently distinct from standard subsampling techniques used in Word2Vec. The authors claim their corruption provides "stronger regularization" than subsampling, but fail to provide convincing evidence that this is actually the case.

2. **Weak Theoretical Justification**: The paper's theoretical argument about "data-dependent regularization" through Taylor expansion is unnecessarily complex and doesn't clearly connect to the empirical benefits. The relationship between the corruption mechanism and the observed performance gains is not convincingly established. The paper states Doc2VecC "does not rely on" subsampling, but doesn't demonstrate that their corruption provides superior regularization compared to standard subsampling.

3. **Questionable Experimental Design**: The comparison with Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB is problematic because Skip-thought was trained on a different corpus (book corpus) than the IMDB reviews, and the authors don't address this potential bias. Additionally, the paper claims Doc2VecC "outperforms" Skip-thought on IMDB (11.70% vs 17.42% error rate), but Skip-thought was designed for shorter sentences while IMDB reviews are longer, making the comparison potentially unfair.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify the Novelty**: The paper should explicitly acknowledge and contrast with Huang et al. (2012) and other prior work on average word embeddings. The authors should clearly articulate why their corruption mechanism provides a meaningful improvement over simply using standard subsampling techniques.

- **Simplify Theoretical Argument**: The Taylor expansion justification is overly complex. The paper would benefit from a simpler, more intuitive explanation of how the corruption mechanism affects the learning process and why it might be beneficial.

- **Strengthen Experimental Comparisons**: The paper should include more careful comparisons, particularly with Skip-thought Vectors. The authors should either train Skip-thought on the IMDB corpus or acknowledge the potential bias in their comparison.

- **Report Statistical Significance**: The paper claims improvements over baselines but doesn't provide statistical significance tests. Including standard deviations or p-values would strengthen the empirical claims.

- **Clarify Corruption Mechanism**: The paper should provide more evidence that the corruption rate q is not sensitive to performance, as claimed. A plot of performance across different q values would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, therefore does not rely on this trick [subsampling]". Can you provide evidence that the regularization provided by your corruption mechanism is actually stronger than standard subsampling? For example, by showing that the learned word embeddings with Doc2VecC have different properties than with Word2Vec+subsample?

2. The paper states Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Can you clarify the relationship between Doc2VecC and DEA? Specifically, how does your corruption mechanism differ from the corruption used in DEA?

3. In Table 3, Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words. How does this specifically benefit document classification? Can you provide examples of how this suppression affects classification performance (e.g., which words are being suppressed and how this impacts sentiment classification)?

4. The paper claims Doc2VecC "outperforms" LSTM-based methods on the IMDB dataset but is worse on the SICK dataset. Can you clarify why Doc2VecC performs better on longer documents (like IMDB reviews) but worse on shorter sentences (like SICK)? This seems inconsistent with the paper's claim that the corruption mechanism improves generalization.
