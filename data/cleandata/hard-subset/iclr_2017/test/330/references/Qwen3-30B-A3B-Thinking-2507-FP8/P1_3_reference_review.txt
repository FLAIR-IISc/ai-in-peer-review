

# Peer Review for "Document Vector through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel model for learning document representations that addresses key limitations of existing approaches. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, but with a crucial innovation: they incorporate a corruption mechanism during training that randomly removes words from documents. This corruption mechanism serves as a data-dependent regularization that favors rare/informative words while suppressing common, non-discriminative words. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC offers several advantages over existing methods: (1) model complexity decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size), (2) efficient training and inference similar to Word2Vec, (3) implicit data-dependent regularization, and (4) superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for the corruption mechanism and comprehensive empirical validation across multiple benchmarks.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The key reasons are: (1) the method is novel, well-motivated, and addresses a practical problem in document representation learning, and (2) the empirical results are thorough, scientifically rigorous, and demonstrate clear advantages over multiple baselines across multiple tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning with several key strengths:

**Novelty and Motivation:** The authors correctly identify limitations in existing approaches (Paragraph Vectors' high parameter count and slow inference, simple averaging's lack of semantic capture during training) and propose a solution that addresses these issues. The corruption mechanism is well-motivated by denoising autoencoders and dropout, and the connection to data-dependent regularization is theoretically sound.

**Theoretical Foundation:** The paper provides a compelling theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism through Taylor expansion (Section 3.1), deriving the data-dependent regularization term (equation 7). This isn't just an empirical observation but has a solid mathematical basis.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experiments are comprehensive and well-designed, covering three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) with multiple baselines including BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors, and others. The ablation studies (corruption rate sensitivity, word embedding visualization) provide strong support for the claims.

**Efficiency Claims:** The paper convincingly demonstrates efficiency advantages: Doc2VecC achieves 4 minutes 17 seconds inference time for Paragraph Vectors' 25,000 test documents (vs. 7 seconds for Doc2VecC), and trains in under 2 hours (vs. 2 weeks for Skip-thought Vectors). This is a significant practical advantage for real-world applications.

**Strong Results:** Doc2VecC outperforms all baselines on IMDB sentiment analysis (beating BoW by 15% error rate), achieves state-of-the-art results on Wikipedia document classification (30.24% error rate with h=1000 vs. 33.02% for Paragraph Vectors), and significantly outperforms competition-winning solutions on SICK semantic relatedness (11.70% error rate).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would enhance it further:

- **Clarity of Corruption Mechanism:** The scaling factor 1/(1-q) in equation (1) is stated as necessary for "unbiased corruption" but could be explained more intuitively. A simple example showing why this scaling is necessary would help readers understand the mechanism.

- **Sensitivity Analysis:** The claim that Doc2VecC is "not sensitive to corruption rate q" is supported by minimal evidence. A figure showing performance across different q values (e.g., 0.1 to 0.9) would strengthen this claim.

- **Comparison to Other Averaging Methods:** The paper compares to Word2Vec+IDF but doesn't include other simple averaging approaches (e.g., TF-IDF weighting). Adding these would better contextualize Doc2VecC's advantage over standard averaging techniques.

- **Theoretical Limitations:** The paper doesn't discuss potential limitations of the approach, such as how it handles documents with very few words (e.g., short tweets or social media posts). A brief discussion would make the contribution more balanced.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could you clarify how the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC differs from standard denoising autoencoders, and why this specific form of corruption is more effective for document representation?

2. In Table 3, Doc2VecC clamps down on embeddings of frequent but uninformative words. Could you provide more detail on which specific words are affected (beyond the examples given) and how their frequency in the training corpus correlates with the regularization effect?

3. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative" without relying on subsampling. How does this compare to the subsampling technique used in Word2Vec (Mikolov & Dean, 2013), and under what conditions might Doc2VecC still benefit from additional subsampling?

4. For the semantic relatedness task on SICK, Doc2VecC performs slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Given that Doc2VecC is designed for longer documents, could you provide more insight into how document length affects the performance of different representation methods, and whether there's a specific length threshold where Doc2VecC starts to outperform LSTM-based approaches?

This paper makes a strong contribution to document representation learning with clear theoretical justification, thorough empirical validation, and significant practical advantages in efficiency. The authors have addressed a meaningful problem with a novel solution that advances the state of the art.
