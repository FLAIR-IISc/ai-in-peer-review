

# Peer Review for "Document Representation Through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel model for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation lies in a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training, which the authors claim provides data-dependent regularization favoring rare, informative words while suppressing common, uninformative ones. The authors argue that Doc2VecC addresses two key limitations of Paragraph Vectors: (1) model complexity growing with corpus size, and (2) expensive inference for unseen documents. The paper claims Doc2VecC matches or exceeds state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Weak Accept**

**Key reasons:**
1. The core idea of using document averages with corruption is novel and well-motivated, with strong empirical results across multiple tasks.
2. The paper's theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer is somewhat tenuous and requires stronger support.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the efficiency advantages of Doc2VecC. The training time (Table 2) shows clear benefits over Paragraph Vectors (which requires learning a unique vector per document) and Skip-thought Vectors (which requires expensive inference for document encoding). The test-time efficiency is particularly well-documented, with Doc2VecC taking only 7 seconds to encode 25,000 documents versus Paragraph Vectors' 4 minutes and 17 seconds.

The empirical results on sentiment analysis (Table 1), document classification (Table 5), and semantic relatedness (Table 6) are generally strong, showing consistent improvements over baselines, particularly on the IMDB sentiment dataset. The visualization in Figure 3 provides compelling evidence of better document clustering with Doc2VecC compared to Paragraph Vectors.

However, the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism (Section 3.1) is the paper's weakest point. The Taylor expansion approximation is not fully justified, and the paper does not clearly establish that the observed regularization effect is directly attributable to the corruption mechanism rather than other factors in the model. The claim that "Doc2VecC intrinsically minimizes" the regularized loss (Equation 7) is not rigorously proven - the derivation relies on approximations that may not hold in practice.

The comparison to Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB is potentially misleading. The authors note that Skip-thought was trained on much shorter paragraphs (order of 10 words) versus IMDB's average length of 296.5 words, which explains its poor performance. However, they do not conduct a fair comparison by training Skip-thought on the IMDB data itself, which would provide a more meaningful benchmark.

The claim that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" (Section 4.2) is interesting but not sufficiently explored. The paper does not investigate how the addition of local context (which is part of the Doc2VecC model) contributes to the performance gains beyond the corruption mechanism.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Strengthen the theoretical justification**: Provide more rigorous justification for the Taylor expansion approximation and clearly demonstrate that the regularization effect is directly attributable to the corruption mechanism. Consider adding ablation studies that remove the corruption mechanism while keeping other components constant.

2. **Fairer comparison to Skip-thought Vectors**: Train Skip-thought on the IMDB dataset (or at least on a dataset with similar paragraph lengths) to provide a more meaningful comparison. The current comparison is skewed by the mismatch in training data and paragraph lengths.

3. **Clarify the relationship to existing work**: The paper mentions Huang et al. (2012) as prior work on document averages, but does not clearly distinguish their approach from Doc2VecC. The key difference is the corruption mechanism during training - this should be emphasized more explicitly.

4. **Include error analysis**: Provide some qualitative examples of documents where Doc2VecC outperforms baselines, particularly highlighting how the regularization mechanism helps with common words.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." How exactly does the corruption mechanism ensure this? What evidence do you have that the document vectors generated by Doc2VecC capture global semantics better than those from Paragraph Vectors (which explicitly learn document vectors)?

2. In Table 3, Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of frequent but uninformative words. How does this relate to the regularization term R(uj) in Equation 7? Specifically, why does the regularization term depend on the confidence σw,c,x rather than just the frequency of the word?

3. The paper states that "Doc2VecC outperforms the existing algorithms by a significant margin" on Wikipedia classification (Table 5). How much of this improvement is attributable to the corruption mechanism versus the fact that Doc2VecC uses the same word embeddings as Word2Vec (which were trained on a different corpus than the Wikipedia dataset)?

4. The paper mentions that "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃." This seems counterintuitive - wouldn't a corrupted document with fewer words require fewer parameters to update? Could you clarify this point?

This paper presents a genuinely interesting approach to document representation learning with clear efficiency advantages. With stronger theoretical justification and fairer comparisons, it would make an excellent contribution to the field. The current work is promising but requires additional evidence to fully support the theoretical claims.
