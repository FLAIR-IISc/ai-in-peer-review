- Decision: Weak Accept with major revisions required.

- Core claim: Doc2VecC averages word embeddings with random-word corruption during training to produce efficient document vectors.

- Claimed advantages: model complexity independent of corpus size, fast training/inference, data-dependent regularization favoring informative words, and simple test-time averaging.

- Empirical claim: matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment, classification, and relatedness while being much faster.

- Strength: simple, efficient model architecture aligned with trends toward lightweight neural methods.

- Strength: corruption mechanism is given a theoretical justification as data-dependent regularization (Section 3.1).

- Strength: comprehensive experiments across tasks with timing results supporting efficiency claims.

- Weakness: performance gains over Paragraph Vectors are marginal (~1–2%), so claims like "matches or beats" are overstated.

- Weakness: theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) is complex and lacks intuitive, concrete examples of the regularization effect.

- Weakness: missing a critical baseline — simple averaging of word embeddings without corruption — to show corruption's added value.

- Weakness: corruption mechanism hyperparameter q=0.9 is used without ablation or evidence that performance is insensitive to q.

- Weakness: claims about better handling of long documents are unsupported by explicit analysis of performance vs. document length.

- Weakness: claim of producing "significantly better" word embeddings than Word2Vec is unexplained — unclear if it's a direct effect of corruption or a side effect.

- Suggestion: more clearly distinguish Doc2VecC's core contribution from prior work (e.g., averaging-based methods like Huang et al., 2012).

- Suggestion: include the basic averaging baseline (no corruption) and ablations over corruption rate.

- Suggestion: provide an intuitive example demonstrating how corruption suppresses stopwords and highlights informative words.

- Suggestion: analyze how performance varies with document length to support claims about long-document suitability.

- Suggestion: clarify the relationship to denoising autoencoders and why Doc2VecC reduces to DEA without local context.

- Suggestion: explain how vocabulary expansion/fine-tuning on a target dataset interacts with the corruption mechanism.

- Question: how does suppression of common words affect semantic relationships and word-analogy performance, and why do embeddings improve?

- Question: how do averaged-document semantics learned by Doc2VecC compare qualitatively to Paragraph Vectors — what semantics are better captured?

- Question: why is scaling by 1/(1-q) necessary for unbiasedness — provide an intuitive explanation.

- Question: could better results with smaller vectors be due to reduced overfitting from averaging rather than data-dependent regularization, and how to distinguish these effects?

- Question: how does increasing the number of surviving words per update change backpropagation cost, and why does corruption yield faster training?
