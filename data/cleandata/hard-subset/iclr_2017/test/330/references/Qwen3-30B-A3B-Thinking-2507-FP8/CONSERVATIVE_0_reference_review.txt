

# Review

## Summary of Paper Claims
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a new method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings with a corruption mechanism during training. The authors claim that their approach: 1) has model complexity decoupled from corpus size; 2) is efficient to train and test; 3) implicitly introduces data-dependent regularization that favors rare/meaningful words; and 4) outperforms established baselines on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The method builds on Word2Vec's idea of representing semantic relationships through vector arithmetic and introduces a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training, which the authors claim provides regularization benefits.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes an incremental contribution with insufficient evidence to justify its claims of significant improvements over established methods. The core idea of averaging word embeddings is not novel, and the paper fails to provide adequate evidence that the proposed corruption mechanism delivers the claimed advantages.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim of "data-dependent regularization" through corruption is theoretically complex but lacks sufficient empirical validation. The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is overly complicated and doesn't clearly demonstrate why this approach provides better regularization than simpler alternatives like standard dropout or denoising autoencoders (which they compare against but don't properly differentiate from).

Most critically, the paper doesn't provide ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the corruption mechanism. The claim that corruption provides "data-dependent regularization" is unsupported by evidence that the regularization is actually responsible for the performance gains. Without showing that removing the corruption mechanism leads to worse performance, the paper cannot credibly claim this is the source of the improvement.

The experimental validation is also insufficient. The paper compares against Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014), which is an older method, but doesn't compare against more recent state-of-the-art approaches like BERT or other transformer-based document representations. For example, the paper doesn't mention how Doc2VecC would perform against these modern methods, which have become the standard for document representation learning.

The paper's claim that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training" is questionable. The authors cite Huang et al. (2012) as having proposed the same idea (averaging word embeddings), and they don't clearly demonstrate that their corruption mechanism makes this approach significantly better than the standard averaging approach.

The comparison to Skip-thought Vectors is problematic since Skip-thought Vectors are designed for sentence-level representation, while Doc2VecC is for document-level. The paper's explanation for Skip-thought Vectors' poor performance on IMDB (due to document length) is reasonable, but this doesn't demonstrate that Doc2VecC is a better solution than other document-level methods.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the corruption mechanism
2. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art document representation methods (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa)
3. Provide clearer evidence that the claimed "data-dependent regularization" is actually responsible for the performance gains
4. Better justify the novelty of the approach compared to existing work on document representation (e.g., denoising autoencoders)
5. Address the fact that the core idea (averaging word embeddings) has been used before (Huang et al., 2012)

## Clarification Questions
1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." How does this differ from simply training word embeddings with standard Word2Vec and then averaging them for document representation? What specific mechanism in Doc2VecC ensures better document representation than standard averaging?

2. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed from the paradigm shown in Figure 1." However, in Table 1, DEA achieves lower accuracy than Doc2VecC. If Doc2VecC is just DEA with added context, why does it perform better? Please provide evidence that the context addition (not just the corruption) is responsible for the improvement.

3. The paper claims Doc2VecC "significantly outperforms" Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB, but Skip-thought Vectors are designed for sentence-level representation while Doc2VecC is for document-level. How would the authors respond to the criticism that this comparison is unfair because the methods are designed for different input lengths?
