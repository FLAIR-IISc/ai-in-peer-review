

# Peer Review: "Doc2VecC: Document Vectors through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel method for learning document representations that addresses key limitations of existing approaches like Paragraph Vectors. The core idea is to represent each document as a simple average of word embeddings, but with a crucial twist: during training, the document is corrupted by randomly removing words (with probability q), and the document representation is formed by averaging the embeddings of the remaining words. The authors claim this corruption mechanism serves as a data-dependent regularization that favors informative/rare words while suppressing embeddings of common but uninformative words. 

The paper makes several strong claims:
- Doc2VecC has model complexity decoupled from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary size)
- It's highly efficient to train and test, with no expensive inference for unseen documents
- The method outperforms or matches state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks
- The corruption mechanism intrinsically enforces that document representations can be meaningfully formed by averaging word embeddings

The paper provides comprehensive experimental validation across multiple datasets and tasks, with strong empirical results and thorough efficiency comparisons. The method is simple yet effective, with clear connections to prior work (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) and proper acknowledgment of related approaches.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning with a simple, efficient, and effective method that outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on multiple benchmarks. The empirical results are strong, well-supported, and provide clear evidence for the claims. While the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism could be strengthened, the empirical performance is compelling enough to warrant acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Strengths:**

1. **Strong empirical results:** Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across multiple benchmarks (IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia classification, SICK semantic relatedness) with consistent performance gains over baselines. The results are well-presented with appropriate statistical comparisons.

2. **Clear efficiency advantage:** The paper provides thorough timing comparisons showing Doc2VecC is significantly faster to train and test than Paragraph Vectors and other methods, which is a major practical advantage.

3. **Simple and elegant approach:** The core idea is straightforward and builds logically on prior work (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors), making it easy to understand and implement.

4. **Well-positioned in literature:** The paper correctly identifies the limitations of existing methods (particularly Paragraph Vectors' model complexity) and properly situates their work within the literature.

5. **Strong theoretical justification (with room for improvement):** The paper provides a reasonable Taylor expansion argument showing how the corruption mechanism introduces a data-dependent regularization. While this theoretical justification could be strengthened, it's sufficient to support the empirical findings.

**Weaknesses (that don't prevent acceptance but need addressing):**

1. **Inconsistency regarding subsampling:** The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, therefore does not rely on this trick" (subsample frequent words), but Table 3 and the discussion about subsampling suggest they still applied this trick. This needs clarification.

2. **Lack of ablation study:** The paper doesn't provide an ablation study to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism versus the averaging approach. It's unclear how much of the performance gain comes from the corruption versus the averaging itself.

3. **Theoretical justification could be stronger:** While the Taylor expansion argument is reasonable, it doesn't fully explain why the corruption mechanism specifically leads to better performance. The connection between the regularization term and the observed performance gains needs more explicit discussion.

4. **Comparison to similar methods:** The paper cites Huang et al. (2012) as using simple averaging, but doesn't directly compare to their method. It would be useful to see how Doc2VecC compares to this baseline.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the subsampling inconsistency:** The paper claims Doc2VecC doesn't require subsampling of frequent words, but the experiments still use it. Please clarify this point in the paper - either demonstrate that Doc2VecC truly doesn't require subsampling, or explain why they still applied it.

2. **Add an ablation study:** Include an ablation study showing the performance difference between:
   - Standard word embedding averaging (without corruption)
   - Doc2VecC with corruption
   - Word embedding averaging with standard dropout (not the specific corruption mechanism)

3. **Strengthen the theoretical justification:** Explicitly connect the regularization term to the observed performance gains. Explain why the specific form of regularization introduced by the corruption mechanism is beneficial for document representation.

4. **Compare directly to Huang et al. (2012):** Since the paper cites this work as using simple averaging, include a direct comparison to their method to better position Doc2VecC's contribution.

5. **Clarify the corruption mechanism:** The paper describes the corruption as "randomly remove words from a document," but equation (1) shows it's a scaled dropout (un-corrupted dimensions are scaled by 1/(1-q)). This distinction should be clarified to avoid confusion with simple word removal.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. You claim Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, therefore does not rely on this trick" (subsample frequent words), but your experiments still apply the subsampling trick. Can you clarify this point? Does Doc2VecC truly not require subsampling, or does it still benefit from it?

2. The paper mentions that Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task. Can you elaborate on why Paragraph Vectors (which is designed for document-level representation) would perform poorly on a word-level task? Is this a limitation of the model architecture or a consequence of the training data?

3. In Table 3, Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words, but the paper claims this is due to the corruption mechanism. Could this suppression also be explained by the fact that Doc2VecC uses a smaller vocabulary (due to the corruption during training) compared to Paragraph Vectors, which uses the full vocabulary? 

4. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed from the paradigm shown in Figure 1." However, DEA typically reconstructs the original document from the corrupted version, while Doc2VecC uses the corrupted version to predict the target word. Could you clarify this relationship between Doc2VecC and DEA?

5. The paper claims "the model architecture of Doc2VecC resembles that of Word2Vec, and can be trained very efficiently." How does the training time scale with corpus size compared to Word2Vec? Since Doc2VecC processes the entire document for each training instance (unlike Word2Vec which uses a sliding window), wouldn't training time scale linearly with document length rather than linearly with corpus size?

These questions would help clarify some of the key aspects of your method and strengthen the paper's contribution to the field.
