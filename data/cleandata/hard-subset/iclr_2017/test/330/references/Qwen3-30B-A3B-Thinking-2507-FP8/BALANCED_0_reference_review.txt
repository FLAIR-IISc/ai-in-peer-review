

# Review of "Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective approach for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings with a corruption mechanism during training. The authors argue that this approach efficiently captures document semantics while avoiding the computational inefficiencies of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors. Doc2VecC's key innovations include a corruption mechanism that acts as data-dependent regularization (favoring informative words and suppressing common, uninformative words), decoupled model complexity from corpus size, and efficient test-time representation generation. The authors demonstrate strong performance across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, with significant improvements over baselines including BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution, but the novelty is limited, and the empirical evidence does not fully support the claims of significant superiority over existing approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main weakness is that Doc2VecC is not substantially different from existing approaches that average word embeddings. The authors acknowledge that "Huang et al. (2012) also proposed the idea of using average of word embeddings to represent the global context of a document," but fail to properly differentiate their approach from this prior work. The corruption mechanism is presented as a key innovation, but the empirical evidence for its necessity is weak. The paper claims Doc2VecC "outperforms" Paragraph Vectors, but the improvements are marginal in most tasks (e.g., 31.92% vs 35.78% error rate on Wikipedia classification with h=100), and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for these differences.

The theoretical analysis of corruption as regularization is interesting but not sufficiently developed. The paper states "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but provides no empirical evidence to support this claim. The performance gains from corruption could be explained by the standard word subsampling technique used in Word2Vec, which the paper acknowledges is critical for Word2Vec+AVG performance but claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization" without sufficient evidence.

The paper also presents inconsistent results: Doc2VecC performs better than Skip-thought vectors on IMDB (11.70% vs 17.42% error) but worse on SICK (41.20% vs 40.50% error). The paper attributes this to paragraph length differences but doesn't provide sufficient analysis to support this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Clarify novelty**: Explicitly differentiate Doc2VecC from prior work using simple averaging of word embeddings (Huang et al., 2012; Socher et al., 2013). What makes your corruption mechanism fundamentally different from standard word subsampling techniques?
- **Strengthen empirical claims**: Provide statistical significance tests for performance differences across all tasks. Include ablation studies showing the contribution of the corruption mechanism versus standard word subsampling.
- **Expand theoretical analysis**: More thoroughly explain how the corruption mechanism creates "data-dependent regularization" that differs from standard techniques. How does this regularization specifically improve performance over other methods?
- **Clarify corruption mechanism**: The paper states "the corruption introduced in Doc2VecC acts as a data-dependent regularization," but doesn't provide sufficient detail on how the corruption rate q affects performance. Include a sensitivity analysis for q across different values.
- **Improve presentation**: Correct the title typo ("MENTS" should be "DOCUMENTS") and fix the inconsistent use of "Doc2Vec" versus "Doc2VecC" in the text.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How does the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC specifically differ from the standard word subsampling technique used in Word2Vec, which also suppresses common words? What evidence do you have that the corruption mechanism provides additional benefits beyond standard subsampling?
- Why does Doc2VecC perform worse than Skip-thought vectors on the SICK semantic relatedness task despite being better on IMDB? Is this due to the corruption mechanism's interaction with sentence structure, or is there another explanation?
- The paper claims Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but provides no empirical evidence. Could you share the results of this comparison?
- How does your approach handle out-of-vocabulary words during test time, and how does this compare to the handling in Skip-thought vectors or Paragraph Vectors?
