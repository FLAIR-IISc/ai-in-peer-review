

# Review of "Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings learned through a corruption process (randomly removing words during training). The authors claim this approach has several advantages: it's computationally efficient (model complexity is decoupled from corpus size), it implicitly introduces a data-dependent regularization that favors rare/important words, and it generates document representations through simple averaging, which is efficient at test time. The paper claims Doc2VecC outperforms established methods like Bag-of-Words, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly faster to train and test.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to justify its novel approach, and the claims about methodological advantages and performance improvements are not rigorously supported by the experimental evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes several strong claims about the advantages of Doc2VecC over established methods, but the experimental validation is insufficiently thorough and lacks key comparisons. The most fundamental issue is that the paper does not compare Doc2VecC against a simple average of Word2Vec embeddings (without the corruption mechanism), which would be the most direct baseline for the authors' core claim that "averaging word embeddings" is the right approach. Instead, they compare against Word2Vec+IDF (which applies post-processing) and Paragraph Vectors (which explicitly learns document vectors), but not the simplest possible baseline: averaging the word embeddings learned by Word2Vec without any corruption.

The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as "data-dependent regularization" is complex and not clearly connected to the empirical results. The paper claims this regularization "favors rare or informative words," but the evidence provided (Table 3) is insufficient to support this claim. The authors compare word embeddings with small l2 norms, but they don't demonstrate that this correlates with better performance on downstream tasks. The paper also doesn't show how the corruption rate q affects performance, despite claiming it's "not sensitive" to this parameter.

The experimental results are not properly contextualized. For example, the paper claims Doc2VecC "significantly outperforms" Paragraph Vectors, but the performance differences are marginal (e.g., 31.92% vs. 35.78% error rate on Wikipedia classification with h=100), and the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests to support these differences. The time comparisons are misleading because they don't account for the different vector dimensions used across methods (4800 for Skip-thought vs. 100 for others), which would significantly impact both training and inference times.

Most critically, the paper fails to demonstrate why the corruption mechanism is necessary. If the simple average of Word2Vec embeddings (without corruption) performs similarly to Doc2VecC, then the paper's core contribution would be substantially weakened. The authors don't provide this comparison, which is a fundamental omission.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:
1. Add a critical comparison against the simplest possible baseline: averaging Word2Vec embeddings without any corruption mechanism.
2. Provide statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.
3. Better contextualize the time comparisons by normalizing for vector dimensionality.
4. Clarify the relationship between the corruption mechanism and the observed performance improvements, rather than relying on complex theoretical arguments that aren't directly supported by empirical evidence.
5. Include ablation studies showing how performance changes with different corruption rates.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Why didn't you compare against a simple average of Word2Vec embeddings (without corruption) as a baseline? This would be the most direct test of your core claim that averaging word embeddings is the right approach.
2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences reported in Tables 1, 2, and 5? The paper claims "significant" improvements but doesn't provide evidence for them.
3. How does the corruption rate q affect performance? The paper claims it's "not sensitive" to q, but doesn't show this in an ablation study.
4. In Table 3, the authors claim Doc2VecC "manages to clamp down the representation of words frequently appear in the training set." However, the words with smallest l2 norms in Doc2VecC (e.g., "the", "of") are common stop words, which is expected. How does this relate to the performance improvements claimed in other tables?
5. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces" document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training. How does this differ from simply training a model that uses averaging as its document representation, without the corruption mechanism?
