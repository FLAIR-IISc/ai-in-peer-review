

# Peer Review: "Document Vector through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel approach for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is the incorporation of a corruption mechanism during training—randomly removing words from documents with unbiased scaling—which the authors theoretically show acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words. The approach is designed to overcome limitations of existing methods: Paragraph Vectors (which have model complexity growing with corpus size) and approaches requiring post-processing of word embeddings (which need heuristic weighting at test time). The authors claim Doc2VecC achieves comparable or better performance than state-of-the-art methods across multiple tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness) while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following key reasons: 
1) The approach is well-motivated and addresses a genuine limitation in document representation learning
2) The theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as regularization is sound and novel

However, the paper would benefit from addressing several concerns regarding experimental design and claims before final acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—using a corruption mechanism to create a data-dependent regularization—is theoretically compelling. The Taylor expansion analysis (Section 3.1) clearly demonstrates how the corruption process introduces a regularization term that specifically penalizes embeddings of common words, which aligns with known benefits of sub-sampling in Word2Vec. This provides a principled explanation for why their approach outperforms simple averaging of word embeddings.

The empirical results are generally strong, particularly on the Wikipedia document classification task where Doc2VecC achieves 30.24% error rate (vs. 33.02% for Paragraph Vectors with h=1000). The visualization in Figure 3 provides compelling evidence that Doc2VecC learns more meaningful document representations.

However, several concerns weaken the paper's claims:

1. **Unfair Skip-thought comparison**: The paper claims Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on IMDB (17.42% error) compared to Doc2VecC (11.70%), but this comparison is flawed. Skip-thought was trained on very short sentences (order of 10 words), while IMDB reviews average 296 words. The authors acknowledge this in their hypothesis but don't test it directly. Without a Skip-thought model trained on IMDB reviews, this comparison is invalid.

2. **Insufficient ablation study**: The paper claims the corruption mechanism is "critical to the performance," but doesn't provide ablation results showing how performance degrades without it. A direct comparison of Doc2VecC with and without corruption would strengthen the claim that this is the key innovation.

3. **Unverified claim about "natural enforcement"**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but provides no evidence beyond the regularization analysis. A qualitative example showing document vectors with and without corruption would be valuable.

4. **Overstated comparison to DEA**: The paper states Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't provide a direct comparison of these two approaches. This claim needs empirical validation.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Provide a direct comparison of Doc2VecC without local context vs. DEA** to confirm the claim that they are equivalent. The current comparison is purely theoretical.

2. **Include ablation studies** showing performance with different corruption rates (q=0.5, 0.7, 0.9) to demonstrate sensitivity to this hyperparameter. The paper currently uses q=0.9 without justification.

3. **Re-evaluate the Skip-thought comparison** by training Skip-thought on IMDB reviews (or using a model trained on longer sentences) to make the comparison valid.

4. **Add a qualitative analysis** showing how the corruption mechanism affects specific word embeddings (e.g., comparing norms of embeddings for common vs. rare words).

5. **Clarify the relationship** between Doc2VecC and previous work on averaging word embeddings (e.g., Huang et al. 2012). The paper mentions this but doesn't explain how their corruption mechanism differs from or improves upon this prior work.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Why did you choose q=0.9 as the corruption rate? Did you test other values, and if so, how did performance vary?

2. Can you provide ablation results showing the performance difference between Doc2VecC with and without the corruption mechanism?

3. How would you respond to the argument that your corruption mechanism is essentially a form of dropout applied to document representations, rather than a novel contribution?

4. Could you provide more evidence for your claim that Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings"? What specific evidence supports this beyond the regularization analysis?

5. Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on the word analogy task? Is this a limitation of the model architecture or a data-specific issue?

The paper presents a theoretically sound and empirically promising approach to document representation learning. With the suggested revisions, it would make a strong contribution to the field. The core idea of using corruption as a form of data-dependent regularization is valuable and warrants publication, but the paper needs to address the experimental concerns to fully support its claims.
