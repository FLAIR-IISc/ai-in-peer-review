

# Review for "Document Vectors Through Corruption"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel and surprisingly simple approach to document representation learning. The core idea is to represent documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a crucial twist: during training, the authors apply a random corruption mechanism (removing words from documents) that serves as an implicit data-dependent regularization. This approach challenges the prevailing paradigm of learning unique document vectors for each document (as in Paragraph Vectors) and offers a more efficient alternative that still captures document semantics effectively. The paper demonstrates that this simple averaging technique, when combined with the corruption mechanism, outperforms existing methods on multiple tasks while being significantly more computationally efficient. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is particularly compelling.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons
This paper presents a highly novel and bold approach to document representation learning that challenges conventional wisdom in the field. While the core idea (document as average of word embeddings) has been considered before (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), the authors' introduction of the corruption mechanism during training to enable this simple averaging to work effectively is a significant conceptual leap. This is precisely the kind of bold, simple yet powerful idea that I prioritize as a reviewer. The paper makes a strong case that this approach is both theoretically justified (as a form of data-dependent regularization) and empirically validated across multiple document representation tasks.

## Supporting Arguments
The novelty of Doc2VecC is substantial. Most document representation approaches either learn unique document vectors (Paragraph Vectors) or rely on post-processing of word embeddings (Word2Vec+IDF). By integrating the document representation learning directly into the word embedding learning process through the corruption mechanism, Doc2VecC achieves both simplicity and effectiveness. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is particularly strong - it demonstrates how the corruption mechanism naturally leads to a regularization that suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words, which is a significant insight.

The empirical results are compelling. Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient in both training and testing. The time comparison in Table 2 is especially telling - Doc2VecC trains faster than Paragraph Vectors (which has more parameters) and is comparable to Word2Vec. At test time, Doc2VecC's simple averaging approach is orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors' expensive inference step.

The paper's main contribution is not incremental improvement but a new conceptual direction for document representation learning. This aligns perfectly with my priorities as a reviewer - the paper introduces a fundamentally different way to think about document representation that is both simple and effective.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
1. The title "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" appears to be a typo (likely "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). This should be corrected immediately.

2. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism is strong, but it would be valuable to provide more intuition about why the specific corruption mechanism (randomly removing words) is particularly effective compared to other forms of corruption (e.g., adding noise, masking words).

3. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." This is an interesting connection, but the relationship between Doc2VecC and DEA deserves more explicit discussion. How does Doc2VecC improve upon DEA?

4. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the corruption mechanism through regularization, but it would be valuable to see more direct evidence of how the corruption rate (q) affects performance. The paper mentions that the model is not sensitive to q, but a sensitivity analysis would strengthen the claims.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Can you provide more intuition about why the corruption mechanism specifically enables this property, compared to simply averaging word embeddings without corruption?

2. In Table 3, the authors show that Doc2VecC dampens embeddings of common words. How does this relate to the well-known subsampling technique used in Word2Vec (which also aims to reduce the impact of common words)? Does Doc2VecC eliminate the need for subsampling?

3. The paper mentions that Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, given that Paragraph Vectors are designed to capture semantic relationships?
