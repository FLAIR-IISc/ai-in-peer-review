- Paper introduces Doc2VecC: document vectors as simple averages of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training.
- Claims corruption acts as data-dependent regularization that suppresses common/uninformative word embeddings.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC outperforms baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors) on sentiment, document classification, and semantic relatedness, with much better training/inference efficiency.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason for accept: clearly identified problem and a well-motivated, theoretically grounded, and empirically effective solution.
- Reason for accept: consistent improvements across multiple tasks and significant efficiency advantages.
- Strength: clear problem statement and motivation, especially compared to Paragraph Vectors’ scaling and inference issues.
- Strength: rigorous theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) deriving corruption-induced regularization (eq.7).
- Strength: strong empirical results with appropriate baselines and ablation studies demonstrating effects of regularization.
- Strength: substantial efficiency advantage demonstrated in training and inference time comparisons.
- Strength: comparisons include a comprehensive set of relevant baselines.
- Minor concern: no direct comparison to recent transformer-based methods (e.g., BERT), though focus on simpler models is reasonable.
- Minor concern: claim of scaling to very large datasets lacks empirical training times beyond IMDB.
- Minor concern: corruption rate q fixed at 0.9; more analysis on sensitivity would be valuable.
- Feedback: clarify how corruption handles very short documents (e.g., expected remaining words near zero).
- Feedback: briefly compare or contextualize Doc2VecC with recent transformer approaches to acknowledge different design spaces.
- Feedback: add statistical significance tests (confidence intervals or p-values) for reported performance improvements.
- Feedback: include more implementation details (negative sample size and other hyperparameters) to aid reproducibility.
- Feedback: discuss limitations, especially for short documents or domain-specific vocabularies where averaging may fail.
- Question: clarify relationship to Denoising Autoencoders when local context words are removed and provide a brief comparison.
- Question: explain why Doc2VecC outperforms on long-paragraph IMDB but underperforms LSTM on short-sentence SICK.
- Question: clarify how training-time corruption (using subsets of words) affects consistency of test-time representations when averaging all words—any theoretical guarantees?
- Question: report sensitivity of performance to vocabulary cutoff (10) in sentiment task and compare its effect to corruption rate q.
