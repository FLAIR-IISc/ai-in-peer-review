

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel approach to document representation learning called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The core contribution is a simple yet powerful method that represents documents as the average of word embeddings, but with a key innovation: during training, the document is corrupted by randomly removing words, which acts as a data-dependent regularizer. This mechanism makes the simple averaging approach effective for capturing document semantics, while avoiding the drawbacks of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors (which require document-specific vectors and suffer from high parameter complexity). The paper provides both theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization and strong empirical results across multiple text understanding tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC outperforms state-of-the-art baselines while being significantly more efficient to train and test.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

The key reason for acceptance is the paper's highly novel and bold approach to document representation learning. The core idea of using corruption during training to regularize word embeddings and enable simple averaging as a document representation is both conceptually elegant and empirically effective. This represents a significant conceptual shift from existing approaches rather than a minor incremental improvement.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the novelty of its approach. While averaging word embeddings has been attempted before (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), Doc2VecC's key innovation is the corruption mechanism during training, which transforms a simple averaging approach into a powerful document representation method. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 convincingly demonstrates that this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularization that naturally suppresses embeddings of common, uninformative words while preserving those of rare, informative words.

The empirical results across three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness) are strong and consistent, with Doc2VecC outperforming all baselines by significant margins. For example, on the IMDB sentiment analysis task, Doc2VecC achieves a 15% improvement over BoW and outperforms Paragraph Vectors, which is particularly impressive given Paragraph Vectors' higher parameter complexity. The efficiency gains are also substantial: Doc2VecC trains and tests significantly faster than Paragraph Vectors (7 seconds vs. 4 minutes 17 seconds for test inference on IMDB), and it avoids the need for document-specific vectors entirely.

What makes this paper particularly appealing to a reviewer prioritizing novelty is that it challenges a fundamental assumption in document representation learning (that document-specific vectors are necessary) with a simple yet powerful alternative. This is the kind of bold conceptual shift that can open new research directions, rather than just making incremental improvements to existing methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, here are a few suggestions to further strengthen it:

1. The theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) is compelling but could be made more accessible. Consider adding a simplified intuitive explanation of why the corruption mechanism leads to the observed regularization effect, perhaps with a small illustrative example.

2. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but doesn't provide a direct comparison to DEA. Including a brief comparison would strengthen the theoretical positioning of the work.

3. The corruption rate q is set to 0.9 throughout experiments, but the paper doesn't thoroughly explore how performance varies with different q values. A small ablation study would help readers understand the sensitivity of the approach to this hyperparameter.

4. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC naturally handles out-of-vocabulary words through the corruption mechanism, but doesn't elaborate on how this compares to other methods' approaches to OOV words. A brief discussion would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that Doc2VecC "outperforms Paragraph Vectors" on the IMDB task, but Paragraph Vectors (PV-DBOW) was shown to perform comparably to the distributed memory version in the original Paragraph Vectors paper. Could the authors clarify whether they're comparing to PV-DM or PV-DBOW, and if there are any specific implementation details that might explain the performance difference?

2. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative." Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how this regularization compares to standard techniques like sub-sampling frequent words in Word2Vec?

3. The corruption mechanism is inspired by denoising autoencoders, but the paper doesn't explicitly compare Doc2VecC to standard denoising autoencoders for document representation. Could the authors clarify why their approach is superior to simply using a denoising autoencoder for document representation?

This paper presents a genuinely novel and bold approach to document representation learning that challenges established assumptions in the field. The combination of theoretical insight, strong empirical results, and significant efficiency gains makes it a compelling contribution that aligns well with ICLR's focus on novel and impactful research.
