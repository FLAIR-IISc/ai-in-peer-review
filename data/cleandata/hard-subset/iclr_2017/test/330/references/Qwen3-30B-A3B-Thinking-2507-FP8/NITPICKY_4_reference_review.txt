

# Review of "Document Vector through Corruption"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning model that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The authors claim this approach offers several advantages: it decouples model complexity from corpus size, enables efficient training and inference, implicitly regularizes word embeddings to favor informative words, and achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple document understanding tasks.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains significant methodological inconsistencies and unsupported claims that undermine its core contributions.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Fundamental Methodological Inconsistencies**: The paper claims Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed" (Section 3.1), but this is factually incorrect. DEA uses reconstruction loss (minimizing the difference between original and corrupted document), while Doc2VecC uses prediction loss (predicting target words given context). The two models are fundamentally different in architecture and training objective, making this comparison invalid. This demonstrates a fundamental misunderstanding of the related work.

2. **Critical Notation and Mathematical Errors**: 
   - Equation (1) has inconsistent notation: "x̃d = { 0, with probability q xd 1−q , otherwise" should be "x̃d = { 0, with probability q; xd/(1-q), otherwise" but the current formatting is ambiguous.
   - Equation (7) contains a mathematical error: "q 1− q v∑ j=1 R(uj)" should be "q/(1-q) * Σj R(uj)" but the formatting implies multiplication of q by 1-q.
   - The claim "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃" (Section 4.2) is incorrect. The number of parameters remains constant; only the number of words being updated changes.

3. **Unsupported Claims About Document Representation**: The paper repeatedly claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training" (Section 1, 3.1) but provides no evidence that this is actually happening. The empirical results don't demonstrate that the averaged embeddings better capture document semantics than other methods.

4. **Misrepresentation of Related Work**: The paper dismisses the relevance of Huang et al. (2012) who proposed using average word embeddings for document representation, yet fails to acknowledge that their approach is fundamentally similar to Doc2VecC. The only difference is the corruption mechanism, which the paper doesn't properly contextualize.

5. **Inconsistent Claims About Paragraph Vectors**: The paper states "Paragraph Vectors, on the other hand, requires another round of inference to produce the vector representation of unseen test documents" (Section 4.2), but this is only true for the PV-DM variant. The PV-DBOW variant (which is also mentioned in the paper) does not require this additional inference step, contradicting the paper's claim.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the Corruption Mechanism**: The paper needs to explain why the specific corruption approach (equation 1) was chosen over other common techniques like dropout. A sensitivity analysis to corruption rate q would strengthen the claims.

2. **Correct Mathematical Notation**: The paper contains multiple mathematical formatting errors that make it difficult to follow the technical claims. These must be corrected.

3. **Provide Statistical Significance**: The performance claims across multiple datasets should be supported by statistical significance tests rather than just point estimates.

4. **Better Contextualize Related Work**: The paper should more thoroughly discuss how Doc2VecC relates to existing work on averaging word embeddings (Huang et al., 2012; Socher et al., 2013) and how it differs from Paragraph Vectors' PV-DBOW variant.

5. **Clarify the "Data-Dependent Regularization" Claim**: The paper claims Doc2VecC introduces a "special form of regularization" but doesn't clearly explain how this differs from standard dropout or other regularization techniques.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide evidence that the document vectors learned by Doc2VecC actually capture more semantic information than other methods, beyond the reported accuracy scores?

2. Why does the paper claim Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders" when they have fundamentally different loss functions? Please clarify this comparison.

3. How does Doc2VecC handle out-of-vocabulary words during inference, and how does this compare to other methods?

4. Why does the paper claim that Paragraph Vectors require "another round of inference" for unseen documents when the PV-DBOW variant does not?

5. What evidence do you have that the corruption mechanism specifically improves performance beyond what would be achieved with standard dropout applied to word embeddings?

The paper's core idea has potential, but the significant methodological errors, unsupported claims, and inconsistent presentation of related work make it difficult to evaluate the true contribution. The authors need to thoroughly revise the technical content and properly contextualize their work within the existing literature before it can be considered for publication.
