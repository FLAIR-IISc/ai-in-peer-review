1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method to learn document representations by averaging word embeddings, where training enforces that this average is predictive of target words. The key idea is to introduce a “corrupted” global document context: during training, a large fraction of words in the document is randomly dropped (with unbiased rescaling), and the model predicts a target word from its local context plus the average of the remaining word embeddings. The authors show via a second-order Taylor expansion that this corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving informative words. The method has several claimed advantages: model size depends only on vocabulary (not corpus size), training and inference are fast, and representations are effective across tasks. Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, and word analogy, and is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness, with large speed gains over Paragraph Vectors and Skip-Thought.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Technical soundness and clarity: The method is simple, well-specified, and the corruption-as-regularization analysis is technically sound and aligns with known results from dropout/mDAE.
- Empirical support and efficiency: Results show consistent improvements over reasonable baselines with substantially better training and inference efficiency.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Learning generic, unsupervised document representations that are both effective and efficient at training and test time. This addresses weaknesses of BoW (sparsity, no semantics) and Paragraph Vectors (model size grows with corpus; expensive test-time inference).
- Motivation and placement: The work is positioned against Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, SDAE, LSTM/CNN encoders, and Skip-Thought. The rationale—enforcing that a simple average is a good document representation during training, and using corruption to regularize—is coherent and connected to prior analyses of dropout and denoising autoencoders.
- Technical soundness: The probabilistic model (CBOW-like with global context) and use of unbiased dropout on the document BoW are clear. The Taylor expansion of the expected objective under corruption is standard and yields a plausible data-dependent regularizer that penalizes frequent, non-informative words unless they contribute to confident predictions. The use of negative sampling is consistent with scalable training (though the choice of uniform negatives deviates from common practice; see feedback).
- Empirical rigor: Experiments cover multiple tasks and include speed measurements. The method consistently matches or improves over Word2Vec+IDF, SDAE, and Paragraph Vectors, with significant runtime advantages. Qualitative analyses (t-SNE; small-norm words) support the claimed regularization effect. Ablation or sensitivity is partially discussed (corruption rate, frequent-word subsampling).

Limitations that temper the strength of acceptance:
- Novelty is incremental: Averaging word embeddings is well-known; the novelty is in training-time corruption to make the average predictive and the associated regularization analysis. This is meaningful but not a large conceptual leap.
- Missing baselines: Comparisons omit strong contemporaneous simple baselines (e.g., supervised linear models with n-grams; fastText-like methods), which are relevant for efficiency and effectiveness.
- Some experimental protocols and details could be tighter (see below).

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify representation definition: You denote x as a binary BoW vector and divide by T (token length) in 1/T Ux. This underweights repeated words if x is binary. Please clarify whether x encodes counts and whether averaging is over tokens or unique types. Report performance differences between type- and token-averaging.
- Negative sampling distribution: You state Pv is uniform. Standard practice (e.g., unigram^0.75) often improves quality. Please justify uniform negatives and/or provide sensitivity comparisons.
- Ablations:
  - No corruption / varying q: Show the effect of removing corruption and a sweep over q to validate the regularization claim empirically.
  - Remove local context: You state Doc2VecC reduces to a denoising autoencoder without local context; include the corresponding quantitative ablation to isolate the benefit of the local context.
  - Subsampling frequent words: You note Doc2VecC is less reliant on subsampling; provide a full table isolating this effect across methods.
- Fairness of protocols:
  - Avoid training representations on test data: while you report both protocols, emphasize results that do not use test data for representation learning.
  - SICK fine-tuning: You fine-tune embeddings on SICK without labels; clarify whether competing methods receive comparable adaptation and ensure identical downstream regressors.
- Missing baselines: Include a comparison to simple, strong baselines such as:
  - Linear models with n-grams (e.g., bag-of-tricks style) for document classification.
  - Averaging pretrained GloVe embeddings with TF-IDF weighting (you include Word2Vec+IDF; ensure corpora and dimensions are matched).
- Reporting and clarity:
  - Provide complete hyperparameters (window size, negatives per positive, learning rates, epochs) and early-stopping criteria.
  - Ensure tables/figures referenced (e.g., word analogy detailed results) are present and legible.
  - Fix reference formatting issues and typos.

Questions for the authors
- Is x binary or count-based? If binary, why divide by T (token length) rather than number of unique words? Have you compared token-average vs type-average representations?
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use? How sensitive are results to this choice and to the context window size?
- Does corruption apply only to the global document context, or also to local context words? If only global, have you tried corrupting local context too?
- Can you provide a no-corruption baseline (q=0) and a quantitative ablation showing gains across tasks as q varies?
- How much of the gain over Word2Vec+IDF stems from the corruption-induced regularizer versus the joint training with local context? An ablation removing one factor at a time would help.
- For the IMDB and Wikipedia experiments, how do results change when using token-frequency-based averages and/or subword information?
- In the SICK setup, what exact regressor is used on [u1·u2; |u1−u2|]? Are all methods treated identically? Is any vocabulary expansion/fine-tuning applied to baselines?
- Can you release scripts to reproduce timing comparisons (including hardware details) and specify memory usage vs. Paragraph Vectors?

Overall, the paper presents a practical, well-motivated approach with solid empirical evidence and a clear speed advantage. Addressing the missing baselines and clarifying the methodological details would strengthen the submission further.
