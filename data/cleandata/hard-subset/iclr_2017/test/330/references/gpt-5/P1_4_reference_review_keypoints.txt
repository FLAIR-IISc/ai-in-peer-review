- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents a document as the average of word embeddings trained with random-word corruption and predicts target words using CBOW local context plus a corrupted global document vector.  
- Theory: A second-order Taylor expansion shows corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes frequent/uninformative words more and preserves embeddings crucial for confident predictions.  
- Empirical performance: Doc2VecC is competitive or superior to BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought, and LSTM methods on IMDB, Wikipedia, SICK, and word analogies, with large speed advantages.  
- Qualitative evidence and release: Paper provides runtime analysis, embedding-norm visualizations (stopwords vs informative words), document-space visualizations, and released code.  
- Decision: Weak accept — strong empirical gains, efficiency, and an insightful theoretical explanation justify acceptance.  
- Placement & motivation: Builds meaningfully beyond simple averaging/IDF by enforcing averaging during training via a corrupted global context and providing a principled regularization view.  
- Novelty: Use of corruption within a CBOW-like predictive framework to learn averaging document vectors and derivation of an adaptive, data-dependent penalty is novel and practically impactful.  
- Empirical support details: Improvements on classification and analogy tasks, suppression of stopwords in embeddings, competitive SICK performance vs sequence models, and clear speed/training benefits.  
- Rigor: Second-order expansion and assumptions (independent corruption, diagonal variance, expansion at mean) are appropriate and lead to a concrete penalty matching observations.  
- Baseline fairness: Request better PV evaluation (PV-DM and PV-DBOW with careful tuning, faster inference variants) and clear hyperparameter/training-budget reporting for PV.  
- Word2Vec baselines: Request results using common negative sampling (unigram^0.75) and standard hyperparameters, and discussion of any mismatch with derivation.  
- Simple-average baseline: Request a strong baseline of simple average with IDF (and possibly SIF) using the same embeddings to isolate corruption benefits.  
- Ablations (corruption): Vary corruption rate q, show performance/training-time curves, try scheduled or per-length q.  
- Ablations (model components): Remove local context to test reliance on it, vary embedding dimension and window size (IMDB + Wikipedia), and turn off frequent-word subsampling for robustness checks.  
- Theory caveats: Discuss gap between second-order approximation and exact objective; empirically compare induced regularizer to IDF/frequency (e.g., scatter of embedding norm vs term frequency).  
- Experimental protocol: Avoid using test set for unsupervised learning, report statistical significance/multiple runs, clarify OOV/vocabulary-extension and fine-tuning effects on SICK.  
- Implementation details: Request full disclosure of hyperparameters (k, negatives, optimizer, lr schedule, epochs), corpora sizes, and preprocessing for reproducibility.  
- Writing & presentation: Fix reference formatting, typos, and ensure tables/figures are legible and self-contained.  
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity and scheduling of q; negative sampling distribution used; window size and negatives per dataset; effect of subsampling; PV variants/hyperparameters used; impact of vocabulary extension/fine-tuning on SICK; results on other benchmarks/short texts; benefit from subword information.
