- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn document vectors by predicting words from local context plus a global vector that is the (scaled) average of a stochastically corrupted bag-of-words.  
- Theoretical contribution: a second-order Taylor analysis shows corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent/uninformative word embeddings.  
- Empirical claim: Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines on IMDB, Wikipedia 100-way, and is competitive on SICK; improves word analogy performance and is more efficient than Paragraph Vectors.  
- Practical advantages: decouples model size from number of documents, works with negative sampling, and uses trivial averaging at test time; code provided.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Strong empirical performance with substantial efficiency gains using a simple, well-motivated model.  
- Key reason 2: Clear analytical justification for corruption-as-regularization that explains average pooling effectiveness.  
- Positioning: Well-situated relative to Word2Vec/CBoW, Paragraph Vectors, SDA/DAE, and Skip-thought, addressing parameter growth and test-time inference issues.  
- Novelty: Training the average to be predictive and the corruption scheme that induces a principled regularizer are the main novelties.  
- Correctness of analysis: The derivation linking unbiased corruption to a second-order penalty is sound under standard approximations and yields convincing qualitative conclusions.  
- Experimental support: Broad, consistent results across IMDB, Wikipedia, SICK, and analogy tasks, with t-SNE visualizations and runtime comparisons.  
- Efficiency evidence: Training time competitive with Word2Vec/DAE, far faster than PV/Skip-thought; inference is simple averaging.  
- Suggestion: Include a strong linear baseline like NB-SVM on IMDB to better contextualize gains.  
- Ablation requests: Report effects of corruption vs. no corruption, sensitivity to q and 1/T scaling, and contribution of local vs. global context.  
- Missing experimental details: Specify negative sampling k, learning rates, window sizes, subsampling details, SICK regressor and metrics with variances, and data usage to rule out leakage.  
- PV comparisons: Clarify which PV variants (PV-DM/PV-DBOW/concatenation) were used and ensure fair tuning and timing.  
- Presentation fixes: Resolve broken references/formatting, ensure all tables/figures are shown, and tighten narrative.  
- Q1 (negative sampling): Which negative sampling distribution was used and have you tried unigram^0.75 vs. uniform Pv?  
- Q2 (corruption ablations): How do performance and training speed vary with q (e.g., 0, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9)?  
- Q3 (local vs global): What performance results arise when removing local context (DAE-like) or removing global context (CBoW-like)?  
- Q4 (length normalization): Is 1/T optimal? How do alternatives like sqrt(T) or IDF-weighting affect results?  
- Q5 (subsampling interaction): How does subsampling interact with the corruption-induced regularizer and does Doc2VecC still outperform without subsampling?  
- Q6 (PV settings): Which PV settings were used/tuned (DM/DBOW, concatenation, inference steps) and can stronger PV configs be reported?  
- Q7 (SICK protocol): What regressor/features were used for SICK and what are the exact Pearson r, Spearman ρ, MSE values with standard deviations?
