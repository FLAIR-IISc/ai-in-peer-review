1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method to learn document representations by averaging word embeddings, trained with a corruption mechanism. The key ideas are: (i) represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, but enforce this compositionality during training by predicting target words from both local context (CBoW-style) and a “global context” computed as the (scaled) average of a randomly masked document BoW; (ii) use unbiased dropout-style corruption of the document features during training, which the authors analyze via a second-order Taylor approximation to yield a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses frequent/non-informative words and preserves informative/rare words; (iii) decouple parameters from the corpus size (no per-document vectors), giving fast training and very cheap test-time inference. Empirically, Doc2VecC is reported to match or outperform strong baselines on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), word analogy, and to be competitive on SICK, while being substantially faster and simpler than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought. The method is easy to implement and comes with code.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong empirical performance with a clean, scalable architecture that yields very fast test-time representations, and consistent improvements over well-known baselines (Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors) on multiple tasks.
- A clear and plausible theoretical motivation via corruption-induced, data-dependent regularization that connects to known phenomena (dropout, mDA), and explains why simple averaging works well when learned end-to-end.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the practicality and quality gap between simple averaging of word embeddings (fast but naive) and models like Paragraph Vectors (better accuracy but heavy and slow, with per-document parameters and expensive inference). This is a real and important problem; the motivation is well articulated and placed in the literature.
- Approach: The architecture is a natural extension of CBoW, adding a global context derived from a corrupted document average. The use of unbiased corruption and the 1/T scaling are reasonable choices to stabilize gradients across documents of varying lengths.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion analysis is standard and provides intuition for the emergent regularization that down-weights frequent, uninformative words. This aligns with the empirical observation that Doc2VecC shrinks embeddings of stopwords while retaining sentiment-bearing rare words.
- Empirics: The paper reports improvements on IMDB sentiment (especially vs Word2Vec+IDF and Paragraph Vectors), a sizable gain on Wikipedia document classification over multiple embedding sizes, and better word analogy scores than Word2Vec (notably on syntactic/semantic subtasks). On SICK, Doc2VecC is close to LSTM-based methods despite being much faster. The speed comparisons and test-time efficiency advantages are meaningful for large-scale deployment.
- Practicality: No per-document parameters; test-time encoding is just an average; training is efficient due to corruption sparsifying updates. These are compelling advantages.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all affect the decision)
Clarity and presentation
- Several tables/figures are referenced (Tables 1–6, Figs 1, 3, 4) but many exact numbers are missing in the text provided. Please ensure all quantitative results, standard deviations (or multiple runs), and full settings are included and readable.
- The references contain many formatting issues and incomplete details. Please fix for camera-ready quality.
- The title in the draft seems truncated; please correct.

Ablations and sensitivity
- Please report ablations: (a) no corruption (q = 0), (b) varying q (e.g., 0.3–0.95), (c) remove local context (global-only), and (d) remove global context (local-only). This would disentangle where the gains come from.
- Provide sensitivity to window size, embedding dimension, number of negative samples, learning rate, and subsampling threshold.
- Report variance across seeds to assess stability.

Fairness and experimental protocol
- Negative sampling: Word2Vec typically uses a unigram^0.75 noise distribution. You state Pv is uniform; is that what you used in all models? Please align noise distributions across baselines and report the number of negatives.
- Paragraph Vectors: Specify whether you used PV-DM, PV-DBOW, or both, with hyperparameters. For analogy, PV is not designed to optimize word embeddings; please clarify training details to ensure a fair comparison for tasks requiring word vectors.
- Skip-thought: The slow encoding and weaker accuracy on IMDB may be due to very long documents and CPU inference. Please clarify whether you truncated or segmented documents, and whether GPU was used. Consider sentence-level averaging to match its intended use case.
- Transductive unsupervised learning: On IMDB and Wikipedia you sometimes learn representations using all available data (including test). While label-free, this is transductive. Please clearly separate inductive vs transductive settings and report both when possible.

Methodological details
- Justify the 1/T scaling for the global context more formally; discuss alternatives (e.g., length normalization or sqrt(T)).
- Provide details on OOV handling and vocabulary growth at test time.
- Training efficiency: report updates/sec and wall-clock times under consistent hardware for all methods, ideally including GPU results for neural baselines.
- Theory: The Hessian-diagonal derivation is sketched. Please include a more explicit derivation (signs, expectations, assumptions), and discuss the independence assumption and approximation error; if possible, provide empirical proxies that correlate the predicted regularization strength with observed embedding norms/frequencies.

Additional baselines
- Consider strong bag-of-tricks baselines (e.g., n-gram features, subword features) and weighted averaging schemes (e.g., TF-IDF, SIF) to contextualize gains from the corruption-induced regularization.
- For document classification, also consider 20 Newsgroups, Reuters, or Yelp to test robustness across domains.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use across Doc2VecC, Word2Vec, and PV? Were they matched?
- How sensitive is performance to q? Is there an optimal range tied to vocabulary size or document length?
- Did you try replacing unbiased masking with standard dropout scaling? Any difference?
- On IMDB/Wikipedia, when including test documents in the unsupervised phase, do you see similar relative gains in the fully inductive setting?
- For SICK fine-tuning: how much gain does fine-tuning (without labels) add, and how many epochs were used?
- Can Doc2VecC be extended with subword information or n-gram tokens to better handle rare/morphologically rich words?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-motivated idea with strong empirical evidence and practical benefits. Addressing the clarity, ablations, and fairness points would further strengthen the work.
