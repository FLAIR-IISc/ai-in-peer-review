Summary
This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient architecture for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings and to enforce this compositionality during training by using a “corrupted” version of the document (randomly masking words with unbiased rescaling) as a global context alongside a local CBOW context. A second-order Taylor expansion shows that this corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, uninformative words—an implicit, learned IDF-like effect. The method decouples model size from corpus size (no per-document parameters), yields test-time representations via simple averaging, trains quickly, and is empirically competitive or superior on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), word analogies, and semantic relatedness (SICK). Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel, elegant use of corruption to both (i) enforce average-of-embeddings document compositionality during training and (ii) induce a principled, data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent words; this is a simple but meaningful conceptual step beyond Word2Vec/Paragraph Vector.
- Strong practicality: significant training/test efficiency, corpus-size-independent parameterization, and competitive to state-of-the-art performance across diverse tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper clearly positions the method relative to Word2Vec/Paragraph Vector and denoising autoencoders. Using a corrupted document average as a global signal in a CBOW-like framework is a well-motivated way to align training with test-time usage (averaging). The theoretical derivation is intuitive and connects to known effects of dropout/mDA (Wager et al., Chen et al.).
- Technical contribution: The expectation/Taylor analysis yields a transparent per-word regularization term that increases with frequency but is modulated by prediction confidence—nicely explaining the empirical suppression of stop-words without hand-crafted IDF at test time.
- Empirical support: Across IMDB and Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms baselines including BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors, while being much faster—especially at test time, where averaging suffices. On SICK, results are competitive with much heavier models (Skip-Thought, Tree-/Bi-LSTM) despite a much simpler architecture and dramatically lower compute. Word analogy results show notable gains over Word2Vec with increasing data/dimension, consistent with the claim that the corruption improves embedding quality.
- Efficiency and scalability: The decoupling from corpus size is a pragmatic advantage over Paragraph Vectors; inference for unseen documents is trivial and orders of magnitude faster.

Caveats (not blocking)
- Fairness and completeness of comparisons: Some choices depart from standard Word2Vec practice (e.g., negative sampling distribution appears uniform rather than unigram^0.75; unclear if both methods share this choice). Strong baselines like fastText and supervised CNN/LSTM document models are not included; Sent2Vec-type approaches are not discussed (likely concurrent, but worth clarifying).
- Ablations: While the paper argues q is not sensitive, a more thorough ablation (q∈[0.5,0.95], with/without corruption, with/without subsampling frequent words) would strengthen the causal claims. Likewise, an ablation removing local context would better isolate the contribution of global corruption.
- Theory scope: The second-order expansion is approximate and derived for the full-softmax likelihood, whereas training uses negative sampling; discussing the approximation’s implications under the actual training objective would improve rigor.
- Reporting details: Hyperparameters (window size, negatives per positive, learning rate schedule), training epochs, and corpus preprocessing for each baseline (especially Paragraph Vectors) should be standardized and reported to bolster reproducibility and fairness.
- Clarity/polish: Some typos/formatting issues in equations and references detract from readability.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add a direct comparison to IDF-weighted averaging and to fastText; include an ablation showing Doc2VecC without the corruption and with TF-IDF at test time.
- Analyze sensitivity to document length (short sentences vs. long reviews), window size, negative sample count, and corruption rate q; report variance across runs.
- Clarify negative sampling distribution and ensure all baselines use the same setting to isolate the effect of the proposed corruption.
- Provide more details on the analogy evaluation setup (data split, vocabulary coverage, OOV handling) and ensure apples-to-apples training schedules for Word2Vec vs. Doc2VecC.
- Improve reference formatting and fix minor notation inconsistencies (e.g., 1/T scaling in the global context term).
- Consider subword features to handle rare/OOV words; discuss multilingual applicability.

Questions for the authors
1) Negative sampling: What distribution do you use for sampling negatives (uniform vs. unigram^0.75)? Are baselines matched? How sensitive are results to this choice?
2) Corruption specifics: Is corruption applied at the token level per occurrence or at the type level? Do you re-sample x̃ for every target position? How does q affect both speed and accuracy across datasets?
3) Local vs. global context: How crucial is the local CBOW context once the global corrupted average is present? Can you report an ablation removing local context?
4) Regularization analysis: Your derivation assumes the full-softmax likelihood. How does the analysis change under negative sampling, and do you observe the same frequency-sensitive shrinkage empirically?
5) Length effects: How do performance and the 1/T scaling behave for very short vs. very long documents? Would alternative normalizations (e.g., sqrt(T)) help?
6) Analogy gains: The reported 20% uplift over Word2Vec is striking. Can you provide controlled details (epochs, learning rate, negative count, window) and error bars?
7) Baselines: Can you compare against fastText and Sent2Vec (if not strictly concurrent), and stronger supervised document models (e.g., CNN/LSTM) to situate performance?

Overall, this is a clean, conceptually fresh, and practically valuable contribution with solid empirical support and a neat theoretical insight. Despite some missing ablations and minor clarity issues, I believe it merits acceptance.
