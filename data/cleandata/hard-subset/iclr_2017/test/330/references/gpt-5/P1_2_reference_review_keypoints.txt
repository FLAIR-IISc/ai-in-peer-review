- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings trained with a corruption (random masking) process in a CBoW-like architecture predicting target from local context + corrupted global vector.
- Corruption is theoretically motivated via a second-order Taylor expansion showing it induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent/uninformative words.
- Decouples parameter count from number of documents, trains as fast as Word2Vec, and yields extremely fast test-time inference (just averaging); code released.
- Strong empirical results on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia-100 classification, SICK semantic relatedness, and improved word analogy performance versus Word2Vec; substantial speed advantages reported.
- Decision: Accept (weak accept) based on simplicity, scalability, empirical performance, and a plausible theoretical intuition linking corruption to useful regularization.
- Key novelty: enforcing meaningful averaging at training time via dropout-like corruption; connects to marginalized denoising/dropout theory.
- Method clarity: negative-sampling CBoW loss with an unbiased mask (scale by 1/(1−q)), enabling Taylor expansion around the mean.
- IMDB results: matches or outperforms Paragraph Vectors and other baselines; evaluated in both transductive and more realistic train+unlabeled settings.
- Efficiency: training comparable to Word2Vec; test-time inference is orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors for generating document embeddings.
- Word analogy: yields consistently better word embeddings than Word2Vec, with larger gains when trained on large corpora, suggesting beneficial regularization.
- Wikipedia-100: significantly outperforms BoW, DAE, Word2Vec(+IDF), and PV across embedding sizes; t-SNE shows improved clustering.
- SICK: competitive with complex RNN-based encoders (LSTM/Skip-thought) while being much faster; outperforms feature-engineered baselines.
- Empirical regularization evidence: Doc2VecC produces smaller norms for common/stop-like words compared to PV/Word2Vec.
- Theoretical caveat: analysis is approximate/hand-wavy, omits negative-sampling specifics and interactions between local and global contexts in the Hessian.
- Baseline fairness concerns: PV’s performance depends strongly on configuration (DM/DBOW, concat/sum, negatives); more tuning/detail needed; missing strong simple baselines like fastText.
- Ablation gaps: need no-corruption (q=0) baseline, sensitivity to corruption rate q and window size, effect of removing local context, and interaction with subsampling frequent words.
- Presentation issues: ambiguous notation, missing hyperparameters (embedding size, negatives, learning rate schedule), corrupted references, and some missing numeric results.
- Suggested experiments/reporting: clarify negative sampling distribution and counts, report full numeric results with significance, provide thorough PV setup and ablations, add fastText/SIF/IDF-weighted baselines, and quantify interplay with subsampling.
- Reproducibility fixes: define all symbols, clean up references/typos, and ensure complete hyperparameter and training details in code/appendix.
- Questions for authors requested: exact negative sampling distribution and number of negatives; ablation for q ∈ {0,0.5,0.7,0.9}; how local/global contexts are combined and window size used; PV variants and hyperparameters tried; SICK fine-tuning objective and impact; effect of unigram^0.75 negative sampling; quantify reduced dependence on subsampling.
