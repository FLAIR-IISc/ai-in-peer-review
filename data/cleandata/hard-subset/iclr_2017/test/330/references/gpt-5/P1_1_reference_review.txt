Summary and contributions
- This paper tackles document representation learning. It proposes Doc2VecC, a simple, efficient model that learns word embeddings such that the average of a document’s word embeddings serves as its document vector. During training, the model predicts target words using both local context (CBoW-style) and a global document vector formed by averaging a stochastically corrupted subset of words from the document.
- The key technical idea is the corruption of the document bag-of-words during training (unbiased dropout/mask-out). A second-order Taylor expansion shows this induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, non-informative words, while preserving informative/rare words—providing a principled alternative to heuristic IDF weighting.
- The method is parameter-efficient (parameters scale with vocabulary size, not corpus size), fast to train/test, and yields document vectors at test time by simple averaging. Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, skip-thoughts, and several LSTM methods on sentiment (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100-way), word analogies, and is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness. The paper also provides speed comparisons and qualitative analyses.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) The approach is simple, well-motivated, and efficient; (2) Strong empirical results across multiple tasks with compelling speed/memory advantages, and a clear (if approximate) theoretical explanation for why corruption helps.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper clearly identifies shortcomings of BoW, average-of-embeddings, and Paragraph Vectors (lack of compositional inductive bias, sparsity, and PV’s poor scalability/test-time inference). The idea of enforcing that simple averaging be predictive during training addresses both performance and efficiency. The corruption-as-regularization link is well positioned relative to dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders.
- Method: The architecture is straightforward and reproducible: predict a target word with local context Uc and a global context (1/T)Ux̃; train with negative sampling. The unbiased corruption and the expansion around the mean explain how the regularizer downweights frequent/undiscriminative words—exactly the behavior one would want for averaging-based representations. While the Taylor expansion is an approximation and assumes infinite sampling of corruptions, the derivation is standard and insightful.
- Empirical support:
  - IMDB: Doc2VecC matches or beats Paragraph Vectors and substantially outperforms BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and denoising autoencoders; it is much faster at test time than PV (no inference).
  - Wikipedia 100-way classification: Consistent improvements across embedding sizes, with clear t-SNE separation indicating meaningful clustering.
  - Word analogies: Notably stronger than Word2Vec across most subtasks, supporting the claim that the word embeddings benefit from the corruption regularizer.
  - SICK: Competitive with, though slightly behind, LSTM/skip-thoughts (reasonable given sentence length), while being orders of magnitude faster to train and apply.
  - Efficiency: Solid timing comparisons; the method benefits from high corruption rates without hurting accuracy, supporting the scalability claim.
- Placement in literature: Related work is broadly covered (Word2Vec, PV, SDA/denoising autoencoders, skip-thoughts, LSTM models). The contribution is incremental conceptually but distinct: it bakes the “average at test time” objective into training with a principled corruption scheme and demonstrates broad, practical benefits.

Caveats and weaknesses (do not preclude acceptance)
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: Some details (negative sampling distribution, window sizes, PV variants/hyperparameters) are not fully specified; PV-DBOW and PV-DM should be carefully tuned and reported. Skip-thought comparisons on long reviews are not entirely apples-to-apples (sentence model applied to long documents), though the paper fairly discusses this limitation.
- Ablations: The paper asserts insensitivity to corruption rate q and includes a qualitative analysis of embedding norms, but lacks more thorough ablations: no “no-corruption” baseline, no q-sweep plots, no ablation of local vs global context contributions.
- Theory: The regularization derivation is approximate; it would help to more explicitly state assumptions (negative sampling objective, diagonal Hessian approximation) and discuss limits.
- Clarity and formatting: There are typos/formatting issues in equations and references; some references are incomplete or malformed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a thorough ablation study:
  - Vary q and show accuracy vs speed trade-offs.
  - Compare to no-corruption, and to naive averaging without the unbiased scaling.
  - Remove the local context term to show its contribution (the paper mentions a connection to denoising autoencoders—quantify it).
- Baselines and settings:
  - Specify negative sampling distributions and other hyperparameters for each method; ensure Word2Vec baselines use the common unigram^0.75 distribution.
  - Include both PV-DM and PV-DBOW with tuned hyperparameters, and clearly state inference settings at test time.
  - For SICK, also report Doc2VecC trained solely on SICK (no large pretraining) to contextualize the role of external data.
- Analysis:
  - Quantify the “data-dependent regularization” effect beyond listing low-norm words: e.g., plot embedding norm vs unigram frequency, and show performance when removing subsampling of frequent words across methods.
  - Report variance across runs (seeds) and include error bars.
- Clarity:
  - Clean up equations (e.g., consistent notation for ct, x, scaling by 1/T) and fix reference formatting.
  - Briefly discuss computational complexity per update and memory footprint.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution did you use for Doc2VecC and baselines (uniform vs unigram^0.75)? If different, how sensitive are results to this choice?
- How does performance change without the unbiased scaling factor in the corruption (i.e., simple dropout of words) and without corruption at all?
- What is the effect of the local context window size and the relative weighting of local vs global context? Could a learned gate between them help?
- For Paragraph Vectors, which variants and hyperparameters were used (PV-DM, PV-DBOW, window sizes, inference steps)? Can you report stronger PV baselines and their test-time costs?
- How sensitive is Doc2VecC to the corruption rate q across tasks? Please provide accuracy vs q and speed vs q plots.
- On SICK, could you report Doc2VecC trained only on SICK (no book-corpus pretraining) and also a sentence-only training variant to better match skip-thoughts?
- Does Doc2VecC still outperform Word2Vec+IDF when subsampling of frequent words is disabled for both methods, across multiple datasets?
- Can you discuss limitations where LSTM-based encoders might still be preferable (e.g., compositional phenomena requiring order sensitivity), and whether combining Doc2VecC with position-aware features helps?
