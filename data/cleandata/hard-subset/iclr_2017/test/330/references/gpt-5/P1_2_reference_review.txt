Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient document representation method that learns to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, but crucially trains with a corruption process that randomly masks out words in the document. The model uses a CBoW-like architecture where the target word is predicted from local context and a global document vector computed from the (corrupted) average of word embeddings.
- The corruption mechanism is theoretically motivated via a second-order Taylor expansion that shows it induces a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings: frequent but uninformative words are penalized, whereas words critical to confident predictions are less penalized. This explains why simple averaging at test time works well without heuristic weighting.
- The approach decouples the number of parameters from the number of documents (in contrast to Paragraph Vectors), is fast to train (Word2Vec-like) and extremely fast at test time (just averaging). The authors report strong results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia-100), semantic relatedness (SICK), and improved word analogy performance versus Word2Vec; they also show substantive speed advantages. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- A compelling combination of simplicity, scalability, and strong empirical performance, with a clear theoretical intuition linking corruption to useful regularization that favors informative words.
- Practical significance: parameter efficiency, very fast test-time inference, and competitive-to-state-of-the-art accuracy across diverse tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a long-standing tension in document embeddings: BoW is simple but ignores semantics; Paragraph Vectors are powerful but scale poorly with corpus size and require expensive test-time inference; simple averaging of word vectors is attractive but naïve. The proposal is well motivated by prior observations that linear operations on word embeddings preserve semantics, and that training-time constraints can enforce useful compositionality.
- Novelty and positioning: Using an average of word embeddings for documents is not new; the key novelty is to enforce the average to be meaningful during training by injecting a dropout-like corruption on the document words while jointly predicting targets with local context. The marginalized analysis connecting corruption to a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent words is a valuable contribution that clarifies why the method works and relates to mDAE/dropout theory.
- Methodological clarity: The learning objective is a CBoW-style negative sampling loss with an additional global context term derived from the (corrupted) average. The corruption is unbiased (mask with probability q and scale by 1/(1−q)), enabling the Taylor expansion around the mean.
- Empirical support:
  - IMDB sentiment: Doc2VecC matches or beats Paragraph Vectors and outperforms BoW, DAE, and Word2Vec+IDF. The authors evaluate both a transductive setting (unsupervised learning on train+test) and a setting using only train+unlabeled, which is fairer and more realistic.
  - Efficiency: Training is close to Word2Vec; test-time embedding generation is orders of magnitude faster than PV (seconds vs minutes for 25k docs).
  - Word analogy: Doc2VecC yields consistently better word embeddings than Word2Vec across subtasks, with large gains when trained on large corpora, supporting the claim that its regularization improves embedding quality.
  - Wikipedia-100 classification: Doc2VecC significantly outperforms BoW, DAE, Word2Vec(+IDF), and PV at multiple embedding sizes; t-SNE visualizations show better clustering.
  - SICK semantic relatedness: Doc2VecC is competitive with more complex LSTM/Skip-thought models despite being much faster; it beats feature-engineered competition baselines.
- The regularization interpretation is backed by an illustrative analysis showing words with small embedding norms under Doc2VecC are common/stop-like, whereas PV/Word2Vec shrink rare words.

Concerns and suggestions that temper enthusiasm (but are not fatal)
- Theoretical analysis is approximate and somewhat hand-wavy in parts. The use of negative sampling, the assumption of uniform negative sampling Pv (Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75), and ignoring interactions between local context and document context in the Hessian could be discussed more carefully. Nonetheless, the marginalized-noise framing is sensible and aligns with prior work.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Paragraph Vectors are reportedly weak on analogy; however, the configuration for PV (DM vs DBOW, concatenation vs sum, negative sampling distribution, window size) strongly affects performance. More detail and tuning fairness would strengthen the claims.
  - The Skip-thought comparison on IMDB highlights the long-document weakness of RNN encoders, but it is also an off-the-shelf model trained on books; a fastText-style baseline (bag-of-ngrams with averaging) would be a strong and relevant comparator.
- Ablations are limited. It would be useful to show:
  - No-corruption variant (q=0) to isolate the gain from the regularizer.
  - Sensitivity to corruption rate q and window size.
  - Effect of removing local context (to connect to DAE) and of removing subsampling of frequent words (since both mechanisms suppress frequent words).
- Some clarity/presentation issues: minor notation ambiguities (e.g., 1/T Ux̃), missing explicit hyperparameters (embedding size, negatives per positive, learning rate schedule), and references appear corrupted in the bibliography.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Clarify negative sampling: distribution used (uniform vs unigram^0.75), number of negatives, and whether this choice impacts the regularization derivation and results.
- Report full numeric results for all tasks in the main text or appendix (some are described qualitatively). Include statistical significance where appropriate.
- Provide a more thorough PV setup (DM, DBOW, concatenation, hierarchical softmax vs negative sampling, epochs) and perhaps an ablation showing PV’s word embedding quality vs document vectors to support the capacity explanation.
- Add ablations for q, window size, and the contribution of local vs global context.
- Consider adding fastText and SIF/Smooth Inverse Frequency baselines, and/or IDF-weighted averaging trained end-to-end, to position against strong simple baselines.
- Discuss interplay between subsampling frequent words and Doc2VecC’s regularizer (you note Doc2VecC relies less on subsampling; show this quantitatively).
- Clean up references and notation typos; define all symbols on first use; ensure reproducibility details are complete in the code.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used across experiments? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Can you provide an ablation with q ∈ {0, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9} to quantify the benefit of corruption and its induced regularization?
- How exactly are local and global contexts combined during training (sum vs concat), and what window size was used?
- For Paragraph Vectors, which variant(s) and hyperparameters were used? Did you try DBOW or DM+Concat, and did you tune inference steps at test time?
- On SICK, you mention fine-tuning on the dataset without labels. What objective is used during fine-tuning, and how much does it improve performance?
- Did you try unigram^0.75 negative sampling (as in Word2Vec)? Does it change the regularization behavior or performance?
- Can you quantify the extent to which Doc2VecC reduces dependence on subsampling frequent words relative to Word2Vec+AVG?

Overall, this paper presents a simple, well-motivated method with strong empirical performance and practical advantages. With minor clarifications and additional ablations, it would make a solid contribution.
