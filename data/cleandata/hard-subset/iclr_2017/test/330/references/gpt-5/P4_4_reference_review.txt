1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings under a corruption scheme. The key ideas are:
- Represent documents as the average of word embeddings, but enforce this compositionality during training by using the document average as a global context in a Word2Vec-style CBoW objective.
- Apply unbiased “mask-out” corruption to documents during training (randomly dropping words with scaling), which both accelerates training and induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, uninformative words and preserves rare/informative ones.
- Provide a second-order Taylor expansion analysis showing how the corruption yields a diagonal regularizer whose strength increases with word frequency and decreases when predictions are confident, connecting to prior analyses of dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders.
- Demonstrate efficiency benefits (model size depends only on vocabulary, not corpus size; fast inference by simple averaging) and strong empirical performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100-class subset), word analogies, and semantic relatedness (SICK), often matching or outperforming Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, SDA/DAE, and Skip-thoughts, while being substantially faster at test time.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is simple, well-motivated, and technically sound, with a clear analysis connecting corruption to a data-dependent regularizer that explains observed behavior.
- Strong empirical results and efficiency: consistent performance gains or parity with baselines across multiple tasks, with significantly improved training and especially test-time speed, and model complexity independent of corpus size.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
- Problem tackled: Learn effective, efficient document embeddings that capture global semantics without per-document parameters and with fast inference. The work targets limitations of BoW (sparsity, no semantic similarity) and Paragraph Vectors (corpus-size parameter growth and expensive test-time inference).
- Motivation and placement: The method is well-situated relative to Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors and prior averaging-based representations. Using global context via an averaged embedding is grounded in observed linear compositionality in word embeddings, while the corruption and the associated analysis connect to established theory on dropout and denoising autoencoders. The paper clarifies differences from Huang et al. (2012) (no corruption) and PV-DM (per-document vectors).
- Support for claims: The derivation uses an unbiased corruption and second-order expansion to obtain a diagonal regularizer with intuitive dependence on word frequency and prediction confidence; this is consistent with prior work and appears technically sound under the stated assumptions (independent corruption, binary BoW, negative sampling approximation). Empirically, the method delivers:
  - IMDB sentiment: competitive or superior accuracy to PV and other baselines; clear test-time efficiency advantage (average pooling at inference vs PV’s inference).
  - Wikipedia classification: lower error than BoW, DEA, Word2Vec(+IDF), and PV across embedding sizes; qualitative t-SNE plots show clearer clustering.
  - SICK relatedness: competitive with strong baselines given a much simpler architecture; consistent with the claim that averaging is robust for longer texts but less optimal for very short sequences.
  - Word analogy: improved performance over Word2Vec with the same training corpus and dimensionality, consistent with the hypothesis that corruption suppresses stopwords and sharpens embeddings.
The time and complexity comparisons substantiate efficiency claims. The code link improves reproducibility.

Caveats and potential weaknesses:
- Some implementation details for baselines (e.g., negative sampling distribution, window sizes, number of negatives, learning rates) are not fully specified; analogy performance can be sensitive to these settings.
- For certain datasets (Wikipedia subset), representation learning uses all available data including test documents; while unsupervised, this should be clearly justified and paralleled for all baselines.
- The analysis relies on independence and approximation assumptions and focuses on diagonal Hessians; while standard, the impact of these approximations on practice could be further probed with ablations.
- Minor notation/typographical issues (e.g., clarity around 1/T U x̃ term) reduce readability.

Overall, the approach’s simplicity, theoretical grounding, efficiency, and breadth of empirical validation outweigh the above concerns.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify training details for all baselines to ensure fairness:
  - Negative sampling distribution (Word2Vec commonly uses unigram^0.75 rather than uniform); number of negatives; window size; subsampling thresholds; learning rate schedules; epochs.
  - Ensure Word2Vec and Doc2VecC use comparable optimization settings.
- Provide ablations:
  - No-corruption vs with-corruption; varying corruption rate q; effect of removing local context (to isolate the contribution of global averaging).
  - Effect of subsampling frequent words vs relying solely on the corruption-induced regularizer.
  - Order sensitivity: compare with position-weighted or attention-weighted averages.
- Report standard deviations or confidence intervals across multiple runs, and perform significance testing on key benchmarks.
- Add clarity to the theoretical section:
  - Explicitly define µx and show why it equals x under unbiased corruption.
  - Expand the derivation steps for the Hessian diagonal and the resulting regularizer; resolve notation around 1/T and 1^T U x̃.
- Extend comparisons:
  - Include PV-DBOW results where word embeddings are absent, as discussed; compare to more recent weighted averaging baselines (e.g., IDF or other frequency-aware weights) under identical preprocessing.
  - Explore short-vs-long document regimes to quantify when Doc2VecC outperforms LSTM/Skip-thoughts and vice versa.
- Clarify whether representation learning on the Wikipedia dataset includes test documents for all methods and why this is acceptable for unsupervised evaluation.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used for Word2Vec vs Doc2VecC? If different, can you provide results with standard Word2Vec settings (unigram^0.75) to confirm fairness, especially for the analogy task?
- How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q and the context window size? Can you provide a small sensitivity analysis?
- On IMDB and Wikipedia, were all baselines trained with identical tokenization, subsampling, and vocabulary cutoffs? Please detail preprocessing steps.
- In the Wikipedia experiment, did representation learning include the test documents for all methods? If so, can you also report a split where only train+unlabeled are used for representation learning?
- Could you provide an ablation that removes local context (Uc) to quantify the contribution of the global document vector during training?
- For SICK, after fine-tuning on unlabeled data, did you observe any risk of overfitting due to small corpus size? How many epochs were used?
- Can you report memory usage and asymptotic complexity vs Paragraph Vectors to concretely demonstrate corpus-size independence?

Overall, the paper addresses a clear problem with a principled, efficient method, and presents credible empirical and theoretical support.
