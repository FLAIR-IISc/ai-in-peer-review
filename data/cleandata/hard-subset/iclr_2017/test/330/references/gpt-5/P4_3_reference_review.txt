1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, an efficient document representation method that learns word embeddings such that simple averaging yields effective document vectors. The key innovation is to introduce unbiased mask-out corruption of document words during training, so that the model predicts target words using both local context (as in CBOW) and a corrupted global document context. A second-order Taylor expansion shows this training induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while preserving informative ones. The approach decouples model size from corpus size, supports fast training and inference, and achieves competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100 classes), semantic relatedness (SICK), and word analogy tasks, while being markedly faster than alternatives like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought. Code is made available.

2) Decision
Accept, due to (i) a clear and simple method with a principled regularization analysis and strong efficiency properties, and (ii) competitive to superior empirical performance across multiple tasks with reproducible setup.

3) Supporting arguments
- Clarity: The method is described with a clear architecture and objective. The corruption mechanism and its unbiased scaling are precisely specified. The Taylor expansion derivation is well-structured and ties the corruption to a data-dependent regularizer that aligns with known properties of dropout/denoising autoencoders.
- Technical soundness: The objective uses negative sampling; the expected loss under corruption is approximated via second-order Taylor expansion at the mean. Independence assumptions yield a diagonal covariance and tractable Hessian diagonals. The regularizer qualitatively explains dampening of frequent function words and preservation of informative words, consistent with observed embedding norms. While approximate, the analysis is standard and reasonable.
- Novelty: The combination of (1) enforcing compositionality during training such that averaging embeddings suffices for document vectors, and (2) using explicit corruption of the global document context to induce a data-dependent regularizer appears novel relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and prior denoising methods. The analysis connecting corruption to frequency-aware regularization is a useful contribution.
- Empirical support: Results on IMDB (reduced error vs BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and Skip-thought; on par or better than Paragraph Vectors), Wikipedia classification (consistent improvements across representation sizes), and SICK (competitive to LSTM/Skip-thought, with far better efficiency) support the claims. The word analogy evaluation shows improved syntactic/semantic regularities over Word2Vec, consistent with the regularization effect. Efficiency claims are supported with timing comparisons for training and inference, highlighting the decoupling from corpus size.
- Practicality: The method is simple to implement, scales well, and yields test-time embeddings by averaging, a strong advantage over Paragraph Vectors.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all affect the accept decision)
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Clarify the negative sampling distribution Pv. The paper mentions uniform sampling; standard practice is unigram^0.75. Report results with both, and use the same for all relevant baselines.
  - DEA baseline setup (KL loss with sampled negatives) is nonstandard; explain rationale and ensure hyperparameters are tuned comparably to other baselines.
  - Consider including FastText or similar strong CBOW baselines with n-grams, and more recent tf-idf weighted baselines.
- Ablations:
  - Provide ablations for the corruption rate q (including q=0), the inclusion/exclusion of local context Uc, and the impact of unbiased scaling 1/(1−q). This will isolate contributions of corruption and the global context.
  - Report whether Doc2VecC maintains strong performance without sub-sampling of frequent words; currently you note using sub-sampling across all models, yet claim Doc2VecC does not rely on it.
- Statistical significance: Report confidence intervals or significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., Wikipedia 100-class errors and IMDB).
- Training details: Provide full hyperparameters (embedding size, window size, negatives per positive, optimizer, learning rate schedules, epochs), vocabulary cutoffs, and pre-processing. Clarify whether x is binary or counts during training, and whether document vectors at test time are averaged over tokens or types.
- Data usage protocols: For settings where test data contributes to representation learning, clearly separate these from strictly train+unlabeled settings in all tables. Emphasize the latter as the primary metric.
- Word analogy: Include full quantitative tables (by subtask) and exact relative improvements; describe training data size, embedding dimension, and all training settings to ensure comparability with Word2Vec.
- Limitations and scope: Discuss the loss of word order in the final document representation and scenarios where sequence models may be preferable (short sentences or tasks requiring compositional order). Consider sentence-level evaluations beyond SICK.
- References and presentation: Clean up references and minor typos; ensure tables/figures are fully legible and included.

Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling: What distribution Pv did you use in all experiments? If uniform, how do results change with unigram^0.75? Are baselines (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) matched on this?
- Corruption rate: Can you provide quantitative ablations over q (e.g., 0.0, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) on IMDB and Wikipedia? How sensitive is performance and training speed?
- Sub-sampling: You state Doc2VecC does not rely on frequent-word sub-sampling yet used it for all reported numbers. What is Doc2VecC’s performance without sub-sampling vs with?
- Local vs global context: What is the impact of removing the local context Uc (pure global) or the global context (pure CBOW) on downstream tasks?
- Training protocol: Please detail window size, negatives per positive, optimizer, batch size, number of passes, and initialization. For SICK/BookCorpus: how many epochs, and what fine-tuning settings?
- Document vector construction: In training, x is defined as binary BoW; in test-time equation d = (1/T)∑w∈D uw, is T the token count? During training, is the global context based on token counts or binary presence? Please clarify consistency.
- Statistical testing: Are the improvements on IMDB/Wikipedia statistically significant? Provide variance over multiple runs.
- Word analogy: The “~20% uplift” claim—could you provide exact micro/macro accuracies for semantic, syntactic, and overall, and the training settings used?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-motivated approach with a useful theoretical explanation and strong practical benefits. The requested clarifications and additional ablations would further strengthen the empirical rigor.
