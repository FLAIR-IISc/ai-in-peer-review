Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a CBOW-style architecture that augments local context with a global document context computed as the average of word embeddings of a (corrupted) subset of words in the document. The corruption is an unbiased dropout on BoW features, which the authors analyze via a second-order Taylor expansion, arguing it induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, uninformative words. The approach is efficient (model size independent of number of documents) and yields document vectors by simple averaging at test time. Empirically, the method achieves strong results on IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia document classification and competitive performance on SICK semantic relatedness, while being much faster than heavy sequential models. Word analogy evaluations suggest the learned word embeddings outperform those from Word2Vec. Code is provided.

Decision: Reject (incremental novelty; missing key baselines and rigor in evaluation)
- Key reason 1: The methodological novelty is modest and insufficiently distinguished from established baselines (e.g., averaging word embeddings, fastText) and existing corruption-based representation learning. The central idea—average-of-embeddings with dropout/corruption—is conceptually close to known techniques, but the paper does not compare against strong, established baselines that are particularly relevant.
- Key reason 2: The empirical validation has several issues that reduce confidence: use of test data in representation learning for a headline comparison; missing statistical significance; apples-to-oranges timing comparisons; and some theoretical/notation inconsistencies that undercut the regularization claim.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: Representing documents as averages of word embeddings is well-known; adding corruption is reminiscent of denoising/marginalized DAEs and dropout-as-regularization (explicitly cited). The model is also very close in spirit to fastText (averaging embeddings and a linear classifier), which is a strong, established baseline for classification; it is not compared here. The paper also omits NB-SVM (Wang & Manning, 2012), tf-idf n-grams, and PV-DBOW, all of which are common yardsticks for IMDB and Wikipedia classification. Without these, it is hard to assess whether Doc2VecC materially advances over strong, simple baselines.
- Theory and clarity: The regularization analysis follows established dropout analyses but contains notational inconsistencies and potential errors (e.g., Eq. 2 alternates between 1/T Ux̃ and 1^T Ux̃; diagonal Hessian term in Sec. 3.1 appears squared in one summand and not in another). The derivation relies on infinite-sample expectations and diagonal Hessian approximations under negative sampling; this is plausible but needs more careful justification. Overall, the analysis is suggestive rather than rigorous.
- Evaluation protocol: Reporting results where the test set is included in representation learning (even if standard in some prior work) is not a strong ICLR-standard evaluation. The paper notes improvements when including test data; the accepted practice should emphasize the training-only setting. No standard deviations/confidence intervals are reported. Timing comparisons to skip-thought are not on matched training regimes and thus are largely anecdotal. Details such as the negative sampling distribution (uniform here, whereas Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75) and the number of negatives are crucial and should be clarified; using uniform may disadvantage baselines.
- Ablations and diagnostics: While the paper claims the corruption is crucial, there is no systematic ablation of q (beyond stating q=0.9) or removing local context (to show reduction to DAE) or replacing corruption with simple IDF/SIF-type weighting. The data-dependent regularization claim would be strengthened by controlled comparisons showing performance vs. frequency weighting alternatives and by quantifying the trade-off between speed and accuracy as q varies. Similarly, analysis by document length would support the claims about robustness to long texts.
- Baselines on each task: IMDB lacks NB-SVM and tf-idf n-grams; Wikipedia lacks fastText; SICK shows Doc2VecC somewhat behind LSTM/skip-thought. Word analogy results are encouraging, but gains could stem from altered negative sampling/corruption and should be isolated with matched training setups to Word2Vec.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper
- Clarify equations and notation throughout (V for vocabulary vs V as projection; T used for both transpose and document length; the 1/T vs 1^T confusion; fix the Hessian expression). Ensure the derivation aligns with the negative sampling objective actually used.
- Provide full training details: number of negatives, sampling distribution (consider the standard unigram^0.75), window sizes, subsampling thresholds, learning rates, epochs, and validation protocol.
- Remove or clearly segregate transductive results; emphasize training-only evaluations and confidence intervals over multiple runs.
- Add strong baselines: fastText (Joulin et al., 2016), NB-SVM and tf-idf n-grams (Wang & Manning, 2012), PV-DBOW. Consider SIF/weighted averaging (Arora et al., 2017) as a post-hoc baseline. For IMDB, also compare to standard CNN/LSTM classifiers if feasible.
- Include ablations: effect of corruption rate q, removal of local context, using idf weights vs. learned regularization, and the effect of subsampling of frequent words (you mention it but do not quantify its effect across methods).
- Ensure fair timing comparisons: same hardware, same corpora, and matched stopping criteria. Report computational complexity and memory use.
- Strengthen the qualitative analysis by demonstrating that the model indeed downweights frequent stop words while preserving useful rare words, and quantify this effect across corpora.

Questions for the authors
1. How does Doc2VecC compare to fastText on the Wikipedia and IMDB tasks (both accuracy and speed)? Please include results with matched embedding sizes and training budgets.
2. What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives are used? How do results change with the standard unigram^0.75 distribution?
3. Can you provide ablations for q (e.g., 0.3/0.5/0.7/0.9) and for removing the local context term? How much of the gain is due to corruption vs. simply averaging embeddings?
4. Please report training-only results (no test data in representation learning) with multiple random seeds and confidence intervals.
5. The Hessian diagonal expression in Sec. 3.1 appears inconsistent (square missing in the first term). Can you clarify and correct the derivation?
6. Why is PV-DBOW omitted from empirical comparisons, given your hypothesis that PV learns document-specific capacity? Including it would be informative.
7. On IMDB, how does Doc2VecC compare to NB-SVM and tf-idf n-grams? On SICK, can you report results with a lightweight supervised head to close the gap with LSTM methods?
