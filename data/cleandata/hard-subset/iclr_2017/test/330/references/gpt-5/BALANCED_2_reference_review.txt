1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method for learning document representations. The key idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, but to learn those embeddings with an objective that includes a corrupted “global context” vector formed by randomly masking words in the document. The authors show that this corruption induces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words and preserves informative ones. The approach retains the computational simplicity and scalability of Word2Vec, avoids the corpus-size parameter growth and test-time inference costs of Paragraph Vectors, and yields document vectors for unseen texts by a single averaging step. Experiments on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100 classes), word analogy, and SICK semantic relatedness show competitive or superior performance with notable training/test-time efficiency; code is released.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong practicality: simple, fast, scalable, and test-time efficient, with consistent empirical gains over competitive baselines on multiple tasks.
- Sound motivation and positioning: ties to Word2Vec compositionality, explicit analysis of corruption as adaptive regularization, and a clear explanation of why the method avoids Paragraph Vector drawbacks.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper seeks an unsupervised document representation that is (i) compact and semantic, (ii) scalable to large corpora, and (iii) cheap at test time. It addresses limitations of BoW (sparsity, no semantics) and Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth with corpus, expensive inference for new documents).

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature? Yes. The method sits naturally between Word2Vec (compositionality of word vectors) and Paragraph Vectors (global context), and adopts corruption/dropout ideas akin to denoising autoencoders and dropout-as-regularization analyses. Related work is broadly covered (Word2Vec, PV, SDA/mDAE, Skip-Thought, LSTM/Tree-LSTM, BoW/IDF). The main novelty is not “averaging” per se, but enforcing that simple averaging is sufficient by training with a corrupted global context, yielding a data-dependent shrinkage on frequent words.

Do results support the claims? Largely yes.
- Empirics: On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches or surpasses Doc2Vec and substantially improves over Word2Vec+IDF, SDA, and Skip-Thought (notably with much lower runtime). On Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC consistently outperforms alternatives across vector sizes. On SICK, it is competitive with strong baselines (slightly behind LSTM/Skip-Thought) while training orders of magnitude faster. Word analogy results show sizable gains over Word2Vec, consistent with the regularization story.
- Efficiency: Training/test time comparisons substantiate the claimed advantages; the model’s complexity depends on vocabulary rather than corpus size, and test-time embedding is a single average.
- Analysis: The second-order Taylor expansion under unbiased mask-out corruption connects to an explicit, data-dependent regularizer that penalizes frequent words unless they are predictive. While approximate, the analysis is well aligned with known results on dropout and mDAE.

Caveats/weaknesses:
- Novelty is incremental in architecture; the contribution lies in the corruption-based training objective and analysis rather than a new representation form.
- Some experimental choices would benefit from deeper ablations (see below), and a few details (e.g., negative sampling distribution, sensitivity curves) are under-specified.
- The method discards word order; performance on short sentences (SICK) lags state-of-the-art LSTMs, which the authors acknowledge.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Clarity of notation: x is defined as binary BoW, but the document vector is 1/T sum over tokens. Please clarify whether Ux (used during training) matches token counts or unique types; otherwise there’s a potential mismatch between training (unique words) and test-time (tokens).
- Negative sampling: You state Pv is uniform. Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75; please justify your choice and report sensitivity. If the analysis requires uniform for simplicity, clarify what is used in practice.
- Ablations:
  - No corruption: quantify the gain from corruption vs. simple averaging training.
  - Corruption rate q: include sensitivity curves for accuracy and runtime; you mention q=0.9 works well—show robustness.
  - Subsampling frequent words: you note Doc2VecC does not rely on it; please provide results without subsampling to isolate your regularizer’s effect.
  - Local context vs. global context: show the effect of removing local context (the DEA-like variant) to support the claim that the global vector focuses on semantics beyond the window.
- Fairness of comparisons: On IMDB include both “train+unlabeled only” and “train+unlabeled+test” settings for all baselines; clearly separate the two in tables and ensure identical access. For Skip-Thought, discuss the impact of corpus/domain mismatch and sequence length.
- Variance: Report means/std over multiple runs or seeds, especially for IMDB and Wikipedia, to establish robustness.
- Discussion of limits: Explicitly note loss of word order and potential remedies (e.g., adding n-grams, position encodings, or combining with CNN/RNN encoders). A brief comparison/connection to contemporary simple baselines (e.g., fastText/supervised bag-of-ngrams; SIF/weighted averaging) would strengthen positioning.
- References: Clean up formatting issues and missing details.

Questions for the authors (to clarify in rebuttal)
- Is x (and x̃) binary or count-based during training? If binary, why is the global context scaled by 1/T (token length), and how do you reconcile training on unique types with test-time token averaging?
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use in practice? Any sensitivity results?
- Can you report an ablation with q ∈ {0.0, 0.3, 0.6, 0.9} and with/without subsampling frequent words?
- How much of the gain over Word2Vec+IDF comes from the corruption-based regularization vs. including the global context term in the training objective?
- On IMDB, when excluding test data from representation learning, what are the exact numbers for all baselines? Are improvements statistically significant over multiple runs?
- For SICK, do you train Doc2VecC on the BookCorpus with the same vocabulary as Skip-Thought? How do you handle OOVs and what is the impact of your fine-tuning step?
- In the analogy experiments, please detail corpus size, window size, embedding dimension, and training epochs across methods. Are improvements consistent across semantic and syntactic subsets?

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated, simple, and effective method with clear efficiency advantages and solid empirical support. The clarifications and ablations above would further strengthen the work.
