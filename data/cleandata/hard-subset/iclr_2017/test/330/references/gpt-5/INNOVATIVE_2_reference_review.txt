1. Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by enforcing that a document’s vector is the (scaled) average of its word embeddings during training, with a corruption mechanism that randomly masks words. The model predicts target words given both local context (CBOW) and a global document vector computed by averaging embeddings of a randomly corrupted document. A second-order Taylor analysis shows that corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving rare/informative ones. Key claimed benefits: (a) model size depends only on vocabulary (unlike PV’s dependence on corpus size); (b) very fast training and extremely fast test-time inference (simple average); (c) better or competitive performance across IMDB sentiment, a 100-class Wikipedia classification, word analogy (substantial gains over Word2Vec), and SICK semantic relatedness (competitive to LSTM/skip-thought with far lower cost). The paper also argues that Doc2VecC’s corruption/regularization obviates the need for heuristic weighting (e.g., IDF) at test time.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novelty and simplicity with clear impact: The central idea—enforcing average-of-embeddings as the document representation via corruption—is a clean, broadly applicable conceptual shift from post-hoc averaging to learning-time compositionality. The corruption-as-regularization perspective is neat and well-motivated.
- Strong empirical signal and practicality: Across several tasks, Doc2VecC is either state-of-the-art or competitive while offering major efficiency gains and vastly simpler test-time inference.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and global-context approaches (Huang et al.). The corruption mechanism is more than a speed hack; the analysis convincingly ties it to a data-dependent regularizer akin to dropout and mDAE, but adapted to the predictive CBOW objective with a document-global component.
- Claims vs evidence:
  - Efficiency: Clear advantage over PV in model size (no per-document parameters) and in test-time inference (no backprop to infer doc vectors). Reported training/inference times match the story; the use of high corruption (q=0.9) reduces updates while maintaining performance.
  - Effectiveness: On IMDB and Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC consistently outperforms BoW, Word2Vec(+IDF), DEA, and PV at comparable dimensionality. The t-SNE visualizations corroborate better cluster structure. The word analogy gains over Word2Vec (up to ~20%) suggest that the corruption-enhanced learning also produces superior word embeddings, not only doc vectors. On SICK, Doc2VecC is slightly below the strongest LSTM/skip-thought, but still strong given its simplicity and speed.
  - Regularization effect: The norm analysis of learned embeddings (small norms for frequent stopwords with Doc2VecC, vs small norms for rare words with Word2Vec/PV) is consistent with the theory and with improved downstream performance.
- Broader significance: The approach offers a compelling middle ground between simple averaging and heavy models (PV/LSTMs), with tangible speed and quality benefits. It is likely to be useful to practitioners needing fast universal document embeddings.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not decisive)
- Fairness and reporting details:
  - Ensure all baselines are tuned comparably (context window, negatives, subsampling, negative-sampling distribution). The paper mentions uniform negative sampling; Word2Vec commonly uses unigram^0.75—please clarify if baselines used their best-known settings, and provide a controlled ablation isolating Doc2VecC’s advantages from hyperparameter choices.
  - For the word analogy experiments, explicitly match training budgets (tokens, epochs), window sizes, negatives, subsampling, and negative-sampling distributions across methods. Given the surprising magnitude of gains over Word2Vec, this control is essential.
  - On IMDB, also report the protocol that excludes test texts during representation learning as a primary result; keep the “using test data in unsupervised pretraining” as a secondary analysis, or at least clearly separate them in the main table.
  - Consider adding NB-SVM (Wang & Manning) and fastText-style n-gram baselines; both are strong and highly relevant for text classification efficiency comparisons.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Systematically study the corruption rate q (including q=0, i.e., no corruption), with accuracy/time trade-offs; verify the “not sensitive” claim.
  - Ablate local context vs global context (Doc2VecC vs DEA-like variant), and the unbiased scaling choice in corruption.
  - Examine doc-length effects (short texts vs long documents), since you hypothesize LSTMs are better for short sentences.
- Analysis/depth:
  - Provide more quantitative evidence for the regularizer’s behavior (e.g., plot embedding norm vs frequency and informativeness measures; measure contribution to downstream accuracy when removing stopwords).
  - Clarify the computational complexity per update and per epoch, including the expected number of nonzero entries after corruption.
  - Discuss extensions to Skip-gram training and subword embeddings (e.g., character n-grams) to handle OOV and morphology; this could further strengthen both analogy and classification performance.
- Clarity and polish:
  - Clean up typos and missing references/figures/tables in the current draft. Some notation (e.g., Pv) and scaling (1/T) could be explained more carefully.
  - Specify training hyperparameters (learning rate, negatives per positive, window sizes, epochs) and hardware for all experiments. The code link is appreciated; summarize settings in the paper for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and to the unbiased scaling? What happens with standard dropout scaling or with no scaling?
- What negative-sampling distribution was used for Doc2VecC and for Word2Vec baselines? Did you tune the exponent (e.g., 0.75) and number of negatives for each?
- In the analogy experiments, were all training hyperparameters matched across methods? If not, can you provide a controlled comparison?
- How does Doc2VecC perform on very short texts (e.g., tweet-length) versus longer documents? Any guidance on when to prefer LSTMs?
- Can Doc2VecC be combined with subword units (e.g., n-grams) and/or position-aware averaging? Any preliminary results?
- At test time, have you tried learned weighting schemes versus pure averaging, now that the embeddings are regularized? Does IDF still help or hurt?
- What is the memory/throughput at larger vocabularies (e.g., 1M words), and does q need to increase to keep training fast?

Overall, this is a simple, elegant idea with strong practical relevance and solid evidence. I recommend acceptance.
