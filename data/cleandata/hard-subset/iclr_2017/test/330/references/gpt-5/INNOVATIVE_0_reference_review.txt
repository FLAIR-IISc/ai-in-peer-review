Summary and contributions
This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective architecture for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, trained with a corruption mechanism that randomly masks words in the document during learning. The global document vector is the scaled average of a (corrupted) subset of word embeddings, combined with local CBoW context to predict target words via negative sampling. The authors show (via a second-order Taylor expansion) that this corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer which dampens embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving rare/informative ones. The resulting model (i) decouples model size from the number of documents; (ii) is trained efficiently (Word2Vec-like); (iii) produces test-time document vectors by a single average; and (iv) achieves strong results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia categories), word analogy, and competitive results on SICK sentence relatedness. The paper emphasizes large gains in training and inference speed relative to Paragraph Vectors.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty with practical impact: training with corruption to make simple averaging a first-class document representation, together with a clear analysis linking the corruption to an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer.
- Strong empirical evidence and compelling efficiency story: consistently competitive or superior accuracy to established baselines alongside large speedups and decoupling from corpus size.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the longstanding tension between document representations that are simple/fast (BoW, averaged embeddings) and those that are expressive but heavy and corpus-size-dependent (Paragraph Vectors). Framing the goal as “make averaging work by design” is crisp and well-motivated by prior observations on linear compositionality in embeddings.
- Placement in literature: The connections to Word2Vec/CBoW, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders/dropout (via the Taylor expansion), and prior averaging/IDF heuristics are appropriate. The paper articulates how Doc2VecC differs: corruption is applied during learning to enforce document-level compositionality and induce an adaptive regularizer, rather than relying on post-hoc weighting.
- Method: The architecture is minimal and scalable, with precise training objective and corruption scheme. Normalizing by document length (1/T) and using unbiased corruption are principled. The derivation of the regularizer is standard but informative, yielding a clear intuition for why frequent, uninformative terms are suppressed without manual IDF.
- Empirical validation: Results on IMDB, Wikipedia classification, and word analogy are strong; SICK results are competitive given the architecture’s bias toward longer texts. The efficiency comparisons (notably inference vs Paragraph Vectors and Skip-Thought) convincingly support the practicality claims. The analysis of low-norm embeddings for frequent words illustrates the intended regularization effect.
- Scope and novelty: While “averaging embeddings” is not new, the key novelty is training so that averaging is the target representational constraint, plus the corruption-as-regularization analysis and its impact on both performance and efficiency. This is a tasteful, bold simplification that offers a new direction for document embeddings.

Areas to strengthen (did not preclude acceptance)
- Ablations: The paper would be stronger with ablations isolating the contributions of (a) corruption vs no-corruption, (b) presence/absence of local context, (c) 1/T normalization, and (d) varying corruption rate q (accuracy/speed tradeoff curves).
- Negative sampling details: The paper states Pv is uniform; Word2Vec commonly uses unigram^0.75. It’s important to clarify and, ideally, control this choice across baselines, as it can impact both word analogy and downstream performance.
- Baselines: A direct comparison to PV-DBOW (Paragraph Vectors without word embeddings) is warranted, as the discussion suggests its behavior differs. Also, include a Word2Vec + learned weighting baseline (e.g., IDF or SIF-like weighting) to separate the effect of learning-time regularization from test-time heuristics.
- Statistical rigor: Provide standard deviations or confidence intervals across multiple runs for key benchmarks, especially when claiming sizable gains (e.g., 20% uplift on analogy).
- Wikipedia dataset construction: More detail on category sampling, potential overlap, and whether train/test splits are strictly disjoint in content and near-duplicates would help assess generalization.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the computational profile: Report training throughput (tokens/sec), number of epochs, window size, and the effect of q on both speed and memory footprint. A plot of accuracy vs wall-clock time would be helpful.
- Discuss long-document behavior: Averaging can dilute rare signals in very long documents. Do alternatives like length-scaling, sublinear T normalization, or learned positional weighting help?
- Theory scope: State assumptions under which the Taylor expansion approximation holds and comment on whether higher-order terms matter empirically.
- Writing/formatting: Clean up typos, incomplete references, and ensure consistent notation (e.g., bold vs non-bold vectors, dimensions). Some figures/tables are referenced but not shown; ensure all are present and legible.
- Reproducibility: The public code is appreciated—ensure scripts reproduce all tables with fixed seeds and specified preprocessing.

Questions for the authors
1) What negative sampling distribution did you use in all experiments? If uniform, how does performance change with the unigram^0.75 distribution used in Word2Vec?
2) How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q and window size? Please provide accuracy vs q curves and a speed/accuracy tradeoff.
3) Can you report ablations for (i) no corruption, (ii) no local context (global-only), and (iii) no 1/T normalization?
4) How does Doc2VecC compare to PV-DBOW and to Word2Vec + SIF/IDF when all are trained/evaluated under identical preprocessing and sampling?
5) For the “20% uplift” on analogy: are training corpora, dimensionalities, and negative sampling hyperparameters matched to the Word2Vec baseline?
6) How do you handle OOV words at test time? Does fine-tuning vocabulary on the target task risk leakage? Please clarify the protocol for SICK.
7) Could you comment on the failure modes? E.g., documents with multiple topics, extremely short/long texts, or heavy use of rare entities.

Overall, this paper introduces a simple, elegant idea with solid empirical performance and a compelling efficiency story. I recommend acceptance.
