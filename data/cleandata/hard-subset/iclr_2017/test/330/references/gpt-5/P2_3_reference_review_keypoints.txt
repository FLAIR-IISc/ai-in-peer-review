- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn document vectors as averages of word embeddings trained with a CBOW-like objective using local context plus a corrupted global document context.  
- Uses random masking (unbiased dropout) for the global context, speeding training and inducing data-dependent regularization on word embeddings.  
- Second-order Taylor expansion analysis shows corruption suppresses embeddings of frequent uninformative words while preserving rare informative ones.  
- Decouples parameter count from corpus size (depends only on vocabulary).  
- Fast to train and test; test-time representation is simple averaging.  
- Produces competitive or superior results on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-class classification, and word analogy; competitive on SICK semantic relatedness.  
- Offers substantial efficiency gains at training (fewer parameters updated) and test time (simple averaging).  
- Code is released, aiding reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: simplicity, clear analytical explanation for averaging via corruption-as-regularization, strong empirical performance, and convincing efficiency benefits.  
- Motivation: targets efficient, corpus-size-independent document representations and improves over post-hoc averaging by training averages to be predictive.  
- Novelty: neat practical combination of CBOW with corrupted global average, plus interpretable regularization via Taylor analysis.  
- Empirical support: substantial gains over BoW, SDAE, Word2Vec+IDF, and Doc2Vec on several tasks; improved analogy accuracy; partial ablations and embedding-frequency analyses included.  
- Rigor/clarity: analysis is approximate but standard and yields qualitatively correct predictions; method and objective are mostly clearly specified.  
- Missing/limited baselines: omits fastText (strong, efficient baseline) and more recent sentence embedding methods.  
- Fairness/details concerns: baselines use differing dimensionalities and pretraining corpora; negative sampling distribution appears uniform rather than the usual unigram^0.75; hyperparameter parity not fully documented.  
- Ablation gaps: more quantitative ablations needed for corruption vs no corruption, local vs global-only context, and varying corruption rates.  
- Recommended additions: include fastText and SIF/SIF+whitening baselines, comprehensive ablations (q values, schedules, global-only/local+global, IDF/SIF at test time), and report sensitivity to subsampling and negative sampling.  
- Reproducibility requests: report full training details (window size, negatives, learning rate schedule, epochs, threading, implementation) and ensure time comparisons are apples-to-apples.  
- Analysis requests: plot embedding norm vs document frequency and show downstream impact when regularization/reweighting is removed.  
- SICK results: strengthen by reporting with/without fine-tuning and comparing to more recent sentence encoders.  
- Limitations to discuss: handling very long documents vs hierarchical models, lack of subword information, and potential gains from n-grams (as in fastText).  
- Questions for authors: which negative sampling distribution was used and sensitivity to unigram^0.75?  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of performance to corruption rate q and unbiased scaling; would Gaussian noise or mDAE-style marginalization help?  
- Questions for authors: provide quantitative ablations removing local context and removing corruption to isolate component contributions.  
- Questions for authors: any benefit at test time from tf-idf weighting or SIF-style debiasing on averaged embeddings?  
- Questions for authors: how does Doc2VecC compare to fastText on IMDB/Wikipedia under matched conditions (dimensionality and preprocessing)?  
- Questions for authors: robustness to OOVs and very large vocabularies; have subword representations been tried?  
- Questions for authors: clarify training details for the analogy task and why PV underperforms; were PV-DBOW variants tried?
