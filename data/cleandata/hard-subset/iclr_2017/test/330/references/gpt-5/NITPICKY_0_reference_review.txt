1. Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, an unsupervised method for document representation that (a) represents a document as the average of its word embeddings and (b) trains these embeddings by predicting target words given both local (context window) and global (corrupted document) contexts. The key idea is to randomly mask words in the document during training (with unbiased rescaling), which both speeds training and induces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses frequent, uninformative words. The authors provide a Taylor-expansion analysis to motivate this regularization effect. Empirically, Doc2VecC is reported to match or outperform Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vector, denoising autoencoders, and Skip-Thought on IMDB sentiment and a Wikipedia document classification task, while being efficient at test time (simple average of word embeddings). Word analogy results show sizable gains over Word2Vec embeddings; SICK semantic relatedness results are close to LSTM and Skip-Thought models while being much faster.

2. Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Numerous technical and presentation issues (mathematical inconsistencies, incomplete or confusing derivations; extensive formatting/reference errors) undermine confidence in correctness and reproducibility.
- Experimental methodology lacks critical details and contains fairness concerns (use of test data during representation learning; inconsistent training corpora/hardware across baselines; unspecified hyperparameters and noise distributions).

3. Supporting arguments
- Mathematical clarity and correctness:
  - Eq. (2) appears dimensionally inconsistent between numerator/denominator (1/T vs 1^T); several typos impede verification.
  - The Hessian-based regularizer derivation (Sec. 3.1) contains apparent mistakes (missing squares and signs in second derivatives), and relies on approximations without sufficient conditions or error bounds; as written, the expression for R(uj) is hard to trust.
  - The claim that Doc2VecC “reduces to denoising autoencoders” if local context is removed is not substantiated; the negative sampling objective is not a reconstruction loss, and the correspondence requires more care.
- Experimental methodology and fairness:
  - The representation-learning setting that includes the test set is inappropriate; while a second setting excludes it, results are not carefully separated or discussed for all baselines.
  - For IMDB, Skip-Thought is evaluated with a pre-trained model on a different large corpus; for SICK, Doc2VecC is trained on the book corpus and then “fine-tuned” without labels, whereas Skip-Thought uses a different OOV handling. These are not apples-to-apples comparisons.
  - Word2Vec baselines: important implementation choices (negative-sampling distribution, window size, negative samples, subsampling thresholds, epochs, optimizer, learning rate schedules) are not consistently reported; the paper states Pv is uniform for negative sampling, which departs from standard unigram^0.75 and affects comparability.
  - Analogy experiments lack precise protocol (3CosAdd/3CosMul, question filtering), absolute numbers, and controlled hyperparameters across methods; the ~20% uplift claim is not audited.
  - Timing comparisons mix CPU times on one machine with GPU times cited from other papers; this is not a fair efficiency comparison.
- Reproducibility and clarity:
  - Many typos, grammatical issues, and formatting artifacts (equation glitches, references with placeholders like “? \Q2014\E”, inconsistent nomenclature “DEA/SDA”, footnotes inline, broken title). Figures are referenced but not included. This significantly hampers comprehension and verification.
  - Important experimental details are missing: context window size k, negatives per update, embedding dimensionalities per experiment, corpus preprocessing, SVM hyperparameters and feature scaling, stopping criteria.
  - The provided code link appears de-anonymizing, which violates double-blind review policy.
- Scope and novelty:
  - The core representation (average of word embeddings) is well-known; the claimed novelty hinges on the corruption-driven training and its regularization analysis. Given derivational issues and limited ablations, the contribution feels incremental.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all part of the decision)
- Fix all mathematical inconsistencies. Provide a clean, self-contained derivation with correct gradients/Hessians, clear notation, and dimensional checks. Consider moving the second-order expansion and assumptions to an appendix, with a proof sketch and conditions under which the approximation is accurate.
- Substantiate the “reduces to denoising autoencoder” claim or remove it. If a precise connection exists (e.g., marginalized denoising with a linear decoder), formalize the objective equivalence.
- Provide comprehensive ablations:
  - Effect of corruption rate q (including q=0, to show relation to Word2Vec+AVG), of local vs global context, and of subsampling frequent words (present results without subsampling to support the claim that Doc2VecC is less reliant on it).
  - Negative-sampling distribution choices (uniform vs unigram^0.75).
  - Weighting schemes for averaging at test time (uniform vs learned).
- Ensure fair baselines:
  - Train Word2Vec, Paragraph Vector, and Doc2VecC on the same corpora with matched hyperparameters and report both quality and wall-clock/throughput on identical hardware.
  - For IMDB and Wikipedia, do not use test data in representation learning; if shown, clearly separate and label transductive results.
  - For SICK, align pretraining and OOV handling across methods where possible.
- Report full details for analogy evaluation and include per-category numbers and configurations.
- Clean up references and formatting to meet conference standards; remove de-anonymizing links and move code to an anonymous repository for review.

Questions for the authors
- Please correct Eq. (2) and the Hessian-based derivation in Sec. 3.1. Can you provide a supplemental with the full derivation and confirm the exact form of R(uj)?
- What were the exact hyperparameters for each method (window size, negatives, learning rate schedule, epochs, subsampling threshold, noise distribution, embedding sizes) across all experiments?
- Can you provide results for Doc2VecC without subsampling frequent words, and for q ∈ {0, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9}, to support the robustness claim?
- For analogy, which evaluation (3CosAdd/3CosMul) was used? What preprocessing (lowercasing, vocabulary filtering)? Please share absolute accuracies per subtask and matched Word2Vec settings.
- On Wikipedia, how did you ensure no leakage between unlabeled and test splits? Are documents with multiple categories excluded or reassigned?
- How is “Doc2VecC reduces to denoising autoencoders” formalized? If not exact, what is the precise relation?
- Can you provide matched hardware timing and throughput (tokens/sec) for all methods and tasks?
