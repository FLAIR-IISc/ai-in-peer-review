- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn document representations by averaging word embeddings with random-word corruption during training, combining local CBOW context and a corrupted global document context.  
- Taylor-analysis shows corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights common/uninformative words and preserves informative ones, explaining effectiveness and training efficiency.  
- Empirically competitive or superior to Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, Denoising Autoencoders, and Skip-thought on IMDB and Wikipedia, with much faster training and test-time inference.  
- Improves word analogy performance over Word2Vec and yields competitive SICK semantic relatedness vs LSTM methods while being far faster.  
- Code is released, aiding reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Clear, well-motivated, elegant idea with solid theoretical justification (data-dependent regularization).  
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical performance and significant speed advantages across tasks versus heavier models.  
- Addresses problem/motivation: tackles semantic, efficient, scalable document representation and overcomes BoW sparsity and Paragraph Vector scalability/test-time costs.  
- Method novelty: averaging embeddings is known, but corruption during training plus principled regularizer analysis is the substantive novel contribution.  
- Theoretical derivation: Taylor expansion around unbiased corruption yields diagonal variance and a clear regularization term that explains suppression of stop-words.  
- Empirics: reports strong accuracy on IMDB and Wikipedia, large test-time speedups, improved word embeddings, and competitive SICK results using a consistent downstream linear SVM.  
- Supporting reproducibility: uses same classifier across methods and releases code.  
- Improvement: Missing ablation of corruption (q=0) and sensitivity analysis across corruption rates and component ablations (global-only/local-only).  
- Improvement: Negative sampling drawn uniformly over vocabulary deviates from unigram^0.75—needs justification and comparison.  
- Improvement: Transductive training noted (using test docs for representation learning); an inductive-only evaluation would strengthen the claims.  
- Improvement: Several experimental hyperparameters and preprocessing/tokenization details are missing for reproducibility.  
- Improvement: Additional baselines recommended (PV-DBOW, fastText averaging, Doc2VecC+IDF, SIF-weighted averages) to better contextualize results.  
- Improvement: Presentation issues—typesetting problems, incomplete references, and figure/table quality need polishing.  
- Question 1: How sensitive is performance to corruption rate q and document length; include q vs accuracy curves and q=0 ablation?  
- Question 2: Exact negative sampling scheme (k negatives, distribution, dynamic vs fixed); any experiments with unigram^0.75?  
- Question 3: What window size, embedding dimensions, epochs, optimizer, and learning rate schedule were used and how were they tuned per task?  
- Question 4: Did you try normalizing the global term by the number of unmasked words instead of 1/T scaling, and how does it affect variance/stability?  
- Question 5: Are input embeddings U used only for document vectors; did you try combining U and V at test time (average/concatenate)?  
- Question 6: Can you report Doc2VecC without local context (global-only) and without global context (local-only) to validate complementary roles?  
- Question 7: On SICK, how much does fine-tuning embeddings on the target corpus without labels help, and how are OOVs handled?  
- Question 8: For Wikipedia, can you provide an inductive variant (no test documents in representation learning) to rule out transductive gains?
