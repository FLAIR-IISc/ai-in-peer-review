Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple document representation learned by predicting target words from both local context (CBOW) and a global document context computed as the average of word embeddings, where the document is randomly corrupted (mask/dropout) during training. At test time, a document vector is the simple average of its word embeddings.
- The corruption yields a closed-form Taylor-based justification as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights embeddings of common/uninformative words and preserves rare/informative ones.
- Claimed advantages: decoupling model size from corpus size (no per-document parameters), efficient training akin to Word2Vec, fast test-time encoding, and strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. The paper also claims superior word-analogy performance compared to Word2Vec.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Numerous clarity, notation, and reporting issues (missing/duplicated tables, inconsistent equations, broken references) that materially impede verification and reproducibility.
2) Empirical evidence is underspecified and lacks essential ablations/controls; several comparisons are arguably unfair or unclear (e.g., negative sampling distribution, dimension/time budgets, domain mismatch), reducing confidence that the claims are well supported.

Supporting arguments
- Clarity/notation: There are pervasive inconsistencies that affect technical understanding.
  - x is defined as a binary BoW (xj=1 iff word j appears in the document) but the document length T is the number of tokens. The model uses 1/T Ux̃ and later defines the test-time vector as 1/T ∑w∈D uw (token average). If x is binary, Ux averages types, not tokens; this is inconsistent with the token-normalized vector used at test time. Please clarify whether x contains counts or binary indicators; otherwise the theory/implementation mismatch undermines the regularization derivation and training-objective alignment with test-time averaging.
  - The Hessian diagonal term appears dimensionally inconsistent: ∂^2 f/∂x_j^2 is written with a factor (1/T v_w^T u_j) without square in the first term, but squared in the second term. For a logistic negative log-likelihood, the second derivative should involve the square of the inner product (up to scaling). This must be corrected and derived carefully; otherwise the form of R(uj) is questionable.
  - Several figures/tables are referenced but are missing or duplicated (e.g., two different “Table 5”, Table 1/2/3 not shown, Figure 1/3/4 referenced but not present). This prevents assessment of key results (IMDB performance/time, word norms, analogy subtasks).
  - References are broken or malformed (e.g., “? \Q2013\E”), and some names/abbreviations are inconsistent (DEA vs SDA, PV vs Paragraph Vectors).
- Methodological details:
  - Negative sampling distribution Pv is stated to be uniform over the vocabulary. This deviates from established practice (unigram^0.75) and can significantly affect both word and document representations. No justification or sensitivity analysis is provided; K (# negatives) is not specified.
  - Corruption rate q is fixed to 0.9 with a claim of insensitivity, but no ablation/curve is reported. Since the regularizer scales as q/(1−q), at q=0.9 the coefficient is 9, which is substantial; quantifying sensitivity is important.
  - Window size, learning rate schedule, number of epochs, optimizer, and subsampling thresholds are missing for several experiments; reported environment mixes CPU times for proposed model with GPU training times from prior work (skip-thought), making wall-clock comparisons hard to interpret.
- Empirical comparisons:
  - Word analogy: The paper claims ~20% improvement over Word2Vec but does not show the full table for all 14 subtasks or confidence intervals. Given the objective is not identical to Word2Vec and uses uniform negatives, this is a surprising result that requires more transparency, including exact training data, dimensions, and hyperparameters matched across methods.
  - IMDB: Comparisons mix 100-d representations for most methods versus 4800-d skip-thought encodings. While the proposed method outperforms skip-thought here, the protocol is not matched in domain, sequence length, or model capacity; moreover, skip-thought is used as a fixed encoder trained elsewhere, whereas Doc2VecC is trained on the target corpus. Conversely, for SICK, Doc2VecC is pre-trained on a large book corpus and then adapted on SICK (without labels), while skip-thought uses a generic model; again, not a fully matched comparison.
  - Paragraph Vectors: Inference time reporting is appreciated, but PV hyperparameters and inference settings (epochs, learning rate, window, negatives, doc vector optimization steps) are not provided. PV is known to be sensitive to these; without careful tuning the comparison may be unfair.
  - Ablations are missing: no study of (i) removing local context, (ii) removing corruption (q=0), (iii) using IDF at test time, (iv) replacing uniform negatives with standard distribution, or (v) using counts vs binary x. These are needed to attribute gains to the core idea (corruption-induced regularization).
- Theory: The Taylor-expansion argument is heuristic, assumes “infinite” corruption samples, diagonal Hessian, and treats parameters as fixed. That is fine as intuition, but the derivation must be internally consistent (see Hessian issue) and aligned with the implemented x (binary vs counts). Without this, the claimed data-dependent regularizer is not convincingly established.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Fix all notation inconsistencies, clarify whether x encodes counts or binary indicators, and reconcile training and test-time averaging. If counts are used, update definitions early and adjust the derivation accordingly.
- Provide complete experimental tables/figures, with standard deviations over multiple runs where possible, and ensure fair, matched settings across baselines (dimensions, negatives, training budget).
- Include ablation studies: effect of q; effect of corruption on frequent-word norms; with/without local context; different negative sampling distributions; with/without subsampling of frequent words.
- Report full training details (optimizer, LR schedule, epochs, batch size, window size k, negatives K, subsampling threshold t, vocabulary cutoffs) and release scripts to reproduce IMDB/Wikipedia/SICK results.
- For time comparisons, either normalize to a common hardware setting or report per-token throughput.
- Clean up references (complete bibliographic data), fix duplicated table numbers, and remove stray footnotes in text.

Questions for the authors
1) Is x binary or count-based? If binary, why divide by T (token length) in 1/T Ux̃ and at test time average over tokens? Please reconcile.
2) Please provide the corrected Hessian derivation with explicit squares and all constants, and the exact form of R(uj).
3) What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives K are used in all experiments? Have you tried the standard unigram^0.75 distribution?
4) How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q? Please provide a performance curve and training-time trade-off.
5) What are the window size, optimizer, LR schedule, epochs, and subsampling t for each dataset?
6) Can you provide ablations removing local context (pure global), removing corruption (q=0), and using IDF at test time to isolate the source of gains?
7) For PV, what inference settings (steps/epochs) were used for test documents? How were PV hyperparameters tuned?
8) For IMDB and SICK, can you equalize representation dimensionality across methods and control training domains to ensure fair comparisons?
9) For the analogy task, please include the full 14-subtask breakdown, exact training corpus, and matched hyperparameters versus Word2Vec.
10) Does Doc2VecC still outperform when negatives use standard distributions and when you do not fine-tune on target datasets?
