- Problem: Learn efficient, general-purpose document representations that capture global semantics without per-document parameters and with fast inference.
- Method: Doc2VecC averages word embeddings and trains them with a predictive objective conditioning on local context and a corrupted global document vector (word dropout).
- Theoretical insight: Corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent uninformative words while preserving informative ones.
- Claims: No per-document parameters, Word2Vec-like efficient training, test-time document vector is a simple average, corruption gives principled regularization, and competitive or superior performance on multiple benchmarks.
- Empirics: Results reported on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia classification, SICK relatedness, and word analogy tasks, with speed comparisons and t-SNE visualizations; code released.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Simple, well-motivated idea with a clean theoretical justification and fast test-time encoding.
- Key reason 2: Strong empirical gains across benchmarks and substantial efficiency improvements.
- Motivation/placement: Builds on Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors and DAE literature, addressing weaknesses of naive averaging without post-hoc heuristics.
- Correctness/rigor: Taylor expansion and variance simplifications provide a plausible, insightful analysis despite standard approximations.
- IMDB results: Matches/exceeds Paragraph Vectors and outperforms Word2Vec+IDF and DAE, with large speedups and no inference at test time.
- Wikipedia classification: Consistent outperformance across embedding sizes and cleaner clustering in visualizations.
- SICK relatedness: Competitive with LSTM/Skip-Thought while being far more efficient and better than earlier tree/RNN baselines.
- Word analogies: Reported gains over Word2Vec suggest corruption benefits word-level semantics as well as document encoding.
- Efficiency: Strong speed/accuracy tradeoff; CPU-only training and millisecond-level test-time encoding are practically compelling.
- Novelty limitation: Contribution is incremental since averaging and corruption/dropout analyses are known; novelty lies in their predictive combination and empirical validation.
- Baseline concerns: Need clarity/tuning for Paragraph Vectors (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW) and consistent negative sampling settings across methods.
- Need for ablations: Required experiments include removing corruption, varying q (e.g., 0, 0.5, 0.9, 0.95), and changing local/global context mixing.
- Question 1: Which Paragraph Vector variants and hyperparameters were used, and can results for the strongest PV setting be reported?
- Question 2: Did all methods use the same negative sampling distribution and number of negatives; if not, can this be aligned and sensitivity reported?
- Question 3: How much gain is from corruption vs training with a global-avg context; please report q ablations and local/global weighting variants.
- Question 4: Provide detailed training hyperparameters for the analogy results (dataset size, epochs, subsampling, negatives) and standard Word2Vec benchmarks for reference.
- Question 5: Have test-time reweightings (e.g., IDF, SIF) or removing the first principal component been tried on Doc2VecC, and do they help?
- Improve clarity: Clarify loss approximation steps, signs, and assumptions in Section 3.1 and intuitively explain the regularizer’s frequency behavior.
- Provide ablation table: Include a compact table isolating corruption, local/global balance, and subsampling effects.
- Standardize comparisons: Use consistent negative sampling and subsampling across methods and report hyperparameters in an appendix.
- DAE claim: Either qualify “reduces to DAE” as “DAE-like” or empirically/analytically demonstrate equivalence.
- Presentation fixes: Clean up malformed citations, references, and formatting.
