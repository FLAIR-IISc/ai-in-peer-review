- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn word embeddings so a document vector is the average of its (stochastically corrupted) word embeddings for efficient document representation.  
- Training predicts target words from local context plus a global document vector formed by averaging a corrupted subset of words.  
- Key technical idea: unbiased token-level corruption (mask-out) during training.  
- Taylor-expansion shows corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent/uninformative words and preserves rare/informative ones (a principled alternative to IDF).  
- Parameter-efficient: parameters scale with vocabulary size not corpus size; fast train/test and simple averaging at test time.  
- Empirically matches or outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, skip-thoughts, and several LSTM methods on IMDB, Wikipedia 100-way, word analogies; competitive on SICK.  
- Paper provides speed comparisons and qualitative analyses supporting scalability and practicality.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Main reasons for acceptance: simplicity, strong multi-task empirical results, clear speed/memory advantages, and a plausible theoretical explanation for corruption.  
- Problem motivation: addresses BoW sparsity, avg-of-embeddings lack of inductive bias, and Paragraph Vectors’ scalability and inference costs.  
- Method is straightforward/reproducible: CBoW-style local context + global corrupted-average, trained with negative sampling.  
- Corruption derivation is insightful though approximate (assumes infinite corruptions, uses Taylor/diagonal Hessian approximations).  
- IMDB: matches/beats PV and outperforms BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, denoising autoencoders; much faster at test time.  
- Wikipedia classification: consistent improvements and meaningful cluster separation (t-SNE).  
- Word analogies: stronger than Word2Vec across many subtasks, indicating better word embeddings from corruption.  
- SICK: competitive with LSTM/skip-thoughts but far faster; caveat that sentences are short relative to model design.  
- Efficiency: benefits from high corruption rates without hurting accuracy; solid timing comparisons.  
- Literature placement: incremental but distinct—integrates averaging objective into training with principled corruption and broad empirical validation.  
- Weakness: some baseline and hyperparameter details (negative sampling dist, window sizes, PV variants/tuning) are under-specified—may affect fairness of comparisons.  
- Weakness: lacks key ablations (no-corruption baseline, q-sweep plots, local vs global context ablation).  
- Weakness: theoretical derivation is approximate and assumptions/limits should be more explicitly stated.  
- Weakness: clarity/formatting issues (typos, inconsistent notation, malformed references) need fixing.  
- Suggested ablations/improvements: vary q and show accuracy vs speed, include no-corruption and no-unbiased-scaling baselines, remove local context to quantify its contribution.  
- Suggested baselines/settings: specify negative sampling distributions (unigram^0.75), include and tune PV-DM and PV-DBOW variants, report PV inference costs, and train Doc2VecC on SICK only for fair SICK comparisons.  
- Suggested analyses: plot embedding norm vs unigram frequency, show effect of subsampling, report variance/error bars across runs.  
- Clarity fixes: unify notation (ct, x, 1/T scaling), clean equations, fix references, and state computational complexity per update/memory.  
- Questions for authors: which negative sampling distribution used and sensitivity to it?  
- Questions: performance without unbiased scaling or without corruption?  
- Questions: effect of local window size and relative weighting of local vs global context; could a learned gate help?  
- Questions: exact PV variants/hyperparameters and stronger PV baselines with test-time costs?  
- Questions: sensitivity of Doc2VecC to corruption rate q (accuracy vs q and speed vs q)?  
- Questions: SICK-specific queries—report Doc2VecC trained only on SICK and a sentence-only variant?  
- Questions: does Doc2VecC still beat Word2Vec+IDF when frequent-word subsampling is disabled?  
- Questions: discuss limitations where order-sensitive LSTM encoders are preferable and whether position-aware features could help.
