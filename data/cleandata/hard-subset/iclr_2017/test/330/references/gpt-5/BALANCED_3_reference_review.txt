1. Summary of contributions (what the paper claims to do), being positive
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient unsupervised document representation method. It enforces that the document embedding is the average of its word embeddings by including a corrupted global context in a CBOW-style objective.
- A key idea is unbiased dropout (“corruption”) of document words during training, which both speeds up learning (fewer surviving words per update) and yields an implicit, data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, uninformative words and preserves rare/informative ones. A second-order Taylor analysis supports this claim.
- The model decouples complexity from corpus size (no per-document parameters), is fast to train and test, and produces document vectors by simple averaging at inference.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors on IMDB sentiment and a 100-class Wikipedia classification task; is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness; and produces word embeddings that perform well on word analogies. It is substantially faster than Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought vectors. Code is released.

2. Decision
Weak accept.
Key reasons: (i) a simple, well-motivated objective with a clear efficiency–performance advantage; (ii) solid empirical results across tasks, plus a plausible theoretical explanation of the regularization effect.

3. Supporting arguments
What is the specific problem?
- Learning general-purpose, unsupervised document representations that are efficient and effective across tasks. The paper aims to get the best of Word2Vec (scalability) and Paragraph Vectors (document semantics) while avoiding per-document parameters and expensive test-time inference.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- The motivation—making the average of word embeddings a first-class representation during training—is clear and practical. The corruption mechanism connects to dropout/denoising autoencoders; the Taylor expansion echoes analyses of dropout-style regularization.
- Related work coverage is reasonable (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, DAEs, skip-thought, LSTMs). That said, some relevant baselines/methods deserve mention or comparison: fastText-style averages for supervised tasks; SIF/weighted averages (Arora et al., 2017) for sentence/document embeddings. The paper could be clearer about differences to Huang et al. (2012) and other “global context” CBOW variants.

Does the paper support its claims?
- Efficiency: convincingly supported—no per-document params; training and test-time numbers are large improvements over Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought. The corruption rate q=0.9 further accelerates training.
- Effectiveness: On IMDB and Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC consistently outperforms BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF and Paragraph Vectors with small vectors. On SICK, it is competitive with LSTM/skip-thought while being far faster. The embedding norms analysis and word analogy results support the “suppress frequent, preserve informative” effect.
- Theory: The second-order approximation provides useful intuition for a data-dependent regularizer. While not a rigorous generalization bound, it aligns with observed behavior and prior analyses of dropout/DAE.

Caveats/concerns:
- Negative sampling distribution: The method section specifies uniform negative sampling, but standard Word2Vec uses unigram^0.75; if the baselines are trained with different distributions than Doc2VecC (or if Word2Vec is handicapped by uniform negatives), the comparisons could be biased. Please clarify and align across methods.
- Paragraph Vectors setup: There are multiple PV variants (DM, DBOW, and their combination). It is unclear which was used and whether hyperparameters were tuned comparably. PV often benefits from DBOW or combining variants.
- Using test data for unsupervised representation learning: The IMDB protocol includes a setting that uses test data in representation learning. While common in unsupervised settings, it should be clearly separated; the paper does provide both settings, which is good.
- Dataset choices: The Wikipedia classification setup is not a standard benchmark; stronger external baselines or public datasets (e.g., 20NG, Reuters, AG News) would strengthen claims.
- Ablations: The role of corruption is central; a more systematic ablation (q sweep, removing local context, vs. simple average without corruption) would solidify the causal claims.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all points affect the decision)
- Clarify negative sampling details and ensure identical choices across Doc2VecC and Word2Vec baselines (distribution, number of negatives).
- Paragraph Vectors: report results for DM, DBOW, and DM+DBOW with tuned hyperparameters. Report both accuracy and training/inference times.
- Provide a more systematic ablation:
  - Vary corruption rate q and show performance vs. speed trade-offs.
  - Remove global corruption (q=0) to show the benefit over simply training CBOW and averaging at test.
  - Remove local context to show the difference from a DAE-only variant.
- Include standard classification benchmarks and/or additional datasets to improve external validity.
- On SICK: state clearly whether Doc2VecC is trained on the same book corpus as skip-thought, whether fine-tuning uses only unsupervised objectives, and how OOV is handled. Report training time and model size for Doc2VecC on the same corpus.
- Word analogy: ensure identical tokenization, dimensionality, training data, and negative sampling for a fair comparison to Word2Vec. If PV performs poorly, discuss whether it is due to objective misalignment with word-level analogies.
- Writing/clarity: fix minor typos and reference formatting; ensure figures/tables are legible and all experimental details (hyperparameters, compute) are consolidated.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives are used for each method? Is it identical across Doc2VecC and Word2Vec? If not, please align and update comparisons.
- Which Paragraph Vector variants were used (DM/DBOW/combined)? How were hyperparameters tuned, and did you use inference tricks (e.g., initializing doc vectors) that affect speed/accuracy?
- Can you provide an ablation varying q and a setting with q=0 to isolate the effect of corruption on both accuracy and training time?
- For SICK and the book corpus pretraining: did you train Doc2VecC on exactly the same corpus as skip-thought? Was any supervision from SICK used in representation learning? How are OOV words handled relative to skip-thought’s vocabulary expansion?
- For the word analogy task, can you confirm identical preprocessing and training setups across methods and explain why uniform negatives were chosen if that differs from standard Word2Vec?
- How sensitive is Doc2VecC to the context window size and to the use of subsampling of frequent words? Can Doc2VecC perform well without subsampling?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-motivated idea with strong practical benefits and broadly positive results. Addressing the fairness/ablation concerns would significantly strengthen the contribution.
