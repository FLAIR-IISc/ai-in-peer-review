- Proposal: Doc2VecC learns document vectors by predicting target words from local CBOW context plus a global document vector enforced as the average of word embeddings.  
- Corruption mechanism: Global context is formed by unbiased dropout on the document BoW, which speeds training and acts as a data-dependent regularizer.  
- Theoretical insight: A second-order Taylor expansion of negative sampling suggests the corruption induces a frequency- and confidence-modulated penalty that down-weights frequent uninformative words.  
- Practical advantages: Model decouples parameter size from corpus size, produces test-time vectors by simple averaging, and yields substantial efficiency gains.  
- Empirical results: Doc2VecC outperforms BoW, Word2Vec+AVG/IDF, PV, and SDAE on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, is competitive on SICK, and shows strong word-analogy embeddings.  
- Decision: Review recommends Weak Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: Simple, well-motivated approach with consistent efficiency and accuracy gains and a plausible corruption-as-regularization explanation.  
- Placement and novelty: Well situated relative to CBOW, Paragraph Vectors, SDAE, and skip-thought; fills gap between post-hoc averaging and explicitly learned document vectors.  
- Method clarity: Architecture and analysis are straightforward; empirical observations align with theoretical claims about stopword damping.  
- Practical impact emphasized: Fast embedding of unseen documents and scalability advantages over PV and sequence encoders.  
- Baseline fairness concerns: Comparisons to skip-thought and PV may be mismatched or under-tuned; negative sampling noise distribution may not be consistent across methods.  
- Theoretical limitations: Taylor expansion and DAE comparison are heuristic and not formally precise given negative sampling and nonlinearity.  
- Representation choice concern: Using binary BoW for x ignores term frequency, which may hurt long-document modeling.  
- Missing ablations: No comprehensive study of corruption rate q, subsampling effects, binary vs count BoW, removal of local context, or negative sampling distributions.  
- Experimental nuances: Custom Wikipedia setup, illustrative (not rigorous) t-SNEs, and unclear SICK fine-tuning procedure reduce reproducibility and comparability.  
- Suggested experiments: Ablate q, subsampling, binary vs counts, remove ct, vary negative sampling; include tuned PV-DBOW, fastText-like baselines, and matched training settings.  
- Suggested clarifications: Report window size, negatives, learning rate schedule, noise distribution, subsampling threshold, OOV handling, and PV inference hyperparameters.  
- Dataset suggestions: Add standard document classification benchmarks (20NG, Reuters, AG News) and truly long-document datasets to validate scalability claims.  
- Theory improvements: State assumptions of Taylor approximation clearly, qualify the DAE comparison, and consider learnable per-word scalars or TF-IDF-like weighting.  
- Main questions for authors: What noise distribution was used and was it matched across baselines? How does performance vary with q and subsampling? Why use binary BoW? Which PV variants/settings were used and tuned?  
- Additional author questions: Provide training details for word-analogy experiments, generalization to standard/long-document benchmarks, and clarify the SICK “fine-tune without labels” procedure and its comparison to skip-thought.
