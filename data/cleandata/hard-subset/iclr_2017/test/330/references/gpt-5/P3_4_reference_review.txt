Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, an efficient unsupervised method for learning document embeddings. The central idea is to (i) represent a document as the simple average of its word embeddings and (ii) train these embeddings within a CBOW-style objective that conditions the target not only on local context but also on a “global” document context constructed by randomly masking document words (mask-out/dropout corruption) and averaging the surviving embeddings. A second-order Taylor expansion of the corrupted objective shows that this procedure induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving those that contribute to confident predictions—thus explaining why a plain average at test time works well. The approach keeps model size independent of the number of documents, trains as fast as Word2Vec-like models, and yields competitive or superior performance on IMDB sentiment analysis, a 100-way Wikipedia document classification task, and semantic relatedness (SICK), with strong improvements on word analogy evaluations. Test-time inference is trivial (an average), offering large efficiency gains over Paragraph Vectors.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- A simple, well-motivated model with a clear analytical justification and strong empirical results across several tasks, coupled with substantial gains in efficiency and scalability.
- The corruption-as-regularization insight is elegant, connects to established theory (dropout, marginalized denoising autoencoders), and provides an interpretable explanation for the success of averaged embeddings.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work sits naturally between Word2Vec (efficient word embeddings) and Paragraph Vectors (document embeddings), with a nod to prior use of global context via averaging (Huang et al., 2012) and denoising/corruption ideas (Vincent et al.; Wager et al.; Chen et al.). The novelty is not the averaging per se but training embeddings in a joint objective where the average is the document representation during learning, combined with unbiased mask-out corruption that both accelerates training (sparser updates) and produces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent words. This makes the simplest possible test-time representation competitive, addressing PV’s inference cost and parameter growth with corpus size.
- Technical soundness: The negative sampling objective and the unbiased corruption are standard; the second-order expansion is appropriate and recovers a familiar q/(1−q) variance factor with a Hessian that leads to a frequency-weighted penalty modulated by σ(1−σ). The qualitative implications (suppress stopwords and symbols; preserve rare but informative words) are consistent with the analysis and the empirical inspection of embedding norms.
- Empirical support: On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches or exceeds Doc2Vec and substantially outperforms Word2Vec+IDF and SDA/DEA, while being much faster at test time than PV. On Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC achieves the lowest error consistently across embedding sizes. On SICK, it is competitive with more complex LSTM/skip-thought baselines despite being significantly cheaper to train and test. The word analogy improvements over Word2Vec are notable and align with the regularization story. The t-SNE visualizations support the clustering quality claim. The reported lack of sensitivity to the corruption rate (q=0.9 used) is encouraging.

Concerns and limitations
- Novelty is incremental. Averaging word embeddings for documents is well-trodden, and corruption-based regularization is known. The contribution is a clean integration and analysis; still, the conceptual step from CBOW + average + dropout to Doc2VecC is modest.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines: Some important contemporaneous or near-contemporaneous baselines are missing or underdeveloped:
  - Paragraph Vectors: Please ensure both PV-DM and PV-DBOW are tuned and reported; DBOW often fares better on large corpora.
  - Stronger averaging baselines (e.g., SIF/weighted averages with common component removal) are not included.
  - fastText-style unsupervised baselines and subword models are absent (even if concurrent, a discussion would help).
- Experimental details: Many training details are missing (window size, negatives per update, negative sampling distribution—uniform vs unigram^0.75, optimizer, learning rate schedule, epochs, subsampling thresholds). These matter for both fairness and reproducibility.
- Ablations: The paper asserts insensitivity to q and the importance of local context, but quantitative ablations are sparse. It would be valuable to isolate the contributions of (i) corruption vs no corruption, (ii) global-only vs local+global, (iii) corruption rate q, and (iv) negative sampling distribution.
- Evaluation protocol: For IMDB, one setting uses test data for representation learning; this should be clearly separated and de-emphasized. On SICK, Doc2VecC is fine-tuned on the target corpus (unsupervised), while skip-thought uses vocabulary expansion; this asymmetry could bias results—please equalize protocols or add variants for fairness.
- Scope: The model is bag-of-words at the document level; tasks depending on word order or negation beyond local windows may remain challenging.

Suggestions to improve (non-blocking)
- Provide complete hyperparameter settings and training details; release scripts to reproduce numbers.
- Add ablations on q, number of negatives, window size, and local/global components.
- Include PV-DM and PV-DBOW with careful tuning; add stronger averaging baselines (e.g., SIF) and discuss fastText/subword variants.
- Report results with and without subsampling of frequent words to substantiate the claim that Doc2VecC relies less on this trick than Word2Vec+AVG.
- Clarify the math (e.g., ensure squared terms in Hessian-derived penalties are consistently presented) and fix reference formatting.
- Discuss limitations (order/long-range dependencies) and potential extensions (n-gram features, subword, sentence-level pretraining).

Questions for the authors
1. What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used? Did you try the unigram^0.75 heuristic, and how does it affect results?
2. How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q and to the window size? Please provide an ablation.
3. Can you report PV-DM and PV-DBOW with strong hyperparameter tuning, and clarify the inference protocol/time for PV?
4. On SICK/IMDB, can you equalize the pretraining/fine-tuning protocol with skip-thought (e.g., allow unsupervised fine-tuning of skip-thought) or, alternatively, remove unsupervised fine-tuning for Doc2VecC to isolate transfer?
5. Do results change if you use IDF weighting at test time on top of Doc2VecC (or SIF reweighting), or is the learned regularization sufficient?
6. How do results vary with embedding dimension and training corpus size for Doc2VecC vs Word2Vec beyond the reported settings?
7. Have you evaluated robustness to domain shift (e.g., train on news, test on Wikipedia/IMDB) to test the claimed suppression of corpus-specific frequent words?
