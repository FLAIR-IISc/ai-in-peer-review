Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method to learn document representations by averaging word embeddings that are trained jointly with a document-level “global context” provided via a corruption (masking) mechanism. The model uses a CBOW-style objective with both local context (sliding window) and a global context formed by a randomly masked bag-of-words representation of the document; the document vector used in prediction is the average of the surviving word embeddings, scaled to make the corruption unbiased. A second-order Taylor expansion of the expected loss under corruption yields an interpretable, data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving those that contribute to confident predictions. The approach decouples model size from the number of documents and enables very fast test-time inference (simple average). Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors across sentiment (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia-100), and word analogy tasks, and is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness, while being substantially faster than paragraph-level encoders (e.g., Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought).

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong practical contribution: a simple, scalable architecture with fast test-time inference and consistent empirical gains over widely used baselines; (2) A clear and useful theoretical perspective (expected-loss expansion) that explains a data-dependent regularization effect and connects to dropout/denoising literature. Concerns remain about novelty relative to averaging-based baselines and some fairness/details in the experimental setup, but overall the method is compelling and useful.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles the classic tension between expressiveness and efficiency in document representations. The motivation to enforce compositionality (averaging works only if embeddings are learned accordingly) is well-placed and connects to established findings about linear regularities in word embeddings.
- Method soundness: The architecture is straightforward and implementable. Using unbiased document-level masking to obtain an average-based global context is a clean idea. The second-order expansion around the mean corruption is a standard and appropriate tool; it yields an interpretable shrinkage effect that preferentially damps frequent/non-informative words, consistent with intuition and related analyses (dropout in logistic regression, marginalized denoising autoencoders).
- Empirical support: 
  - IMDB: Comparable or better accuracy than PV and better than Word2Vec+IDF/DEA, with significantly faster test-time inference than PV. The long-document regime is a good setting to highlight the advantage over sequence encoders.
  - Wikipedia-100 classification: Clear gains across representation sizes; t-SNE plots qualitatively support better clustering.
  - Word analogy: Substantial improvements over Word2Vec reported across semantic/syntactic subtasks; this supports the claim that corruption encourages higher-quality embeddings (beyond the document task).
  - SICK: Competitive with more complex sequence models and much faster to train/test.
- Efficiency: Training/test-time numbers and the analysis of corruption rate (q=0.9) support the computational benefits.

Concerns and clarifications needed
- Novelty and positioning: Averaging word embeddings for document representations is well known; the main novelty is learning embeddings to make averaging optimal via a global context with corruption. This is incremental conceptually, albeit useful in practice. The paper could better distinguish from prior “global context” work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012) and recent strong baselines like fastText (bag-of-ngrams + linear classifier) which are very relevant in this space.
- Regularization derivation details: Some constants (e.g., 1/2 factor from the Taylor expansion) and sign/scaling in Eq. (7) appear to be absorbed; please verify the exact form and discuss sensitivity to these constants. Also, the independence assumption and single-sample stochastic approximation vs. the “infinitely many samples” assumption used in the derivation should be discussed (why the regularization effect holds in practice with one sample per update).
- Experimental fairness/details:
  - Negative sampling distribution is stated as uniform over the vocabulary; most word2vec setups use unigram^0.75. Please justify and ensure consistent settings across baselines.
  - Paragraph Vectors configuration (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW, concatenation vs sum, window sizes, inference steps) should be specified; PV is known to be sensitive to inference settings.
  - Skip-thought: using a large pretrained model without adaptation to long documents is a weak baseline; this supports your claim about long sequences but should be framed accordingly.
  - Hyperparameters across methods (window size, negatives, subsampling threshold, learning rates, epochs) should be fully reported for reproducibility and fairness.
- Scope and breadth: Results on two classification datasets plus SICK are good, but adding more standard text classification benchmarks (e.g., news/reviews with varied lengths) and ablations (see below) would strengthen the case.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily gating)
- Provide ablations:
  - Remove corruption (q=0), vary q to show the accuracy vs. speed tradeoff and the strength of the regularization effect.
  - Remove local context (Uc term) to isolate the contribution of global document averaging.
  - Replace unbiased corruption with biased dropout or word-drop proportional to frequency, to test the mechanism’s specifics.
- Report full training details and ensure matched settings for word2vec/PV baselines (negative sampling distribution, subsampling, window size, negatives, epochs).
- Compare to fastText (bag-of-ngrams) and to simple TF-IDF-weighted averages with tuned smoothing; these are strong baselines in this regime.
- Analyze the learned embedding norms and their relation to word frequency/informativeness quantitatively (histograms, correlation).
- Clarify complexity: give per-token update cost and how corruption reduces backprop cost; add wall-clock on larger corpora and with GPU/CPU details.
- Improve clarity/typos: several equations and references have formatting issues; standardize citations and fix minor notation inconsistencies.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were finally used? Did you try unigram^0.75, and how does it affect performance vs. uniform?
- How sensitive is performance to q, window size, embedding dimension, and subsampling threshold? Please provide plots or a table.
- Can you report PV-DM vs PV-DBOW separately, and detail the inference procedure (steps, learning rate) for test documents?
- In the regularization derivation, can you provide the exact constant factors and discuss how closely the empirical training objective matches the derived expected loss?
- How much do local vs global contexts contribute? What happens if you train with only global context (no Uc) or only local context?
- For word analogy, were training corpora and hyperparameters identical across Word2Vec and Doc2VecC? Can you share exact settings to substantiate the ~20% uplift?
- Have you compared against fastText or bag-of-ngrams baselines, especially on the Wikipedia classification task?
- How does the method handle extremely long documents where T is large—does the 1/T scaling dilute the signal? Any benefit from TF-IDF weighting during averaging at test time?
