- Problem: Learn efficient, general-purpose document embeddings that capture global semantics without Paragraph Vector parameter blow-up or costly test-time inference, while remaining fast like Word2Vec.  
- Approach: Doc2VecC trains word embeddings with a CBOW-like objective using a global document context formed by averaging embeddings of a randomly corrupted subset of words; test-time document vectors are simple averages of word embeddings.  
- Key idea/theory: Unbiased mask-out corruption yields a data-dependent regularizer via a second-order Taylor approximation of the negative-sampling objective, down-weighting common words and preserving informative/rare words.  
- Empirics: Matches or improves strong baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, DAE, PV, Skip-Thoughts) on IMDB, Wikipedia classification, and SICK; faster training and much faster test-time inference; better word-analogy performance; code released.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main accept rationale 1: Simple, well-motivated method with clean theoretical justification and consistent empirical gains.  
- Main accept rationale 2: Strong practical impact—parameter footprint independent of corpus size, trivial averaging at test time, and robustness to common-word noise via induced regularization.  
- Novelty/placement: Novel integration of corruption during training to optimize mean-of-embeddings with analysis linking corruption to an adaptive regularizer, situating Doc2VecC between Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors.  
- Motivation: Directly addresses PV weaknesses (parameter growth, costly inference) and naive averaging’s susceptibility to common words with a principled corruption-derived mechanism.  
- Theory assessment: Second-order approximation is standard and intuitive; yields frequency-based penalties that align with dropout/marginalized DAE literature.  
- Empirical rigor: Multiple datasets and baselines, consistent wins on long-document tasks, embedding-norm analysis supports the regularization claim, and code release aids reproducibility.  
- Efficiency claim support: Parameters scale with vocabulary not corpus size; high corruption reduces updates per step explaining speedups; test-time averaging is a clear advantage over PV inference.  
- Improvement 1: Add ablations isolating corruption rate q, no-local-context variant, and training without corruption to quantify each component’s contribution.  
- Improvement 2: Use standard negative-sampling distribution (unigram^0.75) instead of uniform to ensure fair comparison and potentially better performance.  
- Improvement 3: Specify and tune PV variants (DM/DBOW), hyperparameters, and inference settings to ensure fair PV baselines (including PV-DBOW).  
- Improvement 4: Add comparisons to modern bag-of-ngrams and weighted-averaging methods (e.g., fastText, SIF) as additional baselines.  
- Improvement 5: Provide full experimental details (window size, negatives, learning rates, epochs, subsampling, initialization, regularization, validation protocols, seeds).  
- Improvement 6: Clarify hardware and parallelization used for timing comparisons to ensure apples-to-apples runtime claims.  
- Improvement 7: Broaden evaluation to test robustness across document lengths, domain shifts, and multilingual corpora.  
- Question 1: How sensitive are results to corruption rate q and window size k; provide speed/accuracy curves and recommended defaults.  
- Question 2: What is the effect of using unigram^0.75 negative sampling vs uniform on analogy and classification gains?  
- Question 3: Which PV variants and inference settings were used; could a stronger/tuned PV baseline narrow the gap?  
- Question 4: Can you report ablations removing local context and removing corruption on IMDB/Wikipedia to quantify their effects?  
- Question 5: On SICK, how much improvement derives from fine-tuning the target vocabulary vs zero-shot transfer?  
- Question 6: Is the data-dependent regularization effect (embedding norms vs frequency) consistent across other corpora/languages and how does it compare to tf-idf/SIF weighting?
