1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient approach to learn document representations by enforcing that a document vector is the average of its word embeddings, learned jointly with a corruption mechanism that randomly masks words during training. The model predicts target words from local context (CBOW) plus a global document vector formed by averaging the embeddings of the surviving words. A second-order Taylor analysis shows that corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses common, uninformative words and preserves rare, informative ones. The approach decouples model size from corpus size (no per-document parameters), enables extremely fast test-time inference (just averaging), and empirically matches or outperforms baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, Doc2Vec, DAE/SAE, RNNLM, Skip-Thought) on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, and SICK relatedness, and yields stronger word embeddings on word analogy. Training is efficient and robust to high corruption rates.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and elegance: A corruption-based objective that explicitly teaches average-of-embeddings to be a good document representation, with a clear analytical link to an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer.
- Strong practicality: Decoupling from corpus size, very fast inference, and competitive-to-state-of-the-art accuracy across diverse tasks with a simple model.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated relative to Word2Vec (CBOW/Skipgram), Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and sentence encoders. It directly targets the well-known shortcomings of PV (per-document parameters; slow inference) while leveraging the compositionality properties of word embeddings. The corruption mechanism is distinct from prior averaging-based representations because it is built into the training objective, not a post-hoc heuristic.
- Theoretical support: The Taylor expansion around the corruption mean shows an explicit regularizer that penalizes embeddings of frequent words unless they contribute to confident predictions, aligning with intuition from dropout/mDAE but tailored to this architecture. This is a compelling, interpretable justification for why the simple average can work well, and why the method is robust to common words.
- Empirical support: Results across sentiment, document classification, and SICK are consistently strong, often surpassing Doc2Vec and Word2Vec+IDF; the model also improves word-analogy scores with the same dimensionality. The speed comparisons are meaningful: Doc2VecC trains quickly and makes test-time representations in seconds where PV requires minutes of inference. Visualizations on Wikipedia show well-separated clusters for Doc2VecC.
- Scope and simplicity: The method delivers competitive quality without per-document parameters, parsers, or sequence models; it scales naturally and is easy to deploy.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Provide an ablation without corruption (i.e., train the same architecture but always use full averages) to quantify the marginal value of corruption.
  - Vary the corruption rate q and show accuracy/speed trade-offs; also include performance at q = 0 to bridge to the ablation.
  - Remove the local context term (pure doc vector) to connect more directly to mDAE and clarify contributions of local vs global context.
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Include stronger classical baselines (e.g., NB-SVM on IMDB) and more modern simple baselines for averaging (e.g., SIF/SMI-weighted averaging) to highlight where Doc2VecC’s learned weighting provides gains.
  - Compare to FastText (bag-of-ngrams) for document classification; it is a strong and efficient baseline with similar simplicity and speed.
  - Ensure consistent training data and embedding dimensionality when possible; clearly separate settings where external corpora are used (e.g., Skip-Thought).
  - Avoid using test data for representation learning, or clearly separate results and discuss leakage risks.
- Analysis and clarity:
  - Clarify negative sampling distribution used in practice (Word2Vec commonly uses unigram^0.75, not uniform) and whether the theoretical analysis still holds qualitatively.
  - Report more training details (window size, negatives per target, learning rate schedule, epochs) and variance across runs.
  - Quantify the regularizer’s effect beyond word lists: e.g., show average L2 norms vs frequency bins, and the impact on down-weighting stop words compared to TF-IDF.
  - Discuss limitations: loss of word order, potential issues on short sequences (as hinted by SICK), and when sequence models might be preferred.
- Extensions:
  - Explore subword modeling (for OOV and morphology) or an initialization with subword embeddings.
  - Consider weighted averaging learned end-to-end (e.g., learn per-token gates or attention derived from the same corruption principle).

5) Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling: What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives are used in all experiments? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Corruption specifics: Is the corruption applied to binary presence features or counts? How do results differ if counts or TF-IDF are used as x prior to corruption?
- Hyperparameters: What window size, learning rates, and training epochs were used? Please include a sensitivity analysis, especially for q.
- Subsampling vs regularization: How much of the gain comes from corruption-induced regularization vs standard subsampling of frequent words? Can you quantify performance with/without subsampling?
- Fairness of comparisons: For IMDB and Wikipedia, can you include NB-SVM and FastText? For SICK, can you report results with the same external corpora and dimensionalities where applicable?
- Theory-practice gap: The analysis assumes unbiased corruption and independence. Do you observe the predicted regularization effect quantitatively across frequency buckets? Does the effect persist if you switch to the more standard negative sampling distribution?
- Efficiency: Could you report memory footprint and throughput across methods, and confirm single-thread vs multi-thread settings to ensure apples-to-apples timing?
- Generalization: Have you tried other long-document benchmarks (e.g., Yelp/Amazon reviews, 20 Newsgroups, Reuters) to test robustness across domains?

Overall, the paper introduces a clean, novel idea with solid justification and compelling empirical results, coupled with strong practical benefits. I recommend acceptance.
