- Proposed Doc2VecC: average word embeddings trained jointly with a corrupted document-level global context using a CBOW-style objective and unbiased scaling of surviving embeddings.
- Theoretical claim: a second-order Taylor expansion of expected loss yields a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks frequent/uninformative word embeddings.
- Practical benefit: decouples model size from number of documents and enables extremely fast test-time inference (simple average).
- Empirical claim: matches or outperforms BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors on IMDB and Wikipedia-100, improves word analogy performance, and is competitive on SICK while being faster.
- Decision: Weak accept.
- Primary reasons for acceptance: strong practical contribution (simple, scalable, fast) and a clear theoretical perspective connecting to dropout/denoising literature.
- Novelty concern: averaging word embeddings is well known; main novelty—training embeddings to make averaging optimal via corrupted global context—is conceptually incremental.
- Positioning concern: paper should better distinguish from prior global-context work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012) and compare to strong baselines like fastText (bag-of-ngrams + linear classifier).
- Derivation concern: some constants (e.g., 1/2 factor) and sign/scaling in the Taylor expansion appear absorbed; exact form should be verified and sensitivity discussed.
- Derivation concern: independence assumption and using one-sample stochastic updates vs. “infinitely many samples” in the derivation need justification for practical settings.
- Experimental fairness: negative sampling reported as uniform, but typical setups use unigram^0.75—this should be justified and standardized across baselines.
- Experimental fairness: Paragraph Vectors configuration (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW, concat vs sum, window sizes, inference steps) must be fully specified as PV is sensitive to these.
- Experimental fairness: Skip-thought baseline uses a large pretrained model not adapted to long documents; this should be framed as a weak/limited comparison.
- Experimental fairness: full hyperparameters (window size, negatives, subsampling threshold, learning rates, epochs) must be reported for reproducibility and fair comparison.
- Scope concern: additional standard text classification benchmarks and more ablation studies would strengthen the paper’s claims.
- Ablation suggestion: vary corruption rate q (including q=0) to show accuracy vs. speed tradeoff and quantify the regularization effect.
- Ablation suggestion: remove the local context term to isolate the contribution of global document averaging.
- Ablation suggestion: replace unbiased corruption with biased dropout or frequency-proportional word-drop to test mechanism specifics.
- Baseline suggestion: compare against fastText and tuned TF-IDF-weighted averages as strong, relevant baselines.
- Analysis suggestion: quantitatively analyze learned embedding norms and correlate them with word frequency/informativeness.
- Complexity suggestion: clarify per-token update cost, how corruption reduces backprop cost, and provide wall-clock timings on larger corpora with CPU/GPU details.
- Clarity fixes: correct equation constants, formatting issues, notation inconsistencies, and citation formatting.
- Question: what negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used, and did you try unigram^0.75?
- Question: how sensitive is performance to q, window size, embedding dimension, and subsampling threshold—please provide plots/tables.
- Question: can you report PV-DM vs PV-DBOW results separately and detail the inference procedure (steps, learning rate) for test documents?
- Question: in the regularization derivation, can you provide exact constant factors and discuss how closely empirical training matches the derived expected loss?
- Question: how much do local vs global contexts contribute—what are results when training with only one of them?
- Question: for the word analogy gains, were training corpora and hyperparameters identical across Word2Vec and Doc2VecC; please share exact settings.
- Question: have you compared against fastText or bag-of-ngrams baselines, particularly on Wikipedia classification?
- Question: how does the method handle extremely long documents—does the 1/T scaling dilute the signal, and would TF-IDF weighting at test time help?
