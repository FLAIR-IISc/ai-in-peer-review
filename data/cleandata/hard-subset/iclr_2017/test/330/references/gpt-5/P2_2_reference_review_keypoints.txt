- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn word embeddings so averaged (corrupted) document words plus local context predict a target word; test-time doc vector is average of learned word embeddings.
- Uses a masking/dropout corruption on document bag-of-words during training to induce a data-dependent regularizer via a Taylor expansion.
- Theoretical analysis shows corruption suppresses embeddings of common/non-informative words and preserves informative ones.
- Claims: model size independent of corpus size, training/inference as fast as Word2Vec and much faster than Paragraph Vector/Skip-thought, and competitive or superior accuracy on several tasks.
- Code released and evaluated on IMDB, Wikipedia, SICK, and large news analogy datasets.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for acceptance: simplicity, solid motivation, computational efficiency, strong empirical results, and principled analysis linking corruption to adaptive regularization.
- Targets efficient, general-purpose document representations avoiding Paragraph Vector’s parameter growth and expensive inference and improving over naive averaging.
- Novelty is moderate: integrates corrupted document average into CBoW and provides an analysis connecting to dropout/mDAE; practically meaningful despite incremental conceptual step.
- The Taylor expansion derivation is technically sound under stated approximations and plausibly explains attenuation of stop-word embeddings.
- IMDB results: matches/exceeds Paragraph Vector and beats BoW/Word2Vec+IDF/DAE, with large test-time speedups over PV and Skip-thought.
- Wikipedia classification: consistent gains and qualitatively better clusters in t-SNE visualizations.
- Word analogy: substantial improvements over Word2Vec as dimension and data grow; attribution that PV wastes capacity on doc IDs is plausible.
- SICK semantic similarity: slightly behind best LSTM/Skip-thought but competitive with much better efficiency.
- Model is corpus-size independent, fast to train, and yields trivial test-time features (averaging); CPU timings highlight practical utility.
- Limitations: moderate novelty due to similarity to CBoW and dropout/mDAE ideas.
- Missing/underdeveloped ablations: effect of corruption rate q, impact of removing local context, true dependence on subsampling, and sensitivity to negative sampling choices.
- Baseline breadth/fairness concerns: should compare to fastText-style averaging (with subword/n-grams) and ensure consistent pretraining/vocab handling for SICK and other baselines.
- Suggested ablations/experiments: vary q, remove local context, train with/without word subsampling, compare negative sampling distributions and counts.
- Implementation clarifications requested: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch size, negatives per update, window sizes, and whether input/output embeddings are tied.
- Reporting improvements requested: full result tables, standard deviations over runs, and justification/comparison for the 1/T normalization choice.
- Minor fixes: clean typos/formatting and complete/consistent references.
- Questions for authors include sensitivity to q and negative sampling, effect of removing local context, necessity of 1/T normalization, comparison to fastText/SIF-weighted baselines, and whether input/output embeddings are tied.
