Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method for learning document representations by enforcing that the average of word embeddings predicts words, augmented with a stochastic corruption (mask/dropout) of the document during training. The model is CBoW-like: the target is predicted from local context embeddings plus a global document vector computed as the (scaled) average of a corrupted bag of words.
- A second-order Taylor analysis shows that corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer on word embeddings that dampens frequent, uninformative words while preserving informative ones. This provides a principled motivation for why simple averaging can work well at test time without heuristic weighting.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines on sentiment (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100-way), and is competitive on semantic relatedness (SICK). It yields better word embeddings than Word2Vec on analogy tasks, and is substantially more efficient at both training and inference than Paragraph Vectors.
- The method decouples model size from the number of documents, works with negative sampling, and requires only averaging at test time. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Strong empirical performance and significant efficiency advantages with a very simple, well-motivated architecture.
2) Clear analytical justification for the corruption-as-regularization mechanism that explains the empirical behavior and supports the design choice of average pooling.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and positioning: Learning document representations that are compact, efficient, and transferable is central in NLP. The paper is well-situated relative to Word2Vec/CBoW, Paragraph Vectors, SDA/DAE, and Skip-thought. The approach addresses key drawbacks of PV (parameter growth and test-time inference) and of naively averaging word2vec embeddings (overweighting common words).
- Novelty and motivation: While averaging embeddings is not new, the core novelty is in (i) training to make the average predictive by including it as a global context during learning, and (ii) the corruption scheme that both accelerates training and induces a principled, data-dependent regularizer that suppresses frequent words’ embeddings. The Taylor expansion connecting dropout-like corruption to an embedding-level regularizer is insightful and aligns with prior theory on dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders.
- Correctness of analysis: The derivation that unbiased corruption causes a second-order penalty proportional to the Hessian’s diagonal is sound under standard approximations. The independence structure yields a diagonal covariance, which is used appropriately. While exact constants depend on negative sampling and the choice of Pv, the qualitative conclusions (penalize frequent/uninformative words unless they contribute confidently) are convincing.
- Experimental support: Results are broad and consistent:
  • IMDB sentiment: Doc2VecC is on par with or better than PV and clearly better than BOW, DAE, and Word2Vec+IDF. It is much faster than PV at test time.
  • Wikipedia 100-way classification: Doc2VecC achieves the lowest error across embedding sizes; t-SNE plots qualitatively support better clustering.
  • SICK relatedness: Slightly behind LSTM/Skip-thought (as expected for short sentences) but better than earlier tree-structured models and feature-engineered baselines; importantly, far more efficient than Skip-thought.
  • Word analogy: Substantial gains over Word2Vec across semantic/syntactic categories, supporting the claim that the induced regularizer improves embedding quality.
- Efficiency: Training time is competitive with Word2Vec/DAE and far faster than PV and Skip-thought; inference is trivial averaging. The decoupling from corpus size is a practical advantage for deployment.

Areas for improvement (not all decision-critical)
- Baseline breadth/fairness: Including a strong linear baseline like NB-SVM (Wang & Manning, 2012) on IMDB would contextualize gains. For Word2Vec, clarify whether negative sampling uses the standard unigram^0.75 distribution and dynamic context windows; the paper mentions a uniform Pv, which deviates from common practice and could affect fairness.
- Ablations: Provide explicit ablations to quantify:
  • Effect of corruption vs. no corruption (q=0) on accuracy, analogy, and speed.
  • Sensitivity to q and to the 1/T scaling in the document average.
  • Contribution of local context vs. global context (the DAE reduction is noted but not quantified).
- Experimental details: Some setup details are missing or scattered:
  • Exact negative sampling k, learning rates, window sizes, and whether subsampling of frequent words is applied uniformly across all baselines.
  • For SICK, specify the regressor/loss used to map features to [1,5], and report standard metrics (Pearson r, Spearman ρ, MSE) with variance over runs.
  • Clarify whether “using all available data” includes test documents for unsupervised pretraining in each setting, and ensure no leakage of labels or supervised signals.
- Comparisons to PV variants: PV-DBOW often performs strongly; please clarify which PV variants are used and ensure tuning for both PV-DM and PV-DBOW, possibly with concatenation, and report times fairly.
- Clarity and presentation: Some references/formatting are broken; tables/figures are referenced but not all are shown here. Tighten the narrative and fix citations.

Questions for the authors
1) Negative sampling: What distribution do you use for drawing negatives in all methods? If Pv is uniform in Doc2VecC, have you tried the standard unigram^0.75, and how does it affect results?
2) Corruption ablations: How do performance and training speed vary with q (e.g., 0, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9)? Could you report a curve and an optimal range?
3) Local vs global context: What is the performance if you remove local context (reducing to a DAE-like model) or if you remove the global context (reducing to CBoW)? This would isolate the value of the proposed objective.
4) Document length normalization: You include a 1/T factor. Is this optimal? How do results change with sqrt(T) or IDF-like weighting in training?
5) Subsampling frequent words: You state this trick is applied to all models. Can you quantify the interaction with your corruption-induced regularizer? Does Doc2VecC still outperform without subsampling?
6) PV baselines: Which PV settings (DM/DBOW, concatenation, inference steps) were used and tuned? Can you include stronger PV configurations, and report multiple runs?
7) SICK protocol: What regressor and features were used, and what are the exact r/ρ/MSE values with standard deviations? How many epochs were used for the SICK fine-tuning without labels?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clean idea with strong empirical results and a useful theoretical perspective. With additional ablations and clarified experimental details, it would be a valuable contribution.
