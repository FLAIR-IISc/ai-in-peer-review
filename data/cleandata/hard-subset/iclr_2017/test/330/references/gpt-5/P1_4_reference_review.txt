1) Summary of contributions (positive, generous)
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a very simple and efficient document representation method that learns to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, but crucially trains with a corruption process that randomly masks words in the document. The model predicts target words using both a local context (CBOW) and a corrupted global document vector.
- A second-order Taylor expansion analysis shows that the corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer on word embeddings: frequent but uninformative words are penalized more, while embeddings that are critical for confident predictions are penalized less. This provides a clear motivation for why simple averaging at test time can work well without heuristic weighting.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC is shown to be competitive or better than strong baselines (BoW, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought, and LSTM-based methods on SICK) on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, SICK relatedness (competitive), and word analogies (substantially better than Word2Vec; PV performs poorly). The method is notably fast to train and test, with test-time representation as a simple average, avoiding PV’s expensive per-document inference.
- The paper provides runtime analysis, qualitative evidence (embedding norms of stopwords vs informative words), and visualization of learned document spaces, and releases code.

2) Decision
Weak accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical performance and significant efficiency gains with a very simple architecture; (2) A clear and insightful analysis linking corruption to a data-dependent regularizer that explains why averaging works well.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-situated relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and Skip-Thought. The idea of averaging word embeddings is not new, but the contribution here is to enforce during training that averaging is sufficient by integrating a corrupted global context into the prediction objective and to theoretically justify it via a principled regularization view. This is a meaningful step beyond post-hoc averaging or IDF weighting.
- Novelty: While corruption/marginalized DAE/dropout ideas exist, their use to learn document vectors that are explicitly averages of word embeddings within a CBOW-like predictive framework, and the derivation of an adaptive, data-dependent penalty on word embeddings tied to document prediction, are novel and practically impactful.
- Empirical support: Results on IMDB and Wikipedia classification show consistent gains and large speed advantages over PV. The word analogy improvements suggest the corruption-based training yields higher-quality word embeddings (especially suppressing stopwords), supporting the regularization claim. On SICK, performance is competitive with stronger sequence models trained on large corpora, while being significantly faster. Visualizations corroborate the quality of document clusters.
- Rigor: The second-order expansion is standard and appropriate, the assumptions are clearly stated (independent corruption; diagonal variance; expansion at the mean). The analysis leads to a concrete penalty form and matches empirical observations (smaller norms for frequent, uninformative words). The training/inference complexity advantages are clear and well quantified.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Fairness and clarity of baselines:
  - Paragraph Vectors: please report both PV-DM and PV-DBOW with careful tuning, and include faster inference variants if applicable. Clarify all hyperparameters and training budget given PV’s sensitivity.
  - Word2Vec baselines: show results with common negative sampling distribution (unigram^0.75) and standard hyperparameters; currently Pv is uniform in the derivation but practice often differs. Report both for fairness and discuss any trade-offs with the analysis.
  - Include a strong “simple average with IDF” baseline using the same embeddings your model learns (and possibly SIF weighting), to isolate the benefit of the corruption-trained embeddings from weighting schemes.
- Ablations:
  - Vary the corruption rate q and provide performance and training-time curves; explore scheduled q or per-length q.
  - Remove the local context (pure document prediction) and show the performance drop to support the claim that local context allows the document vector to focus on global semantics.
  - Report sensitivity to embedding dimension and window size on IMDB, not only on Wikipedia.
  - Turn off frequent-word subsampling to demonstrate robustness; you argue Doc2VecC relies less on it—quantify this.
- Theory:
  - Discuss the gap between the second-order approximation and the exact objective; any empirical check that the induced regularizer correlates with IDF or frequency? A scatter of embedding norm vs term frequency would help.
  - The assumption of diagonalizing the Hessian via independence of corruption ignores interactions across words; briefly discuss when this may matter (e.g., short texts).
- Experimental protocol:
  - Using the test set for unsupervised representation learning: keep this clearly separated and emphasize the training-only setting in the main claims to avoid any perception of leakage.
  - Report statistical significance (e.g., multiple runs, confidence intervals) for key comparisons.
  - For SICK, clarify OOV handling and vocabulary extension; provide a small ablation showing the effect of fine-tuning on SICK without supervision.
- Implementation details: document all hyperparameters (k, negatives per positive, optimizer, learning rate schedule, epochs), corpora sizes, and preprocessing to ensure full reproducibility.
- Writing and references: fix numerous formatting issues in the references and some typos; ensure tables/figures are fully legible and self-contained.

5) Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q? Did you try scheduling q during training or adapting it by document length?
- What negative sampling distribution did you use in practice (uniform vs unigram^0.75)? If different from the derivation, how does it affect both results and the regularization interpretation?
- What window size k and number of negatives were used across datasets? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- You claim reduced reliance on subsampling of frequent words. Can you show a systematic comparison with/without subsampling across tasks?
- For PV, which variants and hyperparameters were used? Did you include PV-DBOW and strong tuning for inference at test time?
- On SICK, what is the exact impact of the vocabulary extension/fine-tuning step? How does Doc2VecC trained only on SICK (no external data) compare?
- Did you evaluate Doc2VecC on other classification benchmarks (e.g., 20NG, AG News, Yelp) and shorter sentence tasks to better map where it excels vs LSTM/CNN methods?
- Does using subword information (e.g., character n-grams) further help Doc2VecC, especially for rare words/OOV?

Overall, the paper presents a simple, well-motivated method with strong empirical performance and a clean theoretical lens; with clearer ablations and baseline tuning, it would be a solid accept.
