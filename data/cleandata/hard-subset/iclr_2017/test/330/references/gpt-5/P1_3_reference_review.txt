Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, trained with a corruption mechanism that randomly masks words during learning. The model predicts target words from local (CBOW) context plus a global document context formed by the (unbiasedly) corrupted average of word embeddings.
- A second-order Taylor analysis shows that corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer on word embeddings: it suppresses embeddings of common but uninformative words while preserving informative ones that contribute to confident predictions. This both explains the effectiveness of simple averaging at test time and improves training efficiency by reducing backpropagated parameters per update.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC is competitive or superior to Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+IDF, Denoising Autoencoders, and Skip-thought on IMDB sentiment and a 100-class Wikipedia document classification task, while being fast to train and especially fast at test time. It yields better word analogy performance than Word2Vec and shows competitive results on SICK semantic relatedness relative to LSTM-based methods, with large speed advantages. Code is released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, well-motivated, and elegant idea with a solid theoretical explanation (data-dependent regularization) that justifies a very efficient averaged embedding representation; (2) Strong empirical performance and speed across multiple tasks, with practical advantages over Paragraph Vectors and more complex sequence encoders.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses the challenge of learning document representations that are semantic, efficient, and scalable. It identifies limitations of BoW (sparsity) and Paragraph Vector (model size growing with corpus, expensive test-time inference), and leverages known compositionality in word vectors to motivate simple averaging if trained appropriately.
- Method novelty and placement: Using average-of-embeddings as the document vector is not new, but the corruption during training that enforces this form, plus the analysis showing a principled, data-dependent regularizer, is the novel and substantive contribution. The method is well situated with respect to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and dropout-as-regularization literature.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion around the unbiased corruption mean with independent masking yields a diagonal variance and a clear regularization term. The derivation is sensible and ties directly to the training objective, predicting suppression of frequent/stop words unless informative—consistent with qualitative findings.
- Empirics: The model achieves (as reported) strong accuracy on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, with significant test-time speedups over Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought. The word analogy gains vs Word2Vec suggest that the induced regularization yields better embeddings, not just better document vectors. SICK results are slightly behind LSTMs/Skip-thought but competitive and much faster. Using the same downstream classifier (linear SVM) across methods makes comparisons cleaner. Code availability supports reproducibility.

Areas for improvement (not blockers)
- Ablations: The paper asserts the criticality of corruption but does not present a direct ablation of Doc2VecC without corruption (q=0), nor a study of how performance varies with q beyond a single choice (q=0.9). An ablation removing local context (global-only) would also clarify the contribution of each component.
- Negative sampling distribution: The paper states negatives are drawn uniformly over the vocabulary, which differs from standard unigram^0.75 used in Word2Vec. Please justify this choice and, if possible, report its effect.
- Fairness/transductive setup: On IMDB, you report both with and without using test data for representation learning; on Wikipedia, all methods are trained on all available data including test documents. While consistent across methods, this is a transductive setup; a purely inductive setting (train+unlabeled only) would strengthen claims.
- Experimental details: Some hyperparameters are missing (window size, number of negatives, learning rate/schedule, training epochs, dimension choices per task), and the exact preprocessing/tokenization. Please add for reproducibility.
- Comparisons: Consider including PV-DBOW, fastText-style averaging baselines, and a Doc2VecC+IDF test-time variant to quantify the effect of test-time weighting. A comparison to SIF-weighted averages would also be informative.
- Clarity and presentation: There are several typesetting issues, incomplete references, and minor typos. Figures/tables are referenced but not fully shown here; ensure high-resolution figures, complete tables, and polished references in the final version.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and to document length? Please provide curves (q vs accuracy) and an ablation with q=0 (no corruption).
2. What is the exact negative sampling scheme (k negatives, distribution, dynamic vs fixed)? Did you try unigram^0.75, and did it change results?
3. What window size k and embedding dimensions were used per task, and how were they tuned? How many epochs and what optimizer/learning rate schedule?
4. In Eq. (2), the global term uses 1/T U x̃ with unbiased scaling. Did you try normalizing by the number of unmasked words instead, and does it change variance/stability?
5. Do you use the input embeddings U only for document vectors? Did you try combining U and V (e.g., averaging or concatenating) at test time?
6. Could you report results for Doc2VecC without local context (global-only) and without global context (local-only) to validate the complementary roles?
7. On SICK, you fine-tune embeddings on the target corpus without labels. How much does this help, and is it necessary for your reported numbers? How do you handle OOVs there?
8. For the Wikipedia dataset, can you provide an inductive variant (no test documents in representation learning) to rule out any transductive gains?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, simple, and practical contribution with both theoretical insight and convincing empirical evidence. With the suggested clarifications and ablations, it would be a strong addition to ICLR.
