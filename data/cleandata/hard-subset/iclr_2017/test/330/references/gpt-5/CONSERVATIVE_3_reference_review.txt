Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, an unsupervised document representation model that predicts target words from local context (CBOW-style) augmented with a global document vector obtained by averaging word embeddings of a corrupted version of the document. The corruption is an unbiased masking (dropout) of words, yielding training-time speedups and, via a second-order Taylor approximation of the expected negative-sampling objective, a data-dependent regularizer that dampens embeddings of frequent, non-informative words. The learned document representation at test time is simply the average of learned word embeddings. Experiments on IMDB sentiment, a Wikipedia document classification benchmark, SICK semantic relatedness, and word analogy tasks suggest Doc2VecC is efficient and competitive, often outperforming BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, denoising autoencoders, and Paragraph Vectors, while being far faster than skip-thought.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty relative to established methods (CBOW with global context, averaging embeddings, dropout/marginalized denoising) with an analysis that is mostly standard and approximate.
- Insufficiently rigorous and potentially unfair empirical validation (use of test data in representation learning, incomplete ablations, baseline configuration concerns, and missing strong contemporary baselines).

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: Averaging word embeddings for documents is long-known, and using global context in CBOW was explored (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). The corruption mechanism and its “implicit regularization” are conceptually close to dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders; the Taylor-expansion argument follows prior treatments of dropout as adaptive regularization. The main change is to enforce, during learning, that averages suffice. This reads as an incremental method rather than a fundamentally new architecture.
- Theoretical support: The regularization result rests on a second-order approximation around the corruption mean and independence assumptions; cross-term structure is discarded, and the analysis is for a negative-sampling objective with a uniform noise distribution. The result is suggestive but does not provide guarantees that the learned averages capture semantics beyond intuitive arguments. Stronger theoretical development or tighter bounds would strengthen the claim.
- Baseline fairness and rigor:
  - Negative sampling distribution: The paper uses a uniform Pv, while established practice for Word2Vec is unigram^0.75. This can materially affect both baselines and the proposed method. It is unclear if baselines were tuned under their best-known settings.
  - Representation learning using test data: For IMDB, results are reported in a setting that includes test data during representation learning; this confounds evaluation. The paper states two settings but does not clearly separate or emphasize the numbers learned only from train+unlabeled.
  - Paragraph Vectors: PV is not designed to yield high-quality word embeddings; comparing PV word embeddings on analogy is not informative. For document classification and sentiment, PV requires careful hyperparameter tuning and inference strategies; details are sparse. The reported PV results appear weak relative to literature.
  - Missing baselines: Strong and very relevant baselines are absent, notably fastText (bag-of-ngrams) and other well-tuned linear models over n-grams, which are known to be strong on IMDB and Wikipedia classification. For sentence similarity, more recent sentence averaging with IDF/SIF weighting and common-component removal would be appropriate comparisons.
  - Skip-thought comparison: It is acknowledged that skip-thought is ill-suited for long documents; using it as a foil on IMDB overstates Doc2VecC’s advantage.
- Ablations and diagnostics: The core claim hinges on corruption; however, the paper lacks comprehensive ablations over q (including q=0), window size, number/distribution of negatives, and the inclusion/exclusion of the target word in the global context. The “data-dependent regularization” evidence is anecdotal (lists of small-norm words) rather than statistical (e.g., frequency-vs-norm plots, performance deltas attributable solely to corruption).
- Clarity and reproducibility: While code is linked, several details are missing: optimization settings, number of negatives, window sizes, subsampling thresholds, whether the target word participates in the global context, and consistent pre-processing across methods. There are typos/notation issues (e.g., Eq. (2): “1T Ux̃” vs “(1/T)Ux̃”), and the references are messy.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Reposition the contribution as a strong, simple baseline with an accompanying analysis; temper claims of state of the art.
- Provide a thorough ablation study:
  - Vary q, including q=0, to isolate the benefit of corruption.
  - Vary negative-sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75), number of negatives, window size, and context inclusion.
  - Remove the target word from the global document vector to avoid leakage and report the delta.
- Ensure strict separation between training and test data in unsupervised representation learning; report both settings but highlight train+unlabeled-only as primary.
- Strengthen baselines: fastText (bag-of-ngrams), tuned PV-DBOW and PV-DM, strong BoW/bigram TF-IDF, and SIF/IDF-weighted average with common-component removal.
- For analogy, compare only methods designed to learn word embeddings; ensure identical corpora, epochs, and hyperparameters.
- Provide statistical significance and confidence intervals where possible.
- Clean up notation, equations, and references; specify all hyperparameters and training settings.

Questions for the authors
- Does the global document vector used during training include the current target word? If so, what happens when it is excluded?
- What negative-sampling distribution and number of negatives were used for each method? Did you tune these for Word2Vec baselines to their commonly optimal settings (unigram^0.75)?
- Can you provide results on IMDB and Wikipedia where representation learning uses only train+unlabeled data (no test leakage), with full hyperparameter tuning for all baselines?
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q? Please provide performance and training time curves vs q.
- Can you report ablations isolating the contribution of the local context vs. the global corrupted average (e.g., remove Uct or remove Ux̃)?
- For the “data-dependent regularization” claim, can you provide quantitative analyses (e.g., correlation between word frequency and embedding norm; controlled experiments showing performance gains from dampening frequent, non-informative words)?
- How does Doc2VecC compare to fastText and strong n-gram baselines on IMDB and Wikipedia?
- For SICK, how much does in-domain fine-tuning (without labels) change performance? Please report with and without fine-tuning.
