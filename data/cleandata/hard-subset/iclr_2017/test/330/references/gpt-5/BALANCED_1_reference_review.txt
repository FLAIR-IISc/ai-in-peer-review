Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient document representation method. Each document is represented as the average of its word embeddings; during training, a corrupted (masked) version of the document BoW is used as a global context together with local word context to predict target words.
- The corruption mechanism acts like a dropout-style, data-dependent regularizer that suppresses frequent, uninformative words and favors informative or rare words. A second-order Taylor expansion is used to justify the form of this regularization.
- The approach decouples model size from corpus size (no per-document parameters), trains efficiently with negative sampling, and enables instantaneous test-time encoding by averaging word vectors.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC performs well on sentiment (IMDB), Wikipedia document classification, and SICK semantic relatedness, with strong improvements over BoW, Word2Vec+averaging/IDF, and Doc2Vec in most settings, while being computationally efficient. Word analogy scores also improve over Word2Vec.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
- Key reasons: (1) Limited novelty relative to existing averaging-based representations and dropout/marginalized DAE-style regularization; (2) Incomplete evaluation against strong, closely related baselines and insufficient ablations to fully support the claims.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement: Representing documents by averaging word embeddings is widely studied; using a global context vector in a Word2Vec-like prediction objective is also not new (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). The corruption-as-regularization idea, and its second-order approximation, strongly echoes marginalized DAEs and dropout-as-adaptive regularization (Wager et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014). The paper’s main novelty is combining these pieces in a specific training objective that forces average embeddings to be predictive. This is interesting but incremental for ICLR standards; the theoretical connection is qualitative and borrowed from prior analyses.
- Empirical baselines: Several highly relevant and strong baselines are missing: fastText (bag of n-grams with efficient training), SIF/weighted averaging with debiasing (Arora et al., 2017), Sent2Vec (averaging with training objectives close to this work), and more recent simple-but-strong averaging schemes. These methods are directly comparable in spirit (averaging + simple training) and often competitive with or stronger than Doc2Vec/Skip-thought on the chosen tasks. Not including them weakens the empirical case for significance.
- Ablations and robustness: The model’s performance is attributed to (i) corruption-induced regularization and (ii) coupling global and local contexts. However:
  - There is no systematic ablation over corruption rate q, or removing local context vs. global context to quantify contributions.
  - The negative sampling distribution is set to uniform over the vocabulary, which is atypical; the effect of using customary unigram^3/4 sampling is not explored.
  - The impact of document length and the 1/T scaling is not examined (e.g., long vs. short documents, which the paper hints matter).
  - The paper claims insensitivity to q but provides no curves.
- Evaluation protocol: For IMDB, training representations on all data including test (an established protocol) is reported, but this can conflate representation learning and outcome leakage; the paper does include a train+unlabeled-only setting, but more emphasis on the latter would be more rigorous.
- Fairness of comparisons: Skip-thought performance on very long documents is known to degrade; the comparison is informative but not decisive. The Paragraph Vector comparisons could be strengthened by including both PV-DM and PV-DBOW with tuned hyperparameters and inference heuristics, and by ensuring negative sampling choices match.
- Theory clarity: The second-order approximation is plausible, but assumptions and derivation details (e.g., treating only diagonal Hessian terms, choice of uniform negatives) limit rigor. The link between the derived regularizer and the observed improvements is supported by a qualitative table, but a more thorough quantitative study (e.g., norm vs. frequency plots; downstream impact when freezing/shrinking specific embeddings) would strengthen the claim.
- Writing/presentation: The paper contains typos, inconsistent notation, and some incomplete references. Figures/tables are referenced but some details are missing in the excerpt (e.g., complete numerical results, settings).

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Add strong, contemporary baselines that share the same design philosophy: fastText, SIF (smooth inverse frequency) + principal component removal, Sent2Vec, and recent TF-IDF+SVM with n-grams. These are essential for judging significance.
- Provide thorough ablations:
  - Vary q and plot performance/speed trade-offs; report the fraction of active words per update.
  - Remove local context or global context to isolate their roles.
  - Replace uniform negative sampling with unigram^3/4 and compare.
  - Examine the effect of 1/T scaling across document lengths.
  - Compare with and without subsampling of frequent words to validate the claimed reduced reliance on this trick.
- Expand analysis of the regularizer:
  - Plot embedding norm versus word frequency across methods.
  - Show that clamping frequent words indeed improves downstream performance by reweighting or ablating them at test time.
- Broaden datasets: Include additional document classification benchmarks (e.g., AG News, Yelp reviews, 20NG) with standardized splits, and a sentence-level benchmark where LSTMs excel to map the trade-offs more clearly.
- Clarify implementation details: exact context window sizes, negatives per positive, learning rate schedule, optimizer, epochs, vocabulary thresholds, and hardware.
- Improve writing and references: fix typos, normalize notation (e.g., vectors vs. indices), ensure all citations are complete and correctly formatted, and clarify footnotes.

Questions for the authors
1. What is the negative sampling distribution and number of negatives used for each dataset? Did you try the commonly used unigram^3/4 distribution, and how does it affect performance?
2. How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and the context window size? Please provide ablation curves.
3. What is the precise role of the 1/T scaling? Does removing it harm short vs. long documents differently?
4. How do results change if you remove frequent-word subsampling entirely? Does Doc2VecC match Word2Vec+IDF without this trick?
5. For Paragraph Vectors, which variants and hyperparameters were used (PV-DM vs. PV-DBOW, concatenation/sum of contexts, inference steps for test docs)? Can you report results with tuned settings and standard negative sampling?
6. Could you compare against fastText, SIF, and Sent2Vec on IMDB/Wikipedia with matched embedding sizes?
7. In SICK, you fine-tune word embeddings without labels. How sensitive are results to this step, and is vocabulary expansion done for other baselines as well?
8. Can you quantify the computational benefit more rigorously (tokens/sec, updates/sec, wall-clock with/without corruption) and the number of parameters updated per step?

Overall, the paper presents a clean and efficient approach with promising results and a useful perspective on corruption as regularization for averaging-based document vectors. With stronger baselines, clearer ablations, and tightened analysis, this work could become a solid contribution.
