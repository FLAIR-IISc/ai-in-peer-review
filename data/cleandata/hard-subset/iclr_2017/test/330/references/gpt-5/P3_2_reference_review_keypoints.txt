- Proposes Doc2VecC: train CBoW-style predictor with local context and a global context equal to the average of corrupted document word embeddings.  
- Corruption is unbiased dropout on the BoW; analysis via Taylor expansion yields a data-dependent regularizer that down-weights common words and preserves informative words.  
- Parameter count depends only on vocabulary (not documents), enabling fast training and extremely fast test-time encoding by simple averaging.  
- Empirically matches or outperforms strong baselines on IMDB sentiment and 100-class Wikipedia classification, is competitive on SICK, and improves word embeddings on analogy tasks.  
- The paper is clear, lightweight in theory, and emphasizes efficiency and simplicity.  
- Decision: Weak accept — simple idea with clear theoretical motivation and solid empirical + efficiency gains.  
- Novelty is incremental: averaging representations and corruption/denoising ideas are known; main contribution is their specific combination and induced regularizer.  
- Methodological soundness: probabilistic CBoW-like model, reasonable Taylor expansion and diagonal approximation, intuitive result that frequent words are penalized.  
- IMDB results: on par or better than doc2vec and stronger than word2vec+IDF/denoising autoencoders, with large test-time speedups; insightful analysis of small-norm words.  
- Wikipedia classification: consistent improvements across embedding sizes and qualitative t-SNE support for better clustering.  
- SICK: competitive with stronger sequence models given simplicity and speed; pretraining/fine-tuning setup is reasonable.  
- Word analogies: corruption-induced regularization appears to improve semantic quality of word embeddings beyond document tasks.  
- Efficiency/practicality: decoupled parameterization and averaging encoder outperform paragraph vectors/skip-thought on speed; corruption reduces per-step backprop compute.  
- Missing baselines: lacks comparisons to SIF (Arora et al.), Sent2Vec, and strong TF-IDF/nb-svm or bag-of-ngrams (e.g., FastText) baselines.  
- Negative sampling choice: uses uniform Pv instead of unigram^0.75; this should be ablated.  
- Data usage fairness: need clarity on pretraining with test data and consistent evaluation settings across methods (IMDB partly reported both).  
- Evaluation breadth limited: should include SentEval and more document benchmarks; t-SNE is qualitative, not rigorous.  
- Clarity/typos: inconsistent reference formatting; some equations need clearer assumptions (e.g., 1/T scaling); provide exact analogy improvement numbers and settings.  
- Recommended ablations: corruption rate sweep, negative sampling distribution, with/without local context, subsampling effects.  
- Recommended evaluations: SentEval tasks, confidence intervals across runs, IMDB without test-data pretraining, and wall-clock/token-throughput and parameter counts on shared hardware.  
- Suggested analyses: quantify relation between learned embedding norms and IDF and compare to word2vec+IDF weighting.  
- Requested clarifications: reasons for uniform negative sampling, sensitivity to q and rescaling, comparisons to SIF/Sent2Vec, contribution of regularizer vs subsampling, detailed analogy experiment settings, normalization for long documents, and OOV handling at test time.
