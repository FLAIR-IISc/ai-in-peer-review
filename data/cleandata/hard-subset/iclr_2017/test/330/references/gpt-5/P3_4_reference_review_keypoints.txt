- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings trained with CBOW-style objective using randomly masked global document context.
- Analytical claim: corruption (mask-out) induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent uninformative words, explaining why simple averaging works at test time.
- Practical benefits: model size independent of document count, trains as fast as Word2Vec, and test-time inference is an inexpensive average.
- Empirical results: competitive or superior performance on IMDB, Wikipedia classification, SICK, and strong improvements on word analogy tasks.
- Decision: Weak accept.
- Key positive reason: simple, well-motivated model with clear analytical justification and strong empirical/efficiency gains.
- Key positive reason: corruption-as-regularization insight is elegant and connects to dropout/marginalized denoising literature.
- Positioning: sits between Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors; novelty is integrating average representation into training with unbiased mask-out corruption.
- Technical soundness: uses standard negative sampling and a valid second-order Taylor expansion yielding intuitive frequency-weighted penalties.
- Empirical support: matches/exceeds Doc2Vec on IMDB, lowest error on Wikipedia task, competitive with LSTM/skip-thought on SICK, and improved analogy performance; t-SNEs support clustering claims.
- Concern: novelty is incremental—averaging and corruption ideas are known; contribution is mainly a clean integration and analysis.
- Concern: missing or underdeveloped baselines—PV-DM/PV-DBOW, stronger averaging baselines (e.g., SIF), and fastText/subword variants.
- Concern: many experimental/training details are omitted (window size, negatives, negative sampling distribution, optimizer, learning rate schedule, epochs, subsampling thresholds).
- Concern: sparse ablations—need quantitative isolation of corruption vs no corruption, global vs local+global, corruption rate q, and negative sampling distribution.
- Concern: evaluation protocol fairness—IMDB uses test data for representation learning in one setting; SICK fine-tuning differences vs skip-thought may bias comparisons.
- Concern: model is bag-of-words at document level and may struggle on tasks requiring long-range order or complex negation.
- Suggestion: provide full hyperparameters, training details, and reproduction scripts.
- Suggestion: add ablations for q, number of negatives, window size, and local/global contributions.
- Suggestion: include carefully tuned PV-DM and PV-DBOW results and stronger averaging baselines (SIF), plus discussion of fastText/subword.
- Suggestion: report results with/without subsampling of frequent words to validate reliance on that trick.
- Suggestion: clarify math (Hessian/penalty terms) and fix reference formatting.
- Suggestion: discuss limitations (order/long-range dependencies) and potential extensions (n-grams, subword, sentence-level pretraining).
- Question: what negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used; was unigram^0.75 tried and how did it affect results?
- Question: how sensitive are results to corruption rate q and window size—please provide ablations?
- Question: can you report well-tuned PV-DM and PV-DBOW and clarify PV inference protocol/time?
- Question: can pretraining/fine-tuning protocols be equalized between Doc2VecC and skip-thought on SICK/IMDB?
- Question: do results change if IDF or SIF weighting is applied at test time on top of Doc2VecC?
- Question: how do results vary with embedding dimension and training corpus size compared to Word2Vec?
- Question: have you evaluated robustness to domain shift to test suppression of corpus-specific frequent words?
