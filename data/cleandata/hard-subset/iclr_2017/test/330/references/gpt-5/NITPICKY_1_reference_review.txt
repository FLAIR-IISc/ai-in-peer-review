Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple document representation method that learns word embeddings by predicting a target word using both local context (as in CBOW) and a global document vector formed by averaging a stochastically corrupted subset of word embeddings from the document. The corruption is unbiased (mask with probability q, reweight survivors by 1/(1−q)) and is argued, via a second-order Taylor expansion, to induce a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of common, uninformative words. The model is efficient to train (no document-specific parameters) and test (document vector is an average). Experiments on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia classification, word analogy, and SICK semantic relatedness show competitive or superior performance and substantial speedups relative to Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought vectors.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The methodological novelty is incremental (averaging word embeddings with dropout-like corruption) and overlaps with prior work on global averaging, marginalized denoising, and dropout-as-regularization; (2) The paper has many technical, methodological, and formatting inconsistencies that undermine confidence (equation errors, unclear training details, fairness issues in baselines); (3) Empirical comparisons lack rigor and several setups are not apples-to-apples, making it difficult to trust the claimed gains.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning
  - Representing documents by averaging word embeddings is well known (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; numerous post-Word2Vec works). The main difference here is enforcing this composition during training with a simple dropout-like corruption. The “data-dependent regularization” argument closely echoes analyses of marginalized denoising autoencoders and dropout for logistic regression; the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contributions from these lines nor articulate what is fundamentally new beyond the combination.
- Theory and correctness
  - Equations contain inconsistencies/typos that complicate understanding and replication: 1/T Ux̃ sometimes appears as 1T Ux̃; the Taylor expansion line lacks a 1/2 factor but later uses 1/2 tr(ΣH); the diagonal Hessian term seems to miss squares or has incorrect signs; σw,c,x definitions appear after being used; symbols switch between bold and scalar. The derivation assumes “infinite sampling” of x̃ and uses negative sampling with Pv uniform, which deviates from common practice (unigram^0.75) without justification. Overall, the argument is plausible but presented sloppily, making it hard to verify.
- Methodological details and reproducibility
  - Critical training details are missing: window size k, number of negative samples, negative sampling distribution, optimizer, learning rate schedule, number of epochs, batching, handling of word counts vs binary BoW (x is defined binary, which ignores term frequency), and vocabulary subsampling thresholds per experiment. The code link is helpful, but the paper should be self-contained enough to reproduce main results.
- Fairness of baselines
  - IMDB: Two settings are used (including test data during representation learning and not). Using test data in representation learning introduces leakage; if reported, it should be clearly separated and de-emphasized. It is unclear whether all baselines receive identical preprocessing, subsampling, tokenization, and hyperparameter tuning.
  - Word analogy: The claim of ~20% uplift over Word2Vec is surprising. It’s unclear if both use identical corpora, same negatives, same sampling distributions, and same hyperparameters. Using Pv uniform for Doc2VecC but possibly not for Word2Vec would bias the comparison.
  - SICK: Doc2VecC is pretrained on a large book corpus and fine-tuned unsupervised on SICK; some baselines (LSTM variants) are trained from scratch on SICK, while skip-thought uses a huge pretrain. Comparisons are not uniform; some methods use 4800 dims, others 100 dims. The paper should standardize pretraining, dimensionality, and fine-tuning protocols.
  - Skip-thought timing comparisons conflate implementation/runtime environments (CPU vs GPU, reused pretrained models) with algorithmic complexity; these numbers are not informative for method quality.
- Ablations and analysis
  - Missing critical ablations: effect of corruption rate q (beyond a single chosen 0.9), no-corruption model, global-only vs local-only context, using counts vs binary x, negative sampling distribution choices, and document-length sensitivity. The regularization claim would be strengthened by controlled studies showing norms/weights vs frequency across methods with statistical tests.
- Clarity, writing, and formatting
  - Numerous typos and grammatical errors (“on the other hands,” “quantatively,” “is splitted,” “women” in analogy example), broken equations, inconsistent notation, and poorly formatted references (many entries incomplete or malformed). Several figures/tables are referenced with minimal detail and crucial numeric results are not actually shown in the text provided.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Sharpen the contribution relative to: (a) global-average context methods; (b) marginalized denoising autoencoders/dropout-as-regularization analyses; and (c) simple weighted averaging baselines (e.g., IDF, SIF/Arora et al., 2017, Sent2Vec/fastText-style sentence embeddings). Explicitly explain what Doc2VecC adds that is not already covered.
- Fix all equations and notation for consistency; include full derivation steps, correct Hessian terms, define all symbols where first used, and justify the negative sampling distribution and number of negatives.
- Provide a thorough ablation study: q ∈ {0, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9}; global-only vs local-only; binary vs count inputs; with/without subsampling; different negative distributions. Report statistical significance over multiple runs.
- Ensure fair and modern baselines: strong bag-of-ngrams with tuned regularization, SIF/Sent2Vec, fastText. For historical baselines (PV), match dimensions, pretraining, negatives, and tuning budgets.
- Standardize evaluation protocols: do not use test data for representation learning; match pretraining resources across methods when possible; control dimensions; use identical tokenization and preprocessing. Clarify timing metrics and hardware.
- Improve writing/editing and references. Ensure all bib entries are complete and consistent.

Questions for the authors
1. What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use for Doc2VecC and for all Word2Vec/PV baselines? Are they identical?
2. What window size, optimizer, batch size, learning rate schedule, and number of epochs were used per dataset? How were these tuned?
3. Is x strictly binary BoW during training? If so, why discard term frequency? What happens if you use counts or normalized TF?
4. Please provide ablations for the corruption rate q (including q=0) and for removing local context (global-only). How sensitive are results?
5. In the word analogy experiment, were the corpora and all hyperparameters exactly matched to Word2Vec? Can you share the exact numeric accuracies per subtask with confidence intervals?
6. For IMDB, when representation learning included the test set, how did you prevent leakage in hyperparameter tuning? Please report primary results using training+unlabeled only.
7. For SICK, can you align pretraining/fine-tuning protocols across methods (including Doc2VecC and skip-thought) and control for embedding dimensionality?
8. How many gradient steps were used for PV inference at test time and could its performance/time change with more steps? Can you report PV results with a fixed inference budget?
9. Can you release full preprocessing scripts and seeds to ensure reproducibility?
