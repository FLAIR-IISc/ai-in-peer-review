Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, trained so that this average can predict target words given local context. A key component is “corruption” of the document-level BoW during training (random mask-out with unbiased scaling), which both accelerates training and induces a data-dependent regularizer. A second-order Taylor expansion shows the corruption yields stronger penalties on frequent, less informative words while diminishing penalties when a word contributes confidently to predictions. Empirically, Doc2VecC is fast to train and test, and achieves strong performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100-class subset), semantic relatedness (SICK), and word analogy, often matching or outperforming baselines including BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, and in some cases Skip-Thought. Test-time inference is trivial: average the learned word embeddings.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Practical impact with strong results and excellent efficiency: a very simple architecture that consistently performs well and drastically simplifies test-time inference.
- Solid and clear motivation with a useful analytic connection between corruption and data-dependent regularization that explains empirical gains over naive averaging.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation. The paper targets the long-standing challenge of compact, semantically meaningful document embeddings that are both efficient and performant. While averaging word vectors is common, Doc2VecC’s training objective explicitly teaches the averaged embedding to carry global semantics, and the corruption scheme serves as both computational saver and principled regularizer. This is well motivated relative to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors: it retains the scalability of the former and avoids the corpus-size coupling of the latter.

2) Placement in literature. The method is positioned appropriately among CBoW/Skip-gram, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and sentence encoders (Skip-Thought/LSTM). The derivation echoes prior analyses of dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders (e.g., Wager et al.; Chen et al.) and articulates the difference: the regularizer acts on word embeddings in a way that emphasizes informative words. The related work could better discuss alternative simple baselines (e.g., supervised fastText) but overall the positioning is sound.

3) Support for claims.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion with unbiased corruption is standard and persuasive as an approximation, and the diagonal Hessian simplification is reasonable under independent corruption. While not fully rigorous for negative sampling, the analysis offers a compelling mechanism: frequent/common words are attenuated while informative words are preserved.
- Empirics: The model shows consistent gains or parity on several benchmarks with substantial speed advantages. The IMDB gains over BoW/DEA/Word2Vec+IDF are credible; Doc2VecC beats Paragraph Vectors in accuracy and is dramatically more efficient at test time. On Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC outperforms all reported baselines across embedding sizes. On SICK, results are competitive with modern LSTM/Skip-Thought despite the much simpler model and order-of-magnitude faster training. Word analogy improvements over Word2Vec suggest the corruption helps learn cleaner lexical relations.

Main weaknesses/concerns (do not outweigh strengths)
- Novelty is moderate: the core representation (average of word embeddings) and CBoW-like prediction are known; the main novelty is the training-time corruption of document BoW with the accompanying analysis. Still, this is a meaningful and practical contribution.
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: Some choices could bias results. For example, Paragraph Vectors have multiple variants (PV-DM and PV-DBOW); it is unclear if the strongest configuration was used. Skip-Thought is applied far out of its typical regime (very long documents) and incurs heavy computational overhead; this limits comparability. Strong supervised baselines like fastText (linear classifier over averaged embeddings) are not included.
- Missing details: Some training hyperparameters are under-specified (negative sampling distribution/count, window size, subsampling thresholds, learning rates). The choice of uniform negative sampling differs from standard unigram^0.75 and may affect both performance and interpretation.
- Use of test data during representation learning in one IMDB setting complicates fairness, though the authors also report a setting without test data.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Provide a thorough ablation:
  - Vary corruption rate q and show its effect on accuracy, speed, and the induced regularization (e.g., embedding norms of stopwords).
  - Remove local context or global context to validate the claim that global averaging learns complementary information.
  - Compare negative sampling distributions (uniform vs unigram^0.75) and number of negatives.
- Baselines:
  - Include PV-DBOW and tuned PV-DM; report the best among them.
  - Add a strong simple supervised baseline (e.g., fastText) and/or NBSVM (Wang & Manning) for IMDB.
  - For SICK, include InferSent/SIF-style averaging if feasible; at least discuss the trade-offs.
- Reporting:
  - Clarify whether subsampling of frequent words (a Word2Vec trick) is applied in Doc2VecC and its impact beyond the corruption.
  - Provide hyperparameters: k (context window), embedding size(s), optimizer, epochs, learning rate schedule, vocabulary thresholds, negative samples per update.
  - Add standard deviations over multiple runs for key results.
  - Use consistent protocols across models with or without using test data during unsupervised pretraining.
- Writing and presentation:
  - Clean up references and typos; ensure all citations are complete and standardized.
  - Make the derivation assumptions explicit (e.g., independence, second-order truncation, negative sampling approximation).

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- What context window size k was used, and how does performance vary with k?
- How does performance vary with the corruption rate q, and does the empirical behavior match the regularization intuition (e.g., norms of frequent vs rare words)?
- Which Paragraph Vector variants and inference procedures were used? Did you tune both PV-DM and PV-DBOW to their best settings?
- How much of the gain over Word2Vec+IDF remains if you remove frequency subsampling in Doc2VecC?
- For SICK, after pretraining on the book corpus, how much improvement comes from fine-tuning on SICK without labels? Could you report results without fine-tuning?
- Can you share training time vs accuracy trade-offs across methods under a fixed compute budget?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, simple, and effective contribution with strong practical appeal. The paper would benefit from a few additional ablations and clearer baseline comparisons, but I recommend acceptance.
