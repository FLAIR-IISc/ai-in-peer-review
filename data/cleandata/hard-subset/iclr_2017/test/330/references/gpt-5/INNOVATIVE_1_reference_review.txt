Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. The key idea is to (i) enforce during training that a document can be represented as the average of its word embeddings, and (ii) apply corruption (dropout) to the document words while predicting target words from local context + global (document) context. A second-order analysis shows that this unbiased corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, uninformative words while preserving informative ones. The method decouples model size from corpus size (only depends on vocabulary), yields very fast training and especially test-time encoding (simple averaging), and demonstrates strong empirical results on sentiment (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), word analogy, and semantic relatedness (SICK). Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Conceptual novelty and elegance: training-time corruption to enforce average-of-embeddings document representations, with a clear theoretical link to an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer that suppresses stopwords/common words.
- Strong practicality: decoupled parameterization, fast training/inference, and competitive or superior performance across diverse tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-situated with respect to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vector (PV), denoising autoencoders, dropout/marginalized denoising, and skip-thought. The paper articulates two PV limitations (parameter growth with corpus size, expensive test-time inference) and directly addresses both.
- Novelty: While averaging word embeddings is a known heuristic, Doc2VecC is distinctive in (a) making averaging the central training constraint via a global context, and (b) using corruption to yield an interpretable, data-dependent regularizer that favors informative words. This is a compelling and bold simplification.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion argument under unbiased corruption is sound and mirrors known analyses of dropout/mDAE. It provides a principled lens on why the model suppresses frequent but uninformative words, aligning with empirical observations.
- Empirical support: 
  - IMDB: Doc2VecC matches or beats PV and other baselines, with large test-time efficiency gains (orders of magnitude faster than PV inference). The analysis on subsampling and the norm-shrinkage of common words aligns with the regularizer’s intent.
  - Wikipedia classification: Consistently superior errors across representation sizes; t-SNE visualizations corroborate class separation.
  - Word analogy: Substantial gains over Word2Vec embeddings trained on the same data; the hypothesis about PV’s capacity focusing on document vectors instead of word semantics is plausible.
  - SICK: Slightly below LSTM/skip-thought but with far greater efficiency and still competitive, supporting the claim that the method is particularly suited to longer text.
- Reproducibility: Code and details provided; overall setup is transparent.

Constructive feedback (not required for acceptance)
- Clarity of representation: There is a potential mismatch between the use of x as BoW with x_j ∈ {0,1} in the global context term (scaled by 1/T) and the test-time representation d = (1/T)∑ over tokens. Please clarify whether x during training is binary or count-based; consider aligning training (global context) with test-time averaging over tokens.
- Ablations:
  - Corruption rate q: Provide a sensitivity plot (q=0, 0.5, 0.9) on at least one task to isolate the benefit of the corruption-induced regularization.
  - Subsampling: Since Doc2VecC purportedly diminishes reliance on subsampling, include results with/without subsampling to quantify the effect.
  - Local vs global context: An ablation removing Uc (local) or the global term would clarify their contributions.
- Negative sampling: The paper uses a uniform negative sampling distribution; Word2Vec commonly uses unigram^0.75. A comparison could strengthen results and fairness.
- Baselines: Consider adding stronger classic baselines (e.g., tf-idf with n-grams; features from Wang & Manning 2012), and simple SIF/IDF-weighted averaging baselines to contextualize gains.
- Significance and variance: Report variance over multiple runs and significance tests for key comparisons.
- Analysis: The word-norm analysis is insightful; complement it with frequency-vs-norm plots and downstream performance deltas when removing top-k common words to further tie the regularizer to performance.

Questions for the authors
1. During training, is x truly binary BoW or term-frequency? If binary, why divide by T rather than the number of unique tokens, and how does that align with test-time token averaging? Please clarify and, if needed, reconcile training/testing normalization.
2. How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q? Is there a principled way to set q or adapt it during training?
3. How much of the empirical gain comes from the corruption-induced regularization vs the inclusion of global context per se? Can you share ablations removing one or the other?
4. Why use uniform negative sampling rather than unigram^0.75? Does changing the distribution alter the qualitative regularizer behavior or final performance?
5. Do results persist without subsampling of frequent words? Can Doc2VecC fully replace that heuristic in practice?
6. For SICK, did you try sentence-level pooling strategies (e.g., averaging per-sentence then composing for documents) to mitigate length effects?
7. Can the approach extend to subword information (e.g., character n-grams) to better handle OOVs, and would the corruption analysis still hold?

Overall, this is a clean, bold idea with strong empirical evidence and a compelling theoretical explanation. I recommend acceptance.
