Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient document representation method that learns to predict target words using both local (CBOW window) and global (document) context, where the document vector is enforced to be the average of word embeddings. The global context is formed by an unbiased dropout corruption of the document’s bag-of-words, which (i) speeds up training and (ii) acts as a data-dependent regularizer. A second-order Taylor expansion of the negative sampling objective shows that the corruption induces a frequency- and confidence-modulated penalty that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving rare but predictive ones. The model decouples model size from the number of documents, produces test-time vectors by simple averaging, and achieves strong empirical results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia subset), word analogy, and competitive results on SICK semantic relatedness, with substantial efficiency gains. The method is conceptually appealing: it explicitly trains word embeddings so that their average encodes document semantics.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is simple, well-motivated, and yields consistent efficiency and accuracy gains over strong unsupervised baselines, with a clear practical benefit (test-time averaging). (2) The corruption-as-regularization analysis, while approximate, offers a compelling explanation for observed behavior (down-weighting stopwords) and differentiates the method from naïve averaging.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well situated relative to Word2Vec (CBOW), Paragraph Vectors (PV-DM/DBOW), SDAE, and skip-thought. The idea of enforcing that an average of embeddings must be predictive during training addresses a longstanding gap between post-hoc averaging and models that learn explicit document vectors. The corruption mechanism connects cleanly to dropout/marginalized DA, with a novel application as a document-level regularizer.
- Methodology: The architecture is straightforward: predict wt from Uc + (1/T)Ux̃, with x̃ formed by unbiased dropout on x. The analysis via a second-order expansion around the corruption mean shows an additional regularization term that scales with word frequency and σ(⋅)(1−σ(⋅)), supporting the claim that frequent but uninformative words get damped. This aligns with observed embedding norms and with known effects of dropout for logistic regression.
- Empirical support: Across IMDB (both with and without using the test set for unsupervised representation learning) and Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC consistently outperforms BoW, Word2Vec+AVG/IDF, PV, and SDAE, with large test-time efficiency gains (simple averaging vs PV inference). On SICK, Doc2VecC is competitive with stronger supervised sequence encoders and much faster than skip-thought. The word analogy results suggest that the embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are high quality, especially in suppressing stopwords, which plausibly helps algebraic composition.
- Practical impact: The decoupling of parameters from corpus size and the ability to embed unseen documents by fast averaging are clear advantages over PV and sequence encoders for large-scale retrieval/classification.

Concerns and limitations
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: (i) Skip-thought is used off-the-shelf on long documents, which underplays its strengths; the paper acknowledges this but the comparison remains somewhat mismatched. (ii) PV settings (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW, inference details) can materially affect performance; it is unclear whether strong PV-DBOW settings or concatenation variants were tried. (iii) For negative sampling, the noise distribution is stated as uniform; Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75. It is not clear whether baselines used identical settings, which could bias comparisons.
- Objective and derivation details: The Taylor expansion relies on diagonal Hessian terms and an unbiased corruption; with negative sampling and a non-linear objective, the approximation is heuristic. The claim that Doc2VecC “reduces to DAE if the local context is removed” is not precise: DAE reconstructs input whereas Doc2VecC still predicts targets; the connection is conceptual rather than formal.
- Representation choice: x is defined as binary BoW, discarding term frequency. This may be limiting, especially for long documents where frequency can be informative; an ablation with counts vs binary would help.
- Ablations: While q=0.9 is reported and insensitivity claimed, there is no ablation over q, document length, or the presence/absence of word subsampling. Given the centrality of the regularization claim, ablations would strengthen it (e.g., performance without subsampling; performance vs q; removing local context to isolate global averaging).
- Dataset and experimental nuances: (i) The Wikipedia classification setup is bespoke; more standard corpora (e.g., 20NG, Reuters, AG News) would increase comparability. (ii) t-SNE plots are illustrative but not rigorous evidence. (iii) On SICK, fine-tuning on the target data without using labels is described but could be better specified.

Suggestions for improvement (to help, not fundamental to the decision)
- Provide ablations: (a) vary corruption rate q and show training speed vs accuracy; (b) remove subsampling of frequent words to quantify Doc2VecC’s robustness relative to Word2Vec; (c) binary vs count BoW for x and ct; (d) remove ct to isolate the effect of global averaging; (e) use different negative sampling distributions.
- Strengthen baseline comparisons: (a) include PV-DBOW with tuned hyperparameters; (b) include a fastText-style baseline (CBOW with averaging at test) if within scope; (c) report Word2Vec embeddings trained under the same negative sampling and subsampling settings.
- Clarify training details: window size k, number of negatives, learning rate schedule, noise distribution, subsampling threshold, and handling of OOV words across datasets.
- Expand datasets: add standard document classification benchmarks and long-form datasets to substantiate claims about scalability to long documents.
- Tighten the theory section: clearly state assumptions behind the Taylor approximation, and delimit its scope; qualify the DAE comparison.
- Consider integrating TF-IDF-like weighting into the model (e.g., learnable per-word scalars) and/or document-length normalization variants, and report whether training indeed learns to approximate IDF.

Questions for the authors
1) Negative sampling: What noise distribution did you use for Doc2VecC and for Word2Vec/Paragraph Vector baselines? Were they matched (e.g., unigram^0.75)? How sensitive are results to this choice?
2) Corruption rate: How does performance vary with q, and what is the speed–accuracy trade-off? Does the implicit regularization persist for lower q?
3) Subsampling: You state Doc2VecC does not rely on subsampling. Can you provide a full ablation table with/without subsampling for both Doc2VecC and Word2Vec baselines?
4) Binary vs counts: Why use binary BoW for x and ct? Does using counts (or log-counts) help or hurt?
5) PV details: Which PV variants were used (DM/DBOW/concatenation), and how were inference hyperparameters tuned? Can you report PV with best-known settings?
6) Word analogy: Can you detail training settings for all methods on the WMT corpus, and explain why Doc2VecC improves substantially over Word2Vec there?
7) Generalization: How does Doc2VecC perform on standard benchmarks like 20NG/Reuters/AG News and on truly long documents (e.g., arXiv abstracts or full articles)?
8) SICK fine-tuning: Please clarify the “fine-tune on SICK without labels” procedure and its effect size. Did you compare to applying the same to skip-thought?
