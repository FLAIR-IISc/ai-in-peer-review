Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient document representation method that learns word embeddings such that the (uncentered) average of a document’s word vectors serves as a good document representation. The model augments a CBOW-style local context with a global document context formed by averaging a randomly corrupted subset of words (unbiased dropout/mask-out). The authors provide a second-order Taylor expansion analysis showing the corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses common, uninformative words while preserving informative ones. Empirically, Doc2VecC is fast to train and test, and matches or improves on several baselines (BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought) on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, word analogy, and SICK semantic relatedness. Code is provided.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The idea is simple, well-motivated, and practically useful: training to make the document average work, coupled with corruption as adaptive regularization, yields strong representations with excellent efficiency.
- The paper provides a clear connection between corruption and data-dependent regularization (building on prior analyses of dropout/mDA), which helps explain the empirical behavior.
- Results are competitive-to-strong across multiple tasks with favorable training/inference cost, and the method scales cleanly.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The method is positioned between Word2Vec/CBOW (which does not explicitly enforce document compositionality) and Paragraph Vectors (which learns per-document parameters and is costly at test time). The explicit incorporation of a corrupted global context is close in spirit to Huang et al. (2012), DAE/mDA, and dropout-as-regularization (Wager et al.), but the integration into a CBOW training objective with negative sampling to specifically make the average embedding work at test time is a neat and practical twist. The discussion of why this reduces test-time cost versus PV is convincing.
- Theory: The second-order expansion around the corruption mean, leading to an additive penalty R(uj) scaled by q/(1−q), is consistent with prior analyses of dropout and marginalized denoising. The qualitative conclusions (rare/informative words less penalized; frequent/stopwords more penalized unless predictive) are reasonable, and the qualitative evidence (small-norm words) aligns with the claims.
- Empirics: The paper evaluates on three common tasks plus word analogy, reports both scenarios with/without using the test set for unsupervised representation learning, and provides timing comparisons. The method’s efficiency advantage and its practical benefit for long documents (IMDB, Wikipedia) are clear. Results on SICK are competitive though not SOTA versus LSTM/Skip-Thought, which aligns with the intuition that short sequences favor sequence models.
- Reproducibility: Code availability, use of standard datasets, and reasonably detailed setups are positives.

Concerns and points to address
- Notation and potential inconsistency: x is defined as binary BoW (xj=1 iff word j appears). However, the document vector in the model uses 1/T U x̃ (and later d = (1/T)∑w∈D uw), which implies averaging over tokens (counts), not unique types. If x is binary, 1/T Ux is not the token average. Please clarify whether x is counts (tf) or binary and ensure the equations and implementation match. This matters for both the unbiased corruption and the theoretical expansion.
- Negative sampling details: The derivation assumes uniform negative sampling Pv, but standard Word2Vec uses unigram^0.75, which significantly affects embedding quality. What do you use in practice across experiments? Please report number of negatives, distribution, and sensitivity.
- Baselines and fairness: 
  - For IMDB, strong classic baselines (e.g., NB-SVM with unigrams/bigrams) are missing; fastText (bag-of-tricks) is also a highly relevant and efficient baseline using averaged embeddings. Including these would strengthen claims of SOTA or near-SOTA simplicity.
  - The Skip-Thought baseline is disadvantaged by long inputs; this is discussed, but including a more length-robust sentence/document baseline (e.g., averaging pretrained GloVe with IDF) is useful (you do include Word2Vec+IDF; please ensure consistent pretraining corpora).
  - For word analogy, comparing Doc2VecC word embeddings to those from PV is arguably not ideal, since PV’s goal is document representations and its word vectors are known to be weaker; a stronger comparison is to Word2Vec/CBOW or SGNS under matched training setups. Please ensure identical corpora, window sizes, negatives, and dimensionalities; provide absolute scores per category (you allude to ~20% uplift but not the exact numbers).
- Ablations: It would help to isolate the contributions of (a) global context vs local context, (b) corruption vs no corruption, and (c) averaging scheme (uniform vs IDF/learned weights). An ablation showing performance drop without corruption would solidify the regularization story. A “Doc2VecC without local context” comparison to a DAE with identical setup would also be informative.
- Hyperparameter/sensitivity: You mention q=0.9 and claim insensitivity. Please provide a sensitivity plot for q and for embedding dimension h. Also report training epochs, learning rate schedule, window size, subsampling threshold, and optimizer.
- Use of test data for unsupervised pretraining: You report both settings, which is good; ensure the main claims emphasize the train+unlabeled setting, as using test documents for representation learning is not always acceptable depending on evaluation protocol.
- Clarity/typos: Several formatting/reference issues and minor formula typos (e.g., 1/T vs 1T, x vs μx) should be fixed. The title appears truncated in the draft.

Questions for the authors
1) Clarify the exact definition of x (binary vs counts) and how T relates to x. If x is binary, why divide by T rather than ∑j xj? What is used in code?
2) What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives are used in each experiment? Did you try unigram^0.75? How sensitive are results?
3) Can you provide ablations: (i) no corruption, (ii) no local context (global-only), (iii) local-only (CBOW), and (iv) average with IDF weights at test time?
4) How does Doc2VecC compare to fastText (Joulin et al.) on IMDB and the Wikipedia classification under matched conditions?
5) For the word analogy evaluation, please report full per-category scores for Word2Vec vs Doc2VecC with identical hyperparameters and training corpora. Also include comparisons at multiple dimensions and training sizes as claimed.
6) How sensitive is performance to q and to subsampling of frequent words? If subsampling is removed, how much does Doc2VecC degrade?
7) For SICK, what is the effect size of the unsupervised fine-tuning on the target dataset? Please report with/without fine-tuning and the vocabulary expansion details.

Overall, this is a well-motivated and useful contribution: conceptually simple, computationally attractive, and with solid empirical results. Addressing the clarity, ablations, and baseline breadth would make it stronger.
