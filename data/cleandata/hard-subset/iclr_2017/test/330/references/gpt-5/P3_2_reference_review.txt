Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method to learn document representations by enforcing that the mean of word embeddings encodes global document semantics. The key idea is to train a CBoW-style predictor with both local context (surrounding words) and a global context constructed as an average of word embeddings from a corrupted version of the document’s bag-of-words. The corruption is unbiased dropout on the document BoW, which reduces compute and, via a second-order Taylor expansion of the expected objective, induces a data-dependent regularizer that down-weights common, uninformative words and preserves informative/rare words. The model keeps parameter count independent of the number of documents (depends only on vocabulary), allowing fast training and very fast test-time encoding via averaging. Empirically, the method matches or outperforms strong baselines (BoW, word2vec+IDF, denoising autoencoders, paragraph vectors) on IMDB sentiment and a 100-class Wikipedia classification task; performs competitively on SICK semantic relatedness; and yields word embeddings that improve over word2vec on analogy benchmarks. The paper is clear, provides a lightweight theoretical rationale, and emphasizes efficiency and simplicity.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons: (1) A compellingly simple approach with a clear theoretical motivation linking corruption to an IDF-like, data-dependent regularizer; (2) solid empirical gains over widely used baselines with strong efficiency advantages and practical utility.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper is well motivated: averaging word embeddings is strong but brittle; enforcing this during training is appealing. The connection to prior work is mostly adequate: word2vec, paragraph vectors, denoising autoencoders, and the global-context idea of Huang et al. (2012) are discussed, and the novelty is the corruption-based training that induces the regularizer while keeping parameter size independent of corpus size.

- Methodological soundness: The probabilistic model mirrors CBoW with an added document-level vector constructed from the corrupted BoW, with negative sampling for scalability. The Taylor expansion around the mean of the corruption is standard and yields a clear regularization term; the diagonal approximation and independence across words are reasonable simplifications. The result that frequent, non-informative words get penalized is intuitive and consistent with dropout analyses.

- Empirical evidence: 
  - IMDB: Doc2VecC is on par with or better than doc2vec and stronger than word2vec+IDF and denoising autoencoders, with large speedups at test time. The analysis showing which words get small-norm embeddings is insightful.
  - Wikipedia classification: Consistent improvements across embedding sizes with simple linear SVMs, and t-SNE visualizations qualitatively support better clustering.
  - SICK: Performance is competitive with stronger sequence models given the extreme simplicity and speed, and the pretraining/fine-tuning setup is reasonable.
  - Word analogies: Gains over word2vec suggest that the corruption-induced regularization can improve the semantic quality of word embeddings, not just document vectors.

- Efficiency and practicality: The parameterization decoupled from number of documents and the fast test-time averaging are strong practical advantages over paragraph vectors and sentence encoders like skip-thought. The corruption also reduces backprop compute per step.

Caveats and weaknesses (not blocking but important)
- Novelty is incremental: representing documents as averages is well-known; Huang et al. (2012) already used global context; dropout/denoising-based regularization is also well studied. The main novelty is combining these with a specific corruption on document-level BoW and showing the induced regularizer. This is a neat synthesis but not a large conceptual leap.

- Baselines and fairness:
  - Missing important averaging baselines such as SIF/weighted averaging with principal component removal (Arora et al., 2017), and more recent unsupervised sentence/document methods (e.g., Sent2Vec). A TF-IDF+SVM baseline with tuned n-grams (e.g., NB-SVM or strong bag-of-ngrams) is also a relevant point of comparison for IMDB and Wikipedia.
  - Negative sampling uses uniform Pv, whereas word2vec typically uses unigram^0.75; this choice may hurt or help and should be ablated.
  - Some settings allow using test data for unsupervised representation learning; while common in unsupervised setups, care is needed to ensure comparisons are apples-to-apples. You do report both settings for IMDB, which is good.

- Evaluation breadth: The classification datasets are limited; including standard SentEval tasks or modern document classification benchmarks would strengthen claims. The t-SNE plots are illustrative but not rigorous.

- Clarity/typos: References are inconsistently formatted; some equations could use more explicit assumptions (e.g., scaling by 1/T and its effect on long documents). Provide precise numbers for the analogy improvements and detailed settings.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Add baselines: SIF (smooth inverse frequency averaging + PC removal), Sent2Vec, and a strong TF-IDF/nb-svm or bag-of-ngrams baseline. Consider FastText (bag-of-ngrams) as a supervised baseline in classification.

- Ablations:
  - Corruption rate q sweep and its effect on accuracy, norm distributions, and speed.
  - Negative sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75).
  - With/without local context term Uc to isolate the contribution of global context (you mention the DEA connection; quantify it).
  - Effect of subsampling frequent words vs relying solely on the induced regularizer.

- Evaluation: Expand to SentEval tasks; report confidence intervals across runs. For IMDB, include comparisons without using test data in pretraining across all baselines.

- Efficiency: Report token-throughput (tokens/sec) and parameter counts, and provide wall-clock comparisons on a shared hardware setup (CPU and GPU where applicable).

- Analysis: Quantify the relationship between the learned word embedding norms and IDF, and show how this differs from simple word2vec+IDF weighting at test time.

- Clarity: Fix reference formatting; clarify whether V is trained or tied to U; detail optimizer, learning rate schedule, and hyperparameters.

Questions for the authors
- Why use uniform negative sampling (Pv) instead of unigram^0.75? How do results change with the standard choice?

- How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q and to the unbiased scaling 1/(1−q)? Would standard dropout without rescaling work as well?

- Could you compare to SIF/PC removal and Sent2Vec on the same tasks? How does Doc2VecC perform when combined with SIF weighting at test time?

- How much of the gain comes from the global corruption-induced regularizer vs subsampling frequent words? Do the methods remain strong if subsampling is disabled?

- For the word analogy experiments, please provide exact scores per category and training details (negative sampling k, window sizes, subsampling thresholds, epochs) to ensure comparability to word2vec.

- In long documents, dividing by T shrinks gradients. Did you explore alternative normalizations (e.g., sqrt(T))?

- How does Doc2VecC handle OOV words at test time without fine-tuning? Any strategy beyond zero/UNK?

Overall, the paper presents a simple and useful idea with a clear theoretical underpinning and strong practical value. With stronger baselines and a few ablations, it would be a clear accept.
