Summary
This paper proposes Doc2VecC, an unsupervised document representation method that (i) enforces document vectors to be simple averages of word embeddings during training, and (ii) introduces a document-level “corruption” (masking/dropout) mechanism when predicting a target word from local and global context. The authors argue this yields an efficient architecture whose parameter count is decoupled from corpus size, and show—via a Taylor expansion analysis—that the corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses common, non-informative words and favors rare/informative ones. Empirically, Doc2VecC reportedly matches or improves upon baselines on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia subset), word analogies, and semantic relatedness (SICK), while being computationally efficient.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
1) The methodological novelty appears incremental relative to established ideas (CBoW averaging, denoising/dropout/marginalized DAEs) and the theory largely reuses known dropout/mDAE analyses without offering a substantively new insight.
2) The empirical evaluation raises concerns about rigor and fairness (e.g., representation learning on test data, incomplete hyperparameter reporting, potentially suboptimal baselines, unclear negative sampling choices), making it difficult to credit the claimed superiority.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: Representing documents by averaging word embeddings is well-established; many works already post-process word2vec embeddings with (weighted) averaging. Introducing dropout/corruption at document-level is conceptually close to denoising autoencoders, word dropout, and prior analyses of dropout as adaptive regularization (e.g., Wager et al., mDAE). The paper’s theoretical section closely mirrors these frameworks, applying a second-order expansion to a negative-sampling objective. While the specific combination (CBoW with global context via averaged embeddings plus corruption) is a reasonable engineering choice, the conceptual advance over existing literature is modest.

- Theoretical clarity/correctness: The Taylor expansion derivation is informal and omits several details (e.g., exact objective under negative sampling, distribution Pv selection, interchange of expectation and Taylor expansion, conditions under which the approximation holds). The resulting regularizer resembles prior dropout/mDAE penalties; however, the presentation contains notational inconsistencies and typos that make it hard to verify (e.g., factors of 1/T, definitions of σ terms, use of uniform Pv whereas standard word2vec uses unigram^3/4). A more careful and complete derivation is needed to substantiate the specific data dependence claimed.

- Experimental rigor:
  - Transductive setting: For IMDB, one setting uses the test set during representation learning. This is not a standard inductive protocol and risks inflating performance, especially for methods that benefit from corpus statistics. The paper notes both settings but does not clearly isolate conclusions to the inductive one.
  - Baseline configuration: Paragraph Vectors is sensitive to hyperparameters (PV-DBOW vs DV-DM, negative sampling vs hierarchical softmax, inference steps, window size, subsampling). Details are sparse; it is unclear whether strong PV configurations were used. Similarly, skip-thought is used off-the-shelf for long documents (known mismatch), and the explanation for its poor performance is speculative. For word2vec, Pv is taken uniform and frequent-word subsampling is applied, but the negative sampling distribution typically uses unigram^3/4; deviations should be justified and controlled across methods.
  - Missing modern baselines: Strong, simple baselines like SIF-weighted averaging, fastText (unsupervised averaging or supervised classifier), and Sent2Vec are absent. Given the paper’s focus on averaging, comparisons to these are critical.
  - Ablations: There is no thorough ablation isolating the contribution of corruption, local vs global context, corruption rate q, negative sampling details, or subsampling of frequent words. The reported l2-norm analysis is suggestive but not a substitute for task performance ablations.
  - Reporting: Several referenced tables/figures are not included here, and many implementation details are missing (number of negatives, window size, optimizer, learning rate schedule, epochs, initialization, vocabulary cutoff handling, tokenization). Time comparisons mix CPU vs GPU settings across methods and include encoding times for very different model sizes, complicating fairness.

- Claims: The paper claims to “match or beat the state-of-the-art” broadly, but on SICK it underperforms LSTM/skip-thought, and on IMDB the state-of-the-art includes supervised CNN/LSTM models not evaluated here. The word analogy “~20% uplift” over word2vec is surprising and requires stronger controls (same corpus, preprocessing, negative sampling distribution, dimension, training budget) and statistics.

Constructive feedback (to help improve the work)
- Provide a rigorous, fully specified training objective and a careful derivation of the regularizer, including assumptions and a discussion of when the approximation is accurate. Align negative sampling choices (e.g., unigram^3/4) with standards and analyze sensitivity.
- Use strictly inductive protocols (no test data in representation learning) for main results. If you include transductive settings, clearly separate them.
- Strengthen baselines: tuned PV-DBOW and DV-DM, SIF-weighted averaging, fastText (both unsupervised averaging and supervised classifier for reference), Sent2Vec. Ensure comparable training budgets, pre-processing, and negative sampling across methods.
- Add thorough ablations: effect of corruption (q sweep including q=0), global vs local context contributions, subsampling on/off, negative count, window size, and dimension. Report confidence intervals over multiple runs.
- Expand datasets to established benchmarks (e.g., 20 Newsgroups, AG News, DBPedia, Yelp) and include standardized splits. For SICK, clarify whether fine-tuning uses labels and detail the vocabulary expansion protocol.
- Improve clarity and presentation: fix notation, equations, and references; clearly define all symbols and matrices; ensure figures/tables are self-contained.

Questions for the authors
1) What negative sampling distribution, number of negatives, window size, optimizer, learning rate schedule, and training epochs were used? Were these matched across word2vec, PV, and Doc2VecC?
2) For PV, which variants (PV-DBOW/DV-DM) and inference procedures were used? How were hyperparameters tuned?
3) Can you report inductive-only results on IMDB (no test data during representation learning) and provide ablations over q (including q=0) to isolate the benefit of corruption?
4) In the analogy experiments, were corpora, preprocessing, sampling distributions, and training budgets identical between word2vec and Doc2VecC? Please provide exact numbers and significance tests.
5) For SICK, did fine-tuning use any supervision from the relatedness scores? How are OOV words handled, and how large is the performance change due to fine-tuning?
6) How sensitive is Doc2VecC to document length and to the balance between local and global context terms? Would a learned weighting outperform the fixed 1/T scaling?

Overall, the idea is simple and potentially useful in practice, but the current paper does not provide sufficient novelty or experimental rigor to support acceptance.
