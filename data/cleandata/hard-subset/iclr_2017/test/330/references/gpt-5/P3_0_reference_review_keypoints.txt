- Proposal: Doc2VecC trains word embeddings so the (uncentered) average of word vectors is a good document representation, using a CBOW-style objective augmented with a corrupted global context.
- Theoretical contribution: A second-order Taylor expansion shows corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights common/uninformative words and preserves informative ones.
- Empirical contribution: Doc2VecC is fast to train/test and matches or improves baselines (BoW, DAE/mDA, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought) on IMDB, Wikipedia classification, word analogy, and SICK; code released.
- Decision: Weak accept.
- Key reason 1: Simple, well-motivated, and practically useful—training for average-compositionality plus corruption yields strong, efficient representations.
- Key reason 2: Clear connection shown between corruption and data-dependent regularization, aligning with prior dropout/mDA analyses.
- Key reason 3: Competitive empirical results across tasks with favorable scaling and inference cost.
- Motivation/placement: Positioned between Word2Vec/CBOW and Paragraph Vectors; corrupted global context is a practical integration of prior ideas to enforce averaged-document usefulness.
- Theory support: Expansion yields penalty R(uj) scaled by q/(1−q); conclusions about penalizing frequent words align with observed small-norm words.
- Empirics details: Evaluated on multiple tasks with timing comparisons; shows advantage on long documents and competitive results on short-sequence tasks like SICK.
- Reproducibility: Code, standard datasets, and reasonably detailed experimental setups provided.
- Concern — notation inconsistency: Clarify whether x is binary BoW or token counts and ensure equations/implementation (1/T U x̃ vs averaging over tokens) are consistent.
- Concern — negative sampling: Specify negative sampling distribution (unigram^0.75 vs uniform), number of negatives, and sensitivity analyses.
- Concern — baselines/fairness: Include strong classic baselines (e.g., NB-SVM, fastText) and ensure matched training setups (corpus, window, negatives) when comparing word embeddings and document methods.
- Concern — ablations needed: Isolate effects of (a) global vs local context, (b) corruption vs no corruption, and (c) averaging scheme (uniform vs IDF/learned).
- Concern — hyperparameter sensitivity: Provide sensitivity plots/reporting for q, embedding dimension, training epochs, learning rate schedule, window size, subsampling threshold, and optimizer.
- Concern — use of test data: Emphasize train+unlabeled setting and clearly separate results that used test documents for unsupervised pretraining.
- Concern — clarity/typos: Fix formula/notation typos (1/T vs 1T, x vs μx), formatting issues, and truncated title.
- Question 1: Clarify exact definition of x (binary vs counts) and how T relates to x; what does the code use?
- Question 2: What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used per experiment; were unigram^0.75 and sensitivity tested?
- Question 3: Provide ablations for (i) no corruption, (ii) no local context (global-only), (iii) local-only (CBOW), and (iv) averaging with IDF at test time.
- Question 4: How does Doc2VecC compare to fastText on IMDB and Wikipedia under matched conditions?
- Question 5: For word analogy, report per-category scores comparing Doc2VecC vs Word2Vec with identical hyperparameters, dimensions, and corpora.
- Question 6: How sensitive is performance to q and to subsampling of frequent words; what happens if subsampling is removed?
- Question 7: For SICK, report effect size of unsupervised fine-tuning on the target dataset and provide with/without fine-tuning results and vocabulary expansion details.
