Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, trained with a CBOW-style objective that predicts target words from local context and a global document vector. The document vector is computed as an average over word embeddings after applying a stochastic “corruption” (masking) process, which the authors analyze via a Taylor expansion to argue it induces a data-dependent regularization that downweights frequent, uninformative words. The method is corpus-size–agnostic (parameters tied to vocabulary size), gives fast test-time inference (just averaging), and shows competitive to superior performance on IMDB sentiment, a Wikipedia document classification task, SICK semantic relatedness, and word analogy, with substantial efficiency gains.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty relative to established methods (averaging word embeddings, global context in CBOW, and dropout-style regularization), with the main contribution being a straightforward combination rather than a substantively new idea.
- Insufficiently rigorous and fair empirical validation and ablation: key baselines and controls are missing or under-tuned; several claims (e.g., reduced dependence on subsampling) are not convincingly supported.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: Representing documents as averaged word embeddings is well-established; using global context in prediction is not new (e.g., Huang et al., 2012); and the analysis of input corruption/dropout as data-dependent regularization closely mirrors prior work (e.g., Wager et al., 2013; marginalized DAEs). The distinctive element—training the average explicitly with corruption while predicting a target—is conceptually reasonable but feels incremental. The claim that Doc2VecC “reduces to DAE” without local context is not accurate: the learning objective is still predictive (negative sampling), not reconstructive.

- Empirical rigor and baselines:
  - Sentiment (IMDB): The paper compares to BoW, SDA/DAE, Word2Vec+AVG/IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought, RNNLM. However, strong established baselines are missing (e.g., NB-SVM with n-grams; TF-IDF n-grams up to 2/3) that historically rival or outperform neural methods on IMDB. FastText (supervised) and Sent2Vec/SIF are also relevant comparison points for simple averaging approaches. The Skip-Thought comparison is arguably unfair due to extreme document length mismatch.
  - Paragraph Vectors: PV is sensitive to configuration (DM vs DBOW, combined training, inference steps, negative sampling, subsampling). It’s unclear whether PV was tuned to its best practice; the notably poor word-analogy performance and t-SNE plots may reflect under-tuning or misuse rather than an inherent limitation.
  - Negative sampling distribution is set to uniform in Doc2VecC (as stated), differing from Word2Vec’s standard unigram^0.75. This choice can materially affect results and confounds direct comparisons unless all methods are aligned.
  - Reported CPU-only timing comparisons are not standardized across methods and may be sensitive to implementation details; presenting throughput under matched settings (same hardware, same vector sizes, same negative sampling, etc.) would be more convincing.

- Missing ablations/controls:
  - No systematic study of the corruption rate q (including q=0 baseline), nor of the necessity/value of local vs global context.
  - The claim that Doc2VecC “does not rely” on subsampling frequent words is not supported by experiments; the paper still uses subsampling for all numbers and does not report the effect of removing it for Doc2VecC.
  - No variance across random seeds or statistical tests; embedding-based methods can vary non-trivially across runs.
  - The data-dependent regularization hypothesis is plausible but lightly validated (a qualitative table of small-norm words). More convincing quantitative evidence (e.g., correlation of embedding norm with IDF, ablation on tasks with/without frequent-word suppression) is needed.

- Clarity and presentation:
  - Several tables/figures are misnumbered; references are messy/duplicated; some statements are imprecise (e.g., equivalence to DAE). Important hyperparameters are missing (window size, negatives per update, learning rate schedule, training epochs, subsampling threshold, vocabulary cutoffs, composition of PV variants, etc.). This hampers reproducibility despite the code link.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide comprehensive ablations:
  - Vary q (including 0), presence/absence of local context, negative sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75), and subsampling frequent words. Report performance and training speed trade-offs.
  - Report mean±std over multiple runs.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Include NB-SVM (Wang & Manning), TF-IDF with n-grams, FastText (supervised), SIF/Sent2Vec, PV-DBOW and DM, and combined PV variants tuned per best practices. Ensure matching embedding dimensionality and negative sampling distribution across methods.
- Tighten the theory–practice link:
  - Empirically validate the regularizer’s effect (e.g., plot embedding norm vs word frequency/IDF; show that removing corruption collapses these trends; show downstream sensitivity to removing stop words).
- Clarify and correct claims:
  - Remove or justify the “reduces to DAE” statement; reconcile the negative sampling choice and ensure fair comparisons with Word2Vec; detail all hyperparameters and training protocols.
- Improve presentation: fix references/tables, ensure figures are readable, and include complete dataset details and preprocessing.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the exact hyperparameters for Doc2VecC (window size, negatives per target, learning rate/optimizer, epochs, subsampling threshold, q values tried)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
2. Why was a uniform negative sampling distribution used instead of the standard unigram^0.75? If you switch to the standard distribution for all methods, do the conclusions hold?
3. Can you provide ablations for q (including q=0), and for removing local context, across all tasks? How much of the gain arises from corruption vs simply enforcing average during training?
4. You state Doc2VecC “does not rely” on subsampling frequent words, yet the reported numbers use it. What are the results for Doc2VecC without subsampling?
5. How were PV baselines configured (DM/DBOW, combined, inference epochs, learning rates)? Can you report stronger PV results under standard tuning and include DBOW?
6. Can you add comparisons to NB-SVM, TF-IDF n-grams, FastText (supervised), and SIF/Sent2Vec?
7. Could you quantify the regularization effect (e.g., correlation between embedding norm and IDF) and show it drives the performance gains?
