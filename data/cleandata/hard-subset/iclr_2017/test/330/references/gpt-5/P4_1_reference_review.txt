1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient approach to unsupervised document representation learning. The key idea is to learn word embeddings such that a document vector, computed as the average of its word embeddings, is predictive of target words when combined with a local CBOW context. During training, the document bag-of-words is subjected to unbiased dropout (“corruption”) so only a random subset of words contributes to the document vector per update. A second-order Taylor expansion shows this induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving informative ones. The model’s complexity depends only on vocabulary size (not corpus size) and yields very fast test-time encoding by simple averaging. Experiments on IMDB sentiment, a 100-class Wikipedia classification dataset, SICK semantic relatedness, and word analogy demonstrate strong or state-of-the-art performance relative to BoW, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought vectors, and LSTM-based methods, with substantial training and inference speed advantages. Code is provided.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- A well-motivated, principled, and efficient objective that enforces average-of-embeddings to carry document semantics, with a clear analytical link to a data-dependent regularizer.
- Strong empirical performance and large practical speedups, with simple test-time use.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Learning document representations that (i) capture global semantics, (ii) scale to large corpora, and (iii) allow fast encoding for unseen documents. The work addresses known weaknesses of Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth with corpus size and expensive test-time inference) and shortcomings of naive averaging (sensitivity to frequent words).

Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- Yes. Building on Word2Vec/CBOW, the paper integrates a global document vector derived from word averages into the prediction objective, and uses corruption to both accelerate training and introduce adaptive regularization. The positioning versus Word2Vec+post-processing, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and sentence encoders is clear. The design choices (unbiased dropout on document BoW, averaging) are justified by compositionality observations and by the analytical derivation.

Do the results support the claims?
- Largely yes. On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches or beats Paragraph Vectors and significantly outperforms BoW and Word2Vec+IDF, with notable training/inference efficiency. On Wikipedia classification, it consistently outperforms baselines across dimensions, and t-SNE visualizations qualitatively support better clustering. On SICK, it is competitive with LSTM and skip-thought despite being much simpler and faster, and surpasses older baselines. Word analogy results show improved embeddings relative to Word2Vec. The analysis of embedding norms illustrates the claimed suppression of frequent/stopwords. Training-time and test-time efficiency measurements are reported. The method is simple to reproduce and code is provided.

Caveats:
- The theoretical analysis is approximate (second-order expansion around the corruption mean, diagonal Hessian, and use of the negative sampling surrogate). It provides intuition rather than a strict guarantee, which is acceptable but should be presented as such.
- Some comparisons could be more controlled: e.g., confirm identical negative sampling distributions and hyperparameter tuning across baselines; clarify if Word2Vec uses standard unigram^0.75 vs uniform negatives (Pv is stated uniform here).
- The reliance on subsampling frequent words is discussed; an explicit ablation without subsampling for Doc2VecC would strengthen the claim that the method is less reliant on it.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify negative sampling: specify Pv for all methods and adopt the standard unigram^0.75 unless there is a reason to deviate; report sensitivity if using uniform.
- Report variance across multiple runs and significance tests for key benchmarks (IMDB, Wikipedia) to establish robustness.
- Provide ablations:
  - Corruption rate q sweep, including q=0 (no corruption), to quantify the trade-off between speed and accuracy and verify the regularization effect.
  - Remove the local context (only document vector) vs only local context (no global vector) to isolate the contribution of each component.
  - With and without frequent-word subsampling for Doc2VecC to support claims about reduced reliance on subsampling.
- Fairness of baselines:
  - Ensure Word2Vec+IDF uses strong settings (e.g., standard negatives, subsampling thresholds, window sizes). Consider stronger averaging baselines (e.g., smooth inverse frequency weighting) if in scope.
  - For Paragraph Vectors, confirm both DM and DBOW variants and standard hyperparameters were tuned.
- Method details:
  - State complexity per update as a function of surviving words and h; detail learning rates, optimizer, negatives per positive, window sizes, and vocabulary thresholds per dataset.
  - Clarify if document length normalization (1/T) is applied consistently during both training and inference.
- SICK setup: clearly separate pretraining on external corpora and any fine-tuning on SICK without labels, to avoid confusion about supervision; ensure no leakage of test labels.
- Presentation: fix minor typos and reference formatting; unify notation and ensure all tables/figures are fully legible and self-contained.

Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling: What distribution Pv is used in Doc2VecC and in the Word2Vec/Paragraph Vector baselines? Did you match it across methods (e.g., unigram^0.75)? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Corruption: How does performance vary with q and with the number of surviving words per update? What is the optimal range of q across datasets?
- Subsampling: Can you report Doc2VecC results without subsampling frequent words to quantify the method’s robustness relative to Word2Vec+AVG?
- Ablation: What is the performance when removing the local context (global-only) and when removing the global context (local-only)? How much does each contribute on IMDB and Wikipedia?
- Stability: What is the variance across random seeds for IMDB and Wikipedia? Are the reported gains statistically significant?
- Efficiency: Can you provide throughput numbers (tokens/sec) and asymptotic complexity, and confirm if speedups persist on GPU?
- Word analogy: Do gains persist when you equalize preprocessing (e.g., same vocabulary and tokenization) across methods?
- Generalization: How does Doc2VecC perform on other long-document datasets or multilingual corpora? Any observed degradation for very short texts?

Overall, the paper is clear, methodologically sound, and offers a practically valuable, scalable alternative to existing document embedding methods with both analytical insight and strong empirical results.
