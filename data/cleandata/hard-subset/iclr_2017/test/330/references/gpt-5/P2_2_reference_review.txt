Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for unsupervised document representation. Core idea: learn word embeddings so that the average over (corrupted) document words, together with local context, predicts a target word. At test time, a document vector is just the average of learned word embeddings.
- A corruption (mask-out/dropout) process on the document bag-of-words is used during training. A second-order Taylor analysis shows this induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses embeddings of common/non-informative words and preserves informative ones.
- Claims: (1) model size decoupled from corpus size; (2) training and inference efficiency comparable to Word2Vec and much faster than Paragraph Vector/Skip-thought; (3) competitive or superior accuracy on sentiment, document classification, and word analogy; (4) competitive sentence similarity.
- Code is released; multiple datasets are used (IMDB, Wikipedia, SICK, large news for analogy).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The method is simple, well-motivated, and computationally attractive; (2) Strong empirical results with clear speed advantages; (3) A useful and principled analysis connecting corruption to an adaptive regularizer that aligns with observed behavior.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets efficient, general-purpose document representations that avoid the drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth and expensive test-time inference) and the brittleness of post-hoc averaging. Using a learned average constrained during training is a clear, well-placed idea, grounded in prior observations on linear compositionality of embeddings and in dropout/mDAE theory.
- Novelty and relation to literature: While averaging word embeddings is known, the key novelty is integrating a corrupted document average into the CBoW objective to “force” the average to be predictive, plus the analysis that yields a data-dependent regularizer penalizing frequent words. This bridges Word2Vec, PV, and mDAE/dropout perspectives. The conceptual step is incremental but nontrivial, and the practical impact (speed+accuracy) is meaningful.
- Correctness and theory: The Taylor expansion-based derivation mirrors prior analyses (dropout, marginalized DAEs) and is technically sound given the approximations (independence, diagonal Hessian, expansion at the mean, negative sampling surrogate). It plausibly explains the attenuation of stop-word embeddings observed empirically.
- Empirical support:
  - IMDB: Doc2VecC matches/exceeds PV and clearly beats BoW/Word2Vec+IDF/DAE; large speedups at test time over PV and over Skip-thought encodings for long documents. The observation that LSTM/Skip-thought degrade on long texts is consistent with prior reports.
  - Wikipedia classification: Consistent gains over baselines at multiple embedding sizes; t-SNE plots qualitatively support better cluster structure.
  - Word analogy: Substantial improvements over Word2Vec across most subtasks as embedding dimension/data size grow; plausible explanation for PV’s poor analogy scores (capacity siphoned into doc IDs).
  - SICK: Slightly behind best LSTM/Skip-thought but still competitive, with far better efficiency.
- Efficiency and practicality: The model is corpus-size independent, trains quickly, and yields trivial test-time features (averaging). The CPU-based timing comparisons underscore practical utility.

Caveats and limitations (do not outweigh the positives)
- Novelty is moderate: the approach is close to CBoW with an added global (averaged) context and to dropout/mDAE; nevertheless, the combination and analysis are coherent and practically useful.
- Some comparisons/ablations are missing or underdeveloped:
  - Effect of corruption rate q on accuracy and speed beyond anecdotal statements.
  - The contribution of the local context vs. pure global averaging (the DEA reduction is mentioned but not ablated thoroughly).
  - Reliance on subsampling: while the paper argues the method “does not rely on” frequent-word subsampling, experiments still apply it; a clean ablation would strengthen the claim.
  - Negative sampling details (uniform noise vs. unigram^0.75) and sensitivity are not justified.
- Fairness and breadth of baselines:
  - A comparison to fastText-style averaging (with/without subword/n-grams) would be very relevant given the objective and the classification focus.
  - For SICK, pretraining and vocabulary extension strategies differ across methods; the setup is reasonable but could be more tightly controlled.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide comprehensive ablations:
  - Vary q and report performance/speed trade-offs.
  - Remove local context to quantify its marginal benefit.
  - Train Doc2VecC with/without word subsampling and report effects on both accuracy and the induced regularization (e.g., norms of common vs. rare words).
  - Compare uniform vs. standard negative sampling distributions and number of negatives.
- Clarify implementation details: optimizer, learning rate schedule, batch sizing, negative samples per update, window sizes, and whether word embeddings are tied between input/output.
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Include fastText-style averaging and possibly SIF-weighted averaging baselines for document/sentence embedding.
  - For PV, ensure strong hyperparameter tuning; for STV and LSTMs, ensure consistent pretraining/fine-tuning where possible.
- Report full tables (with numbers) for all referenced results and include standard deviations over multiple runs when feasible.
- Justify the 1/T scaling choice theoretically or empirically (e.g., compare to length-normalized vs. unnormalized global sums).
- Clean up minor typos/formatting; ensure references are complete and consistent.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is Doc2VecC to the corruption rate q and to the number/distribution of negative samples? Please provide a sensitivity plot.
- Do the claimed robustness to frequent-word subsampling hold without applying subsampling during training? What is the performance delta?
- Why choose a uniform negative sampling distribution Pv rather than the commonly used unigram^0.75? Does this choice materially affect results?
- What is the quantitative impact of removing the local context term Uc (i.e., using only the global corrupted average) on each task?
- Is the 1/T normalization essential? How do results change with alternative normalizations (e.g., IDF-weighted averages learned end-to-end)?
- Can you compare to fastText-style baselines (including subword/n-gram features) on IMDB and Wikipedia?
- For SICK, can you equalize pretraining and vocabulary handling across methods to validate the relative ranking more cleanly?
- Are input and output embeddings tied or separate? If separate, which are averaged at test time?

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant problem with a simple, well-motivated method, provides theoretical insight, and demonstrates strong empirical performance with compelling efficiency.
