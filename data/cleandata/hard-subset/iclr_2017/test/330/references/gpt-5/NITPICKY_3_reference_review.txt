Summary of contributions
- Proposes Doc2VecC, a simple CBOW-style architecture that augments local context with a global document vector formed by averaging word embeddings of (corrupted) words from the document. During training, a dropout/mask-out corruption of the document features acts as data-dependent regularization, intended to suppress frequent/less-informative words.
- Provides a Taylor expansion analysis arguing that unbiased dropout on the document vector yields an explicit regularizer stronger on frequent words unless they are predictive.
- Empirically evaluates on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, SICK semantic relatedness, and word analogy; claims competitive or superior performance to BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, skip-thought, and some LSTM baselines; highlights strong efficiency at test time (simple averaging).

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Numerous technical and presentation issues (notation inconsistencies, unclear/mistyped equations, incomplete experimental details, unfair/unclear comparisons, and problematic reference formatting) collectively undermine confidence in correctness and reproducibility.
2) The core idea—average embeddings with dropout/corruption acting as regularization—is interesting but incremental relative to CBOW/Paragraph Vector variants, and the work does not sufficiently isolate or ablate the novel components to support the strongest claims.

Supporting arguments
- Methodological clarity and correctness:
  - x is defined as binary BoW (xj ∈ {0,1}), yet the model repeatedly states it averages embeddings over “all words” (tokens). In equations, 1/T Ux uses T = token count but multiplies by a binary type vector x, which averages types scaled by token length. This token/type mismatch affects both training and the derivation and must be clarified/corrected (e.g., use count vector or sum over tokens consistently).
  - Several equations appear mistyped/ambiguous: e.g., in (2) and (3) “1T Ux̃” likely means (1/T)Ux̃; please disambiguate. In the variance term, Σx diagonal is written as q1−q x2j; presumably it should be (q/(1−q)) xj^2 for unbiased dropout. These are non-trivial, since they change the regularizer’s scale.
  - Negative sampling distribution Pv is stated as uniform, which differs from standard unigram^0.75 and can materially affect results. Was uniform actually used? If so, justify; if not, correct the text.
  - The Taylor expansion-based regularization ignores the local-context corruption (none) and assumes linearity in x; however, the logits include both local and global terms—please discuss cross-terms and whether higher-order interactions are negligible.
  - The link between the derived objective (using x) and the test-time representation d = (1/T)∑w∈D uw (over tokens) is asserted but not rigorously aligned with the features the model actually uses during training when x is binary.

- Experimental design and reporting:
  - Using the test set in the unsupervised representation learning phase (IMDB “all data” setting) is not best practice unless clearly separated and justified. The paper reports both settings but still emphasizes the “all data” gains. Please emphasize and analyze the “train+unlabeled only” setting.
  - Important training details are missing: number of negative samples, learning rates/schedules, window size, corruption rates beyond q=0.9, subsampling thresholds, early stopping, epochs. No confidence intervals or multiple runs are reported.
  - Comparisons to skip-thought are not apples-to-apples: you used a pre-trained model (not retrained) and report long encoding times for long documents; for SICK, you fine-tune Doc2VecC embeddings on the target corpus (unsupervised), but do not apply the analogous vocabulary expansion/fine-tuning used in the skip-thought paper consistently here. These choices likely favor your method.
  - Word analogy results are described (“~20% uplift”) but no explicit numbers or standard errors are provided in the text you included; PV is said to do “surprisingly bad,” but PV-DBOW does not learn word embeddings and PV-DM embeddings depend strongly on setup—please specify which variant and training details, and provide full per-category breakdowns.
  - On Wikipedia classification, only linear SVM is used; no ablations showing the effect of: removing corruption, varying q, removing the local context term (to test “global-only”), or replacing uniform negatives with unigram^0.75. These are critical to isolate the contribution of the corruption.
  - The “data-dependent regularization” evidence relies on a table of smallest-norm words; the “cutoff of 100” is unclear (frequency threshold?); results are anecdotal without quantitative measures of correlation between frequency and norm across vocabularies.

- Novelty and positioning:
  - The idea of representing documents by averaged embeddings is well-known; the novelty is enforcing this during training with a corrupted global context inside a CBOW objective. This is a reasonable, simple idea, but the paper should better distinguish itself from prior global-context CBOW variants (e.g., Huang et al. 2012) and from standard dropout/denoising objectives, with careful ablations.
  - Related work coverage is broad but several citations are incorrectly formatted and sometimes inconsistent (e.g., SDAs vs DAEs; multiple Mikolov refs; “women” vs “woman” in analogies).

- Presentation and formatting:
  - Title truncated (“MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION”), many equation typos/ambiguities, inconsistent notation (ct vs c, vectors vs columns), missing tables/figures in the provided text, and references contain numerous formatting errors and placeholders. These issues are pervasive and impede understanding.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Unify the definition of document representation during training and testing: use counts (tokens) consistently, and reflect that in the derivation. If you intentionally use binary x during training and token-average at test time, justify and analyze the effect.
- Provide full training details and standard deviations over multiple runs. Add ablations: (i) no corruption; (ii) vary q; (iii) remove local context; (iv) different negative sampling distributions; (v) use TF-IDF weighting during training vs test-time IDF.
- Ensure fair baselines: retrain skip-thought-like models on comparable corpora where possible; or clearly position the comparison as illustrative, not definitive. For PV, specify PV-DM vs PV-DBOW and the exact training setup.
- Report full numbers for the analogy benchmark (semantic/syntactic categories) and learning curves w.r.t. data and dimension.
- Clean up notation and proofs; explicitly derive the variance term and the Hessian diagonal; clarify scaling constants. Consider a simple synthetic study that demonstrates the frequency-dependent regularization effect quantitatively.

Questions for the authors
1) Do you use binary or count-based x during training? If binary, why divide by token length T, and why does this align with test-time token averaging?
2) What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were actually used in all experiments?
3) Can you provide ablations isolating the effect of corruption (q) and the global-context term vs local context?
4) Which Paragraph Vector variant(s) did you evaluate for word embeddings, and with what hyperparameters?
5) For skip-thought and SICK, did you apply vocabulary expansion/fine-tuning symmetrically to ensure fair comparison?
6) Can you include variance/error bars over multiple runs and details on hyperparameter tuning?
