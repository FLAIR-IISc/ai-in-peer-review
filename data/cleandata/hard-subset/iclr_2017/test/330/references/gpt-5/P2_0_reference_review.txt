Summary and contributions
- Problem: Learn efficient, general-purpose document embeddings that capture global semantics, avoid the parameter blow-up and test-time inference of Paragraph Vectors, and remain fast to train/test like Word2Vec.
- Approach: Doc2VecC learns word embeddings with a CBOW-like objective augmented with a global document context formed by averaging embeddings of a randomly corrupted subset of words in the document. At test time, a document vector is simply the average of its learned word embeddings.
- Key idea and theory: The unbiased mask-out corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer via a second-order Taylor approximation of the negative-sampling objective. This regularizer down-weights common, less informative words and preserves informative/rare words—providing a principled reason why simple averaging can work well when trained jointly with corruption.
- Empirics: Doc2VecC matches or improves over strong baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, DAE, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thoughts) on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-way document classification, and SICK semantic relatedness, with clear speed advantages in training and especially test-time inference. It also yields better word embeddings on word analogy tasks compared to Word2Vec under the authors’ setup. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Simple, well-motivated method with a clean theoretical justification that translates into consistent empirical gains and substantial efficiency improvements.
2) Strong practical impact: parameter footprint independent of corpus size, trivial test-time inference by averaging, and robustness to common-word noise via the induced regularization.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement: Averaging word embeddings as document representations is known, and global context has been used (e.g., Huang et al., 2012). The novelty here is to integrate corruption during training so that the model explicitly optimizes for the mean-of-embeddings document vector, together with an analysis connecting corruption to an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer. This positions Doc2VecC between Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, with a compelling trade-off: Word2Vec-level simplicity and PV-like ability to model global document semantics.
- Motivation: Clear pain points of PV (parameter growth with corpus size; costly test-time inference) and of naive averaging (common words dominate) are addressed. The corruption-derived regularizer provides a principled mechanism for suppressing frequent, uninformative words.
- Theory: The second-order approximation around the unbiased corruption mean is standard and yields intuitive terms: frequent words receive larger penalties, reduced when they are crucial to confident predictions. While approximate, the analysis is sound and aligns with prior results on dropout/marginalized DAEs.
- Empirics and rigor: Multiple tasks and baselines, including strong representation learners. On IMDB and Wikipedia, Doc2VecC consistently beats PV and Word2Vec+IDF, and is far more efficient at test time than PV. The SICK results are slightly behind LSTM/Skip-Thoughts (expected for short sentences), yet training/testing are orders of magnitude faster. The word analogy gains and the embedding norm analysis support the regularization story. A code release enhances reproducibility.
- Efficiency claims: The parameter count depends on vocabulary rather than corpus size, and training with high corruption (q=0.9) reduces updates per step, explaining speedups. At test time, simple averaging is a clear win over PV inference.

Points for improvement (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Ablations: Provide explicit ablations isolating (i) effect of corruption rate q (performance vs speed), (ii) no-local-context variant (DEA-like), and (iii) training without corruption but the same architecture, to quantify the contribution of each component.
- Negative sampling distribution: You state Pv is uniform. Standard practice in Word2Vec is unigram^0.75; please include results with that choice to ensure fairness and possibly improved performance.
- Paragraph Vectors details: Specify PV variant(s) (DM, DBOW), hyperparameters, and ensure a fair, tuned comparison including PV-DBOW, which often performs well on word analogy/classification. Consider reporting PV with inference tricks at test time (e.g., more iterations) vs speed/accuracy trade-offs.
- Additional baselines: Compare to strong post-2015 bag-of-ngrams methods (e.g., fastText) and SIF/weighted averaging approaches to control for improved weighting schemes.
- Experimental details: Add window size, negatives per positive, learning rates, epochs, subsampling thresholds, initialization, and regularization settings per task. Include validation protocols and seeds to facilitate replication.
- Timing fairness: Some timing comparisons use CPU for Doc2VecC and GPU for Skip-Thoughts training (or vice versa). Clarify hardware and parallelization to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Broader evaluation: Consider reporting robustness to document length, domain shifts, and multilingual corpora, given the motivation around long documents.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and window size k? Can you provide curves to show the speed/accuracy trade-off and recommend defaults?
2) What is the effect of using the standard unigram^0.75 negative sampling distribution vs uniform? Do the word analogy and classification gains persist?
3) Which PV variants were used (DM, DBOW, or both), and with what inference settings at test time? Could a stronger/tuned PV baseline reduce the observed gap?
4) Can you report an ablation removing local context (reducing to a denoising objective) and an ablation removing corruption, to quantify their contributions on IMDB and Wikipedia?
5) On SICK, you fine-tune on the target vocabulary without using labels. How much performance comes from this fine-tuning versus zero-shot transfer?
6) Do you observe the same data-dependent regularization effect (embedding norms vs frequency) across other corpora/languages, and how does it compare to tf-idf/SIF weighting applied post hoc?

Overall, this paper presents a simple, well-motivated idea with theoretical backing and solid empirical benefits, especially on long-document tasks and efficiency, making it a strong and practical contribution.
