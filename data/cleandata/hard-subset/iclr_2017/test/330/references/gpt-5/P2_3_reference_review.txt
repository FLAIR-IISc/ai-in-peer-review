1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient method to learn document representations by enforcing that a document vector is the average of its word embeddings, learned within a CBOW-like prediction objective that uses both local context and a global document context. The key idea is to form the global context by randomly masking words from the document (unbiased dropout), which both speeds training and induces a data-dependent regularization on word embeddings. A second-order Taylor expansion shows that the corruption yields a regularizer that suppresses embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving rare/informative ones. Empirically, Doc2VecC:
- decouples parameter count from corpus size (depends only on vocabulary),
- is fast to train and test,
- produces competitive or better results than strong baselines on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-class classification, and word analogy; it is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness,
- offers large efficiency gains at test time (simple averaging) and training time (fewer parameters updated due to corruption),
with code released.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The approach is simple, well-motivated, and provides a clear analytical explanation (corruption as data-dependent regularization) for why averaging can work well; (2) strong and consistent empirical performance with convincing efficiency advantages and good reproducibility.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets document representation learning that is efficient, corpus-size-independent (unlike PV), and general-purpose, improving over post-hoc averaging of word vectors by ensuring during training that an average is predictive. The motivation is clear and well placed relative to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, with a principled corruption mechanism connecting to dropout/mDAE analyses.
- Novelty and relation to prior work: Averaging embeddings is known, and corruption/denoising is known, but combining (i) a CBOW objective with (ii) a corrupted global document average used during training to (iii) induce a specific, interpretable regularization and (iv) enable fast test-time averaging is a neat, practical contribution. The Taylor expansion analysis strengthens the novelty by making the effect explicit.
- Empirical support: The method achieves substantial gains on IMDB and Wikipedia classification over BoW, SDAE, Word2Vec+IDF, and Doc2Vec; is competitive on SICK; and substantially improves word analogy accuracy. Efficiency measurements (training and test-time) strongly support the scalability claims. Ablations are partially present (e.g., subsampling discussion, sensitivity to q qualitatively), and analyses (norms of embeddings vs frequency) are informative.
- Rigor and clarity: The analysis is approximate but standard for corruption/dropout (second-order expansion). It gives the right qualitative predictions (down-weighting frequent stopwords unless predictive). The method section is mostly clear, and the objective is well specified.

Caveats:
- Missing/limited baselines: Notably absent is fastText (Joulin et al., 2016), a very strong and efficient bag-of-tricks baseline using averaged word embeddings with n-grams and supervised training, which is highly relevant for the classification experiments. Also, more recent sentence embedding baselines (depending on submission date) could strengthen comparisons.
- Fairness/details: Some baselines use different dimensionalities (skip-thought at 4800-D), different pretraining corpora, and domain mismatch; negative sampling distribution appears uniform in the text (whereas Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75), which might disadvantage Word2Vec baselines. Hyperparameter parity across methods is not fully documented.
- Ablations: The paper could more thoroughly quantify the contribution of (i) corruption vs no corruption, (ii) local context vs global-only, and (iii) different corruption rates.

Despite these, the method’s simplicity, sound motivation, consistent gains, and clear efficiency benefits merit acceptance.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add strong bag-of-tricks baselines: fastText (supervised and unsupervised variants) and tf-idf weighted averaging with SIF/SIF+whitening (if within timeline) for a fair, lightweight comparison class.
- Provide comprehensive ablations: no corruption; different q values and schedules; global-only vs local+global; test-time averaging with/without IDF; effect of word subsampling threshold; and number/distribution of negatives.
- Clarify negative sampling: use the standard unigram^0.75 distribution and report sensitivity; otherwise justify uniform negatives.
- Report full training details for reproducibility: window size, negatives per step, learning rate schedule, epochs, threading, implementation language; ensure time comparisons are apples-to-apples.
- Expand analysis of the regularization effect: plot embedding norm vs document frequency; show impact on downstream performance when reweighting is removed.
- Strengthen SICK results: report with/without fine-tuning, and compare to more recent sentence encoders if possible.
- Discuss limitations: very long documents vs hierarchical models; no subword information; potential gains from n-grams (as in fastText).

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution did you ultimately use in all experiments? If uniform, how do results change with unigram^0.75?
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and to the unbiased scaling? Could Gaussian noise or mDAE-style marginalization offer further gains?
- Can you provide quantitative ablations for removing the local context (global-only) and removing corruption (global average of full document) to isolate the contribution of each component?
- At test time, do you see any benefit from tf-idf weighting or SIF-style debiasing on top of averaging the learned embeddings?
- How does Doc2VecC compare to fastText on IMDB and Wikipedia under matched conditions (same dimensionality, same preprocessing)?
- How robust is the method to OOVs and large vocabularies; have you tried subword representations?
- For the analogy task, can you clarify training details and why PV underperforms so markedly? Did you try PV-DBOW?
