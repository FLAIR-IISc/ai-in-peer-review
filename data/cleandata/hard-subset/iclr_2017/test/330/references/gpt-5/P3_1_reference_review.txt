1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient architecture for learning document representations. The model predicts a target word from (i) a local context (CBOW-style) and (ii) a global document vector formed as the average of word embeddings of a randomly corrupted bag-of-words. The corruption (mask-out/dropout) is unbiased and aggressive, both accelerating training and, via a second-order Taylor expansion, yielding a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving rare/informative ones. At test time, documents are represented by the simple average of learned word embeddings. The paper claims: (a) decoupled parameter size from corpus size (no document parameters), (b) Word2Vec-like training efficiency, (c) theoretically motivated regularization, and (d) strong empirical performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia subsets), word analogies, and semantic relatedness (SICK). Code is provided.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty relative to prior work on using (corrupted) averages of word embeddings as global context (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), PV-DM/DBOW (Le & Mikolov, 2014), and dropout/marginalized denoising analyses (Wager et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014). The main contributions feel incremental (replace learned document vector with average and apply corruption).
- Missing or weak comparisons to strong, closely related baselines that already popularized the “average of embeddings” idea for document classification, notably fastText (Joulin et al., 2016), SIF/weighted averaging (Arora et al., 2017), and Sent2Vec (Pagliardini et al., 2018). This gap makes it hard to assess the true empirical value-add of Doc2VecC.

3) Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Clarity of the core idea and its practical appeal: training-time enforcement that averaging works, plus not learning per-document parameters, makes inference extremely fast and memory-light.
- The Taylor-expansion-based analysis aligns with known interpretations of dropout as adaptive regularization and provides an intuitive explanation for the observed down-weighting of stopwords.
- Empirical results show solid gains over PV and Word2Vec+IDF on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, and a notable uplift on word analogies, with substantial speed advantages.
- Code availability and some practical insights (e.g., high corruption rates work well) increase usability.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Novelty/scope: The architecture is very close to CBOW with an added global context equal to a corrupted whole-document average; Huang et al. (2012) already used sentence/document-level averages as global context. The main new ingredient is heavy corruption of the global context. The theoretical analysis reuses existing techniques (dropout/mDAE Taylor expansions) and adapts them to this setup; this adds intuition but limited new theory.
- Baselines: Missing fastText is a major omission for document classification; fastText’s supervised target is different, but it squarely addresses the same application and is known to be a strong, simple baseline. For unsupervised sentence/document embeddings, SIF and Sent2Vec are also highly relevant. Without these, the empirical story is incomplete.
- Ablations and fairness:
  - The paper claims insensitivity to corruption rate q but provides no systematic ablation curves or error bars.
  - The claim that removing local context “reduces to denoising autoencoders” is not precise; the objective differs from reconstructing inputs and targets (it is a word prediction task with negative sampling). A proper ablation comparing “global-only,” “local-only,” and “both” is necessary.
  - Subsampling of frequent words is applied, but the fairness and impact across methods is not exhaustively reported. If Doc2VecC “doesn’t rely” on subsampling, show results without it for all methods to isolate the effect of the proposed regularization.
  - The negative sampling distribution is stated as uniform over V, deviating from the common unigram^0.75; this could disadvantage or advantage some methods. Please justify and/or include the standard choice.
  - Skip-thought comparisons are not entirely fair due to sentence-length mismatch; the paper acknowledges this but emphasizes poor performance nonetheless.
- Statistical rigor: No variances or multiple runs are reported; significance is unclear. Hyperparameter tuning details are sparse; e.g., number of negative samples, window sizes, learning rates.
- Clarity and presentation: The draft has formatting and reference issues (broken author-year formatting, missing details), and some equations and symbols are introduced informally. This reduces polish and reproducibility.

4) Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Essential experimental additions:
  - Compare against fastText (supervised and, if you want parity of supervision, train a simple linear classifier on averaged pretrained embeddings with n-grams).
  - Include SIF weighted averaging with common component removal and Sent2Vec baselines.
  - Provide ablations: effect of q; with/without subsampling; with/without local context; window sizes; negative sampling distributions (uniform vs unigram^0.75).
  - Report averages over multiple runs with standard deviations; include statistical tests where improvements are small.
- Theory/analysis:
  - Clarify the approximation when using negative sampling (you analyze log-likelihood but train with negative sampling); state assumptions and limitations explicitly.
  - The statement that Doc2VecC “reduces to DAE” without local context should be revised or justified; the objectives differ in supervision signal and loss.
  - Discuss how the regularization depends on document length T and whether long-document bias remains after the 1/T scaling.
- Implementation details:
  - Provide details on number of negatives, window sizes, optimizer, learning rates, and training schedules across datasets. Clarify whether hierarchical softmax was ever used and why not.
  - For the word analogy experiments, specify training corpora, epochs, and whether subword information was used (for fair comparison to strong baselines).
- Scope and limitations:
  - Acknowledge the loss of word order and potential weaknesses on syntax-heavy tasks; consider simple extensions (e.g., averaging n-gram embeddings) and report results.
  - Discuss OOV handling and vocabulary expansion in more depth; quantify the effect of fine-tuning on SICK.
- Clarity:
  - Fix reference formatting and minor typos; ensure all tables/figures are fully legible and included.
  - Make the training-time computational complexity explicit (per-token cost) and compare to PV and CBOW quantitatively.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q across tasks? Please provide curves and an interpretation (e.g., trade-off between speed and signal).
- What is the contribution of local context vs global context? Do you observe redundancy or complementarity?
- Why use uniform negative sampling instead of the standard unigram^0.75? Does switching distributions change conclusions?
- Can Doc2VecC match fastText on standard text classification benchmarks (AG News, Yelp, DBPedia), and how does adding n-grams affect results?
- Is the strong uplift on word analogies reproducible across multiple runs and negative sampling settings? What aspects of Doc2VecC most drive this gain?

Overall, the idea is appealing and practical, the analysis is sensible, and the empirical results are promising. However, the work lacks critical baselines and ablations and feels incremental relative to prior art. With stronger comparisons and a more thorough experimental and theoretical treatment, this could become a compelling contribution.
