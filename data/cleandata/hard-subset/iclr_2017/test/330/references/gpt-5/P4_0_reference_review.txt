1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation method that learns word embeddings so that simple averaging of a document’s words yields an effective document vector. During training, the model predicts a target word from (a) local context (CBOW-style) and (b) a global document vector formed by averaging a randomly corrupted subset of the document’s words. The corruption is unbiased dropout over the document BoW, scaled by 1/(1−q). A second-order Taylor expansion of the expected log-likelihood under corruption yields a data-dependent regularizer that downweights embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while preserving important ones. The approach has several advertised advantages: training complexity independent of the number of documents (no per-document parameters), efficient test-time encoding via simple averaging, and improved robustness through the implicit regularizer. Empirically, the method performs competitively or better than BoW, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, and Paragraph Vectors on IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia document classification, produces stronger word embeddings on analogy tasks, and is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness. It is also substantially faster at test time than Paragraph Vectors. Code is provided.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) a simple, well-motivated architecture with a clear theoretical explanation (corruption-induced regularization) and strong efficiency benefits; (ii) broad empirical support showing consistent gains over strong baselines across multiple tasks.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper addresses learning document vectors that are semantically meaningful, scalable, and efficient at test time. It is well-positioned relative to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors: unlike PV, there are no per-document parameters; unlike post-hoc averaging, the training objective shapes word embeddings to make averaging effective.

- Motivation and placement in the literature: Prior work averages word embeddings for global context (Huang et al., 2012) or constructs document vectors post hoc. The key novelty here is enforcing the averaging composition during training and introducing unbiased dropout over the document to both accelerate training and induce a principled, data-dependent regularizer. The connection to dropout/mDAE theory and the derivation via Taylor expansion are appropriate and well grounded.

- Technical soundness: The objective and negative sampling formulation are standard. The corruption model is unbiased; the variance Σ is diagonal under independent masking; the Taylor expansion yields a regularizer scaling as q/(1−q) with terms proportional to σ(1−σ)(v^T u_j / T)^2. While the derivation is approximate (second-order, negative sampling), it is standard practice in analyzing dropout-like schemes and the resulting qualitative behavior (penalizing frequent but uninformative words unless crucial to confident predictions) is coherent.

- Empirical support: Results cover sentiment (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100-class), word analogy, and semantic relatedness (SICK). On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches/exceeds PV and substantially improves over BoW and Word2Vec+IDF; run-time comparisons demonstrate clear test-time advantages. On Wikipedia classification, Doc2VecC improves error across embedding sizes versus DEA, Word2Vec variants, and PV. On analogy tasks, Doc2VecC embeddings outperform Word2Vec with the same data and dimensions. On SICK, Doc2VecC is close to LSTM/skip-thought methods, while being far faster to train/evaluate. The analysis of embedding norms supports the claimed regularization effect.

- Rigor and completeness: The paper compares to relevant baselines and explains two IMDB protocols (including and excluding test data during representation learning). Training data are matched across models in several experiments, and code is available. Some details (e.g., sensitivity to q, negative sampling distribution) could be expanded, but overall the evidence is sufficient.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Report sensitivity to corruption rate q (accuracy/runtime trade-off), and to the choice of negative sampling distribution (e.g., unigram^0.75 vs uniform).
  - Include an ablation without corruption (pure averaging global context) to isolate the contribution of the regularizer.
  - Evaluate with/without subsampling of frequent words to quantify how much the intrinsic regularizer reduces reliance on that heuristic.

- Baselines and fairness:
  - Consider adding PV-DBOW and FastText-style baselines (averaging with learned word n-grams), and SIF/IDF-weighted averaging methods.
  - For SICK, clarify whether all compared methods use identical pretraining data and tokenization; discuss fairness of comparisons to skip-thought (trained on BookCorpus).

- Experimental reporting:
  - Provide variance across multiple runs and significance testing for key results.
  - Detail hyperparameters (embedding size, window size, negatives per step, learning rates) and training budgets for reproducibility.
  - Clarify the exact vocabulary handling in SICK fine-tuning and its quantitative impact.

- Theory/clarity:
  - Summarize the assumptions behind the Taylor expansion (e.g., magnitude of higher-order terms), and discuss when the approximation might be inaccurate.
  - Improve notation consistency (e.g., reuse of V for vocabulary vs matrix, transposes) and correct minor typos/formatting.

Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling: What distribution over negatives was ultimately used (uniform vs unigram^α)? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Corruption rate: How does performance vary with q across tasks? Is there a sweet spot balancing regularization and training efficiency?
- Ablation: What is the performance if the global context is computed without corruption during training? Conversely, if test-time representation averages only non-corrupted words (e.g., sub-sampling), does it change results?
- Frequent-word handling: If subsampling frequent words is disabled, how much performance drop remains for Word2Vec+AVG vs Doc2VecC?
- Complexity: Can you quantify per-update computational cost relative to Word2Vec CBOW (as a function of document length and q)? Any memory bottlenecks?
- Length effects: Beyond IMDB, how does performance scale with document length? For very short texts (e.g., tweets), does Doc2VecC still outperform IDF-weighted averages?
- Generalization: Have you tried subword representations (e.g., character n-grams) within Doc2VecC to address OOV and morphology?
- SICK protocol: How many epochs/steps of fine-tuning on SICK were used, and did you use relatedness labels or only unsupervised objectives? What is the exact gain from fine-tuning?
- Analogy results: Please provide full quantitative tables and training details to enable replication of the reported ~20% uplift over Word2Vec.
