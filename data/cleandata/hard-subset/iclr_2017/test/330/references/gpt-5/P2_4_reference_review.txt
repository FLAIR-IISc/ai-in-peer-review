Summary and contributions
- Problem: Learn efficient, general-purpose document representations that capture global semantics without per-document parameters and with fast inference.
- Method: Doc2VecC represents a document as the average of its word embeddings, but crucially trains those embeddings with a predictive objective that conditions on both local context (CBOW-style) and a “global” document vector constructed from a randomly corrupted version of the document (unbiased word dropout). The authors analyze the corruption as inducing a data-dependent regularizer that dampens embeddings of frequent, uninformative words while preserving informative ones.
- Claims: (i) model complexity depends only on vocabulary size (no per-document parameters), (ii) training is Word2Vec-like and efficient, (iii) at test time the document vector is a simple average, (iv) corruption yields a principled regularization favoring informative words, and (v) the approach matches or outperforms state of the art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and performs competitively on semantic relatedness, while producing strong word embeddings on analogy tasks.
- Empirics: Results on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-way classification, SICK relatedness, and word analogies; speed comparisons and t-SNE visualizations; code released.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) A simple, well-motivated idea with a clean theoretical justification (corruption-as-regularization) and strong practical benefits (no per-doc parameters; very fast test-time encoding).
2) Solid empirical evidence with consistent gains over strong baselines on multiple benchmarks, plus substantial efficiency improvements.

Supporting arguments
- On motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned relative to Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors, DAE/mDAE, and prior uses of averaging as a global context (Huang et al., 2012). The main novelty is training-time enforcement that a document’s average embedding is predictive via a corruption mechanism, together with a clear analysis showing how the corruption induces a frequency-aware regularizer. This addresses well-known weaknesses of naive averaging (dominance of stop words) without post-hoc heuristics like IDF or subsampling.
- On correctness/rigor: The Taylor expansion around the unbiased corruption mean, diagonal variance simplification, and connection to a data-dependent penalty mirror established analyses for dropout/mDAE, and the derivation is plausible and insightful. Some approximations (e.g., second-order truncation, independence assumptions) are standard; while not exact, they support the intended intuition and align with observed behavior (e.g., norms of stop-word embeddings shrink).
- On empirical support: 
  - IMDB: Doc2VecC matches or exceeds Paragraph Vectors and clearly improves over Word2Vec+IDF and DEA, with large speedups, especially at test time (no inference). The discussion of sequence length harming Skip-Thought is reasonable.
  - Wikipedia classification: Doc2VecC consistently outperforms baselines across embedding sizes; visualizations show cleaner clustering.
  - SICK: Competitive with LSTM/Skip-Thought while being far more efficient, and substantially above earlier tree/RNN baselines.
  - Word analogies: Reported gains over Word2Vec are notable; if reproduced, they suggest the corruption benefits word-level semantics, not only document encoding.
- Efficiency: The model delivers a strong speed/accuracy tradeoff; CPU-only training and milliseconds-level test-time averaging is compelling in practice.

Reservations and limitations (do not block acceptance)
- Novelty is incremental: averaging word embeddings is standard; corruption/dropout and mDAE-style analyses are known. The contribution is in combining them within a predictive document objective and demonstrating that this makes simple averaging truly competitive.
- Some baselines could be stronger or more controlled:
  - Paragraph Vectors: clarify whether PV-DM and PV-DBOW are both tuned; PV-DBOW often fares better on classification.
  - Word2Vec/negative sampling: the paper mentions uniform negatives; standard practice uses unigram^0.75. Ensure identical settings across methods for fair word-analogy comparisons.
  - Skip-Thought wasn’t retrained; although this reflects a realistic usage pattern, it disadvantages it on IMDB. Still, you provide both rationale and alternative comparisons.
- Ablations would strengthen the case: e.g., removing corruption; varying corruption rate q; varying the mixing of local-vs-global context; training without local context to test the “reduces to DAE” claim empirically.

Questions for the authors
1) Baselines and tuning: Which Paragraph Vector variants and hyperparameters were used (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW, window size, negative sampling rates)? Could you report results for the strongest PV setting you tried?
2) Negative sampling distribution: Did all methods (Word2Vec, Doc2VecC, PV) use the same negative sampling distribution (e.g., unigram^0.75) and same number of negatives? If not, can you align them and report sensitivity?
3) Ablations: How much of the gain comes from corruption vs. just training with a global-avg context? Results for q in {0, 0.5, 0.9, 0.95} would be helpful; also, a variant with tuned weighting between local and global contexts.
4) Analogy results: These are surprisingly strong vs Word2Vec. Can you share more training details (dataset size, epochs, subsampling thresholds, ns negatives) and include standard Word2Vec benchmark numbers for reference?
5) Test-time weighting: Have you tried simple reweightings (e.g., IDF or SIF) on top of Doc2VecC embeddings at test time, or removing the first principal component? Do they help or are they redundant given the learned regularization?

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the loss approximation steps and signs in Section 3.1; be explicit about assumptions and provide a brief intuition for why the regularizer grows with word frequency yet diminishes when a word is informative for confident predictions.
- Provide a compact ablation table isolating the effect of corruption, local/global balance, and subsampling.
- Standardize negative sampling and subsampling across methods to ensure fair comparisons; report key hyperparameters in an appendix.
- Improve the “reduces to DAE” claim: either qualify it as “DAE-like” with rationale or demonstrate equivalence/empirical behavior.
- Clean up references and formatting; some citations are malformed.

Overall, this paper offers a simple, well-justified, and practically valuable approach that performs strongly and is easy to adopt. Despite modest novelty, the combination of theory, empirical results, and efficiency warrants acceptance.
