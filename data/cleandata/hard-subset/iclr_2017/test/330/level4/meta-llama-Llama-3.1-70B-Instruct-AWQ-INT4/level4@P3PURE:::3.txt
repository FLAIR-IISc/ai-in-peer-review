This paper proposes learning document embeddings as a sum of the constituent word embeddings, which are jointly learned and randomly dropped out ('corrupted') during training. Although the individual components of this model are not particularly novel, the resulting algorithm is efficient for learning document representations and achieves good empirical performance.

The joint training of word and document embeddings is not a new concept, nor is the idea of representing documents as the sum of their word embeddings (see, e.g., "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism employed in this paper is essentially traditional dropout applied to the input layer. When combined with the word2vec-style loss and training methods, this paper offers limited novelty.

However, the proposed model is very efficient at generation time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than a complicated inference step, as in the case of Doc2Vec. Moreover, by construction, the embedding captures salient global information about the document, specifically the information that aids in local-context prediction. Considering its simplicity, the performance of this model on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the lack of novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study. Therefore, I recommend acceptance.
