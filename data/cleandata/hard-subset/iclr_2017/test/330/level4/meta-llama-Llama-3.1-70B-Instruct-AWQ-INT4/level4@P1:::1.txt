This paper proposes a novel method for computing vector representations of documents by leveraging a skip-gram style learning mechanism with an added regularizer in the form of a global context vector with various bits of drop out. While none of the individual components proposed in this paper are new, I believe that the combination in this fashion is innovative and contributes to the field. Furthermore, I appreciated the detailed analysis of model behavior in Section 3, which provides valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed approach.

However, the main downside to this submission is its relative weakness on the empirical front. The evaluation is limited to sentiment analysis and k-way classification tasks, which, although relevant, do not fully demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. I would have liked to see more diverse and challenging tasks, such as topic modeling, information retrieval, or question answering, to further assess the capabilities of the proposed approach.

Additionally, I found the use of t-SNE projections to be somewhat underwhelming. While the visualizations are aesthetically pleasing, they do not provide significant insights into the model's performance or behavior. I would have preferred to see this space used for further analysis, such as ablation studies, sensitivity analysis, or comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.

Despite these limitations, I believe that this paper should be accepted. The proposed algorithm is interesting, well-composed, and very efficient, making it a valuable contribution to the field. I assume that other readers might find some of the ideas presented here useful, and I appreciate the authors' efforts to provide a detailed and well-structured presentation of their work.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest the following:

* Consider adding more diverse and challenging tasks to the evaluation to better demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed method.
* Replace the t-SNE projections with more informative analysis, such as ablation studies or comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods.
* Provide more detailed comparisons with other methods, including a discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
* Consider adding more visualizations or tables to support the claims made in the paper and to provide a clearer understanding of the results.

Overall, I believe that this paper has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field, and with some revisions, it could become an even stronger submission.
