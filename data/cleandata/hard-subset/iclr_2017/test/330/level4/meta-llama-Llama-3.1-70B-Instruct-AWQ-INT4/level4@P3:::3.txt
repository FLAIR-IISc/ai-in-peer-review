This paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The approach is well-motivated, building upon the idea that linear operations on word embeddings can sustain substantial syntactic and semantic meanings of phrases and sentences. The authors also introduce a corruption model that randomly removes words from a document during learning, which acts as a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The results show that Doc2VecC outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms, including Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors, while being more efficient in terms of training and testing time.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. While the individual components of the model may not be novel, the combination of joint training of word and document embeddings, corruption mechanism, and word2vec-style loss and training methods results in an efficient and effective algorithm for document representation learning. The simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the model, its motivations, and the experimental results. The related work section is comprehensive, and the authors provide a thorough comparison of their approach with existing methods.

Some minor suggestions for improvement:

* The authors could provide more insight into why the corruption mechanism is effective in improving the performance of the model. While they provide some analysis of the regularization term, it would be helpful to have more intuition about how this term affects the learning process.
* The paper could benefit from more visualizations of the learned document embeddings. While the authors provide some visualizations, it would be helpful to have more examples to illustrate the quality of the learned representations.
* The authors could provide more discussion on the limitations of the model and potential avenues for future work. While the model performs well on several tasks, it would be helpful to have more insight into its limitations and potential applications.

Overall, this is a well-written paper that proposes an efficient and effective algorithm for document representation learning. With some minor revisions, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
