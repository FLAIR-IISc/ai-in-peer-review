This paper proposes a novel approach to learning document embeddings by representing them as a sum of their constituent word embeddings, which are jointly learned and randomly dropped out ('corrupted') during training. Although the individual components of this model are not particularly new, the resulting algorithm is efficient and achieves good empirical performance in document representation tasks.

The idea of jointly training word and document embeddings is not novel, as seen in previous works such as "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders" by Lebret and Collobert. Similarly, the concept of representing documents as the sum of their word embeddings has been explored before. Furthermore, the corruption mechanism employed in this paper is essentially traditional dropout applied to the input layer. When combined with word2vec-style loss and training methods, this paper does not offer significant contributions in terms of novelty.

However, the proposed model excels in its efficiency during generation time, requiring only a simple average of the word embeddings rather than a complex inference step, as is the case with Doc2Vec. Moreover, by design, the embedding captures salient global information about the document, specifically the information that aids in local-context prediction. Given the simplicity of the model, its performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is quite encouraging.

In conclusion, despite the lack of novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study. I recommend acceptance, as the paper provides a valuable contribution to the field by demonstrating the effectiveness of a straightforward yet efficient approach to document representation learning.
