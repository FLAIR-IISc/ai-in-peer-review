This paper presents a framework for creating document representations by averaging word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing common words to be close to 0. The authors claim that their proposed method, Doc2VecC, outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms in sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

I accept this paper, mainly because of its simplicity and efficiency in generating high-quality document representations. The experimental results demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive performance with existing methods, while requiring significantly less training time and computational resources.

The main technical contribution of this paper is the introduction of a corruption model that acts as a data-dependent regularization, which helps to suppress the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words. This is a useful technique that can be applied to other document representation learning methods. However, I agree with the authors that most of the techniques used in this paper are not new, and the main selling point is the simplicity and speed of the proposed method.

To further strengthen the paper, I would like to see results for more tasks, such as question answering, text summarization, or machine translation, to demonstrate the generalizability of the proposed method. Additionally, I would like to see a more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as bidirectional LSTM-based approaches, which are widely used in document representation learning.

Some specific questions I have for the authors are:

* For the RNN-LM baseline, was the language model trained to minimize classification error or was it trained as a language model? Was the final hidden state used as the representation, or the average of all hidden states?
* How does the proposed method compare to bidirectional LSTM-based approaches, which are widely used in document representation learning, for tasks such as document classification or sentiment analysis?
* Can the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the proposed method and the baselines?
* How does the corruption rate q affect the performance of the proposed method, and what is the optimal value for q?

Overall, I think this paper presents a useful and efficient method for document representation learning, and with some additional experiments and comparisons, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
