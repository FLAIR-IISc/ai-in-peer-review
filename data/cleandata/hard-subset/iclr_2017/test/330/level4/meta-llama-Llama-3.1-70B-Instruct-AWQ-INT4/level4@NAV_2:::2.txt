This paper presents a framework for creating document representations, which is based on representing a document as an average of its word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing common words to be close to zero. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness tasks, showing that the proposed method has competitive or better performance compared to baseline document embedding methods.

I am inclined to accept this paper, with the main reason being that it presents a novel and efficient approach to learning document representations. The proposed method is simple, fast, and effective, making it a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the proposed method and its technical contributions. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of their approach, comparing it to several baseline methods and demonstrating its effectiveness on multiple tasks. The inclusion of a data-dependent regularization term is a key innovation of the paper, and the authors provide a clear explanation of its benefits and how it improves the performance of the proposed method.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the technical contributions, while significant, are not necessarily groundbreaking. The authors build on existing work in the field, and the proposed method is an extension of existing techniques rather than a completely new approach. However, the simplicity and effectiveness of the proposed method make it a valuable contribution to the field, and the authors provide a clear and compelling argument for its use.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors provide more details on the experimental setup and the hyperparameters used for each task. Additionally, it would be useful to see more analysis of the results, including error bars or confidence intervals, to give a better sense of the variability in the performance of the proposed method.

I also have a few specific questions about the experimental setup. For the RNN-LM, is the LM trained to minimize classification error, or is it trained as a language model? Did you use the final hidden state as the representation, or the average of all hidden states? Additionally, one of the most widely used methods to represent documents now is to have a bidirectional LSTM and concatenate the final hidden states as the document representation. I think it would be useful to know how the proposed method compares to this approach for tasks such as document classification or sentiment analysis.

Overall, I think this is a strong paper that presents a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing. With a few minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, I believe it would be ready for publication.
