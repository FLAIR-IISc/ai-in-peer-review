This paper proposes learning document embeddings as a sum of the constituent word embeddings, which are jointly learned and randomly dropped out ('corrupted') during training. While the individual components of this model are not particularly novel, the resulting algorithm is an efficient method for learning document representations, achieving good empirical performance.

Joint training of word and document embeddings is not a new concept, nor is the idea of representing documents as the sum of their word embeddings (see, e.g., 'The Sum of Its Parts': Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is essentially traditional dropout applied to the input layer. When combined with the word2vec-style loss and training methods, this paper does not offer significant contributions on the novelty front.

However, the model excels in efficiency at generation time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than a complex inference step, as in Doc2Vec. Moreover, by design, the embedding captures salient global information about the document – specifically, the information that aids in local-context prediction. Given the simplicity of the model, its performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the lack of novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study. I recommend acceptance.
