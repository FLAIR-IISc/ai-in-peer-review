This paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The authors introduce a corruption model that randomly removes words from a document during learning, providing a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative ones to be close to zero. 

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons for this choice: the simplicity and efficiency of the model, and its good empirical performance on various tasks. 

The authors' approach is not particularly novel, as joint training of word and document embeddings, as well as the idea of enforcing the document to be represented by the sum of its word embeddings, have been explored in previous works. Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is essentially traditional dropout on the input layer. However, the combination of these components results in an efficient learning algorithm for document representation.

The model's efficiency is a significant advantage, requiring only an average of the word embeddings at generation time, rather than a complicated inference step as in Doc2Vec. Additionally, the embedding captures salient global information about the document, specifically the information that aids in local-context prediction. The performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is quite encouraging, considering the simplicity of the model.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide more analysis on the corruption mechanism and its impact on the learned embeddings. For instance, they could explore the effect of different corruption rates on the performance of the model and provide more insights into why the corruption mechanism is effective in suppressing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words.

Additionally, the authors could provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameter tuning process and the specific settings used for each task. This would help to increase the reproducibility of the results and provide a clearer understanding of the model's performance.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following points: 

1. How does the corruption rate affect the performance of the model, and is there an optimal corruption rate for different tasks?

2. Can the authors provide more insights into why the corruption mechanism is effective in suppressing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words?

3. How does the model's performance change when using different word embedding dimensions, and is there a trade-off between the embedding dimension and the model's performance?

4. Can the authors provide more details on the experimental setup, such as the hyperparameter tuning process and the specific settings used for each task?
