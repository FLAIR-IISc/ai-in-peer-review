This paper proposes a framework for creating document representations by averaging word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization term. This term favors informative or rare words, while forcing common words to be close to zero. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their method through experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, achieving lower error rates compared to baseline document embedding methods.

While the motivation behind finding an optimal way to encode a document into a vector is compelling, the paper lacks significant technical contributions. Most of the techniques employed are not novel, and the primary advantage of the proposed method lies in its simplicity and speed. To be convinced of the superiority of this approach for learning document representations, I would like to see strong results on a more diverse set of tasks.

Some clarifications are needed regarding the experimental setup. Specifically, for the RNN-LM baseline, it is unclear whether the language model was trained to minimize classification error or as a traditional language model. Additionally, was the final hidden state used as the representation, or was it the average of all hidden states?

It would also be beneficial to compare the proposed method to more widely used approaches, such as using a bidirectional LSTM and concatenating the final hidden states as the document representation. This comparison would be particularly useful for tasks like document classification or sentiment analysis, as it would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the proposed method's strengths and weaknesses.

Overall, while the paper presents an efficient and simple method for document representation, its lack of technical novelty and limited experimental evaluation make it difficult to assess its impact. Further experiments and comparisons to more established methods would strengthen the paper's contributions and validity.
