This paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The approach is well-motivated, building on the idea that linear operations on word embeddings learned by Word2Vec can sustain substantial amounts of syntactic and semantic meanings of a phrase or a sentence.

The paper's contribution lies in its simplicity and efficiency, allowing for fast training and generation of document representations. By jointly learning word and document embeddings, Doc2VecC captures salient global information about the document, specifically that information that aids in local-context prediction. The corruption mechanism, although not novel, serves as a form of regularization that favors informative or rare words while dampening the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words.

The experimental results demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, often outperforming more complex models. The efficiency of the model is also highlighted, requiring only an average of the word embeddings at generation time, unlike more complex models that require additional inference steps.

I recommend acceptance of this paper, despite the lack of novelty in its individual components. The simplicity, efficiency, and performance of Doc2VecC make it a valuable contribution to the field, and its presentation in this paper is clear and well-motivated.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide more insight into the effects of the corruption mechanism on the learned word embeddings and document representations. Additionally, exploring the impact of different corruption rates and its relationship to the model's performance could provide valuable insights.

Minor suggestions:

* In Section 3.1, the explanation of the corruption mechanism and its effects on the word embeddings could be clarified.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed comparison with other document representation learning algorithms, highlighting the advantages and disadvantages of each approach.
* The authors may consider providing more visualizations or examples to illustrate the quality of the learned document representations and their applications in downstream tasks.

Overall, the paper presents a well-motivated and efficient document representation learning framework, and with some minor revisions, it has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
