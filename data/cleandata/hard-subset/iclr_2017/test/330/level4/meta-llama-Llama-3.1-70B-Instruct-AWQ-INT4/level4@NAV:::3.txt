This paper presents a method for learning document embeddings by summing the embeddings of their constituent words, which are learned jointly and subjected to random dropout during training. Although the individual components of this model are not groundbreaking, they collectively yield an efficient algorithm for document representation that achieves strong empirical results.

The concept of joint training for word and document embeddings is well-established, and the idea of representing documents as the sum of their word embeddings has been explored previously (e.g., Lebret and Collobert's '"The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders'). Moreover, the corruption mechanism employed in this paper is essentially equivalent to traditional dropout applied to the input layer. When combined with the word2vec-style loss function and training methods, this work does not introduce significant novel concepts.

However, the proposed model excels in terms of efficiency at generation time, requiring only the averaging of word embeddings rather than a complex inference step, as seen in Doc2Vec. By design, the resulting embedding captures salient global information about the document, specifically the information that facilitates local-context prediction. Given its simplicity, the model's performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is noteworthy.

In conclusion, despite the lack of novelty, the model's simplicity, efficiency, and performance make it a valuable contribution worthy of broader dissemination and study. I recommend accepting this paper.
