This paper proposes a document embedding model that learns representations by jointly training word and document embeddings, where the latter is represented as a sum of the former. During training, the constituent word embeddings are randomly dropped out ('corrupted'). Although the individual components of this model are not particularly novel, the resulting algorithm is efficient for learning document representations and demonstrates good empirical performance.

The idea of jointly training word and document embeddings is not new, and previous work has explored representing documents as a sum of their word embeddings (e.g., "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders" by Lebret and Collobert). The corruption mechanism used in this paper is essentially traditional dropout applied to the input layer, combined with a word2vec-style loss function and training method. Consequently, the paper does not offer significant novelty.

However, the proposed model has several advantages. It is efficient during generation, requiring only the average of word embeddings, unlike more complex inference steps in models like Doc2Vec. By design, the embedding captures salient global information about the document, specifically the information that aids in local-context prediction. Despite its simplicity, the model's performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is encouraging.

In conclusion, although the paper lacks novelty, its simplicity, efficiency, and performance make it a worthy contribution to the field. I recommend acceptance, as it is likely to be of interest to a wider readership and deserving of further study.
