This paper presents a framework for creating document representations by representing a document as an average of its word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing common words to be close to zero. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification, achieving the lowest error rates compared to baseline document embedding methods.

I am inclined to reject this paper due to two main reasons. Firstly, the paper does not offer significant technical contributions, as most of the techniques presented are not new. The main selling point of the proposed method is its simplicity and speed. Secondly, the experimental evaluation is limited to only two tasks, and I would like to see good results for more tasks to be convinced that this is the best way to learn document representations.

I have several supporting arguments for my decision. While I appreciate the motivation behind finding the best way to encode a document into a vector, I am not convinced that the proposed method is the optimal solution. The authors' claim that their approach is more efficient than existing methods is not entirely clear, as the computational complexity of the proposed method is not thoroughly analyzed.

Furthermore, I have some questions regarding the experimental setup. For the RNN-LM baseline, is the language model trained to minimize classification error, or is it trained as a standard language model? Did the authors use the final hidden state as the representation, or the average of all hidden states? Additionally, I think it would be useful to know how the proposed method compares to more recent approaches, such as using a bidirectional LSTM and concatenating the final hidden states as the document representation, for tasks such as document classification or sentiment analysis.

In terms of additional feedback, I suggest that the authors provide more detailed information about the computational complexity of their proposed method and compare it to existing methods. Moreover, they should consider evaluating their approach on more tasks to demonstrate its generalizability. Finally, the authors could benefit from providing more insights into the interpretability of their proposed method, such as analyzing the learned word embeddings and document representations to understand what the model is capturing.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details about the computational complexity of your proposed method and compare it to existing methods?
* How does your proposed method compare to more recent approaches, such as using a bidirectional LSTM and concatenating the final hidden states as the document representation, for tasks such as document classification or sentiment analysis?
* Can you provide more insights into the interpretability of your proposed method, such as analyzing the learned word embeddings and document representations to understand what the model is capturing?
* Can you evaluate your approach on more tasks to demonstrate its generalizability?
