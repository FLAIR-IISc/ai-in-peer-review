This paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The approach is well-motivated by the observation that linear operations on word embeddings learned by Word2Vec can sustain substantial syntactic and semantic meanings of phrases and sentences. The corruption model introduced in Doc2VecC acts as a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while dampening the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The main reason for my decision is that despite the lack of novelty in individual components, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of the proposed model make it worthy of wider readership and study.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The results show that Doc2VecC outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms, including Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors. The paper also provides a detailed analysis of the corruption model and its effect on the word embeddings, demonstrating its ability to clamp down the representation of frequent but uninformative words.

One potential weakness of the paper is that it does not provide a thorough comparison with other document representation learning algorithms that use similar techniques, such as the work by Lebret and Collobert. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed model and potential avenues for future work.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors consider the following:

* Provide a more detailed comparison with other document representation learning algorithms that use similar techniques.
* Discuss the limitations of the proposed model and potential avenues for future work.
* Consider adding more analysis on the corruption model and its effect on the word embeddings, such as visualizing the word embeddings and analyzing the effect of different corruption rates.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the corruption model, such as the specific dropout rate used and the effect of different dropout rates on the performance of the model?
* How does the proposed model handle out-of-vocabulary words, and what are the implications for the corruption model?
* Can you provide more analysis on the effect of the corruption model on the word embeddings, such as visualizing the word embeddings and analyzing the effect of different corruption rates?
