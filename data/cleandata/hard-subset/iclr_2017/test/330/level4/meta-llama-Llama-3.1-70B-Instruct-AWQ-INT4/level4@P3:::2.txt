This paper presents a framework for creating document representations by representing a document as an average of its word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing common words to be close to 0. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach, called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), through experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, showing that it achieves lower error rates compared to baseline document embedding methods.

I recommend accepting this paper, with the primary reason being its simplicity and speed, which are significant advantages over existing methods. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated approach to document representation learning, and their experimental results are convincing.

To further support this decision, I provide the following arguments:

* The authors' approach is well-placed in the literature, building upon existing work on word embeddings and document representation learning.
* The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of Doc2VecC on multiple tasks, including sentiment analysis and document classification.
* The authors provide a thorough analysis of their approach, including an explanation of the data-dependent regularization and its effects on the word embeddings.

However, I do have some suggestions for improvement:

* To further establish the effectiveness of Doc2VecC, it would be beneficial to include results on additional tasks, such as semantic relatedness or question answering.
* The authors could provide more details on the training procedure for the RNN-LM baseline, including whether the LM is trained to minimize classification error or as a language model, and whether the final hidden state or the average of all hidden states is used as the representation.
* A comparison to the widely used approach of using a bidirectional LSTM and concatenating the final hidden states as the document representation would be useful for tasks such as document classification or sentiment analysis.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper:

* Can the authors provide more details on the corruption model used in Doc2VecC, including the probability distribution used to randomly remove words from the document?
* How does the choice of corruption rate q affect the performance of Doc2VecC, and what is the optimal value of q for different tasks?
* Can the authors provide more insights into the data-dependent regularization term and its effects on the word embeddings, including how it favors informative or rare words while forcing common words to be close to 0?
