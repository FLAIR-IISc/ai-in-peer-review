This paper proposes a method for computing vector representations for documents by using a skip-gram style learning mechanism with an added regularizer in the form of a global context vector with various bits of drop out. While none of the individual components proposed in this paper are new, I believe that the combination in this fashion is. Further, I appreciated the detailed analysis of model behavior in section 3.

I decide to accept this paper, with one key reason being that it presents an interesting and efficient algorithm for document representation learning. The paper's combination of a skip-gram style learning mechanism with a global context vector and dropout regularizer is a novel contribution, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the model's behavior.

My second key reason for accepting this paper is that it demonstrates strong performance on several tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. While I agree with the other reviewers that the empirical evaluation could be stronger, I believe that the results presented in the paper are sufficient to demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

One area for improvement is the empirical evaluation, which could be expanded to include more tasks and datasets. Additionally, the authors could provide more analysis of the model's behavior, such as ablation studies or sensitivity analyses. However, I do not believe that these limitations are sufficient to warrant rejection.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors consider adding more tasks and datasets to their empirical evaluation, as well as providing more analysis of the model's behavior. Additionally, the authors could consider using more robust baselines, as some of the baselines used in the paper are relatively weak.
