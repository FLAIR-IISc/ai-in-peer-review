This paper proposes learning document embedding as a sum of component word embeddings, which are jointly learned during training and randomly dropped ('corrupt'). Although no part of this model is particularly new, the results are an efficient learning algorithm for document representation with good empirical performance.

The joint training of word and document embeddings is not a new idea, nor is the idea of representing a document by the sum of its word embeddings (see, for example, "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebrite and Colbert). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is nothing more than traditional dropout at the input layer. Combined with Word2Vec-style loss and training methods, this paper offers very little on the front of novelty.

On the other hand, it is very efficient in terms of generation time, requiring only the average of word embeddings rather than a complex inference step like Doc2Vec. Moreover, by construction, the embedding captures important global information about the document – it specifically captures information that aids in local-context prediction. For such a simple model, the performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the lack of novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of a broad readership and study, and I recommend acceptance.
