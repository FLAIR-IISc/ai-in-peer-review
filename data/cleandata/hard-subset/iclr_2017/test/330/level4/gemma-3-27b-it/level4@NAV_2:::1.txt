This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as the average of word embeddings, incorporating a corruption model for regularization. The approach leverages the efficiency of Word2Vec while aiming to improve document representation quality. The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in word and document embeddings, and clearly articulates the rationale behind the proposed corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization. The detailed analysis of this mechanism in Section 3 is particularly insightful.

I recommend accepting this paper. While the empirical evaluation focuses on established tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification), the results demonstrate competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly considering the model’s simplicity and efficiency. The significant speed advantages, especially during test time, are a key strength.

The paper's main limitation lies in the scope of the empirical evaluation. The choice of tasks, while reasonable, doesn’t fully showcase the potential of the approach. The extensive use of t-SNE visualizations (occupying significant space) could have been replaced with more in-depth analysis or additional experiments. The comparison to baselines, while present, could be strengthened with more recent and competitive methods. Further, while the paper highlights efficiency, a more detailed discussion of computational resources and scalability would be beneficial.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 1) Consider evaluating Doc2VecC on more challenging tasks that better demonstrate its capabilities, such as topic modeling or information retrieval. 2) Provide a more thorough comparison to recent state-of-the-art document representation methods. 3) Elaborate on the computational cost and scalability of the approach, potentially including results on larger datasets. 4) Justify the choice of parameters, such as the corruption rate 'q', and explore their impact on performance. 5) Could the authors elaborate on the specific types of words that are most effectively regularized by the corruption mechanism – are there particular parts-of-speech or semantic categories where the effect is most pronounced?
