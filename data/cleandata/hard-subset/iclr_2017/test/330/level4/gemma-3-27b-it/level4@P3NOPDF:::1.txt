This paper introduces a method for learning document vector representations using a skip-gram model augmented with a global context vector and dropout regularization. While the individual components – skip-gram, global context vectors, and dropout – are well-established, the combination presented here offers a novel approach. I particularly appreciated the detailed analysis of model behavior provided in Section 3, which offers valuable insights into the learned representations.

The primary weakness of this submission lies in the scope of the empirical evaluation. The chosen tasks – sentiment analysis and k-way classification – feel somewhat limited, and do not fully demonstrate the potential of the proposed method. Exploring more challenging and diverse tasks would have strengthened the paper considerably. Furthermore, the extensive use of space dedicated to t-SNE visualizations (approximately two-thirds of a page) feels disproportionate; that space could have been more effectively used for additional experiments, analysis, or discussion of related work. 

I share the concerns raised by other reviewers regarding the lack of comparison to stronger, more recent baselines. The absence of these comparisons weakens the claim of the method’s superiority. However, despite these shortcomings, I believe this paper warrants acceptance. The algorithm itself is interesting and well-designed, demonstrably efficient, and offers a compelling combination of techniques. It is reasonable to anticipate that the ideas presented here will be of value to other researchers working in document representation learning, and the clear presentation of the method facilitates this. 

Specifically, the regularization strategy combining a global context vector and dropout appears promising, and the ablation studies in Section 3 help illuminate the contribution of each component. While a more comprehensive evaluation would be beneficial, the current results are sufficient to justify publication, particularly given the efficiency of the proposed method and the potential for future work building upon these foundations.
