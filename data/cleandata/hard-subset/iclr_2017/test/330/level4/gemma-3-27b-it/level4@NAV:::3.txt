This work introduces a method for learning document embeddings by representing them as the sum of their constituent word embeddings, trained jointly and with a dropout ('corruption') mechanism. Although the individual components – joint word and document embedding learning, sum-of-embeddings document representation, and dropout – have precedents in existing work (e.g., Lebret & Collobert’s “The Sum of Its Parts”), the combination yields an efficient algorithm for document representation, demonstrating strong empirical results.

The paper does not present substantial novelty; joint training and the sum-of-embeddings approach are established techniques, and the corruption method is effectively standard dropout applied to the input layer, alongside a word2vec-style training objective. 

However, a key advantage of this approach is its computational efficiency at inference time, requiring only averaging of word embeddings, unlike more complex models such as Doc2Vec. Furthermore, the model's construction ensures the embedding inherently encodes globally informative features relevant to local context prediction. Given this simplicity, the performance achieved on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is promising.

In conclusion, while lacking in originality, the model’s simplicity, efficiency, and encouraging performance warrant its dissemination to the research community, and I therefore recommend acceptance.
