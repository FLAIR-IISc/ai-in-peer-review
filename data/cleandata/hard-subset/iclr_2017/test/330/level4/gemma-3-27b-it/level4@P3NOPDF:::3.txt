This paper presents a method for learning document embeddings by representing each document as the sum of its constituent word embeddings. These word embeddings are jointly learned with the document embeddings and a dropout-based corruption mechanism is employed during training. While the individual components of this model – joint word and document embedding learning, representing documents as sums of word embeddings, and the use of dropout – are not novel in isolation, their combination results in an efficient and empirically effective algorithm for document representation.

The approach builds upon existing work. Jointly training word and document embeddings has been explored previously, as has the constraint of representing a document as the sum of its word embeddings (e.g., Lebret and Collobert’s “The Sum of Its Parts”). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism utilized is effectively standard dropout applied to the input layer. The paper leverages a word2vec-style loss function and training procedure, contributing limited novelty in terms of algorithmic innovation.

However, this simplicity is precisely where the method’s strength lies. The model is exceptionally efficient at inference time; generating a document embedding requires only averaging its word embeddings, a significant advantage over more complex approaches like Doc2Vec which necessitate a dedicated inference step. Moreover, the inherent construction of the model encourages the embedding to capture salient global document information – specifically, information relevant to local context prediction, as dictated by the training objective. Given this simplicity, the reported performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is notably encouraging. The results demonstrate a competitive performance despite the minimalistic design. 

In summary, while this work does not introduce groundbreaking new concepts, its combination of well-established techniques yields a compellingly simple, efficient, and performant method for document representation. The clarity of the approach and its practical benefits warrant wider attention within the research community. I recommend acceptance of this paper.
