This paper presents a method for learning document vector representations, Doc2VecC, combining a skip-gram-style learning approach with a novel corruption model acting as a regularizer. The core idea of representing a document as an average of word embeddings, enforced during learning, is compelling, and the detailed analysis of the corruption mechanism’s effect as a data-dependent regularization (Section 3.1) is a strong point of the paper. While the individual components aren't entirely novel, the combination and resulting efficiency are valuable contributions.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core idea is sound, the method is efficiently implemented, and the results, while not overwhelming, demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks. The efficiency gains alone are a noteworthy contribution.

The paper effectively tackles the problem of learning document representations in a computationally efficient manner. The motivation for Doc2VecC stems from the observation that simple averaging of word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning, a concept supported by previous work (Mikolov et al., 2013a, 2013b). The approach is well-motivated within the existing literature on word and document embeddings, building upon Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors while addressing their limitations – specifically, the large parameter space of Paragraph Vectors and the computational cost of generating representations for unseen documents.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The experimental results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness demonstrate that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The detailed ablation study in Section 4.3, analyzing the data-dependent regularization effect, further strengthens the claims. The reported speed improvements (Table 2) are significant and support the efficiency claims. The comparison to baselines, while as the authors acknowledge, somewhat soft, does demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

Here are some suggestions to further improve the paper:

*   **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** While the chosen tasks are standard, the paper could benefit from evaluation on more challenging datasets or tasks. Exploring tasks that require more nuanced semantic understanding could better highlight the strengths of Doc2VecC.
*   **Refine Visualization:** The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) are somewhat simplistic. Consider visualizing the embeddings in a way that better highlights the relationships between different document categories or semantic clusters. Perhaps a dimensionality reduction technique combined with a more informative labeling scheme could improve the insights gained from the visualization.
*   **Clarify Hyperparameter Choices:** The paper mentions hyperparameter tuning but lacks specifics. Detailing the hyperparameter search process and the final values used for each experiment would enhance reproducibility and provide further insight into the model's sensitivity.
*   **Discuss Limitations:** Explicitly discussing the limitations of the approach, such as potential biases introduced by the averaging mechanism or limitations in handling long documents, would strengthen the paper.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The corruption rate `q` is fixed at 0.9 throughout the experiments. Was any effort made to tune this parameter, and if so, what were the results? Does the optimal `q` vary across different datasets or tasks?
*   The data-dependent regularization described in Section 3.1 appears to be a key benefit of Doc2VecC. Could the authors provide examples of specific words whose embeddings were significantly impacted by this regularization, and how this impacts downstream task performance?
*   In the semantic relatedness experiments, Doc2VecC performs slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Could the authors discuss potential reasons for this difference and suggest avenues for future research to address this gap?
*   Is there any analysis of the impact of vocabulary size on the performance of Doc2VecC? Does the model's performance degrade significantly when dealing with very large vocabularies?
