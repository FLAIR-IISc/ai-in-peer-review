This paper presents a method for computing vector representations for documents using a skip-gram style learning mechanism with an added regularizer, implemented as a global context vector and dropout. While none of the individual components are novel, the combination is a worthwhile contribution. I particularly appreciated the detailed analysis of model behavior in Section 3.1.

The main limitation of this submission lies in its empirical evaluation. The choice of tasks – sentiment analysis and document classification – feels somewhat conventional, and stronger baselines could have provided a more rigorous comparison. Furthermore, the space devoted to t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) could have been better used for additional analysis or experimentation.

Despite these concerns regarding the evaluation, I recommend accepting this paper. It introduces an interesting and well-designed algorithm that is both efficient and clearly explained. The simplicity and efficiency of the method make it likely that other researchers will find the ideas presented here valuable. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the motivation for the proposed method, drawing on the success of Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors while addressing their limitations. The approach of averaging word embeddings, coupled with the corruption model, provides a compelling way to generate document representations efficiently. The theoretical justification for the corruption model as a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3.1) is a significant strength. The experimental results, while not groundbreaking, demonstrate competitive performance on standard benchmarks, and the efficiency gains are notable (Table 2). The ablation study regarding the data-dependent regularization (Section 4.3) further strengthens the claim of its benefit.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Baselines:** Including more challenging baselines, such as more recent state-of-the-art deep learning models for document classification and semantic similarity, would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation.
*   **Task Selection:** Exploring tasks that are more sensitive to document-level understanding, beyond sentiment analysis and classification, might reveal more nuanced strengths and weaknesses of the model.
*   **t-SNE Visualization:** While the t-SNE visualization provides some qualitative insight, it's not a strong argument on its own. Consider replacing it with a more quantitative analysis, such as examining the learned word embeddings in relation to known semantic relationships.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., the corruption rate *q*) would be helpful.
*   **Vocabulary Size**: Explain how vocabulary size affects performance.
*   **Computational Resources**: The experiments were run on a specific machine, consider reporting the RAM size.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the CBoW model specifically? Were other architectures (e.g., Skip-gram) explored, and if so, why was CBoW preferred?
*   The paper highlights the efficiency of Doc2VecC. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the training and inference processes compared to other methods?
*   In section 4.4, the Skip-thought vector model was not retrained. How much do you think this impacts the comparisons?
*   How does Doc2VecC perform on datasets with very long documents? Are there any specific considerations or adjustments needed for such cases?
*   The description of the data dependent regularization is detailed, however could you provide a more intuitive explanation of this mechanism?
