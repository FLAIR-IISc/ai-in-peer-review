This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a method for learning document embeddings by representing each document as the average of its word embeddings. These embeddings are learned jointly and a ‘corruption’ mechanism—randomly dropping out words—is applied during training. While the individual components aren’t novel, the resulting algorithm is efficient and achieves strong empirical performance.

The approach of jointly learning word and document embeddings, and representing a document as a sum of its word embeddings, has precedents in existing work (e.g., Lebret & Collobert’s “The Sum of Its Parts”). The corruption mechanism employed is also a standard technique—dropout—applied to the input layer. Consequently, the paper’s core contribution lies more in its combination of existing ideas than in groundbreaking novelty.

However, Doc2VecC offers significant advantages in terms of efficiency, particularly during inference. Generating document representations requires only averaging word embeddings, unlike more complex inference steps required by methods like Doc2Vec. Furthermore, the model inherently emphasizes global document information by learning embeddings that contribute to local context prediction. Given its simplicity, the performance achieved on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is impressive.

Overall, while lacking substantial novelty, the model’s simplicity, computational efficiency, and strong performance warrant broader attention. I recommend acceptance.

**Additional Feedback (not part of the decision):**

*   It would be helpful to include a more detailed comparison of the computational cost (training and inference) with other methods in terms of Big-O notation.
*   The authors mention data-dependent regularization. Providing a more intuitive explanation of how the corruption mechanism achieves this regularization would be valuable.
*   Regarding the word analogy experiments, the authors mention Paragraph Vectors performing poorly. A possible explanation for this could be the model’s focus on document-level information rather than fine-grained word relationships. Elaborating on this point would strengthen the discussion.
*   It would be interesting to see the impact of different dropout rates (the ‘q’ parameter) on the performance and learned embeddings. A brief ablation study could provide insights into the sensitivity of the model to this hyperparameter.
*   Could the authors comment on the potential limitations of averaging word embeddings, particularly for documents with nuanced meaning that depends heavily on word order or complex syntactic structures?
