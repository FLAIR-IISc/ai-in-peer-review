This paper introduces a framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, incorporating a novel data-dependent regularization technique. This regularization aims to emphasize informative and rare words while suppressing the influence of common words, effectively driving their corresponding embedding components towards zero. The authors demonstrate through experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification that their method achieves competitive, and in some cases, state-of-the-art performance compared to several baseline document embedding techniques.

The motivation behind finding an effective method for encoding documents into dense vector representations is sound. However, while the approach is conceptually clear and computationally efficient – representing a key strength – the paper's technical contributions feel incremental. The core components rely on established techniques, and the primary innovation lies in the specific combination and application of data-dependent regularization to a simple averaging scheme. Therefore, stronger empirical evidence is needed to substantiate the claim that this method represents a significant advance in the field. 

Currently, the evaluation is limited to two tasks. To more convincingly demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed approach, I recommend including results on a broader range of document representation benchmarks. This would help establish the method’s generalizability and robustness beyond the tested scenarios. 

Several details regarding the experimental setup require clarification. Specifically, concerning the RNN-LM baseline, it is crucial to understand the training objective. Was the language model trained directly to minimize classification error, or was it trained as a standard language model with a separate downstream classification layer? Furthermore, please clarify whether the document representation was derived from the final hidden state of the RNN, or from an average of hidden states across the sequence.

A particularly relevant comparison point, given current best practices, is the use of bidirectional LSTMs to generate document embeddings. Concatenating the final hidden states from a bidirectional LSTM is a widely adopted and strong-performing technique for document representation learning. I believe including a comparison to this approach, on tasks such as document classification and sentiment analysis, would significantly strengthen the paper and contextualize the performance of the proposed method. Understanding the relative performance—in terms of both accuracy *and* computational cost—would be especially valuable.

In summary, the paper presents a simple and efficient method for document representation learning with promising initial results. However, the limited scope of the evaluation and the incremental nature of the technical contributions raise concerns. Addressing the questions above and expanding the experimental validation would substantially improve the paper and increase confidence in the proposed approach.
