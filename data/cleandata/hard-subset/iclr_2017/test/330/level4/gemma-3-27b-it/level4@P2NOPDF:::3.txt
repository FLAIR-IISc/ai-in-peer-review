This paper introduces a method for learning document embeddings by representing each document as the sum of its constituent word embeddings. These word embeddings are learned jointly with the document embeddings and are randomly dropped out during training, a technique akin to dropout regularization. While the individual components of this model – joint word and document embedding learning, representing documents as sums of word embeddings, and the use of dropout – are not novel in isolation, their combination results in a computationally efficient algorithm for document representation that achieves strong empirical performance.

The approach builds on existing work. Jointly training word and document embeddings has been explored previously, as has the concept of representing documents as the sum of their word vectors (e.g., Lebret & Collobert, 2016, “The Sum of Its Parts”). The dropout mechanism employed is effectively standard dropout applied to the input layer. Consequently, the paper’s novelty is limited, particularly given its reliance on a word2vec-style loss function and training procedure. 

However, the model’s simplicity translates to significant efficiency during document embedding generation. It requires only the averaging of word embeddings, avoiding the complex inference procedures often needed in models like Doc2Vec. Importantly, the summation-based construction inherently encourages the embedding to capture salient global document information – specifically, information useful for predicting local word context. This is a valuable property. Considering its straightforward design, the performance achieved on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is notably encouraging. 

In summary, while this work does not present groundbreaking new ideas, its simplicity, computational efficiency, and competitive performance make it a valuable contribution. The model is well-suited for scenarios where fast document embedding generation is crucial. I recommend acceptance for publication and believe this work will be of interest to a broad audience within the representation learning community.
