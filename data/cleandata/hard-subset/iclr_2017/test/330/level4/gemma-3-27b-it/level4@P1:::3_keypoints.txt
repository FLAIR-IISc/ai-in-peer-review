- Proposes learning document embeddings as the sum of jointly learned word embeddings with random input dropout (corruption) during training.
- Components (joint word/doc embedding, summation, dropout, Word2Vec-style loss) are not novel, so overall novelty is limited.
- Model is very efficient at test time, requiring only averaged word embeddings rather than complex inference like Paragraph Vectors.
- Embeddings capture salient global document information useful for local-context prediction.
- Empirical results are strong on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
- Despite limited novelty, simplicity, efficiency, and performance justify acceptance and wider readership.
- Addresses an important problem and offers a scalable alternative to methods with heavy inference or post-processing.
- Authors show the method matches or outperforms state-of-the-art approaches and demonstrates significant test-time efficiency gains.
- Argues corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that improves performance, supported by results.
- Suggest clarifying connections to prior work on averaging word embeddings and explicitly differentiating contributions.
- Recommend adding an ablation study to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism (performance without corruption).
- Ask for discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly the corruption rate q and its optimal range or robustness.
- Request more details on computational resources used in experiments (CPU cores, GPU model).
- Questions for authors: why does corruption favor informative words, how do different dropout rates affect performance, and how does Doc2VecC compare to transformer-based document embeddings?
