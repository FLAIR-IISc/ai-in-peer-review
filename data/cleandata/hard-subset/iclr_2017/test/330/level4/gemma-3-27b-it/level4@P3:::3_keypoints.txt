- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as the average of jointly learned word embeddings with a dropout-inspired corruption mechanism.  
- Novelty: Individual components are not new, but their combination yields a simple, efficient, and effective method.  
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends accept due to simplicity, efficiency, and competitive empirical performance.  
- Motivation: Addresses sparsity and semantic limits of bag-of-words and test-time inefficiency of Paragraph Vectors.  
- Theoretical intuition: Builds on Word2Vec and linear combination of embeddings; corruption framed as data-dependent regularization (dropout-like).  
- Related work: Acknowledges similar averaging approaches (e.g., Lebret & Collobert, 2015) and calls for clearer contrast.  
- Empirical results: Strong performance across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness; often matches or outperforms baselines.  
- Ablation and efficiency: Ablation validates the corruption effect; training time comparisons highlight fast inference.  
- Suggestion — novelty discussion: Paper should more explicitly compare and contrast with other averaging-based methods.  
- Suggestion — corruption explanation: Provide a simpler, more accessible intuition alongside mathematical derivation.  
- Suggestion — hyperparameter sensitivity: Discuss sensitivity to corruption rate q and report experiments with different q values.  
- Suggestion — error analysis: Include qualitative examples where Doc2VecC fails to reveal limitations.  
- Suggestion — Skip-thought clarification: Elaborate on why Skip-thought vectors performed poorly beyond paragraph length considerations.  
- Question — subsampling: Why doesn’t Doc2VecC need Word2Vec-style frequent-word subsampling — is it solely due to data-dependent regularization?  
- Question — Paragraph Vectors: Why did Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on word analogy tasks — could training procedure or hyperparameters be responsible?  
- Question — hardware: Request details on hardware used (CPU/GPU) for computational efficiency claims.
