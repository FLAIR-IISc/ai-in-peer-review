This paper presents a framework for learning document representations. The central idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, incorporating a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while suppressing common words. Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification demonstrate that the proposed method achieves competitive or state-of-the-art error rates compared to baseline document embedding methods. While the motivation for finding an effective way to encode a document into a vector representation is sound, the paper’s technical contributions appear incremental. The primary advantage highlighted is the simplicity and speed of the proposed method. Therefore, I would appreciate seeing results on a broader range of tasks to strengthen the claim that this is a particularly effective approach for learning document representations.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a reasonable approach and demonstrates competitive performance, the lack of substantial novelty and limited scope of experiments do not warrant acceptance at this time.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper builds upon well-established techniques like Word2Vec and denoising autoencoders. The core contribution – averaging word embeddings with a corruption-based regularization – feels more like a well-engineered application of existing concepts rather than a fundamentally new approach. Although the authors demonstrate good results on sentiment analysis and document classification, these tasks are relatively standard benchmarks. The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating performance on a more diverse set of tasks or by presenting a deeper theoretical analysis of the proposed regularization method. The claim of significantly outperforming existing methods is not fully convincing with the current experimental setup.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The writing is generally clear, but some phrases could be more concise.
*   The connection between the corruption mechanism and the data-dependent regularization could be explained more intuitively.
*   The ablation study demonstrating the effect of the corruption model (Table 3) is helpful.
*   The discussion of related work is thorough and provides good context.
*   The authors mention comparing against a bidirectional LSTM but do not include it in the results. Adding this comparison would be beneficial.
*   The visualization in Figure 3 is useful for understanding the learned representations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   For the RNN-LM baseline, was the language model trained specifically to minimize classification error, or was it trained as a general language model? If trained as a language model, was the final hidden state or an average of all hidden states used as the document representation?
*   A widely used and strong baseline for document representation is a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states. How does the proposed method compare to this approach on tasks such as document classification or sentiment analysis?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the corruption rate *q*? Was a sensitivity analysis performed to determine the optimal value?
*   The paper claims Doc2VecC does not rely on the subsampling trick used in Word2Vec+AVG. However, the experiments still apply it. Is the performance significantly affected if the subsampling is removed from *all* methods in the comparison?
*   The results on the semantic relatedness task (SICK dataset) show slightly worse performance than LSTM-based methods. Could you discuss the potential reasons for this and whether further tuning or modifications could improve performance on this task?
