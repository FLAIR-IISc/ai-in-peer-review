This paper proposes learning document embeddings as a sum of the constituent word embeddings, which are jointly learned and randomly dropped out ('corrupted') during training. While none of the pieces of this model are particularly novel, the result is an efficient learning algorithm for document representation with good empirical performance.

Joint training of word and document embeddings is not a new idea, nor is the idea of enforcing the document to be represented by the sum of its word embeddings (see, e.g., '"The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders' by Lebret and Collobert). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is analogous to traditional dropout applied to the input layer. Coupled with the Word2Vec-style loss and training methods, this paper offers limited novelty.

However, the model is very efficient at generation time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than a complicated inference step as in Paragraph Vectors. Moreover, the model’s construction ensures the embedding captures salient global information about the document – specifically, information that aids in local-context prediction. Given its simplicity, the performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is encouraging.

Overall, despite the incremental novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study, and I recommend acceptance. I would be interested to see a more detailed ablation study regarding the contribution of the corruption mechanism. Specifically, how does performance degrade when the dropout rate is set to zero? Also, it would be helpful to better characterize the types of documents/tasks where this approach is most advantageous compared to more complex methods like Skip-thought Vectors or LSTM-based approaches, as the results suggest some task dependence.
