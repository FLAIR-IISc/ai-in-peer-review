This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that leverages the simplicity of averaging word embeddings while incorporating a corruption model to enhance performance. The approach builds on established techniques like Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors but distinguishes itself through its efficiency and data-dependent regularization. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the model’s properties, particularly regarding the role of the corruption mechanism as a regularizer.

I recommend accepting this paper. Despite not presenting radically novel components, the combination of techniques is well-motivated and the resulting algorithm is both efficient and effective. The detailed theoretical analysis in Section 3 is a significant strength.

The primary concern lies in the empirical evaluation. While the results are competitive, the chosen tasks – sentiment analysis and document classification – feel somewhat conventional and do not fully showcase the potential of the method. The extensive use of t-SNE visualizations (occupying significant space) feels less impactful than additional, more rigorous experimentation could have been. The comparison to baselines, while present, could be stronger, particularly with more recent or competitive methods. The paper demonstrates good performance, but a broader evaluation would have bolstered the claims.

To further improve the paper, consider the following:

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Explore more challenging tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification. Consider tasks requiring a deeper understanding of semantic relationships or reasoning.
*   **Strengthen baseline comparisons:** Include comparisons to more recent and state-of-the-art document representation methods, and provide a more detailed analysis of why Doc2VecC outperforms them.
*   **Refine the visualization section:** Replace the extensive t-SNE plots with a more focused analysis. Perhaps explore visualizations that reveal *why* Doc2VecC generates better representations (e.g., embedding spaces clustered by specific semantic features).
*   **Clarify the role of the corruption rate (q):** While mentioned, a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different 'q' values on performance would be beneficial.
*   **Discuss limitations:** A dedicated section outlining the limitations of Doc2VecC would add to the paper’s completeness. For example, are there types of documents or datasets where it might underperform?

I have several questions for the authors:

*   The paper highlights the efficiency of Doc2VecC. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (training and inference) compared to other methods, potentially with runtime graphs?
*   How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the quality of the underlying word embeddings? Do you observe different results using different pre-trained word embedding models (e.g., GloVe, FastText)?
*   The corruption mechanism seems crucial. Have you explored alternative corruption strategies beyond the random mask-out approach described in the paper?
*   Could you elaborate on why Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the word analogy task? Is this behavior consistent across different datasets and embedding dimensions?
*   The paper mentions "data-dependent regularization". Can you provide more intuitive examples of how this regularization affects the learned word embeddings for different types of words (e.g., frequent vs. rare, informative vs. non-discriminative)?
