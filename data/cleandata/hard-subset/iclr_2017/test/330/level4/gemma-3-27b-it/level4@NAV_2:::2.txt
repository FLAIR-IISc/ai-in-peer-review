This paper presents a framework for document representation learning, Doc2VecC. The core idea is to represent each document as the average of its constituent word embeddings, incorporating a corruption model to introduce a data-dependent regularization. This regularization favors informative or rare words while suppressing the embeddings of common, less discriminative terms. The authors demonstrate through experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance.

I recommend rejecting the paper in its current form. While the approach is well-motivated and the results are promising, the technical novelty is limited, and the empirical evaluation, while thorough, does not sufficiently demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods to warrant acceptance at a highly selective conference.

The paper effectively summarizes related work and clearly articulates the motivation for Doc2VecC, building on the success of Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The proposed method addresses limitations of previous approaches, specifically the scalability issues of Paragraph Vectors and the need for post-processing in methods like Word2Vec+IDF. The experiments are well-designed and compare Doc2VecC against a reasonable set of baselines across multiple tasks. The reported performance gains are generally statistically significant, and the analysis of the data-dependent regularization is insightful. The efficiency gains, both in training and inference, are also noteworthy.

Despite these strengths, the technical contribution feels incremental. The core idea of averaging word embeddings is not entirely novel, and the corruption model, while effective, builds upon established regularization techniques. To strengthen the paper, the authors should provide a more compelling demonstration of the benefits of Doc2VecC, ideally through more extensive experimentation or a deeper theoretical analysis. Specifically, a comparison to strong, current baselines like bidirectional LSTMs would be beneficial.

Several questions remain that, if addressed, could improve my understanding and potentially increase my confidence in the assessment. For the RNNLM baseline, was the language model trained to directly minimize classification error, or was it trained solely as a language model? If the latter, was the final hidden state, or an average of all hidden states, used as the document representation? Furthermore, the paper does not compare against a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states – a widely used and often highly effective document representation technique. How does Doc2VecC compare to this approach on the document classification and sentiment analysis tasks? Finally, could the authors elaborate on the impact of the corruption rate *q*? Was the value of 0.9 chosen through extensive hyperparameter tuning, or is the method relatively insensitive to this parameter?
