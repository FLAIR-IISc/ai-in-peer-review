This paper proposes a document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents documents as the average of their word embeddings, learned jointly and with a dropout-inspired ‘corruption’ mechanism during training. While the individual components of this model are not novel, the resulting approach offers an efficient and effective method for generating document representations with strong empirical performance.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. Despite lacking significant novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and competitive performance of Doc2VecC make it a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The model’s practical advantages, particularly its fast inference time, are noteworthy.

The paper tackles the problem of learning effective document representations. Existing methods like bag-of-words suffer from sparsity and lack of semantic understanding, while more recent neural approaches like Paragraph Vectors can be computationally expensive, especially at test time. Doc2VecC addresses these issues by proposing a simple yet effective model that leverages the power of word embeddings and introduces a data-dependent regularization through its corruption mechanism.

The approach is well-motivated. The authors build on the success of Word2Vec and the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings can capture semantic relationships. They correctly identify the limitations of Paragraph Vectors in terms of scalability and test-time efficiency. The connection to dropout and data-dependent regularization is clearly explained and strengthens the motivation behind the corruption model. While similar ideas have been explored (e.g., averaging word embeddings as in Lebret & Collobert, 2015), this work presents a compelling combination of techniques and demonstrates strong empirical results.

The paper supports its claims with a comprehensive set of experiments. The authors compare Doc2VecC against several strong baselines on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The results consistently show that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods, particularly in terms of efficiency. The ablation study examining the effect of the corruption mechanism further validates the authors’ hypothesis. The inclusion of training time comparisons is particularly helpful.

Here are some suggestions to further improve the paper:

*   **Novelty Discussion:** While I recommend acceptance, the paper could benefit from a more nuanced discussion of related work. Acknowledging and contrasting Doc2VecC more explicitly with other averaging-based approaches (beyond just citing them) would strengthen the contribution. Specifically, elaborating on *how* Doc2VecC differs and improves upon those approaches would be beneficial.
*   **Corruption Mechanism Explanation:** The explanation of the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization is good, but could be made even more accessible. Perhaps a simpler intuitive explanation alongside the more mathematical derivation would be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., the corruption rate *q*) would be valuable. Were different values of *q* explored? If so, what were the results?
*   **Error Analysis:** Providing some qualitative error analysis (e.g., examples of documents where Doc2VecC performs poorly) could offer insights into the model's limitations and potential areas for future work.
*   **Clarify "Skip-thought Vectors" Performance:** The paper notes Skip-thought Vectors performed "surprisingly poorly". Further elaboration on potential reasons for this—beyond paragraph length—would be helpful. Was the pre-trained model specifically suited to this type of data?

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The paper mentions that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on the frequent word subsampling technique used in Word2Vec+AVG. Can you provide further intuition as to *why* this is the case? Is it solely due to the data-dependent regularization, or are there other contributing factors?
*   In the word analogy experiments, Paragraph Vectors performed poorly. Is this consistent with the authors’ expectations? Could this be due to the specific training procedure or hyperparameter settings used for Paragraph Vectors in this study?
*   Regarding computational efficiency, can you provide more details about the hardware used for the experiments? Specifically, the CPU and GPU (if any) used.
