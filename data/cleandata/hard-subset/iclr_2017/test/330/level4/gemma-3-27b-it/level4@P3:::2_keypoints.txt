- Proposes Doc2VecC: document = average of word embeddings plus a corruption model that induces data-dependent regularization favoring rare/informative words and suppressing common terms.  
- Demonstrates competitive or state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness while being computationally efficient.  
- Recommendation: Reject — contribution seen as incremental; averaging embeddings is not novel and corruption builds on existing regularization, with only marginal empirical gains.  
- Motivation and literature placement are clear, linking Word2Vec limitations to the need for scalable document representations.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough across three tasks and multiple baselines, with highlighted test-time efficiency gains.  
- Improvements over existing methods are often small, making the SOTA claims feel overstated.  
- Needs a detailed ablation study to isolate the effects of corruption vs. data-dependent regularization and to test different corruption rates.  
- Should compare to stronger, more recent baselines (e.g., BiLSTM+attention, BERT/RoBERTa fine-tuned) to better assess competitiveness.  
- Recommend qualitative analysis of learned embeddings to show whether rare/informative words are indeed more distinct.  
- A more rigorous theoretical justification of the corruption mechanism/data-dependent regularization is needed.  
- Report sensitivity to hyperparameters (embedding dimension, learning rate, corruption rate) to assess robustness.  
- Clarify RNN-LM baseline setup: was it trained for classification or as a standard LM, and which hidden state(s) were used as document rep.  
- Explain how the method reduces to a Denoising Autoencoder when context words are removed and why context words are critical given typical DEA reconstruction objectives.  
- Provide examples of common words whose embeddings are suppressed by corruption and explain how that suppression aids downstream performance.  
- Supply a detailed computational comparison (training time, memory) with other methods, particularly pre-trained contextual models.
