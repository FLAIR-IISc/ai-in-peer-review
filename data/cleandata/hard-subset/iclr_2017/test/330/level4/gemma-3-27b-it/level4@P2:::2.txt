This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, incorporating a corruption model that acts as a data-dependent regularization. This regularization encourages informative and rare words to have stronger embeddings while suppressing those of common, less discriminative words. The authors demonstrate strong performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, achieving competitive error rates compared to existing document embedding methods.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonable approach to document representation learning, its technical contributions are incremental. The method’s primary advantage appears to be its simplicity and efficiency, but this is insufficient to warrant acceptance given the existing landscape of document embedding techniques. More compelling results across a wider range of tasks are needed to demonstrate a clear advantage over established methods.

The paper is well-motivated, building on the success of Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous approaches, particularly the computational cost and parameter growth associated with methods like Paragraph Vectors. The proposed approach addresses these issues by offering a simpler, more scalable alternative. The intuition behind the corruption model – to emphasize informative words – is also sound and aligns with principles of regularization.

The experimental results demonstrate that Doc2VecC performs favorably compared to several baselines on sentiment analysis and document classification. However, the comparison is limited. The authors acknowledge the prominence of bidirectional LSTMs for document representation, yet a comparison to this widely used approach is missing. The performance gains, while present, are not substantial enough to justify a significant departure from current best practices. Furthermore, the detailed analysis of the regularization effect (e.g., Table 3) is helpful but not groundbreaking. The experiments, while thorough, don’t provide enough evidence to establish Doc2VecC as a superior method.

Here are some suggestions for improving the paper:

*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include a comparison to bidirectional LSTM-based document representations, a current state-of-the-art approach. Consider additional tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification, such as topic modeling or information retrieval.
*   **Clarify experimental setup:** For the RNN-LM baseline, specify whether the language model was trained to directly optimize classification performance or as a standard language model. Clarify whether the document representation was derived from the final hidden state or an average of all hidden states.
*   **Ablation study:** Investigate the impact of the corruption mechanism more thoroughly. Quantify the performance difference with and without corruption to better understand its contribution.
*   **Theoretical justification:** While the paper provides an intuitive explanation of the regularization effect, a more formal theoretical analysis would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Discussion of limitations:** Explicitly address the potential limitations of the approach, such as its sensitivity to the quality of the underlying word embeddings.



I have the following questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the corruption rate *q*?
*   Could you provide more details on the computational resources used for the experiments? Specifically, how long did it take to train Doc2VecC and the baselines on the larger datasets?
*   The paper states that Doc2VecC "implicitly introduces a data-dependent regularization." Is this regularization solely due to the corruption mechanism, or are there other factors at play?
*   Given that Doc2VecC relies on averaging word embeddings, how does it handle negation or other forms of contextual nuance that might be lost through simple averaging?
