This paper introduces a method for learning document embeddings by representing them as the sum of their constituent word embeddings, learned jointly and with random dropout ('corruption') applied during training. Although each component of this model – joint word and document embedding learning, representing documents as sums of word embeddings, and the use of dropout – has appeared in prior work, the combination yields an efficient algorithm for document representation that achieves strong empirical performance. 

The joint training of word and document embeddings is not novel (e.g., Lebret and Collobert’s '"The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders' demonstrates a similar approach), and representing a document as the sum of its word embeddings is also a previously explored concept. Furthermore, the proposed corruption mechanism is effectively standard dropout applied to the input layer. Combined with a word2vec-style loss function and training procedure, the paper presents limited novelty.

However, the model is computationally efficient at inference time, requiring only the average of word embeddings instead of a more complex inference process, as is often the case with Doc2Vec. Crucially, the embedding inherently captures salient global document information, specifically that which is useful for local-context prediction. Given its simplicity, the performance observed on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is notably encouraging.

Overall, despite its lack of significant novelty, the model’s simplicity, efficiency, and strong performance warrant broader attention, and I recommend acceptance.
