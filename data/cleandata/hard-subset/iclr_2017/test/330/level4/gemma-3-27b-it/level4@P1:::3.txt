This paper proposes learning document embeddings as a sum of the constituent word embeddings, which are jointly learned and randomly dropped out (‘corrupted’) during training. While none of the components of this model are particularly novel, the result is an efficient learning algorithm for document representation with strong empirical performance.

Jointly training word and document embeddings is not a new idea, nor is representing a document as the sum of its word embeddings (e.g., “The Sum of Its Parts”: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is simply dropout applied to the input layer. Coupled with the Word2Vec-style loss and training methods, this paper offers limited novelty.

However, the model is very efficient at generation time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than a complex inference step as in Paragraph Vectors. Moreover, by design, the embedding captures salient global information about the document – specifically, information that aids in local-context prediction. For such a simple model, the performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is encouraging.

Overall, despite the limited novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study, and I recommend acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper addresses the important problem of learning effective document representations. While many approaches exist, they often suffer from scalability issues (Paragraph Vectors) or rely on complex post-processing steps (Word2Vec + IDF). Doc2VecC provides a compelling alternative that is both computationally efficient and achieves competitive performance. 

The authors demonstrate that their method matches or outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The efficiency gains, particularly at test time, are significant. The explanation for the good performance – that the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer – is plausible and supported by the results.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally well-written, a few areas could be improved:

*   **Clarify the connection to existing work:** The authors could more explicitly acknowledge the related work on averaging word embeddings for document representation and clearly differentiate their contributions (primarily the corruption mechanism and the theoretical justification).
*   **Ablation Study:** It would be helpful to include an ablation study to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism. What is the performance of the model *without* corruption?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the model to the corruption rate *q*. Is there an optimal range, or is the model robust to different values?
*   **Computational Resources:** Provide more specific details about the computational resources used in the experiments (e.g., number of CPU cores, GPU model).

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on why the corruption mechanism leads to a data-dependent regularization that favors informative words? Is there an intuitive explanation for this effect?
*   Have you investigated the impact of different dropout rates (q) on performance? 
*   How does the performance of Doc2VecC compare to more recent and sophisticated document embedding models (e.g., those based on transformers)?
