This paper presents a novel and efficient framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization. This regularization favors informative and rare words while suppressing the impact of common, less-discriminative terms. The authors demonstrate strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results while maintaining computational efficiency.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonable approach and demonstrates competitive results, the contribution feels incremental rather than groundbreaking. The core idea—averaging word embeddings—is not novel, and the corruption mechanism, while effective, builds upon established regularization techniques. The empirical evaluation, while thorough, does not conclusively demonstrate a significant advantage over existing methods to justify publication at a competitive venue like ICLR.

The paper effectively tackles the problem of learning document representations, motivated by the limitations of bag-of-words models and the computational cost of more complex approaches like Paragraph Vectors. The authors correctly identify the need for a scalable and efficient method that can capture document semantics. The approach is well-placed within the literature, building on the successes of Word2Vec and addressing its limitations in the document representation context. The connection to data-dependent regularization is clearly articulated and provides a compelling justification for the corruption mechanism.

The empirical evaluation is a strength of the paper, with experiments conducted on three distinct tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness) and comparisons made to a variety of baseline methods. The reported results are generally favorable, with Doc2VecC matching or outperforming state-of-the-art methods in many cases. The efficiency gains, particularly in test time, are also highlighted effectively. However, the improvements over existing methods are often marginal, and the paper lacks a convincing demonstration of significant superiority. The claim of outperforming state-of-the-art methods feels somewhat overstated given the incremental nature of the technical contribution.

Here are some suggestions for improving the paper:

*   **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study would be beneficial to understand the individual contributions of different components of Doc2VecC. Specifically, what is the impact of the corruption mechanism *without* the data-dependent regularization? How does the performance change with different corruption rates?
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** The authors should include a comparison to more recent and powerful baselines, such as bidirectional LSTMs with attention mechanisms or pre-trained contextualized word embeddings (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa) fine-tuned for document representation. This would provide a more robust assessment of the method's strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Analysis of Learned Embeddings:** A qualitative analysis of the learned word embeddings could provide further insights into the effectiveness of the corruption mechanism and the data-dependent regularization. Are the embeddings of rare/informative words indeed more distinct?
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While the authors provide an intuitive explanation for the corruption mechanism as a form of regularization, a more rigorous theoretical analysis would strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameter settings (e.g., embedding dimension, learning rate, corruption rate) would be valuable.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   For the RNN-LM baseline, was the language model trained to directly optimize classification performance (e.g., using a multi-task learning objective), or was it trained as a standard language model? If the latter, was the final hidden state or an average of all hidden states used as the document representation?
*   The paper mentions that Doc2VecC reduces to a Denoising Autoencoder (DEA) if context words are removed. However, DEA typically uses a more complex reconstruction objective. Can you elaborate on the differences between this simplified DEA and typical DEA implementations, and why the context words are critical for performance?
*   Could you provide some examples of common words whose embeddings are effectively suppressed by the corruption mechanism, and explain how this contributes to improved performance on the downstream tasks?
*   The paper highlights the speed of training and inference. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of computational costs (training time, memory usage) with other methods, particularly those using pre-trained models?
