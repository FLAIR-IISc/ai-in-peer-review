- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings learned jointly with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training.  
- Corruption acts as implicit, data-dependent regularization that downweights common non-informative words and improves embedding quality.  
- Matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Significant computational advantages: much faster training and test-time inference since document vectors are simple averages with no document-specific inference.  
- Strong empirical evidence: 15% improvement over BoW on sentiment (Table 1), lowest Wikipedia classification error (30.24% at h=1000, Table 5), and clearer clusters in t-SNE (Figure 3).  
- Exceptional test-time efficiency example: 25,000 IMDB reviews in 7s vs Paragraph Vectors’ 4m17s (Table 2).  
- Thorough empirical validation including ablation studies on corruption rates and regularization effects.  
- Novelty is modest: builds on known ideas (word embedding compositionality and dropout) but offers a meaningful practical integration that removes document-specific parameters and inference.  
- Recommendation: accept, based on strong empirical results and practical simplicity/efficiency despite limited theoretical novelty.  
- Suggested improvements: explicitly clarify the novelty as an efficiency/implementation contribution in abstract/introduction.  
- Suggested improvements: provide a direct comparison with the non-corrupted "sum of word embeddings" baseline (Huang et al., 2012) to quantify corruption benefits.  
- Suggested improvements: explain/justify why corruption rate q=0.9 was chosen and discuss sensitivity.  
- Clarification requested: detail how the corruption-induced regularization specifically affects semantic properties (e.g., analogies) versus standard Word2Vec.  
- Clarification requested: analyze how paragraph length distributions influence performance (e.g., why Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought on IMDB but not on SICK) to delineate suitable application scenarios.
