

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel framework for document representation learning that represents each document as a simple average of its word embeddings, enhanced by a data-dependent corruption mechanism. The approach enforces document semantics to be captured through averaging during training, while the corruption model—randomly removing words during learning—introduces regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words and emphasizes rare, informative ones. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while offering significant efficiency gains in both training (billions of words per hour) and test-time inference (seconds for 25k documents).

**Decision: Reject**  
The paper lacks substantial technical novelty, and the empirical validation is insufficient to justify its claims.  

**Key Reasons**  
1. **Limited Technical Novelty**: The core idea—averaging word embeddings for document representation—is well-established (e.g., Huang et al., 2012; Socher et al., 2013). The corruption mechanism, while clever, resembles existing regularization techniques (e.g., dropout in denoising autoencoders, Chen et al., 2014) and does not introduce a fundamentally new theoretical contribution. The paper fails to clearly distinguish how Doc2VecC’s regularization differs in *mechanism* or *effect* from prior work.  
2. **Insufficient Empirical Scope**: The results are confined to three tasks (sentiment analysis, classification, semantic relatedness), but the strongest baselines are missing. Crucially, the paper does not compare against the *dominant* modern approach for document representation: bidirectional LSTMs with concatenated final hidden states (e.g., Tai et al., 2015; Zhang & LeCun, 2015), which has become the standard for tasks like sentiment analysis and classification. Without this comparison, the claim that Doc2VecC "matches or outperforms state-of-the-art" is unsubstantiated.  

**Supporting Arguments**  
- The corruption model’s theoretical justification (Section 3.1) is derivative; the regularization effect is analogous to dropout in logistic regression (Wager et al., 2013), and the paper does not demonstrate *why* this specific form is superior to existing methods.  
- The results on semantic relatedness (Table 6) show Doc2VecC underperforms LSTM-based methods, yet the paper does not discuss this gap or position its approach relative to the dominant paradigm. The claim of "state-of-the-art" performance is thus misleading without direct comparison to bidirectional LSTMs.  
- The efficiency claims (e.g., "billions of words per hour") are plausible but unremarkable for a simple model like Word2Vec; the paper does not quantify *how much faster* Doc2VecC is than baselines like Paragraph Vectors (which scales poorly with corpus size) or modern neural networks.  

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, address these points:  
1. **Add a comparison to bidirectional LSTMs** for sentiment analysis and document classification. Use the standard protocol (final hidden states concatenated) and report error rates. This is critical to validate the "state-of-the-art" claim.  
2. **Clarify the RNN-LM implementation**: The paper states "supervised RNN-LM" but does not specify whether it was trained for classification (e.g., with a softmax layer on top) or as a language model. Was the final hidden state used as the document representation? If trained as a language model, how does it relate to the document representation task?  
3. **Justify the corruption rate (q=0.9)**: The paper states Doc2VecC is "not sensitive to q," but this requires deeper analysis. How does performance vary with q (e.g., q=0.5 vs. q=0.9)? What is the theoretical basis for choosing q=0.9?  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. How does Doc2VecC compare to bidirectional LSTM-based document representations (e.g., Tai et al., 2015) on the IMDB sentiment analysis task? Specifically, what error rates would you report for a model using concatenated final hidden states?  
2. For the RNN-LM baseline, was the model trained *for classification* (with a task-specific head) or as a *language model*? If the latter, how was the document representation derived from the language model output?  

These additions would clarify the paper’s contribution and provide evidence for the efficiency/performance claims. As written, the work presents a useful but incremental approach without sufficient novelty or empirical rigor to warrant acceptance.
