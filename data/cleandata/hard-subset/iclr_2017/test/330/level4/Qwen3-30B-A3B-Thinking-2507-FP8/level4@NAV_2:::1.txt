

The paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel framework for document representation learning that leverages a corruption-based regularization mechanism during training. It represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with the corruption model (random word removal) implicitly enforcing data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while preserving informative ones. The approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks with significantly improved efficiency in both training and inference compared to existing methods.

I recommend acceptance with two key reasons: (1) The integration of corruption-based regularization into a simple averaging framework constitutes a meaningful and novel combination of established techniques, yielding efficient and high-performing document representations; (2) The model’s computational advantages—training on billions of words per hour on a single machine and instant test-time inference—make it highly practical for real-world applications.

The supporting arguments are as follows: The paper thoroughly demonstrates Doc2VecC’s superiority over strong baselines (BoW, DEA, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought) across multiple tasks, with statistically significant gains in accuracy (e.g., 15% over BoW in sentiment analysis) and efficiency (4-minute inference for Paragraph Vectors vs. 7 seconds for Doc2VecC). The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 rigorously establishes how corruption acts as data-dependent regularization, explaining the empirical success without relying on heuristics like IDF weighting. Crucially, the model’s simplicity (decoupled complexity from corpus size) and strong performance on diverse tasks validate its contribution as a practical and effective alternative to more complex approaches.

For further improvement, consider: (1) Expanding empirical evaluation to include more diverse tasks beyond sentiment classification (e.g., topic modeling or cross-lingual transfer) to strengthen claims about generalizability; (2) Reducing the t-SNE visualization space (currently two-thirds of a page) to dedicate more room to analyzing the regularization mechanism’s impact on word embedding quality across different vocabulary frequencies. These additions would provide deeper insights into the model’s broader applicability.

To clarify the evaluation scope: Could the authors elaborate on why sentiment analysis and document classification were prioritized over other standard NLP tasks (e.g., topic modeling or entity recognition), and how the corruption rate q=0.9 was selected given its sensitivity to model performance? Additionally, does the data-dependent regularization explicitly handle out-of-vocabulary words during inference, and if so, how?
