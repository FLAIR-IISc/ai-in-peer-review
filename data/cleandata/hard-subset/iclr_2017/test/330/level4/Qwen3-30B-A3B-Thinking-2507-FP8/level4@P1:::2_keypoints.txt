1. Paper proposes Doc2VecC: document representation as averaged word embeddings with a data-dependent random-word-corruption regularizer.  
2. Decision: Reject.  
3. Main critique — Limited technical novelty: averaging embeddings is well-known and the corruption resembles existing regularizers (dropout/denoising), with no clear new theoretical contribution.  
4. Main critique — Insufficient empirical scope: evaluations limited to three tasks and omit dominant modern baselines (notably bidirectional LSTMs).  
5. Supporting argument: Theoretical justification of the corruption model is derivative and similar to dropout effects in logistic regression.  
6. Supporting argument: Doc2VecC underperforms LSTM-based methods on semantic relatedness, but the manuscript does not analyze or position this gap.  
7. Supporting argument: Efficiency claims (e.g., “billions of words per hour”) are plausible but not quantified relative to strong baselines (Paragraph Vectors, modern neural nets).  
8. Requested experiments: Add direct comparisons to bidirectional LSTMs (concatenated final hidden states) for sentiment and classification to validate "state-of-the-art" claims.  
9. Requested clarification: Specify the RNN-LM baseline setup — trained for classification or as a language model, and how document representations were extracted.  
10. Requested analysis: Justify the chosen corruption rate (q=0.9) and report sensitivity (e.g., performance across q values).  
11. Questions for authors: What are BiLSTM error rates on IMDB using final-state concatenation? Was the RNN-LM trained as a classifier or language model and how was its representation derived?
