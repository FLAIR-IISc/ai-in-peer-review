- Introduces Doc2VecC: document representations via averaged word embeddings with a corruption model that yields data-dependent regularization; claims strong accuracy and efficiency.

- Recommendation: reject — technical contribution insufficient given prior work on averaged embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012) and similarity of corruption to dropout.

- Simplicity and speed alone are not a substantial technical advance relative to existing literature on document representation.

- Insufficient experimental evidence to support the claim that Doc2VecC is “the best” — evaluation limited to sentiment analysis and document classification; semantic relatedness is secondary and under-detailed.

- Lacks comparisons to the most relevant recent methods (e.g., bidirectional LSTMs and transformer-based models), making it hard to assess novelty and practical value.

- Theoretical claim that corruption introduces “data-dependent regularization” is weakly justified (Taylor expansion only) and not clearly shown to outperform simpler alternatives like Word2Vec+IDF.

- Assertion that Doc2VecC “naturally enforces” document semantics in averaged embeddings lacks sufficient empirical validation.

- Suggested improvements: broaden experimental tasks/datasets, directly compare to bi-LSTMs and transformers, provide stronger theoretical analysis of corruption, and clarify relation to prior averaging work.

- Questions needing clarification: how was the RNN-LM baseline trained/combined with classification objective; comparisons to Doc2Vec (Le & Mikolov) and transformer methods; rationale for removing local context and relation to Denoising Autoencoders; evidence that corruption improves semantic quality over standard averaging; and generalization of improved word embeddings beyond one analogy dataset.
