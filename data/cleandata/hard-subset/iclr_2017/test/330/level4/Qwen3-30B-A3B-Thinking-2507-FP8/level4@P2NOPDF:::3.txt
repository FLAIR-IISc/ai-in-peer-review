

This paper introduces a method for learning document embeddings by summing constituent word embeddings, with word embeddings jointly trained and subject to dropout during training. While the individual components are not novel—joint word-document embedding learning and sum-based document representations have precedent (e.g., Lebret and Collobert’s autoencoder approach), and the dropout mechanism is a standard application to input layers—the resulting model achieves efficient inference and strong empirical performance.  

The paper’s core contribution is incremental: it combines established techniques (word2vec-style training, embedding averaging, and dropout) without introducing significant theoretical or methodological innovations. The citation to Lebret and Collobert (2014) explicitly demonstrates prior work on sum-based document representations via autoencoders, and the dropout implementation aligns with conventional practice for input regularization. Consequently, the novelty is limited to the integration of these existing elements into a cohesive framework.  

However, the model’s strengths lie in its practicality. Inference requires only a simple average of word embeddings—unlike Doc2Vec’s computationally intensive inference step—making it highly efficient for large-scale applications. Crucially, the embedding inherently captures context-predictive features through its training objective, encoding global document properties that directly support local-context prediction (e.g., sentiment or classification tasks). Despite its simplicity, the model achieves competitive results on benchmark sentiment analysis and document classification datasets, suggesting its effectiveness for downstream applications.  

While the lack of theoretical novelty is notable, the paper’s focus on a lightweight, high-performance solution addresses a practical need in document representation. The efficiency gains and robust empirical results justify its consideration, particularly for scenarios where inference speed is critical. The work is well-motivated, clearly presented, and demonstrates consistent performance across tasks without overcomplicating the approach.  

Given the balance between simplicity, efficiency, and performance, this paper offers a valuable, immediately applicable method for document representation. It advances the field not through radical innovation but through a streamlined, effective implementation of established principles. For a conference like ICLR, which prioritizes both technical soundness and practical impact, this work merits publication. I recommend acceptance.
