- Introduces Doc2VecC: document representation learning via corruption-based regularization.
- Represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings.
- Uses random-word removal corruption to enforce data-dependent regularization that suppresses common/non-discriminative words.
- Preserves informative words while reducing reliance on heuristics like IDF.
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
- Demonstrates large empirical gains over strong baselines (e.g., ~15% over BoW in sentiment).
- Shows strong theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) explaining why corruption yields effective regularization.
- Delivers major computational advantages: training billions of words/hour on one machine and very fast test-time inference (e.g., 7s vs 4min for Paragraph Vectors).
- Simplicity and decoupled complexity from corpus size make the method practical for real-world use.
- Recommendation: accept—novel, meaningful combination of techniques and strong computational practicality.
- Suggest expanding empirical evaluation to more diverse tasks (e.g., topic modeling, cross-lingual transfer) to validate generalizability.
- Suggest reducing the t-SNE visualization footprint and adding analyses of regularization effects on embedding quality across vocabulary frequencies.
- Requests clarification on why sentiment/classification tasks were prioritized and how corruption rate q=0.9 was chosen given sensitivity to performance.
- Requests clarification on whether and how the data-dependent regularization handles out-of-vocabulary words at inference.
