- Recommendation: Accept — simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance justify publication.
- Proposal: Introduces Doc2VecC, which represents documents by averaging word embeddings and trains with random word corruption.
- Innovation: Corruption model acts as data-dependent regularization, favoring informative/rare words and suppressing common terms.
- Empirical results: Competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, often matching or exceeding baselines.
- Efficiency: Training and inference are computationally cheap; claimed ability to train on billions of words/hour on a single machine.
- Practical advantage: Avoids expensive inference for unseen documents (unlike Paragraph Vectors) and eliminates need for post-processing like IDF weighting.
- Technical novelty: Components are not individually novel (averaging, dropout-like corruption), but their combination is practical and effective.
- Experimental validation: Thorough comparisons across IMDB, Wikipedia, and SICK datasets with t-SNE visualizations supporting representation quality.
- Weakness — novelty claim: Paper should clarify how the corruption model differs from standard dropout in document representation learning.
- Weakness — related work: Stronger connection needed to prior work such as "The Sum of Its Parts" by Lebret and Collobert.
- Weakness — hyperparameter analysis: More analysis required on the effect of corruption rate q (current experiments use q=0.9 only).
- Weakness — unexpected result: Paper should investigate why Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on IMDB despite strong results elsewhere.
- Question 1: How do added local context words improve global document semantics relative to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA)?
- Question 2: How does the corruption-induced regularization differ theoretically and practically from standard L2 or other embedding regularizers, and why is it effective?
