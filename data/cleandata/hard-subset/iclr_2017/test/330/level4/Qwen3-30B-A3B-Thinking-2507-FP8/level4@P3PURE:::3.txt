

This paper proposes learning document embeddings as the sum of constituent word embeddings, where word embeddings are jointly learned and randomly corrupted (i.e., dropped out) during training. While the individual components of this approach are not novel, the resulting algorithm achieves efficient document representation learning with strong empirical performance.  

The joint training of word and document embeddings is not new (see, e.g., "The Sum of Its Parts: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders" by Lebret and Collobert), nor is the constraint that a document must be represented as the sum of its word embeddings. Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is equivalent to standard dropout applied to the input layer. Combined with a word2vec-style loss and training methodology, the paper offers limited novelty.  

Nonetheless, the model excels in inference efficiency, requiring only the average of word embeddings—avoiding the complex inference step inherent in Doc2Vec. Crucially, the embedding inherently captures salient global document information that aids local-context prediction. For such a simple architecture, the performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is encouraging.  

Despite the limited novelty, the model’s simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results render it worthy of wider readership and study. I recommend acceptance.
