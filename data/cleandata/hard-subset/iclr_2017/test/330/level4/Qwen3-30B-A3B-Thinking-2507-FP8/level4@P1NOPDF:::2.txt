

This paper proposes a framework for document representation by averaging word embeddings, augmented with data-dependent regularization that emphasizes informative or rare words while suppressing common words (driving their weights toward zero). Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification demonstrate that the method achieves the lowest error rates among baseline document embedding approaches. While the motivation for optimizing document encoding is sound, the paper lacks substantial technical novelty. The core techniques are largely derivative, and the primary advantage lies in the method's simplicity and computational efficiency. To convincingly establish this as the optimal approach for document representation, the authors would need to present compelling results across more than two tasks.  

The review of the RNN-LM component requires clarification: was the language model trained as a standard LM (minimizing perplexity) or optimized directly for classification? Additionally, was the document representation derived from the final hidden state or the average of all hidden states?  

Crucially, the paper omits a comparison against the widely adopted bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) approach, where document representations are formed by concatenating the final hidden states of forward and backward passes. Given that this is a dominant method in current practice for tasks like document classification and sentiment analysis, a direct comparison with BiLSTM would significantly strengthen the paper's claims and provide necessary context for evaluating the proposed method's relative merits. Without such a comparison, the assertion of superior performance remains under-supported, particularly since the proposed technique’s simplicity may not outweigh the representational power of established sequence models. The current empirical validation, limited to two tasks and lacking benchmarking against the most prevalent baseline (BiLSTM), does not sufficiently justify the method’s proposed advantages.
