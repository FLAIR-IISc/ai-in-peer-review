

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel framework for document representation learning. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a key innovation being a corruption model that randomly removes words during training. This corruption mechanism introduces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while preserving those of rare, informative words. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC produces high-quality document representations that match or outperform state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while maintaining exceptional computational efficiency.

I recommend acceptance. Doc2VecC makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that document representations can be generated through simple averaging of word embeddings when combined with an appropriate corruption-based regularization mechanism. The model's efficiency—training on billions of words per hour on a single machine and enabling immediate test-time document representation through simple averaging—represents a significant practical advantage over existing approaches.

The paper's strengths are several. First, it provides a clear theoretical justification for using document averaging as a representation method, supported by the insightful analysis in Section 3.1 that demonstrates how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization. Second, the experimental evaluation comprehensively compares Doc2VecC against multiple baselines across three distinct tasks, showing consistent performance gains. Third, the authors provide a thorough explanation of the model's efficiency advantages, including detailed training and inference time comparisons that highlight the practical benefits of their approach.

The main limitation of the paper is its empirical scope. While the evaluation on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness is thorough, the authors could have expanded to include more diverse or challenging tasks to further demonstrate the model's versatility. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3, while useful for illustrating document clustering, consume significant space without providing additional insights that couldn't be conveyed through quantitative metrics. The authors might have better utilized this space to provide deeper analysis of the model's behavior or comparisons with additional baselines.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:
1. Include a more diverse set of evaluation tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification to demonstrate broader applicability.
2. Replace the t-SNE visualization with additional quantitative analysis of the model's behavior, particularly regarding the effect of different corruption rates on performance.
3. Provide more detailed discussion of how the corruption rate q affects the regularization strength and model performance, including results for different values of q.
4. Clarify the relationship between Doc2VecC and previous work on document representation using word embeddings, particularly in relation to the "average of word embeddings" approach by Huang et al. (2012).

I have a few questions to help clarify my understanding of the paper:
1. In Section 3.1, the authors claim Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training. How does this differ from the approach in Huang et al. (2012), who also used document averaging without corruption? What specific advantage does the corruption mechanism provide?
2. The paper claims Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec. However, the word analogy task results (Section 4.3) show only modest improvements. Could the authors clarify why the word embedding improvements are not more substantial given the corruption mechanism?
3. In the sentiment analysis experiments (Section 4.2), Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors but Skip-thought vectors perform poorly. The authors hypothesize this is due to paragraph length differences. Could they provide evidence that the poor Skip-thought performance isn't simply due to not fine-tuning on the IMDB dataset, as they did for Doc2VecC on the SICK dataset?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of document representation learning. The combination of theoretical insight, empirical validation, and practical efficiency advantages makes it a valuable addition to the literature. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded, the paper's core contribution is significant and the model's simplicity and efficiency make it particularly appealing for practical applications. The authors have presented a well-motivated, clearly articulated approach that deserves publication at ICLR.
