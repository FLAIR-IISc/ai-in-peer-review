

**Review for ICLR_2017**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel framework for efficient document representation learning. Doc2VecC represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a key innovation being the inclusion of a corruption model that introduces data-dependent regularization during training. This regularization mechanism encourages the model to favor informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The authors demonstrate that this approach produces high-quality document representations that match or outperform state-of-the-art methods across multiple tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. The paper's strengths include its elegant theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism, the simplicity of the model architecture, and its exceptional efficiency—Doc2VecC can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine while enabling near-instant document representation generation at test time.

**2. Decision**

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper presents a well-motivated, theoretically sound approach that achieves strong empirical results with remarkable efficiency.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper addresses a significant problem in document representation learning: balancing expressiveness with computational efficiency. The key insight—that document semantics can be effectively captured through averaging word embeddings when trained with an appropriate corruption mechanism—is both novel and well-justified. The corruption model provides a theoretically grounded form of data-dependent regularization, which the authors rigorously derive in Section 3.1. This regularization naturally suppresses embeddings of common words while preserving those of informative terms, eliminating the need for post-processing techniques like IDF weighting that are commonly used with Word2Vec.

Empirically, Doc2VecC demonstrates consistent improvements across multiple benchmarks. On the IMDB sentiment analysis task, it achieves comparable or better results than Paragraph Vectors (the closest competitor) while being significantly more efficient. The document classification results on Wikipedia further validate the approach, with Doc2VecC outperforming all baselines across representation sizes. The semantic relatedness results on SICK are also compelling, showing Doc2VecC significantly outperforms most baselines (though it lags slightly behind specialized LSTM models for short sentences).

The efficiency claims are well-supported: training and inference times are substantially better than Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought vectors, which is critical for large-scale applications. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC's simplicity does not come at the cost of performance, as it achieves state-of-the-art results with a much more scalable architecture.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance it:
- The empirical evaluation could be strengthened by including results on more diverse tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification. For instance, evaluating on natural language inference or question answering would demonstrate broader applicability.
- The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) are useful but occupy significant space. Consider replacing one with additional empirical analysis or a more detailed discussion of the regularization effects.
- The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but this relationship isn't fully explored. A brief comparison of Doc2VecC with DEA would clarify its distinct advantages.
- The analysis of word embeddings in Section 4.2 (Table 3) is insightful but could be expanded to discuss how the regularization affects specific word types (e.g., stop words vs. content words) across different datasets.

**5. Questions for Authors**

To better understand the paper's claims, I would appreciate clarification on:
1. The authors note that "Paragraph Vectors on the other hand performs surprisingly bad on this dataset [word analogy]." Could you elaborate on why Paragraph Vectors fails on word analogy tasks while Doc2VecC excels? Is this primarily due to the corruption regularization, or is it related to the document representation approach?
2. In Section 4.5, the authors state that Doc2VecC "significantly outperforms skip-thought vectors on the IMDB movie review dataset (11.70% error rate vs 17.42%)." Given that skip-thought vectors are designed for sentence-level representation, how does Doc2VecC's document-level approach generalize to shorter text units like individual sentences? Could you provide results on a sentence-level task to clarify this?
3. The paper claims Doc2VecC "does not rely on the trick of subsampling frequent words" (Section 4.2). Could you elaborate on how the corruption mechanism specifically compensates for the imbalance between frequent and rare words, and how this might differ across datasets with varying vocabulary distributions?
