

This paper proposes a simple framework for document representation via averaging word embeddings with data-dependent regularization that suppresses common words (pushing their embeddings near zero) while amplifying informative/rare words. Empirical results on sentiment analysis and document classification demonstrate lower error rates compared to standard baselines like TF-IDF, bag-of-words, and standard averaging. The method’s computational efficiency and conceptual simplicity are notable strengths.

However, the technical novelty is limited. The core idea—using regularization to weight word embeddings—builds directly on established concepts (e.g., word importance weighting in NLP, sparse regularization in embeddings), with no substantial theoretical or algorithmic innovation. The claimed "best" performance rests on results from only two tasks, which is insufficient to establish broad superiority. To convincingly position this as a *superior* document representation approach, the authors should validate performance across a wider range of tasks (e.g., topic modeling, cross-lingual transfer, or multi-label classification) and datasets. Without this, the results may reflect task-specific quirks rather than generalizability.

A critical omission is the lack of comparison against the current standard for document representation: bidirectional LSTM encoders with concatenated final hidden states. This approach dominates recent literature (e.g., in sentiment analysis and classification) due to its ability to capture sequential context. The authors should explicitly benchmark against this method, as its absence undermines the paper’s central claim. For instance, if the proposed method underperforms on standard BERT or LSTM baselines, the argument for its "best" status collapses.

Regarding the RNN-LM baseline:  
1. Was the language model trained to minimize classification error (treated as a discriminative model) or as a standard generative LM?  
2. If the latter, was the document representation derived from the final hidden state or the average of all hidden states?  
Clarifying these points is essential for reproducibility and fair comparison. The current description leaves ambiguity about whether the LM was adapted for classification or used naively as a feature extractor.

The regularization mechanism also warrants deeper scrutiny. The paper states it "favors informative words" but does not specify how "informativeness" is quantified (e.g., via mutual information, entropy, or frequency-based heuristics). If the regularization relies solely on word frequency (e.g., inverse document frequency), it merely rephrases TF-IDF weighting—a well-known baseline. If it uses a more sophisticated measure (e.g., task-specific feature relevance), the authors must justify its derivation and demonstrate its advantage over existing weighting schemes.

The experimental section lacks ablation studies to isolate the impact of the regularization. Without showing how the method’s performance changes when removing the regularization term or varying its hyperparameters, the contribution of this component remains unverified. For example:  
- How much does the regularization improve accuracy over simple word-embedding averaging?  
- Does the regularization’s effectiveness depend on dataset characteristics (e.g., domain, size)?  

Additionally, the paper does not address scalability. While the method is fast, it processes entire documents at once, which may not suit streaming or real-time applications where incremental processing (e.g., via RNNs) is preferred. A brief discussion of computational trade-offs would strengthen the practical argument.

In summary, the paper presents a straightforward, efficient approach that achieves competitive results on two tasks. However, the absence of novel technical contributions, limited empirical validation, and omission of key baselines (particularly bidirectional LSTMs) prevent it from making a compelling case for a *new standard* in document representation. The work would benefit significantly from:  
1. Extended experiments across ≥3 diverse tasks/datasets,  
2. Direct comparison to bidirectional LSTM encoders,  
3. Clearer specification of the regularization’s informativeness metric and ablation studies,  
4. Clarification of the RNN-LM baseline’s training and representation strategy.  

As currently written, the paper’s value lies in its simplicity rather than its innovation. It may serve as a useful baseline for future work but does not advance the state of the art in document representation. I recommend rejection unless these gaps are addressed in a revision.
