

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a framework for efficient document representation learning. Doc2VecC represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, with the key innovation being a corruption model that randomly removes words during training. This mechanism introduces data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative terms. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while offering significant computational advantages over existing methods.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) the model's simplicity and efficiency in both training and inference provide substantial practical benefits, and (2) the empirical results demonstrate consistent, strong performance across multiple NLP tasks, often matching or exceeding state-of-the-art approaches.

The paper makes a compelling case for the practical merits of Doc2VecC. Unlike Paragraph Vectors, which requires expensive inference for unseen documents and scales with corpus size, Doc2VecC generates document representations through a straightforward average of word embeddings, making it highly efficient at test time. The corruption mechanism during training provides a form of data-dependent regularization that naturally suppresses uninformative words, eliminating the need for post-processing techniques like IDF weighting. The empirical results across multiple tasks demonstrate that this approach produces high-quality document representations without the computational overhead of more complex models. The paper's claim that Doc2VecC can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine is particularly compelling for practitioners working with large-scale datasets.

The technical contribution, while not entirely novel in its individual components, effectively combines existing ideas (word embedding learning, simple averaging, and dropout-like regularization) into a practical and efficient framework. The paper's strength lies in demonstrating that this combination yields strong empirical results with minimal computational cost. The authors provide thorough experimental validation across multiple tasks, including sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK), with detailed comparisons to relevant baselines. The visualization of document representations using t-SNE (Figure 3) provides compelling evidence of the quality of the learned representations.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend addressing the following points:
1. The novelty claim could be more precisely articulated. The paper should more explicitly clarify how the corruption model differs from standard dropout in the context of document representation learning and why this specific formulation provides advantages over simply applying dropout to word embeddings.
2. The connection to related work, particularly "The Sum of Its Parts" by Lebret and Collobert (which also uses averaging of word embeddings), could be strengthened to better position the paper's contribution.
3. The paper would benefit from more detailed analysis of how the corruption rate q affects performance. The current experiments use q=0.9 without thorough investigation of the impact of varying this parameter.
4. The paper could provide more insight into why Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on the IMDB dataset (Table 1), which is a notable finding given Paragraph Vectors' strong performance on other tasks.

I have two specific questions to help clarify the paper's contribution:
1. The paper states that Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) when local context words are removed. How does the addition of local context words (as in Figure 1) specifically enhance the model's ability to capture global document semantics compared to DEA?
2. In Section 3.1, the authors describe the corruption mechanism as introducing a data-dependent regularization. How does this regularization specifically differ from standard L2 regularization or other regularization techniques applied to word embeddings, and what is the theoretical justification for why this particular form of regularization is effective for document representation?

The paper's main contribution is its demonstration that a simple, efficient model architecture can achieve strong performance on document representation tasks. While the individual components are not entirely novel, the effective combination of these components into a practical framework that addresses key limitations of existing approaches (computational efficiency and the need for post-processing) makes this work valuable for both practitioners and researchers. The model's simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance across multiple tasks provide compelling reasons for its acceptance, particularly for a venue like ICLR that values practical, implementable approaches.
