- Introduces Doc2VecC: document representations via simple averages of word embeddings with a corruption model for data-dependent regularization.
- Corruption mechanism theoretically favors informative/rare words and suppresses common, non-discriminative words, removing need for IDF/subsampling tricks.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
- Achieves exceptional efficiency: training on billions of words/hour on a single machine and near-instant inference.
- Recommendation: accept — method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, empirically strong, and highly efficient.
- Key insight: averaging embeddings with corruption is a novel, simple, and scalable alternative to more complex models.
- Empirical highlights: matches/betters Paragraph Vectors on IMDB, outperforms baselines on Wikipedia classification, and shows strong SICK results (slightly behind LSTMs on short sentences).
- Efficiency validated: substantially faster than Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought vectors for training and inference.
- Suggest expanding empirical evaluation to more diverse tasks (e.g., natural language inference, question answering).
- Recommend reducing or replacing large t-SNE visualizations with additional empirical analysis or deeper discussion of regularization effects.
- Request clarifying comparison with Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) since Doc2VecC reportedly reduces to DEA under certain conditions.
- Suggest expanding analysis of how regularization affects different word types (stop words vs content words) across datasets.
- Question: Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on word analogy tasks compared to Doc2VecC — due to corruption regularization or representation approach?
- Question: How well does Doc2VecC generalize to sentence-level tasks given skip-thoughts are sentence-focused; request sentence-level results.
- Question: How exactly does the corruption mechanism compensate for frequent vs rare word imbalance and how does this vary with dataset vocabulary distributions?
