

This paper introduces a method for learning document embeddings by summing constituent word embeddings, which are jointly trained and subject to random dropout ('corruption') during training. While none of the constituent components are novel, the resulting approach yields an efficient document representation learning algorithm with strong empirical results.

Joint training of word and document embeddings is not novel, nor is the use of a sum-to-represent documents (as demonstrated in Lebret and Collobert's "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism constitutes no more than standard dropout applied to the input layer. Combined with a word2vec-style loss function and training methodology, this work offers minimal novelty.

Conversely, the model is notably efficient during generation, requiring merely the average of word embeddings rather than the complex inference process in Doc2Vec. By design, the embedding inherently captures salient global document information—specifically, the features that facilitate local-context prediction. Given its simplicity, this model achieves promising results on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks.

Overall, despite the limited novelty, the model's simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance warrant broader attention, and I recommend acceptance.
