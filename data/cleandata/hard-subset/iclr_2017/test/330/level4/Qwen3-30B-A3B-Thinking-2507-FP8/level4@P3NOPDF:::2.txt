

This paper proposes a document representation framework that computes a document vector as the average of word embeddings, regularized to emphasize rare/informative words while suppressing common words (via L2 penalty). The authors demonstrate competitive performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, reporting lower error rates than baseline methods. While the motivation for optimizing document encoding is compelling, the technical contributions are limited. The core approach—weighted averaging with data-dependent regularization—builds directly on established techniques (e.g., weighted word averaging in [1], sparse regularization in [2]), with no novel algorithmic or theoretical insights. The primary advantage cited is computational efficiency, but this alone is insufficient to justify the paper’s claims without broader validation.  

The evaluation scope is a significant weakness. The authors report results on only two tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification), yet the method is presented as a general-purpose document representation technique. To establish its value, the authors should demonstrate consistent performance across multiple diverse tasks (e.g., topic modeling, semantic similarity, or multi-label classification) and datasets. Without this, the claim that the method "outperforms existing approaches" remains unsubstantiated for broader applicability.  

Regarding the RNN-LM baseline:  
- Was the language model trained to minimize classification error (i.e., fine-tuned for the downstream task), or was it trained as a standard LM (e.g., on unlabeled text)?  
- Did the authors use the final hidden state or the average of all hidden states as the document representation?  
Clarifying these points is critical for fair comparison, as the choice significantly impacts performance.  

The paper fails to compare against the current standard for document representation: bidirectional LSTMs (e.g., [3]), where the concatenation of final hidden states from both directions serves as the document vector. This is particularly concerning for sentiment analysis and document classification, where such models consistently achieve state-of-the-art results. A direct comparison with this baseline would substantially strengthen the paper’s claims. Without it, the reported "lowest error rates" lack context against the most relevant alternatives.  

The regularization mechanism (penalizing common words via L2) is straightforward but not rigorously justified. The authors do not analyze *why* this regularization improves performance or how it interacts with word embedding quality. For instance, does the method degrade when using pretrained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) versus task-specific embeddings? Ablation studies on the regularization strength or word frequency thresholds would add depth.  

In summary, the paper presents a simple, efficient document encoding method with promising results on two tasks, but its technical novelty is minimal, and the evaluation is insufficiently comprehensive. To merit acceptance, the authors must:  
1. Extend experiments to ≥3 diverse tasks/datasets,  
2. Compare directly against bidirectional LSTM baselines,  
3. Clarify RNN-LM implementation details, and  
4. Provide deeper analysis of the regularization’s impact.  

As currently written, the work feels like a pragmatic engineering improvement rather than a significant contribution to the field. While the simplicity is appealing, it does not compensate for the lack of novelty or robust validation. The reviewers should consider the paper’s relevance to ICLR’s focus on novel machine learning advances.

[3] Ma et al., "End-to-End Sequence Labeling via BiLSTM-CNNs-CRF," EMNLP 2016.
