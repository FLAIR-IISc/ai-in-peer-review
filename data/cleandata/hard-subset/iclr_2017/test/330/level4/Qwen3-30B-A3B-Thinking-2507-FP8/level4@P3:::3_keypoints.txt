- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as averaged word embeddings with a training-time corruption that randomly drops words.  
- Mechanism: Corruption serves as data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words.  
- Empirical claim: Strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Efficiency claim: Very fast training and inference (matches Word2Vec speed; example: 7s vs 257s inference for 25k docs).  
- Theoretical support: Section 3.1 analyzes how corruption induces regularization and penalizes frequent words; empirical validation in Table 3.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons: Simple, elegant, highly efficient approach achieving state-of-the-art results without Paragraph Vector overhead; corruption obviates need for subsampling/IDF.  
- Comparative results: Outperforms baselines (Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought); better classification error on Wikipedia (31.92% vs 35.78%).  
- Suggested clarifications: Explicitly relate corruption to dropout, provide more intuitive explanation of Hessian/regularization, discuss limitations on very short documents.  
- Question 1: How does Doc2VecC’s enforcement of semantics via corruption differ from Huang et al. (2012) averaging approach?  
- Question 2: Why does simple averaging with corruption outperform Paragraph Vectors on Wikipedia—does Paragraph Vectors fail to capture semantics effectively?  
- Question 3: How does document length (e.g., short SICK sentence pairs) affect Doc2VecC’s relative performance compared to sequence/LSTM models?
