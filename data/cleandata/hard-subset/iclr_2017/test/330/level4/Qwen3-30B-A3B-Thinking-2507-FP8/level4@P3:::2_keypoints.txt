- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings with a corruption mechanism that regularizes toward informative/rare words.
- Claims Doc2VecC outperforms existing methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness while being computationally efficient.
- The corruption mechanism is theoretically motivated as data-dependent regularization favoring rare informative words.
- Decision: Reject — main reason is insufficient technical novelty for ICLR despite good empirical efficiency.
- Core critique: Averaging word embeddings is already well-explored (e.g., Huang et al., 2012); corruption is not a major conceptual advance.
- Theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) is interesting but not a strong argument that this regularizer is uniquely preferable to other techniques.
- Experimental scope (three tasks) is adequate but not compelling; results don’t convincingly beat stronger or more recent baselines.
- Missing comparisons to stronger baselines, especially bidirectional LSTM-based document encoders and recent state-of-the-art methods.
- Comparison with Skip-thought and LSTM methods is limited and doesn’t establish practical preference for Doc2VecC.
- Strength of paper is empirical efficiency, but efficiency alone is insufficient without stronger novelty or stronger empirical advantages.
- Suggested improvements: better articulate novelty over prior averaged-embedding work (e.g., Huang et al. 2012).
- Suggested improvements: add comparisons with stronger baselines (bidirectional LSTMs) and more diverse tasks/datasets.
- Suggested improvements: provide detailed analysis/visualization of corruption’s effect on different word frequencies and types.
- Suggested improvements: discuss limitations, especially performance on very long documents where averaging may fail.
- Clarifying question: clarify relationship between their corruption mechanism and denoising autoencoders — how does their data-dependent regularization differ from standard denoising loss?
- Clarifying question: for the RNN-LM baseline, was it trained discriminatively for classification or as a standard language model?
- Clarifying question: provide direct comparisons to bidirectional LSTM representations (e.g., concatenated final hidden states) on the same datasets.
- Clarifying question: clarify model complexity and exact parameter counts relative to methods like Skip-thought and Paragraph Vectors with fixed embedding sizes.
