

# Review of "Document Vector through Corruption"

## Summary
This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple yet efficient framework for document representation learning. The core idea is to represent each document as a simple average of its word embeddings, with a novel corruption mechanism that introduces data-dependent regularization. This regularization favors informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The authors claim that this approach outperforms existing methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while offering significant computational advantages in both training and inference efficiency. The paper is well-motivated, building on the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning, and presents a theoretically justified corruption mechanism that enhances document representation quality.

## Decision
Reject. While the paper presents a simple and efficient approach with compelling empirical results, it lacks sufficient technical novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The main contribution appears to be the application of a corruption mechanism to the well-known technique of averaging word embeddings, rather than a fundamentally new method. The authors would need to demonstrate significantly stronger results across more diverse tasks or provide a more substantial theoretical contribution to justify acceptance.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim is that Doc2VecC produces better document representations than existing methods through its corruption mechanism. While the experimental results are generally positive and the method is indeed efficient, the technical novelty is limited. The core idea of using simple averaging of word embeddings for document representation has been explored in prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), and the corruption mechanism, while theoretically justified, doesn't represent a major conceptual leap. The paper's strongest contribution is the empirical demonstration of efficiency, but this alone is insufficient for acceptance at ICLR, where technical novelty is paramount.

The paper's theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is interesting but doesn't provide a compelling reason to prefer Doc2VecC over other regularization techniques. The data-dependent regularization is well-explained, but the paper doesn't sufficiently demonstrate that this specific form of regularization is necessary or uniquely beneficial compared to existing approaches like sub-sampling frequent words in Word2Vec.

The experimental results are presented across three tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness), which is adequate but not exceptional for a paper claiming to outperform state-of-the-art methods. The paper does not compare against the most recent or strongest baselines in the field, particularly for document classification where bidirectional LSTM-based approaches have become standard. The comparison with Skip-thought Vectors and LSTM-based methods in Section 4.5 is limited, and the paper doesn't clearly establish why Doc2VecC would be preferred over these more sophisticated methods in practical applications.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. More clearly articulate the novelty of their approach compared to prior work on document representation, particularly Huang et al. (2012) which directly uses averaged word embeddings.
2. Include comparisons with stronger baselines, especially bidirectional LSTM approaches that have become standard for document representation.
3. Provide more detailed analysis of the corruption mechanism's impact on different types of words (e.g., through visualization or more extensive word frequency analysis).
4. Discuss the limitations of the approach more thoroughly, particularly regarding its performance on very long documents where the simple averaging approach might be less effective.

## Clarifying Questions
1. The paper mentions that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could the authors clarify the relationship between their corruption mechanism and standard denoising autoencoders? How does the data-dependent regularization introduced by their corruption mechanism differ from the standard denoising loss?

2. For the RNN-LM baseline, was the language model trained to minimize classification error (as a discriminative model) or as a standard language model (as an unsupervised model)? The paper states that "the supervised RNN-LM is learned on the training set only," but doesn't clarify whether it's trained for classification or language modeling.

3. The paper claims that Doc2VecC "matches or out-performs the state-of-the-art in generating high-quality document representations." Given that bidirectional LSTM-based methods have become the standard for many document representation tasks, could the authors provide a direct comparison with these approaches on the same datasets? For instance, how would Doc2VecC perform compared to a bidirectional LSTM with final hidden state concatenation (a common practice for document representation)?

4. The paper states that "the model complexity of Doc2VecC is decoupled from the size of the training corpus, depending only on the size of the vocabulary." How does this compare to other approaches that use fixed-size word embeddings, such as Skip-thought Vectors or Paragraph Vectors with document vector size fixed to a constant? Could the authors clarify the exact parameter count of Doc2VecC compared to these methods?
