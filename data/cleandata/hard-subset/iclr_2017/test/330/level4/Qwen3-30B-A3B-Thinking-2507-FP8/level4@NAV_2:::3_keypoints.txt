- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings learned with corruption (random word removal) to downweight common non-discriminative words and emphasize rare informative words.

- Leverages the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings preserve semantic structure; corruption acts to improve discriminative power of embeddings.

- Reports state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

- Claims exceptional computational efficiency: trains on billions of words/hour and enables instantaneous test-time inference.

- Recommends acceptance based on two main reasons: strong empirical performance and exceptional computational efficiency.

- Presents a theoretical derivation (Sec 3.1) framing corruption as data-dependent regularization that explains gains over Word2Vec+IDF without subsampling.

- Efficiency claims supported by timing experiments (Table 2): faster training than Paragraph Vectors and ~340× faster inference than document-vector-based methods.

- Empirical tables (1, 5, 6) consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming baselines, with particularly strong results on semantic relatedness versus heavily engineered systems.

- Acknowledges prior components (embedding averaging, dropout-like regularization) but argues their integration produces a uniquely efficient and effective representation.

- Suggested clarifications: explicitly contrast novelty with Lebret & Collobert (2014) and differentiate the corruption mechanism from standard dropout.

- Suggested additional experiments: ablation study on corruption rate q (justify q=0.9) and t-SNE visualizations of word embeddings to illustrate regularization effects.

- Questions to address: how corruption differs from dropout theoretically and empirically on embedding quality; why Paragraph Vectors performs poorly on IMDB and how Doc2VecC overcomes that; whether simpler regularization (e.g., L2) could replicate the gains.
