

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple yet efficient framework for document representation learning. The authors propose representing documents as averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption-based regularization mechanism that favors informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The model achieves competitive performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, with notable advantages in training and inference speed compared to existing approaches.

I recommend rejecting this paper. The primary reason is the lack of substantial technical novelty. The core concept of using averaged word embeddings for document representation is not new, and the corruption-based regularization mechanism appears to be a straightforward extension of existing techniques rather than a significant theoretical advancement. The paper's claim that the model "matches or out-performs the state-of-the-art" is not sufficiently supported, as it only evaluates on two tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification) without comprehensive validation across diverse benchmarks.

The experimental validation is limited and does not adequately address the current state of the art. While Doc2VecC shows promising results on the two tasks tested, the paper fails to provide meaningful comparisons with recent deep learning approaches, particularly bidirectional LSTM architectures, which have become standard for document representation. The performance gains reported do not appear to be substantial enough to justify the paper's claims, especially given the simplicity of the approach.

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include additional tasks such as semantic relatedness, topic modeling, or question answering to demonstrate broader applicability.
2. Providing a more thorough comparison with recent state-of-the-art methods, particularly deep learning approaches like bidirectional LSTMs, which are now widely adopted for document representation.
3. Including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the corruption mechanism versus the averaging approach.

To clarify my assessment, I would like the authors to address:
1. For the RNN-LM baseline, was the language model trained to minimize classification error or as a standard language model? Did you use the final hidden state or the average of all hidden states as the document representation?
2. How does Doc2VecC specifically compare to the widely adopted bidirectional LSTM approach for document representation on the tasks evaluated in the paper?
