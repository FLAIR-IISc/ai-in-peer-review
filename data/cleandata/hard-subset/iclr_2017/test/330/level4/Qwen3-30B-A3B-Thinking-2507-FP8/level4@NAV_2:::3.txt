

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings learned with a corruption-based regularization mechanism. The approach leverages the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings preserve semantic structure, while the corruption model (random word removal during training) implicitly suppresses embeddings of common non-discriminative words and enhances those of rare informative words. Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks with significantly improved training and inference efficiency compared to existing methods.

I recommend acceptance with two key reasons: (1) the model's exceptional computational efficiency (training on billions of words per hour and instantaneous test-time inference) and (2) its strong empirical performance across multiple benchmark tasks, which consistently matches or exceeds state-of-the-art methods despite its simplicity.

The supporting arguments for acceptance are as follows. The corruption mechanism effectively serves as data-dependent regularization, as rigorously derived in Section 3.1, which explains why Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF without requiring subsampling tricks (Section 4.2). The efficiency claims are well-substantiated through comprehensive timing experiments (Table 2), demonstrating that Doc2VecC trains faster than Paragraph Vectors and achieves test-time inference 340× quicker than document-vector-based approaches. The empirical results (Tables 1, 5, 6) consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming baselines across all tasks, with particularly compelling evidence in the semantic relatedness task where it surpasses heavily engineered competition solutions. While the core components (word embedding averaging, dropout-like regularization) have precedents, the paper convincingly demonstrates that their integration yields a uniquely efficient and effective document representation method.

For further improvement, consider clarifying the novelty claim in the introduction by explicitly contrasting with Lebret and Collobert (2014) and noting how Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism differs from standard dropout. An ablation study on the corruption rate (q) would strengthen the justification for the chosen value (q=0.9). Additionally, including t-SNE visualizations of word embeddings (beyond document representations) would better illustrate the regularization effect on word vectors.

Please address these questions to solidify the contribution: (1) How does the corruption mechanism specifically differ from standard dropout in terms of theoretical guarantees or empirical impact on word embedding quality? (2) Why does Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on IMDB despite its design for document representation, and how does Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism uniquely address this limitation? (3) Could the observed performance gains be replicated with a simpler regularization technique (e.g., L2 on word embeddings) without corruption, and if not, why?
