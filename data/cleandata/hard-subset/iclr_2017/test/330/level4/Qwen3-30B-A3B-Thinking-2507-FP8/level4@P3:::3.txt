

**Review for "Document Vector through Corruption"**

**Summary of Contributions:**  
This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective framework for document representation learning. The authors propose representing documents as the average of their constituent word embeddings, with a novel corruption mechanism that randomly drops words during training. This approach leverages the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings can preserve semantic structure, while the corruption process serves as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The method demonstrates strong performance across multiple document representation tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, while maintaining exceptional training and inference efficiency. The authors provide thorough empirical validation and insightful theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism's regularization effect.

**Decision:** Accept  

**Key Reasons for Acceptance:**  
1. The paper presents a simple, elegant, and highly efficient approach to document representation learning that achieves state-of-the-art results across multiple tasks without the computational overhead of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors.  
2. The corruption mechanism provides a theoretically grounded regularization effect that naturally suppresses uninformative words, eliminating the need for additional techniques like subsampling or IDF weighting.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
The paper's core contribution—using a corruption-based regularization to enable simple averaging of word embeddings for document representation—is both practical and theoretically sound. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC outperforms several strong baselines (including Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors) on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks while requiring significantly less training and inference time. For instance, Doc2VecC achieves 31.92% classification error on Wikipedia data (vs. 35.78% for Paragraph Vectors), with inference time of 7 seconds compared to Paragraph Vectors' 257 seconds for 25,000 test documents.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 convincingly demonstrates how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularization that penalizes embeddings of frequent but uninformative words. This explains why Doc2VecC naturally handles common words without requiring additional preprocessing techniques like subsampling (which is critical for Word2Vec+AVG but unnecessary for Doc2VecC). The empirical validation of this effect in Table 3 is particularly compelling.

The paper's efficiency claims are well-supported: Doc2VecC trains on billions of words per hour on a single machine (matching Word2Vec's speed) while maintaining superior document representation quality. This combination of performance, simplicity, and efficiency makes the method highly valuable for real-world applications.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**  
While the paper is strong, a few refinements would enhance its clarity and impact:
- The connection between the corruption mechanism and dropout regularization could be more explicitly articulated, as the paper's description of "corruption" as distinct from dropout might confuse readers familiar with standard neural network techniques.
- The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of how the Hessian terms relate to the regularization effect.
- The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of potential limitations (e.g., performance on extremely short documents where averaging may not capture meaning well).

**Questions for Authors:**  
1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." Could you clarify how this differs from the approach in Huang et al. (2012), which also used averaging of word embeddings without the corruption mechanism? What specific aspect of your corruption process enables this "enforcement" that their method lacked?
2. In Table 5, Doc2VecC achieves better results than Paragraph Vectors across all representation dimensions. Given Paragraph Vectors' more complex architecture, why does the simpler averaging approach outperform it on the Wikipedia dataset? Does this suggest that Paragraph Vectors' document vectors may not effectively capture the semantic information that Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism helps retain?
3. The paper states that Doc2VecC "matches or out-performs the state-of-the-art" on semantic relatedness tasks. However, it still lags behind LSTM-based methods on the SICK dataset (Table 6). Given your hypothesis that Doc2VecC is better suited for longer documents, could you elaborate on how the document length in the SICK dataset (which consists of sentence pairs) might impact the relative performance of Doc2VecC versus sequence-based models?
