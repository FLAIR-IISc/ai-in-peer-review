

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple yet effective framework for document representation learning. Doc2VecC represents documents as the average of word embeddings learned through a corruption-based regularization mechanism. The key innovation lies in the corruption model that randomly removes words during training, which implicitly introduces a data-dependent regularization favoring informative/rare words while suppressing common, non-discriminative terms. This approach ensures document representations generated by simple averaging capture semantic meaning during learning, eliminating the need for post-processing. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly more efficient than existing methods.

I recommend acceptance. Doc2VecC offers a compelling combination of theoretical insight, practical efficiency, and strong empirical performance. The corruption mechanism provides a novel perspective on regularization that naturally leads to meaningful document representations through simple averaging, which is both theoretically grounded and practically advantageous.

The paper's main strengths are its elegant simplicity and theoretical justification. The corruption model serves as a data-dependent regularizer that automatically suppresses uninformative words without requiring manual feature engineering or post-processing. This is well-motivated by the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings preserve semantic relationships (e.g., "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen"). The authors provide a thorough theoretical analysis of how corruption acts as regularization (Section 3.1), showing it naturally penalizes embeddings of common words while preserving critical semantic information. This theoretical contribution is particularly valuable as it explains why simple averaging works well for document representation.

Empirically, Doc2VecC demonstrates strong performance across multiple tasks. It matches or exceeds state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK). The efficiency advantages are particularly compelling: Doc2VecC trains on billions of words per hour and generates document representations orders of magnitude faster than methods like Paragraph Vectors, which require expensive inference for unseen documents. The ablation study in Section 4.2 shows that Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism effectively suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words, as evidenced by the comparison of word embeddings in Table 3.

While the empirical evaluation is strong, I would have preferred more diverse tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification. The paper dedicates significant space to t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) that, while visually appealing, don't substantially advance the core argument compared to more quantitative analysis. The paper could have used this space to explore additional tasks or deeper analysis of the corruption mechanism's effects.

For further improvement, I recommend:
1. Including a comparison with more recent document representation methods beyond the baselines listed in Section 4.1
2. Providing more detailed analysis of how the corruption rate q affects performance across different tasks
3. Adding a discussion of how Doc2VecC handles out-of-vocabulary words compared to other methods
4. Expanding the semantic relatedness experiments to include more nuanced evaluation metrics

I have two specific questions for the authors:
1. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors on word analogy tasks, while Paragraph Vectors performs poorly. Given that Paragraph Vectors was designed to capture document semantics through unique document vectors, could you elaborate on why the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC leads to better word embeddings compared to Paragraph Vectors, particularly regarding the preservation of semantic relationships between words?

2. In Section 4.5, the paper notes that Doc2VecC performs better than Skip-thought Vectors on longer paragraphs (IMDB) but slightly worse on shorter sentences (SICK). Could you share more insights about how the corruption mechanism specifically adapts to different text lengths, and whether there are optimal corruption rates for different text lengths that could improve performance across both domains?
