

This paper proposes learning document embeddings by summing constituent word embeddings, which are jointly learned and subject to random dropout ("corruption") during training. While the individual components of this approach are not novel, the resulting method provides an efficient algorithm for document representation with strong empirical performance.  

Joint training of word and document embeddings is not a new concept, nor is the constraint that a document must be represented as the sum of its word embeddings (as demonstrated in "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). Additionally, the proposed corruption mechanism is equivalent to standard input-layer dropout. Combined with a word2vec-style loss and training procedure, this work does not substantially advance the state of the art in terms of novelty.  

Nevertheless, the model excels in generation efficiency, requiring only the average (or sum) of word embeddings rather than the complex inference step characteristic of Doc2Vec. By design, the embedding captures salient global document information—specifically, the features most relevant for local-context prediction. For its simplicity, the model achieves encouraging performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks.  

Overall, despite the lack of novelty, the model's simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results warrant broader attention and further study. I recommend acceptance.
