

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel framework for document representation learning that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation lies in a corruption model that introduces data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The authors claim Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods in sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient in both training and inference.

I recommend rejection. While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a simple, efficient approach, the technical contributions do not advance the field sufficiently. The core idea of using average word embeddings for document representation has been explored in prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), and the corruption mechanism is similar to dropout regularization. The paper's main selling point—simplicity and speed—does not constitute a significant technical contribution when compared to the substantial body of prior work in document representation.

The paper's primary weakness is insufficient evidence to support its claim that Doc2VecC represents "the best way to learn document representations." The experimental evaluation is limited to two main tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification), with semantic relatedness being a secondary task that lacks sufficient detail. To convincingly demonstrate the superiority of their approach, the authors should show consistent performance improvements across a broader range of document representation tasks and datasets. The paper currently lacks compelling evidence that Doc2VecC's advantages are generalizable beyond the specific benchmarks presented.

Another significant concern is the paper's failure to properly position itself against the most relevant recent work. The authors claim their method outperforms Paragraph Vectors, but they do not compare against the most effective recent approaches, particularly bidirectional LSTM-based document representations, which have become the standard for many text understanding tasks. The paper's experimental section does not address how Doc2VecC compares to these dominant approaches, making it difficult to assess the true novelty and value of the proposed method.

Regarding the technical claims, the paper's assertion that the corruption model introduces "data-dependent regularization" requires stronger theoretical justification. The paper presents a Taylor expansion argument, but it doesn't clearly demonstrate how this regularization mechanism specifically improves performance over simpler approaches like standard Word2Vec with IDF weighting. The paper's claim that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training" lacks sufficient empirical validation.

For additional feedback to improve the paper, I recommend the authors: (1) expand their experimental evaluation to include more document representation tasks and datasets; (2) directly compare against state-of-the-art bidirectional LSTM approaches; (3) provide a more thorough theoretical analysis of how the corruption mechanism specifically improves document representations; and (4) clarify the relationship between their approach and prior work on averaging word embeddings.

I have several questions that would help clarify the paper's claims:

1. For the RNN-LM baseline, was the language model trained to minimize classification error, or was it trained as a standard language model? If the former, how was the classification objective integrated with the language modeling objective?

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors, but Paragraph Vectors have been largely superseded by more recent approaches like Doc2Vec (Le & Mikolov, 2014) and transformer-based models. How does Doc2VecC compare to these more recent document representation methods?

3. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." However, the paper doesn't explain why removing local context would be beneficial for document representation learning, especially since local context typically contains important semantic information.

4. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." What specific evidence supports this claim? How does the corruption model specifically improve the semantic quality of the averaged embeddings compared to standard averaging?

5. The paper states that Doc2VecC "produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec." However, the experiments on word analogy tasks are limited to a single dataset. How does this improvement generalize across different datasets and tasks?
