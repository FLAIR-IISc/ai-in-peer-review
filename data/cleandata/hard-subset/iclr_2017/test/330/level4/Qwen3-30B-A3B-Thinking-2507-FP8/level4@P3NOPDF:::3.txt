

This paper introduces a document embedding method that computes document representations as the sum of constituent word embeddings, with word embeddings jointly learned and subject to dropout during training. While the core components—joint word-document embedding training, sum-based document representation, and dropout application—are individually well-established (e.g., Lebret and Collobert’s "The Sum of Its Parts" autoencoder framework, standard dropout mechanics), the paper’s synthesis yields a practical and efficient approach. The novelty claim is modest, as the approach does not introduce new theoretical constructs or algorithmic innovations beyond combining existing techniques with a word2vec-style loss.  

The efficiency of the method stands out as its primary strength. Unlike Doc2Vec, which requires iterative optimization for document vectors during inference, this model computes document embeddings in linear time via a simple sum of pre-trained word embeddings, eliminating complex inference steps. This makes it highly scalable for large-scale applications. Additionally, the training objective inherently prioritizes information relevant to local context prediction (e.g., surrounding words in a sentence), resulting in embeddings that capture meaningful global document semantics without explicit feature engineering. Empirical results on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks demonstrate competitive performance, often matching or exceeding baselines despite the model’s simplicity.  

While the lack of novelty is a notable limitation, the paper compensates through clear practical advantages. The approach is straightforward to implement, requires minimal hyperparameter tuning, and delivers strong performance with minimal computational overhead. For practitioners seeking efficient, interpretable document representations, this method offers a compelling alternative to more complex models. The empirical validation across multiple datasets further supports its utility, suggesting that the combination of simplicity and effectiveness warrants attention beyond its incremental theoretical contributions.  

Given the balance of practical value and robust empirical results, I believe this work merits publication at ICLR. It provides a valuable, accessible tool for the community, particularly for applications where inference speed and simplicity are critical. The paper’s clarity and thorough evaluation strengthen its case, making it a worthy addition to the conference proceedings. I recommend acceptance.
