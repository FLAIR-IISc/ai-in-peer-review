- Proposes Doc2VecC: document vectors as the average of word embeddings learned with a corruption-based regularizer.  
- Corruption randomly removes words during training, implicitly down-weighting common/uninformative words and favoring rare/informative ones.  
- This corruption-based regularization enables meaningful document embeddings via simple averaging, removing the need for post-processing.  
- Provides theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) showing corruption penalizes embeddings of common words while preserving semantic information.  
- Strong empirical results: matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on IMDB (sentiment), Wikipedia (classification), and SICK (semantic relatedness).  
- Demonstrates substantial efficiency gains: trains on billions of words per hour and produces document vectors orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors.  
- Ablation study (Section 4.2 / Table 3) supports that corruption suppresses embeddings of non-discriminative words.  
- t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) are visually appealing but add less value than further quantitative analyses would.  
- Reviewer would have liked more diverse downstream tasks beyond sentiment and document classification.  
- Suggested improvements: compare to more recent methods, analyze impact of corruption rate q across tasks, discuss out-of-vocabulary handling, and expand semantic relatedness metrics.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance, citing elegant simplicity, theoretical grounding, practical efficiency, and strong empirical performance.  
- Two author questions: why corruption yields better word embeddings than Paragraph Vectors for preserving semantic relationships, and how corruption rate/text length interact (optimal q for short vs. long texts).
