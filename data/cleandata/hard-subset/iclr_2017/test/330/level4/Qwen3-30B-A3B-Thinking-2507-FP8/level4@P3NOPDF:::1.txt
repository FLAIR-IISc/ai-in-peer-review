

This paper proposes a document representation learning method combining skip-gram training with a global context vector regularizer employing multiple dropout strategies. While the individual components (skip-gram, dropout, global context) are largely derivative, the specific integration and regularization mechanism present a novel contribution. The analysis in Section 3 is particularly strong, offering clear insights into model behavior and the impact of the proposed regularizer.

The primary weakness lies in the limited empirical evaluation. The authors focus exclusively on sentiment analysis and multi-class classification tasks—standard benchmarks that do not sufficiently demonstrate the method's broader utility. Crucially, the paper fails to compare against strong baselines (e.g., state-of-the-art contextual embeddings or recent regularized models), which undermines the claimed advantages. Furthermore, dedicating two-thirds of a page to t-SNE visualizations represents a poor allocation of space; this could have been replaced with additional analysis (e.g., ablation studies, comparisons on more diverse tasks, or exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity), strengthening the empirical claims significantly.

While I share the other reviewers' concerns about the insufficient evaluation scope and baseline comparisons, the paper's core contribution merits consideration. The method is computationally efficient, well-motivated, and clearly presented—features that enhance its practical appeal. The regularization technique, though not entirely unprecedented, offers a simple yet effective refinement to document embedding training that could benefit multiple downstream applications. The clarity of the exposition and the method's demonstrated efficiency further elevate its potential utility for the NLP community.

Given the novelty of the integration, the thorough theoretical analysis, and the method's practical efficiency, I recommend acceptance. The empirical limitations are notable but do not outweigh the paper's substantive contribution to the field. The authors should address the evaluation gaps in future work, but the current work provides a valuable, actionable approach that aligns well with ICLR's focus on impactful, well-executed research.
