

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple yet efficient framework for document representation learning. The core idea is to represent each document as the average of its word embeddings, learned jointly with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. This approach ensures the averaged embeddings capture semantic meaning while implicitly regularizing embeddings toward informative words. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while offering significant computational advantages in both training and test-time efficiency.

I recommend **acceptance** for two key reasons: (1) the model achieves strong empirical results across multiple benchmarks, and (2) its simplicity and efficiency provide a compelling practical alternative to existing methods, despite modest novelty.

The paper’s strengths are well-supported. Doc2VecC outperforms BoW by 15% on sentiment analysis (Table 1) and achieves the lowest classification error (30.24% at h=1000) on Wikipedia (Table 5), with t-SNE visualizations confirming superior cluster separation (Figure 3). Crucially, its test-time efficiency is exceptional: generating representations for 25,000 IMDB reviews takes 7 seconds versus Paragraph Vectors’ 4 minutes 17 seconds (Table 2). This stems from its core design—document vectors are simply the average of word embeddings, eliminating the need for expensive inference on unseen documents. The corruption mechanism (equivalent to dropout on the document input) introduces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common non-discriminative words (Table 3), reducing reliance on post-hoc techniques like subsampling frequent words (Section 4.2). The empirical validation across three tasks and thorough ablation studies (e.g., comparing corruption rates, regularization effects) robustly support the claims.

Regarding novelty, the approach builds on established ideas: the compositionality of word embeddings (Mikolov et al., 2013b) and dropout for regularization (Wager et al., 2013) are well-known. The paper correctly cites similar work (e.g., Lebret & Collobert on autoencoders for word/phrase representations), and the corruption mechanism is indeed a straightforward application of dropout. However, the *integration* of these elements into a single, highly efficient framework for document representation—particularly the elegant avoidance of document-specific parameters and inference steps—represents a meaningful practical contribution. The model’s simplicity (training on billions of words/hour) and strong performance make it a valuable tool for practitioners, even if the theoretical novelty is incremental.

For further improvement, I suggest:
1. **Clarify the novelty contribution** in the abstract/introduction. Explicitly state that the primary innovation is the *efficient implementation* of a known compositional approach via corruption-induced regularization, not a new theoretical framework.
2. **Compare more directly with the "sum of word embeddings" baseline** used in Huang et al. (2012). The paper mentions this but doesn’t quantify how Doc2VecC’s corruption mechanism improves upon their non-corrupted averaging.
3. **Add a sentence explaining why the corruption rate q=0.9 was optimal** (Section 4.2), as the paper notes the method is "not sensitive" to q but doesn’t justify this choice.

To fully assess the claims, please clarify:
- How does Doc2VecC’s regularization specifically interact with the *semantic* properties of words (e.g., does it preserve analogies better than Word2Vec, as Table 5 suggests)? The word analogy results (Section 4.3) hint at this but could be tied more explicitly to the corruption mechanism.
- In the semantic relatedness task (Section 4.5), Skip-thought vectors performed worse than Doc2VecC on IMDB but better on SICK. Given Doc2VecC’s strength on longer documents (per your IMDB hypothesis), could you analyze how paragraph length distribution affects model performance across datasets? This would strengthen the argument for Doc2VecC’s suitability in specific scenarios.
