- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings with a training-time corruption model that randomly removes words.
- Corruption mechanism acts as data-dependent regularization, suppressing common non-discriminative words and preserving rare informative ones.
- Demonstrates Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
- Claims exceptional computational efficiency: trains on billions of words per hour on a single machine and enables immediate test-time representation via averaging.
- Recommendation: accept — compelling contribution showing averaging + corruption suffices for strong document representations.
- Strength: clear theoretical justification (Section 3.1) explaining how corruption induces regularization for averaging-based representations.
- Strength: comprehensive experimental comparison against multiple baselines across three tasks with consistent gains.
- Strength: thorough explanation and empirical evidence of efficiency advantages, including training and inference time comparisons.
- Main limitation: empirical scope is narrow—could include more diverse or challenging tasks to show broader applicability.
- Criticism: t-SNE visualizations occupy significant space without adding insights beyond quantitative metrics.
- Suggestion 1: expand evaluation to a more diverse set of tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification.
- Suggestion 2: replace t-SNE with additional quantitative analyses, especially on corruption-rate effects.
- Suggestion 3: provide detailed discussion and results showing how corruption rate q affects regularization strength and performance.
- Suggestion 4: clarify relationship to prior work on averaging word embeddings, notably Huang et al. (2012).
- Question 1: how does Doc2VecC's enforcement of semantics via averaging differ from Huang et al. (2012) and what specific advantage does corruption provide?
- Question 2: why are word embedding improvements over Word2Vec modest on analogy tasks despite the corruption mechanism?
- Question 3: could Skip-thought's poor sentiment results be due to lack of fine-tuning on IMDB rather than paragraph length differences?
- Overall: strong, well-motivated, efficient, and simple method that merits publication despite recommendable expansions in empirical analysis.
