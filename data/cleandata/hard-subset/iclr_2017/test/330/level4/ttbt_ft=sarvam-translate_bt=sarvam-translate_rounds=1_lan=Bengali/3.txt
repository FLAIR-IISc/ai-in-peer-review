This paper proposes learning document embedding as a collection of component word embeddings, which are jointly learned and randomly dropped ('corrupted') during training. While no part of this model is particularly new, its results are an efficient learning algorithm for document representation with good empirical performance.

Joint training of word and document embeddings is not a new concept, nor is the idea of representing a document by its collection of word embeddings (see, for example, "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebrite and Colbert). Furthermore, this 'corruption' process is nothing more than traditional dropout at the input level. Combined with loss and training methods like word2vec, this paper offers very little in terms of novelty.

On the other hand, it is very efficient when generating, requiring only the average of word embeddings instead of complex inference steps like Doc2Vec. Moreover, structurally, embedding contains important global information about the document – it specifically contains information that helps in local-context prediction. For such a simple model, its performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the lack of novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and performance of this model make it worthy of wider readership and study, and I recommend adopting it.
