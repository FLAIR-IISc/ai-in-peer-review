Summary
The paper proposes a simple framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization that suppresses common words (driving them toward zero) and up-weights informative or rare words. Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification indicate lower error rates than several baseline document embedding methods. The approach is attractive for its simplicity, speed, and ease of implementation.

Strengths
- Clear motivation: representing documents with minimal architectural complexity while emphasizing informative words.
- Practicality: fast, scalable, and easy to integrate into existing pipelines.
- Empirical promise: competitive results on two standard tasks.
- Potential interpretability: learned word weights could reveal which words are deemed informative.

Weaknesses and Concerns
- Limited technical novelty: the core idea resembles weighted averaging or IDF-style weighting combined with shrinkage/regularization. Most components appear incremental, and the paper does not articulate a strong theoretical contribution.
- Narrow empirical scope: results are shown on only two tasks. Given the simplicity of the method, stronger claims require broader validation across diverse datasets and settings.
- Missing comparisons to strong neural baselines: in particular, bidirectional LSTMs that concatenate final states (a widely used baseline), CNN-based encoders, and hierarchical models. Without these, it is hard to assess the method’s standing against current practice.
- Insufficient clarity on baseline implementations: details for RNN-based baselines are unclear (training objective, representation extraction), which could materially affect comparisons.
- Limited analysis: no ablation isolating the benefit of the proposed regularization over simple averaging or classical TF-IDF-weighted averaging, and no analysis of how learned weights relate to word frequency/IDF.

Requests for Clarification
- RNN-LM baseline:
  - Was the model trained purely as a language model or fine-tuned to minimize classification error?
  - Which document representation was used: the final hidden state, the average of hidden states, max/mean pooling over time, or something else?
  - If a bidirectional variant was used, did you concatenate final forward/backward states or pool over the sequence?
- Embedding training:
  - Were word embeddings pre-trained, learned from scratch, or fine-tuned? Are they shared across tasks?
  - How are OOV and rare words handled (e.g., subword features)?
- Regularization details:
  - Precisely define the data-dependent regularizer and its optimization. Are weights learned per word, tied to frequency, or parameterized otherwise?
  - How is the regularization strength chosen? Is there a risk of overemphasizing spurious rare words, and how is this mitigated?
- Experimental protocol:
  - Which baselines were tuned with comparable effort and search space?
  - Are improvements statistically significant across runs/seeds?

Suggested Experiments and Analyses
- Stronger baselines:
  - Compare against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final states, as well as pooled BiLSTM/GRU encoders.
  - Include CNN-based and hierarchical attention baselines where appropriate.
  - Include simple but strong non-neural baselines such as TF-IDF with logistic regression and fast linear text classifiers.
  - Compare to paragraph vectors/doc2vec and fastText-style averaging with n-grams.
- Ablations:
  - Uniform averaging vs. your regularized weighting.
  - TF-IDF-weighted averaging vs. your learned weights.
  - Sensitivity to the regularization coefficient and to vocabulary pruning.
  - Correlation between learned word weights and IDF/frequency; qualitative examples of up-/down-weighted words.
- Broader evaluation:
  - Add more tasks and domains (e.g., topic classification and long-form sentiment). Demonstrating robustness to document length and domain shift would strengthen the claim of generality.
  - Report runtime and memory relative to RNN/CNN baselines to substantiate the speed/efficiency claim.
  - Evaluate stability across random seeds and dataset sizes (low-resource vs. full data).
- Practical details:
  - Clarify hyperparameters, optimization settings, early stopping criteria, and preprocessing choices to ensure reproducibility.
  - If possible, release code or a minimal reference implementation.

Overall Assessment
The paper addresses an important and practical problem with a method that is simple, efficient, and empirically promising. However, the technical novelty is limited, and the current empirical evidence is not broad or compelling enough to justify strong claims about being the “best” or even a generally preferable way to learn document representations. Clearer baseline definitions and stronger comparisons—especially to bidirectional RNNs and other competitive encoders—are necessary. Ablations and analyses that disentangle the contribution of the regularization from simple averaging or TF-IDF would also improve the paper.

Recommendation: Weak reject. I would be inclined to reconsider if the authors (1) add rigorous comparisons to strong neural and non-neural baselines, including bidirectional LSTMs; (2) provide thorough ablations and interpretability analyses of the learned weights; and (3) expand the evaluation to more tasks and datasets with careful reporting of efficiency and statistical significance.

Confidence: Medium.
