This paper presents a method that constructs document embeddings as the sum of their constituent word embeddings, with both word and document vectors trained jointly and randomly dropped (“corrupted”) during training. While none of these components are particularly original, the approach yields an efficient procedure for learning document representations with strong empirical results.

Jointly training word and document embeddings is established practice, as is constraining the document vector to be the sum of its word vectors (cf. “The Sum of Its Parts”: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). The corruption step is simply standard input-layer dropout. Combined with a word2vec-style objective and optimization, the paper offers limited novelty.

On the positive side, it is very efficient at inference time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than the more complex inference procedure used in Doc2Vec. Additionally, by design, the representation encodes salient global information about the document—specifically the information that facilitates local-context prediction. Given the model’s simplicity, the results on sentiment analysis and document classification are quite compelling.

Overall, despite the novelty gap, the model’s simplicity, efficiency, and empirical performance merit broader attention, and I recommend acceptance.
