This paper proposes a simple framework for document representations: a document vector is the average of its word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization that upweights informative or rare words and pushes frequent words toward zero. Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification show that the method achieves the lowest error rates relative to baseline document embedding approaches.

I appreciate the motivation of finding an effective way to encode a document into a vector, and the simplicity and speed of the approach are appealing. However, the paper offers limited technical novelty; most of the techniques are not new, and the primary contribution appears to be a streamlined, efficient instantiation rather than a fundamentally new method. Given this, I would like to see stronger empirical validation across a broader set of tasks to substantiate the claim that this is the best way to learn document representations. Results on only two tasks are not sufficient to be fully convincing.

Clarifications and comparisons that would strengthen the paper:
- For the RNN-LM baseline, is the model trained to minimize classification error directly, or is it trained purely as a language model? What representation is used for classification—the final hidden state or the average of all hidden states?
- A widely used approach for document representation is a bidirectional LSTM with the final forward and backward hidden states concatenated. It would be very helpful to report how the proposed method compares to this BiLSTM representation on document classification and sentiment analysis.

Overall, the method is well-motivated and empirically strong on the reported tasks, but the technical contribution is modest. A more comprehensive evaluation (beyond two tasks) and clearer comparisons against strong RNN-based baselines, especially bidirectional LSTMs, are needed to fully assess the approach.
