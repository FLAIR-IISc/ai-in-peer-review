1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. Each document is represented as the average of its word embeddings, and both word and document representations are learned jointly using a word2vec-style objective. During training, the document input is “corrupted” by randomly dropping words (unbiased dropout), which the authors analyze as inducing a data-dependent regularization that shrinks embeddings for frequent, non-discriminative words while preserving informative ones. The approach scales well, produces high-quality word embeddings, and yields fast test-time inference (a simple average). Empirically, the method matches or outperforms strong baselines on sentiment analysis and document classification, and is competitive on semantic relatedness, all with a much simpler architecture and lower computational cost than prior methods such as Paragraph Vectors.

2. Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong empirical performance with a remarkably simple and efficient model, especially at test time (no inference step; averaging only).
- Clear practical value despite limited novelty: the combination of input corruption and averaging is well-motivated and effective, and the analysis provides intuition for why it works.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and approach: The paper targets efficient, scalable document representation learning that captures global semantics useful for predicting local context. Representing documents as the average of word embeddings aligns with prior observations on compositionality in embedding spaces, and the corruption mechanism emphasizes informative words by diminishing frequent stopwords.
- Novelty: The components are largely known. Representing a document as the sum/average of its word embeddings and jointly learning word and document representations have been explored (e.g., Lebret & Collobert’s “The Sum of Its Parts”). The corruption mechanism is standard dropout applied to the input. The training objective and optimization follow word2vec. As such, conceptual novelty is limited.
- Motivation and placement: Despite limited novelty, the paper is well positioned in the literature and makes a compelling case for the design. The analysis linking corruption to data-dependent regularization, while closely related to prior work on dropout, helps explain the improved focus on informative words and the resulting empirical gains.
- Empirical support and rigor: The results on IMDB sentiment and a Wikipedia document classification task are strong and consistent with the stated claims. The method achieves comparable or better performance than Paragraph Vectors while being far more efficient at test time. The semantic relatedness results are competitive given the model’s simplicity and training budget. The speed comparisons and practical scalability are convincing.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify novelty: More explicitly position Doc2VecC relative to (i) Lebret & Collobert’s autoencoder-based averaging, (ii) marginalized/denoising autoencoders, and (iii) PV-DBOW and PV-DM variants. A concise comparison table of objectives and inference-time costs would help.
- Ablations: Include ablations for (a) no corruption vs corruption, (b) with/without local context (to isolate the contribution beyond a document-only denoising AE), (c) IDF weighting vs pure averaging at test time, and (d) subsampling frequent words. This would solidify the claim that Doc2VecC “naturally” suppresses frequent words without heuristic weighting.
- Sensitivity analyses: Report sensitivity to corruption rate q, embedding dimension, number of negative samples, context window size, and document length. The IMDB discussion suggests length matters; showing performance vs length would be informative.
- Fairness of baselines: Ensure preprocessing is matched across baselines (tokenization, vocabulary cutoff, subsampling). The Skip-thought comparison is informative but likely disadvantaged by very long documents; state this caveat clearly in the main text.
- Word analogy: The hypothesis that PV focuses capacity on document vectors at the expense of word embeddings is interesting; consider an experiment where PV word vectors are further fine-tuned without document vectors, or report PV-DBOW vs PV-DM more thoroughly.
- Reproducibility: The code link is appreciated. Please include detailed training hyperparameters (optimizer, learning rates, negative sampling rates, epochs), hardware specs for reported times, and any tricks (e.g., gradient clipping, normalization).

Questions for the authors
- What is the precise conceptual difference between Doc2VecC and applying standard input dropout to a bag-of-embeddings model trained with a word2vec CBoW objective? Beyond the analysis, is there any architectural or training nuance that is novel?
- Do you average word embeddings uniformly at test time, or do you apply any weighting (e.g., IDF)? Are embeddings normalized before averaging?
- Which embeddings are used to form document vectors—the “input” embeddings U only, or any combination with the “output” embeddings V?
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q? Please provide a plot or table showing performance and training speed vs q.
- Can you report an ablation removing the local context during training (the DEA-like variant) to quantify the contribution of the local-context term?
- How does performance vary with document length (short sentences vs long reviews)? Does the model degrade on very short inputs relative to LSTM/Skip-thought?
- In the unsupervised representation learning that includes test data (IMDB), how do you ensure there is no label leakage? Please clarify the protocol.
- For the word analogy experiments, were all models trained on identical corpora with matched preprocessing and hyperparameters? Please detail negative sampling, subsampling thresholds, and dimension settings.

Overall, while the method’s components are not novel, the paper offers a compelling, well-executed, and practical approach with strong empirical results and excellent efficiency. I recommend acceptance.
