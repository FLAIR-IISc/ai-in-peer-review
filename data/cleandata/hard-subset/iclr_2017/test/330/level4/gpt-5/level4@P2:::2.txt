Summary
This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. Each document is represented as the average of its word embeddings, learned jointly with a corruption (masking) process that acts as a data-dependent regularizer. The corruption encourages embeddings of frequent, uninformative words to shrink toward zero while preserving informative or rare words. The model is lightweight (parameters scale with vocabulary, not corpus size), trains quickly via negative sampling, and produces test-time document vectors by a single average. Empirically, the method reports competitive or better performance than several baselines on sentiment analysis and document classification, with notable speed advantages.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) Limited technical novelty—the core ideas (averaging embeddings, denoising/dropout-style corruption, negative sampling) are largely known, and the theoretical justification is a standard second-order Taylor argument. (2) Empirical evaluation is not sufficiently broad against current strong baselines (e.g., bidirectional LSTM/CNN/Hierarchical encoders), making it hard to conclude this is the best general-purpose approach to document representation.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets efficient, scalable document embeddings that preserve semantics without per-document parameters. This is well motivated relative to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, and the efficiency benefits are clear.
- Placement in literature: The approach sits between Word2Vec with post-hoc averaging and Paragraph Vectors. The corruption-as-regularization connection is related to denoising autoencoders and dropout; these links are acknowledged but could be more thoroughly contrasted.
- Technical contribution: The main novelty is enforcing, during training, that simple averaging yields meaningful document vectors, together with an unbiased masking corruption. The theoretical analysis (expected objective via a Taylor expansion) explains the data-dependent shrinkage but does not introduce fundamentally new theory or estimation techniques.
- Claims vs. evidence: The reported results show solid improvements over several baselines on the included tasks, and the speed claims are credible given the architecture. However, conclusions about state-of-the-art document representations are weakened by missing comparisons to widely used neural encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/GRU/CNN and hierarchical variants) that are standard for sentiment and document classification. It is also unclear how the RNN-LM baseline was trained and how its representation was formed, making that comparison hard to interpret.
- Strengths: Simplicity, ease of deployment, strong training and inference efficiency, and a clear mechanism that suppresses stopwords and other uninformative tokens. The empirical gains over classical baselines and Word2Vec+IDF are encouraging.
- Weaknesses: Incremental novelty; limited breadth of evaluation; some important baselines and ablations are missing (e.g., effect of corruption rate, removing global or local context, different negative sampling distributions).

Suggestions for improvement (not all affect the current score)
- Broaden comparisons:
  - Include strong, widely used neural baselines: bidirectional LSTM/GRU encoders (concatenate final states), CNN-based document encoders, and hierarchical encoders for long documents.
  - Consider FastText-style bag-of-ngrams baselines as a very strong lightweight competitor.
- Clarify and strengthen the empirical study:
  - Add ablations: (i) no corruption, (ii) different corruption rates q, (iii) remove the local context term (global-only), (iv) alternative document scalings (with/without 1/T), (v) Word2Vec averaging with/without IDF and subsampling under identical preprocessing.
  - Report statistical significance and variance across runs.
  - Evaluate on additional tasks/datasets, including longer/shorter texts, and show robustness to domain shifts.
- Baseline details and fairness:
  - Specify the negative sampling distribution (Word2Vec commonly uses unigram^0.75, whereas the paper mentions uniform); discuss impact on results.
  - For Paragraph Vectors, clarify which variants (DM, DBOW, or both) and tuning used, and ensure fair hyperparameter search.
  - For Skip-thought and other transfer baselines, discuss sequence length sensitivity and provide alternatives better suited for long documents.
- Analysis and clarity:
  - Provide more intuition on why averaging + corruption suffices when local context is present, and how much each contributes.
  - Visualize or quantify the shrinkage effect beyond a word list (e.g., plot embedding norms vs. frequency/informativeness).
  - Discuss limitations: scenarios where averaging may underperform (e.g., order-sensitive tasks, long-range discourse).

Questions for the authors
- RNN-LM baseline:
  - Was the RNN trained purely as a language model or with a classification objective? How was the document representation formed (final hidden state, average/max over time, or something else)? Were both unidirectional and bidirectional variants considered?
- Strong neural baselines:
  - How does Doc2VecC compare to a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final states as the document embedding for sentiment and document classification? Please include results and training/inference time comparisons.
- Negative sampling:
  - What negative sampling distribution was used (uniform vs. unigram^0.75)? Have you evaluated sensitivity to this choice?
- Ablations:
  - What is the performance without corruption, and how does it vary with q? What is the impact of removing local context or the 1/T scaling in the global term?
- Paragraph Vectors:
  - Which variants (DM/DBOW) and hyperparameters were used? Did you include PV-DBOW, which can be strong despite lacking word embeddings?
- Preprocessing:
  - How were frequent-word subsampling and IDF weighting handled across baselines to ensure fairness?
- Generalization:
  - Can you report results on additional datasets and on shorter texts to test the claim that Doc2VecC is robust to length?
