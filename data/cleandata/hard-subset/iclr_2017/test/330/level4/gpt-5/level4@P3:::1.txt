Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, but to learn those embeddings with a CBOW-style objective that conditions on both local context and a corrupted global document context. The corruption randomly masks words in the document (with unbiased rescaling), which the authors show induces a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings for frequent, non-informative words while preserving informative or rare ones. The model decouples parameter size from corpus size, trains quickly with negative sampling, and yields test-time document vectors by a single average of learned embeddings. Empirically, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, and produces word embeddings that perform well on analogy tasks. The paper also provides a clear derivation via a second-order Taylor expansion that explains the regularization effect of corruption.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) An elegant, well-motivated objective that couples a global corrupted context with a CBOW predictor, accompanied by a solid analytical justification of the induced regularization; (2) Strong practical appeal due to speed, simplicity, and competitive accuracy, with particularly compelling test-time efficiency.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and motivation: While the components (averaging, dropout/corruption, negative sampling) are known, the way they are combined here is insightful. Crucially, learning is structured so that simple averaging at test time is not a heuristic but an architectural constraint, and the corruption scheme is theoretically grounded as a data-dependent regularizer.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion argument is clear and connects the corruption mechanism to penalizing frequent, uninformative words—explaining improved document vectors and word embeddings. This aligns with intuitions from dropout while being specialized to the document-averaging setting.
- Empirical performance: Across sentiment (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), semantic relatedness (SICK), and word analogy, the method is competitive or superior to Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and Skip-thought on long texts. The speed advantages at both training and inference are significant; the decoupling of parameters from corpus size is practically important.
- Practicality: Test-time encoding is a single pass and extremely fast; training is simple and scales, making the approach easy to adopt.

Areas for improvement (not gating the decision)
- Experimental breadth and baselines: Although the paper includes several tasks, the supervised text classification baselines could be stronger and more contemporary (e.g., FastText/Bag of Tricks for Efficient Text Classification). For SICK, comparisons to more recent sentence-averaging baselines (e.g., strong IDF/SIF-weighted averages) would be informative. A clearer discussion of domain mismatch (e.g., Skip-thought trained on books) would help.
- Ablations: The paper would benefit from systematic ablations: removing the global document vector; removing corruption; varying the corruption rate q; using different negative sampling distributions; and assessing sensitivity to context window size and embedding dimension. This would strengthen the causal link from the proposed ingredients to the gains.
- Space allocation: The t-SNE figures are visually appealing but could be shortened in favor of deeper quantitative analyses (e.g., frequency-binned performance, clustering metrics, or task-specific error analyses).
- Fairness and details: Ensure hyperparameters for baselines (especially Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought) are well tuned and comparable; clarify negative sampling distribution (Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75 rather than uniform).

Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling: You state Pv is uniform. Did you also try the standard unigram^0.75 distribution? How does this choice affect both word analogy and downstream tasks?
- Corruption rate and ablations: How sensitive are results to q? Please provide a small study of q ∈ {0.5, 0.7, 0.9} and an ablation with no corruption. Similarly, what is the impact of removing local context (i.e., using only the global document vector) and vice versa?
- Subsampling and weighting: You mention subsampling frequent words (a Word2Vec trick). How much do results depend on this? Does Doc2VecC still outperform Word2Vec+IDF when subsampling is disabled? At test time, does adding IDF weighting on top of averaging further help or is it redundant?
- Paragraph Vectors: PV often requires careful tuning and inference settings. What dimensions, training epochs, and inference steps did you use? Did you evaluate both PV-DM and PV-DBOW under comparable budgets? Any attempt to regularize PV to reduce reliance on per-document parameters?
- Fairness of pretraining: For SICK and IMDB, can you clarify pretraining corpora and vocabulary alignment across methods, especially for Skip-thought and RNN baselines? To what extent do domain differences (books vs reviews/news) explain performance gaps?
- Implementation details: Are input and output embeddings tied or separate (U vs V)? Is the document representation built only from U? Any benefit from tying weights or using averaged (U+V)/2 as the word embedding for the document vector?
- Efficiency claims: Please report tokens/sec and wall-clock for key setups (and CPU/GPU specs) to substantiate “billions of words per hour” and the <2 hours claim on the book corpus.
- Very long documents: How does performance scale with document length? Does the 1/T normalization and unbiased rescaling interact in any adverse way for very long or very short documents?
- Reproducibility: The code link is appreciated. Please include scripts, seeds, and full hyperparameters for all baselines, along with preprocessed data splits.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, simple, and practical method with a clear theoretical story and strong efficiency-profile. With a few additional experiments and clarifications, the paper would be even more compelling. I recommend acceptance.
