Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, an efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings learned jointly with a word-prediction objective. During training, the document “global context” is formed by a stochastically corrupted (masked) version of the document’s bag-of-words; at test time, a document vector is simply the average of its word embeddings. The corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words toward zero while preserving informative ones. The method decouples model size from the number of documents, trains as quickly as Word2Vec-style methods, and yields fast test-time inference. Across sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK), Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines, and its word embeddings show substantial gains over Word2Vec on the word-analogy benchmark. The paper also provides a useful Taylor-expansion analysis that makes the regularization effect explicit.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical results coupled with extreme simplicity and test-time efficiency; (2) A clear and well-motivated connection between corruption and data-dependent regularization that explains why simple averaging suffices.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed (Q1): The paper targets the classic challenge of obtaining compact, semantically meaningful, and efficient document representations that scale in training and are trivial to compute at test time.

- Motivation and placement in literature (Q2): The approach builds on well-known components—CBOW/negative sampling objectives, averaging word embeddings, and input corruption—but places them together in a way that directly enforces compositionality during training and yields a principled, fast document encoder. The analysis connects the corruption to adaptive regularization, which is a helpful unifying perspective (related to dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders). The paper situates itself among Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, SDA/DAE, and Skip-Thought, and discusses strengths/limitations of each.

- Claims and rigor (Q3): The qualitative and quantitative results generally support the paper’s claims:
  • Efficiency: Training and inference costs are convincingly reported; inferring representations for unseen documents is orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors.
  • Effectiveness: Doc2VecC performs comparably or better than Doc2Vec and other baselines on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, and is competitive on SICK. The improved word-analogy performance indicates that the learned embeddings are stronger than Word2Vec’s in this setting.
  • Regularization effect: The derivation and the empirical analysis (small-norm stopwords vs informative words) align well and explain observed gains over naive averaging.

Novelty: Conceptual novelty is moderate—the pieces (averaging, input dropout/corruption, CBOW-style training) are known, and related ideas (e.g., autoencoder-based composition, Paragraph Vectors, marginalized denoising) exist. However, the particular formulation, its analysis, and the demonstrated practical benefits form a coherent and useful contribution with clear impact.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify the exact Paragraph Vectors variant(s) used (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW) and ensure both are included in comparisons, as PV-DBOW can be competitive for document classification.
- Negative sampling distribution: Word2Vec commonly uses the unigram^0.75 distribution; the paper states a uniform distribution. Please discuss this choice, provide ablations, and confirm that baselines use their standard settings.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  • Remove local context to show the incremental value of global context (you partially discuss DEA as a limit case; a controlled ablation would help).
  • Vary corruption rate q (you state limited sensitivity and use q=0.9); include a plot for accuracy vs q and training time vs q.
  • Show performance when subsampling frequent words is disabled in both Doc2VecC and Word2Vec baselines to isolate the corruption effect.
- Fairness of baselines: For Skip-Thought, the documents are much longer than its typical domain. Consider adding hierarchical encoders or CNN/LSTM baselines tuned for long documents (e.g., region-CNN or hierarchical attention) for a more balanced comparison.
- Analysis clarity: The Taylor expansion section is helpful. Consider tightening notation, explicitly stating assumptions (e.g., independence leading to diagonal Σ) and adding a short intuitive summary before the math.
- Word-embedding quality: Include cosine-similarity neighbors for a few sentiment-heavy and topic words to illustrate the shrinkage of non-informative words and the preservation of semantics.
- Reporting details: Provide full hyperparameters for all baselines, training epochs, early stopping criteria, and compute resources for reproducibility. Clean up the references formatting.

Questions for the authors
1) Paragraph Vectors: Which variants did you implement and report? If PV-DBOW was excluded, can you add it or justify its omission? How does Doc2VecC compare to PV-DBOW in speed and accuracy?

2) Negative sampling: Why uniform sampling rather than unigram^0.75? How do results change with the standard distribution and with different numbers of negatives?

3) Corruption rate and document length: How does performance vary with q across short vs long documents? Is there an interaction between q and subsampling of frequent words?

4) Role of local vs global context: Can you quantify the marginal contribution of including local context Uc compared to using only the corrupted global document vector?

5) Transfer and domain shift: Have you evaluated training Doc2VecC on a large external corpus and transferring to downstream tasks without fine-tuning? How does it compare to Skip-Thought in true zero-shot settings?

6) OOV handling and vocabulary growth: For the SICK setup, you extend the vocabulary on the target data. How robust is this procedure when OOV rates are higher? Any risk of overfitting during the fine-tuning step?

7) Regularization analysis: Your derivation suggests stronger shrinkage for frequent, non-informative words. Can you provide a corpus-level statistic (e.g., correlation between word frequency and embedding norm) and contrast it with Word2Vec?

8) Practical guidance: Given a budget, what defaults (embedding size, q, negatives, window size) do you recommend? Any known failure modes (e.g., very short texts, highly specialized jargon)?

Overall, the paper offers an appealing balance of simplicity, speed, and accuracy, with a clear explanation for why it works. Addressing the questions and suggested analyses would further strengthen the contribution.
