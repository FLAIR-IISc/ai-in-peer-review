1. Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a document representation framework that encodes each document as the mean of its constituent word embeddings, combined with a data-dependent regularization that emphasizes informative or rare words while driving embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words toward zero.
- The approach is motivated by simplicity and efficiency, aiming to preserve document-level semantics via averaging during training rather than post hoc aggregation.
- Empirical results on sentiment analysis and document classification indicate that the method achieves the lowest error rates among the compared document embedding baselines, while also being fast to train and deploy.

2. Decision
- Reject, primarily due to limited technical novelty and insufficient breadth of empirical validation to substantiate the central claim that this is the preferred approach for learning document representations.

3. Supporting arguments
- Many of the underlying components are established (averaging of word embeddings, corruption/dropout-style regularization, and negative sampling), and the main advantages emphasized are computational simplicity and speed rather than a fundamentally new modeling idea.
- Given the modest methodological contribution, stronger and broader empirical evidence is needed. Demonstrating robust gains across more than two tasks would be important to justify the claim that this is the best way to learn document vectors.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Expanding the experimental suite beyond sentiment analysis and document classification would strengthen the paper’s case and its generality claims.
- Clarify the setup and reporting for sequential baselines to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons with mainstream alternatives used in practice.
- Include a competitive, widely used baseline based on bidirectional LSTMs for document encoding (e.g., concatenation of final forward/backward states) to contextualize the proposed method’s strengths and weaknesses on common benchmarks.

Questions for the authors
- For the RNN-LM baseline: was the model trained to minimize classification error directly, or purely as a language model? Which representation was used downstream—the final hidden state or an average over all hidden states?
- How does the proposed method compare to a bidirectional LSTM document encoder that concatenates the final hidden states, on tasks like sentiment analysis and document classification?
