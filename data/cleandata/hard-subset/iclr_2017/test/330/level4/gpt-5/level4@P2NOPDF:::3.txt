This paper presents a document representation model in which a document vector is the sum (or average) of its constituent word embeddings. Word and document embeddings are learned jointly, and during training individual words are randomly dropped (corrupted) via input-layer dropout. While the components are well known, their combination yields an efficient approach with strong empirical results.

The novelty is limited. Joint training of word and document embeddings, as well as additive document composition, has been explored before (e.g., Lebret and Collobert, “The Sum of Its Parts”: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders). The corruption mechanism is standard input dropout, and the objective and training largely follow the word2vec paradigm. As a result, the methodological contribution is incremental.

That said, the paper’s practical strengths are clear. At inference time, computing a document vector requires only averaging word embeddings, avoiding the more involved inference step of Doc2Vec. By construction, the representation encodes global document signals that are predictive of local context. Given the model’s simplicity, the reported performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is notably strong.

Overall, despite the lack of conceptual novelty, the approach is simple, efficient, and empirically effective. I recommend acceptance.
