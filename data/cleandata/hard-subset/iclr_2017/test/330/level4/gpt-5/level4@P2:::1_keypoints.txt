- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as the average of word embeddings trained with a corruption mechanism to encode semantics.  
- Corruption mechanism: Random masking of words during training induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-informative terms.  
- Practical properties: Model size depends only on vocabulary, training is Word2Vec-like and fast, and test-time inference is trivial averaging.  
- Theoretical analysis: Section 3 gives a second-order approximation showing how corruption yields the regularizer.  
- Empirical coverage: Evaluated on sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and word analogies; results are competitive and efficient.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reason: Simple, well-motivated, and efficiently bridges word-level training and document-level semantics via principled corruption-based regularization.  
- Main reason: Careful analysis explains why the approach works and the model is practically useful.  
- Problem fit: Addresses scalable document vector learning and leverages linear compositionality of word embeddings.  
- Novelty/placement: Combination of averaging, negative sampling, and dropout-like corruption is novel and well-situated relative to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors.  
- Theory–empirics alignment: Theoretical penalty on common words matches observed behavior and efficiency claims.  
- Empirical limitation: Evaluation is somewhat narrow; more diverse or challenging benchmarks would strengthen generality claims.  
- Presentation critique: Excessive space devoted to t-SNE visualizations that could be reallocated to ablations or robustness studies.  
- Suggested ablations: Vary corruption rate q, window size, negative sampling, and presence/absence of local context to quantify contributions.  
- Reproducibility request: Provide more hyperparameter, optimization, and computational-budget details (CPU/GPU, wall-clock time).  
- Subsampling interaction: Clarify how existing subsampling of frequent words interacts with the proposed regularizer.  
- Representation analysis: Go beyond t-SNE with intrinsic measures (cluster purity, nearest-neighbor coherence) or probing tasks.  
- Scope/limitations: Discuss regimes where the method may be less effective (very short texts, very long documents) and compare to recurrent/sentence encoders.  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and is there a speed–accuracy trade-off?  
- Question: What are the contributions of global vs. local context; how do results change when one is removed?  
- Question: How does performance scale with document length and for very short texts like tweets?  
- Question: Can you systematically analyze the regularization effect (e.g., correlation between word frequency and embedding norm)?  
- Question: How robust are results to vocabulary size and out-of-vocabulary handling at test time?  
- Question: Does weighting the average (e.g., IDF) improve test-time performance despite the model’s regularization of frequent words?
