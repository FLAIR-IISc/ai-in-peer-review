Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, but to train those embeddings with a word-dropout corruption mechanism that encourages informative/rare words and shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words toward zero. The authors provide a Taylor-series argument showing that the corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer, and they emphasize that model complexity depends only on vocabulary size, not corpus size. Empirically, Doc2VecC is reported to be fast to train and test, and competitive or better than several baselines on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), semantic relatedness (SICK), and produces strong word embeddings on analogy tasks.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Limited novelty: averaging word embeddings and using dropout/corruption builds directly on CBOW/Word2Vec and marginalized/denoising autoencoders; the theoretical analysis connects to known dropout-style regularization. The contribution feels incremental rather than a substantive new modeling or theoretical advance.
- Incomplete empirical validation against strong modern baselines: important sequence encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/GRU with pooling or final-state concatenation, CNN text classifiers) and fast bag-of-ngrams methods are missing; RNN-LM baseline details are unclear, and some comparisons (e.g., Skip-Thought on long reviews) are arguably mismatched.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a central question—learning compact, semantically meaningful document representations with high efficiency. The motivation is sound: simple additive composition of embeddings can work surprisingly well, and decoupling parameters from corpus size is attractive. The use of corruption to bias the model toward informative words is also intuitive.
- Approach and positioning: The method sits between Word2Vec/CBOW (local context prediction) and denoising autoencoders (corruption) while enforcing that simple averaging produces useful document vectors. However, averaging word embeddings for documents is well known, and earlier works have used global context/averaging; the key differentiator here is joint training with corruption plus a derivation of the induced regularizer. That derivation, while reasonable, mirrors prior analyses of dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders and thus is not particularly novel.
- Correctness and rigor: The second-order expansion to motivate the regularizer is standard and plausible under the unbiased corruption and independence assumptions. However, details are sparse on when the approximation is accurate, how the choice of negative sampling distribution (uniform vs the more common unigram^0.75) affects the regularizer and results, and how sensitive performance is to the corruption rate q and document length scaling. Some baseline setups need clarification (e.g., RNN-LM objective and representation, Paragraph Vector inference/tuning).
- Empirical support: Results on IMDB and Wikipedia classification show strong performance and substantial speed benefits, and SICK performance is competitive though slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Word analogy improvements suggest better word embeddings. Still, the absence of comparisons to widely used sequence encoders for document classification (e.g., BiLSTM with max/mean pooling or final-state concatenation), CNN text classifiers, and efficient bag-of-ngrams approaches (e.g., fastText) leaves a gap. Some baselines are arguably disadvantaged (Skip-Thought on very long documents). Ablations isolating the value of corruption vs simple averaging, and the contribution of local vs global context, are limited.

Suggestions to improve the paper (these are intended as constructive feedback, not necessarily decisive for my recommendation)
- Baselines:
  - Include strong sequence baselines: BiLSTM/GRU with final-state concatenation and with mean/max pooling; CNN-based text classification; and fast bag-of-ngrams (e.g., fastText-style) methods.
  - Clarify and strengthen the RNN-LM baseline: Was it trained as a language model or with supervised labels? What representation was used (final hidden state vs averaged states)? Report hyperparameters and tuning protocol.
  - Ensure a fair Paragraph Vector comparison: specify PV-DM vs PV-DBOW settings, inference steps, and hyperparameters; report both accuracy and inference time.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Quantify the effect of corruption: no corruption vs various q values; different corruption schemes (masking vs dropout on embeddings); with/without local context.
  - Compare average vs IDF-weighted average at test time to verify that the learned regularization indeed obviates heuristic weighting.
  - Examine the impact of the negative sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75).
  - Report robustness across document lengths; the claim that Doc2VecC handles long documents well is compelling—please substantiate with stratified analysis by length.
- Analysis and clarity:
  - Expand the regularization derivation with assumptions and limitations; discuss off-diagonal Hessian terms and when the diagonal approximation is justified.
  - Clarify which embedding matrix is averaged at test time (input U vs output V), and whether both are learned and used.
  - Detail OOV handling and vocabulary extension strategies in all experiments, not only SICK.
  - Provide statistical significance tests where differences are small; include compute details (hardware, throughput) to support speed claims.
- Scope:
  - Add more datasets for document classification and sentiment (e.g., additional review corpora, news/topic datasets) to demonstrate generality.
  - Consider multilingual or domain-shift settings to highlight the claimed efficiency and robustness.

Questions for the authors
- RNN-LM baseline: Was it trained purely as a language model, or jointly for classification? Which representation was used (final hidden state, mean/max pooling over hidden states, or something else)? How were sequences truncated or padded for long IMDB reviews?
- Sequence encoders: How does Doc2VecC compare to a BiLSTM/GRU encoder with final-state concatenation or mean/max pooling on IMDB and Wikipedia? Could you add these baselines?
- Negative sampling: You state Pv is uniform. Did you also try the standard unigram^0.75 distribution? How does this choice affect both performance and the induced regularization?
- Sensitivity to corruption: Please report performance vs q and document length. Is there an optimal range for q beyond the qualitative statement that the model is “not sensitive”?
- Role of local vs global context: What is the delta when removing the local context (i.e., only using the corrupted document vector)? Conversely, what happens if you keep local context but remove the global document vector?
- Embedding usage: At test time, do you average the input embeddings U only, or combine U and V? Have you tried learning a small per-word scalar weight (learned during training) to further improve the average?
- Paragraph Vectors: What exact PV variants and inference procedures were used? How many inference steps and learning rate schedule at test time?

Overall, Doc2VecC is a clean, well-motivated, and efficient approach with promising results. With stronger baselines, fuller ablations, and tighter theoretical and empirical analysis, it could make a compelling contribution. In its current form, the incremental novelty and incomplete comparisons lead me to recommend rejection.
