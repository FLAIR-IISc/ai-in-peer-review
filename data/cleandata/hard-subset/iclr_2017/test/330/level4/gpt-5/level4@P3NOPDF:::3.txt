This paper proposes learning document embeddings as the sum (or average) of constituent word embeddings. Word and document vectors are trained jointly with a word2vec-style objective, while input words are randomly dropped during training (i.e., input-layer dropout). Although none of these components are individually novel, the combination yields an efficient method for producing document representations with strong empirical performance.

Novelty is limited. Jointly training word and document vectors is well established, as is enforcing a document representation to be the sum of its words (see, for example, Lebret and Collobert, “The Sum of Its Parts”). The corruption mechanism is equivalent to standard input dropout. Paired with conventional optimization (e.g., negative sampling), the contribution is primarily in engineering a clean, simple recipe rather than introducing a new modeling principle.

Despite this, the approach has clear practical strengths:
- Inference-time efficiency: document vectors are computed by averaging word embeddings, avoiding the costly inference required by methods like Doc2Vec.
- Conceptual clarity: by optimizing local-context prediction, the learned document representation captures global information salient for downstream tasks.
- Empirical results: for such a simple model, performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is compelling.

A few points would improve the paper’s clarity and impact:
- Provide a more thorough comparison to Doc2Vec variants (PV-DM, PV-DBOW), fastText and bag-of-ngrams baselines, and strong tf–idf features.
- Include ablations isolating the effect of input corruption (dropout rate), and comparing joint training versus fixing word embeddings.
- Report training and inference speed, memory footprint, and wall-clock comparisons to common baselines.
- Analyze sensitivity to document length, vocabulary size, and domain shift; clarify handling of rare and OOV words and whether subword modeling would help.
- Discuss limitations of purely additive composition and whether simple weighting (e.g., tf–idf) or normalization affects results.

Overall, while the methodological novelty is modest, the simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance make the approach practically valuable and of interest to the community. I recommend acceptance.
