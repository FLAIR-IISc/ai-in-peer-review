Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document embeddings. Each document is represented as the average of its word embeddings, which are learned jointly with a corruption mechanism that randomly drops words during training.
- The model combines local context (neighboring words) with a global document vector (the average of surviving word embeddings) to predict target words. The authors show that the corruption induces a data-dependent regularization that shrinks embeddings of common, non-informative words and preserves informative ones.
- Doc2VecC trains quickly (Word2Vec-like complexity), decouples model size from the number of documents, and produces document vectors for unseen texts by a single pass averaging at test time. Empirically, it matches or improves upon state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis and document classification, and yields strong word embeddings on analogy tasks. It is substantially faster and more scalable than methods that require per-document inference (e.g., Paragraph Vectors).

Decision: Accept
- Primary reason: The method is exceptionally simple and efficient while achieving competitive or superior performance across several tasks. The ability to generate high-quality document vectors by averaging at test time is practically valuable.
- Secondary reason: The paper offers a clear motivation and an intuitive regularization view of the corruption mechanism, and provides thorough empirical evidence including timing and large-scale results.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Document representation learning that is fast, scalable, and effective remains important. The paper targets high-quality document embeddings with minimal inference cost. This is well motivated by the limitations of BoW (sparsity), Word2Vec post-processing (heuristics), and Paragraph Vectors (per-document parameters and expensive inference).
- Relation to prior work: The components—averaging word embeddings, input corruption/dropout, and Word2Vec-style objectives—are individually known. Related ideas include learning document vectors jointly with word vectors (Paragraph Vectors) and representing documents as sums/averages of word embeddings (e.g., work by Lebret and Collobert; global context in Huang et al.). The paper’s novelty lies less in the components and more in their specific combination with an analysis showing that corruption yields a useful, data-dependent regularizer aligned with the prediction objective.
- Empirical support: Results on IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia document classification are strong, with consistent gains over BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and Doc2Vec in accuracy and a large advantage in test-time efficiency. Word analogy performance improves markedly over Word2Vec, indicating higher-quality word embeddings. Timing comparisons convincingly demonstrate training and inference scalability.
- Rigor: The Taylor expansion argument provides a helpful lens on why corruption suppresses common words, aligning with observed norms of word vectors. While the derivation is approximate, it is sufficient to motivate the design, and the empirical evidence supports the claim.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not central to the decision)
- Positioning and novelty: More explicitly situate Doc2VecC relative to prior work that uses (weighted) averaging of word embeddings, joint learning of word/document vectors, and input-level dropout/corruption. Clarify conceptual and practical differences, e.g., how enforcing compositionality during training differs from post-hoc averaging.
- Ablations:
  - Remove corruption (q = 0) to quantify its impact on accuracy, speed, and the norm distribution of word embeddings.
  - Vary corruption rate q and show performance/speed trade-offs. Include robustness to document length.
  - Remove local context to compare directly with denoising autoencoders; or remove global context to quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Compare to tf-idf or learned reweighting within the training objective (rather than only post-hoc IDF).
- Fairness and completeness of baselines: Ensure comparable hyperparameters, vector sizes, and training data when comparing to Paragraph Vectors (PV-DM and PV-DBOW). For Skip-thought/LSTM results, emphasize differences in sentence/paragraph lengths and training corpora; consider adding a short-sequence benchmark where those models excel.
- Reporting: Add standard deviations over multiple runs, details on negative sampling (number of negatives, sampling distribution), frequency subsampling settings, preprocessing, and memory footprint (model size vs. vocabulary).
- Analysis: Provide quantitative evidence that the learned embedding norms anticorrelate with word frequency and correlate with informativeness (e.g., stop-word list analysis, frequency-binned norm statistics).

Questions for the authors
1. How does performance vary with the corruption rate q, number of negatives, and the size of the local context window? Is there a consistent sweet spot across datasets?
2. What is the effect of removing the local context (global-only) or global context (local-only)? How much does each contribute?
3. How does Doc2VecC compare to PV-DBOW (document-only objective) under matched embedding sizes and training time?
4. Can you provide quantitative evidence for the data-dependent regularization claim beyond the illustrative table (e.g., correlation between word frequency and vector norm; impact on stop words vs. sentiment/topic words)?
5. How sensitive is the method to document length, and does the corruption rate need adjustment for very short texts?
6. For IMDB and Wikipedia, what are the exact negative sampling and subsampling settings? How many negatives per update?
7. At test time, how do you handle OOV words and vocabulary mismatch across domains? Does extending the vocabulary post hoc affect performance?
8. Could weighting the in-training average (e.g., learned per-word weights or IDF-like priors) further improve results without sacrificing efficiency?
9. What is the memory footprint for large vocabularies, and how does training speed scale with vocabulary size?
10. For SICK, you fine-tune on the target sentences without labels. Which parameters are updated, and how much does this step contribute to the final score?
