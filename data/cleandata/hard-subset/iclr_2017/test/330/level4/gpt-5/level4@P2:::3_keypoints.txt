- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of learned word embeddings with random-word corruption during training.  
- Combines local context prediction with a global document vector (average of surviving words) to predict target words.  
- Shows corruption acts as data-dependent regularization that shrinks embeddings of common/non-informative words.  
- Trains with Word2Vec-like complexity, decouples model size from number of documents, and produces test-time vectors by single-pass averaging.  
- Empirically matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment and document classification and yields strong word embeddings on analogy tasks.  
- Substantially faster and more scalable than methods requiring per-document inference (e.g., Paragraph Vectors).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Primary reason for acceptance: exceptional simplicity and efficiency with competitive or superior performance and practical single-pass inference.  
- Secondary reason: clear motivation, intuitive regularization interpretation of corruption, and thorough empirical evidence including timing and large-scale results.  
- Well-motivated problem targeting fast, scalable, effective document embeddings addressing BoW, post-hoc Word2Vec, and Paragraph Vector limitations.  
- Components are individually known; novelty lies in their specific combination and analysis showing useful regularization from corruption.  
- Strong empirical support: consistent gains over BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and Doc2Vec in accuracy, large test-time efficiency advantages, and improved analogy performance.  
- The Taylor expansion-based derivation is approximate but provides a helpful explanatory lens supported by experiments.  
- Suggest explicitly situating Doc2VecC relative to prior averaging, joint-learning, and input-dropout works and clarifying differences from post-hoc averaging.  
- Suggest ablations: remove corruption (q=0) to measure impact on accuracy/speed/norms.  
- Suggest ablations: vary corruption rate q and show performance/speed trade-offs and robustness to document length.  
- Suggest ablations: remove local context (global-only) or global context (local-only) to quantify each component’s contribution.  
- Suggest comparing to tf-idf or learned reweighting within training (not just post-hoc IDF).  
- Ensure fairness of baselines: match hyperparameters, vector sizes, and training data for Paragraph Vectors and clarify Skip-Thought/LSTM differences.  
- Reporting improvements: include standard deviations, negative sampling details, subsampling settings, preprocessing, and memory/model size.  
- Analysis improvements: provide quantitative evidence that embedding norms anticorrelate with word frequency and correlate with informativeness.  
- Questions for authors: how does performance vary with corruption rate q, negative count, and context window — is there a consistent sweet spot?  
- Questions: what is the effect of removing local or global context — how much does each contribute?  
- Questions: how does Doc2VecC compare to PV-DBOW under matched embedding sizes and training time?  
- Questions: can you provide quantitative evidence for the data-dependent regularization beyond illustrative tables (e.g., frequency vs. norm correlations)?  
- Questions: how sensitive is the method to document length and does corruption rate need adjustment for very short texts?  
- Questions: what exact negative sampling and subsampling settings were used for IMDB and Wikipedia, and how many negatives per update?  
- Questions: how are OOV words and vocabulary mismatches handled at test time, and does extending vocabulary post hoc affect performance?  
- Questions: could in-training weighted averaging (learned per-word weights or IDF-like priors) improve results without losing efficiency?  
- Questions: what is the memory footprint for large vocabularies and how does training speed scale with vocabulary size?  
- Questions: for SICK fine-tuning on unlabeled target sentences, which parameters are updated and how much does this step improve final score?
