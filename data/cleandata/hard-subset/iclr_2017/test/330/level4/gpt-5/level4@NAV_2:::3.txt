1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework that represents a document as the average of its word embeddings, learned jointly with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. Although the components are individually familiar, their combination yields a fast, scalable approach to document representation that performs well empirically. The design ensures the learned document vector captures global information helpful for predicting local context, and at test time requires only averaging word vectors, avoiding costly inference.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) strong practical value due to simplicity and test-time efficiency; (ii) consistently solid empirical performance on sentiment analysis and document classification for a model of this simplicity.

3. Supporting arguments
- Limited novelty: Joint learning of word and document representations, enforcing a document to be the sum/average of its word embeddings, has precedents (e.g., “The Sum of Its Parts” by Lebret and Collobert). The corruption mechanism largely amounts to input-layer dropout, and the training objective follows a word2vec-style formulation. As a result, the methodological ingredients are not new.
- Practical merits: Despite modest novelty, the method is extremely efficient at inference—unlike Doc2Vec, it needs only an average over word embeddings with no per-document optimization. Moreover, by construction the document embedding encodes the global information that most aids local-context prediction, which appears to translate into strong results on sentiment analysis and document classification.

4. Suggestions for improvement (not required for the accept decision)
- Clarify positioning vs. prior work. More explicit discussion of how Doc2VecC differs from earlier “sum/average of word vectors” approaches and from input dropout would help sharpen the contribution and expectations.
- Provide ablations and sensitivity. A brief study of the impact of the corruption rate and the role of corruption versus no corruption would strengthen the claim that corruption is critical to performance and efficiency.
- Expand comparisons. Since the approach emphasizes test-time efficiency, include a more detailed runtime and memory comparison against Doc2Vec and strong “average of word vectors” baselines (e.g., with/without IDF or other simple weighting).
- Qualitative diagnostics. Simple analyses (e.g., which words are damped by corruption; case studies where Doc2VecC succeeds/fails relative to Doc2Vec) would make the regularization effect and its benefits more tangible.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate and to whether you use sum versus average (i.e., length normalization) in forming document vectors?
- Can you provide an ablation showing the effect of removing corruption entirely, or replacing it with standard input dropout implemented equivalently?
- How does Doc2VecC compare to a carefully tuned “word2vec + weighted averaging” baseline (e.g., IDF or other weighting) on your tasks, both in accuracy and in inference speed?
- Are there particular domains or document lengths where Doc2VecC degrades relative to Doc2Vec or LSTM-based encoders, and can you characterize those regimes?
