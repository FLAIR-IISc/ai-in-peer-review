This paper proposes learning document embeddings as the sum of their constituent word embeddings, with both word and document representations trained jointly while randomly dropping out (“corrupting”) inputs during training. Although none of the components are particularly novel, the approach yields an efficient learning algorithm for document representation with strong empirical performance.

Joint training of word and document embeddings is not new, nor is constraining the document representation to be the sum of its word embeddings (see “The Sum of Its Parts”: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). The corruption mechanism amounts to traditional dropout applied to the input layer. Combined with a word2vec-style loss and training procedure, the paper offers little in terms of novelty.

On the positive side, the method is very efficient at generation time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than the more complex inference step used in Doc2Vec. By construction, the embedding captures salient global information about the document—specifically, the information that is useful for local-context prediction. Given the model’s simplicity, the reported results on sentiment analysis and document classification are quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the limited novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and empirical performance make this work worthy of wider readership and study. I recommend acceptance.
