This paper proposes a simple framework for document representation: average the word embeddings and apply a data-dependent regularization that downweights frequent words (pushing them toward zero) while emphasizing informative or rare words. Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification indicate that the method achieves lower error rates than several baseline document-embedding approaches.

The motivation—seeking an effective, fast way to encode documents into vectors—is compelling, and the empirical results are promising. However, the technical novelty appears limited. Most components are known, and the primary contribution is the combination’s simplicity and speed rather than new modeling or theoretical insights. To support the claim that this is a strong general-purpose approach to document representation, I would like to see evidence across a broader set of tasks beyond the two considered here.

Clarifications on the RNN-LM baseline would help interpret the comparisons:
- Was the RNN language model trained purely as a language model or optimized directly for classification?
- Which representation was used: the final hidden state, the average of all hidden states, or another pooling strategy?

In addition, a comparison to a strong and widely used baseline—bidirectional LSTMs with concatenated final hidden states as the document representation—would be informative. Reporting results against this model on the same sentiment and document classification benchmarks (and ideally additional tasks) would better situate the proposed method’s performance and practical value.

In summary, the approach is attractive for its simplicity, speed, and competitive results on two tasks, but the paper would benefit from:
- Broader empirical validation across diverse tasks and datasets.
- Clearer details on baseline training objectives and representation choices.
- Direct comparison to a biLSTM final-state representation, which is a common and strong baseline.

These additions would strengthen the case for the method as a preferred choice for learning document representations.
