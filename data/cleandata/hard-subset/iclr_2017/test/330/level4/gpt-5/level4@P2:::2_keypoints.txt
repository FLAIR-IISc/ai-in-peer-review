- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as the average of jointly learned word embeddings with a masking corruption that regularizes frequent/uninformative words.  
- Efficiency claim: Model is lightweight (parameters scale with vocabulary), trains fast with negative sampling, and produces test vectors by a single average.  
- Empirical claim: Reports competitive or better results than several baselines on sentiment and document classification, with notable speed advantages.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary reason 1: Limited technical novelty—averaging, denoising/dropout-style corruption, and negative sampling are largely known; theory is a standard Taylor expansion.  
- Primary reason 2: Empirical evaluation is insufficiently broad—missing comparisons to strong neural encoders (BiLSTM/CNN/hierarchical) so claims about general-purpose performance are weak.  
- Problem & motivation: Well-motivated goal to get scalable document embeddings without per-document parameters; efficiency benefits over Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors are clear.  
- Literature placement: Method sits between post-hoc Word2Vec averaging and Paragraph Vectors; corruption-as-regularization relates to denoising autoencoders/dropout but needs deeper contrast.  
- Technical contribution: Enforces during training that simple averaging yields useful document vectors and uses an unbiased masking corruption that yields data-dependent shrinkage of common words.  
- Theory assessment: The expected-objective analysis via Taylor expansion explains shrinkage but is not fundamentally novel.  
- Claims vs. evidence: Results show improvements over classical baselines and speed gains, but missing comparisons to standard neural encoders and unclear RNN-LM baseline weaken conclusions.  
- Strengths: Simplicity, easy deployment, strong training/inference efficiency, and a clear mechanism to suppress stopwords; empirical gains over classical baselines are encouraging.  
- Weaknesses: Incremental novelty, limited evaluation breadth, and missing important baselines and ablations (e.g., corruption rate, context terms, negative-sampling choices).  
- Suggestion — broaden comparisons: Add bidirectional LSTM/GRU, CNN, hierarchical encoders, and FastText-style bag-of-ngrams baselines.  
- Suggestion — ablations: Report no-corruption, various corruption rates q, remove local context, alternate document scalings (with/without 1/T), and Word2Vec averaging with/without IDF/subsampling under identical preprocessing.  
- Suggestion — statistical rigor: Report variance across runs and statistical significance.  
- Suggestion — datasets: Evaluate on more tasks/datasets including varied text lengths and domain shifts to show robustness.  
- Suggestion — baseline fairness: Specify negative sampling distribution (unigram^0.75 vs. uniform), clarify Paragraph Vector variants and hyperparameter tuning, and explain RNN/Skip-thought training choices.  
- Suggestion — analysis & clarity: Provide intuition for averaging+corruption with/without local context, visualize embedding norm shrinkage vs. frequency/informativeness, and discuss failure modes (order-sensitive tasks, long-range discourse).  
- Question — RNN-LM baseline: Was the RNN trained purely as LM or with classification objective, how were document vectors formed (final state/average/max), and were bidirectional variants used?  
- Question — BiLSTM comparison: How does Doc2VecC compare to a BiLSTM concatenating final states on accuracy and training/inference time?  
- Question — negative sampling: Which negative sampling distribution was used and how sensitive are results to this choice?  
- Question — ablation details: What performance without corruption and how does it vary with q; impact of removing local context or 1/T scaling?  
- Question — Paragraph Vectors: Which PV variants (DM/DBOW) and hyperparameters were used; was PV-DBOW included?  
- Question — preprocessing fairness: How were frequent-word subsampling and IDF weighting handled across baselines?  
- Question — generalization: Can you report results on additional datasets and shorter texts to support robustness claims?
