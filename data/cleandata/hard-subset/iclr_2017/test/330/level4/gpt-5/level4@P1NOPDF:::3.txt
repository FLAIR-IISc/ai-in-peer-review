This paper proposes learning document embeddings as the sum of their constituent word embeddings, with word and document vectors trained jointly and randomly “corrupted” via dropout during training. While none of these components is particularly novel, the approach yields an efficient algorithm for learning document representations with solid empirical performance.

Jointly training word and document embeddings is not new, nor is constraining a document representation to be the sum of its word vectors (see, for example, “The Sum of Its Parts: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders” by Lebret and Collobert). The corruption mechanism is standard dropout applied to the input layer. Combined with a word2vec-style loss and training procedure, the paper offers limited novelty.

On the positive side, the method is very efficient at inference time: it requires only averaging the word embeddings, avoiding the more involved inference step used in Doc2Vec. In addition, by construction, the document embedding captures salient global information—that is, the information most useful for predicting local context. Given the model’s simplicity, the reported performance on sentiment analysis and document classification is quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the lack of conceptual novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and empirical results make this work worthy of wider readership and further study. I recommend acceptance.
