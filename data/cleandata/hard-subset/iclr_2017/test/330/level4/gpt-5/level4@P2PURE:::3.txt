This paper proposes learning document embeddings as the sum of their constituent word embeddings, with word and document vectors trained jointly and randomly dropped out (“corrupted”) during training. While none of the components are particularly novel, the approach yields an efficient algorithm for document representation with good empirical performance.

Jointly training word and document embeddings is not new, nor is enforcing a document to be represented by the sum of its word vectors (see, e.g., “The Sum of Its Parts”: Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert). Moreover, the corruption mechanism is simply standard input-layer dropout. Coupled with a word2vec-style loss and training procedure, the paper offers little in terms of novelty.

On the other hand, it is highly efficient at generation time, requiring only an average of the word embeddings rather than the more complex inference step used in Doc2Vec. In addition, by construction, the embedding captures salient global information about the document—specifically the information that supports local-context prediction. For such a simple model, the results on sentiment analysis and document classification are quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the lack of novelty, the model’s simplicity, efficiency, and performance make it worthy of broader attention and study, and I recommend acceptance.
