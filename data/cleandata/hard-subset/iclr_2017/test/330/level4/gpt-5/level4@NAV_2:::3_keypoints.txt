- Doc2VecC represents a document as the average of word embeddings learned jointly with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training, enabling fast, scalable document vectors computed by simple averaging at test time.  
- Decision: Accept — cited strengths are simplicity, test-time efficiency, and consistently strong empirical performance on sentiment analysis and document classification.  
- Limited novelty: components (sum/average of word vectors, joint learning, corruption ≈ input dropout, word2vec-style objective) have clear precedents (e.g., Lebret & Collobert).  
- Practical merits: no per-document optimization at inference, encodes global information useful for local-context prediction, and delivers strong results given its simplicity.  
- Clarify positioning: explicitly contrast Doc2VecC with prior sum/average approaches and input-dropout to sharpen contribution.  
- Ablation/sensitivity: evaluate impact of corruption rate and of using sum versus average (length normalization).  
- Ablation: show performance when corruption is removed or when replaced by standard input dropout implemented equivalently.  
- Expanded comparisons: provide detailed runtime and memory comparisons against Doc2Vec and strong “average of word vectors” baselines (e.g., with/without IDF weighting).  
- Qualitative diagnostics: analyze which words are damped by corruption and include case studies where Doc2VecC succeeds or fails relative to Doc2Vec.  
- Question: how sensitive is performance to the corruption rate and to sum vs average in forming document vectors?  
- Question: what is the effect of removing corruption entirely or replacing it with standard input dropout?  
- Question: how does Doc2VecC compare (accuracy and inference speed) to a carefully tuned word2vec + weighted averaging baseline?  
- Question: are there specific domains or document lengths where Doc2VecC degrades relative to Doc2Vec or LSTM-based encoders, and can those regimes be characterized?
