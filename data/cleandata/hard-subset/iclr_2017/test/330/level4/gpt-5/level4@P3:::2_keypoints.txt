- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as averaged word embeddings trained with word-dropout corruption to emphasize informative words and induce a data-dependent regularizer.

- Main claim: Doc2VecC is simple, fast, and competitive on IMDB, Wikipedia classification, SICK, and word analogy tasks.

- Decision: Reject.

- Primary reason 1: Limited novelty—method closely builds on CBOW/Word2Vec and marginalized/denoising autoencoders; theoretical analysis echoes known dropout regularization.

- Primary reason 2: Incomplete empirical validation—missing comparisons to strong modern baselines and some mismatched/unclear baseline setups.

- Novelty critique: Averaging embeddings and using corruption is incremental; the regularizer derivation parallels prior dropout/marginalized denoising analyses.

- Baseline omissions: No comparisons to BiLSTM/GRU encoders (with pooling or final-state), CNN text classifiers, or fast bag-of-ngrams methods (e.g., fastText).

- Baseline clarity issues: RNN-LM baseline details (training objective, representation, truncation) and Paragraph Vector settings/inference are insufficiently specified.

- Theoretical rigor concerns: Second-order Taylor expansion is standard; paper lacks discussion on approximation validity, off-diagonal Hessian terms, and assumptions.

- Sensitivity/robustness gaps: Missing analysis on sensitivity to corruption rate q, different corruption schemes, negative sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75), and document length effects.

- Ablation weaknesses: Limited isolation of corruption vs simple averaging, limited study of local vs global context contributions, and no test-time IDF-weighted average comparison.

- Empirical strengths: Shows strong speed improvements and competitive accuracy on several tasks; word analogy results suggest improved word embeddings.

- Empirical caveats: SICK performance is slightly worse than LSTM-based methods; some baselines (e.g., Skip-Thought) may be unfairly disadvantaged on long documents.

- Experimental clarity needed: Specify which embedding matrix is averaged at test time (input U vs output V), OOV handling, vocabulary extension, hyperparameters, and compute/hardware for speed claims.

- Statistical/reporting improvements: Provide stratified results by document length, statistical significance tests, and broader dataset coverage (more sentiment/topic datasets, multilingual/domain-shift).

- Concrete suggestions: Add BiLSTM/GRU and CNN baselines, fastText-style bag-of-ngrams, detailed RNN-LM and Paragraph Vector setups, ablations over q and corruption schemes, negative sampling variants, and expanded theoretical discussion.

- Key questions for authors: Was RNN-LM trained as LM or supervised? Which sequence representation was used? How does Doc2VecC compare to BiLSTM/GRU and CNN baselines? Did you try unigram^0.75 negative sampling? How sensitive is performance to q and document length? What happens when removing local or global context? Which embeddings are averaged at test time and were learned per-word weights tried? What PV variants and inference steps were used?
