1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings. During training, the model applies a corruption scheme (masking words with probability q and reweighting the rest) so that averaging embeddings captures document semantics. This corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that pushes embeddings of common, non-discriminative words toward zero while preserving informative or rare words. The approach retains Word2Vec-like scalability and decouples model size from the number of documents, enabling fast training and test-time inference (simple averaging). Empirically, the paper reports lower error rates than several baselines on sentiment analysis and document classification, while being computationally efficient.

2. Decision: Reject
- Primary reason: Limited novelty. The core ideas—averaging word embeddings and corruption/dropout-style regularization—are well-known, and the paper does not present a substantial new technical contribution beyond combining these elements within a CBOW-style objective.
- Secondary reason: The empirical validation does not convincingly establish that this is the best way to learn document representations. Strong contemporary baselines (e.g., bidirectional LSTM encoders) are missing, and the breadth of tasks is limited in the main comparisons.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles the important problem of learning compact, semantically meaningful document vectors with efficient training and inference. The motivation to encode compositionality via averaging and to reduce reliance on document-specific parameters (as in Paragraph Vectors) is sound. Placing the method relative to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors is appropriate.
- Technical novelty and rigor: Representing documents via averaged embeddings is a standard baseline; the corruption mechanism resembles dropout/denoising autoencoders and has known regularization effects. The theoretical discussion linking corruption to a data-dependent regularizer is reasonable but follows established analyses (e.g., marginalized denoising, dropout-as-regularization). Overall, the technical delta appears incremental.
- Empirical evidence: The reported gains on sentiment analysis and document classification are encouraging, and the speed benefits are compelling. However:
  - It is unclear whether the comparisons include strong neural baselines commonly used for document encoding, e.g., bidirectional LSTMs with concatenated final states or with pooling/attention. These are widely used and would provide a more decisive benchmark.
  - Details for the RNN-LM baseline are unclear (training objective and pooling scheme), making it hard to assess fairness.
  - Given the emphasis on simplicity and speed, broader evaluations across diverse tasks would strengthen the claim that Doc2VecC is a generally superior representation method.

4. Suggestions and questions for the authors (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Add stronger neural baselines:
  - Bidirectional LSTM encoders with (i) concatenated final states and (ii) mean/max pooling over hidden states; optionally, compare to CNN-based document encoders. These are standard practice and would better contextualize Doc2VecC’s performance on sentiment and classification.
- Clarify the RNN-LM baseline:
  - Was the RNN trained purely as a language model or fine-tuned for classification?
  - Which representation was used: final hidden state, averaged hidden states, or another pooling scheme?
  - Provide hyperparameters and training details to ensure comparability.
- Broaden empirical validation:
  - Evaluate on additional tasks beyond sentiment and topic classification (e.g., multi-domain classification, entailment/paraphrase, or retrieval) to support claims of generality.
  - Include statistical significance testing where applicable, given the modest margins reported in some settings.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Report sensitivity to the corruption rate q (and document length), and include ablations removing (i) corruption, (ii) local context, to quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Discuss how subsampling of frequent words interacts with the proposed regularization; show results with and without subsampling.
- Practical aspects:
  - Provide comprehensive runtime comparisons including hardware details (CPU/GPU), vocabulary sizes, and corpus sizes for all baselines, and detail inference-time costs on unseen documents.
  - Discuss handling of out-of-vocabulary words and any fine-tuning on target datasets.

Questions for the authors
- For the RNN-LM baseline: Is the model trained solely with a language modeling objective, or is it optimized for classification? Which representation is extracted (final state vs. averaged states), and why?
- How would Doc2VecC compare to a standard bidirectional LSTM document encoder that concatenates the final hidden states, on sentiment and document classification?
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and to document length? Are there regimes (very short vs. very long documents) where Doc2VecC degrades relative to neural encoders?
- What is the impact of removing the subsampling of frequent words? Does the proposed data-dependent regularization fully subsume this heuristic?
- Can you provide significance tests for the reported improvements and more detail on hyperparameter tuning to ensure fair comparisons?

Overall, the paper is well-motivated and empirically promising, especially in efficiency, but the contribution is incremental and the experimental comparisons miss key strong baselines. Adding these results and clarifications would substantially strengthen the work.
