Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a Word2Vec-style document representation method that averages word embeddings to form a global document vector and introduces a corruption (dropout/mask-out) mechanism during training. This corruption yields a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of common, non-discriminative words toward zero while preserving or emphasizing informative/rare words. The model is simple, efficient to train (scales to very large corpora) and to use at test time (just average embeddings), and the authors provide a clear derivation of the regularization effect via a second-order Taylor expansion. Empirically, the paper reports competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, and stronger word embeddings than Word2Vec on analogy benchmarks. I especially appreciated the careful analysis in Section 3 that clarifies why corruption acts as an adaptive regularizer.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- A simple, well-composed algorithmic combination that appears novel in this specific form and is highly efficient in both training and inference.
- Solid analysis (Section 3) that provides insight into why the method works, beyond purely empirical claims.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in the literature: While none of the individual components (averaging word embeddings, corruption/dropout, negative sampling) are new, combining them so that the model explicitly learns word embeddings whose averages are meaningful document vectors is well motivated. The derivation makes a strong case that the corruption induces principled, data-dependent regularization that downweights frequent stop words without hand-tuned heuristics.
- Empirical support: The results suggest that the method is competitive or better than several baselines and that its word embeddings are strong on analogies. Efficiency claims are plausible given the architecture (no per-document parameters; test-time averaging).
- Clarity and rigor: The paper reads clearly, and the analysis in Section 3 meaningfully supports the claims.

That said, I found the empirical evaluation relatively weak and somewhat conservative in scope. Focusing on sentiment analysis and k-way classification is useful but not especially ambitious; these tasks are now standard and may not stress-test the representational benefits claimed. I also agree with other reviewers that some baselines are soft.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are part of my accept decision)
- Strengthen the experimental scope:
  - Include more challenging or diverse tasks (e.g., long-document retrieval, topic classification with domain shift, downstream tasks where test-time efficiency matters, or transfer to tasks with limited supervision).
  - Add stronger neural baselines (modern CNN/LSTM variants) trained under comparable data budgets, and ensure fair training data parity for models like Skip-Thought.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Provide a systematic ablation of the corruption component (vary q; remove corruption; remove local context) to quantify each contribution.
  - Report sensitivity to negative sampling rate, context window size, and embedding dimensionality.
  - Evaluate whether test-time weighting (e.g., IDF) changes performance, given the claim that learned embeddings already encode the desired downweighting.
- Analysis over t-SNE: The heavy emphasis on t-SNE visualizations could be replaced with more quantitative analyses (e.g., nearest-neighbor coherence, probing tasks, clustering metrics) and error analyses on misclassified examples.
- Fairness and clarity in comparisons:
  - For time/efficiency comparisons, standardize hardware and clearly separate training, encoding, and inference costs.
  - Clarify the handling of subsampling of frequent words and its interaction with the corruption-induced regularization.
- Reproducibility: The provided code is appreciated; a short recipe for reproducing the main tables and default hyperparameters would help.

Questions for the authors
- Novelty: Which specific aspects of Doc2VecC are new relative to Huang et al. (2012), Paragraph Vectors, and marginalized denoising autoencoders? In particular, what is novel about combining global average pooling with corruption in a predictive objective?
- Architecture: You describe using a Word2Vec-style predictive setup. Is your training closer to CBOW or Skip-gram in practice, and does it matter for performance?
- Corruption: How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q? Is there a principled way to set q, or can it be learned?
- Subsampling vs. corruption: You apply subsampling of frequent words. How much does Doc2VecC rely on subsampling once corruption is in place? Can you quantify their interaction?
- Inference: At test time, you simply average embeddings. Did you try any variants (e.g., IDF weighting or learned weights) and do they help or hurt?
- Efficiency: Please detail the exact hardware, data sizes, and hyperparameters used in timing comparisons, and the number of negatives per update.
- Generalization: The method seems strong on long documents but less so on short sentences relative to LSTM-based models. Can you comment on when you expect Doc2VecC to be preferable, and whether hybrid approaches (e.g., averaging within an RNN/CNN framework) help?
- Word embeddings: Do the improved embeddings on analogy tasks translate directly to better downstream document performance, or are there cases where the document objective trades off against pure word-level quality?

Overall, despite a limited empirical scope and some soft baselines, the paper presents a neat, efficient algorithm with a clear and informative analysis. I believe the community will benefit from these ideas.
