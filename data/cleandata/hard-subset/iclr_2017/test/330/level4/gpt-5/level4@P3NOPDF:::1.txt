Summary
This paper proposes a method for learning document representations using a skip-gram–style objective augmented with a global context (document) vector and dropout-based regularization. While the individual components are not novel in isolation, the particular integration is, to my knowledge, original. The paper is clearly written, and the analysis of model behavior in Section 3 is careful and informative. The approach is computationally efficient and easy to implement, which increases its practical relevance.

Strengths
- Clear and coherent algorithmic design that combines well-understood ideas into a compact, efficient method.
- Good exposition and a thoughtful qualitative and quantitative analysis of model behavior in Section 3.
- The regularization via a global context vector and targeted dropout is a sensible way to balance local and global signals, and the design choices are well motivated.
- Simplicity and efficiency suggest the method could be widely usable as a fast baseline or component in larger systems.

Weaknesses
- The empirical evaluation is narrow. Focusing primarily on sentiment analysis and k-way topic classification limits the evidence for generality and utility. These tasks alone are not sufficient to demonstrate broad applicability of document embeddings.
- Baselines appear soft. Strong contemporaneous baselines such as Paragraph Vector (PV-DBOW/DM), fastText, bag-of-ngrams with linear classifiers, NBSVM for sentiment, skip-thought, and simple sentence/document averaging with SIF weighting are either missing or underemphasized. Without such comparisons, it is hard to assess the true gains.
- A substantial portion of space is devoted to t-SNE visualizations, which are hard to interpret and can be misleading. That space would be better used for more rigorous quantitative experiments or ablations.
- Limited ablation. It is not fully clear how much each component (global context vector, the specific regularizer, dropout variants) contributes to the final performance. A clean ablation would strengthen the case for the proposed composition.
- Statistical rigor is light. There is little discussion of variance across runs, sensitivity to hyperparameters (e.g., dropout rates, global vector weight), or statistical significance.

Questions and suggestions for improvement
- Clarify the relationship to Paragraph Vector and related methods: In what precise sense does the global context vector differ from PV-DBOW’s document vector? Is it used only as a regularizer during training or also at inference? A side-by-side comparison with matched training budgets would help situate the contribution.
- Strengthen baselines and tasks:
  - Include PV-DBOW/DM, fastText, NBSVM for sentiment, CNN/LSTM document classifiers, skip-thought or similar sentence encoders aggregated to documents, and SIF/Sent2Vec-style averaging.
  - Evaluate on broader tasks: document retrieval (precision@k), semantic textual similarity (correlation on STS), clustering quality (NMI/ARI), duplicate/near-duplicate detection, and transfer to out-of-domain classification.
- Provide ablations isolating:
  - Skip-gram base vs. +global context vs. +regularizer vs. +dropout variants.
  - Different dropout placements and rates; show how they affect overfitting and generalization.
  - The contribution of negative sampling choices and context window sizes.
- Replace or reduce t-SNE plots with:
  - Learning curves (data efficiency), per-class performance and confusion matrices, calibration metrics, and robustness under domain shift.
  - Significance tests across multiple random seeds.
- Report efficiency details: wall-clock training time, memory footprint, and throughput compared to fastText and PV-DBOW under equal hardware and corpus size. Efficiency is a key advertised benefit; quantify it.
- Expand Section 3’s analysis to include how the global context vector evolves during training, whether it captures topical vs. stylistic information, and its interaction with local context embeddings. Probing tasks could be informative.
- Reproducibility: provide code or sufficiently detailed pseudocode, hyperparameter settings, and preprocessing details to allow faithful reproduction.

Minor comments
- Tighten the exposition of the objective and regularizer with explicit equations and a brief discussion of optimization (e.g., negative sampling vs. hierarchical softmax) and complexity.
- If space is constrained, prioritize ablations and stronger baselines over visualization figures.

Recommendation: Weak Accept
Despite a relatively narrow and somewhat soft empirical evaluation, the paper offers a neat and efficient composition of existing ideas that appears novel and is presented with clarity. The method is simple, fast, and potentially useful to practitioners. With stronger baselines, broader tasks, and more thorough ablations, the contribution would be significantly strengthened. As it stands, I believe the paper is of interest to the community and merits acceptance, provided the authors address the evaluation gaps in the final version.
