This paper proposes a method for learning document vectors using a skip-gram–style objective augmented with a global context vector as a regularizer and employing variants of dropout. While the individual components are not novel, their integration in this particular configuration appears to be, and the approach is conceptually clean. I also appreciated the careful behavioral analysis presented in Section 3.

The primary weakness lies in the empirical evaluation. The tasks considered—sentiment analysis and k-way classification—are limited and not the most compelling tests for document representations. A broader and more challenging set of benchmarks would strengthen the case for the method’s generality and usefulness. In the same vein, I question the decision to devote roughly two-thirds of a page to t-SNE visualizations; that space would be better used for additional quantitative analysis or ablations. I also share the concerns raised by other reviewers about the softness of the baselines; stronger, more competitive comparisons are needed to contextualize the reported gains.

Despite these limitations, I lean toward acceptance. The algorithm is interesting, well-composed, and computationally efficient. Given its simplicity and speed, I expect that readers may find the core ideas—particularly the use of a global context regularizer within a skip-gram framework—useful in their own work. Strengthening the empirical section with more challenging tasks and tougher baselines would greatly improve the paper, but even in its current form, it offers a worthwhile contribution.
