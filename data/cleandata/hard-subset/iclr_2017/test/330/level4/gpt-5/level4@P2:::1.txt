Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. Each document is represented as the average of its word embeddings, but crucially, the model is trained so that this averaging encodes semantic information. A corruption mechanism randomly masks words in the document-level context during training, which induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-informative terms and emphasizes rare, informative ones. The approach has several attractive properties: model size depends only on vocabulary (not corpus size), training is Word2Vec-like and fast, and test-time inference is trivial averaging. The paper also includes a clear analysis (Section 3) showing how corruption yields the regularizer via a second-order approximation, and presents empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and word analogies.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The method is simple, well-motivated, and efficiently bridges word-level training and document-level semantics via a principled corruption-based regularization.
- The analysis in Section 3 is careful and illuminates why the approach works; the model is practical and likely to be useful to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning document vectors that capture semantics while remaining scalable. It builds on established findings that linear operations on word embeddings preserve compositional meaning and integrates a global (document) context into a Word2Vec-like objective.
- Placement in the literature: While individual components (averaging, negative sampling, dropout-like corruption) are known, their combination here is novel and well-situated among Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and autoencoders. The contrast with Paragraph Vectors (decoupling parameters from corpus size and enabling cheap test-time inference) is compelling.
- Evidence for claims: The theoretical development explains how the corruption induces a data-dependent penalty that shrinks embeddings of common, non-discriminative words, aligning with observed behavior. Empirically, Doc2VecC is competitive or better on several benchmarks and notably efficient in both training and inference, consistent with the scalability claims.

That said, the empirical evaluation could be broader and stronger:
- The main supervised tasks (sentiment analysis and k-way classification) are standard but limited; stronger or more diverse benchmarks would better substantiate generality.
- The paper devotes substantial space to t-SNE visualizations; that space could be used for deeper ablations or robustness studies.

Constructive feedback (to improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Strengthen baselines and ablations:
  - Include more challenging or diverse tasks (e.g., topic transfer, multi-label classification, retrieval).
  - Add ablations on the corruption rate q, window size, negative sampling choices, and the presence/absence of local context to quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Report variance across random seeds and statistical significance where feasible.
- Clarify training details:
  - Provide more specifics on hyperparameter selection, optimization settings, and computational budget (CPU/GPU, wall-clock time per epoch) to aid reproducibility.
  - Detail how subsampling of frequent words interacts with the proposed regularizer.
- Analyze representation quality:
  - Beyond t-SNE, consider intrinsic measures (e.g., cluster purity, nearest-neighbor coherence) or probing tasks to substantiate the qualitative claims about the document vectors.
- Discuss limitations and scope:
  - Briefly characterize when the approach might be less effective (e.g., very short texts vs. long documents) and how it compares to recurrent or sentence-encoder methods in those regimes.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q? Is there a trade-off between speed and accuracy, and does an optimal range emerge across datasets?
- What is the contribution of the global context vs. local context? If you remove local context terms and only use the corrupted global context (or vice versa), how do results change?
- How does performance scale with document length? Do gains persist for very short texts (e.g., tweets) or very long documents?
- Can you provide a more systematic analysis of the regularization effect (e.g., correlation between word frequency and embedding norm across datasets)?
- How robust are the results to vocabulary size and out-of-vocabulary handling at test time?
- At test time, do you observe benefits from weighting the average (e.g., IDF) given that the model already regularizes frequent words, or is plain averaging consistently best?

Overall, this is a neat, efficient method with a clear motivation and analysis. Despite a relatively narrow empirical scope, the ideas are sound and practically useful, and I recommend acceptance.
