This paper proposes a framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a weighted average of its word embeddings, guided by a data-dependent regularization term. This regularization assigns higher weights to informative or rare words while shrinking the contributions of common words toward zero. Experimental results on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks indicate that the proposed method achieves lower error rates than the baseline methods.

While the motivation to find an optimal document encoding is compelling, the paper's technical contribution appears limited, as its components are largely based on existing techniques. The method's primary advantages are its simplicity and speed. For this reason, the empirical evaluation on only two tasks is insufficient to be convinced of its general superiority for learning document representations. Stronger results across a broader range of tasks would be necessary to substantiate such a claim.

The following points regarding the experimental setup also require clarification:
1.  For the RNN-LM baseline, was the model trained to minimize classification error, or was it trained as a standard language model?
2.  Was the document representation from the RNN-LM derived from the final hidden state or an average of all hidden states?

Furthermore, the comparisons are missing a widely-used and powerful baseline: a bidirectional LSTM where the document representation is formed by concatenating the final forward and backward hidden states. An evaluation against this approach for tasks like document classification and sentiment analysis would provide a much more thorough and convincing assessment of the proposed method's effectiveness.
