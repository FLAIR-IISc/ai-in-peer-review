### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The model is trained using a CBOW-style objective: predicting a target word from both its local context (neighboring words) and a global context (the document vector).

The key contribution is the "corruption" mechanism, where the global context vector is formed by averaging word embeddings from a randomly sampled subset of the document's words. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, demonstrating that this corruption process is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the training objective. This regularizer has the desirable effect of penalizing the embeddings of words that are frequent but non-discriminative, effectively learning to down-weight uninformative words like stop words without explicit heuristics.

The authors demonstrate experimentally that Doc2VecC matches or exceeds the performance of several state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly faster to train and use for inference. Additionally, they show that the word embeddings produced by Doc2VecC are of higher quality than those from the standard Word2Vec model, as measured by the word analogy task.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Efficiency and Simplicity:** The proposed model is methodologically simple, easy to implement, and computationally efficient for both training and inference. The ability to generate a document vector via a simple average of pre-computed word embeddings makes it highly practical for real-world applications.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Motivation:** The paper provides an excellent theoretical justification for its core mechanism. The analysis framing the corruption scheme as an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer is insightful and elevates the work from a simple heuristic to a well-principled approach. This analysis clearly explains *why* the model is effective at handling frequent, uninformative words.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strengths of this paper are its elegance and strong motivation. While the individual components—averaging word embeddings to represent documents and using dropout/corruption for regularization—are not new in isolation, their synthesis within this framework and the accompanying theoretical justification are novel and valuable. The analysis in Section 3.1 is a significant contribution, providing a clear link between the stochastic corruption process and a deterministic objective function with an intuitive regularization term. This offers a principled explanation for the model's empirical success.

The experimental results, while perhaps not groundbreaking, are solid and effectively support the paper's claims. Doc2VecC consistently performs on par with or better than more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and, in some cases (long documents), Skip-thought vectors. The efficiency gains are particularly impressive; the contrast in inference time between Doc2VecC and Paragraph Vectors on the IMDB dataset (7 seconds vs. over 4 minutes) highlights a major practical advantage. The improved performance on the word analogy task is also a strong piece of evidence that the model learns higher-quality underlying word representations.

However, the empirical evaluation has some weaknesses. The chosen tasks (sentiment analysis, topic classification) are standard but do not fully demonstrate the versatility of the learned representations. The baselines, while reasonable, could be stronger. For instance, the performance of Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors can be highly sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, and it is not clear if they were tuned to their optimal potential on these specific tasks. Despite these minor limitations in the evaluation, the strengths of the algorithm's design, its efficiency, and its clear theoretical underpinnings are compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The work presents a practical and well-founded method that is likely to be useful to other researchers and practitioners.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would be strengthened by an ablation study. For example, what is the performance impact of removing the local context, making the model more akin to a denoising autoencoder? How does the performance vary with the corruption rate `q`? While the text mentions the model is "not sensitive" to `q`, showing a plot of performance vs. `q` would make this claim more concrete.
*   **Use of Visualizations:** The t-SNE plots in Figures 3 and 4 take up considerable space. While they provide a nice qualitative illustration, this space could be more effectively used for further quantitative analysis, such as the suggested ablation studies or a more detailed error analysis.
*   **Clarification on Fine-tuning:** For the SICK dataset evaluation, the paper states the model was fine-tuned on the target dataset. Was this unsupervised fine-tuning procedure also applied to the baselines (e.g., Skip-thought vectors) to ensure a fair comparison?
*   **Hypothesis on Skip-thought Vectors:** The authors hypothesize that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to long document lengths. This is a plausible explanation. To test this, it would be interesting to see a comparison on truncated versions of the IMDB reviews (e.g., using only the first 50 or 100 words). This would provide direct evidence for the hypothesis.
*   **Word Embeddings:** The claim that Doc2VecC "produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec" is well-supported by the word analogy task. It would be interesting to know if this benefit translates to downstream tasks. For example, if you were to use the pre-trained word embeddings from Doc2VecC (instead of Word2Vec embeddings) in a separate, fixed downstream model (e.g., a CNN/LSTM classifier), would you observe a performance improvement? This would further validate the quality of the learned word vectors themselves.
