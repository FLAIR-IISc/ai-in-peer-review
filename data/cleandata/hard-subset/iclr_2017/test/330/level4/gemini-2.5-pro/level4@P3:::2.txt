### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Doc2VecC (Document Vector through Corruption), a simple yet powerful framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The key contribution lies in the training process, which incorporates a "corruption" mechanism: during training, the global document context is formed by randomly dropping a significant portion of its words. The authors provide a compelling theoretical justification, showing that this corruption process is equivalent to applying a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer has the desirable effect of suppressing the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words (e.g., stop words) while favoring those of rarer, more informative words. The paper empirically validates its claims across four different NLP tasks—sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness—demonstrating that this highly efficient model achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a strong contribution that is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically validated. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Excellent Empirical Performance from a Simple and Efficient Model:** The proposed method, despite its architectural simplicity, achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on a diverse set of benchmarks. Its significant advantage in training and inference speed over more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors makes it a practically valuable contribution.
2.  **Novel and Well-Justified Training Technique:** The central idea of using corruption as a form of implicit regularization is insightful. The paper provides a clear theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) that connects the stochastic corruption process to a specific regularization term, offering a principled explanation for why the model performs well. This moves beyond heuristic approaches and provides a valuable contribution to our understanding of representation learning.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength is demonstrating that a very simple composition function (averaging) can produce high-quality document embeddings when trained with an appropriate learning objective. The proposed corruption scheme is the key element that enables this.

**On Performance and Efficiency:** The experimental evaluation is thorough. On the IMDB sentiment analysis task (Table 1), Doc2VecC matches the performance of the more complex Paragraph Vectors while being significantly faster at both training and, critically, inference on unseen documents (Table 2). The poor performance of Skip-thought Vectors on this long-document task, contrasted with Doc2VecC's success, highlights the robustness of the proposed method. On the Wikipedia classification task (Table 5), Doc2VecC consistently outperforms all baselines across different embedding dimensions, and the t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) provide qualitative support for the superiority of its learned representations.

**On the Corruption-as-Regularization Thesis:** The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as a data-dependent regularizer, is a highlight of the paper. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method. The empirical validation in Section 4.2 (Table 3) is also convincing; Doc2VecC correctly learns to assign small-norm embeddings to frequent, uninformative words, unlike Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors, which tend to push the embeddings of infrequent words towards zero regardless of their semantic content. Furthermore, the impressive results on the word analogy task (Section 4.3) show that the model does not sacrifice the quality of its word embeddings to learn good document vectors; in fact, it learns significantly better word embeddings than the standard Word2Vec baseline.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Comparison to a Bidirectional LSTM Baseline:** While the comparison to Skip-thought Vectors is valuable, a more direct and now-standard baseline for document representation is a bidirectional LSTM (or GRU). Typically, the final hidden states of the forward and backward LSTMs are concatenated to form the document representation. Including a comparison to this type of model on the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks would provide a more complete picture of where Doc2VecC stands relative to sequential models. This is particularly relevant given the discussion about model performance on texts of varying lengths.
*   **Sensitivity to the Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and that `q = 0.9` was used throughout. While this is acceptable, a small ablation study or a plot showing how performance varies with `q` would strengthen this claim and provide readers with a better practical understanding of this key hyperparameter.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Your responses to these questions would help clarify my understanding of the experiments.

*   Regarding the RNN-LM baseline in the sentiment analysis experiment: Was this model trained solely as a language model on the unlabeled data, or was it fine-tuned for the classification task using the labeled training data? Furthermore, how was the document representation extracted from the RNN-LM—was it the final hidden state, an average of all hidden states, or another method?
*   In the semantic relatedness task, you fine-tune the model on the SICK dataset. Could you clarify whether this fine-tuning was performed in an unsupervised manner (i.e., continuing the language model-style training on the SICK sentences) or in a supervised manner using the relatedness scores? The text suggests the former, but a confirmation would be helpful.
