This paper proposes a framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a weighted average of its word embeddings, guided by a data-dependent regularization term. This regularization encourages higher weights for informative or rare words while pushing the embeddings of common words towards the zero vector. The authors evaluate their method on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, demonstrating lower error rates compared to the selected baseline methods.

While the motivation for finding an efficient and effective method for encoding documents is well-founded, the paper's primary weakness is its limited technical novelty. The constituent techniques are largely established, and the main contribution appears to be the specific combination and its resulting simplicity and computational efficiency. Given that the primary claims hinge on simplicity and effectiveness, the empirical evidence presented is not sufficiently comprehensive. To be compelling, the method's superiority should be demonstrated on a broader range of tasks beyond the two presented.

Additionally, I have the following questions and suggestions:

*   Clarity is needed regarding the RNN-LM baseline. Was the model trained specifically for the downstream classification task (i.e., to minimize classification error), or was it a pre-trained language model? Furthermore, was the document representation derived from the final hidden state or an average of all hidden states?

*   The evaluation would be significantly strengthened by including a comparison against a more powerful and widely-used baseline, such as a bidirectional LSTM where the final hidden states are concatenated to form the document representation. A comparison against such a model on the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks would provide a more convincing assessment of the proposed method's performance.
