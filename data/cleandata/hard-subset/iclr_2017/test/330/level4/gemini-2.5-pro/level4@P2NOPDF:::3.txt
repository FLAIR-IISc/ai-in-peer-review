This paper proposes a simple yet effective method for learning document embeddings by summing the embeddings of their component words. The model is trained by jointly learning the word vectors while applying dropout to the input words, a process the authors refer to as 'corruption'. While the individual components of this model are not novel, their combination results in a highly efficient learning algorithm for document representation that demonstrates strong empirical performance.

The primary weakness of the paper lies in its limited technical novelty. The concept of jointly training word and document embeddings is well-established, as is the idea of representing a document via the sum of its word vectors (e.g., "The Sum of Its Parts": Joint Learning of Word and Phrase Representations with Autoencoders by Lebret and Collobert, 2014). Furthermore, the proposed 'corruption' mechanism is functionally equivalent to applying standard dropout to the input layer. When combined with a training objective and optimization procedure inspired by the word2vec framework, the paper's contribution is more of an effective synthesis of existing techniques rather than the introduction of a new paradigm.

Despite the modest novelty, the proposed model has several significant strengths. A key advantage is its efficiency, particularly at inference time. Unlike models such as Doc2Vec that require a potentially costly optimization step to compute the representation for a new document, this method only requires a simple averaging of its constituent word embeddings. Moreover, the resulting embeddings are effective by design. Because the model is trained to predict local word contexts from a global document representation, the final embedding is incentivized to capture the most salient semantic information about the document as a whole. For such a straightforward model, the performance on standard sentiment analysis and document classification benchmarks is surprisingly strong and highly encouraging.

In summary, while the paper does not introduce fundamentally new concepts, it presents a compelling case for a simple, elegant, and practical model. The trade-off for limited novelty is a significant gain in simplicity and efficiency, without a substantial compromise in performance. This work provides a valuable and powerful baseline for document representation that deserves consideration. For these reasons, I believe the paper is worthy of attention from the research community, and I recommend acceptance.
