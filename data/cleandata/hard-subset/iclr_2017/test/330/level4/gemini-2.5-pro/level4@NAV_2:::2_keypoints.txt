- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings and training with a corruption mechanism that drops words from the global context.  
- Theoretical analysis: corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that favors informative/rare words and suppresses common, non-discriminative words.  
- Empirical claim: matches or exceeds more complex state-of-the-art models on sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and word analogy tasks, while being much faster to train and infer.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection.  
- Main critique 1: technical contribution is incremental—an elegant but not fundamentally new combination of existing ideas.  
- Main critique 2: experimental evaluation omits key contemporary baselines (notably Bi-LSTMs) for document-level tasks, weakening claims of state-of-the-art performance.  
- Pointed out that averaging word embeddings is a well-established baseline; novelty lies mainly in the corruption scheme, which resembles dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders.  
- Suggestion: include Bi-LSTM (or GRU) baselines for IMDB and Wikipedia classification to better compare trade-offs between efficiency and sequential modeling.  
- Suggestion: provide sensitivity analysis for the corruption rate q (e.g., plot validation performance for q from 0.1 to 0.9) rather than reporting a single value.  
- Suggestion: temper state-of-the-art claims and emphasize that Doc2VecC is competitive and much more efficient, noting LSTM-based methods outperform on some tasks (e.g., SICK).  
- Request clarification: was the RNNLM baseline trained purely as a language model on unlabeled data or fine-tuned discriminatively for downstream tasks?  
- Request clarification: how was the final document representation derived from the RNN hidden states (final hidden state, average pooling, or another strategy)?
