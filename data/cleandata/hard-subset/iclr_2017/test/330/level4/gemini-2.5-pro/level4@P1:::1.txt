### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient method for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent each document as the average of the embeddings of its constituent words. These word embeddings are learned using a CBoW-style objective that predicts a target word from both its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. The key innovation is how this global context is formed: it is the average of word embeddings from a randomly corrupted version of the full document. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis in Section 3, demonstrating that this corruption process is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words. The paper claims that this approach is extremely fast, scales to massive datasets, and produces document representations that are competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: **Accept**

This paper presents an interesting, elegant, and very efficient algorithm for an important problem. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The proposed method is simple, well-motivated, and its impressive efficiency makes it highly practical for real-world, large-scale applications.
2.  The theoretical analysis connecting the corruption mechanism to data-dependent regularization is a significant contribution, providing clear insight into why the model works and justifying its design.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its elegant and well-analyzed approach. While the individual components (averaging word embeddings, CBoW, dropout) are not new, their synthesis within the Doc2VecC framework is novel and effective. The analysis in Section 3, which frames the corruption as a regularizer, is particularly insightful and elevates the paper beyond a purely empirical contribution. It provides a principled explanation for why simply averaging word embeddings, a technique often used as a simple baseline, can be made to work so well.

The empirical results demonstrate that this simple architecture matches or exceeds the performance of more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors on several standard benchmarks. The reported training and inference speeds are a major practical advantage, making the method an attractive option for practitioners. The analysis in sections 4.2 and 4.3, showing how Doc2VecC learns more meaningful embeddings for rare words compared to Word2Vec and produces superior word analogy results, provides strong evidence supporting the claims about the benefits of the corruption-based regularization.

However, the submission has a notable weakness in its empirical evaluation. The tasks, while standard, are primarily classification-focused (sentiment analysis, topic classification). The evaluation could have been more comprehensive and impactful by including tasks where high-quality document representations are arguably more critical, such as information retrieval or question answering. Furthermore, as noted by other reviewers, some of the baselines could be considered soft. These limitations, while significant, do not outweigh the novelty and insight of the proposed method.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Evaluation Tasks:** To better showcase the model's capabilities, I would encourage the authors to consider evaluating on a more diverse set of downstream tasks. For example, showing strong performance on a document retrieval or clustering task would make a more compelling case for the general-purpose nature of the learned representations.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study. For example, how does performance vary with the corruption rate `q`? The paper states `q=0.9` was used throughout, but showing the sensitivity to this hyperparameter would be valuable. Similarly, an experiment explicitly comparing the proposed corruption strategy to standard dropout or a simple, uniform subsampling of words would help isolate the benefits of the proposed data-dependent regularization.
*   **Use of Space:** The t-SNE visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 take up a considerable amount of space (nearly a full page). While visually appealing, this space could be used more effectively for the additional quantitative analysis or ablation studies suggested above.
*   **Comparison with Skip-thought Vectors:** The paper hypothesizes that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to long document lengths. This is an interesting point. Have the authors considered a more controlled experiment on datasets with varying document lengths to systematically test this hypothesis? This could help to clearly delineate the respective strengths and weaknesses of Doc2VecC and RNN-based encoders.
