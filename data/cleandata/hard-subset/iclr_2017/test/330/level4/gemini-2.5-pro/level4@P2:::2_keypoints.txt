- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings and training with random-word corruption as a data-dependent regularizer.  
- Claims high efficiency, very fast inference, and superior word embeddings; reports competitive or state-of-the-art results on IMDB, Wikipedia, and SICK.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason: Limited technical novelty—uses established ideas (averaging, CBoW, dropout) and is primarily an elegant engineering simplification.  
- Main reason: Incomplete empirical comparison—missing crucial baselines like bidirectional LSTMs/GRUs, so state-of-the-art claims are under-supported.  
- Supporting: Motivation and simplicity are strong; corruption-as-regularizer analysis provides useful theoretical grounding.  
- Concern: Paper acknowledges LSTMs beat Doc2VecC on SICK, so LSTM performance on longer texts (IMDB, Wikipedia) must be evaluated.  
- Concern: Averaging-based representations and corruption resemble prior work (e.g., denoising autoencoders, dropout), making the contribution incremental.  
- Suggestion: Add a bidirectional LSTM baseline in results tables to position Doc2VecC relative to common strong baselines.  
- Suggestion: Clarify RNN-LM baseline training details and how document representations were extracted for reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Strengthen empirical/theoretical claims with visual analysis (e.g., plot L2 norm of word vectors vs frequency for Word2Vec and Doc2VecC).  
- Questions for authors: Was the RNN-LM trained purely as a language model or fine-tuned for classification, and how was the document vector derived?  
- Questions for authors: Why was a bidirectional LSTM baseline omitted for sentiment analysis and document classification?
