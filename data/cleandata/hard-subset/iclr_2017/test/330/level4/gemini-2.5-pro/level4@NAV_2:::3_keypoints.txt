- Presents Doc2VecC: represent documents as the simple average of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism that randomly drops words during training.  
- Corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that favors informative words and suppresses common ones.  
- Claims: produces high-quality document embeddings that match or outperform state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Major practical advantage: extremely fast to train and apply, no expensive inference step for unseen documents (unlike Paragraph Vectors).  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Primary reasons for acceptance: compelling combination of simplicity, computational efficiency, and strong empirical performance.  
- Secondary reason: clear, insightful theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) explaining the corruption mechanism.  
- Novelty: constituent ideas are not wholly new, but their synthesis into an effective, efficient framework is a valuable contribution.  
- Empirical support: strong results on IMDB (sentiment) and Wikipedia (document classification) datasets.  
- Suggestion: better position the work relative to compositional-document prior work (e.g., Lebret & Collobert 2014).  
- Suggestion: explicitly frame the corruption model as input dropout and connect to the dropout literature.  
- Suggestion: include an ablation or plot showing sensitivity to corruption rate q (claims of insensitivity currently under-supported).  
- Questions for authors: Could the corruption insight inspire alternative, more direct regularizers to suppress uninformative words?  
- Questions for authors: Any analysis showing Skip-thought vectors’ poorer IMDB performance is due to long document length (e.g., evaluate on shorter reviews)?
