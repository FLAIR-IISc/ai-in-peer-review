This paper introduces a method for learning document representations by computing a weighted average of word embeddings. The core idea is to employ a data-dependent regularization that up-weights informative words and pushes the embeddings of common, uninformative words towards the zero vector. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, where their method achieves lower error rates than the reported baselines.

**Strengths:**
*   The primary strength of the proposed method lies in its simplicity and efficiency. It provides a fast and straightforward way to encode documents into fixed-size vectors.
*   The motivation is clear: to create a document embedding that intelligently filters out non-essential words.
*   Despite its simplicity, the model performs very well on the two tasks presented, outperforming several other document embedding techniques.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**
The main weakness of this work is its limited technical novelty. The core components—word embeddings, weighted averaging, and regularization—are all well-established techniques. The primary contribution appears to be a specific, simple, and effective combination of these ideas. While practical utility is a valid contribution, the paper's claims would be significantly strengthened by more comprehensive empirical validation. Given that the method's main selling point is its practical performance and efficiency, demonstrating its effectiveness on a broader range of tasks beyond the two presented would be necessary to establish its general applicability.

A significant omission in the experimental comparison is a bidirectional RNN/LSTM baseline. Currently, a standard and powerful approach for sentence and document encoding involves using a bidirectional LSTM and pooling its hidden states (e.g., by concatenating the final forward and backward states or through attention). A comparison against such a model is crucial for contextualizing the performance of the proposed method. It would be very informative to see how this simple averaging scheme stacks up against a more complex, sequential encoder on the same tasks.

**Questions for the Authors:**
Could you please clarify some details about the RNN-LM baseline used in your experiments?
1.  Was the model trained purely as a language model, or was it fine-tuned on the downstream classification task?
2.  How was the final document representation generated from the RNN? Did you use the final hidden state, an average of all hidden states, or another pooling method?
