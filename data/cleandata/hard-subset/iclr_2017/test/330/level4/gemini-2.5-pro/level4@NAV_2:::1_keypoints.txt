Proposes Doc2VecC: represent each document as the average of word embeddings with a corruption mechanism that removes words during training.  
Corruption model is the main contribution, randomly removing words from the global context during training.  
Theoretical analysis shows corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that favors rare/informative words and suppresses common, non-discriminative ones.  
Claims that Doc2VecC produces document and word embeddings that match or outperform several state-of-the-art models on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
Paper is elegant, simple, and efficient; recommended for acceptance based on algorithmic clarity and theoretical insight.  
Key advantage: efficiency in training and in generating representations for unseen documents, addressing a drawback of Paragraph Vectors.  
Section 3.1 derivation linking corruption to regularization is a strong, insightful contribution.  
Empirical evaluation is limited to standard sentiment/topic benchmarks and could be more comprehensive.  
Too much space is devoted to t-SNE visualizations instead of deeper quantitative analyses or ablation studies.  
Suggest expanding evaluation to harder/other tasks (information retrieval, short-text similarity, question answering).  
Recommend supplementing visualizations with quantitative cluster-quality metrics (e.g., silhouette score, Davies-Bouldin index).  
Request a sensitivity analysis for the corruption rate q (paper sets q = 0.9 and claims low sensitivity).  
Ask for clarification whether the local context ct is learned via a CBoW-style objective to clarify interplay between local and global contexts.
