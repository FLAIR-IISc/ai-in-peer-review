This paper presents a method for learning document embeddings by jointly training word embeddings and a document representation. The proposed model represents a document as the sum of its constituent word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism—effectively dropout on the input words—applied during training. While the individual components of the model are not novel, their combination results in an efficient and effective learning algorithm for document representation with strong empirical performance.

The primary weakness of this work lies in its limited novelty, as its core components are based on well-established techniques. The concept of representing a document as a composition of its word embeddings, particularly as a sum, has been explored previously (e.g., "The Sum of Its Parts" by Lebret and Collobert, 2014). Similarly, the joint learning of word and document vectors is a known approach. The proposed corruption mechanism is a straightforward application of dropout to the input word embeddings, and the training objective is heavily inspired by the word2vec framework. While the specific combination of these elements is unique to this paper, no single component introduces a new fundamental concept.

Despite the limited novelty, the paper's contribution is significant from a practical standpoint, primarily due to the model's simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results. A key advantage is its efficiency at inference time. Unlike models such as Doc2Vec that require a separate, often complex, inference step to generate an embedding for a new document, this method only requires a simple averaging of the pre-trained word embeddings. This makes the model highly scalable and easy to deploy.

Furthermore, the training objective provides an elegant justification for why such a simple compositional vector should work well. By construction, the learned document embedding must capture the global semantic context necessary to predict local word occurrences. This ensures that the representation is not merely a bag-of-words but distills salient thematic information from the entire document. The empirical results presented on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks are surprisingly strong, particularly for a model of this simplicity. Its performance is competitive with more complex architectures, highlighting the effectiveness of the approach.

In summary, while the paper does not introduce fundamentally new techniques, it demonstrates that a thoughtful combination of existing ideas can lead to a highly effective and practical model. The trade-off of conceptual novelty for significant gains in simplicity and efficiency is a valuable contribution to the field. The model serves as a strong baseline, and its performance suggests that for many tasks, complex recurrent or convolutional architectures may not be necessary. For these reasons, I believe the work will be of interest to the wider research community and recommend acceptance.
