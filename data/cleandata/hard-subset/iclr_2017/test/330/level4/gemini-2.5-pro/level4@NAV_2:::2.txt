This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and simple framework for learning document representations. The proposed method represents a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The core contribution lies in its training objective, which incorporates a corruption mechanism that randomly drops words from the document's global context. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer, which implicitly favors informative or rare words while suppressing the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and word analogy tasks that this simple architecture can match or exceed the performance of more complex state-of-the-art models, while being significantly faster to train and use for inference.

I vote for rejection. My primary reasons are:
1.  The technical contribution, while elegant, appears to be an incremental combination of existing ideas rather than a significant conceptual advance.
2.  The experimental evaluation is missing a key class of contemporary baselines (e.g., Bi-LSTMs) for the main document-level tasks, which makes the claims of state-of-the-art performance difficult to fully validate.

The main idea of representing a document by averaging its word embeddings is a well-established baseline. The novelty of this work comes from the corruption scheme and its theoretical justification as a regularizer. While this analysis is insightful, the corruption technique itself is conceptually similar to dropout and marginalized denoising autoencoders, which are cited by the authors. The contribution, therefore, seems to be a very effective and well-engineered application of these principles to the CBoW architecture for creating document vectors, rather than a fundamentally new approach to representation learning.

Furthermore, to substantiate the claim of matching or outperforming state-of-the-art methods, a more thorough comparison is needed. For document-level tasks like sentiment analysis (IMDB) and document classification (Wikipedia), a standard and powerful baseline is a bidirectional LSTM (or GRU) where the final hidden states are used as the representation. This class of models is known to be very effective at capturing sequential information and often serves as a strong benchmark. While the paper compares against Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, the absence of a direct comparison with a standard Bi-LSTM baseline on these specific tasks weakens the empirical claims.

(These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision)
*   I strongly encourage the authors to include a comparison against a Bi-LSTM baseline for the IMDB and Wikipedia classification tasks. This would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs between Doc2VecC's efficiency and the performance of more complex sequential models.
*   The claim that the model performance is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" could be strengthened. Showing a plot of performance on a validation set versus different values of `q` (e.g., from 0.1 to 0.9) would be more convincing than stating a single value was used throughout.
*   The phrasing of the state-of-the-art claims could be more nuanced. For instance, on the SICK dataset, the paper acknowledges that LSTM-based methods perform better. Highlighting that Doc2VecC is competitive with the state-of-the-art while being much more efficient might be a more precise and compelling way to frame the contribution.

Could you please clarify the experimental details for the RNNLM baseline?
*   Was the model trained purely as a language model on the unlabeled data, or was it trained/fine-tuned discriminatively for the downstream classification tasks?
*   How was the final document representation derived from the RNN's hidden states? Was it the final hidden state, an average of all hidden states, or another pooling strategy?
