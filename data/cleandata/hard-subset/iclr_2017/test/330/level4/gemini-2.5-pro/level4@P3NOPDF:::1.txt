This paper presents a method for computing vector representations for documents. The approach adapts a skip-gram learning objective by introducing a global document context vector as a regularizer, with dropout applied to this vector to improve generalization.

**Strengths**

While the individual components of the model—such as the skip-gram objective and dropout—are well-established, their combination in this particular fashion for document representation is novel. The introduction of the global context vector with dropout as a regularizer is an interesting and intuitive idea.

A significant strength of the paper is the detailed analysis of the model's behavior in Section 3. The authors provide valuable insights into the properties of their proposed method, which helps build confidence in the approach beyond its performance on downstream tasks. Furthermore, the paper is well-written, and the proposed algorithm is conceptually clear and computationally efficient.

**Weaknesses**

The primary weakness of this submission lies in the scope and depth of its empirical evaluation. The chosen tasks, primarily sentiment analysis and k-way classification, are somewhat limited. The evaluation could be made significantly more compelling by demonstrating the method's effectiveness on a broader or more challenging set of downstream tasks where high-quality document representations are crucial, such as information retrieval or semantic textual similarity.

Additionally, I concur with the sentiment expressed by other reviewers that the chosen baselines are not particularly strong. The comparison would be more convincing if the proposed model were benchmarked against more recent or state-of-the-art document embedding methods.

Finally, the use of page space could be optimized. For instance, the two-thirds of a page dedicated to t-SNE visualizations, while illustrative, seems excessive. This space could have been better utilized for a more thorough quantitative analysis, ablation studies (e.g., on the impact of the dropout rate), or the inclusion of results on additional datasets.

**Recommendation**

Despite my concerns regarding the limited empirical evaluation and the choice of baselines, I lean towards accepting this paper. The proposed algorithm is interesting, well-presented, and highly efficient. Its conceptual simplicity makes it a practical method that could be valuable for other researchers and practitioners. It is reasonable to assume that the ideas presented here could be usefully applied or built upon by the community. Therefore, I believe the strengths of the core idea and its clear presentation outweigh the weaknesses in the current experimental setup.
