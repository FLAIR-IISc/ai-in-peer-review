- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings learned jointly in a word2vec-style model.  
- Uses a corruption (dropout) model on input words, analyzed as a data-dependent regularizer favoring informative/rare words.  
- Corruption pushes embeddings of common, non-discriminative words toward zero.  
- Claims high efficiency and simplicity, with competitive or superior performance on sentiment, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Recommends acceptance based on simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results.  
- Major strength: fast test-time inference (simple averaging) vs costly inference in Paragraph Vectors.  
- Empirical results match or outperform more complex baselines (Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought); theoretical analysis explains corruption benefits.  
- Primary weakness: limited novelty—combines well-known ideas (word-embedding averaging, joint training, dropout/CBoW) rather than presenting fundamentally new concepts.  
- Suggests stronger positioning against prior composition-based document models (e.g., Lebret & Collobert 2014).  
- Requests an ablation study on subsampling: compare Doc2VecC (with/without subsampling) and Word2Vec+AVG (with/without subsampling) to isolate corruption effects.
