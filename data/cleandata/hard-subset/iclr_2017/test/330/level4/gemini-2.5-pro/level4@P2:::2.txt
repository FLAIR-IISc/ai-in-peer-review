### Review

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The main contribution lies in a training procedure that incorporates a corruption mechanism, where words are randomly dropped from the document's global context. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer, which encourages the model to learn smaller embeddings for frequent, non-discriminative words while preserving the signal from rarer, more informative words.

The paper claims several advantages for this approach: the model is computationally efficient, scaling to billions of words per hour on a single machine; inference for new documents is extremely fast; and the resulting word embeddings are of higher quality than those produced by Word2Vec. Empirically, the authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK) tasks, often outperforming more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While the combination of ideas is effective, the core components of the model (averaging word embeddings, CBoW architecture, corruption/dropout) are based on well-established techniques. The primary contribution is an elegant simplification and effective application of these ideas rather than a fundamental algorithmic advance.
2.  **Incomplete Experimental Comparison:** The empirical results are strong against the chosen baselines. However, the paper omits a comparison to a crucial and widely-used baseline for document representation: bidirectional LSTMs/GRUs. Without this comparison, the claims of matching or outperforming the state-of-the-art are not fully substantiated.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The motivation for creating a simple, fast, and effective document representation method is excellent. The proposed Doc2VecC model is compelling in its simplicity and achieves impressive performance. The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as a data-dependent regularizer, provides a nice theoretical grounding for the method's success.

However, the argument for acceptance rests heavily on the model's empirical strength, which makes a comprehensive comparison to relevant baselines essential. My primary concern is the absence of a comparison with recurrent neural network models, such as a bidirectional LSTM, for the document classification and sentiment analysis tasks. It is now standard practice to use the concatenated final hidden states of a bi-LSTM as a strong baseline for document representation. The paper itself acknowledges that LSTM-based methods outperform Doc2VecC on the shorter sentences of the SICK dataset. This makes it critical to understand how they would perform on the longer documents in the IMDB and Wikipedia datasets, where LSTMs might also be very effective. Given that the paper's main selling point is achieving state-of-the-art performance with a much simpler model, demonstrating superiority over this strong and popular class of models is necessary to make a convincing case.

Furthermore, while the combination of techniques is clever, the technical novelty is incremental. The idea of representing documents by averaging word embeddings has been explored before. The model architecture is a direct extension of Word2Vec's CBoW, and the corruption mechanism is conceptually similar to dropout and the principles behind denoising autoencoders. The contribution is thus more about showing how to effectively combine these existing parts into a highly efficient and performant system, which is a valuable engineering contribution but may fall short of the bar for a significant research contribution at this conference without more comprehensive evidence of its superiority.

**4. Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by including a bidirectional LSTM baseline in Tables 1 and 5. This would provide a more complete picture of where Doc2VecC stands in the landscape of document representation techniques.
*   The details of the RNN-LM baseline are sparse. Clarifying how it was trained (e.g., as a pure language model or fine-tuned for classification) and how the document representation was extracted would be helpful for reproducibility.
*   The analysis of the data-dependent regularization is a highlight of the paper. This could be further strengthened with a more qualitative or visual analysis. For example, a plot showing the L2 norm of word vectors against their frequency for both Word2Vec and Doc2VecC could visually demonstrate the claimed regularization effect more directly than the example words in Table 3.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

Your answers to these questions would help clarify my understanding of the experiments.
1.  For the RNN-LM baseline used in the sentiment analysis experiment, was the model trained purely as a language model on the unlabeled data, or was it trained/fine-tuned to minimize classification error on the labeled training data? Furthermore, how was the final document representation derived from the model (e.g., the final hidden state, an average of all hidden states)?
2.  Could you elaborate on the decision not to include a bidirectional LSTM-based model as a baseline for the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks? This approach is a very common and powerful method for generating document representations, and its inclusion seems critical for substantiating the claim that your much simpler model is competitive with the state-of-the-art.
