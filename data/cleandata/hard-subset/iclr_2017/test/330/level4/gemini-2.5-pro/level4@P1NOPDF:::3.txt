This paper proposes a method for learning document embeddings by averaging their constituent word embeddings. These word and document representations are learned jointly, with a random subset of words being dropped out ('corrupted') during each training step. While the model's components are not in themselves novel, the result is an efficient learning algorithm for document representations that demonstrates strong empirical performance.

The core ideas are well-established in prior work. Neither the joint training of word and document embeddings nor the representation of a document as an average of its word vectors is new (e.g., Lebret and Collobert, "The Sum of Its Parts," 2014). Furthermore, the proposed 'corruption' mechanism is equivalent to applying standard dropout to the input layer. Combined with a word2vec-style loss function, the paper's contribution in terms of technical novelty is therefore limited.

On the other hand, the model is highly efficient at inference time, requiring only a simple average of word embeddings rather than a complex optimization step as in Doc2Vec. By construction, the resulting embedding captures salient global information about the document—specifically, the information most useful for predicting words in their local contexts. For such a simple model, its performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks is quite encouraging.

Overall, despite the limited novelty, the model's simplicity, efficiency, and strong performance make it a noteworthy contribution. It presents a valuable and effective baseline that is worthy of wider readership and study, and I recommend acceptance.
