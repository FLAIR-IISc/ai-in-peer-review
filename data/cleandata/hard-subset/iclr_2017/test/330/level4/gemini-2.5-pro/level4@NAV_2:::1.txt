This paper presents Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and straightforward framework for learning document vector representations. The proposed method represents each document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The main contribution lies in the introduction of a corruption model that randomly removes words from a document's global context during training. The authors provide a detailed theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process functions as an effective data-dependent regularizer, which favors rare or informative words while suppressing the embeddings of common, non-discriminative ones. The paper claims that this simple and fast architecture produces document and word embeddings that match or outperform several state-of-the-art models on tasks including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.

I recommend that this paper be accepted. The primary reasons are the elegance and efficiency of the proposed algorithm and the insightful theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism. The approach is well-motivated, and the idea of framing the corruption as a regularizer is a valuable contribution that could inspire further work.

My recommendation is based on the strength of the proposed method and its theoretical justification. Doc2VecC combines simple, known components into a novel and effective framework. Its main advantage is its efficiency, both in training and, crucially, in generating representations for unseen documents, which is a significant drawback for competing methods like Paragraph Vectors. The derivation in Section 3.1, which connects the corruption process to a data-dependent regularizer, is a strong point that provides a clear understanding of why the model behaves as it does.

However, this positive assessment is moderated by the empirical evaluation, which could be more comprehensive. While the model performs well, the experiments are largely confined to standard sentiment and topic classification benchmarks. These tasks may not fully highlight the nuances of the learned representations compared to more challenging natural language understanding tasks. Additionally, considerable space is dedicated to t-SNE visualizations. While visually appealing, this space might have been better utilized for deeper quantitative analysis, such as ablation studies that more directly demonstrate the impact of the corruption regularizer.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.
*   **Evaluation Tasks:** The paper would be strengthened by expanding the evaluation to more complex benchmarks. Have the authors considered applying Doc2VecC to tasks such as information retrieval, short text similarity, or question answering, where the quality of document representations is critical?
*   **Quantitative Visualization Analysis:** The t-SNE plots in Figure 3 are illustrative. Could you supplement this visualization with a quantitative measure of cluster quality (e.g., silhouette score or Davies-Bouldin index) to more rigorously support the claim that Doc2VecC produces better-separated representations than Doc2Vec?
*   **Role of Corruption Rate:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and sets it to 0.9. It would be helpful to include a small sensitivity analysis or a plot showing how performance varies with different values of `q`. This would provide readers with more practical guidance for implementing the model.
*   **Clarification on Model:** The introduction states the model "resembles that of Word2Vec," and Section 2 mentions focusing on CBoW. Could you clarify if the local context (`ct`) is also learned via a CBoW-style objective? A brief clarification could help readers better understand the interplay between the local and global context objectives.
