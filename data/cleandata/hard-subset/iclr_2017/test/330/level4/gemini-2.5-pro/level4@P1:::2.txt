This paper introduces Doc2VecC, an efficient framework for learning document representations. The proposed method represents a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The core idea is a corruption mechanism applied during training, where words are randomly dropped from the document's global context. The authors show that this acts as a data-dependent regularizer, which favors informative or rare words while shrinking the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words towards zero. This approach is presented as being highly efficient, scalable, and simple, while producing high-quality document representations. The authors demonstrate through experiments that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

I recommend rejecting this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper lacks significant technical novelty, as its primary components are adaptations of well-established techniques.
2.  The experimental evidence, while positive, is not comprehensive enough to convincingly establish the proposed method's general superiority over other approaches.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The main contribution appears to be the specific application of a corruption scheme to the global document context and the theoretical analysis that frames it as a data-dependent regularizer. While this analysis is insightful, the practical implementation is a straightforward combination of existing ideas. The model architecture builds directly on Word2Vec (specifically, the CBOW model), the corruption is a form of dropout, and representing documents by averaging word vectors is a very common baseline. The paper's strength lies in cleverly combining these elements to create a fast and simple method, but it does not introduce a fundamentally new approach to representation learning.

The primary selling point of the method is its simplicity and speed, coupled with strong performance. To fully support such a claim, I would expect a more thorough evaluation. While the results on the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks are strong, the set of baselines could be stronger. The claim that this is a superior way to learn document representations would be more convincing if tested against a wider range of tasks and more contemporary, powerful models.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision.

A significant baseline is missing from the comparison. By the time of this work, recurrent models, particularly bidirectional LSTMs (Bi-LSTMs), have become a very strong and widely used approach for sentence and document encoding. A common method is to concatenate the final forward and backward hidden states to form the document representation.

Including a comparison against a Bi-LSTM baseline on the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks would be crucial. This would provide a more complete assessment of Doc2VecC's performance against contemporary methods and help contextualize the trade-off between its simplicity/speed and the expressive power of more complex recurrent models.

**Questions for the Authors:**

I have one question to better understand the experimental setup:

1.  Regarding the RNN-LM baseline: Was the model trained purely as a language model (i.e., to predict the next word), or was it fine-tuned to minimize classification error for the downstream tasks? Furthermore, how was the document representation extracted from the RNN-LM—was it the final hidden state, an average of all hidden states, or another method?
