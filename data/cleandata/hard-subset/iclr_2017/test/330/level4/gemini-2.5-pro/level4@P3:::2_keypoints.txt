- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings and train with random-word "corruption" to form global context.  
- Shows theoretical justification that corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-discriminative words.  
- Empirically validates Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness, achieving competitive or superior results to Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought.  
- Highlights strong efficiency: much faster training and especially inference on unseen documents compared to more complex models.  
- Decision: Accept—method is well-motivated, theoretically sound, and empirically strong.  
- Key reason 1: Simple model yields excellent empirical performance across diverse benchmarks.  
- Key reason 2: Novel corruption-as-regularization training technique with clear theoretical analysis.  
- On IMDB, matches Paragraph Vectors while being significantly faster; Skip-thought performs poorly on long documents.  
- On Wikipedia classification, outperforms baselines across embedding sizes; t-SNE visualizations qualitatively support representation quality.  
- Empirical evidence shows Doc2VecC learns small-norm embeddings for frequent stop-words and better word embeddings (strong word analogy results) than Word2Vec.  
- Suggestion: include a bidirectional LSTM/GRU baseline (concatenated final states) for more complete comparisons on sentiment and classification.  
- Suggestion: provide an ablation or plot showing sensitivity to corruption rate q (they used q=0.9) to support robustness claims.  
- Question: Was the RNN-LM baseline trained solely as a language model or fine-tuned for classification, and how was its document representation extracted (final hidden state, average, etc.)?  
- Question: For the SICK semantic relatedness task, was fine-tuning done unsupervised (continue LM-style training) or supervised using relatedness labels?
