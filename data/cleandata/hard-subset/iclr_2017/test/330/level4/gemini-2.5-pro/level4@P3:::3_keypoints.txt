1. Proposes Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of word embeddings trained with a corrupted global context.  
2. Corruption = randomly sampling words from the document to form global context.  
3. Training objective mirrors Word2Vec CBoW: predict target word from local + corrupted global context.  
4. Theoretical analysis frames corruption as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-discriminative words.  
5. Empirically matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
6. Much faster to train and infer than models like Paragraph Vectors (no per-document inference step).  
7. Recommendation: Accept — main reasons are efficiency/simplicity and strong empirical performance.  
8. Strength: effective synthesis of averaging, CBoW objective, and input-level dropout.  
9. Section 3.1 analysis convincingly explains why stop words get smaller embedding norms than in other models.  
10. Results show substantial inference-time advantage (seconds vs. minutes for large test sets).  
11. Word embeddings learned are high quality, improving on Word2Vec in analogy tasks.  
12. Novelty is incremental but the clear, well-executed baseline is a valuable contribution.  
13. Suggest explicitly framing corruption as dropout to clarify connections to existing regularization.  
14. Suggest reporting sensitivity analysis for corruption rate q (e.g., plot performance from q=0.1 to 0.9).  
15. Suggest ablation comparing unbiased scaling (1/(1-q)) vs simple mask-out to validate theoretical choice.  
16. Question: How does the model scale to very long documents (e.g., books) — when does averaging wash out signal?  
17. Question: Any qualitative analysis showing improved representations for polysemous words due to global context?  
18. Question: Clarify differences from Huang et al. (2012) — is corruption the primary distinction or are there other architectural differences?
