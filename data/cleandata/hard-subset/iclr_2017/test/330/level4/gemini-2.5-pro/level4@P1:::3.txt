### **Review**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and simple framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent each document as the average of its constituent word embeddings. These embeddings are learned jointly within a word2vec-style framework, where the averaged document vector provides a global context for predicting a target word from its local context. A key contribution is the use of a "corruption" model—effectively dropout on the input words—which the authors analyze as a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer is shown to favor informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words towards zero. The authors demonstrate that this straightforward approach is highly efficient and achieves performance that is competitive with, or superior to, state-of-the-art models on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

Overall, I recommend **acceptance**. The primary reasons are the model's compelling simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance. While the individual components of the model are not particularly novel, their combination results in an effective and practical method for document representation that is valuable to the community.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of the proposed method is its efficiency, particularly at test time. Unlike Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), which requires a costly inference step to generate representations for unseen documents, Doc2VecC computes the document vector via a simple average of word embeddings. This makes it highly scalable and practical for real-world applications.

This efficiency does not come at the cost of performance; the experimental results are quite strong. The paper shows that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms several more complex baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, on tasks like sentiment analysis and document classification. For such a simple model to achieve these results is encouraging and highlights the power of the approach. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption mechanism as an adaptive, data-dependent regularizer, is also a valuable contribution that provides insight into why the model performs well.

However, the paper's primary weakness is its limited novelty. The core ideas are largely a recombination of existing techniques. The concept of representing a document as a composition of its word embeddings (e.g., by summing or averaging) is well-established. Jointly training word and document-level representations has also been explored previously (e.g., Lebret and Collobert, 2014, "The Sum of Its Parts..."). Furthermore, the corruption mechanism is functionally identical to applying dropout to a bag-of-words input, and the training objective is a straightforward extension of the CBoW model from word2vec. Despite this, the paper successfully demonstrates that this particular combination of simple, well-understood components can be surprisingly powerful, rivaling more sophisticated architectures. The simplicity and strong performance make the method a worthy contribution.

#### **4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Positioning against related work:** The discussion of related work could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning Doc2VecC against prior work that also represents documents as a composition of word embeddings. A more direct comparison to models like that of Lebret and Collobert (2014) would help clarify the specific contributions of this paper beyond the corruption mechanism.
*   **Ablation study on subsampling:** The paper notes that because of the data-dependent regularization from corruption, the model does not rely on the frequent word subsampling trick used in word2vec. This is an interesting claim. Could you provide a more direct ablation study to support this? For example, it would be illuminating to see a comparison of Doc2VecC (with and without subsampling) against Word2Vec+AVG (with and without subsampling) to more clearly isolate the effects of the corruption regularizer versus the subsampling heuristic.
