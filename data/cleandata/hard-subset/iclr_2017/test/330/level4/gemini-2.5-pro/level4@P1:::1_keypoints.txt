- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings learned with a CBoW-style objective using a randomly corrupted global context.  
- Corruption of the document is theoretically shown to act as a data-dependent regularizer penalizing frequent, non-discriminative words.  
- Method is simple, highly efficient, and scales to large datasets.  
- Empirically matches or outperforms more complex models (Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought) on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Produces more meaningful embeddings for rare words and strong word analogy results due to corruption-based regularization.  
- Decision: Accept — due to practical efficiency and the theoretical contribution explaining the corruption mechanism.  
- Main weakness: empirical evaluation is limited to classification-focused tasks and could be broadened to retrieval or QA.  
- Some baselines in comparisons may be weak or “soft,” limiting empirical conclusiveness.  
- Recommends additional ablation studies (e.g., sensitivity to corruption rate q and comparison to standard dropout or uniform subsampling).  
- Suggests reallocating space from t-SNE visualizations to more quantitative analyses or ablations.  
- Proposes controlled experiments varying document length to validate the claim about Skip-thought performance on long documents.
