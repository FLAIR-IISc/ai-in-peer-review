- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings and learn embeddings via a CBoW-style objective using the averaged (global) context.  
- Novel "corruption" mechanism: randomly drop words during training to create the corrupted document vector.  
- Theoretical analysis shows corruption is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes frequent non-discriminative words.  
- Empirical evidence (Table 3) supports the theory: stop words get near-zero embeddings while informative rare words are preserved.  
- Achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Learns higher-quality word embeddings than Word2Vec (demonstrated on analogy task).  
- Major practical advantage: very efficient training and near-instantaneous inference (scales to billions of words/hour on a CPU).  
- Avoids per-document vectors and expensive recurrent computations, outperforming Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thoughts in speed and some tasks.  
- Main strengths: elegant, simple design and principled theoretical justification beyond empirical results.  
- Empirical evaluation is somewhat narrow; could include tasks like information retrieval or question answering.  
- Baseline comparisons (e.g., Paragraph Vectors) may lack sufficient hyperparameter tuning discussion to ensure fairness.  
- Suggest replacing some t-SNE visual space with quantitative analyses (e.g., ablation on corruption rate q and context window).  
- Recommend analyzing performance as a function of document length to validate the claim that Doc2VecC suits longer documents.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to corruption rate q (they use q=0.9), clarification of fine-tuning objective on SICK, and whether Skip-thoughts' poorer IMDB performance could be due to domain mismatch rather than document length.
