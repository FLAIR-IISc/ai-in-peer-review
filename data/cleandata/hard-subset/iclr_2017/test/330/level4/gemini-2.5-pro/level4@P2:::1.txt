### Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its constituent word embeddings. The model learns these embeddings in a way that makes this simple averaging meaningful. This is achieved by incorporating a "global context" vector (the averaged document representation) into a Word2Vec-style CBoW prediction task.

The main novelty lies in the "corruption" mechanism, where words are randomly dropped from the document context during training. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer has the desirable effect of penalizing the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words, pushing their norms towards zero, while preserving the signal from rarer, more informative words.

The authors demonstrate that their method achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. A key advantage of Doc2VecC is its efficiency; it can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single CPU and performs inference for unseen documents almost instantaneously. Furthermore, the paper shows that the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are of higher quality than those produced by Word2Vec on a word analogy task.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method is elegant, simple, and highly efficient. The novel combination of known techniques—averaging embeddings and corruption—is well-motivated and produces a strong practical model.
2.  The paper includes a valuable theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 that connects the corruption mechanism to a data-dependent regularization term. This provides a principled explanation for the model's effectiveness, which is a significant contribution beyond a purely empirical result.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its thoughtful design and analysis. While representing documents by averaging word vectors is not a new idea, the innovation here is to make this composition method an integral part of the learning objective. By including the corrupted average of document word vectors as a global context, the model is forced to learn word embeddings that are meaningful when combined linearly.

The theoretical justification for the corruption process is a standout feature. The derivation showing that random word dropout approximates a specific regularizer (Equation 7) that penalizes common but non-informative words is insightful. It elevates the method from a heuristic trick to a well-understood mechanism. This theoretical claim is further supported by empirical evidence in Table 3, which clearly shows that Doc2VecC, unlike Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors, successfully learns near-zero embeddings for frequent stop words while retaining embeddings for rare but meaningful words.

The model’s efficiency is another major advantage. By avoiding the need to learn a unique vector for each document (unlike Paragraph Vectors) and the expensive recurrent computations of LSTMs or Skip-thought vectors, Doc2VecC is highly scalable for both training and inference. The reported test-time inference speed (7 seconds for 25,000 documents vs. over 4 minutes for Paragraph Vectors) is a compelling practical benefit for real-world applications.

While the empirical evaluation could be broader, the results presented are strong. The model matches or surpasses strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, on tasks involving longer documents like sentiment analysis (IMDB) and document classification (Wikipedia), validating the authors' hypothesis about the model's strengths.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Scope of Empirical Evaluation:** The choice of tasks (sentiment analysis, topic classification) is somewhat standard. The paper could have been strengthened by evaluating on a more diverse set of tasks where high-quality document representations are crucial, such as information retrieval or question answering.
*   **Analysis of Baselines:** The performance of some baselines, particularly Paragraph Vectors, seems lower than expected on certain tasks (e.g., word analogy and Wikipedia classification). A brief discussion of the hyperparameter tuning for these baselines would add confidence that the comparisons are fair.
*   **Use of Space for Visualizations:** While the t-SNE plots in Figures 3 and 4 are illustrative, they occupy a significant amount of space. This space could have been used for more quantitative analysis. For instance, an ablation study analyzing the impact of the corruption rate `q` or the local context window size would provide deeper insight into the model's behavior and sensitivity to its hyperparameters.
*   **Length Dependency:** The authors hypothesize that Doc2VecC is better for longer documents while LSTMs excel at shorter ones. This is a plausible and interesting claim. A more direct analysis, perhaps by plotting performance against document length for both Doc2VecC and a recurrent baseline, would provide stronger evidence for this conclusion and help define the ideal use cases for the proposed model.

**5. Questions for Authors**

*   Could you please comment on the sensitivity of the model to the corruption rate `q`? The paper states the model is "not sensitive" and uses `q=0.9` throughout. This is a very high rate of corruption. An ablation showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., from 0.1 to 0.9) would strengthen this claim and provide valuable guidance for practitioners.
*   In the semantic relatedness task (Section 4.5), you fine-tune the model on the SICK dataset without using the labels. Could you clarify what the training objective was during this fine-tuning stage? Was it the same Doc2VecC objective applied to the SICK sentences?
*   The comparison with Skip-thought vectors on the IMDB dataset shows a large performance gap in favor of Doc2VecC. The paper hypothesizes this is due to document length. Is it possible that the gap is also due to the domain mismatch, since the Skip-thought model was pre-trained on books while Doc2VecC was trained on the target corpus?
