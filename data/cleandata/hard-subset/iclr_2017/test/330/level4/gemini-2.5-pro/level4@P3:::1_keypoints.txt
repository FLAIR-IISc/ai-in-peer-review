- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of word embeddings and train with a CBOW-style objective using local and global (corrupted) context.
- Corruption mechanism: global context is averaged over a random subset of words, acting as data-dependent regularization that down-weights frequent, non-discriminative words.
- Theoretical analysis: Section 3.1 shows corruption ≈ adaptive regularizer, providing principled explanation for handling stop words.
- Empirical results: matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on sentiment, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.
- Efficiency: significantly faster training and inference (e.g., IMDB inference 7s vs Paragraph Vectors >4 minutes).
- Word embeddings quality: yields better word vectors than Word2Vec as shown by word analogy tasks.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason — simplicity & efficiency: method is easy to implement and practical due to averaging precomputed embeddings for inference.
- Key reason — strong theoretical motivation: principled justification elevates the approach beyond a heuristic.
- Strength: novel synthesis of averaging and corruption with solid theoretical grounding in Section 3.1.
- Weakness: empirical evaluation is limited in task diversity and baselines may not have been optimally tuned.
- Ablation request: show impact of removing local context and performance vs. corruption rate q.
- Visualization feedback: t-SNE plots use space that could be better spent on quantitative analyses or error analysis.
- Clarification request: confirm whether baselines (e.g., Skip-thought) were fine-tuned on target datasets like SICK for fair comparison.
- Hypothesis test suggested: evaluate Skip-thought on truncated IMDB reviews to test whether long document length explains poor performance.
- Downstream validation request: test whether Doc2VecC pre-trained word embeddings improve performance in separate downstream models (e.g., CNN/LSTM).
