This paper presents a framework for generating document representations. The central idea is to represent a document as a weighted average of its word embeddings, guided by a data-dependent regularization term. This regularization favors informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words to be close to the zero vector. Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks show that the proposed method achieves lower error rates compared to the presented baseline document embedding methods.

**Strengths**

1.  **Simplicity and Efficiency:** The primary strength of the proposed method is its simplicity and computational efficiency. By avoiding complex recurrent architectures, it offers a fast and scalable approach to learning document embeddings.
2.  **Clear Motivation:** The motivation for the work is well-founded. The goal of creating a document vector that systematically down-weights common words in favor of more informative ones is intuitive and addresses a common challenge in text representation.
3.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The method demonstrates strong performance on the two tasks presented, outperforming the selected baselines.

**Weaknesses and Areas for Improvement**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While the combination of techniques is effective, the paper does not offer a significant technical contribution. The core components—averaging word embeddings and applying regularization—are well-established. The main contribution lies in the specific formulation and its successful application, which feels more incremental than foundational.
2.  **Insufficient Empirical Validation:** The paper's main selling point is the simplicity and effectiveness of the proposed method. For such a claim to be convincing, its superiority needs to be demonstrated across a broader range of applications. The evaluation on only two tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification) is not sufficient to generalize the method's utility. I would be more convinced of its value if it were successfully applied to a more diverse set of tasks.
3.  **Need for Stronger Baselines:** The set of baseline models could be strengthened to better contextualize the performance of the proposed method. In particular, a comparison against a standard bidirectional LSTM is missing.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  **RNN-LM Baseline:** Could you please clarify the implementation details of the RNN-LM baseline?
    *   Was the language model trained specifically to minimize classification error for the downstream task, or was it a pre-trained language model whose representations were used as features?
    *   How was the final document representation extracted from the RNN? Did you use the final hidden state, an average of all hidden states, or another pooling strategy?

2.  **Comparison with Bidirectional LSTMs:** One of the most widely used methods for document representation today involves using a bidirectional LSTM and concatenating the final forward and backward hidden states. This approach captures contextual information from the entire sequence and often serves as a very strong baseline. I believe it would be highly informative to compare your proposed method against this approach for tasks like document classification and sentiment analysis. This comparison would provide a more robust assessment of where your method stands relative to powerful, standard sequence-based models.
