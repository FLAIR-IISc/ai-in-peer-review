### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and effective framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The model's key contribution is a corruption mechanism—functionally a dropout on the input bag-of-words representation—which is applied during a joint training process inspired by Word2Vec's CBOW architecture. The authors provide a compelling theoretical justification, framing this corruption as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, non-discriminative words while preserving the signal from rarer, more informative ones. The resulting model is shown to be highly efficient at both training and inference time, and achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  **Practicality and Efficiency:** The proposed model is remarkably simple and computationally efficient, particularly at inference time where it only requires an average of word vectors, in stark contrast to the expensive inference step required by models like Paragraph Vectors.
2.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** Despite its simplicity, Doc2VecC achieves impressive results across multiple standard benchmarks, demonstrating that this straightforward approach is not just fast but also highly effective.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant combination of simplicity, efficiency, and performance. While individual components of the model—such as representing documents as a sum of word vectors or applying dropout to inputs—are not entirely novel, the paper's contribution is in their effective synthesis and the insightful analysis that explains *why* this combination works so well.

The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer, is a significant contribution. It provides a principled explanation for the model's success, elevating the approach beyond a mere heuristic application of dropout. This theoretical justification explains how the model learns to suppress the influence of overly frequent words, making the simple averaging of word embeddings a viable and powerful composition function for creating document representations.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The model's performance on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia document classification tasks is particularly strong, outperforming several more complex baselines. The efficiency gains are also clearly demonstrated; the ability to generate embeddings for 25,000 test documents in seconds, compared to minutes for Paragraph Vectors, is a significant practical advantage that makes the model highly appealing for real-world applications. Furthermore, the experiments show that Doc2VecC produces higher-quality word embeddings than the standard Word2Vec, as evidenced by the word analogy task results. This demonstrates that the global document context, modulated by corruption, provides a valuable training signal that improves the underlying word representations themselves.

In summary, while the paper does not propose a fundamentally new architectural paradigm, it presents a well-motivated, well-analyzed, and highly effective refinement of existing ideas. The resulting model is a strong and practical baseline for document representation, and its simplicity, efficiency, and performance make it a valuable contribution to the field.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are offered to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper states a high corruption rate of `q=0.9` was used throughout the experiments. How sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter? A brief ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 0.9) would strengthen the paper by demonstrating the robustness of the approach.

2.  **Baseline Clarification:** The primary baseline for composing word vectors is "Word2Vec + IDF". For a more direct comparison that isolates the benefit of the joint training and corruption, it would be insightful to include a "Word2Vec + Average" baseline where document vectors are formed by a simple, unweighted average of pre-trained Word2Vec embeddings. This would help quantify how much of the performance gain comes specifically from the Doc2VecC training procedure versus the composition method itself.

3.  **Empirical Validation of Regularization:** The theoretical analysis of the data-dependent regularization is excellent. To further validate this, could the authors provide a more dynamic visualization of this effect? For instance, plotting the L2 norms of embeddings for a set of frequent (e.g., 'the', 'a') and rare but informative words over the course of training could provide powerful empirical evidence for the claims made in Section 3.1.

*   The formatting of the references could be improved for consistency and readability.
