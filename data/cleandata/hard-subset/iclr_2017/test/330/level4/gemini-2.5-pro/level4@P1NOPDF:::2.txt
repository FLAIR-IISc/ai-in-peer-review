This paper proposes a simple yet effective method for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a weighted average of its word embeddings, guided by a data-dependent regularization term. This regularization up-weights informative or rare words while pushing the embeddings of common words towards the zero vector. Experiments on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks show that the proposed method achieves lower error rates than the selected baseline methods.

While the motivation to find an efficient and effective way to encode documents is compelling, the paper's technical contribution appears limited. Most of the techniques employed are not new, and the method's primary appeal lies in its simplicity and speed. For this reason, the empirical evaluation needs to be particularly strong to be convincing. The current results, based on only two tasks, are insufficient to demonstrate that this approach is a generally superior way to learn document representations. The paper would be significantly strengthened by demonstrating competitive performance across a wider range of tasks.

A few points regarding the experimental comparison require clarification:
*   For the RNN-LM baseline, was the model trained solely with a language modeling objective, or was it fine-tuned to minimize classification error on the downstream task?
*   Was the document representation from the RNN-LM taken from the final hidden state or from the average of all hidden states?

Furthermore, the evaluation would be more comprehensive if it included a comparison against a stronger, more contemporary baseline. One of the most common methods for document representation today is a bidirectional LSTM where the final hidden states from both directions are concatenated. It would be very informative to see how the proposed method compares to this approach on the reported document classification and sentiment analysis tasks.
