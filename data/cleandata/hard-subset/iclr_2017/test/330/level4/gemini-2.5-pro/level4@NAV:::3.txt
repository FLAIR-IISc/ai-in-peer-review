This paper introduces a method for learning document representations by summing the embeddings of their constituent words. These word vectors are learned jointly and are subjected to a random dropout process, termed 'corruption,' during training. While the individual components of this approach are not especially novel, the resulting algorithm is efficient and achieves commendable empirical performance.

The concept of co-training word and document embeddings is not new, and neither is the core idea of constructing a document vector from the sum of its word vectors (e.g., as seen in Lebret and Collobert's '"The Sum of Its Parts"...'). Furthermore, the proposed 'corruption' technique is functionally equivalent to applying standard dropout at the input layer. Considering these points, alongside the use of a word2vec-inspired loss and training methodology, the paper's novelty is marginal.

On the other hand, the proposed method offers significant practical advantages. It is remarkably efficient during inference, requiring only a simple averaging of word vectors, in contrast to the more demanding inference step of architectures like Doc2Vec. By design, the resulting document embedding captures important global semantics—specifically, the information that proves most useful for local context prediction. For a model of this simplicity, its performance on standard sentiment analysis and document classification benchmarks is highly encouraging.

In summary, while the paper's theoretical novelty is limited, its blend of simplicity, efficiency, and strong performance makes it a valuable contribution. The model is worthy of attention from the broader community and warrants further study. Consequently, I recommend the paper for acceptance.
