This paper presents Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and effective framework for learning document embeddings. The core idea is to represent each document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The model introduces a corruption mechanism—randomly dropping words from the document's representation during training—which serves as a data-dependent regularizer. This approach is claimed to favor informative words while suppressing the influence of common ones, leading to high-quality document representations that match or outperform state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, all while being exceptionally fast to train and apply.

I recommend **Acceptance**. My decision is based on two key factors:
1.  The model demonstrates a compelling combination of simplicity, computational efficiency, and strong empirical performance.
2.  The paper provides a clear and insightful analysis of its core mechanism, justifying why such a simple approach is effective.

The primary strength of this work lies in its elegant simplicity and efficiency. Unlike models such as Paragraph Vectors which require an expensive inference step for unseen documents, Doc2VecC generates document vectors via a simple average of pre-computed word embeddings. This is a significant practical advantage. The experimental results convincingly support the authors' claims, showing that this simple model is highly competitive with, and often superior to, more complex baselines on diverse tasks like sentiment analysis (IMDB dataset) and document classification (Wikipedia dataset).

Furthermore, the theoretical justification in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption process as a form of data-dependent regularization, is a key contribution. This analysis lucidly explains why the model learns to down-weight common, uninformative words and provides a solid foundation for the model's empirical success. While the constituent ideas (jointly training word/document vectors, summing word vectors, and using input dropout) may not be entirely novel in isolation, their synthesis into this well-motivated, high-performing, and efficient framework constitutes a valuable contribution to the field that deserves to be shared with the community.

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help improve the manuscript and are not central to my decision.
*   **Positioning in Literature:** The paper could be strengthened by a more direct comparison to prior work that also represents documents compositionally during training (e.g., "The Sum of Its Parts" by Lebret and Collobert, 2014). Explicitly discussing the similarities and differences would better situate the work and clarify its unique contributions.
*   **Corruption as Dropout:** The "corruption model" is effectively dropout applied to the bag-of-words input vector. While the analysis in Section 3.1 is excellent, explicitly framing it this way and connecting it more closely to the dropout literature might make the method more immediately recognizable to readers.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q` and a value of 0.9 is used. Including a small ablation study or a plot showing performance across a range of `q` values would make this claim more robust and provide valuable practical guidance for others implementing the model.

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
*   Your analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer is very clear. Did you consider if this insight could lead to alternative, more direct regularization schemes that might achieve a similar or even better effect of suppressing uninformative words?
*   You hypothesize that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to long document lengths. Have you performed any analysis to support this? For example, does the performance of Skip-thought vectors improve when evaluated only on the shorter reviews within the dataset?
