Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient document representation method that averages word embeddings learned jointly with a corruption process. The unbiased mask-out corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that downweights common/non-discriminative words and preserves rare/informative ones, enabling average pooling to capture global semantics. The approach is computationally light, decouples model size from corpus size, and yields strong empirical results on sentiment analysis and document classification, with additional analyses on word analogies and SICK.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) Limited technical novelty—the main components (averaging, corruption/dropout, negative sampling) are known, and the regularization link is closely related to prior work on dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders; (2) Experimental validation lacks several widely used and stronger baselines and broader task diversity to convincingly support claims of general superiority.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Clear and well-motivated—making average-of-embeddings work via training-time regularization is appealing, and the efficiency benefits are compelling.
- Placement in literature: The method sits between Word2Vec+pooling and PV/mDAE. However, the conceptual step (unbiased corruption leading to IDF-like regularization) is incremental relative to mDAE/dropout analyses.
- Evidence and rigor: The reported gains on IMDB and Wikipedia are solid and speed results are persuasive. Yet, comparisons miss strong neural baselines standard for document representation, e.g., a bidirectional LSTM (final-state concatenation) and simple CNNs; the RNN-LM baseline is under-specified; and significance testing and ablations (e.g., varying q, removing context, unbiased vs biased corruption) are limited. The SICK results show competitiveness but not superiority; thus broader coverage beyond two main downstream tasks is needed.

Feedback to improve the paper (not all tied to my decision)
- Add baselines: BiLSTM (concat final states), BiLSTM/avg pooling, CNN text classifiers, and a robust Word2Vec/SIF-style weighted averaging.
- Clarify RNN-LM: Was it trained as a language model (perplexity) or with a classification objective? What representation was used—final hidden state, average of hidden states, or something else? Was it bidirectional?
- Provide ablations: Effect of q; remove local context to connect to mDAE; compare unbiased vs standard dropout; sensitivity to window size and negative sampling distribution; correlation between learned word norms and IDF/term informativeness.
- Expand evaluation: Add more tasks (e.g., additional topic/sentiment datasets, multi-label news, cross-domain transfer), and report statistical significance.
- Fairness details: Ensure subsampling and preprocessing are matched across baselines; avoid representation learning on test data (or clearly separate settings).

Questions for the authors
- RNN-LM details as above (training objective; representation; uni vs bi; pooling).
- Did you try a BiLSTM with concatenated final states and how does Doc2VecC compare?
- How sensitive are results to q and to removing the unbiased scaling?
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used, and are results robust to these choices?
- Do learned word norms correlate with IDF or mutual information as hypothesized?
