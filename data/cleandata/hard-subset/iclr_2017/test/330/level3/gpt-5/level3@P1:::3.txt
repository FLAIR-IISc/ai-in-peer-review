This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation framework that learns to represent a document as the average of its constituent word embeddings while training with a corruption mechanism that randomly drops words. The global (document-level) vector, formed as an average of the surviving word embeddings, is used together with the local (context window) embeddings to predict target words in a Word2Vec-style negative sampling objective. The corruption is shown to induce a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of frequent/non-informative words and preserves rare/informative ones. The model is simple, scalable, and delivers strong empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness, and word analogy benchmarks, while enabling very fast test-time inference via averaging.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical performance with compelling efficiency and simplicity, including markedly faster test-time inference relative to Paragraph Vectors; (2) Clear, well-motivated formulation that ties document-level semantics to word prediction and provides an intuitive regularization effect.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper aims to learn document embeddings that are both semantically meaningful and efficient to compute, addressing limitations of BoW (sparsity, lack of semantics) and Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth, costly inference). The choice to enforce compositionality via averaging during training is well-motivated by observed linear regularities in word embeddings and prior work that uses averaged embeddings for sentence/document representations.
- Placement in the literature: The approach sits naturally between Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The global-context-as-average idea connects to Huang et al. (2012), and the corruption mechanism relates to dropout/denoising autoencoders. The paper is honest about these connections. The novelty is incremental: the specific integration of (i) on-the-fly corrupted document averages as global context in the CBOW objective and (ii) a second-order analysis showing a data-dependent regularizer that favors informative words.
- Methodology: The model is straightforward, implemented with negative sampling; complexity depends only on vocabulary size. The derivation using a Taylor expansion around the unbiased corruption mean is clear and leads to an interpretable regularization term. The training/inference pipeline is simple and scalable.
- Empirical support: Results are robust across tasks. On IMDB and a 100-class Wikipedia classification benchmark, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms strong baselines (BoW, denoising autoencoders, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors), while offering large gains in test-time speed versus Paragraph Vectors. Word analogy results show improved word embeddings over Word2Vec, consistent with the regularization’s effect on diminishing stopwords. On SICK, Doc2VecC is competitive with LSTM-based methods and skip-thought, despite being far simpler and faster. The efficiency claims are backed with concrete timing comparisons.
- Rigor and correctness: The training objective and corruption scheme are standard and sound; the regularization derivation is appropriate and offers insight into why the model works. The experiments are comprehensive, include public code, and consider both transductive and non-transductive settings on IMDB.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not prerequisites for acceptance)
- Novelty framing: Be crisper in articulating the contribution beyond “average + dropout.” Emphasize the coupling between global corrupted averages and local CBOW during training and the induced regularization; contrast more explicitly with Huang et al. (2012) and Paragraph Vectors (both DM and DBOW).
- Ablations: Add ablations to isolate the impact of (i) corruption vs no corruption, (ii) local-only vs global-only context, (iii) different corruption rates q, and (iv) weighting schemes (e.g., simple IDF at test-time) to solidify the case for training-time corruption over post-hoc weighting.
- Negative sampling distribution: You use a uniform Pv; standard Word2Vec often uses unigram^0.75. Please report sensitivity to this choice and confirm that comparisons to Word2Vec use matched settings.
- Baselines: Consider including fastText-style linear classifiers with subword/averaged embeddings as a competitive baseline for text classification, and PV-DBOW if not already included in all tables.
- Fairness and transparency: Ensure that all models (Doc2VecC, Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors) are compared under matched embedding sizes, training data, epochs, window sizes, subsampling thresholds, and negative samples. For SICK and book-corpus pretraining, detail how vocab expansion and fine-tuning are performed for all methods.
- Clarify representation details: There is a minor notational mismatch between binary BoW x (xj ∈ {0,1}) in equations and the average over tokens in Eq. (5). Clarify whether document averages use counts (with multiplicities) or unique words during training and at test time, and how this interacts with the 1/T scaling.
- Error analysis: The “small-norm words” analysis is compelling. Consider adding quantitative analyses showing gains attributable to downweighting frequent non-informative tokens (e.g., performance change when removing stopword suppression).

Questions for the authors
- Corruption details: How sensitive are results to the corruption rate q and to document length T? Did you try frequency-dependent corruption (e.g., higher drop rates for frequent words) and how did that compare?
- Negative sampling: What distribution and number of negative samples were used across experiments? Did you compare uniform vs unigram^0.75? If so, how did it affect performance and the regularization behavior?
- Fairness of Word2Vec comparisons: Were Word2Vec baselines trained with identical corpora, epochs, subsampling thresholds, window sizes, and embedding dimensions? Can you provide a table of matched hyperparameters?
- Paragraph Vectors variants: Did you include PV-DBOW in all tasks? If not, can you report those numbers and the inference-time costs for completeness?
- Representation construction: During training, is the global vector computed over unique words (binary x) or all tokens (counts)? At test time, do you always average over tokens with multiplicity? Please clarify and, if they differ, justify the choice.
- Throughput claims: “Billions of words per hour on a single machine” – please specify hardware, implementation details (e.g., multithreading), and batch/optimization settings.
- Stability and variance: What is the variance across random seeds for key benchmarks? Any notable failure modes (e.g., very short or extremely long documents)?
- SICK fine-tuning: You mention fine-tuning on SICK without labels. What exactly is optimized (language-modeling objective on sentence pairs?). How much does fine-tuning contribute?

Overall, despite limited methodological novelty, the paper offers a well-motivated, elegant formulation that achieves strong results with excellent efficiency and clarity. The contribution is practical and of interest to the community.
