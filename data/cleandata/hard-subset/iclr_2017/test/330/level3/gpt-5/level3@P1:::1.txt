Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a very simple yet effective document representation method that predicts a target word using both the local context and a global document vector formed by averaging corrupted word embeddings. The corruption is an unbiased word dropout that, via a second-order approximation, induces a data-dependent regularizer: frequent and non-informative words are driven toward zero, while informative words are preserved.
- The approach keeps the model complexity independent of corpus size (like Word2Vec), enables extremely fast training with negative sampling, and yields test-time document vectors by a single average over learned embeddings.
- Section 3 gives a clean analysis showing how corruption yields an explicit regularizer and why averaging works at test time, providing rare theoretical clarity for an otherwise pragmatic architecture.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC is competitive or superior to established baselines on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia 100-way), and word analogies, while remaining close to stronger sequence models on SICK. The method is remarkably efficient in both training and inference.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Elegant, well-motivated combination of known ingredients with a principled analysis that explains why it works and how it differs from post-hoc averaging. The simplicity and efficiency make it likely to be widely adopted.
- Despite relatively standard datasets, the empirical results are strong and consistent, with notable advantages in speed and test-time simplicity over Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought encoders.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets fast, corpus-size-independent document representations that preserve semantics and are trivial to compute at test time, improving upon Word2Vec post-hoc averaging and Paragraph Vectors’ heavy inference. The literature placement is sound and acknowledges related models (Word2Vec, PV-DBOW/DM, SDA/DAE, skip-thought, LSTM/CNN).
- Method: The CBoW-style objective with an auxiliary global document vector defined by a corrupted average is simple and scalable. The corruption is unbiased, so the test-time uncorrupted average matches the training-time expectation, avoiding train–test mismatch.
- Theory: The Taylor expansion with unbiased dropout gives a transparent view into the induced data-dependent regularization. The analysis neatly explains empirical behaviors (e.g., downweighting stopwords without ad hoc IDF).
- Empirics: On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms PV and substantially improves over BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, and SDA in accuracy while being vastly faster than PV at test time. On Wikipedia 100-way classification, Doc2VecC consistently beats alternatives across embedding sizes. Word analogy results suggest notably better word embeddings than Word2Vec under comparable training. On SICK, Doc2VecC is competitive with more complex sequence models while being orders of magnitude faster to train. These collectively support the paper’s core claims of efficacy and efficiency.
- Practicality: Training/inference speed, small model footprint, and a trivial test-time encoder are compelling advantages for practitioners; the open-source implementation increases impact.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Strengthen baselines and fairness:
  - Include stronger “bag-of-ngrams” baselines (e.g., fastText-style supervised and unsupervised variants), tuned PV-DBOW and PV-DM, and a carefully tuned TF-IDF+SVM with standard preprocessing.
  - Clarify negative sampling settings and use of subsampling; Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75 rather than uniform negatives—matching this might change relative performance.
- Provide ablations:
  - Remove the corruption (q=0), vary q, and compare to standard dropout on U and V; ablate the local vs global context contributions.
  - Compare uniform vs frequency-based negative sampling; test sensitivity to context window size and number of negatives.
  - Replace learned averaging with fixed IDF at test time to isolate the benefit of data-dependent regularization.
- Add more rigorous analyses:
  - Reduce reliance on t-SNE plots; include quantitative clustering metrics or retrieval evaluations.
  - Report variance over multiple runs and show learning curves vs throughput.
  - Analyze performance vs document length to substantiate claims about long vs short sequences.
- Broaden evaluation:
  - Include additional downstream tasks (e.g., topic classification on other corpora, retrieval, transfer to out-of-domain sentiment).
  - Examine multilingual or domain-shift settings to test robustness.
- Clarify details:
  - Precisely specify hardware, throughput, and hyperparameters for all methods to ensure fair timing comparisons.
  - Discuss memory footprint and scaling with vocabulary size.

Questions for the authors
1. Negative sampling and subsampling: What distribution and number of negatives were used (uniform vs unigram^0.75)? How sensitive are results to these choices? Are Word2Vec baselines matched on these settings?
2. Corruption rate: How does performance vary with q beyond the single value (0.9)? Is there a trade-off between speed and accuracy, and an optimal range across datasets?
3. Ablation of components: What is the performance when (a) removing the corruption, (b) removing local context (global-only), and (c) using only local context (global-only ablated)? This would disentangle the contributions.
4. Regularization effect: Can you quantify the induced downweighting beyond the qualitative word-norm table, e.g., correlation between embedding norms and IDF, and the impact on downstream performance when masking highest-norm vs lowest-norm words?
5. Fair baselines: How were Paragraph Vectors tuned (DM vs DBOW, inference steps at test time)? Could stronger PV baselines (with subword features or better tuning) close the gap?
6. Length sensitivity: Do you observe a clear relationship between document length and gains over Word2Vec+IDF or PV? Any degradation for very short inputs?
7. Generalization: How does Doc2VecC perform under domain shift (e.g., train on books/news, test on reviews) compared to Word2Vec+IDF?
8. Implementation details: What optimizer and learning rate schedule were used? Any tricks (e.g., adaptive optimizers, gradient clipping) that affect speed or stability?

Overall, this is a concise, well-argued paper with a simple idea that is both effective and easy to adopt. With stronger baselines and a few ablations, it would be even more compelling.
