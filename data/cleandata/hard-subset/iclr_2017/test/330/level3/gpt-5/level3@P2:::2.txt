1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for document representation that encodes each document as the average of its word embeddings. During training, it augments a standard CBoW objective with a “global” document vector formed from a randomly corrupted bag-of-words, which both accelerates training and induces a data-dependent regularizer.
- A second-order Taylor expansion analysis shows that unbiased corruption yields a regularization term that suppresses embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while keeping informative/rare words salient—thereby making the simple average a strong document representation at test time.
- The method decouples model size from corpus size (no per-document parameters), trains very quickly, and produces test-time embeddings by a single average. Empirically, the paper reports competitive or better performance than strong baselines on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia-100), and semantic relatedness (SICK), and also shows improved word-analogy performance over Word2Vec embeddings. Code is provided.

2. Decision: Reject (borderline)
Primary reasons:
- Limited technical novelty: the core components (averaging word embeddings, dropout/corruption, negative sampling, CBoW-style objective) are well known; the proposed combination and analysis feel incremental.
- Empirical evaluation, while promising, lacks several strong and widely used neural baselines and ablations that are needed to convincingly establish superiority across tasks and settings.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Learning document representations that are efficient to train/infer, corpus-size agnostic, and robust to frequent non-informative words.

Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature?
- Motivation is clear: averaged embeddings are fast and surprisingly strong; prior results show compositionality from linear operations. The corruption-as-regularization connection is plausible and ties to dropout and denoising autoencoders.
- Related work coverage is reasonable (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vector, denoising autoencoders, Skip-thought). However, comparison/discussion is incomplete with respect to contemporaneous simple/efficient baselines such as fastText (bag-of-ngrams + averaging) and standard BiLSTM encoders, which are highly relevant to the stated goals of simplicity and performance.

Do the results support the claims?
- Positives: Strong results on IMDB and Wikipedia classification with compelling efficiency; sensible qualitative analysis showing norms of stop-word embeddings are damped; improvements on word analogy tasks suggest cleaner word embeddings under the proposed training.
- Concerns about rigor/fairness:
  - Word2Vec baselines: It is unclear if strongest settings were used (e.g., skip-gram vs CBoW, negative sampling distribution ∝ unigram^0.75, number of negatives, dynamic context windows). The paper states a uniform negative sampling distribution Pv, which is not the common best practice for Word2Vec and could bias the comparison.
  - Paragraph Vector inference is known to be slow; but performance sensitivity to PV hyperparameters (window size, DBOW vs DM, concatenation, hierarchical softmax vs negative sampling) is not fully explored.
  - LSTM baselines: only an RNN-LM variant is included, without enough detail to assess strength; widely adopted BiLSTM document encoders are missing.
  - Generality: Only one sentiment dataset, one document classification dataset, and one relatedness dataset are shown; broader coverage and variance across domains/datasets would strengthen the claims of general superiority.
- Theory is an approximation (second-order expansion around the mean); while standard in dropout analyses, there is no quantitative check of approximation quality, nor bounds; ablations varying corruption rate q and directly measuring expected objective vs approximation would add credibility.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decisive)
- Baselines:
  - Add a strong BiLSTM document encoder baseline (e.g., concatenated final states or pooled hidden states), and a simple CNN text classifier baseline.
  - Include fastText-style bag-of-ngrams baselines, which are extremely strong, simple, and directly comparable in efficiency.
  - For Word2Vec, report both CBoW and skip-gram with recommended settings (unigram^0.75 negatives, subsampling, dynamic windows), and control for embedding dimension and training data.
- Ablations and analysis:
  - Vary the corruption rate q and report performance, training speed, and the distribution of embedding norms to support the regularization story. Include the extremes q=0 (no corruption) and high q.
  - Remove local context (global-only) and remove global context (local-only) to quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Compare average vs IDF-weighted average at test time to show the necessity of the learned regularization.
  - Report results across multiple datasets per task (e.g., Yelp, AG News/DBPedia, 20 Newsgroups; STS benchmarks beyond SICK), and include statistical significance.
- Fairness and details:
  - Clarify negative sampling distributions and number of negatives across methods; match Word2Vec’s best practices.
  - For word analogy, include Word2Vec skip-gram as a baseline; state training corpora, preprocessing, and subword handling, since morphology-heavy syntactic analogies can benefit from subword models.
  - Provide precise throughput numbers (“billions of words per hour”) with hardware specs and batch settings; report wall-clock times per epoch and total.
- Theory:
  - Discuss assumptions behind the expansion (independence, diagonal Hessian use), quantify approximation error empirically (Monte Carlo expectation vs second-order approximation), and clarify how this guides hyperparameter choices.
- Clarity:
  - Clean up typos and references; ensure all datasets, hyperparameters, and preprocessing steps (tokenization, vocabulary thresholds, case, OOV handling) are fully specified.

5. Questions for the authors
- RNN-LM baseline: Was the model trained purely as a language model or supervised for classification? What representation was used (final hidden state, average/max pooling, or something else)? Was it unidirectional or bidirectional, and what were the hyperparameters?
- Negative sampling: What distribution and number of negatives were used for Doc2VecC, Word2Vec, and Paragraph Vector? If uniform Pv was used for Doc2VecC, did you also test unigram^0.75? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- Ablations on corruption: How does performance and embedding-norm suppression vary with q? Please report curves and training speedups. What happens at q=0 and very high q?
- Local vs global context: What is the impact of removing local context (Doc2VecC → DEA-like) or removing global context (Doc2VecC → Word2Vec-like)? Please quantify on IMDB and Wikipedia.
- BiLSTM baseline: Can you add a standard BiLSTM document encoder (concatenated final states or pooled states) as a baseline, and report its speed/accuracy tradeoffs?
- Dataset breadth: Do results hold on additional sentiment and classification datasets (e.g., Yelp, Amazon, AG News/DBPedia, 20NG) and on standard STS benchmarks beyond SICK?
- Analogy fairness: Did you compare to Word2Vec skip-gram with recommended settings, and to subword models? Please confirm identical corpora, preprocessing, dimensions, and training budgets.
- Efficiency claims: Please provide exact throughput, epochs, and wall-clock time for all methods with hardware details, and include the time to produce test-time vectors for PV vs Doc2VecC.
