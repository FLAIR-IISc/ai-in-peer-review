- Introduces Doc2VecC: efficient document vectors formed by averaging word embeddings trained with a Word2Vec-style predictor that includes a global (corrupted) document context.
- Key idea: unbiased dropout on document words during training induces a data-dependent regularizer that down-weights frequent non-informative words while preserving rare informative ones.
- Section 3 derives the regularizer via Taylor expansion and links the corruption mechanism to adaptive dropout, explaining when averaging works at test time.
- Empirically Doc2VecC is simple, fast, and competitive on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-way classification, SICK relatedness, and improves word analogy performance.
- Training and inference are very efficient compared to Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought encoders; test-time document vectors require only averaging.
- Decision: Accept — method is well-composed, efficient, analytically justified, and novel in its combination of components.
- Clarity/motivation: Well-motivated by linear compositionality and inference speed needs; architecture separates local syntax (context) and global semantics (corrupted average).
- Novelty: While components are known, their integration (global context + unbiased corruption + derived regularizer) is novel and valuable.
- Usability: Likely useful in practice for large corpora and latency-constrained applications due to fast training/inference and simple test-time averaging.
- Empirical evidence: Strong results on IMDB/Wikipedia and improved word embeddings; SICK results reasonable given sentence-level focus.
- Baselines: Several important, strong baselines are missing or under-optimized (NB-SVM, fastText supervised, tuned n-gram TF-IDF, tuned BoW).
- Averaging baselines: More sophisticated frequency-smoothing schemes (e.g., SIF-like weighting) should be compared to plain IDF.
- Evaluation scope: Document-level evaluation is limited (IMDB + one Wikipedia task); broader datasets (AG News, DBPedia, Yelp, Amazon, Reuters, 20NG) and tasks (retrieval, clustering, transfer) are needed.
- t-SNE use: Over-reliance on 2D projections; replace or compress with quantitative analyses (kNN purity, ARI/NMI, retrieval metrics).
- Ablations: More ablations needed (remove global context, remove corruption, vary q, document-length normalization, negative-sampling distribution, window size).
- Training fairness: Some baselines may be disadvantaged by poor tuning or mismatched objectives; choice of uniform negative sampling vs. unigram^3/4 needs justification and sensitivity analysis.
- Question on skip-gram: Did the authors try a skip-gram variant and how did it compare in accuracy and speed?
- Question on corruption rate q: How sensitive are results to q across tasks and document lengths; is there a dataset-dependent sweet spot?
- Question on length normalization: Why use 1/T for global context and final vector; did sqrt(T) or other normalizations help?
- Question on negative sampling: Why uniform negatives instead of unigram^3/4; does theory rely on uniformity and what's the empirical trade-off?
- Baseline tuning question: How were Doc2Vec, Word2Vec+IDF, DEA, and RNNLM hyperparameters tuned; can NB-SVM and fastText supervised and wall-clock comparisons be added?
- Generality question: Any evaluations on more large-scale or multilingual datasets, and observations about domain shift?
- Practicality question: For very large vocabularies, what are memory/runtime constraints and recommendations (subword modeling, vocabulary pruning)?
- Actionable improvements: add stronger baselines, provide extensive ablations, expand datasets/tasks, replace t-SNE with quantitative metrics, and clarify training choices (negatives, subsampling).
- Overall: A neat, efficient method with clear analysis that would benefit from stronger baselines and broader experiments to validate generality.
