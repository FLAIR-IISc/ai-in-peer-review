Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces Doc2VecC, an efficient document representation method that learns word and document vectors with a word-prediction objective in the Word2Vec family (CBoW/skip-gram style with negative sampling). Each document vector is the simple average of its word embeddings, enforced during training by including a global context term in the predictor.
- A key idea is to corrupt the document with unbiased dropout (mask-out) during training. The resulting Taylor-expansion analysis shows this corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while preserving rare/informative ones—making simple averaging at test time effective.
- Section 3 provides a careful derivation of the regularizer and connects it to adaptive dropout, clarifying when/why the method works.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC is simple, fast, and competitive: it achieves strong results on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-way classification, and competitive performance on SICK relatedness, and yields superior word embeddings on word analogies. Training and inference are very efficient, especially compared to Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought encoders.

Decision
Accept. The method is well-composed and efficient, the analysis is insightful, and despite components being individually known (CBoW/negative sampling, document averaging, dropout), the combination and its principled regularizer are novel and practically useful.

Supporting arguments
- Clarity and motivation: The paper is well-motivated by linear compositionality of word vectors and the practical need for fast document inference. The architecture is minimal yet targeted: local context for syntax, global (corrupted) average for semantics. Section 3’s analysis convincingly supports the claim that the corruption acts as a frequency-aware, data-dependent regularizer that improves both word and document representations.
- Novelty in combination: While document averaging, negative sampling, and dropout are not new, the particular way a global context vector is integrated into a skip-gram/CBoW-style predictor, combined with unbiased corruption and the derived regularizer, appears novel and valuable.
- Efficiency and usability: Training/inference times are excellent; test-time representations require only averaging learned word embeddings. This will likely be useful to practitioners with large corpora and latency constraints.
- Empirical evidence: Results on IMDB and Wikipedia are competitive or better than Doc2Vec and other unsupervised baselines, and word analogy gains suggest the regularizer improves word embeddings themselves. The SICK results are reasonable given the focus on longer documents.

Areas for improvement (did not drive the overall decision)
- Breadth/strength of baselines: Several baselines are “soft.” Strong and inexpensive baselines known to perform well on text classification are missing or under-optimized, e.g., NB-SVM (Wang & Manning, 2012), fastText supervised (Joulin et al., 2016), high-order n-gram TF-IDF, and tuned BoW with feature hashing. For sentence/document averaging, frequency-smoothing schemes beyond plain IDF (e.g., SIF-like weighting) would be informative. This aligns with other reviewers’ concerns.
- Scope of evaluation: The main document-level evidence centers on sentiment analysis and a single 100-way Wikipedia classification. Broader coverage (e.g., AG News, DBPedia, Yelp, Amazon, Reuters, 20NG), retrieval/semantic search, clustering purity, and cross-domain transfer would better validate generality. The SICK result helps but emphasizes sentence-level rather than document-level strengths.
- Overuse of t-SNE: Too much space is devoted to 2D projections. These are illustrative but not a substitute for deeper empirical ablations (e.g., sensitivity to q, window size, negative sampling distribution, document length normalization).
- Fairness of training setup: Some baselines are disadvantaged by not being tuned or by mismatched objectives/domains (e.g., skip-thought). The uniform negative-sampling choice is unusual relative to unigram^3/4; its effect deserves discussion.

Three key questions
1) Problem: Learn fast, high-quality document representations that generalize well and can be computed by simple averaging at test time.
2) Motivation/placement: Yes. It is well-positioned in the Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors literature and addresses PV’s scaling/inference drawbacks with a principled global context and corruption mechanism.
3) Do claims hold? The theoretical analysis is sound and insightful; empirical support is positive but could be broader/stronger with tougher baselines and more diverse tasks.

Actionable feedback to improve the paper
- Add stronger baselines: NB-SVM, fastText supervised, tuned n-gram TF-IDF, and stronger averaging schemes for sentences/documents. Ensure all baselines are carefully tuned on validation sets.
- Provide ablations: Remove global context; remove corruption; vary corruption rate q; vary document-length normalization (1/T vs sqrt(T)); vary negative-sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^3/4); vary context window size. Report effects on both word analogies and downstream tasks.
- Expand tasks/datasets: Include additional document classification benchmarks, document retrieval/duplication detection, clustering purity/NMI, and cross-domain sentiment or topic transfer.
- Replace or compress t-SNE figures with quantitative analyses (kNN purity, ARI/NMI, retrieval metrics).
- Clarify training choices: Why uniform negatives? How sensitive are results to subsampling of frequent words, and how much of Doc2VecC’s gains persist without subsampling?

Questions for the authors
- Objective: You focus on CBoW and state skip-gram is straightforward. Did you try the skip-gram variant, and how did it compare in accuracy and speed?
- Corruption rate: How sensitive are results to q across tasks and document lengths? Is there a sweet spot tied to vocabulary size or average document length?
- Length normalization: Why 1/T in the global context and in the final document vector? Did sqrt(T) or other normalizations help for variable-length documents?
- Negative sampling: Why choose a uniform negative distribution rather than unigram^3/4? Does the theoretical analysis rely on uniformity, and what is the empirical trade-off?
- Baseline tuning: How were hyperparameters tuned for Doc2Vec, Word2Vec+IDF, DEA, and RNNLM? Could you include NB-SVM and fastText supervised baselines and report wall-clock comparisons?
- Generality: Have you evaluated Doc2VecC on additional large-scale classification or retrieval tasks (e.g., AG News, DBPedia, Yelp/Amazon, 20NG), or on multilingual corpora? Any observations about domain shift?
- Practicality: For very large vocabularies, what are the memory/runtime constraints, and do you recommend subword modeling or vocabulary pruning with Doc2VecC?

Overall, this is a neat, efficient method with a clear and helpful analysis. With stronger baselines and broader experiments, it can make a solid contribution.
