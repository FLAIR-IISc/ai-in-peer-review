- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings trained with a CBoW-like objective augmented by a randomly corrupted global document vector for faster training and data-dependent regularization.
- Theoretical claim: a second-order Taylor expansion shows corruption induces regularization that downweights frequent non-informative words and preserves rare informative words.
- Practical benefits claimed: no per-document parameters, fast training, single-average test-time inference, competitive results on IMDB, Wikipedia-100, SICK, and improved word-analogy performance; code released.
- Decision: Reject (borderline) due to limited novelty and insufficient empirical comparisons/ablations.
- Main critique: Limited technical novelty—combines well-known components (averaging, corruption/dropout, negative sampling, CBoW) in an incremental way.
- Main critique: Empirical evaluation omits several strong, widely used baselines and necessary ablations to convincingly demonstrate superiority.
- Problem addressed: efficient, corpus-size-agnostic document representations robust to frequent non-informative words.
- Motivation and literature placement: clear and plausible, but comparisons miss relevant contemporaneous baselines like fastText and BiLSTM encoders.
- Positive empirical notes: strong IMDB and Wikipedia results, sensible qualitative analysis of embedding norms, and improved analogy scores indicating cleaner word embeddings.
- Fairness concerns: Word2Vec baselines may not use strongest settings (skip-gram, unigram^0.75 negatives, dynamic windows), potentially biasing comparisons.
- Fairness concerns: Paragraph Vector hyperparameter sensitivity and slow inference not thoroughly explored or controlled.
- Missing baselines: BiLSTM encoders, CNN classifiers, and fastText-style bag-of-ngrams are not included but are highly relevant.
- Limited dataset breadth: only one dataset per task; broader evaluation (Yelp, AG News, DBPedia, 20NG, more STS) is needed to claim generality.
- Theory limitations: second-order expansion is standard but lacks quantitative checks, error bounds, or empirical validation of the approximation.
- Ablation gaps: need experiments varying corruption rate q (including q=0 and high q), removing/keeping local/global contexts, and comparing average vs IDF-weighted test-time averaging.
- Experimental detail gaps: clarify negative sampling distributions, number of negatives, training corpora, preprocessing, subword handling, and hyperparameter choices.
- Efficiency claims need precise reporting: wall-clock times, throughput with hardware specs, epochs, and test-time encoding times (PV vs Doc2VecC).
- Suggested analyses: report performance vs q, distribution of embedding norms, Monte Carlo vs second-order approximation checks, and statistical significance across runs.
- Clarity and reproducibility: fix typos, fully specify datasets, tokenization, vocabulary thresholds, casing, and OOV handling.
- Questions for authors: clarify RNN-LM baseline setup (supervised vs LM, pooling, uni-/bi-directional, hyperparameters).
- Questions for authors: specify negative sampling distribution and sensitivity (uniform vs unigram^0.75) and number of negatives used across methods.
- Questions for authors: provide ablation results for varying q, and quantify training speedups and embedding-norm suppression.
- Questions for authors: quantify impact of removing local or global context components on IMDB and Wikipedia.
- Questions for authors: add a BiLSTM baseline and report its speed/accuracy tradeoff; extend experiments to more datasets and fair word-analogy comparisons.
