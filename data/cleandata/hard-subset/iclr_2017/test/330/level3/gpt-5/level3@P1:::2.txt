Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for unsupervised document representation that represents a document as the average of its word embeddings. During training, a corruption mechanism randomly masks words in the document and uses the corrupted average as a “global context” alongside a local CBOW context to predict target words via negative sampling. A second-order Taylor analysis shows the corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words toward zero while preserving rare/informative words. The approach decouples model size from corpus size (depends only on vocabulary), enables very fast training and test-time encoding (simple averaging), and claims improved word embeddings and document vectors. Experiments on IMDB sentiment analysis, a 100-class Wikipedia document classification task, SICK semantic relatedness, and word analogy benchmarks show strong performance and significant speedups over Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+averaging/IDF, denoising autoencoders, and Skip-Thought (on long documents). The method is robust to the corruption rate and provides intuitive qualitative effects (stopword embeddings shrink).

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) The technical novelty is limited and largely an incremental combination of known components (CBOW with an averaged document vector, dropout-style corruption, and a known adaptive regularization effect); (2) Empirical validation, while encouraging on a few benchmarks, does not yet convincingly demonstrate general superiority over widely used neural sequence encoders (e.g., competitive bidirectional LSTM/CNN document encoders) or stronger bag-of-ngrams baselines across a broader set of datasets.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The goal—fast, corpus-size-independent document vectors that retain semantics—is clear and compelling. Representing documents by averaging word embeddings is a sensible design aligned with prior observations about compositionality, and adding stochastic corruption is well motivated both computationally and as regularization.
- Placement in the literature: The work is well connected to Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders/marginalized DAE, and dropout-as-adaptive-regularization. However, the key technical components are not novel per se; the main contribution is their particular instantiation and solid engineering. Related supervised baselines such as CNN/LSTM document encoders and simple bag-of-ngrams (e.g., strong fast baselines) are missing from direct comparison, and PV-DBOW (often stronger) is not reported in the main tables.
- Claims and rigor: The Taylor expansion argument is standard and plausible, but important details/assumptions are not fully justified (e.g., ignoring off-diagonal Hessian terms, the choice of uniform negative sampling distribution vs. the common unigram^3/4, and the effect of subsampling frequent words). Empirically, Doc2VecC is strong on IMDB and Wikipedia and produces better word analogies than Word2Vec, but underperforms LSTM/Tree-LSTM on SICK. The claim of across-the-board superiority is therefore context-dependent. Some baselines are disadvantaged (e.g., Skip-Thought on very long documents), and results could be more comprehensive and controlled.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Strengthen baselines: Add a bidirectional LSTM document encoder that concatenates final states; a CNN text classifier (e.g., Zhang & LeCun 2015) with pre-trained embeddings used as fixed encoders; and a strong bag-of-ngrams baseline. Report PV-DBOW and PV-DM with careful tuning.
- Broaden evaluation: Include additional datasets (e.g., 20 Newsgroups, AG News, Yelp/AMAZON reviews, Reuters, DBPedia), possibly a non-English corpus, and tasks beyond binary sentiment and Wikipedia classification (e.g., multi-label tagging). Provide length-sensitivity analysis (short vs. long documents) to substantiate the “long text” advantage claim.
- Ablations and analysis: Provide ablations for (a) no corruption, (b) corruption but no local CBOW context, (c) different corruption rates q, and (d) different negative sampling distributions. Quantify how much the adaptive regularization vs. CBOW context drives the gains. Report statistical significance across multiple runs.
- Fairness and preprocessing: Clarify whether using the test set for unsupervised representation learning affects fairness; if so, report results where only train+unlabeled are used for representation learning and test is strictly held out.
- Implementation details: Report the number of negatives, window sizes, subsampling thresholds, learning rates, and wall-clock throughput under identical hardware for all methods. Include memory footprint and inference latency per document vs. PV.
- OOV/subword handling: Discuss how OOVs are treated and whether subword/compositional character features (as in recent LM work) can be integrated.
- Negative sampling choice: The use of uniform Pv is unusual; a stronger distribution could alter both performance and the derived regularizer. Please explore and report.

Questions for the authors
- RNN-LM baseline: Was it trained as a language model or as a supervised classifier? What representation was used (final hidden state, average of hidden states, or something else)? Was it uni- or bidirectional?
- Negative sampling: What distribution over negatives did you use in all experiments? If uniform, why depart from the common unigram^3/4 heuristic, and how does this choice affect results and the regularizer?
- Subsampling and IDF: You state subsampling frequent words is applied and that Doc2VecC “does not rely on this trick.” Are the reported gains robust when subsampling is disabled across methods? How is IDF computed for the Word2Vec+IDF baseline (on train only or train+test)?
- Use of test data in unsupervised stage: For settings where the representation is learned using all available data including test, how do you ensure no leakage into the supervised evaluation (e.g., hyperparameter tuning)?
- Regularizer behavior: Do frequent but informative tokens (e.g., negations, domain-specific markers) get overshrunk? Can you provide targeted analyses on such terms and on stopwords?
- Sensitivity: Please provide performance vs. corruption rate q and vs. number of negatives, and report variance over multiple random seeds.
- Computational details: What is the exact training throughput (tokens/hour) and memory usage per vocabulary size? How does training time scale with document length and q?
