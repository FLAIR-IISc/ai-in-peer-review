- Proposal: Doc2VecC learns document vectors by averaging word embeddings trained end-to-end with a word2vec-style CBoW objective plus random word “corruption.”
- Claim (a): Joint training makes simple averaging encode global document semantics.
- Claim (b): Mask-out corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks frequent/non-informative word embeddings toward zero, favoring rare discriminative words.
- Claim (c): Method is simple and fast; complexity depends on vocabulary size not corpus size, and test-time embeddings for unseen documents are trivial.
- Claim (d): Empirically matches or outperforms strong baselines on sentiment, document classification, and word analogy, while being substantially faster than heavier models.
- Analysis: Taylor-expansion links corruption to an adaptive regularizer; ablations show frequency-dependent shrinkage; code released.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reasons: strong empirical performance, excellent efficiency, and simplicity with practical test-time embedding computation.
- Novelty assessment: Components are known (CBoW, averaging, dropout), but their combination is well-motivated, analyzed, and effective.
- Problem framing: Addresses need for semantically meaningful, scalable, and fast document embeddings versus costly inference or post-hoc heuristics.
- Placement in literature: Properly situated relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and prior averaging methods; corruption is essentially unbiased dropout/denoising.
- Missing comparisons: Should compare or discuss fastText (bag-of-ngrams) and SIF-weighted averaging baselines.
- Empirical support: Results on IMDB and a 100-class Wikipedia benchmark show Doc2VecC matches/outperforms BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-Thoughts.
- Word embeddings quality: Word analogy results suggest better word vectors than Word2Vec due to joint global-context training.
- Reproducibility caveat: Some experiments trained on all available data including test sets — common for unsupervised pretraining but should be separated from strict out-of-sample settings.
- Suggested clarity: Explicitly state corruption is unbiased dropout and connect to marginalized denoising and dropout-as-regularization literature.
- Ablation requests: Provide quantitative ablations for no corruption vs corruption, varying q, with/without local context, negative sampling variants, and subsampling effects.
- Representation clarity: Clarify whether x is binary BoW or counts, how repeated tokens are handled, and how 1/T normalization combines with unbiased scaling 1/(1−q).
- Fairness in timing: Ensure timing comparisons use matched hardware and fully report hyperparameters (embedding size, window, negatives, learning rate).
- Additional analysis: Strengthen regularization claim with empirical checks (e.g., correlation between word frequency/informativeness and learned L2 norms).
- Scope request: Report sensitivity to document length and provide controlled studies showing performance vs length.
- Presentation fixes: Correct citation/style inconsistencies, typos, and improve figure readability.
- Question 1: Is dropout applied at token level or type level, and how does unbiased scaling combine with 1/T — does T count pre- or post-dropout tokens?
- Question 2: Are repeated words counted multiple times in the averaged document vector during training and testing, and if not, why not use counts?
- Question 3: What is performance at q=0 and how do accuracy and training speed vary with corruption rate q — provide a curve.
- Question 4: How much does local context Uc contribute vs the global document vector; what happens when removing local or global context?
- Question 5: What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Question 6: Can you quantify performance with/without subsampling across baselines to support the claim Doc2VecC is less reliant on subsampling?
- Question 7: Can you add comparisons to fastText bag-of-ngrams and SIF-weighted averaging or discuss expected differences?
- Question 8: Does the 1/T scaling reduce document-vector contribution for very long documents, and is there empirical evidence on length effects?
- Question 9: In word analogy comparisons, were training data, embedding size, and runtime controlled between Word2Vec and Doc2VecC?
- Question 10: How are OOVs handled at test time (e.g., in SICK experiments) beyond vocabulary expansion, and what is the impact on results?
