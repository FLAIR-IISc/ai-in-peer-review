- Proposal: Doc2VecC — learn document embeddings by averaging jointly trained word embeddings with a random word-dropout (“corruption”) that acts as a data-dependent regularizer suppressing common words.
- Practical benefits: decouples model size from number of documents, trains at word2vec speeds, and enables extremely fast test-time encoding via simple averaging.
- Empirical performance: strong results on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, SICK semantic relatedness, and good word embeddings (analogy tasks).
- Decision: Accept — main reasons are strong empirical performance with substantial efficiency gains (especially test-time) and clear, easy-to-adopt method despite limited novelty.
- Problem addressed: scalable document embeddings that capture global semantics while allowing cheap inference for unseen documents.
- Motivation and placement: well-motivated by compositional word embeddings and positioned appropriately relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, DAEs, and sentence encoders.
- Theoretical support: Taylor expansion analysis plausibly explains corruption as a regularizer; qualitative analyses (small-norm words) align with theory.
- Method novelty: components (sum-of-words, joint training, dropout corruption, negative sampling) are not new individually, but their combination is executed well and yields consistent gains.
- Baselines and comparisons: experiments compare to strong baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, PV, SDAE, Skip-thoughts) in an appropriate and mostly fair way.
- Clarify novelty: explicitly frame corruption as standard input-layer dropout and present the contribution as an effective synthesis delivering practical benefits.
- Ablation requests: evaluate no corruption; vary corruption probability q; train Word2Vec with identical corruption plus averaging; test weighting schemes (IDF/SIF) at inference.
- Reproducibility requests: report statistical significance and variance over multiple runs, and detail hyperparameter tuning budgets and compute resources.
- Implementation detail request: expand on negative sampling specifics (distribution, k) and how subsampling interacts with corruption.
- Limitations to discuss: behavior on very short vs long texts, OOV handling, and multilingual/cross-domain applicability.
- Open questions: sensitivity of gains to q and document length; whether corruption primarily regularizes embedding matrix U or effectively reweights words in document vectors; comparison to a jointly trained SIF/IDF baseline; robustness across random seeds and other domains.
