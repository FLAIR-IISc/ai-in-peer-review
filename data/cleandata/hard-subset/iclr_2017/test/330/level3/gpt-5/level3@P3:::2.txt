Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework that learns document representations as the average of word embeddings. During training, a corruption model masks words in the document-level bag-of-words context, leading—via a Taylor expansion analysis—to a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words and preserves/inflates informative or rare ones. This encourages average pooling to be semantically meaningful at test time without heuristic weighting.
- Empirically, Doc2VecC reports the lowest error rates among compared embedding-based baselines on IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia document classification, while being extremely fast to train and to infer representations. It is competitive on SICK semantic relatedness and produces markedly better word embeddings than Word2Vec on word analogy tasks.
- The method’s main strengths are its simplicity, scalability (parameters depend on vocabulary, not corpus size), and speed at both train and test time.

Decision: Reject (novelty and evaluation breadth)
- While well-motivated and practically compelling, the technical contribution is limited: the core ideas (averaging embeddings, corruption/dropout-style regularization, negative sampling CBoW) are not novel, and the regularization analysis largely adapts prior marginalized/corruption analyses to this setting. The empirical evaluation, though positive on two core tasks, lacks several strong and widely used baselines and broader task coverage needed to support claims of general superiority.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning efficient, general-purpose document representations that capture semantics, are fast to learn/infer, and decouple model size from corpus size.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The work connects Word2Vec compositionality, Paragraph Vector limitations (per-document parameters, expensive inference), and denoising/dropout-style regularization (mDA, dropout analyses). The motivation for enforcing average pooling during training and using corruption to downweight common words is clear.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Strengths: On IMDB and Wikipedia, Doc2VecC achieves the best reported errors among the compared unsupervised embedding baselines and is orders faster than PV/Skip-Thought at test time. The analysis shows the induced data-dependent regularizer, and qualitative evidence indicates common words get near-zero vectors.
  - Weaknesses: Missing or unclear baselines reduce confidence in general superiority. In particular, no comparison to a strong, widely used supervised or self-supervised sequence baseline such as a BiLSTM that concatenates final states (or pooled states), which is a standard document encoder. The RNN-LM baseline is underspecified (objective and representation unclear), making its results hard to interpret. FastText-like baselines (bag-of-ngrams with learned weights) would also be highly relevant in this space. Finally, results on SICK trail LSTM-based encoders, suggesting the method’s advantages may be task/length dependent; broader evaluation would clarify scope.
  - Rigor: The regularization derivation follows standard second-order expansions under unbiased corruption with independent dimensions; this is sensible but not particularly novel. Key choices (uniform negative sampling vs. the common unigram^3/4, corruption rate q) are not systematically ablated, and the quantitative effect of the regularizer on performance (beyond a qualitative word-norm table) is limited.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all points affect my decision)
- Baselines and comparisons
  - Add a BiLSTM (and/or BiGRU) document encoder that concatenates final states, and a pooled variant (mean/max over hidden states). This is a widely used baseline for document representation.
  - Clarify and strengthen the RNN-LM baseline: Was it trained purely as a language model or with a classification loss? Which representation was used (final hidden, average of hidden states, or something else)? Provide architecture/hyperparameter details and ensure competitive tuning.
  - Consider including a fastText-style baseline (bag-of-words/bi-grams with learned weights) to position the method against strong, simple supervised baselines.
- Evaluation breadth and analysis
  - Add more tasks/datasets to demonstrate generality: e.g., 20 Newsgroups, AG News/Yahoo Answers, Yelp/Amazon reviews, TREC question classification, paraphrase/STS beyond SICK, multi-label tagging. Report transfer performance when training representations on one corpus and evaluating on another.
  - Provide ablations: q=0 (no corruption), varying q; with/without local context; uniform vs. unigram^3/4 negative sampling; effect of subsampling frequent words; document-length normalization choices. Quantify how the induced regularizer affects accuracy beyond word-norm lists.
  - Report robustness to document length and vocabulary size; analyze performance stratified by document length to support the long-document advantage claim.
  - Efficiency: include GPU/CPU wall-clock comparisons vs. PV, BiLSTM, and fastText, and report memory footprint.
- Clarity/details
  - Specify training details for all baselines (embedding sizes, negatives, learning rates, epochs, stopping criteria) and ensure comparable tuning budgets.
  - Clarify whether the global context normalization by T is essential; try alternative normalizations or learned scalar.
  - Discuss handling of OOV and subword information; comment on whether subword modeling (e.g., character n-grams) would complement the approach.

Questions for the authors
1) RNN-LM baseline: Was it trained as a language model (next-word prediction) or directly to minimize classification error? Which representation was used for documents (final hidden state, average/max over hidden states, or another pooling)? Please provide architecture/hyperparameters and validation protocol.
2) Why use a uniform negative-sampling distribution Pv instead of the typical unigram^3/4? Does this choice materially affect performance or the derived regularizer?
3) How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate q and to removing the local context term (i.e., global-only)? Please include quantitative ablations.
4) Can you report results against a bidirectional LSTM encoder with concatenated final states and a pooled variant? This is a widely used baseline for document representation.
5) Beyond IMDB, Wikipedia, and SICK, can you include results on additional text classification and similarity tasks to substantiate general superiority?
6) At test time, is pure averaging always best, or do light-weight weights (e.g., IDF) improve further? How does this interact with the learned regularizer?
7) Do the induced word norms correlate with IDF or mutual information with labels? A quantitative correlation would strengthen the regularizer’s interpretation.
