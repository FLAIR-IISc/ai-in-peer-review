1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a word2vec-style (CBOW) framework that learns document representations by predicting targets from local context plus a global document context computed as the average of a corrupted subset of word embeddings. The corruption acts as dropout and, via a second-order analysis, yields a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses frequent, non-informative words while preserving rare, informative ones. The resulting document vectors are simple averages at test time, providing strong efficiency advantages. Section 3 delivers a clear and appreciated analysis linking corruption to adaptive regularization and explaining why averaging works.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons: (i) while individual components are known, their integration—global context via corruption with a principled regularization view—appears novel and is executed cleanly; (ii) the method is simple, efficient, and likely to be broadly useful.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets efficient, scalable document representation that preserves semantics without per-document parameters. It is well positioned relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, Denoising Autoencoders, and prior global-context averaging.
- Technical soundness: The Taylor expansion–based derivation is careful, and the resulting intuition (attenuating frequent words) is compelling. The architecture is coherent and efficiently trainable.
- Empirical support: Results on sentiment and multi-class categorization are competitive, and the efficiency gains are substantial. However, the empirical coverage is relatively narrow and devotes too much space to t-SNE projections rather than deeper quantitative analysis. Several baselines are “soft,” echoing other reviewers’ concerns.

4. Suggestions to improve (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Replace a large portion of t-SNE plots with more ablations: remove local context; remove corruption; vary corruption rate q; analyze document length sensitivity.
- Strengthen baselines and tuning: strong TF-IDF+SVM, fastText-style n-gram features, tuned PV-DBOW, SIF/Sent2Vec-style averaging; clearer hyperparameter search and variance across runs.
- Broaden tasks: add retrieval, topic/category transfer, and robustness to domain shift.
- Clarify negative sampling distribution, training budget, and memory footprint.

Questions for the authors
- Precisely delineate novelty relative to Huang et al. (global context averaging) and dropout/word dropout variants.
- How sensitive is performance to q, negative sampling distribution, and document length? Please include curves.
- Can the approach be ported to skip-gram; if so, any differences in behavior?
- What is the impact of the 1/T scaling in the global context term?
- How were baselines tuned, and how do results compare to a strong fastText baseline on the same splits?
