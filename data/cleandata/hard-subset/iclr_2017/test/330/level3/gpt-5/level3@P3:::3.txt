1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations. The key idea is to jointly learn word and document embeddings while enforcing that a document vector is the average of its constituent word embeddings. During training, the “global” document representation is formed from a randomly corrupted version of the document (input-layer dropout with unbiased scaling), which both accelerates training and induces a data-dependent regularization that shrinks embeddings of common, uninformative words toward zero while preserving informative/rare words. The model uses a standard CBoW/negative-sampling loss, making it highly scalable. At test time, document vectors are formed by a simple average of word embeddings—no inference is required. Empirically, the method achieves strong results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK), and also yields word embeddings that outperform word2vec on analogy tasks. The approach is extremely efficient in both training and inference and comes with an intuitive analysis that explains the IDF-like effect of corruption.

2. Decision
Accept. Primary reasons: (a) strong empirical performance combined with exceptional simplicity and efficiency, and (b) a clear, well-motivated analysis showing how input corruption induces beneficial, data-dependent regularization that explains the gains.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and significance: The paper tackles learning document embeddings that capture global semantics, scale to large corpora, and support fast inference. This is important because popular alternatives (e.g., Paragraph Vectors) increase parameters with the number of documents and require costly test-time inference.
- Approach and motivation: The model is a natural and well-motivated synthesis of known components: CBoW-style training, sum/average document representations, and input dropout. The novelty is limited, but the combination is purposeful and addresses an acute practical bottleneck. The theoretical approximation using a second-order Taylor expansion connects corruption to an adaptive regularizer that downweights frequent/common words, offering a principled explanation for why averaging can work so well if trained jointly.
- Correctness and rigor: The loss and training procedures follow established practice (negative sampling, subsampling frequent words); the corruption is unbiased; and the analysis mirrors prior work on dropout-as-regularization. While the derivation uses standard approximations (diagonal variance, second-order), it is sound and predictive: norms of embeddings correlate with word informativeness as expected.
- Empirical support: Results are consistently strong: competitive or better than Doc2Vec, Word2Vec+IDF, DAE, and Skip-Thought on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, and close to strong LSTM baselines on SICK. The method also improves word analogy accuracy over word2vec. Efficiency is a major win: training scales to large corpora; test-time document generation is trivial (simple averaging) and orders of magnitude faster than Doc2Vec inference.
- Positioning in the literature: The paper acknowledges and compares to relevant baselines (BoW/TF-IDF, DAE, Word2Vec averaging with/without IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thought, LSTM-based methods) and situates the corruption analysis alongside prior theory on dropout.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Novelty and framing: Be explicit that the architectural components (joint word/document training; sum-of-words representation; input corruption) have precedents (e.g., Huang et al. 2012; Wager et al. 2013; denoising autoencoders). Emphasize the contribution as a clean, unified recipe with a clear analysis and strong evidence.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Report performance without corruption, and with different corruption rates q, to show the magnitude of the regularization effect and training-speed tradeoff.
  - Compare average vs weighted-average at test time when trained with Doc2VecC (e.g., does learned weighting emerge implicitly?).
  - Evaluate removing local context (pure global objective) to quantify how much local vs global terms contribute.
- Regularization analysis:
  - Add empirical plots of embedding norm vs term frequency and vs mutual information/IDF to substantiate the “shrink frequent words” claim.
  - Discuss limits of the second-order approximation (e.g., any observed mismatch at high q or with very skewed distributions?).
- Baseline fairness:
  - Ensure all models have comparable embedding sizes and training budgets; include PV-DBOW carefully tuned, and a strong “tuned averaging” baseline (e.g., IDF/SIF weighting).
  - For SICK, clarify whether all methods are fine-tuned on the same data and whether any external pretraining differs substantially.
- Practical details:
  - Provide hyperparameters (window size, negatives per step, learning rate schedule) and report sensitivity.
  - Discuss handling of OOV and morphology (e.g., subword embeddings) and whether the method benefits from phrase detection.
- Scope and limitations:
  - Elaborate on behavior for very short texts vs long documents (the results hint that Doc2VecC excels on long texts; LSTMs may be better on short sentences).
  - Comment on multilingual or highly inflected languages.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain comes from corruption? Can you report a clean ablation: (i) no corruption; (ii) corruption-only global term (no local context); (iii) varying q (e.g., 0.3/0.5/0.7/0.9) with accuracy and training time?
- How sensitive is performance to the choice of negative sampling distribution and the number of negatives?
- Does Doc2VecC still outperform when the test-time representation uses simple averaging of pretrained word2vec embeddings fine-tuned on the same corpus with strong weighting schemes (e.g., IDF/SIF and post-hoc de-biasing)?
- Can you quantify the predicted regularization effect more directly (e.g., correlation between embedding norm and IDF or document frequency; effect on stopword norms over training)?
- Have you tried subword models (e.g., character n-grams) within Doc2VecC to improve OOV handling and morphology? Any impact on classification or analogies?
- For SICK, did you explore training only on that dataset (no external pretraining) to isolate the contribution of the Doc2VecC objective vs pretraining size?
- How does the method perform on very short documents (tweets, headlines) and on extremely long ones (full articles), and is there an optimal window size or q that varies with length?

Overall, despite limited methodological novelty, the paper offers a compelling, scalable recipe with clear analysis and strong results. I recommend acceptance.
