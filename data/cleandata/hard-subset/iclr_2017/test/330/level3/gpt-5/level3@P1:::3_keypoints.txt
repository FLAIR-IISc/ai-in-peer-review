- Decision: Accept.

- Key reasons: Strong empirical performance with notable efficiency and simplicity; clear formulation linking document-level semantics to word prediction with an intuitive regularization effect.

- Problem & motivation: Learns semantically meaningful, efficient document embeddings, addressing BoW sparsity and Paragraph Vectors’ inference cost by enforcing compositionality via averaging.

- Conceptual placement: Sits between Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, connecting to Huang et al. (2012) and denoising autoencoders; novelty is incremental but specific (on-the-fly corrupted averages + data-dependent regularizer).

- Method: Simple CBOW-style negative sampling using corrupted averaged global vectors plus local context; Taylor expansion yields an interpretable regularizer; complexity depends only on vocabulary size.

- Empirical results: Robust improvements across IMDB, 100-class Wikipedia, SICK, and word analogy benchmarks; matches/outperforms baselines and yields much faster test-time inference than Paragraph Vectors.

- Efficiency: Concrete timing comparisons demonstrating large test-time speedups and claimed very high training throughput.

- Theoretical rigor: Training objective and corruption scheme are sound; derivation of regularization is appropriate and insightful.

- Reproducibility: Experiments are comprehensive, include public code, and consider transductive and non-transductive IMDB settings.

- Novelty framing suggestion: More crisply articulate contribution beyond “average + dropout,” emphasizing the coupling of corrupted global averages with local CBOW and the induced regularizer.

- Ablation suggestion: Add experiments isolating corruption vs no corruption, local-only vs global-only context, different corruption rates q, and alternative weighting schemes (e.g., IDF).

- Negative sampling suggestion: Report sensitivity to negative sampling distribution (uniform vs unigram^0.75) and ensure matched settings when comparing to Word2Vec.

- Baselines suggestion: Include fastText-style linear classifiers and PV-DBOW as stronger or more complete baselines for classification and inference comparisons.

- Fairness suggestion: Ensure all methods are compared with matched embedding sizes, corpora, epochs, window sizes, subsampling, and negative samples; detail vocab expansion/fine-tuning for SICK/book-corpus.

- Notation/representation clarification: Clarify the mismatch between binary BoW x in equations and token-averaging in Eq. (5): specify whether averages use counts or unique words and how 1/T scaling is applied.

- Error-analysis suggestion: Provide quantitative analyses showing gains from downweighting frequent non-informative tokens (e.g., ablations removing stopword suppression).

- Question on corruption: How sensitive are results to corruption rate q and document length T; was frequency-dependent corruption (higher drop for frequent words) tried?

- Question on negatives: What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives were used across experiments, and how did choices affect performance and regularization?

- Question on Word2Vec fairness: Were Word2Vec baselines trained with identical corpora and hyperparameters; please provide a table of matched hyperparameters.

- Question on Paragraph Vectors: Was PV-DBOW included in all tasks; if not, report PV-DBOW results and inference-time costs.

- Question on representation construction: During training is the global vector computed over unique words (binary) or all tokens (counts), and what is done at test time?

- Question on throughput claim: Specify hardware, implementation details (multithreading), and batch/optimization settings for the “billions of words per hour on a single machine” claim.

- Question on stability: What is the variance across random seeds on key benchmarks and are there notable failure modes for very short or very long documents?

- Question on SICK fine-tuning: What exactly is optimized during unsupervised fine-tuning on SICK (objective and procedure) and how much does this fine-tuning contribute?
