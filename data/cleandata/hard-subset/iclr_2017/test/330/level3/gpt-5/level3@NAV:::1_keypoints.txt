- Proposes Doc2VecC: a CBOW-style model that predicts targets from local context plus a global document context formed by averaging a corrupted subset of word embeddings.  
- Corruption acts like dropout and, via second-order analysis, yields a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent non-informative words while preserving rare informative ones.  
- At test time document vectors are simple averages, offering substantial efficiency advantages.  
- Section 3 provides a clear analysis linking corruption to adaptive regularization and explaining why averaging works.  
- Decision: Accept — novelty comes from integrating global context via corruption with a principled regularization view, implemented cleanly.  
- Method is simple, efficient, broadly useful, and fills a niche between Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and denoising autoencoders.  
- Taylor-expansion derivation is careful and gives a compelling intuition for attenuating frequent words; architecture is coherent and trainable.  
- Empirical results on sentiment and multi-class tasks are competitive and show large efficiency gains, but experiments are somewhat narrow.  
- Critique: too much space given to t-SNE visualizations instead of deeper quantitative ablation and robustness analyses.  
- Recommend additional ablations (remove local context/corruption, vary corruption rate q, analyze document length sensitivity).  
- Suggest strengthening baselines and tuning (TF-IDF+SVM, fastText n-grams, tuned PV-DBOW, SIF/Sent2Vec) and reporting hyperparameter search and variance across runs.  
- Propose broadening evaluation to retrieval, transfer, and domain-shift robustness, and clarifying negative sampling distribution, training budget, and memory footprint.  
- Questions for authors: clarify novelty vs. Huang et al. and other dropout variants; report sensitivity to q, negative sampling, and document length with curves.  
- Additional questions: can the approach be ported to skip-gram and how would it differ; what is the impact of the 1/T scaling in the global context; how were baselines tuned and how does performance compare to a strong fastText baseline on same splits?
