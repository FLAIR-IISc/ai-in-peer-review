1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document embeddings by averaging constituent word embeddings that are jointly trained with a word2vec-style CBoW objective. A random word-dropout (“corruption”) mechanism is applied during training, which the authors analyze as inducing a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses common, nondiscriminative words while preserving informative ones. The method decouples model size from the number of documents, trains at word2vec speeds, and enables extremely fast test-time encoding via simple averaging. Empirically, the approach yields strong results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK), and produces high-quality word embeddings (word analogy).

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) Strong empirical performance with substantial efficiency and practical impact (especially test-time encoding), and (ii) conceptual clarity and simplicity that make the method easy to adopt, despite limited methodological novelty.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: How to learn document embeddings that capture global semantics while retaining word2vec’s scalability and enabling cheap inference for unseen documents.
- Motivation and placement: The approach is well-motivated by compositional properties of word embeddings and the drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (parameter growth and expensive inference). The paper situates itself appropriately among Word2Vec, PV, DAEs, and sentence encoders.
- Support for claims: Results are compelling across multiple tasks, with clear speed advantages (notably test-time generation vs Doc2Vec). The Taylor expansion argument provides a plausible explanation of the corruption’s regularization effect; qualitative analyses (small-norm words) align with the theory. While the components (sum-of-words, joint training, dropout corruption, negative sampling) are not novel, the combination is executed well and yields consistent gains. Comparisons to strong baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, PV, SDAE, Skip-thoughts) are appropriate and mostly fair.

4. Suggestions and questions to improve the paper
- Clarify novelty: explicitly position the corruption as standard input-layer dropout and frame the contribution as an effective synthesis yielding practical benefits.
- Provide ablations: (a) no corruption; (b) varying q; (c) Word2Vec trained with identical corruption and simple averaging; (d) weighting schemes (IDF/SIF) at test time.
- Report statistical significance and variance over multiple runs; detail hyperparameter tuning budgets and compute.
- Expand on negative sampling details (distribution, k) and subsampling’s interaction with the corruption.
- Discuss limitations: behavior on very short texts vs long documents; OOV handling; multilingual settings.
Questions:
- How sensitive are gains to q and document length? Is there an optimal q by corpus?
- Does corruption primarily help by regularizing U or by reweighting words in the document vector?
- How does Doc2VecC compare to a strong SIF/IDF-averaging baseline trained jointly with CBoW?
- Are the improvements robust across random seeds and domains beyond those reported?
