- Proposes Doc2VecC: predict a target word using local context plus a global document vector formed by averaging corrupted word embeddings.  
- Corruption is an unbiased word dropout that, via a second-order Taylor approximation, induces a data-dependent regularizer downweighting frequent/non-informative words.  
- Model complexity is independent of corpus size, trains extremely fast with negative sampling, and yields test-time document vectors by a single average over learned embeddings.  
- Provides a clean theoretical analysis explaining why corruption induces regularization and why test-time averaging matches training expectation.  
- Empirically competitive or superior on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia 100-way classification, and word analogies; close to stronger sequence models on SICK while being much faster.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Key reason for acceptance: elegant, well-motivated combination of known components with principled analysis, simplicity, and efficiency likely to encourage adoption.  
- Empirical strengths: consistent improvements and notable speed/test-time simplicity advantages over Paragraph Vectors and skip-thought encoders.  
- Problem framing and literature placement are sound, acknowledging relevant baselines (Word2Vec, PV, SDA/DAE, skip-thought, LSTM/CNN).  
- Method is scalable and avoids train–test mismatch because corruption is unbiased so test-time averages reflect training expectation.  
- Theory (Taylor expansion with unbiased dropout) neatly explains empirical behaviors such as implicit downweighting of stopwords without ad-hoc IDF.  
- IMDB: matches or outperforms PV and beats BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, SDA while being far faster at test time.  
- Wikipedia 100-way: consistently outperforms alternatives across embedding sizes.  
- Word analogies: yields notably better word embeddings than Word2Vec under comparable training.  
- SICK: competitive with complex sequence models but orders of magnitude faster to train.  
- Practical advantages: fast training/inference, small model footprint, trivial test-time encoder, and open-source code increase impact.  
- Suggested stronger baselines: add bag-of-ngrams (fastText variants), tuned PV-DBOW/DM, and carefully tuned TF-IDF+SVM.  
- Experimental clarifications requested: specify negative sampling distribution/settings (uniform vs unigram^0.75) and subsampling to ensure matched baselines.  
- Ablation suggestions: vary/remove corruption (q), ablate local vs global contexts, compare standard dropout on U/V, and test negative-sampling alternatives and context window sensitivity.  
- Analysis suggestions: replace t-SNE with quantitative clustering/retrieval metrics, report variance across runs and learning curves vs throughput, analyze performance vs document length.  
- Broaden evaluation: add more downstream tasks, retrieval, out-of-domain transfer, and multilingual/domain-shift tests.  
- Clarify experimental details: hardware, throughput, hyperparameters, memory footprint, and scaling with vocabulary size for fair timing comparisons.  
- Questions for authors: negative-sampling/subsampling choices and sensitivity; how performance varies with corruption rate q; ablation results for corruption/local/global removal.  
- Additional author questions: quantify regularization effect (correlation of embedding norms with IDF and impact of masking high/low norm words); how Paragraph Vectors were tuned; length sensitivity effects; domain-shift generalization; optimizer and learning-rate/training tricks used.
