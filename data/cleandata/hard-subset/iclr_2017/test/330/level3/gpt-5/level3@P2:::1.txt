Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings in conjunction with a global context term learned under a corruption (dropout) model. The key idea is to enforce during training that a document’s mean of word embeddings is a sufficient representation for predicting target words, combining local CBoW context with a corrupted global bag-of-words context.
- A second-order Taylor expansion analysis shows that unbiased dropout on the global context induces a data-dependent regularizer that attenuates embeddings of frequent/non-informative words while preserving informative/rare words—providing a principled explanation for why simple averaging can work well.
- Empirically, the method matches or outperforms state-of-the-art document representation baselines on IMDB sentiment, a 100-way Wikipedia classification task, and performs competitively on SICK semantic relatedness, while being highly efficient at both training and test time. Code is released and the model scales to very large corpora.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The approach is conceptually clean, well-motivated, and likely novel in its particular combination: enforcing average-embedding document representations during training with an explicit corruption mechanism and an accompanying analysis that connects to data-dependent regularization.
- The method is efficient, easy to reproduce, and likely to be useful to the community as a strong, practical baseline for document embedding, with empirical gains over widely used alternatives.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The paper tackles learning document vectors that are both semantically meaningful and efficient to compute, addressing well-known limitations of BoW sparsity and Paragraph Vector’s inference cost. The positioning relative to Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors/Denoising Autoencoders and sentence encoders (Skip-thought) is clear.
- Motivation and novelty: While the ingredients (CBoW objective, dropout, averaging) are known, the specific training-time enforcement that the simple average must carry the global semantics, together with a theoretically grounded corruption scheme that yields a data-dependent regularizer, appears novel and well-motivated. The analysis in Section 3.1 is a strong point and ties to prior work on dropout-as-adaptive-regularization.
- Technical correctness: The derivation using a second-order Taylor approximation around the unbiased corruption mean is sound in spirit and consistent with prior analyses. The induced regularizer’s behavior (down-weight frequent, non-discriminative words) aligns with empirical findings (e.g., reduced norms for stopwords).
- Empirical support: Experiments demonstrate improvements over Word2Vec-based averages (with/without IDF), DAE, and Paragraph Vectors on IMDB and Wikipedia classification, plus competitive performance on SICK. Efficiency comparisons are favorable and practically important. However, the empirical section is somewhat narrow and could use stronger baselines and ablations (see feedback below).
- Practicality and impact: The method’s simplicity (no per-document parameters, fast inference via averaging) and scalability are compelling. The released code further increases potential impact.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen baselines:
  - Include stronger and more diverse baselines such as n-gram TF-IDF + linear classifier (a robust classic baseline), fastText-style bag-of-ngrams with subword features, and competitive supervised CNN/LSTM baselines on IMDB.
  - For Paragraph Vectors, include both PV-DM and PV-DBOW variants and ensure fair hyperparameter tuning and training budgets.
  - For Word2Vec, consider the standard negative sampling distribution (unigram^0.75) in addition to uniform; report sensitivity.
- Broaden and deepen empirical analysis:
  - Add more datasets (e.g., 20 Newsgroups, AG News, Amazon reviews, RCV1) and STS-style sentence similarity benchmarks to probe generalization.
  - Provide ablations: remove corruption; vary corruption rate q; remove local context; vary context window size; analyze effect of 1/T scaling; test IDF-style reweighting at training time vs test time.
  - Control for representation dimensionality across methods; consider reporting results at matched dimensions and with/without vector normalization.
  - Replace some t-SNE figures with quantitative analyses (e.g., clustering metrics, intra/inter-class cosine distributions) and error analyses.
- Clarify training details for reproducibility:
  - Exact negative sampling distribution and number of negatives, learning rate schedule, epochs, subsampling threshold, vocabulary cutoff, preprocessing (tokenization, lowercasing), and random seeds.
  - Ensure that comparisons using external pretraining (e.g., Skip-thought) are clearly marked and that any use of test data for unsupervised representation learning is explicitly separated from supervised training on labels.
- Theory/exposition:
  - Discuss assumptions and limitations of the second-order approximation and diagonal Hessian use; comment on when the analysis might break down.
  - Provide more intuition for the 1/T scaling of the global context and potential alternatives (e.g., length normalization variants).
  - Contrast Doc2VecC’s regularization with Word2Vec’s subsampling of frequent words; provide an ablation disentangling their effects.

Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling and context:
  - What negative sampling distribution did you use (uniform vs unigram^0.75)? How sensitive are results to this choice and to the number of negatives?
  - What context window sizes were used and how sensitive are results to this hyperparameter?
- Corruption:
  - Please provide a plot of performance vs corruption rate q across tasks. Is there a broad plateau (e.g., q in [0.7, 0.95])? How does q interact with document length?
  - Did you compare unbiased dropout to alternative noise models (e.g., masking without rescaling, Gaussian noise on counts)?
- Ablations:
  - Performance when removing local context (leaving only the global corrupted average) and when removing the global term (reducing to CBoW).
  - Effect of removing subsampling of frequent words to isolate the contribution of the proposed regularizer.
- Implementation details:
  - How many training epochs were used on IMDB/Wikipedia/SICK? What were the learning rates and schedules? Are U and V both updated for global and local terms, and are input/output embeddings tied?
  - For SICK, how exactly was vocabulary expansion and fine-tuning performed, and were any label signals used during fine-tuning?
- Fairness and scope:
  - On IMDB, how does Doc2VecC compare to strong supervised baselines like NBSVM or shallow CNNs under similar constraints?
  - On Wikipedia, many pages are multi-label; your setup selects a single label per page. How would Doc2VecC fare in a multi-label setting?
- Generalization:
  - Have you tried a Skip-gram variant of Doc2VecC? Does the analysis and performance carry over?
  - Any observations on cross-domain robustness (training on one domain, testing on another) given the claimed regularization effect on frequent words?

Overall, this is a well-composed, efficient method with a solid analysis and promising empirical results. With stronger baselines and ablations, the paper would be even more convincing.
