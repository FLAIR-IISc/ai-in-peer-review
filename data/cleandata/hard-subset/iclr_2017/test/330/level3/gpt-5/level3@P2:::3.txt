Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, an efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, trained end-to-end with a word2vec-style CBoW objective augmented by a “corruption” mechanism that randomly drops words from the global document context during training. The key claims are: (a) averaging word embeddings can be made to encode global document semantics if trained jointly with the prediction objective; (b) the mask-out corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent/non-informative words toward zero, favoring rarer, discriminative words; (c) the method is simple and fast (complexity depends on vocabulary size, not corpus size), and test-time representations of unseen documents are trivial to compute; and (d) empirically, the method matches or outperforms state-of-the-art document representations on sentiment analysis, document classification, and performs strongly on word analogy; it is also substantially faster than heavier models (e.g., Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thoughts). The paper provides a Taylor-expansion analysis linking corruption to an adaptive regularizer, ablations illustrating the frequency-dependent shrinkage effect, and thorough comparisons with common baselines. Code is provided.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
- Strong empirical performance and excellent efficiency with a very simple architecture. The ability to train on very large corpora and produce instant test-time embeddings is highly practical.
- While the components are individually known (word2vec CBoW, averaging, dropout/denoising), the combination is well-motivated, clearly analyzed, and shown to work surprisingly well in practice.

Supporting arguments
- What is the problem? Learning document-level embeddings that are (i) semantically meaningful, (ii) scalable to large corpora, and (iii) fast at test time. Existing methods either require expensive inference for unseen documents (Paragraph Vectors) or post hoc heuristics over word embeddings (e.g., IDF-weighted averages), or are computationally heavy (Skip-Thoughts/LSTM encoders).
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper situates itself relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, denoising autoencoders, and prior averaging-based document context (Huang et al., 2012). The corruption scheme is essentially unbiased dropout/denoising, which is standard; the novelty lies in enforcing during training that simple averaging must carry global semantics and in leveraging dropout to induce a frequency-aware shrinkage on embeddings. The positioning is mostly fair; a few closely related/simple baselines (e.g., fastText bag-of-ngrams; SIF-weighted averaging) could be discussed or compared.
- Do the results support the claims? Broadly yes. On IMDB sentiment and a 100-class Wikipedia document classification benchmark, Doc2VecC consistently matches or outperforms strong baselines (BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-Thoughts) while being far more efficient. The analysis connecting corruption to data-dependent regularization is sound and aligns with observed shrinkage of stop-word embeddings. Word analogy results indicate higher-quality word embeddings than Word2Vec, suggesting the joint global-context training is beneficial. The experimental protocols are mostly clear and reproducible (code link provided). One caveat: some experiments learn representations on all available data including test sets; while common for unsupervised pretraining, this should be clearly separated from strictly out-of-sample settings (which are also reported).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not critical to the decision)
- Novelty/positioning: Be explicit that the corruption is standard unbiased dropout and connect more directly to marginalized denoising autoencoders and dropout-as-adaptive-regularization (e.g., Wager et al.). Clarify how Doc2VecC differs from “averaging + IDF/SIF” baselines conceptually and empirically.
- Baselines: Consider adding fastText (bag-of-ngrams) and SIF (Smooth Inverse Frequency) or other weighted-averaging baselines; these are strong, simple document/sentence baselines contemporaneous with this work.
- Ablations: Provide quantitative ablations for (i) no corruption vs. corruption; (ii) varying corruption rate q; (iii) with/without local context (i.e., only global doc vector); (iv) different negative sampling distributions; (v) impact of subsampling frequent words vs. relying solely on the induced regularizer.
- Clarifications on representation: x is defined as binary BoW, but the method averages word embeddings over tokens. Please clarify how multiple occurrences are handled during training and at test time, and how the 1/T scaling interacts with the unbiased scaling 1/(1−q).
- Fairness and reproducibility: Ensure timing comparisons use matched hardware and clearly state training/inference hardware and hyperparameters (e.g., embedding size, window, negatives, learning rate schedule).
- Analysis: The Taylor expansion is approximate; adding empirical checks (e.g., correlation between word frequency/informativeness and learned L2 norms) beyond a small table would strengthen the regularization claim.
- Scope: Report sensitivity to document length. The paper argues Doc2VecC handles long texts better than RNNs; a controlled study varying length would be informative.
- Presentation: Fix minor citation/style inconsistencies and typos in the references; improve figure readability.

Questions for the authors
1) Corruption details: Is dropout applied at the token level (each occurrence) or at the type level (per vocabulary dimension) within a document? How is unbiased scaling combined with the 1/T normalization in practice? Does T count pre- or post-dropout tokens?
2) Multiple occurrences: Training defines x as binary BoW. Do repeated words contribute multiple times to the averaged document vector during training and testing? If not, why not use counts?
3) Ablations: What is the performance without corruption (q=0)? How does accuracy and training speed vary with q? Please provide a curve.
4) Local vs. global context: How much does the local context Uc contribute relative to the global document vector? What happens if you remove the local context (reducing to a denoising autoencoder variant) or the global context (reducing to CBoW)?
5) Negative sampling: Which proposal distribution (e.g., unigram^0.75) and number of negatives were used? How sensitive are results to these choices?
6) Subsampling: You note Doc2VecC is less reliant on subsampling frequent words. Could you quantify performance with/without subsampling across baselines?
7) Baselines: Can you add comparisons to fastText bag-of-ngrams and SIF-weighted averaging? If not, can you discuss expected differences?
8) Length and scaling: Because of the 1/T factor, does the regularization or contribution of the document vector diminish for very long documents? Any empirical evidence?
9) Word analogy: Were training data, embedding size, and runtime controlled between Word2Vec and Doc2VecC in the analogy evaluation?
10) OOV handling: In the SICK experiments, how do you handle OOVs at test time beyond fine-tuning vocabulary expansion? What’s the impact on results?
