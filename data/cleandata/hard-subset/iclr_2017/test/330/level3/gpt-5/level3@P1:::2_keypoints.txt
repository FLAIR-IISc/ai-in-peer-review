- Proposal: Doc2VecC—represent documents by averaging word embeddings, training with random word masking (corruption) plus local CBOW context and negative sampling.  
- Theoretical claim: Corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent/non-informative words while preserving rare/informative ones.  
- Practical claim: Decouples model size from corpus size, enables very fast training and inference (simple averaging), and improves word/document embeddings.  
- Empirical results: Strong performance on IMDB, 100-class Wikipedia, SICK (but underperforms LSTM/Tree-LSTM), and word analogy tasks; large speedups vs Paragraph Vectors, Word2Vec+averaging/IDF, denoising autoencoders, and Skip-Thought.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary reason 1: Limited technical novelty—mainly an incremental combination of known components (CBOW, averaging, dropout-style corruption, adaptive regularization).  
- Primary reason 2: Empirical validation is insufficient—lacks convincing comparisons to competitive bidirectional LSTM/CNN encoders and strong bag-of-ngrams baselines across more datasets.  
- Strengths: Clear motivation, sensible design leveraging compositionality, and well-connected placement in prior literature.  
- Literature placement critique: Key components are known; related supervised baselines (CNN/LSTM) and strong bag-of-ngrams are missing; PV-DBOW results not shown in main tables.  
- Theoretical rigor critique: Taylor expansion argument is plausible but omits important details/assumptions (ignored off-diagonal Hessian, negative sampling distribution choice, subsampling effects).  
- Empirical nuance: Doc2VecC shows context-dependent superiority—strong on some tasks/datasets but not universally better than sequence encoders.  
- Baselines fairness: Some baselines (e.g., Skip-Thought) may be disadvantaged by document length or other factors; comparisons need tighter control.  
- Suggested baseline additions: Bidirectional LSTM encoder (concatenated final states), CNN text encoder with pretrained embeddings, strong bag-of-ngrams, and tuned PV-DBOW/PV-DM.  
- Suggested dataset expansion: Add 20 Newsgroups, AG News, Yelp/Amazon, Reuters, DBPedia, non-English corpora, multi-label tasks, and length-sensitivity analyses.  
- Ablation experiments requested: No corruption, corruption without CBOW, varying corruption rate q, varying negative sampling distributions, and quantification of adaptive regularization vs. CBOW contributions.  
- Statistical rigor: Report statistical significance and variance over multiple runs.  
- Fairness/preprocessing: Clarify whether test data were used during unsupervised representation learning and, if so, provide strictly held-out results.  
- Implementation details requested: Number of negatives, window sizes, subsampling thresholds, learning rates, wall-clock throughput on identical hardware, memory footprint, and per-document inference latency.  
- OOV/subword handling: Discuss treatment of OOVs and potential integration of subword/character features.  
- Negative sampling critique: Using a uniform negative distribution is unusual; explore and report effects of using unigram^3/4 or other distributions.  
- Questions for authors—RNN-LM baseline: Was it trained as a language model or supervised classifier, what representation was used, and was it uni- or bidirectional?  
- Questions for authors—negative sampling: What negative distribution was used, and why depart from unigram^3/4?  
- Questions for authors—subsampling/IDF: Are reported gains robust when subsampling is disabled, and how was IDF computed for Word2Vec+IDF?  
- Questions for authors—test data usage: Were test documents included in unsupervised training, and how was leakage avoided during evaluation/hyperparameter tuning?  
- Questions for authors—regularizer behavior: Do frequent but informative tokens (e.g., negations) get overshrunk; provide targeted analyses.  
- Questions for authors—sensitivity and compute: Provide performance vs. q and number of negatives, variance over seeds, exact throughput (tokens/hr), memory usage, and how training time scales with document length and q.
