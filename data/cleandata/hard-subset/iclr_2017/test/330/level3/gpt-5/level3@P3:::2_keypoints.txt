- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents by averaged word embeddings trained with a corruption model that induces a data-dependent regularizer downweighting common words.  
- Empirical claim: Achieves lowest errors among compared embedding baselines on IMDB and Wikipedia, is very fast to train/infer, competitive on SICK, and improves word embeddings for analogy tasks.  
- Strengths: Method is simple, scalable (parameters depend on vocab, not corpus size), and computationally efficient at train and test time.  
- Decision: Reject due to limited novelty and insufficient breadth of evaluation.  
- Novelty concern: Core ideas (averaging embeddings, dropout-like corruption, negative-sampling CBoW) are not novel and the regularization analysis largely adapts prior marginalized corruption work.  
- Baseline gaps: Missing strong and widely used baselines (BiLSTM/BiGRU encoders, well-specified RNN-LM, fastText-like bag-of-ngrams) which undermines claims of general superiority.  
- Task coverage: Evaluation limited to IMDB, Wikipedia, and SICK; broader datasets and transfer experiments are needed to show generality.  
- Ablation/analysis gaps: No systematic ablations on corruption rate q, negative-sampling distribution, local context term, subsampling, normalization choices, or quantitative measure of the regularizer’s impact.  
- Rigor concerns: Regularization derivation is sensible but standard; key hyperparameter choices (uniform negatives, q) are not systematically ablated.  
- Robustness/efficiency reporting: Need stratified analysis by document length, vocabulary size, and wall-clock/memory comparisons vs. PV, BiLSTM, and fastText.  
- Clarity/details needed: Provide full training/hyperparameter details for all baselines, clarify global context normalization, and explain OOV/subword handling.  
- Suggested experiments: Add BiLSTM/BiGRU baselines (final-state and pooled variants), fastText-style baselines, more datasets (20NG, AG News, Yelp, TREC, paraphrase/STS), and transfer evaluations.  
- Questions for authors: Clarify RNN-LM training/objective and representation used; justify uniform negative sampling vs. unigram^3/4; report sensitivity to q and local-context removal; provide BiLSTM comparisons; extend dataset coverage; test weighted averaging (e.g., IDF); quantify correlation between induced word norms and IDF/MI.
