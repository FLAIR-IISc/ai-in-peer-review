- Proposes Doc2VecC: document vectors as averaged word embeddings with random-word "corruption" used as global context in a skip-gram objective.  
- Corruption is theoretically shown to act like a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-informative words.  
- Model is simple, efficient, scalable, avoids per-document parameters, and enables trivial inference for unseen documents.  
- Empirically achieves competitive or superior results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Strong theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) explaining why the corruption mechanism improves embeddings.  
- Qualitative results show the method pushes stop-word embeddings toward zero compared to baselines.  
- Main empirical limitation: evaluation tasks are limited and could be expanded (e.g., retrieval, clustering) to demonstrate generality.  
- Some baseline comparisons may be weak or under-tuned (notably Paragraph Vectors on Wikipedia), reducing confidence in relative gains.  
- Comparison to stronger compositional baselines beyond Word2Vec+AVG is lacking.  
- t-SNE visualizations are illustrative but occupy space that could be used for deeper empirical analysis.  
- Missing ablation studies (e.g., effect of corruption rate q, removing local context) to justify design choices.  
- Suggested improvements: expand evaluation tasks, provide detailed baseline hyperparameter tuning, add ablation studies, and move one t-SNE plot to the appendix.  
- Questions for authors: clarify baseline hyperparameter tuning (especially Paragraph Vectors), sensitivity and empirical match of the second-order Taylor approximation/regularizer, and details of fine-tuning on the SICK semantic relatedness task (which parameters were updated).
