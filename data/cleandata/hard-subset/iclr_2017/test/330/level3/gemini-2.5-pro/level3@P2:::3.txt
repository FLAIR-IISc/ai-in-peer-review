### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper presents Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. Crucially, the model is trained jointly to ensure that the learned word embeddings are suitable for this compositional task. The training objective is similar to Word2Vec's CBoW model, where the goal is to predict a target word from its local context. Doc2VecC extends this by also conditioning the prediction on a "global context," which is the average of word embeddings from the entire document. A key element of the proposed method is a corruption process, where a random subset of words from the document is used to form this global context at each training step. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, non-informative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that this simple and fast approach matches or exceeds the performance of more complex state-of-the-art models on sentiment analysis, document classification, and word analogy tasks, while being significantly more efficient, especially at inference time.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The proposed method, despite its architectural simplicity, achieves results that are highly competitive with or superior to established and more complex baselines like Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec) and Skip-thought vectors across a variety of tasks, including sentiment analysis and document classification.
2.  **Exceptional Efficiency:** The model is very fast to train and, most importantly, extremely efficient at inference. Generating an embedding for an unseen document requires only a simple averaging of its word vectors, a significant practical advantage over methods like Paragraph Vectors which require a costly optimization procedure for each new document.

### **Supporting Arguments**
The primary contribution of this work lies not in the invention of entirely new components, but in the effective and well-motivated combination of existing ideas to create a system that is simple, fast, and powerful. Representing documents as an average of word vectors is a common baseline, and using dropout on inputs is a standard technique. However, the authors integrate these ideas into a joint training framework where the word vectors are explicitly learned to be averaged, and they demonstrate this combination is surprisingly effective.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides strong support for the paper's claims.
*   On the IMDB sentiment analysis task (Table 1), Doc2VecC achieves an error rate of 11.70%, on par with the much more complex Paragraph Vectors (11.77%) and significantly better than Word2Vec+IDF (12.33%) and the surprisingly poor Skip-thought vectors (17.42%).
*   The efficiency gains are a major selling point. As shown in Table 2, generating representations for the IMDB test set takes Doc2VecC only 7 seconds, compared to over 4 minutes for Paragraph Vectors. This makes the method far more practical for real-world applications where inference speed is critical.
*   The results on the word analogy task are particularly interesting, showing that the word vectors learned by Doc2VecC are of significantly higher quality than those from Word2Vec and especially Paragraph Vectors. This suggests that forcing the model to reconstruct local context from a global document representation encourages the learning of more meaningful word semantics.
*   The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer, provides a compelling explanation for why the model works well. It elegantly explains how the model learns to suppress the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words, a claim supported by the qualitative analysis in Table 3.

In summary, while the novelty of the individual techniques is limited, the paper presents a cohesive framework that is well-justified, extensively evaluated, and offers a compelling combination of performance and efficiency that is of great value to the research community and practitioners.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Positioning and Novelty:** The paper could be strengthened by more explicitly positioning the "corruption" mechanism with respect to standard techniques. In essence, the method applies dropout to the input bag-of-words representation used for the global context. Framing it this way would help clarify the connection to prior work, while still allowing the authors to emphasize their novel analysis and successful application of it in this specific context. The contribution is the effective combination and analysis, not necessarily the invention of a new corruption scheme.
*   **Ablation Study:** The paper claims the corruption mechanism is "critical to the performance". This is a strong claim that would be best supported by a direct ablation study. A simple experiment showing the performance of the model when trained *without* corruption (i.e., using the average of *all* words in the document for the global context) would provide a clear measure of the corruption's impact and solidify this key claim.
*   **Corruption Rate `q`:** The authors state that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and use q=0.9 throughout. While this is reasonable, a small plot showing performance on a validation set across a range of `q` values (e.g., 0.1, 0.5, 0.9, 0.99) would make this claim more empirically robust.

**Questions:**
1.  Could you clarify your view on the novelty of the corruption model itself? Is it best understood as a new mechanism, or as a novel and highly effective application of input-layer dropout to the problem of learning compositional document embeddings?
2.  Regarding your hypothesis that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to long document lengths: have you considered a simple experiment to test this, for instance, by truncating the reviews to the first 50 or 100 words and observing if the performance of Skip-thought vectors improves relative to Doc2VecC? This could provide strong evidence for your claim.
3.  The analysis in Section 3.1 provides an elegant justification for the regularization effect. Does this theoretical insight suggest other potential forms of corruption? For example, would a non-uniform corruption probability (e.g., based on IDF) yield further improvements?
