### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The proposed method represents a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The core contribution is a corruption model, which randomly drops words from the document's global context vector during training. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis in Section 3, showing that this corruption mechanism is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of frequent but non-discriminative words, while favoring those that are rare and informative. The model is shown to be extremely fast to train and test, and the resulting document vectors achieve state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. Furthermore, the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are shown to be of higher quality than those produced by the standard Word2Vec model.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**
**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method is simple, elegant, and very well-motivated. The theoretical analysis connecting the corruption process to a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3.1) is a significant contribution that provides valuable insight into why this type of model works.
2.  The model is extremely efficient, making it highly practical for large-scale applications. It achieves strong empirical results across multiple benchmarks, often matching or outperforming much more complex and computationally expensive models. This combination of performance and efficiency makes it a useful contribution to the community.

### **Supporting Arguments**
The primary strength of this paper is the clear and insightful connection it draws between a simple training procedure (randomly dropping words) and its effect as a regularizer. The Taylor expansion analysis in Section 3.1 is well-executed and appreciated. It elevates the method from a heuristic combination of existing ideas (averaging embeddings, dropout) to a principled approach for learning document representations. It clearly explains *why* simply averaging word embeddings can be made to work well: the model is explicitly regularized during training to down-weight the impact of common, uninformative words and produce embeddings that compose well.

This theoretical elegance is matched by strong practical benefits. The model's simplicity, inherited from the CBoW architecture, makes it remarkably fast. As shown in Table 2, training is orders of magnitude faster than for models like Skip-thought vectors, and inference is near-instantaneous, in contrast to Paragraph Vectors which require an expensive inference step for new documents. This efficiency is critical for real-world use cases.

The empirical evaluation, while having some minor weaknesses, is broadly supportive of the paper's claims. Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks (Tables 1 and 5), outperforming strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors. The analysis of the learned word embedding norms in Table 3 provides direct evidence for the claimed regularization effect, showing that Doc2VecC successfully suppresses stop words while retaining informative rare words, a clear advantage over Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The improved performance on the word analogy task (Table 4) further demonstrates that the model learns higher-quality word semantics.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**
These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   **Deeper Empirical Analysis:** The paper dedicates significant space to t-SNE visualizations (Figures 3 and 4). While illustrative, this space might be better used for a more in-depth error analysis. For instance, on the Wikipedia classification task, what kinds of categories are confused by the baselines but correctly identified by Doc2VecC? This would provide more concrete evidence for the quality of the learned representations beyond aggregate accuracy scores.
*   **Baseline Comparisons:** The performance of the Skip-thought vector baseline on the IMDB dataset is noted as "surprisingly poor" and attributed to document length. This raises a question about the fairness of the comparison. A stronger paper would either demonstrate that this is an inherent limitation of the baseline or attempt a more robust application of it (e.g., by breaking long documents into sentences and averaging their representations). Acknowledging this potential weakness in the comparison would also be valuable.
*   **Corruption Rate Sensitivity:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and uses q=0.9 throughout. While this simplifies the experiments, showing a small ablation study on a validation set with different values of q would make this claim more convincing and provide readers with better guidance for implementation.

### **Questions for the Authors**
*   The poor performance of Skip-thought vectors on the IMDB dataset is an interesting result. Have you considered alternative strategies for applying this baseline to long documents, such as chunking the document and averaging the resulting sentence vectors, to ensure the comparison is on the strongest possible footing for the baseline?
*   Your analysis in Section 3.1 of the implicit data-dependent regularization is very insightful. Could you elaborate on the relationship between this effect and the explicit frequent word sub-sampling heuristic used in Word2Vec? Your results show Doc2VecC does not rely on sub-sampling, but does your regularization approximate a similar outcome, or does it learn a more nuanced, data-driven weighting scheme that goes beyond simple frequency counts?
