- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of its word embeddings learned jointly in a word2vec-style predictive task.  
- Uses a corruption mechanism (random word dropout) during training, argued to act as a data-dependent regularizer favoring informative words.  
- Claims comparable or superior performance to more complex models on sentiment analysis and document classification benchmarks.  
- Major practical advantage: far faster inference for unseen documents (just average precomputed word vectors) compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors.  
- Empirical evaluation is solid and shows the simple architecture is competitive with state-of-the-art.  
- Methodological novelty is limited: averaging word vectors, input-layer dropout, and negative sampling closely follow established techniques.  
- The paper’s main contribution is the effective synthesis and empirical validation of simple components rather than inventing new methods.  
- Recommendation: Accept, based on simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results despite limited novelty.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly position the work as a synthesis of existing ideas and acknowledge prior baselines to strengthen the message.  
- Suggestion: Expand the Section 3.1 analysis (dropout → data-dependent regularization) with a small experiment visualizing word-norm distributions for different dropout rates.  
- Question 1: Why is the implicit data-dependent regularization from corruption more effective than a standard L2 penalty on embeddings?  
- Question 2: Have you evaluated Doc2VecC on very short-text benchmarks (e.g., tweets) to test whether it loses out to recurrent models on short documents?
