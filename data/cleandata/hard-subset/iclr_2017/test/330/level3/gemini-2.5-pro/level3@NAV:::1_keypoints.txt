- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of its word embeddings learned with a skip-gram style objective using local and global contexts.  
- Global context is formed by averaging word vectors from a corrupted version of the document where words are randomly dropped out.  
- Theoretical analysis shows corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-informative words.  
- Novelty lies in combining skip-gram, dropout-style corruption, and context vectors into an efficient document representation method.  
- Claims substantial inference-time advantages over Paragraph Vectors for unseen documents.  
- Reviewer recommends Accept, citing the method’s elegance and the strong theoretical explanation of its behavior.  
- Main empirical weakness: limited evaluation tasks (sentiment and k-way classification) and insufficiently strong contemporary baselines.  
- Critique that too much space is spent on t-SNE visuals instead of deeper quantitative analysis or ablation studies.  
- Suggested improvements: expand evaluations to retrieval/clustering and include stronger baselines (e.g., averaged embeddings from large pre-trained models/transformers).  
- Questions for authors: justify baseline choices and positioning vs transformer-based methods given efficiency/performance trade-offs; whether other corruption strategies (e.g., IDF-based) were explored and if theory extends to them; explain unusually low Skip-thought performance on IMDB and role of document length.
