### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes an efficient and simple framework for learning document representations, named Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. The model learns these word embeddings in a manner that ensures this simple averaging produces a semantically meaningful document vector. The key mechanism is a corruption process, where words are randomly dropped from the document's global context during training. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption acts as an effective data-dependent regularizer. This regularization penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words, pushing them towards zero, while preserving the magnitude of embeddings for rare or informative words.

The paper demonstrates through experiments that Doc2VecC is highly efficient, capable of training on billions of words per hour on a single machine. The resulting document representations are shown to match or exceed the performance of several state-of-the-art methods, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks. Furthermore, the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are shown to be of higher quality than those from the standard Word2Vec model on a word analogy task.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Weak Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The technical novelty of the proposed method is limited. The core components—representing documents by averaging word embeddings and using dropout/corruption as a regularizer—are well-established techniques. The primary contribution is the specific combination and application of these ideas within a CBoW-like framework, which feels more incremental than groundbreaking.
2.  While the empirical results are strong on the selected tasks, the claims of general superiority are not fully substantiated. The evaluation on only two main document-level tasks (sentiment analysis and topic classification) is insufficient to conclude that this method produces generally superior document representations compared to more complex models like LSTMs or Skip-thought vectors, especially given its weaker performance on the sentence-level semantic relatedness task.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The main strength of the paper is its emphasis on simplicity and efficiency, which are highly desirable properties. The Doc2VecC model is well-motivated by the success of linear operations on word embeddings and the need for a scalable alternative to Paragraph Vectors that doesn't require a costly inference step for unseen documents. The empirical results on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia document classification tasks are impressive, with Doc2VecC outperforming a range of strong baselines. The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption process as a data-dependent regularizer, provides a nice theoretical justification for why this simple approach works well, particularly in down-weighting uninformative common words.

However, the novelty of the core technical ideas is a significant concern. Representing documents via averaged word embeddings is a very common and strong baseline. The use of corruption (effectively, a form of dropout on the input BoW vector) is also a standard technique for training robust models. The paper's contribution lies in integrating this corruption into the global context part of a CBoW-style objective and analyzing its regularizing effect, which builds directly on prior work (e.g., Wager et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014) that provided similar analyses for dropout. While the combination is effective, it does not feel like a significant conceptual advance in representation learning.

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation could be more comprehensive to fully support the paper's strong claims. The method excels on document-level classification tasks with relatively long documents (IMDB, Wikipedia). However, it performs less well than LSTM-based methods and Skip-thought Vectors on the SICK dataset, which involves shorter sentences. The authors hypothesize this is due to document length, but this suggests the method's effectiveness may be task- or domain-dependent. A convincing claim of a new state-of-the-art unsupervised document representation method requires evaluation on a wider suite of transfer learning tasks, demonstrating that the learned representations are truly generic and useful across different domains and downstream applications.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Evaluation Breadth:** To make a stronger case for the general utility of Doc2VecC representations, I would strongly encourage evaluating on a broader set of transfer tasks, similar to the evaluation protocol used for Skip-thought vectors. This would help establish how well the representations capture different facets of semantics beyond what is required for topic and sentiment classification.
*   **Strengthening Baselines:** The comparison could be made more compelling by including a more modern and widely-used baseline for sentence/document representation. A bidirectional LSTM where the document representation is formed by concatenating the final hidden states of the forward and backward passes is a very strong and common baseline. Comparing against such a model would provide a more calibrated assessment of Doc2VecC's performance.
*   **Analysis of Corruption Rate:** The paper states that q=0.9 was used throughout, and that the model is not sensitive to this hyperparameter. It would be helpful to include a small ablation study or a plot showing performance as a function of the corruption rate `q` to substantiate this claim.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

Your response to the following questions would help clarify my understanding of the experimental setup.

1.  Regarding the RNN-LM baseline in the sentiment analysis experiment (Section 4.2): Could you please clarify how this model was trained and used? Specifically, was it trained as a pure language model on the available text, or was it a supervised model fine-tuned for the classification task?
2.  Following up on the RNN-LM, how was the final document representation derived from the model? Was it the final hidden state of the RNN after processing the entire document, or an aggregation (e.g., average- or max-pooling) of the hidden states across all timesteps?
