- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents by averaging word embeddings with a corruption (random-drop) training process that acts as a data-dependent regularizer.  
- Corruption implicitly upweights rare/informative words and pushes common/non-discriminative word embeddings toward zero.  
- Claims major advantages: computational efficiency, scalability to massive datasets, corpus-size independent complexity, and extremely fast inference for new documents.  
- Reports strong empirical performance, often matching or outperforming Paragraph Vectors and RNNs on IMDB, Wikipedia, and SICK, especially for long-document classification.  
- Decision: Weak Accept.  
- Positive reason: method is simple, highly efficient, practically appealing, and gives impressive empirical results for long documents.  
- Negative reason: limited technical novelty—averaging embeddings and corruption/dropout are established techniques; contribution is mainly an effective combination/engineering.  
- The paper’s analysis frames corruption as an adaptive regularizer, but this connection parallels prior work on DAEs and regularization (e.g., Wager et al., Chen et al.).  
- Evaluation concerns: model excels on long documents but is outperformed by LSTMs on short sentence tasks, so general superiority is not fully established.  
- Suggestion: include a standard BiLSTM baseline (concatenated final forward/backward states) for fairer comparison with recurrent encoders.  
- Suggestion: perform ablation studies (e.g., remove local context, remove global corruption/CBOW variant) to isolate contributions of each component.  
- Clarification requested on the RNN-LM baseline: was it trained unsupervised as a language model or supervised end-to-end for classification?  
- Clarification requested on RNN representation: was the document representation the final hidden state or pooling (average/max) over hidden states?
