### **Summary**

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The model learns these word embeddings jointly with a document-level objective, leveraging a word2vec-style CBoW architecture. Specifically, it predicts a target word using both its local context window and a global document context. This global context is formed by averaging the embeddings of a randomly sampled subset of words from the full document. The authors show that this corruption mechanism, which is equivalent to applying dropout to the bag-of-words input, acts as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-informative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that this straightforward approach is not only extremely fast—both at training and, crucially, at inference time—but also achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to more complex state-of-the-art models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:
1.  The proposed method, despite its simplicity, demonstrates remarkably strong empirical performance across multiple benchmarks, often matching or exceeding more complex and computationally expensive models. Its efficiency at inference time is a significant practical advantage over methods like Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec).
2.  The paper provides a valuable insight by analyzing the word-level dropout mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer. This theoretical justification elevates the work beyond a simple empirical result and helps explain *why* such a simple combination of known techniques is so effective.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its compelling blend of simplicity, efficiency, and performance.

**Efficiency and Simplicity:** The proposed method is conceptually simple, building directly upon the well-understood word2vec CBoW architecture. The key difference is the addition of a global context vector derived from the document's words. Representing a document as the average of its word embeddings is a classic baseline, but this paper shows that jointly training the embeddings with this objective is highly effective. Critically, generating a vector for an unseen document requires only a single pass to look up and average word embeddings, a trivial operation. This stands in stark contrast to Paragraph Vectors, which requires a costly iterative inference process to derive a new document vector. The timing results in Table 2 clearly quantify this practical advantage, which is a significant contribution for real-world applications.

**Strong Empirical Results:** The claims of high performance are well-supported by the experiments. On the IMDB sentiment analysis task, Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art results, outperforming Paragraph Vectors and substantially beating the much more complex Skip-thought Vectors. On the Wikipedia document classification task, it consistently outperforms all baselines across different embedding dimensions. The analysis of the learned word embeddings on the word analogy task is also convincing, showing that the joint training process yields higher-quality word vectors than standard Word2Vec.

**Methodological Contribution:** While the individual components of the model are not novel—joint word/document training has precedent in Paragraph Vectors, compositional sum-of-words models are well-established, and the corruption is essentially input-layer dropout—their combination and the analysis thereof are valuable. The Taylor expansion in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as an adaptive regularizer that suppresses common, non-discriminative words, is a key contribution. It provides a principled explanation for the model's success and why it does not need heuristics like the subsampling of frequent words that is critical for standard word2vec. This analysis adds significant depth to the paper.

In summary, while the paper does not invent a new architectural paradigm, it demonstrates that a thoughtful and well-analyzed combination of simple, existing techniques can lead to a model that is superior in both performance and practicality to more complex state-of-the-art approaches. This is a valuable and important finding for the community.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Novelty:** The term "corruption model" is used throughout. While the analysis of its effect is novel and insightful, the mechanism itself is identical to standard dropout applied to the bag-of-words input. It might be beneficial to explicitly state this connection earlier in the paper to manage reader expectations and more precisely situate the contribution, which is the application and analysis of this technique in this specific context, rather than the invention of a new corruption process.
*   **Ablation Study:** The joint training of word and document representations seems crucial. An interesting ablation would be to compare against a two-stage process: 1) Train standard word2vec embeddings, 2) Freeze them and learn only a document-level composition function with the same corruption objective. This could help isolate and quantify the benefits of the end-to-end joint training.
*   **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** In Section 4.2, you mention that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and that `q=0.9` was used. This is a strong claim. Could you include a small plot or table in an appendix showing the performance on a validation set (e.g., IMDB error rate) for different values of `q` (e.g., 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.9)? This would provide stronger evidence for this robustness.
*   **Document Length Hypothesis:** You hypothesize that Doc2VecC is better for longer documents (IMDB) while RNN-based models like Skip-thought are better for shorter sentences (SICK). This is a plausible and interesting point. To make this argument more concrete, could you provide an analysis where you stratify the IMDB test set by document length and plot the error rate for Doc2VecC and Skip-thought Vectors as a function of length? This would provide direct evidence for your hypothesis.
