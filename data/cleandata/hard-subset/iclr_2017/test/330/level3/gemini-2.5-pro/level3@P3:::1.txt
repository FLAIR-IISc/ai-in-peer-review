### Review of "Document Vector through Corruption"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes an efficient and effective method for learning distributed document representations, called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The core idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. This document vector is then used as a global context, in addition to the traditional local word context, to predict a target word in a skip-gram style objective. The key innovation is the "corruption" mechanism, where words are randomly dropped from the document when forming the global context vector for each training step. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, showing that this corruption process is equivalent to a data-dependent regularization term. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of frequent but non-informative words, effectively forcing the model to rely on more discriminative terms. The paper demonstrates that this simple and efficient architecture produces high-quality document and word embeddings, achieving competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks compared to several baselines.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper for two primary reasons:

1.  The proposed method, Doc2VecC, is an elegant, efficient, and well-motivated model for learning document representations. It cleverly combines existing ideas (word averaging, skip-gram objective, dropout) into a novel framework that directly addresses major drawbacks of prior work like Paragraph Vectors, namely the need for per-document parameters and expensive inference on unseen documents.
2.  The paper provides a strong theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism, connecting it to an explicit data-dependent regularizer. This analysis in Section 3 provides valuable insight into *why* the model works and justifies the architectural choice beyond simple empirical performance, which is a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strengths of this paper lie in the model's design and the accompanying analysis. Doc2VecC builds on the highly successful and efficient Word2Vec architecture, making it easy to understand, implement, and scale. By defining the document vector as a composition of word vectors, it avoids the linear growth in parameters with corpus size that affects Paragraph Vectors. This also makes inference for unseen documents trivial and fast—a simple averaging of word vectors—which is a major practical advantage demonstrated clearly in Table 2.

The analysis in Section 3.1 is a standout feature. Showing that dropout on the input document words approximates a regularizer that penalizes common words is an excellent piece of analysis. It provides a principled explanation for the model's effectiveness and moves beyond the typical "dropout is a regularizer" hand-waving argument to show the specific form of this regularization. This is strongly supported by the qualitative results in Table 3, which show Doc2VecC successfully pushes the embeddings of stop words towards zero, unlike Word2Vec or Paragraph Vectors.

However, the paper's empirical evaluation, while generally positive, has some weaknesses. The experiments are limited to sentiment analysis, k-way classification, and semantic relatedness. While the results are strong, the paper would be more convincing if the representations were tested on a more diverse set of tasks, such as information retrieval or document clustering, to better substantiate claims of generality.

Furthermore, some of the comparisons feel weak. For instance, the performance of the Paragraph Vectors baseline on the Wikipedia classification task (Table 5) seems surprisingly low, raising questions about whether it was strongly tuned. The comparison against a simple `Word2Vec+AVG` is a necessary sanity check, but the field could benefit from comparisons against more sophisticated compositional baselines.

Finally, while the t-SNE visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 are illustrative, they occupy a significant amount of space that could have been better used for deeper empirical analysis. For example, an ablation study systematically examining the effect of the corruption rate `q`, or the relative contribution of the local versus global context, would have provided more robust support for the model's design choices.

Despite these empirical weaknesses, the strengths of the proposed method—its simplicity, efficiency, and the insightful theoretical justification—are significant enough to warrant acceptance. The model is likely to be highly useful to other researchers and practitioners.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Expand Evaluation:** To better prove the general-purpose nature of the learned vectors, consider adding experiments on tasks like document retrieval or clustering. This would provide a more comprehensive picture of the representation quality.
*   **Strengthen Baselines:** Providing more detail on the hyperparameter tuning for all baseline models would increase confidence in the comparisons. If possible, comparing against other contemporary methods for compositional document representations would further contextualize your results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit greatly from an ablation study. For example: How does performance change as the corruption rate `q` varies? What is the performance if only the global context is used (i.e., local context window is size 0)? This would offer deeper insight into the model's behavior.
*   **Space Management:** Consider moving one of the t-SNE plots (e.g., Figure 4) to an appendix to make room for the suggested ablation studies or a more detailed error analysis.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you please elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process for the baseline models, particularly Paragraph Vectors? The reported performance on the classification task was lower than I might have expected, and understanding the experimental setup would help clarify the comparison.
*   The analysis in Section 3.1 relies on a second-order Taylor approximation. How sensitive is this approximation? Does the model's empirical behavior (e.g., the L2 norms of word vectors for varying frequencies) closely match the behavior predicted by the derived regularizer `R(uj)`?
*   Regarding the semantic relatedness task in Section 4.5, could you provide more detail on the fine-tuning process on the SICK dataset? Was the entire model (including pre-trained word vectors from the book corpus) updated, or were only the embeddings for the new, out-of-vocabulary words trained?
