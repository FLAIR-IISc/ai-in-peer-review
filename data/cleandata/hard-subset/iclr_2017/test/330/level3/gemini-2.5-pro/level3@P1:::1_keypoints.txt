- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings, trained with a CBoW-style objective using local and corrupted global context.  
- Global context formed by averaging embeddings from a randomly corrupted version of the document (many words dropped).  
- Theoretical analysis shows corruption ≈ a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes embeddings of frequent, non-informative words.  
- Method is simple, highly efficient, and scalable; model size depends on vocabulary only and inference is a fast averaging step.  
- Training and inference are orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors.  
- Empirical results competitive or superior to state-of-the-art on sentiment, document classification, semantic relatedness, and notably word analogy.  
- Corruption mechanism helps learn higher-quality word embeddings and down-weight stop words without heuristics like IDF.  
- Qualitative evidence (Table 3) shows Doc2VecC pushes frequent uninformative word embeddings toward zero compared to PV/Word2Vec.  
- Evaluation is reasonably broad (four tasks) and uses solid baselines for comparison.  
- Recommendation: accept, based on practical effectiveness and strong theoretical justification.  
- Suggestion: include an ablation study (e.g., Doc2VecC with q=0) to quantify the corruption effect.  
- Suggestion: provide more details on baseline hyperparameter tuning, especially Paragraph Vectors on Wikipedia classification.  
- Suggestion: consider replacing some t-SNE space with more analysis (ablation, error analysis, or q sensitivity).  
- Question: what is performance when trained without corruption (q=0) but using the global average context?  
- Question: provide a sensitivity analysis of corruption rate q (performance curve for q from 0 to 0.99).  
- Question: have you tried applying the corruption-based global context to a Skip-gram architecture, and would benefits transfer?
