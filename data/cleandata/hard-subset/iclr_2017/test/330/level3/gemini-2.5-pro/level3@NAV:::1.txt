This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its word embeddings. These embeddings are learned using a skip-gram style objective that predicts a target word from both its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. The key contribution is how this global context is formed: by taking an average of word vectors from a "corrupted" version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The paper provides a compelling theoretical analysis showing this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer, penalizing frequent, non-informative words.

I recommend that this paper be **Accepted**. The primary reasons are the elegance of the proposed method and the valuable analysis of its behavior. While the constituent parts—skip-gram, dropout, and context vectors—are not new, their composition into a single, efficient model for learning document representations is novel and well-motivated. The model directly addresses the shortcomings of prior work like Paragraph Vectors by being much faster at inference time for unseen documents. The detailed analysis in Section 3, which frames the corruption mechanism as an explicit regularizer, is a significant strength, providing a clear explanation for why the model works and lending strong support to the authors' claims.

The primary weakness of the paper is its empirical evaluation. The experiments are limited to sentiment analysis and k-way classification tasks. The choice of baselines is also a concern; comparing against more recent and powerful models would provide a better sense of where Doc2VecC stands. For instance, methods based on averaging embeddings from large pre-trained language models have become strong baselines. Furthermore, too much space is dedicated to t-SNE projections, which could have been better used for a deeper quantitative analysis or ablation studies.

For improvement, I would suggest expanding the evaluation to include tasks like document retrieval or clustering to test the generality of the representations. Strengthening the baselines would also make the paper's contributions clearer.

Finally, I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of baselines? How do you see your method positioned against more recent, transformer-based baselines, considering the trade-off between efficiency and performance?
2.  Your analysis of corruption as regularization is insightful. Have you explored other corruption strategies (e.g., based on IDF) and does your theoretical framework extend to them?
3.  The performance of Skip-thought vectors seems unusually low on the IMDB dataset. Can you provide further analysis to support the claim that this is due to document length?
