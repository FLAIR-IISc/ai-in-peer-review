### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and simple framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The novelty of the approach lies in its training procedure, which incorporates a corruption model that randomly removes words from the document's global context. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process acts as a data-dependent regularizer. This regularization scheme is shown to favor informative or rare words while pushing the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words towards zero. The authors evaluate Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that this simple and computationally efficient model achieves performance that is competitive with or superior to several state-of-the-art baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors. Key strengths of the proposed method are its speed, scalability, and the high quality of the word embeddings it produces as a byproduct.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Weak Reject**

My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** While the combination of techniques and the analysis are well-presented, the core components of the model—representing documents as an average of word embeddings and using a corruption/dropout mechanism for regularization—are not new. The main contribution is the specific application and theoretical justification, which, while valuable, feels more incremental than groundbreaking.
2.  **Insufficient Empirical Evidence for General Superiority:** The paper makes strong claims about matching or outperforming the state-of-the-art. While the results on the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks are impressive, the evidence from only two primary tasks is not sufficient to fully substantiate these broad claims. The model's weaker performance on the SICK dataset compared to LSTM-based methods further suggests its superiority is task-dependent.

### Supporting Arguments

The main strength of this paper is its simplicity and the compelling motivation behind the corruption-as-regularization idea. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption process as an explicit data-dependent regularizer, is insightful and provides a solid justification for why this simple method works well. The experiments effectively demonstrate the model's key advantages: it is extremely fast to train and test, and it achieves excellent results on the tasks presented. The analysis in Table 3, showing how Doc2VecC effectively down-weights uninformative but frequent words, is a nice qualitative validation of the method's mechanics.

However, the paper's contributions must be weighed against existing work. The idea of averaging word embeddings to form a document vector is a widely-used baseline, often with IDF weighting (which the paper compares against). The use of corruption to learn robust representations is the foundation of Denoising Autoencoders, which are also included as a baseline. The paper’s main novelty lies in combining these ideas within a predictive CBoW-style framework and analyzing the implicit regularization. While this is a neat contribution, it feels like a clever engineering refinement and analysis of existing components rather than a fundamentally new approach to document representation.

Furthermore, the empirical evaluation, while strong on the selected tasks, does not fully support the claim of general-purpose, state-of-the-art representations. The model excels on document-level classification tasks (IMDB, Wikipedia) where an aggregated "bag-of-concepts" representation is effective. However, on the SICK dataset, which requires a more nuanced understanding of sentence structure and semantics, it falls short of LSTM-based models and Skip-thought vectors. The authors hypothesize this is due to document length, which is a plausible explanation, but it also weakens the claim of general applicability. To be truly convincing as a state-of-the-art general document encoder, the method should be evaluated on a more diverse set of tasks, such as information retrieval or document clustering, where the quality of the vector space is tested in different ways.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision.

1.  **Strengthening Baselines:** The comparison to an RNN-LM is welcome, but a more standard and powerful baseline for sentence/document representation today is a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM). A simple BiLSTM encoder where the final forward and backward hidden states are concatenated often provides a very strong baseline. Comparing Doc2VecC against such a model would provide a more robust assessment of its performance, especially on the sentiment analysis task.
2.  **Expanding Evaluation:** To better support the claims of creating a general-purpose representation, I would encourage the authors to add experiments on at least one other type of task. For instance, a document clustering task could demonstrate how well the learned representations separate documents in an unsupervised setting. An information retrieval task could also be a compelling addition.
3.  **Ablation Study on Corruption:** The paper uses a fixed corruption rate `q=0.9`. While the authors state the model is not sensitive to this parameter, an ablation study showing performance across a range of `q` values (e.g., from 0.1 to 0.9) would strengthen this claim and provide more insight into the model's behavior. It would be particularly interesting to see if there is a trade-off between training speed and performance.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding of the experiments, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Regarding the RNN-LM baseline in the sentiment analysis experiment: was this model trained purely as a language model on the unlabeled data with features extracted afterward, or was it trained end-to-end on the labeled training set to minimize classification error? Furthermore, was the final hidden state used as the document representation, or an average of all hidden states? The details of this baseline are important for a fair comparison.
2.  In Section 4.5, you mention fine-tuning the model on the SICK dataset without using the relatedness scores. Could you elaborate on the objective function used for this fine-tuning? Was it the same CBoW-style predictive objective used for the initial training on the book corpus?
