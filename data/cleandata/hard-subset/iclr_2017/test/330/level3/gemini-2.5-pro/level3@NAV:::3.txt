### Review of "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a simple and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The central idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of its constituent word embeddings. These embeddings are learned jointly in a word2vec-style predictive task, where the document vector provides a global context to aid in predicting a target word from its local context. A key component of the training process is a corruption mechanism—randomly dropping out words from the document context—which the authors show acts as a data-dependent regularizer that favors informative words. The paper claims this approach matches or exceeds the performance of more complex state-of-the-art models on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, while being significantly faster to train and, most notably, to generate embeddings for unseen documents at inference time.

**Recommendation: Accept**

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The primary reasons are the model's compelling combination of simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance. While the methodological novelty is limited, the practical value of a simple and effective baseline in this space is significant and warrants the attention of the community.

**Supporting Arguments**

The main strength of this work lies in its impressive results, achieved with a surprisingly simple architecture. The efficiency at inference is a major practical advantage over competing methods like the original Doc2Vec (Paragraph Vectors), which requires a costly optimization step for new documents. Doc2VecC, by contrast, only needs to average the pre-computed embeddings of words in the new document. The empirical evaluation is solid, demonstrating competitive or superior performance on standard sentiment and classification benchmarks against more complex models.

However, the paper's claims of novelty should be tempered. The core components are not, in themselves, new. Representing documents as a sum or average of word vectors is a well-established baseline. Similarly, the "corruption model" is effectively a standard implementation of input-layer dropout, a widely used technique. The training objective also closely follows the established negative sampling setup from word2vec. The true contribution here is not the invention of new components, but rather the demonstration that their judicious combination results in a highly effective and efficient system that challenges more complex state-of-the-art approaches.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

To strengthen the paper, I would encourage the authors to more explicitly position their work as an effective synthesis of existing ideas rather than presenting the components as entirely novel. Acknowledging the precedent for sum-of-words representations and input dropout would clarify that the paper's primary contribution is empirical and practical. Doing so would not weaken the paper; on the contrary, it would strengthen its core message about the power of simple, well-executed baselines. Furthermore, the analysis in Section 3.1 connecting dropout to data-dependent regularization is insightful; expanding on this with a small-scale experiment visualizing the effect on word norm distributions for different dropout rates (`q`) could be a valuable addition.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  The Taylor expansion in Section 3.1 provides a compelling theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism. Could you provide further intuition on why this implicit data-dependent regularization is more effective than simply applying a standard L2 penalty to the embeddings during training?
2.  Your hypothesis that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to document length is plausible. Have you considered evaluating Doc2VecC on a benchmark with very short texts (e.g., tweet classification) where recurrent models typically excel, to better delineate the performance boundaries of your approach?
