- Proposes Doc2VecC: document vectors as averages of word embeddings trained with random-word corruption acting as data-dependent regularizer.  
- Theoretical analysis claims corruption favors informative/rare words and pushes common words' embeddings toward zero.  
- Empirically competitive on IMDB sentiment and Wikipedia classification; fast and scalable with high-quality word embeddings.  
- Decision: Weak reject.  
- Primary reason 1: Limited technical novelty—averaging embeddings and corruption/dropout ideas are incremental and not fundamentally new.  
- Primary reason 2: Insufficient empirical evidence—evaluation on only a few tasks and weaker performance on SICK questions generality and superiority claims.  
- Strength: Simple method with insightful theoretical framing and clear qualitative evidence of down-weighting frequent uninformative words.  
- Weakness: Similarities to existing baselines (IDF-weighted averages, denoising autoencoders) make contribution feel like engineering refinement.  
- Weakness: Method seems task-dependent—works well for document-level classification but underperforms on sentence-level semantic tasks (SICK).  
- Recommendation: Compare against stronger baselines such as BiLSTM encoders for a fairer assessment.  
- Recommendation: Expand evaluation to other tasks (e.g., document clustering, information retrieval) to support general-purpose claims.  
- Recommendation: Provide an ablation study varying corruption rate q (e.g., 0.1–0.9) to validate robustness and trade-offs.  
- Question 1 for authors: Clarify how the RNN-LM baseline was trained (language model vs. end-to-end classification) and which hidden-state representation was used.  
- Question 2 for authors: Specify the objective used for SICK fine-tuning—was it the same CBoW-style predictive objective as initial training?
