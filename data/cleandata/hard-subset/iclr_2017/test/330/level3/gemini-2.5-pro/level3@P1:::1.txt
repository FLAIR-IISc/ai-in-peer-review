This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The model is trained using a CBoW-style objective, where the model predicts a target word from its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. The key contribution lies in how this global context is formed: it is an average of word embeddings from a "corrupted" version of the document, where a significant fraction of words have been randomly dropped out. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption mechanism is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes the embeddings of frequent but non-informative words. The paper demonstrates that this simple and highly efficient method achieves results that are competitive with or superior to state-of-the-art models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors on tasks including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, while also producing higher-quality word embeddings.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  The proposed method is simple, highly efficient, and effective. It provides a practical and scalable alternative to more complex models like Paragraph Vectors, especially in its training and inference speed.
2.  The paper provides a valuable theoretical justification for its core mechanism (corruption), connecting it to a data-dependent regularization scheme. This analysis provides significant insight into why the method works and elevates the paper beyond a purely empirical contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of Doc2VecC is its elegant simplicity and efficiency. By representing a document as an average of word embeddings, it avoids the major drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (PV), namely that PV's parameter count scales with the corpus size and that generating representations for unseen documents requires an expensive inference step. Doc2VecC's model complexity depends only on the vocabulary size, and inference is a fast averaging operation. The training times reported in Table 2 highlight this practical advantage, showing it to be orders of magnitude faster to train and test than models like PV or Skip-thought vectors. Despite this simplicity, the empirical results are strong, matching or beating PV on several tasks. This makes Doc2VecC a compelling choice for large-scale applications.

The second major strength is the analysis in Section 3.1. The authors use a Taylor expansion to show that the stochastic corruption process is approximately equivalent to adding a specific regularization term to the objective function. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the norms of embeddings for words that are frequent but not predictive, effectively learning to down-weight stop words and other non-discriminative terms without heuristics like IDF weighting or explicit stop-word removal. This provides a principled explanation for the model's effectiveness and is a significant contribution to our understanding of such embedding methods. The qualitative results in Table 3 provide strong evidence supporting this analysis, showing that Doc2VecC successfully pushes the embeddings of frequent, uninformative words towards zero, while PV and Word2Vec do so for rare (and potentially useful) words.

The empirical evaluation is reasonably broad, covering four different tasks (sentiment, classification, word analogy, semantic relatedness) and comparing against a solid set of baselines for the time, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors. The results demonstrate that Doc2VecC is a strong performer across the board. The significant improvement on the word analogy task over both Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors is particularly impressive and suggests that the model learns a more robust semantic space for individual words.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, the following points could help improve it further. These are intended as constructive suggestions and are not critical to my decision.

1.  **Ablation Study:** The core novelty is the corruption mechanism. The paper's claims would be significantly strengthened by a direct ablation study. For example, a comparison against a "Doc2VecC (no corruption)" baseline, which would be equivalent to setting the corruption probability `q=0` but is otherwise identical to the proposed model, would precisely quantify the contribution of the data-dependent regularization.

2.  **Baseline Performance:** The performance reported for Paragraph Vectors on the Wikipedia classification task (Table 5) seems somewhat low. Could the authors provide more details on the hyperparameter search for the baselines? Ensuring that all baselines are strongly tuned is crucial for making convincing claims of superiority.

3.  **Use of Space:** The t-SNE visualizations in Figures 3 and 4 are illustrative, but they take up a considerable amount of space. This space might have been better used for a more detailed analysis, such as the suggested ablation study, an error analysis, or a sensitivity analysis of the corruption rate `q`.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Following up on the suggestion for an ablation study, what is the performance of the model if you train it without the corruption step (`q=0`) but still use the global average context vector? This would directly test the efficacy of the proposed regularization.

2.  The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate and uses `q=0.9` throughout. This is a very high rate of corruption. Could you provide a sensitivity analysis, perhaps a plot of performance on a validation set versus different values of `q`? It would be interesting to see the performance curve as `q` varies from 0 to 0.99.

3.  The analysis and experiments are based on the CBoW architecture. Have you experimented with applying the corruption-based global context to a Skip-gram style architecture? Do you expect the same benefits to transfer?
