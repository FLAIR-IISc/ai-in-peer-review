- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings trained with a corruption (word-dropout) mechanism.  
- The corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that pushes frequent, non-discriminative word embeddings toward zero while preserving informative ones.  
- Provides theoretical analysis justifying the regularization effect of corruption.  
- Claims high efficiency (training billions of words per hour on a single machine) and strong empirical results on sentiment and document classification.  
- Shows learned word embeddings outperform Word2Vec on a word analogy task.  
- Decision: Weak reject.  
- Main concern: technical novelty is limited—averaging embeddings and input corruption/dropout are well-established techniques.  
- Main concern: empirical claims of general superiority are not fully substantiated—evaluation is narrow (mostly long-document tasks) and weaker on sentence-level semantic relatedness (SICK).  
- Strength: simplicity and scalability provide a practical, inference-free alternative to Paragraph Vectors.  
- Strength: theoretical framing explains why the method down-weights uninformative common words.  
- Weakness: contribution feels incremental, building on prior dropout analyses (e.g., Wager et al., Chen et al.).  
- Weakness: evaluation should be broader to support claims of general-purpose representations (e.g., transfer tasks like Skip-thought protocol).  
- Suggested improvement: include stronger baselines such as bidirectional LSTM document encoders.  
- Suggested improvement: provide an ablation or plot showing sensitivity to corruption rate q (currently fixed at 0.9).  
- Questions for authors: clarify how the RNN-LM baseline was trained (pure LM or supervised fine-tuned) and how its document representation was extracted (final hidden state vs. aggregation).
