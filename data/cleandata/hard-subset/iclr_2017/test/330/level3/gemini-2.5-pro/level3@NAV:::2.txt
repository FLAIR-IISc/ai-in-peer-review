This paper introduces Doc2VecC, an efficient and simple method for learning document representations. The core contribution is to represent documents as a simple average of word embeddings, guided by a data-dependent regularization scheme introduced through a "corruption" process during training. This regularization is shown to upweight rare, informative words while effectively shrinking the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words toward zero. The authors report that this straightforward approach achieves state-of-the-art or competitive error rates on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, while being significantly faster to train and deploy than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors.

Decision: Reject

My decision is based on two primary reasons: 1) the technical contribution, while effective, is limited in novelty, and 2) the empirical evaluation is not yet comprehensive enough to fully support the paper's claims of general superiority.

The main strengths of the proposed model are its simplicity and computational efficiency, which are well-motivated and clearly demonstrated. However, the core techniques—representing documents by averaging word embeddings and using a corruption/dropout mechanism—are established concepts in the literature. While the analysis framing the corruption as a data-dependent regularizer is insightful, the overall technical depth feels incremental. The primary contribution appears to be a skillful combination of existing ideas rather than a fundamentally new approach. Furthermore, the empirical evidence, while strong on the tasks presented, is confined to sentiment analysis and a single document classification benchmark. To convincingly claim general-purpose superiority, the method's effectiveness must be demonstrated across a broader range of NLP tasks.

To improve the paper, I suggest further experiments to strengthen the empirical claims. Evaluating on more diverse tasks, such as information retrieval or semantic textual similarity on additional datasets, would provide more robust evidence of the model's general applicability. I also strongly recommend including a comparison against a bidirectional LSTM that uses a concatenation of the final forward and backward hidden states. This is a powerful and widely-used baseline for document representation that is currently missing from the evaluation.

Finally, to help clarify my understanding of the current experiments, could you please provide more details on the RNN-LM baseline? Specifically, was it trained solely as a language model, or was it fine-tuned to minimize classification error for the downstream tasks? Additionally, was the final hidden state of the RNN used as the document representation, or was it an average of the hidden states across all timesteps?
