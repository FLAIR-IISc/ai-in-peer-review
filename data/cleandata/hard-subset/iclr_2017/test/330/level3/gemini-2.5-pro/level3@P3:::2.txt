### Review of "Document Vector through Corruption"

**1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and effective framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The model's key contribution is a corruption process applied during training, where words are randomly dropped from the document context. The authors show that this mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer, which implicitly upweights rare and informative words while pushing the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words towards zero. The paper claims several advantages for this approach: it is computationally efficient, scaling to massive datasets; its complexity is independent of the corpus size; and inference for new documents is extremely fast. Empirically, the authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), and semantic relatedness (SICK) tasks, often outperforming more complex models like Paragraph Vectors and RNN-based approaches.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Weak Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed method is simple, highly efficient, and demonstrates impressively strong empirical performance on the tasks presented, particularly for long-document classification. Its speed and simplicity make it a very practical and appealing baseline.
2.  However, the technical novelty of the method is limited. The core components—averaging word embeddings and using corruption/dropout as a regularizer—are well-established techniques. The primary contribution lies in the effective combination and application of these existing ideas within a Word2Vec-like framework, which feels more like a strong engineering contribution than a fundamental research advance.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The main strengths of this paper are its clear motivation and compelling results. The goal of creating a document representation model that is as fast as Word2Vec but more powerful than a simple average of pre-trained embeddings is well-justified. The proposed Doc2VecC model elegantly achieves this. The speed, both in training and inference, is a significant practical advantage over methods like Paragraph Vectors (which requires a costly inference step) and sequence models like LSTMs (which can be slow for long documents). The empirical results, especially the low error rates on the IMDB and Wikipedia datasets, strongly support the claim that this simple compositional approach can be very powerful. The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption process as an adaptive regularizer, provides a nice intuition for why the model works well.

Despite these strengths, my enthusiasm is tempered by the limited technical novelty. The idea of representing a document by averaging word embeddings is a standard, often-used baseline. The central mechanism, corruption, is conceptually identical to the input corruption in Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs) or dropout. The authors themselves acknowledge this connection and show that their model reduces to a DAE if the local context is removed. Furthermore, the mathematical analysis showing that corruption is approximately equivalent to a specific regularizer has been previously established in the literature for DAEs and logistic regression (e.g., Wager et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014), which the authors cite. Therefore, the novelty is not in the technique itself, but in its specific application to jointly learn word embeddings and a compositional document representation. While this is a clever and effective combination, it does not introduce a fundamentally new concept to the field.

Furthermore, while the paper reports results on three tasks, the claim of general superiority would be strengthened by a broader evaluation. The model shows its best performance on long document classification tasks (IMDB, Wikipedia) but is outperformed by LSTM-based methods on the short sentence semantic relatedness task (SICK). This suggests that the model's effectiveness might be tied to specific data characteristics. Testing on a wider variety of NLP tasks (e.g., information retrieval, question answering) would be necessary to be fully convinced of its general applicability as a universal document encoder.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I would appreciate clarification on some experimental details.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Stronger Baselines:** For the document classification tasks, it would be beneficial to compare against a standard bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) baseline where the document representation is formed by concatenating the final forward and backward hidden states. This is a widely used and powerful baseline for document/sentence encoding and would provide a more complete comparison against recurrent models.
*   **Ablation Study:** A more direct ablation study could further strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, comparing the full Doc2VecC model against a version without the local context (which you state is similar to a DAE) and a version without the global (corrupted) context (i.e., standard CBOW) on the same tasks would more clearly isolate the contribution of each component of your architecture.

**Questions for Clarification:**
*   In the sentiment analysis experiments (Section 4.2), you include an "RNN-LM" as a baseline. Could you please provide more details on this model?
    1.  Was this model trained purely as a language model on the unlabeled data, with its features then used in a classifier? Or was it a supervised model trained end-to-end to minimize classification error on the labeled training set? The text refers to it as a "supervised RNN-LM", suggesting the latter, but clarification would be helpful.
    2.  How was the final document representation extracted from the RNN? Was it the final hidden state, or an average/max-pooling over all hidden states? These choices can significantly impact performance.
