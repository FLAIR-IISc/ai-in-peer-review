- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent a document as the average of its word embeddings learned jointly via a CBoW-style objective with a corrupted (subsampled) global context.  
- Corruption (random word dropout) is equivalent to input-layer dropout and acts as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes frequent, non-informative words.  
- The method is extremely efficient at inference: unseen document vectors require only a single pass of lookup-and-average, unlike Paragraph Vectors’ costly iterative inference.  
- Empirically competitive or superior to more complex models (Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought) on sentiment analysis and document classification benchmarks.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on IMDB sentiment and consistently outperforms baselines on Wikipedia classification across embedding sizes.  
- Joint training yields higher-quality word embeddings (better on word analogy tasks) than standard Word2Vec.  
- The combination of simple components (CBoW, averaging, dropout) plus analysis is the paper’s methodological value rather than a novel architectural invention.  
- The Taylor expansion analysis in Section 3.1 provides a principled explanation for the corruption’s adaptive regularization effect.  
- The paper’s strengths are its simplicity, practical efficiency, strong empirical performance, and theoretical insight into the dropout-like corruption.  
- Suggest explicitly stating the connection to standard dropout earlier to clarify novelty and set reader expectations.  
- Recommend an ablation comparing end-to-end joint training vs. two-stage (pretrained word2vec then frozen composition) to quantify joint training benefits.  
- Ask for sensitivity analysis of corruption rate q (report performance across multiple q values) to support the robustness claim.  
- Propose stratifying results by document length to test the hypothesis that Doc2VecC excels on longer documents while RNNs like Skip-thought perform better on short sentences.
