### Summary

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple, efficient, and effective method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document vector as the simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. Crucially, these word embeddings are learned jointly within a framework that uses this averaged document vector as a global context to predict words, similar to the CBOW architecture of Word2Vec. A key element of the proposed method is a corruption process, where a random subset of words from the document is used to form the global context at each training step. The authors provide a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer, encouraging the model to learn meaningful embeddings for rare, informative words while shrinking the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words towards zero. The paper presents strong empirical results across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that this simple model matches or outperforms more complex state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, while being significantly faster to train and, most importantly, to use for inference on unseen documents.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The primary reasons are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance and Efficiency:** The proposed method, despite its architectural simplicity, achieves results that are competitive with or superior to much more complex models on several standard benchmarks. Its efficiency, particularly the extremely fast inference time for generating vectors for new documents by simple averaging, represents a significant practical advantage over methods like Paragraph Vectors that require expensive optimization at test time.
2.  **Valuable Contribution of Simplicity:** The paper demonstrates convincingly that a simple, well-motivated combination of existing ideas (averaging embeddings, dropout, CBOW-style training) can create a powerful and highly practical tool for document representation. This finding is valuable for the community, offering a strong baseline that is both effective and computationally inexpensive.

### Supporting Arguments

The main contribution of this paper is not a single novel component, but rather the highly effective and elegant synthesis of several ideas into a cohesive framework. While representing documents as an average of word embeddings is not new, nor is the use of dropout on inputs, the authors show that *jointly* training the word embeddings with this specific objective and corruption process yields surprisingly powerful results.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and provides compelling support for the paper's claims.
*   On the IMDB sentiment analysis task (Table 1), Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming Paragraph Vectors and substantially beating Word2Vec+IDF and Skip-thought Vectors. The analysis of training and inference times (Table 2) highlights the model's key practical advantage: inference for 25,000 documents takes seconds, compared to several minutes for Paragraph Vectors and hours for Skip-thought Vectors.
*   On the Wikipedia document classification task (Table 5), Doc2VecC again outperforms all baselines across different embedding dimensions, and the t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) provide a clear qualitative illustration of the superior separability of its learned representations compared to Doc2Vec.
*   The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption as a data-dependent regularizer, provides a principled explanation for the model's success. This theoretical justification elevates the work beyond a simple empirical observation. The qualitative results in Table 3, showing that Doc2VecC correctly pushes the embeddings of frequent, uninformative words towards zero while preserving those of rare but informative words, provide strong evidence for this regularization effect.
*   Furthermore, the paper shows that the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are of higher quality than those from standard Word2Vec, as demonstrated by the significant improvements on the word analogy task (Table 4). This indicates that forcing the embeddings to be composable into a meaningful document vector improves their individual semantic quality.

While one could argue that the methodological novelty is limited, the paper's value lies in its demonstration that much of the complexity in recent models may not be necessary. The combination of simplicity, speed, and high performance makes Doc2VecC an excellent and highly practical method that is likely to be widely adopted.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Framing of "Corruption":** The term "corruption" and its presentation as a novel mechanism could be slightly misleading. The process described in equation (1) is functionally equivalent to applying dropout to a bag-of-words input vector. It would improve clarity to acknowledge this connection explicitly. The contribution is not dropout itself, but its successful application and analysis within this specific document representation learning framework. Reframing this would strengthen the paper by placing it more clearly in the context of existing techniques.
*   **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper states that the model is not sensitive to `q` and uses `q=0.9` throughout. This is a very high rate of dropout. Including a small ablation study or a graph showing performance (e.g., classification error) as a function of `q` would substantiate this claim and provide valuable practical guidance for practitioners looking to apply the model.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  In Section 3.1, you provide a compelling analysis of the regularization effect. You also note that Doc2VecC does not rely on the frequent-word subsampling trick used by Word2Vec. Could you provide a direct experimental comparison of the two mechanisms? For instance, an ablation showing the performance of Doc2VecC with/without subsampling versus Word2Vec+AVG with/without subsampling would make the benefits of the implicit regularization clearer.
2.  The hypothesis that Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset due to long document lengths is plausible. Have you considered a simple experiment to test this, for example, by training and evaluating Doc2VecC on documents truncated to the average length seen in the Skip-thought paper's experiments?
3.  The analysis of the regularizer R(uj) suggests that it penalizes frequent words unless they are "critical to a confident prediction." Could you provide some examples of words that are very frequent in the corpus but whose embeddings are *not* pushed towards zero, thereby illustrating cases where the model has identified a common word as being highly informative?
