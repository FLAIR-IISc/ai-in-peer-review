## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as the average of their word embeddings, enhanced with a corruption mechanism during training. The core idea – leveraging the simplicity and efficiency of averaging word embeddings – is well-motivated by prior work demonstrating the compositional nature of word embeddings. The corruption step, while framed as a novel regularization technique, is essentially a form of dropout applied to the input layer. The training process utilizes established methods like word2vec-style loss, limiting methodological novelty.

Despite these limitations, I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The strength lies in the compelling empirical results and the practical benefits of the approach. Doc2VecC achieves strong performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, matching or exceeding state-of-the-art results. More importantly, the model’s efficiency – particularly its fast document generation time – is a significant advantage, especially for large-scale applications. The authors convincingly demonstrate that the simplicity of the model doesn’t come at the cost of performance. 

The paper effectively addresses the problem of efficient document representation learning. The approach is well-motivated by the observation that averaging word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning, and the corruption mechanism is a reasonable attempt to improve the quality of these representations. The results presented strongly support the claims of improved performance and efficiency. The inclusion of detailed experimental results, comparisons to strong baselines, and an ablation study (demonstrating the effect of the corruption mechanism) adds to the rigor of the evaluation.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors: 
*   Elaborate on the choice of the corruption rate *q*. Was this value empirically determined, and if so, how?
*   Discuss the limitations of the approach. Are there specific types of documents or tasks where Doc2VecC might underperform compared to more complex models?
*   Provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned word embeddings. Are there specific patterns or characteristics that distinguish them from those learned by Word2Vec?
*   Could the authors comment on the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter choices beyond the corruption rate?



I would appreciate clarification on how the authors addressed potential issues with out-of-vocabulary words during training and testing, particularly given the use of pre-trained word embeddings. Also, could they elaborate on the computational resources used for training and inference, providing more specific details about the hardware configuration?
