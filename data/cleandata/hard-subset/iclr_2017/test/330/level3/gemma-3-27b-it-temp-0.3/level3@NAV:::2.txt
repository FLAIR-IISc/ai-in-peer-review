## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization. The regularization upweights rare/informative words and drives common words towards zero, aiming to improve document representation quality. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results in some cases, while also highlighting the efficiency of their approach.

I recommend **Reject**ing this paper in its current form. While the motivation is sound and the approach is clearly presented, the technical contribution feels incremental. The core idea of averaging word embeddings is not novel, and the added regularization, while potentially beneficial, builds upon established techniques. The primary strengths of the paper lie in its simplicity and speed, but these are not sufficient to warrant acceptance at a competitive conference. The empirical evaluation, while promising, is limited to only two tasks.

The paper adequately motivates the need for efficient and effective document representations, placing it within the existing literature on BoW, Word2Vec, and Paragraph Vectors. The authors clearly explain their approach and its connection to existing regularization techniques. However, the claims of superiority are not convincingly supported by the limited experimental results. Achieving strong performance on two tasks is a good start, but more comprehensive evaluation is needed to demonstrate the generalizability of Doc2VecC. The comparison to baselines is reasonable, but lacks a crucial element – a strong, widely-used bidirectional LSTM baseline.

To improve this paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand the evaluation:** Include more diverse tasks (e.g., topic modeling, question answering) to demonstrate the robustness of Doc2VecC.
*   **Clarify RNN-LM details:** The description of the RNN-LM baseline is vague. Was it trained as a traditional language model, or specifically to minimize classification error? Furthermore, was the final hidden state or an average of hidden states used as the document representation?
*   **Compare to a bidirectional LSTM:** Include a comparison against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates the final hidden states, as this is a common and strong baseline for document representation.
*   **Ablation study:** Provide an ablation study to isolate the impact of the corruption mechanism. How much does it contribute to the overall performance?



I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The paper mentions the corruption model introduces a "special form of regularization." Could you elaborate on *how* this regularization differs from standard L1/L2 regularization techniques?
*   The experiments section mentions using a cutoff of 10 for vocabulary size in the document classification task. How was this cutoff determined, and did you experiment with different cutoff values?
*   In the word analogy task, you mention that Doc2VecC significantly outperforms Word2Vec. Can you provide a more detailed breakdown of the performance on individual analogy types (e.g., semantic, syntactic)?
