## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that leverages a skip-gram style objective combined with a novel corruption model and global context vector regularizer. The core idea – representing documents as the average of word embeddings while actively encouraging informative word representations – is compelling and efficiently implemented. The authors clearly articulate the motivation behind their approach, grounding it in the success of Word2Vec and addressing limitations of existing document vector methods like Paragraph Vectors. The detailed analysis in Section 3, explaining the corruption model as a data-dependent regularization, is particularly well-done and strengthens the theoretical foundation of the work.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While the individual components aren’t groundbreaking on their own, the combination is a valuable contribution. The method is well-composed, efficient, and likely to be useful to other researchers in the field. The efficiency gains, particularly regarding test time representation generation, are a significant practical advantage.

My reasoning centers on the novelty of the *combination* of techniques and the clear theoretical justification provided. The authors successfully demonstrate how the corruption model acts as a regularizer, favoring informative words and suppressing noise. This is a clever and effective approach. Furthermore, the paper highlights a practical benefit – the simplicity and speed of generating document vectors – which is often overlooked in more complex models. 

However, the empirical evaluation feels somewhat limited. While the results are positive, the experiments are primarily confined to sentiment analysis and k-way classification. The heavy reliance on t-SNE visualizations, while helpful for intuition, doesn’t provide rigorous quantitative evidence of the model’s strengths. The use of relatively weak baselines, as also noted by other reviewers, raises concerns about the strength of the comparisons.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand Empirical Evaluation:** Explore tasks beyond sentiment analysis and classification. Consider semantic similarity tasks with more diverse datasets or topic modeling applications.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include more competitive baselines, such as more recent transformer-based document embedding models (e.g., Sentence-BERT).
*   **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study examining the impact of the corruption rate (q) and the regularization strength would be beneficial.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide examples of words whose embeddings are significantly affected by the corruption model, illustrating the regularization in action.

Finally, I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the embedding dimension and the corruption rate?
*   The paper mentions the model’s efficiency. Could you provide a more detailed comparison of training and inference times with other methods, perhaps on larger datasets?
*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the negative sampling distribution (Pv) and whether alternative distributions were considered?
