- Paper proposes Doc2VecC: average word embeddings with a corruption-based, data-dependent regularizer favoring rare/informative words.  
- Authors claim state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness with high training/test efficiency.  
- Decision: Reject — judged incremental with limited novelty and evaluation.  
- Strength: Simple, computationally efficient and suitable for large-scale training and fast inference.  
- Strength: Empirical performance competitive or superior to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought in tested tasks.  
- Strength: Corruption mechanism is an elegant way to downweight common, non-discriminative words.  
- Weakness: Limited technical novelty — builds on averaging embeddings and corruption-based regularization without fundamental new methods.  
- Weakness: Narrow evaluation scope — lacks diverse NLP tasks to support generalizability claims.  
- Weakness: Missing comparisons with modern architectures (bidirectional LSTMs, transformers), weakening empirical rigor.  
- Weakness: Some methodological details unclear (RNN-LM training objective and rationale for averaging hidden states).  
- Suggestion: Broaden evaluation to tasks like summarization, information retrieval, and question answering to show generalizability.  
- Suggestion: Compare against bidirectional LSTMs, transformers, and other state-of-the-art document representations.  
- Suggestion: Clarify RNN-LM objective and justify using averaged hidden states for representations.  
- Suggestion: Perform ablation studies to quantify the impact of corruption and data-dependent regularization.  
- Suggestion: Provide theoretical insights into why the corruption mechanism is effective and how it relates to other regularizers.  
- Question: How does Doc2VecC handle long documents or hierarchical structures (e.g., multi-document summarization, topic modeling)?  
- Question: Why were bidirectional LSTM representations excluded, and would Doc2VecC still outperform them in accuracy and efficiency?  
- Question: Can the corruption mechanism be adapted to structured-output tasks like sequence labeling or dependency parsing?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the corruption rate (q), and are there guidelines for setting it?  
- Conclusion: Doc2VecC is promising and efficient but lacks sufficient novelty and broader evaluation to warrant acceptance; addressing concerns would strengthen the paper.
