- Proposal: Doc2VecC, a document representation method combining skip-gram, a global context vector, and dropout-like corruption for efficient embeddings.  
- Key innovation: A corruption mechanism that acts as data-dependent regularization, boosting rare/informative words and suppressing common ones.  
- Claims: Doc2VecC is computationally efficient, produces high-quality document embeddings, and outperforms prior methods on sentiment analysis, classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason for acceptance 1: Method is efficient and decouples model complexity from training corpus size, addressing a key scalability challenge.  
- Reason for acceptance 2: Simple architecture and combination of techniques make the approach valuable despite empirical limitations.  
- Problem tackled: Balances semantic quality and computational efficiency via corruption and averaged word embeddings.  
- Motivation/literature placement: Well-grounded on Word2Vec/Paragraph Vectors and clearly identifies limitations of Paragraph Vectors and naive averaging.  
- Scientific rigor: Theoretical justification of corruption as data-dependent regularization is sound, but empirical evaluation is limited.  
- Empirical evaluation critique: Experiments are narrow (sentiment, classification, relatedness); broader benchmarks (e.g., topic modeling, IR) are needed for generalizability.  
- t-SNE concern: Heavy emphasis on t-SNE visuals provides little rigorous insight and could be replaced with ablation studies or stronger comparisons.  
- Baselines critique: Comparisons use relatively soft baselines; inclusion of strong modern baselines (e.g., Transformer-based models like BERT) is recommended.  
- Efficiency claims critique: Efficiency assertions need more detailed training/inference time analyses across diverse hardware (including GPUs).  
- Questions for authors: Rationale for chosen evaluation tasks and expected performance on topic modeling/IR?  
- Questions for authors: Were alternative corruption strategies explored and how do they compare?  
- Questions for authors: How does Doc2VecC compare to modern Transformer models in both performance and efficiency?  
- Conclusion: Despite empirical and baseline limitations, Doc2VecC is a simple, efficient, and potentially impactful contribution that warrants acceptance with minor revisions.
