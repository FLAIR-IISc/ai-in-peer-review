This paper proposes a novel document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which combines a skip-gram style objective with a global context vector regularizer and dropout. While the individual components of the model are not novel, their combination in this form is likely a new contribution.

The paper provides a thorough analysis of the model behavior in Section 3, which is appreciated. The authors demonstrate that the corruption model introduced in Doc2VecC acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words, leading to better performance.

The empirical evaluation of the model is relatively weak and limited to sentiment analysis and k-way classification tasks. The authors compare their model to several baselines, including Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors, and show that Doc2VecC outperforms these models in some cases. However, the evaluation could be strengthened by including more tasks and datasets, as well as more robust baselines.

One concern is that the authors devote too much space to t-SNE projections, which are not particularly informative, instead of deeper empirical analysis. Additionally, the use of weak or "soft" baselines, such as Word2Vec+IDF, may not provide a comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance.

Despite these limitations, the method proposed in this paper is interesting, well-composed, efficient, and likely useful to others. The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their model and its components, making it easy to understand and implement. The code for the model is also made available, which is a plus.

Decision: Accept

Reasons for acceptance:

* The paper proposes a novel combination of techniques for document representation learning.
* The model is well-motivated and well-explained, making it easy to understand and implement.
* The empirical evaluation, although limited, shows promising results.

Reasons for not rejecting:

* The paper does not contain any major flaws or errors.
* The authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of their model and its components.
* The code for the model is made available, which is a plus.

Additional feedback:

* The authors should consider including more tasks and datasets in their empirical evaluation to strengthen their claims.
* The use of more robust baselines, such as LSTM-based models or other state-of-the-art document representation learning models, would provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the model's performance.
* The authors should consider devoting more space to deeper empirical analysis, such as ablation studies or analysis of the model's behavior on different types of documents.
* The authors should consider providing more details about the hyperparameter tuning process and the computational resources used to train and evaluate the model.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process and the computational resources used to train and evaluate the model?
* How did you select the specific combination of techniques used in Doc2VecC, and what other combinations did you try?
* Can you provide more analysis of the model's behavior on different types of documents, such as short vs. long documents, or documents with different styles or genres?
* How does the model perform on other tasks, such as information retrieval or question answering?
