This paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The authors claim that this approach captures the semantic meanings of the document during learning, while a corruption model introduces a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words and suppresses common and non-discriminative ones.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons: (1) the proposed method is simple and efficient, making it suitable for large-scale document representation learning, and (2) the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method on several tasks, including sentiment analysis and document classification.

The authors provide a clear motivation for their approach, building upon the idea of word embeddings and the need for efficient document representation learning. The technical contribution, while not entirely novel, is well-executed and easy to follow. The use of a corruption model to introduce data-dependent regularization is an interesting aspect of the proposed method. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate the superiority of the proposed method over several baseline methods.

However, I would like to see additional evaluation on more than two tasks to be convinced of the general superiority of the proposed method. The authors report results on sentiment analysis and document classification, but it would be beneficial to see results on other tasks, such as semantic relatedness or text summarization.

I also have a few questions regarding the experimental setup. Specifically, I would like clarification on the RNN-LM baseline: was it trained as a language model or to minimize classification error, and was the final hidden state or the average of hidden states used as the representation? Additionally, I suggest comparing against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final hidden states, a widely used baseline for document representation.

Some minor points for improvement:

* The authors could provide more insight into the effect of the corruption rate on the performance of the proposed method.
* The visualization of document representations using t-SNE could be improved by adding more categories or using a different visualization technique.
* The authors could provide more details on the computational resources used for training and testing the proposed method.

Overall, the paper is well-written, and the proposed method is simple and efficient. With some additional evaluation and clarification on the experimental setup, I believe this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of document representation learning.
