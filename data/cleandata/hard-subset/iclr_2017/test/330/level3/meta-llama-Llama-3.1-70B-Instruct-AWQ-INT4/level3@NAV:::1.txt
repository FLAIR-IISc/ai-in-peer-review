**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

The paper proposes a novel document representation learning framework called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The framework represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings and incorporates a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while dampening the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words. The authors claim that Doc2VecC outperforms existing document representation learning algorithms in terms of testing efficiency and expressiveness of the generated representations.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Based on the review, I recommend accepting the paper. The two key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper proposes a novel combination of existing techniques, which is likely to be useful to others in the field.
2. The empirical evaluation, although limited, shows promising results, especially in terms of testing efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's contribution is not necessarily in the individual components, but in their combination, which is novel and interesting. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the model's behavior, which is appreciated. The empirical evaluation, although limited to sentiment analysis and k-way classification, shows that Doc2VecC outperforms existing algorithms in terms of testing efficiency.

However, I do have some concerns about the empirical evaluation. The use of weak or "soft" baselines, such as bag-of-words and Word2Vec+IDF, may not be sufficient to demonstrate the superiority of Doc2VecC. Additionally, the paper devotes too much space to t-SNE projections, which, while visually appealing, do not provide deeper insights into the model's performance.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Provide a more comprehensive empirical evaluation, including more challenging tasks and stronger baselines.
* Consider adding more theoretical analysis of the model's behavior, particularly with regards to the corruption model and its effects on the word embeddings.
* Provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process and the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the corruption model and how it affects the word embeddings? Specifically, how does the corruption rate q affect the performance of the model?
2. How do you plan to address the issue of overfitting, particularly in the context of the corruption model?
3. Can you provide more insights into the model's performance on the semantic relatedness task? Specifically, how does the model's performance compare to other state-of-the-art methods on this task?
