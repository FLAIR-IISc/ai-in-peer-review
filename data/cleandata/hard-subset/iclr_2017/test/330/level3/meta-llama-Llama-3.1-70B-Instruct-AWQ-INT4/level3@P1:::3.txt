This paper proposes a novel document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The framework incorporates a corruption model that randomly removes words from a document during learning, introducing a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative ones to be close to zero. The authors claim that Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec and outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

I recommend accepting this paper. The main reasons for this decision are the simplicity and efficiency of the proposed framework, as well as its strong empirical results. While the individual components of the model are not novel, the authors have successfully combined them to achieve state-of-the-art performance on several tasks.

The supporting arguments for this decision are as follows:

* The proposed framework is very efficient to train and test, thanks to its simple model architecture. This is a significant advantage over other document representation learning algorithms, such as Paragraph Vectors, which require expensive inference to produce vector representations of unseen documents.
* The corruption mechanism introduced in Doc2VecC acts as a data-dependent regularization, which favors informative or rare words while dampening the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words. This is a desirable property, as it allows the model to focus on capturing the global semantics of the document.
* The empirical results presented in the paper are strong, with Doc2VecC outperforming several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* While the authors have provided a clear explanation of the proposed framework, it would be helpful to include a more detailed analysis of the corruption mechanism and its effects on the learned word embeddings.
* The authors could also provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters, such as the corruption rate, and how they affect the performance of the model.
* It would be interesting to see how Doc2VecC performs on other tasks, such as machine translation or question answering, to further demonstrate its versatility and effectiveness.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the corruption mechanism and how it affects the learned word embeddings? For example, how does the corruption rate affect the performance of the model?
* How do you choose the hyperparameters, such as the corruption rate, and what is the effect of different values on the performance of the model?
* Have you considered applying Doc2VecC to other tasks, such as machine translation or question answering, and if so, what were the results?
