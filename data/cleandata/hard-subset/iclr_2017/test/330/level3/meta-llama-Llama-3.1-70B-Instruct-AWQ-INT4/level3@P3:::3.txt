**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a novel document representation learning framework called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The framework represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings and includes a corruption model that randomly removes words from a document during learning. The corruption model introduces a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words to be close to zero. The authors evaluate Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks and demonstrate that it outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. **Strong Empirical Results**: The paper presents strong empirical results on various tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. The results demonstrate that Doc2VecC outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms.
2. **Efficient Document Generation**: The paper proposes a novel approach to document representation learning that allows for efficient document generation. The approach represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, which eliminates the need for expensive inference.

**Supporting Arguments**

While the paper's approach may not be entirely novel, the combination of joint word/document training and random dropout of words during training is an innovative solution to the problem of document representation learning. The corruption model introduced in the paper is a key component of the approach, as it allows for data-dependent regularization and favors informative or rare words.

The paper's evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with a comprehensive comparison to several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms. The results demonstrate that Doc2VecC outperforms these algorithms on various tasks, including sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
