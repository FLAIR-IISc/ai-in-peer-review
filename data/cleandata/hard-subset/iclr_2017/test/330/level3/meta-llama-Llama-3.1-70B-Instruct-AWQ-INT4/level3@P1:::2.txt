This paper proposes a novel document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), which represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The framework incorporates a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while driving common and non-discriminative words towards zero. The authors claim that this approach outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms in sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons: (1) the proposed approach is well-motivated and grounded in the literature, and (2) the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach in various tasks.

The authors provide a clear and concise motivation for their approach, highlighting the limitations of existing document representation learning methods. They also provide a thorough review of related work, demonstrating a good understanding of the current state of the field. The proposed approach is simple yet effective, and the authors provide a convincing explanation of how the corruption model helps to upweight rare and informative words.

The experimental results are also impressive, with the proposed approach outperforming several state-of-the-art methods in sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The authors provide a detailed analysis of the results, including a comparison of the performance of different methods on various tasks.

However, I have some concerns regarding the technical contribution of the paper. While the proposed approach is novel and effective, many of the techniques used are not new. The authors could have done a better job of highlighting the specific contributions of their work and how it builds upon existing research.

Additionally, I would like to see more evaluation on additional tasks to be convinced of the general superiority of the proposed approach. The authors could have also compared their approach to other widely used baselines, such as bidirectional LSTM, to demonstrate its effectiveness.

I also have some questions regarding the RNN-LM used in the experiments. Was it trained as a language model or to minimize classification error? Was the final hidden state or the average of hidden states used as the representation? The authors could have provided more details on the experimental setup to clarify these points.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning, and I am happy to accept it.
