The paper "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION" proposes an efficient document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The authors claim that Doc2VecC can learn high-quality document representations that outperform several state-of-the-art algorithms in sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The proposed model architecture is simple and efficient, allowing for fast training and testing on large datasets.

I recommend accepting this paper, with the primary reason being that the proposed method is interesting, well-composed, efficient, and likely useful to others. The combination of a skip-gram style objective with a global context vector regularizer and dropout is novel and provides a unique approach to document representation learning.

One of the strengths of the paper is the detailed analysis of the model behavior in Section 3, which provides valuable insights into the regularization effects of the corruption model. The empirical evaluation, although limited, demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed method in various tasks.

However, there are some concerns regarding the empirical evaluation. The authors could have provided a more comprehensive evaluation by including additional baselines, such as other state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms. Furthermore, the use of t-SNE projections, while visually appealing, takes up too much space and could be replaced with more in-depth empirical analysis.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors consider the following:

1. Provide a more comprehensive evaluation by including additional baselines and comparing the proposed method to other state-of-the-art algorithms.
2. Reduce the space devoted to t-SNE projections and replace it with more in-depth empirical analysis, such as analyzing the effects of different hyperparameters or exploring the robustness of the proposed method to different datasets.
3. Address the concerns about using weak/“soft” baselines by including more competitive baselines in the evaluation.

Questions for the authors:

1. Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for the proposed method and the baselines?
2. How does the proposed method perform on other datasets, such as the 20 Newsgroups dataset or the Reuters Corpus?
3. Can you provide more insights into the regularization effects of the corruption model and how it compares to other regularization techniques, such as dropout or L1/L2 regularization?

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting and efficient method for document representation learning, and with some additional evaluation and analysis, it has the potential to make a valuable contribution to the field.
