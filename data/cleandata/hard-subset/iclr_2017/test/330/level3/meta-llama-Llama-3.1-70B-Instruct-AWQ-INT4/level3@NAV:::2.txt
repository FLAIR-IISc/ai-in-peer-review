**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a document representation learning framework called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). This framework represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, while incorporating a corruption model that introduces data-dependent regularization. The regularization favors informative or rare words and dampens the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words. The authors evaluate Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, and report state-of-the-art performance on several datasets.

**Decision**

I decide to accept this paper, with the primary reason being its simplicity and efficiency. The proposed framework is easy to implement and train, and it achieves competitive performance on various tasks.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Simplicity and Efficiency**: The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC can be trained efficiently on large datasets, and it achieves competitive performance on various tasks. This is a significant advantage over other document representation learning methods that require more complex architectures and longer training times.
2. **State-of-the-Art Performance**: The authors report state-of-the-art performance on several datasets, including the IMDB movie review dataset and the Wikipedia dataset. This suggests that Doc2VecC is a competitive method for document representation learning.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Comparison to Other Methods**: While the authors compare Doc2VecC to several other document representation learning methods, it would be helpful to include a comparison to a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final hidden states, which is a widely used baseline for document representation.
2. **Clarity on RNN-LM**: The authors mention that they use a supervised RNN-LM, but it is unclear whether it was trained as a language model or to minimize classification error. Additionally, it is unclear whether the final hidden state or the average of hidden states was used as the representation.
3. **Evaluation on More Tasks**: While the authors evaluate Doc2VecC on several tasks, it would be helpful to include evaluations on additional tasks to demonstrate the generalizability of the method.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on the corruption model and how it introduces data-dependent regularization?
2. How did you select the hyperparameters for Doc2VecC, and are there any guidelines for tuning these hyperparameters in practice?
3. Can you provide more insights into why Doc2VecC performs well on longer paragraphs, while LSTM-based methods perform well on shorter sentences?
