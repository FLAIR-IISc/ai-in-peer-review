**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes an efficient document representation learning framework called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). It represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, which captures the semantic meanings of the document during learning. The model includes a corruption mechanism that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative ones to be close to zero. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec and outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper with two key reasons: (1) the simplicity and efficiency of the proposed model, and (2) the strong empirical results on various tasks.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's contribution lies in its simplicity and efficiency. By representing documents as a simple average of word embeddings, Doc2VecC avoids the need for expensive inference, making it much faster than other methods like Doc2Vec. The corruption mechanism, although not novel, is effectively used to introduce a data-dependent regularization that favors informative or rare words. The empirical results are strong, with Doc2VecC outperforming several state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks.

While the model components and training methods are not novel, the combination and application of these techniques to document representation learning are well-executed and justified. The authors provide a clear explanation of the model and its components, making it easy to follow and understand.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the authors consider the following:

* Provide more analysis on the corruption mechanism and its effect on the model's performance. While the authors mention that the corruption mechanism is essential to the model's performance, they do not provide a detailed analysis of its impact.
* Compare the proposed model with other efficient document representation learning methods, such as those using convolutional neural networks (CNNs) or recurrent neural networks (RNNs).
* Consider applying the proposed model to other tasks, such as text classification or information retrieval, to further demonstrate its effectiveness.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on the corruption mechanism and its effect on the model's performance? How does the corruption rate affect the model's performance, and what is the optimal corruption rate for different tasks?
* How does the proposed model handle out-of-vocabulary words? Do you plan to extend the model to handle out-of-vocabulary words in future work?
* Can you provide more information on the computational resources used to train and test the model? How does the model's performance scale with the size of the training data and the number of parameters?
