**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a document representation learning framework called Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). The framework represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings and includes a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative words to be close to zero. The authors evaluate Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, achieving state-of-the-art performance in some cases.

**Decision**

I decide to accept this paper, with the main reason being the simplicity and speed of the proposed framework. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC can achieve competitive performance with existing state-of-the-art methods while being more efficient in terms of training and testing time.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing methods, such as bag-of-words and paragraph vectors. The proposed framework is simple and intuitive, and the corruption model is a novel contribution that helps to improve the performance of the model.

The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate the effectiveness of Doc2VecC on various tasks. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the word embeddings generated by Doc2VecC, showing that they are more informative and discriminative than those generated by Word2Vec.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I would like to see more evaluations on different tasks and datasets. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of Doc2VecC on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, it would be beneficial to see how the framework performs on other tasks, such as text classification, clustering, or topic modeling.

Additionally, I would like to see a more detailed analysis of the corruption model and its impact on the performance of the framework. The authors provide some insights into the effect of the corruption model on the word embeddings, but a more thorough analysis would be helpful in understanding the strengths and limitations of the framework.

**Questions**

I have a few questions that I would like the authors to clarify:

1. Can you provide more details on the RNN-LM used in the experiments? Was it trained as a language model or to minimize classification error, and was the final hidden state or the average of hidden states used as the representation?
2. How does the corruption model affect the performance of the framework on different tasks? Are there any tasks where the corruption model is particularly effective or ineffective?
3. Can you provide a comparison with a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final hidden states, a widely used baseline for document representation?
