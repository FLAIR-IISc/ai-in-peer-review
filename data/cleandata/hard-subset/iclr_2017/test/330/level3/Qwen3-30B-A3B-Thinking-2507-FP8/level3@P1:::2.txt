

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption model that introduces data-dependent regularization. The key innovation is the corruption mechanism that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while emphasizing rare, informative ones, theoretically justified through a Taylor expansion analysis. The authors claim this approach achieves state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while being significantly more efficient than existing methods.

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents a compelling motivation and demonstrates good empirical results, the technical contribution is limited and the novelty is overstated. The core idea of representing documents as averages of word embeddings is not novel (Huang et al., 2012), and the corruption mechanism is a minor extension of existing techniques like dropout and denoising autoencoders. The paper's main strengths are simplicity and efficiency, but these alone don't justify the claim of "significantly better" performance compared to state-of-the-art methods.

The empirical evidence is insufficient to support the paper's broad claims. The authors evaluate only on three tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness), which is inadequate to demonstrate general superiority across diverse NLP applications. The performance gains over existing methods are often marginal (e.g., 1-2% improvement on some classification tasks), and the paper doesn't provide sufficient analysis of when Doc2VecC excels versus when it doesn't. For instance, the paper notes Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on IMDB but doesn't explore why this might be the case or how Doc2VecC would perform on other long-text tasks where Skip-thought might be more appropriate.

The comparison to RNN-based methods is particularly problematic. The paper mentions an RNN-LM baseline but fails to clarify whether it was trained as a language model or to minimize classification error, and which representation was used (final hidden state vs. average of hidden states). This lack of clarity undermines the comparison. Additionally, the paper doesn't compare against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final hidden states, a widely used baseline for document representation that would provide a more meaningful comparison to their approach.

The theoretical justification for the corruption model is interesting but not sufficiently novel. The paper's analysis of the regularization effect is a straightforward extension of existing work on dropout and denoising autoencoders (Wager et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2014), and the authors don't clearly articulate how their approach differs in meaningful ways from these prior methods.

For improvement, the authors should: (1) more clearly position their work against existing approaches to averaging word embeddings, (2) conduct more comprehensive evaluations across diverse tasks to demonstrate general applicability, (3) clarify the RNN-LM baseline details, and (4) compare against bidirectional LSTM baselines. The paper's claim of "significantly better" performance needs stronger empirical backing and more nuanced discussion of the results.

To help clarify my assessment, I have three specific questions: First, the RNN-LM baseline mentioned in the sentiment analysis section—was it trained as a language model or to minimize classification error, and which representation (final hidden state or average of hidden states) was used for document representation? Second, could the authors provide results on at least one additional task beyond the three evaluated (e.g., topic modeling or named entity recognition) to better demonstrate general applicability? Third, could the authors include a comparison against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final hidden states, a widely used baseline for document representation that would provide a more meaningful comparison to their approach?
