

# Review of "Document Vectors Through Corruption" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation is the incorporation of a corruption model that randomly removes words from documents during training, which serves as a data-dependent regularizer. This regularization mechanism favors informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec, matches or outperforms state-of-the-art document representation methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, and maintains high training and inference efficiency (processing billions of words per hour on a single machine). The paper provides a detailed theoretical analysis of how the corruption model acts as a regularizer, explaining its benefits for document representation learning.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

Key reasons: The method is interesting, well-composed, efficient, and likely to be useful to the community. The combination of skip-gram style learning with a corruption-based regularizer represents a novel and effective approach to document representation learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for its method. While the individual components (skip-gram objective, dropout, simple averaging) are not novel, their specific combination in Doc2VecC is novel and well-motivated. Section 3 provides a particularly strong theoretical analysis of how the corruption model functions as a data-dependent regularizer, explaining why it benefits document representation learning. This theoretical foundation is rare and valuable in the field.

The empirical evaluation, while limited in scope, consistently shows improvements over strong baselines on standard document representation tasks. The efficiency claims are well-supported by the timing results in Table 2, showing Doc2VecC trains faster than Paragraph Vectors and is comparable to Word2Vec in training time while being significantly faster at test time. The analysis of word embeddings in Table 3 demonstrates the regularization effect of the corruption model, showing Doc2VecC successfully suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words.

The paper's main strength is its simplicity and efficiency without sacrificing performance. The model architecture resembles Word2Vec, making it easy to implement and train on large datasets, while the corruption-based regularization provides significant performance benefits.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper:

1. The empirical evaluation could be expanded to include more diverse NLP tasks beyond sentiment analysis and document classification. While the current tasks are standard, additional experiments on tasks like text summarization or question answering would better demonstrate the generalizability of the approach.

2. The paper devotes excessive space to t-SNE visualizations (Figures 3 and 4) that don't provide deep insights. More quantitative analysis of the document representations would be more valuable than visualizations.

3. The baseline comparisons could be strengthened. For example, using Word2Vec+IDF as a baseline is problematic because it's a simple heuristic, while Doc2VecC naturally incorporates document-level information during training. Comparing to more sophisticated document representation methods (e.g., those using attention mechanisms) would be more meaningful.

4. The paper doesn't fully address the performance discrepancy between Doc2VecC and Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB (outperforms) versus SICK (underperforms). A deeper analysis of this difference would provide valuable insights.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims Doc2VecC "matches or outperforms the state-of-the-art" but doesn't compare to more recent approaches like BERT or other transformer-based models. How would Doc2VecC perform against these more modern methods?

2. The corruption rate q is set to 0.9 throughout the experiments. What happens when q is varied (e.g., q=0.5 vs q=0.9)? Is there an optimal value for q, and how sensitive is the model to this parameter?

3. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." How does this relate to the standard DEA approach that uses reconstruction error? What's the theoretical connection between these two approaches?

4. In Table 5, Doc2VecC shows significant improvement over other methods on Wikipedia classification. Can the authors share the full classification results (not just error rates) to better understand the performance across different categories?
