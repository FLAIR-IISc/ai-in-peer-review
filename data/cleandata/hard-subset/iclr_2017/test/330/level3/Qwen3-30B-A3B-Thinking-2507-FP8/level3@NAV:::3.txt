

Doc2VecC introduces a simple yet effective framework for document representation learning, where documents are represented as averages of their constituent word embeddings. The authors incorporate a corruption mechanism during training that serves as data-dependent regularization, favoring informative words while suppressing common, non-discriminative ones. The approach ensures document representations captured through averaging word embeddings during training also work effectively at test time. The paper demonstrates strong empirical performance across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while achieving significant efficiency gains in both training and inference compared to existing methods.

I recommend acceptance with two key reasons: (1) the simplicity and efficiency of the approach, which enables training on billions of words per hour and fast document representation generation; (2) the strong empirical results that consistently outperform or match state-of-the-art methods across multiple tasks. The efficiency gains are particularly noteworthy, with Doc2VecC requiring only 7 seconds to generate representations for 25,000 test documents compared to 4 minutes and 17 seconds for Paragraph Vectors.

The paper provides convincing evidence for its claims through comprehensive experiments on multiple datasets. The empirical results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods, with significant improvements in classification accuracy and semantic relatedness tasks. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is well-supported through the Taylor expansion analysis.

For improvement, the authors should more explicitly clarify the novelty of their approach relative to prior work, particularly the relationship to existing sum-of-words methods. A more detailed comparison with the specific regularization effects of their corruption mechanism versus standard dropout would strengthen the theoretical foundation. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, especially regarding the handling of very long documents beyond the scope of their experiments. The authors should address how their corruption mechanism differs in practice from standard input dropout, as this is a critical aspect of their contribution.
