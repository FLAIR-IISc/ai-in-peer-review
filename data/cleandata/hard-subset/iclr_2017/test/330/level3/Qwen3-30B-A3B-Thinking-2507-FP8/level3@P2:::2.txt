

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The key innovation lies in introducing a corruption model during training that acts as a data-dependent regularization, favoring rare or informative words while suppressing common, non-discriminative words. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables document representations generated by simple averaging to capture semantic meanings effectively. The method is highly efficient, training on billions of words per hour on a single machine, and generates document representations for unseen documents at test time with minimal computational overhead. The paper shows that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

While the paper presents an interesting and well-motivated approach to document representation learning, I cannot recommend acceptance for the following reasons:

First, the technical contribution appears limited in novelty. The core idea of representing documents as averages of word embeddings is not new (Huang et al., 2012), and the corruption mechanism as a regularization technique has parallels with dropout training and denoising autoencoders. The paper's main innovation is the specific implementation and empirical demonstration of this combined approach, but the theoretical foundation does not significantly advance beyond existing regularization techniques.

Second, the empirical evaluation is insufficient to support the claim of general superiority. The paper evaluates on only three tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness), which is inadequate to demonstrate consistent performance across diverse document representation scenarios. The paper does not compare against a widely adopted baseline like bidirectional LSTM with concatenation of final hidden states, which is a common approach for document representation. Without such a comparison, it's difficult to assess whether the performance gains are truly significant or task-specific.

The paper's strength lies in the simplicity and efficiency of the approach, which is well-documented. However, this alone does not constitute a sufficient contribution for acceptance in a top-tier conference like ICLR, especially when the technical novelty is limited and the evaluation is not comprehensive.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Expanding the experimental evaluation to include more diverse document representation tasks and comparisons with stronger baselines, particularly bidirectional LSTM approaches that are commonly used in recent literature.

2. Providing a more detailed discussion of how the corruption rate q affects performance, including a sensitivity analysis across different values of q.

3. Including ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the corruption mechanism versus the simple averaging approach. For instance, how much of the performance gain comes from the regularization versus the averaging itself?

4. Clarifying the relationship between Doc2VecC and the Word2Vec+IDF baseline. The paper states that Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but it's not entirely clear how this differs from the Word2Vec+IDF approach, which also uses word embeddings to form document representations.

## 5. Clarification Questions

1. Regarding the RNN-LM baseline: The paper mentions using RNN-LM in the sentiment analysis experiments. Was this model trained as a language model (predicting next words) or was it trained to minimize classification error directly? Additionally, was the final hidden state or the average of hidden states used as the document representation?

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought Vectors on IMDB but is slightly worse on the SICK dataset. Given that Skip-thought Vectors were trained on a large book corpus, could the performance difference be attributed to dataset characteristics (sentence length, domain) rather than inherent superiority of Doc2VecC? If so, could the authors provide more analysis on this?

3. The paper compares against Paragraph Vectors but does not compare against the PV-DBOW variant (which removes word embeddings entirely, as mentioned in the paper). How does Doc2VecC compare to this more efficient variant of Paragraph Vectors?

4. In the corruption model, the authors mention that "the number of parameters that needs to be back-propagated in each update was increased by the number of surviving words in x̃." Could the authors clarify how this affects the computational complexity compared to standard Word2Vec?
