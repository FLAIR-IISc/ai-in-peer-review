

# Review for "Document Vector through Corruption" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings. The key innovation lies in incorporating a corruption mechanism during training—randomly removing words from documents—which serves as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while preserving those of rare, informative words. The authors demonstrate that this simple approach yields state-of-the-art performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while offering significant efficiency advantages in both training (billions of words per hour on a single machine) and inference (instant document representation generation by simply averaging word embeddings).

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong case for the practical utility of a simple yet effective approach that combines existing techniques in a novel but straightforward way. Despite limited methodological novelty, the combination of simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance justifies wider attention.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's contribution is not in theoretical novelty but in demonstrating a simple, efficient, and effective approach that can be widely adopted. The authors successfully address a practical problem in document representation learning: the inefficiency of existing methods for generating representations of unseen documents.

While the sum-of-words approach has precedent (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), the paper provides compelling evidence that Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism fundamentally changes how word embeddings are learned. The corruption mechanism is not merely standard dropout but acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common words while preserving those of rare, informative words. This is a crucial distinction that explains the performance gains.

The empirical results are strong across multiple benchmarks: Doc2VecC achieves significant improvements over Word2Vec+IDF (15% better on IMDB sentiment analysis), outperforms Paragraph Vectors on Wikipedia classification, and surpasses the competition on SemEval semantic relatedness. The efficiency gains are equally impressive—Doc2VecC trains on billions of words per hour and generates document representations in seconds, compared to minutes for Paragraph Vectors.

The paper's strength lies in the clear demonstration that a simple model can achieve state-of-the-art results while offering substantial practical advantages. The authors provide thorough empirical evidence to support their claims, including ablation studies on the corruption mechanism and comparisons across multiple tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Better acknowledge prior work on sum-of-words document representations (Huang et al., 2012) and clarify how their corruption mechanism differs from standard dropout used in other NLP models.
2. Explicitly state that the key contribution is the combination of these techniques with careful justification for why this combination works better than using them separately.
3. Include a more detailed comparison of how their corruption mechanism specifically improves upon standard word2vec subsampling (which is already used to address frequent words).

## 5. Clarifying Questions
1. The paper uses q=0.9 throughout without sensitivity analysis. How does performance vary with different corruption rates? Is there an optimal q for different datasets or tasks?
2. The authors claim Doc2VecC "naturally" enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings. Can they provide more theoretical justification for why the corruption mechanism specifically enables this, beyond the empirical results?
3. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders when removing local context words. How does this compare to the standard DEA approach in terms of implementation and performance?
