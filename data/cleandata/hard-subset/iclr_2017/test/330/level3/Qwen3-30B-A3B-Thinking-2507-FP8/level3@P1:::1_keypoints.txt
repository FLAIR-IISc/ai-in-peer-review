- Introduces Doc2VecC: representing documents by averaging word embeddings learned via a corruption-based training process.
- Main contribution: combines skip-gram objective with corruption that acts as a data-dependent regularizer to suppress common non-discriminative words.
- Preserves simplicity of averaging while capturing semantics during training, avoiding costly inference for unseen documents.
- Strong theoretical analysis (Section 3) deriving how corruption yields a frequency- and confidence-dependent regularization term.
- Empirical results show state-of-the-art or competitive performance on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.
- Extremely efficient: trains billions of words per hour on a single machine and produces test-time embeddings via simple averaging.
- Advantage over Paragraph Vectors: avoids parameter explosion while matching or improving performance.
- Limitations: empirical evaluation focused mainly on sentiment and classification, lacking broader task coverage.
- Baselines are somewhat weak (e.g., BoW, Word2Vec+IDF); missing comparisons to more recent neural sentence/document representation methods.
- Excessive emphasis on t-SNE visualizations at the expense of deeper empirical analysis.
- Needs clearer discussion of how corruption differs from standard dropout and other regularizers.
- Reviewer questions: effect of corruption rate q (and its dependence on document length), and comparison to weighted averaging methods like Word2Vec+IDF.
