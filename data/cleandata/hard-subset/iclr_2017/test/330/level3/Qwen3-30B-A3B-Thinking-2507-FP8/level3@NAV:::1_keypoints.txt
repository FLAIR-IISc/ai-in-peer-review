- Introduces Doc2VecC: documents represented as simple averages of word embeddings with a corruption-based regularizer.  
- Method combines a skip-gram style objective, a global context vector regularizer, and dropout to enforce averaging during training.  
- Training is efficient—time scales with vocabulary size rather than corpus size.  
- Produces high-quality representations and significantly outperforms baselines on sentiment analysis and document classification.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance; core contribution is novel and well-motivated by the averaging observation.  
- Section 3 provides strong analysis showing corruption acts as a data-dependent regularizer that favors informative words.  
- Main limitation: empirical evaluation is narrow, focused only on sentiment and k-way classification.  
- Paper devotes excessive space to t-SNE visualizations instead of deeper, more rigorous analysis of model behavior.  
- Baselines (e.g., BoW, Word2Vec+IDF) are somewhat weak and omit more recent strong approaches.  
- Suggested improvement: broaden experiments to more diverse tasks (e.g., summarization, question answering).  
- Suggested improvement: replace some t-SNE plots with more rigorous analyses of model behavior.  
- Suggested improvement: compare against stronger baselines such as BERT-based representations.  
- Question 1: Why does the corruption-based regularizer outperform standard Word2Vec word subsampling?  
- Question 2: How does Doc2VecC scale to extremely long documents (e.g., full books) versus sequence-based models like LSTMs, and can the authors justify claims about handling long paragraphs better than skip-thought vectors?
