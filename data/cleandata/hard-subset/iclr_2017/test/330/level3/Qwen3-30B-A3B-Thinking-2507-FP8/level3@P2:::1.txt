

# Review for "Document Vector through Corruption" (Doc2VecC)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning framework that represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings. The key innovation lies in a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization during training, which favors informative/rare words while suppressing common/non-discriminative words. The authors demonstrate that this approach naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings, eliminating the need for post-processing. Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks while maintaining computational efficiency—training on billions of words per hour and enabling fast test-time inference. The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 provides valuable insights into how the corruption mechanism functions as a data-dependent regularizer.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, efficient, and elegant approach to document representation learning that makes meaningful contributions to the field. The simplicity of the model architecture combined with its strong empirical performance and theoretical justification makes it a valuable addition to the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution—using a corruption model to regularize word embeddings during document representation learning—is well-justified and theoretically supported. The authors effectively demonstrate how the corruption mechanism (Eq. 1) acts as a data-dependent regularizer that naturally suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while preserving those of rare, informative words. This is a significant insight that explains the empirical success of the method.

The empirical evaluation, while limited to three tasks, is well-executed and demonstrates clear advantages over existing approaches. Doc2VecC outperforms strong baselines like Paragraph Vectors on sentiment analysis and document classification, and achieves competitive results on semantic relatedness. The efficiency gains are particularly noteworthy—Doc2VecC trains faster than Paragraph Vectors and enables near-instant test-time document representation generation.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is particularly strong, providing a clear explanation of how the corruption model functions as a regularizer. This level of theoretical justification is uncommon in document representation papers and significantly strengthens the paper's contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- **Expand the empirical evaluation**: While the three tasks covered are relevant, including more diverse datasets or tasks (e.g., topic modeling, question answering) would strengthen the claim that Doc2VecC is broadly useful.
- **Address baseline comparison**: The paper compares to some weaker baselines (e.g., BoW, Word2Vec+IDF) rather than more recent state-of-the-art methods. Including comparisons to more modern approaches like BERT or other transformer-based methods (even if they're not directly comparable) would help position the contribution more clearly.
- **Deepen the analysis of the corruption mechanism**: The paper includes t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3), but the analysis could be strengthened by examining how the corruption rate q affects performance across different tasks or document lengths.
- **Clarify the relationship to previous work**: The paper mentions Huang et al. (2012) as having used average word embeddings, but the connection to Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism could be more explicitly contrasted.

## 5. Questions for Clarification
- In the corruption model (Eq. 1), the authors set uncorrupted dimensions to 1/(1-q) times their original value to maintain unbiasedness. Could the authors provide more intuition about why this specific scaling is necessary for the regularization effect?
- The paper states that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." Could the authors clarify how this reduction occurs in the optimization objective (Eq. 4) and whether the performance of Doc2VecC would match DEA when the local context is removed?
- In Table 5, Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors across all representation dimensions. However, Paragraph Vectors have a more complex model architecture. Could the authors provide insight into why Doc2VecC's simpler approach outperforms a more expressive model?
