- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings with a corruption model that randomly removes words during training as a data-dependent regularizer favoring informative/rare words.

- Claims Doc2VecC yields better word embeddings than Word2Vec, matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness, and is highly efficient (billions of words/hour).

- Provides detailed theoretical analysis explaining how corruption acts as a regularizer that suppresses common, non-discriminative word embeddings.

- Decision: Accept — method is interesting, well-composed, efficient, and likely useful; novel combination of skip-gram style learning and corruption-based regularization.

- Strength: Simplicity and efficiency (resembles Word2Vec, easy to implement) while preserving or improving performance; strong timing results vs Paragraph Vectors and Word2Vec.

- Empirical support: Consistent improvements over strong baselines on standard tasks; Table 3 shows the regularization effect on embeddings.

- Limitation: Empirical evaluation is somewhat limited in task diversity; additional tasks (e.g., summarization, QA) would strengthen generalizability claims.

- Critique: Excessive space devoted to t-SNE visualizations that provide limited insight; more quantitative analyses of representations preferred.

- Baseline comparisons could be stronger—Word2Vec+IDF is a weak heuristic baseline; should compare to more sophisticated document models (e.g., attention-based).

- Analysis gap: Paper does not fully explain why Doc2VecC outperforms Skip-thought on IMDB but underperforms on SICK; deeper analysis needed.

- Question: No comparison to modern transformer-based models (e.g., BERT); how would Doc2VecC perform against them?

- Question: Corruption rate q fixed at 0.9—need experiments varying q (e.g., 0.5 vs 0.9), optimal value, and sensitivity analysis.

- Question: Clarify relation to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) and the theoretical connection to reconstruction-error based approaches.

- Request: Share full Wikipedia classification results (not just error rates) to understand performance across categories.
