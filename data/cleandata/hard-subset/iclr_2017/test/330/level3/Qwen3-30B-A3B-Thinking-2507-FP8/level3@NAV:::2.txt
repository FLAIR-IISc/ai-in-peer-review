

**Review**

The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation method that averages word embeddings with data-dependent regularization through a corruption mechanism. This regularization upweights rare/informative words while suppressing common words, aiming to produce document vectors that capture semantic meaning without requiring document-specific parameters. The authors claim superior performance on sentiment analysis and document classification compared to baselines.

I recommend **reject** for two key reasons: First, the technical contribution is limited as the core idea (averaging word embeddings) and corruption mechanism are not novel, building directly on existing work like denoising autoencoders and Word2Vec. The main strengths are simplicity and speed, but these alone do not constitute sufficient novelty for acceptance. Second, the evaluation is insufficiently broad - the authors only test on two tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification), failing to demonstrate general superiority across multiple domains as required to support their claims.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence for the two tasks, with clear improvements over baselines, but lacks critical comparisons. Specifically, the authors should compare against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates final hidden states, a standard baseline for document representation. Additionally, the paper is unclear about the RNN-LM implementation: was it trained as a language model or for classification, and which representation (final hidden state or average) was used? The authors also fail to address whether their method generalizes to other tasks beyond the two evaluated.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: 1) expand evaluation to at least three distinct tasks; 2) clarify the RNN-LM implementation details; 3) include the bidirectional LSTM baseline; and 4) discuss limitations of the corruption-based regularization approach. Without these additions, the paper's claims about general superiority remain unconvincing. The simplicity and speed advantages are notable but insufficient to overcome the lack of novelty and limited evaluation scope.
