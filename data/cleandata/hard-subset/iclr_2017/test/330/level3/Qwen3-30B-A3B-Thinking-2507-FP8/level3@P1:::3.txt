

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The authors claim their approach produces high-quality document representations that match or outperform state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being extremely efficient in both training and test time.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are the strong empirical performance of Doc2VecC across multiple benchmarks and its remarkable efficiency in both training and inference, which are critical considerations for real-world applications of document representation learning.

The paper makes a compelling case for Doc2VecC's effectiveness. The empirical results demonstrate that the method achieves competitive or superior performance compared to established baselines like Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought Vectors, and Word2Vec+IDF across three distinct tasks. On the IMDB sentiment analysis task, Doc2VecC achieves 87.9% accuracy (compared to 86.5% for Paragraph Vectors), while on the Wikipedia document classification task, it achieves a 30.24% error rate with 1000-dimensional representations (compared to 33.02% for Paragraph Vectors). The semantic relatedness task on SICK also shows strong results (11.70% error rate), outperforming most baselines and approaching the performance of more complex LSTM-based methods. These results are consistently strong across multiple evaluation metrics and datasets.

The efficiency claims are equally compelling. Doc2VecC can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine, and test-time representation generation is nearly instantaneous (7 seconds for 25,000 documents compared to 4 minutes 17 seconds for Paragraph Vectors). The paper convincingly demonstrates that Doc2VecC's simple model architecture, which avoids the document-specific parameters of Paragraph Vectors, enables this efficiency. The corruption mechanism, while similar to standard dropout, is effectively applied to introduce a data-dependent regularization that suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words while preserving those of rare, informative words.

While the paper's methodological novelty is somewhat limited (the averaging of word embeddings and corruption mechanism are not entirely new concepts), the authors successfully demonstrate how these components, when combined and properly implemented, yield significant practical benefits. The paper acknowledges related work, including prior use of averaging word embeddings (Huang et al., 2012), but effectively argues that their corruption mechanism provides a novel way to regularize word embeddings to better serve document representation tasks.

The experimental design is sound, with thorough comparisons against multiple strong baselines, and the authors provide clear explanations for performance differences (e.g., explaining Skip-thought Vectors' poor performance on IMDB due to paragraph length mismatch). The code is publicly available, and the results are reproducible.

For future work, I recommend the authors more explicitly acknowledge the prior use of averaging word embeddings in Huang et al. (2012) and clarify the precise novelty of their corruption mechanism. While the paper mentions that their corruption is "data-dependent," it would be helpful to explicitly contrast their approach with standard dropout regularization used in other contexts. Additionally, a more detailed analysis of how the corruption rate q affects performance would strengthen the methodological justification.

I would also like to ask the authors to clarify whether the corruption mechanism's regularization effect is specifically designed to address the "curse of dimensionality" in document representation learning, or if it's primarily a technique to improve the quality of word embeddings for downstream document tasks. This would help clarify the theoretical contribution of their approach.

Finally, while the paper demonstrates strong performance on sentiment analysis and document classification, it would be valuable to see a more detailed analysis of how Doc2VecC performs on tasks where document structure (beyond word order) is particularly important, such as text summarization or question answering. This would further establish the method's general applicability.

In conclusion, while the methodological novelty is modest, the paper presents a simple, efficient, and highly effective approach to document representation learning that delivers strong empirical results. The combination of performance, efficiency, and simplicity makes this an important contribution to the field that deserves wider attention.
