- Doc2VecC represents each document as the simple average of word embeddings with a corruption model that favors informative/rare words and suppresses common ones.  
- The corruption model acts as a data-dependent regularizer that enforces document semantics in averaged embeddings and removes need for post-processing.  
- The method achieves state-of-the-art results on sentiment analysis and document classification and is competitive on semantic relatedness.  
- Doc2VecC is computationally efficient—trains on billions of words per hour and enables near-instant test-time inference.  
- Theoretical analysis (Section 3.1) provides a clear explanation of how the corruption mechanism functions as a regularizer.  
- Decision: Accept—well-motivated, efficient, elegant approach with strong empirical and theoretical support.  
- Empirical evaluation shows clear advantages over baselines like Paragraph Vectors, including faster training and inference.  
- Corruption mechanism is well-justified: it suppresses embeddings of common/non-discriminative words while preserving rare/informative ones.  
- Suggest expanding empirical evaluation to more diverse datasets/tasks (e.g., topic modeling, question answering).  
- Recommend comparing to more recent state-of-the-art methods (e.g., BERT/transformers) to better position the contribution.  
- Request deeper analysis of how corruption rate q affects performance across different tasks and document lengths beyond t-SNE visualizations.  
- Ask for clearer contrast and relationship to prior work that uses averaged embeddings (e.g., Huang et al., 2012).  
- Clarification requested on why uncorrupted dimensions are scaled by 1/(1-q) for unbiasedness and its intuition for regularization.  
- Clarify how Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders when local context is removed and whether performance would match DEA.  
- Ask why the simpler Doc2VecC outperforms the more expressive Paragraph Vectors across representation dimensions.
