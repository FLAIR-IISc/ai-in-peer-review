

The paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel approach for document representation learning. Doc2VecC represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings learned through a corruption-based training process. The key contribution lies in combining a skip-gram style objective with a corruption mechanism that acts as a data-dependent regularizer, naturally suppressing embeddings of common but non-discriminative words while preserving those of rare and informative words. This approach maintains the simplicity of averaging word embeddings for document representation while ensuring semantic meaning is captured during learning, avoiding the need for post-processing or expensive inference for unseen documents.

I recommend acceptance. The paper presents a well-motivated, efficient, and theoretically grounded approach to document representation learning. The combination of the simple averaging strategy with the corruption-based regularization is novel and provides a compelling alternative to existing methods.

The paper's strengths include a thorough theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer (Section 3), which elegantly explains how the model naturally suppresses common words. The authors provide a detailed derivation showing how the corruption process introduces a regularization term that depends on word frequency and predictive confidence. This theoretical foundation is strong and well-executed, making the paper more than just a collection of empirical results.

The empirical evaluation demonstrates that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The model's efficiency is particularly impressive: it trains on billions of words per hour on a single machine and generates document representations at test time through simple averaging. The comparison to Paragraph Vectors is especially compelling, as Doc2VecC avoids the parameter explosion issue that plagues that approach while maintaining or improving performance.

However, the empirical evaluation has notable limitations. The paper focuses primarily on sentiment analysis and document classification, with limited exploration of other relevant tasks. The baselines are somewhat weak (e.g., BoW and Word2Vec+IDF), and the paper doesn't include comparisons to more recent neural approaches that might be more competitive. While the authors note that Skip-thought vectors underperform on IMDB, they don't compare against more modern sentence representation methods that might be more appropriate baselines. The paper also devotes excessive space to t-SNE visualizations at the expense of deeper empirical analysis.

The paper could be strengthened by including more comprehensive comparisons against stronger baselines, expanding the empirical evaluation to additional tasks, and providing more detailed analysis of why Doc2VecC performs well on longer documents (as suggested in the IMDB results). The authors should also clarify how their corruption mechanism differs from standard dropout, as the connection to existing regularization techniques could be more explicitly discussed.

I have two key questions for the authors: First, could you elaborate on how the corruption rate q affects performance, and whether there's an optimal range for q that might depend on document length? Second, how does Doc2VecC compare to other methods that use similar averaging approaches but with different regularization mechanisms, such as the weighted averaging used in Word2Vec+IDF with IDF weighting?

The paper makes a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning. The simplicity of the model architecture combined with its strong performance and efficiency makes it particularly appealing for practical applications. The theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer is particularly strong and adds significant depth to the paper. While the empirical evaluation could be expanded, the core contribution is novel, well-executed, and likely to be useful to the broader community. The authors have successfully demonstrated that a simple averaging approach can be made highly effective through an elegant regularization mechanism, and this insight is valuable for future research in document representation learning.
