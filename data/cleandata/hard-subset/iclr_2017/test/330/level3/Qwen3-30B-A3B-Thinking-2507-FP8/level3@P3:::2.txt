

# Review for "Document Vectors through Corruption" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective document representation learning framework. The authors propose representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a data-dependent regularization mechanism that upweights rare/informative words and suppresses common, non-discriminative words. This regularization is achieved through a corruption model that randomly removes words during training, which the authors theoretically justify as introducing a special form of regularization. The method demonstrates strong performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB dataset), document classification (Wikipedia dataset), and semantic relatedness (SICK dataset), while maintaining exceptional training and inference speed. The authors claim Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art results while being significantly more efficient than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents a well-motivated and practical approach with clear advantages in efficiency, the empirical evidence provided is insufficient to convince that the method generalizes as claimed across diverse tasks. The technical contribution is limited, and the paper would benefit from more comprehensive evaluation and clarification.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its simplicity and efficiency. The approach is refreshingly straightforward, avoiding the complex architectures of many competing methods while demonstrating strong performance. The corruption mechanism is well-motivated and theoretically justified, and the experimental results on sentiment analysis and document classification are compelling.

However, the paper's main weaknesses are significant. First, the technical contribution is not novel - the core idea of averaging word embeddings for document representation has been explored in prior work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), and the corruption mechanism is conceptually similar to dropout and denoising autoencoders. The paper's contribution is primarily in the specific implementation and empirical validation rather than in new technical concepts.

Second, the empirical evaluation is limited to only two primary tasks (sentiment analysis and document classification) with a third task (semantic relatedness) showing more modest gains. The reviewer requested additional evaluation on more than two tasks to establish general superiority, and the current paper does not provide this. The semantic relatedness task results are particularly underwhelming compared to the other two tasks, suggesting the method may not generalize as broadly as claimed.

Third, the paper lacks a critical comparison to a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states, which is a widely used and effective baseline for document representation. The authors compare to RNN-LM but do not clarify whether this was trained as a language model or for classification, nor whether they used the final hidden state or the average of hidden states.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen this work, the authors should:
- Expand the experimental evaluation to include at least three diverse tasks where document representation is critical, with results clearly demonstrating general superiority.
- Add a comparison against a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states, which would provide a more meaningful benchmark against contemporary methods.
- Clarify the RNN-LM details (training objective, representation used) in the experiments section.
- Consider including a more detailed analysis of the regularization effect, perhaps by visualizing how word embeddings change with different corruption rates.
- Discuss the limitations of the approach more thoroughly, particularly why it performs well on longer documents (IMDB) but less well on shorter sentences (SICK).

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states "RNN-LM" as a baseline, but does not clarify whether this was trained as a language model or to minimize classification error. Please specify the training objective and whether the final hidden state or average of hidden states was used as the document representation.

2. The paper claims superiority over multiple baselines, but does not compare against a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states, which is a standard baseline for document representation. Why was this comparison omitted, and how would Doc2VecC perform against this specific architecture?

3. The paper reports strong results on sentiment analysis and document classification, but only modest gains on semantic relatedness. Can you provide more insight into why the method performs differently across these tasks, and whether there are specific characteristics of the semantic relatedness task that make it less suitable for Doc2VecC?

4. The corruption mechanism is presented as data-dependent regularization, but the paper does not explore how the regularization strength varies across different types of words (e.g., nouns vs. verbs) or how it interacts with the vocabulary size. Could you elaborate on these aspects?
