

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel framework for document representation learning. Doc2VecC represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words during training. The authors argue this approach efficiently captures semantic meaning while introducing a data-dependent regularization that favors informative words and suppresses common, non-discriminative ones. The model achieves strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, with the key advantages of efficient training (billions of words per hour on a single machine) and fast inference (no expensive document-specific parameter learning required). The paper demonstrates that document representations generated by averaging word embeddings can be as effective as more complex models when trained with this corruption mechanism.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a simple yet effective approach to document representation that achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple tasks with significant efficiency advantages over existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling case for Doc2VecC's effectiveness. The empirical results are strong: Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis (IMDB dataset), document classification (Wikipedia subset), and semantic relatedness (SICK dataset), with the added benefit of much faster training and inference. The key advantage is the elimination of the expensive inference step required by models like Paragraph Vectors, which must learn document-specific vectors for unseen documents.

While the core idea (averaging word embeddings) has been explored previously (e.g., Huang et al., 2012), the authors make a significant contribution by demonstrating how to effectively train word embeddings such that they can be meaningfully averaged to form document representations. The corruption mechanism is not entirely novel (it's essentially input-layer dropout), but the authors provide a thorough theoretical justification for how this mechanism functions as data-dependent regularization that favors informative words. The paper convincingly shows that this approach naturally incorporates the regularization needed to make simple averaging effective, eliminating the need for additional techniques like subsampling frequent words or IDF weighting.

The paper's contribution is particularly valuable for practical applications where efficiency is critical. The ability to train on billions of words per hour on a single machine and generate document representations in seconds (vs. minutes for competing methods) represents a significant practical advantage, especially for large-scale applications. The empirical results consistently show that Doc2VecC achieves comparable or better performance than more complex models while being orders of magnitude faster to train and infer.

## 4. Additional Feedback
While the paper is strong, the following improvements would enhance its clarity and impact:

- The paper could better distinguish its contribution from prior work on averaging word embeddings (Huang et al., 2012). A more detailed discussion of how the corruption mechanism enables effective averaging where previous approaches failed would strengthen the novelty claim.

- The theoretical analysis of the corruption as regularization is insightful, but the connection to standard dropout techniques could be more explicitly addressed. A brief comparison to how standard dropout works in neural networks would help readers understand the relationship.

- The paper mentions that "the model complexity of Doc2VecC is decoupled from the size of the training corpus," but it doesn't explicitly state how this compares to other methods in terms of memory requirements. A brief discussion of memory efficiency would complement the training speed claims.

- The visualization of document representations in Figure 3 is compelling, but the paper could more clearly explain why Doc2VecC produces better clustering than Doc2Vec. A sentence or two about how the data-dependent regularization leads to more discriminative word embeddings would strengthen this.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
To help me better assess the paper's claims, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper states that "Doc2VecC reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed from the paradigm shown in Figure 1." However, DEA typically uses reconstruction as the objective, while Doc2VecC uses a word prediction objective. Could you clarify the precise relationship between these two approaches?

2. The paper mentions that "the corruption introduced in Doc2VecC acts as a data-dependent regularization that suppresses the embeddings of frequent but uninformative words." In Table 3, it shows that Doc2VecC suppresses common words, but it's not clear how this differs from standard subsampling techniques used in Word2Vec. Could you provide more detail on how this regularization mechanism differs from and improves upon subsampling?

3. The paper states that "Doc2VecC naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of words that are frequent but uninformative, therefore does not rely on [subsampling]." However, the Word2Vec+IDF baseline in Table 1 does use subsampling. Could you elaborate on why the authors believe Doc2VecC's regularization is stronger than standard subsampling?
