- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as averages of word embeddings with a corruption-based, data-dependent regularizer that downweights common words and upweights informative ones.  
- Claim: The method yields strong performance on IMDB (sentiment), Wikipedia (document classification), and SICK (semantic relatedness) while being much faster than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought.  
- Decision: Reject — reviewer finds the empirical evidence insufficient and the technical contribution limited.  
- Strength: Simple, efficient, and practically appealing approach with a sensible corruption mechanism and theoretical justification.  
- Strength: Compelling results on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks.  
- Weakness: Limited novelty — averaging word embeddings has prior precedent and the corruption idea resembles dropout/denoising autoencoders.  
- Weakness: Empirical evaluation is narrow (primarily two tasks) and semantic relatedness gains are modest, casting doubt on broad generalization.  
- Weakness: Missing critical baseline — no comparison to a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states.  
- Weakness: Unclear experimental details for the RNN-LM baseline (training objective and representation used).  
- Requested improvements: Expand evaluation to at least three diverse tasks to demonstrate general superiority.  
- Requested improvements: Add comparison against bidirectional LSTM (concatenated final hidden states) and clarify RNN-LM setup.  
- Requested improvements: Provide deeper analysis of the regularization effect (e.g., visualization across corruption rates) and discuss limitations (why it fits long documents better than short sentences).  
- Questions for authors: Clarify RNN-LM training objective and representation used; justify omission of BiLSTM comparison; explain differing task performance (IMDB vs SICK); detail how corruption strength interacts with word types and vocabulary size.
