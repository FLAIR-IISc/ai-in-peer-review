- Introduces Doc2VecC: document vectors as averages of word embeddings trained with random-word corruption as data-dependent regularization.
- Decision: Accept — simple, practical approach with strong empirical and efficiency gains despite limited theoretical novelty.
- Key strength: Combines simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical performance to address inefficient unseen-document representation methods.
- Corruption mechanism: Randomly removing words suppresses common non-discriminative embeddings while preserving rare informative ones, distinct from standard dropout.
- Empirical gains: Substantial improvements across tasks (e.g., +15% vs Word2Vec+IDF on IMDB), outperforming Paragraph Vectors and SemEval baselines.
- Efficiency: Trains billions of words/hour on one machine and produces document representations instantly by averaging.
- Ablation and comparisons: Paper provides ablation studies supporting the importance of corruption and multi-task comparisons.
- Limited novelty: Contribution is primarily empirical/practical rather than theoretical, justified by performance and efficiency.
- Suggestion: Better acknowledge prior sum-of-words work (Huang et al., 2012) and clarify distinctions.
- Suggestion: Explicitly state and justify that the main contribution is the combination of averaging with corruption, not individual components.
- Suggestion: Provide detailed comparison to word2vec subsampling to show specific improvements from the corruption mechanism.
- Clarifying questions: Sensitivity of performance to corruption rate q (q=0.9 used without analysis).
- Clarifying questions: Request more theoretical justification for why corruption enforces document semantics in averaged embeddings.
- Clarifying questions: How does reducing to Denoising Autoencoders (when removing local context) compare in implementation and performance to standard DEA?
