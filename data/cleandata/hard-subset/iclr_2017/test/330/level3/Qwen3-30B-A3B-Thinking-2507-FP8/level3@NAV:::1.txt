

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, enhanced with a corruption-based regularization mechanism. The authors demonstrate that their approach, which combines a skip-gram style objective with a global context vector regularizer and dropout, naturally enforces that document semantics can be captured through averaging word embeddings during training. The method is efficient (training time scales with vocabulary size, not corpus size), produces high-quality representations, and significantly outperforms baselines on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks.

I recommend acceptance. The core contribution—combining these components in this specific form—is novel and well-motivated by the observation that document semantics can be captured through simple averaging of word embeddings, with corruption providing a data-dependent regularizer that favors informative words. The detailed analysis in Section 3 of how corruption serves as regularization is particularly strong.

The main limitations concern the empirical evaluation: the paper is relatively weak in scope, limited to sentiment analysis and k-way classification tasks, and devotes excessive space to t-SNE visualizations rather than deeper analysis of model behavior across diverse tasks. The baselines used (e.g., BoW, Word2Vec+IDF) are somewhat weak compared to more recent approaches, which limits the impact of the results.

To improve the paper, I suggest: (1) expanding empirical evaluation to more diverse tasks (e.g., text summarization, question answering) to better demonstrate generalizability; (2) replacing some t-SNE visualizations with more rigorous analysis of model behavior; (3) comparing against stronger baselines like BERT-based representations. 

I have two key questions: (1) Could the authors elaborate on why their corruption-based regularizer specifically outperforms standard word subsampling techniques used in Word2Vec? (2) How does Doc2VecC scale to extremely long documents (e.g., full books) compared to sequence-based models like LSTMs, given the authors' claim that it handles longer paragraphs better than skip-thought vectors?
