- Summary: Proposes Doc2VecC — represent documents by averaging word embeddings with random-word corruption during training, yielding data-dependent regularization that favors informative words and enables fast, effective document representations.

- Decision: Accept — simple, effective method achieving state-of-the-art results with major efficiency gains.

- Empirical strengths: Matches/outperforms baselines on IMDB, Wikipedia subset, and SICK; eliminates expensive per-document inference (vs. Paragraph Vectors); trains/infers orders of magnitude faster.

- Novelty/analysis: Contribution lies in training embeddings that can be meaningfully averaged; corruption is framed as data-dependent regularization (similar to input dropout) that suppresses common non-discriminative words.

- Practical value: Scales to billions of words per hour on a single machine and generates document vectors in seconds, making it attractive for large-scale applications.

- Prior-work distinction: Needs clearer differentiation from prior averaging-of-embeddings work (e.g., Huang et al., 2012) and explanation of why corruption enables effectiveness where earlier methods did not.

- Dropout connection: Theoretical analysis is insightful but should more explicitly relate the corruption mechanism to standard dropout and clarify similarities/differences.

- Memory comparison: Claim that model complexity is decoupled from corpus size should be supported with explicit memory-efficiency comparisons to other methods.

- Visualization explanation: Figure 3 clustering is compelling but would benefit from a clearer explanation of how data-dependent regularization yields more discriminative embeddings than Doc2Vec.

- Clarifying Question 1: Clarify the precise relationship between Doc2VecC and Denoising Autoencoders, given Doc2VecC uses a word-prediction objective while DEA uses reconstruction.

- Clarifying Question 2: Explain how corruption-based suppression of frequent words differs from and improves upon standard Word2Vec subsampling (as illustrated in Table 3).

- Clarifying Question 3: Elaborate why the authors claim Doc2VecC's regularization is stronger than standard subsampling and how this relates to the Word2Vec+IDF baseline using subsampling.
