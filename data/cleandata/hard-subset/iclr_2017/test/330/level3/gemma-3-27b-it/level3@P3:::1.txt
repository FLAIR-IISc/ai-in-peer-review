This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings, incorporating a corruption model for regularization and efficiency. The core idea – leveraging the compositionality of word embeddings through a simple averaging mechanism – is well-motivated by prior work demonstrating semantic relationships within vector space (e.g., “king” – “man” + “woman” ≈ “queen”). The authors convincingly argue that the combination of skip-gram style objectives with a global context vector regularizer and dropout is a valuable contribution, despite the individual components being established techniques. Section 3 provides a particularly insightful analysis of the model's behavior, explaining the intuition behind the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularization.

**Accept.** The method is interesting, well-composed, efficient, and likely useful to others. While the empirical evaluation is limited, the theoretical justification and clear explanation of the approach are strong enough to warrant acceptance.

The paper effectively addresses the problem of learning document representations, specifically aiming for an efficient and scalable approach compared to methods like Paragraph Vectors that suffer from parameter growth with corpus size. The approach is well-motivated, building upon the successes of Word2Vec and acknowledging the limitations of prior document vector methods. The authors accurately position their work within the broader landscape of text representation learning, referencing relevant literature from BoW models to more recent deep learning techniques. The novel contribution lies in the specific combination of averaging word embeddings *with* the corruption-based regularization, which addresses a key challenge in representing documents effectively. 

The paper largely supports its claims. The ablation study and analysis in Section 3 strongly suggest that the corruption mechanism acts as a beneficial regularizer, suppressing uninformative words and focusing on those that contribute to document semantics. The experimental results, though limited in scope, demonstrate competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The efficiency gains, especially regarding test time representation generation, are also substantiated by the reported timings. However, the reliance on relatively simple datasets and limited baselines weakens the overall empirical support. The t-SNE visualizations, while helpful for qualitative understanding, do not constitute rigorous empirical evidence.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Baselines:** The choice of baselines is a concern echoed by other reviewers. While including standard approaches like BoW and Word2Vec+IDF is good, the "soft" baselines (DEA, Paragraph Vectors) don’t represent strong, state-of-the-art comparisons. Stronger baselines, potentially including more recent transformer-based approaches (even if computationally expensive to train for comparison), would significantly strengthen the empirical evaluation.
*   **Dataset Diversity:** The experiments are concentrated on relatively standard datasets. Evaluating Doc2VecC on a more diverse set of datasets, including those with different characteristics (e.g., longer documents, different domains), would increase confidence in the model’s generalizability.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While Section 3 provides a good analysis of the corruption mechanism, more detailed ablation studies examining the impact of different corruption rates (q) and embedding dimensions would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discussing the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters (learning rate, window size, etc.) would be beneficial.
*   **Negative Sampling:** A bit more detail on the negative sampling implementation would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that Paragraph Vectors suffer from computational expense during inference for unseen documents. Could you quantify this computational cost more precisely, perhaps providing a breakdown of the steps involved and their respective runtimes?
2.  You claim the data-dependent regularization suppresses uninformative words. Is there a way to directly inspect *which* words are being suppressed and to what degree? Could you provide examples of words with significantly reduced embedding norms after training with Doc2VecC compared to Word2Vec?
3.  The performance on the semantic relatedness task (SICK) is competitive. However, the task itself has been criticized for issues with annotation quality. How confident are you that the observed performance accurately reflects the model's capabilities, given these potential limitations of the dataset?
4.  You mention that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate 'q'. Could you show a graph or table demonstrating performance across a range of 'q' values?
