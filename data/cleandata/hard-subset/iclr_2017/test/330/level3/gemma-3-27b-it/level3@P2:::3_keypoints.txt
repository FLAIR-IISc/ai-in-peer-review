- Decision: Accept — simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results justify publication despite limited methodological novelty.
- Contribution: Proposes Doc2VecC — represent documents as the average of word embeddings learned jointly with a corruption (random word dropout) mechanism.
- Main advantage: Efficient test-time document vector generation compared to methods that require inference for unseen documents (e.g., Paragraph Vectors).
- Empirical performance: Matches or surpasses state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
- Evaluation quality: Thorough comparisons to baselines (BoW, DEA, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thought), timing benchmarks, ablation study, and word analogy results support claims.
- Theoretical/interpretive claim: Corruption encourages learning meaningful word embeddings and acts as a data-dependent regularizer.
- Novelty assessment: Individual components (averaging, dropout) are not novel, but their combination and empirical demonstration constitute a valuable, practical contribution.
- Clarify corruption: Recommend explicitly framing corruption as input-layer dropout and discussing optimal dropout rates.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Request a sensitivity analysis showing model robustness to corruption rate q (e.g., a small plot).
- Negative sampling: Ask for details on negative sampling parameters (number of negative samples) for reproducibility.
- Computational resources: Suggest reporting RAM in addition to CPU used.
- Qualitative examples: Recommend including example documents and their vectors to illustrate semantic capture.
- Pre-trained embeddings: Ask for discussion/experiments on impact of initializing with pre-trained vectors (e.g., GloVe) versus training from scratch.
- Question on scaling: How does test-time efficiency scale with document length and are there memory limits for very long documents?
- Request elaboration: Ask authors to detail how corruption yields data-dependent regularization and its effect on overfitting and interpretability.
- Skip-thought question: Is poor Skip-thought performance on IMDB due to document length or differences in pre-training datasets?
- Paragraph Vectors question: Why did Paragraph Vectors perform poorly on word analogy — known limitation or experimental setup issue?
