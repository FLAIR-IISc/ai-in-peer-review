This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as the average of their word embeddings. The core idea – leveraging the compositional properties of word embeddings – is combined with a corruption mechanism during training, achieved through random word dropout, and a word2vec-style loss function. While the individual components aren’t novel, the paper demonstrates strong empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, alongside a significant efficiency advantage in generating document representations.

**Accept** – Despite the limited methodological novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and strong performance of Doc2VecC justify its acceptance. The model offers a compelling alternative to more complex document embedding techniques, particularly in scenarios demanding fast inference.

The paper tackles the problem of learning effective document representations, a crucial step in many NLP tasks. It specifically aims to address the limitations of traditional methods like bag-of-words (high dimensionality, sparsity) and more recent approaches like Paragraph Vectors (high parameter count, expensive inference). Doc2VecC proposes a solution that is both computationally efficient and performs competitively with state-of-the-art methods.

The approach is well-motivated, building on the established idea that linear combinations of word embeddings can capture semantic meaning (as demonstrated by Mikolov et al.’s word analogy examples). The paper appropriately positions itself within the existing literature, acknowledging previous work on word and document embeddings (Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, Denoising Autoencoders). While the core components are not new, the combination – learning document representations as simple averages *with* corruption during training – provides a practical and effective solution. The rationale for the corruption mechanism as a form of data-dependent regularization is clearly explained and theoretically grounded.

The paper provides strong support for its claims. The experimental results across three diverse tasks – sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness – consistently demonstrate that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods. The ablation study regarding the corruption mechanism and the data-dependent regularization further strengthens the findings. The reported training and inference times showcase the model’s efficiency advantage. The visualization using t-SNE provides qualitative evidence of the learned representations’ effectiveness. The detailed comparison with other algorithms across different embedding sizes, alongside the word analogy experiment, provides sufficient evidence to justify the claim of improved word embeddings.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify “corruption”**: While the paper explains the mathematical formulation of the corruption, it could benefit from a more intuitive explanation of *why* randomly removing words leads to better representations. Is it forcing the model to rely more on the remaining informative words? A more in-depth discussion here would be valuable.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: The paper mentions that the model isn’t sensitive to the corruption rate (q), but it would be helpful to include a small ablation study visualizing the performance variation with different q values. This would provide further confidence in the robustness of the approach.
*   **Choice of Word Embedding Initialization**: The paper doesn’t mention if the word embeddings were initialized randomly or using pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe). Specifying this would be useful for reproducibility and understanding the impact of pre-training.
*   **Impact of Vocabulary Size**: The performance of Doc2VecC may be sensitive to the vocabulary size. It would be helpful to discuss the impact of different vocabulary cutoffs and potentially include results with varying vocabularies.
*   **Negative Sampling**: The paper mentions negative sampling but does not detail the number of negative samples used. Adding this parameter to the experimental setup would improve reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the corruption mechanism introduces a "special form of regularization." Could you elaborate on how this regularization differs from, or complements, other regularization techniques commonly used in word embedding models (e.g., L2 regularization)?
2.  You mention that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on the word subsampling trick used in Word2Vec+AVG. Is this due to the inherent regularization effect of the corruption mechanism, or are there other contributing factors?
3.  The word analogy results show a significant improvement with Doc2VecC. Do you have any hypotheses as to why this improvement is observed, given that the method is primarily focused on document-level representation learning? Does the corruption process specifically benefit the learning of word-level relationships?
4.  The paper primarily focuses on averaging word embeddings. Have you explored other combination methods (e.g., weighted averaging using TF-IDF) and if so, how do they compare to simple averaging in terms of performance and efficiency?
