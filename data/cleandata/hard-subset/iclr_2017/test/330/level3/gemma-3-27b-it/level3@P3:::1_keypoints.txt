- Proposes Doc2VecC: simple averaged word-embedding document representation with a corruption (dropout) regularizer for efficiency and scalability.  
- Leverages compositionality of word embeddings; approach is well-motivated by prior vector-space semantic work.  
- Combines skip-gram objectives, a global context vector regularizer, and dropout — individually known techniques but usefully integrated.  
- Section 3 gives insightful analysis interpreting corruption as a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses uninformative words.  
- Recommendation: Accept — method is interesting, efficient, and likely useful despite limited empirical breadth.  
- Positioned well in literature, addressing Paragraph Vector’s parameter growth and building on Word2Vec successes.  
- Novelty lies in the specific pairing of simple averaging with corruption-based regularization to improve document representations.  
- Ablation study supports the regularization effect of corruption, focusing embeddings on informative words.  
- Empirical results are competitive on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness, and show clear efficiency gains at test time.  
- Empirical evaluation is limited by simple datasets, narrow baselines, and reliance on qualitative t-SNE visualizations.  
- Baseline choice is a concern — stronger, more recent baselines (e.g., transformer-based models) are missing.  
- Need more dataset diversity (longer documents, varied domains) to establish generalizability.  
- Requests further ablation on corruption rates (q), embedding dimensions, and hyperparameter sensitivity (learning rate, window size).  
- Asks for more implementation detail on negative sampling.  
- Questions for authors: quantify Paragraph Vectors’ inference cost; show which words are suppressed and example embedding-norm changes; comment on reliability of SICK results; provide performance vs. corruption rate (q) plots.
