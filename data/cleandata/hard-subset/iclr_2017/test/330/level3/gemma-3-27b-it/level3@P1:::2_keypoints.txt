- Decision: Reject — approach is clear and efficient but technical novelty is limited and empirical validation is not sufficiently compelling.
- Proposed method: Doc2VecC — average word embeddings with a corruption-based regularizer to learn document representations efficiently.
- Strengths: Simple, fast to train/test and produces competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.
- Limited novelty: Averaging word embeddings is not new and the corruption mechanism resembles dropout/denoising autoencoders; contribution is mainly their combination.
- Theoretical gap: Taylor expansion explanation is detailed but its practical impact and link to observed gains are not fully elucidated.
- Empirical limitations: Evaluation covers only three tasks, so generalizability beyond tested settings is not demonstrated.
- Performance gains: Reported improvements are statistically significant but incremental; LSTM methods still outperform on semantic relatedness.
- Recommendation: Expand empirical evaluation to more diverse tasks (e.g., topic modeling, QA, summarization) to establish broader applicability.
- Recommendation: Conduct ablation studies on the corruption mechanism (optimal rate, different strategies) to clarify its effect.
- Recommendation: Compare against stronger baselines (e.g., bidirectional LSTM concatenating final hidden states).
- Recommendation: Provide in-depth analysis of learned word embeddings and how they differ from Word2Vec (e.g., whether informative words are upweighted).
- Clarify RNN-LM experiment: specify whether it was trained as a standard language model or directly for classification, and whether final hidden state or averaged hidden states were used as the representation.
- Questions for authors: Explain intuition behind corruption and its link to improvements; quantify gains from removing subsampling; report computational cost of Taylor approximation; clarify RNN-LM training details.
