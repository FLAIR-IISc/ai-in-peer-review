- Decision: Reject.  
- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as averaged word embeddings with a corruption model acting as a data-dependent regularizer.  
- Strength: Simple, easy to implement, scales well, and shows strong speed and empirical performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Strength: Data-dependent regularization is a clever mechanism that favors informative words and suppresses common ones.  
- Weakness: Technical novelty is limited; many ideas (embedding averaging, data-dependent regularization, dropout-like corruption) have precedents.  
- Weakness: Evaluation scope is narrow (only three tasks), so general superiority is not convincingly established.  
- Concern: Comparisons to Skip-thought Vectors are limited because that model was not retrained specifically for these evaluations.  
- Missing baseline: No direct comparison with a strong bidirectional LSTM baseline (concatenated hidden states), which is a common competitive approach.  
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameter tuning details (search strategy and ranges) are not provided.  
- Statistical rigor: Results lack standard deviations or confidence intervals to assess significance.  
- Explanation gap: The paper needs a clearer, more intuitive explanation of why the data-dependent regularizer works beyond stop-word suppression.  
- Presentation: References formatting is inconsistent and could be improved.  
- Requested ablation: Authors should provide an ablation study isolating the corruption model's contribution.  
- Embedding-size question: Need evidence whether Doc2VecC’s advantage over Word2Vec holds across different embedding dimensions.  
- Generalizability question: Authors should evaluate on additional tasks (e.g., question answering, summarization) to strengthen generalizability claims.  
- Hyperparameter question: Report how performance varies with corruption rate q and identify an optimal range.  
- RNN-LM clarification: Clarify how the RNN-LM baseline was trained and which document representation (final hidden state or averaged hidden states) was used.
