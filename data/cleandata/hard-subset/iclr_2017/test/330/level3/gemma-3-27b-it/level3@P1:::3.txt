## Review of MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION

This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a method for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings. The key innovation lies in a corruption model—randomly dropping words during training—that encourages informative words to have larger embeddings while suppressing less discriminative ones. The authors demonstrate strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, alongside significant gains in training and inference efficiency.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. While the individual components of Doc2VecC – averaging word embeddings for document representation and the use of dropout-style corruption – are not entirely novel, the combination is effective, well-motivated, and offers a compelling trade-off between simplicity, efficiency, and performance. The experimental results are strong and demonstrate a clear benefit over several established baselines. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of learning effective document representations, acknowledging the limitations of bag-of-words models and the computational expense of more complex approaches like Paragraph Vectors. Doc2VecC offers an attractive solution by leveraging the established success of Word2Vec and building upon it with a simple yet effective regularization technique. 

The motivation for the corruption model is sound. By randomly dropping words, the model is forced to rely on the remaining, potentially more informative words to reconstruct the document’s meaning. This leads to a data-dependent regularization, as claimed, and effectively pushes down the embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The authors convincingly explain how this process relates to second-order Taylor expansion of the loss function, solidifying the theoretical justification. 

Crucially, the experimental results support the claims. The paper provides a comprehensive comparison against a range of relevant baselines, including BoW, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec, Doc2Vec, and Skip-thought Vectors. Doc2VecC consistently matches or outperforms these methods on all three tasks, demonstrating its effectiveness. The reported speed improvements, particularly during test time, are substantial and represent a significant practical benefit. The visualizations in Figure 3 further support the claim that Doc2VecC produces meaningful document representations.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, a few points could be addressed to further improve clarity and impact:

* **Novelty Discussion:** The authors acknowledge the existing work on averaging word embeddings and dropout techniques. Expanding the discussion on *how* Doc2VecC differs from and improves upon these prior approaches would strengthen the contribution. For example, how does the specific corruption scheme contribute to performance beyond standard dropout?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate *q*. Providing some empirical evidence for this claim (e.g., a small ablation study) would be beneficial. Similarly, exploring the sensitivity of the model to other hyperparameters, such as embedding dimensionality, would strengthen the analysis.
* **Computational Details:** While the paper mentions the hardware used for experiments, providing more precise details regarding training time (per epoch, total) would enhance reproducibility and facilitate comparison with other works.
* **Error Analysis:** A brief error analysis on the classification tasks could provide insights into the types of documents or categories where Doc2VecC struggles, potentially suggesting areas for future improvement.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the corruption mechanism introduces a data-dependent regularization. Could you elaborate on how this regularization differs from a simple L1 or L2 regularization applied directly to the word embeddings?
2.  In the semantic relatedness task, Doc2VecC’s performance is slightly worse than LSTM-based methods. Do you have any hypotheses as to why Doc2VecC might be less effective on short sentences compared to longer documents?
3.  You mention that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate *q*. Could you briefly present the results of a small experiment varying *q* to demonstrate this robustness?
4.  Are there any concerns about potential biases present in the pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., GloVe) influencing the performance of Doc2VecC? If so, how might these biases be mitigated?
