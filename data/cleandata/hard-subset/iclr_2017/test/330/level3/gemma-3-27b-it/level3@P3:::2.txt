## Review of “Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC)”

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework based on averaging word embeddings with a data-dependent regularization component. The core idea is to upweight informative/rare words while driving common words towards zero, thereby enhancing the quality of document representations. The authors demonstrate improvements over baseline embedding methods on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks, highlighting the model's simplicity and speed.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper proposes a reasonable approach and delivers competitive empirical results, the technical contribution feels incremental. The strengths lie primarily in the efficient implementation and combination of existing techniques, rather than fundamentally novel ideas. Furthermore, the evaluation, though promising, lacks sufficient breadth to convincingly demonstrate general superiority.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for Doc2VecC is well-articulated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of bag-of-words and the challenges with scaling Paragraph Vectors. The idea of incorporating a corruption mechanism to introduce regularization during training is intuitive and potentially beneficial. The reported speed advantage—training on billions of words per hour—is significant and adds practical value. 

However, the individual components of Doc2VecC are not entirely new. Averaging word embeddings has been explored previously (Huang et al., 2012, as cited in the paper), and data-dependent regularization techniques are common in machine learning. The corruption model itself, while effective, resembles dropout, a well-established regularization method. The primary contribution appears to be the specific application and combination of these techniques within the document representation learning context. 

The empirical evaluation, while showing strong performance on sentiment analysis and document classification, feels limited. The authors compare against a diverse set of baselines, but demonstrating superiority on only two tasks isn’t conclusive. The SICK dataset for semantic relatedness provides a third data point, but its relevance to typical document representation tasks is less clear. A more comprehensive evaluation across a wider range of tasks and datasets would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. 

The analysis of the learned word embeddings and the demonstration of data-dependent regularization's effect (Table 3) are insightful and provide some justification for the observed performance gains. However, a more in-depth analysis of *which* words are being upweighted or downweighted, and *why*, would be valuable.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **RNN-LM Clarification:** The reviewer rightly points out the need for clarification regarding the RNN-LM baseline. Was it trained as a traditional language model (predicting the next word), or was it specifically trained to minimize classification error? Furthermore, was the final hidden state, or an average of the hidden states across the sequence, used as the document representation? Knowing this will help understand the comparative strengths of Doc2VecC.
*   **Bidirectional LSTM Comparison:** The suggestion to compare against a bidirectional LSTM (BiLSTM) concatenating final hidden states is excellent. BiLSTMs are a widely-used and strong baseline for document representation, and a comparison would provide a more rigorous evaluation of Doc2VecC's performance.
*   **Ablation Study:** Consider performing an ablation study to isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism. How much does the corruption contribute to the overall performance? Does varying the corruption rate (q) significantly impact results?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Briefly discuss the sensitivity of Doc2VecC to its hyperparameters (embedding dimension, learning rate, corruption rate). This would provide insights into the model's robustness.
*   **Vocabulary Size:** The paper mentions applying a cutoff to the vocabulary size. Discuss the impact of this cutoff on performance. Is there a sweet spot for vocabulary size?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Please clarify the training objective and representation method used for the RNN-LM baseline.
2.  Could you provide the results of a comparison between Doc2VecC and a bidirectional LSTM with concatenated final hidden states on the sentiment analysis and document classification tasks?
3.  Can you provide examples of words that are significantly upweighted or downweighted by the corruption mechanism, and explain why this occurs in the context of the training data?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the corruption rate (q)? Please provide results for different values of q.
