- Introduces Doc2VecC: represents documents as the average of word embeddings with random word-dropout corruption and a word2vec-style loss.  
- Core components (averaging, dropout, word2vec loss) are not novel individually but effective in combination.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Shows significant efficiency advantages in training and especially inference for generating document representations.  
- Recommended for acceptance due to simplicity, efficiency, and competitive performance despite limited methodological novelty.  
- Aims to address bag-of-words sparsity/high dimensionality and Paragraph Vector’s parameter/inference costs.  
- Well-motivated by prior work on compositionality of word embeddings and appropriately situated in related literature.  
- Corruption mechanism is theoretically framed as data-dependent regularization and improves robustness.  
- Ablation studies (including corruption effect) and comparisons across embedding sizes strengthen empirical claims.  
- t-SNE visualization provides qualitative evidence of representation quality; word analogy experiments suggest improved word embeddings.  
- Request to clarify intuitively why random word removal yields better representations (mechanism/explanation).  
- Recommend an ablation or plot showing performance sensitivity to corruption rate q for robustness claims.  
- Ask to specify word embedding initialization (random vs. pre-trained) for reproducibility and impact assessment.  
- Suggest discussing impact of vocabulary size / cutoff and providing results for different vocabularies.  
- Request details on negative sampling (number of negatives) to improve experimental reproducibility.  
- Question: how does the corruption-induced regularization differ from or complement standard regularizers (e.g., L2)?  
- Question: is the omission of Word2Vec-style word subsampling due to the corruption’s regularization or other factors?  
- Question: why does Doc2VecC improve word analogy performance despite being focused on document-level learning?  
- Question: have other combination methods (e.g., TF-IDF weighted averaging) been explored and how do they compare in performance and efficiency?
