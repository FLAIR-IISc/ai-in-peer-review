## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that represents documents as the average of their word embeddings, trained with a corruption mechanism (random word dropout). The authors motivate this approach by noting the success of averaging word embeddings for compositional semantics and aim to enforce this property during training. While the individual components – joint word/document training and sum-of-words representation – aren’t novel, the paper demonstrates strong empirical results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, alongside a significant efficiency advantage during document representation generation.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The core contribution lies in the successful combination of simple ideas – averaging word embeddings and dropout – leading to a surprisingly effective and computationally efficient document representation method. The empirical validation is robust, consistently outperforming several baselines and offering competitive performance with more complex models. The efficiency gain during inference is particularly noteworthy and addresses a practical limitation of methods like Doc2Vec.

The paper effectively establishes its core premise. It clearly articulates the motivation for averaging word embeddings and how the corruption mechanism acts as a regularizer, favoring informative words. The experimental setup is comprehensive, with comparisons to a diverse set of strong baselines on standard datasets. The reported results consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of Doc2VecC, and the ablation studies (e.g., the effect of the corruption mechanism) provide valuable insights. The authors clearly explain the limitations of their approach, acknowledging the existing work in the field.

However, a few points could strengthen the paper. The description of the corruption mechanism, while intuitive, could benefit from a more in-depth theoretical justification for *why* it functions as a data-dependent regularizer. While the derivation is provided, a more accessible explanation of its impact on the learned embeddings would be beneficial. Additionally, the impact of varying the corruption rate 'q' could be further explored. While mentioned briefly, a more detailed analysis of its effect on performance would be valuable. 

Finally, it would be helpful to understand how the performance scales with document length. The authors mention LSTM-based methods struggle with long documents, and Doc2VecC performs well. Quantifying this relationship would strengthen the contribution. 

Specifically, I’d like to ask the authors:

*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation of *how* the corruption mechanism specifically downweights less informative words and provides a concrete example with learned embeddings?
*   Did you observe any sensitivity to the vocabulary cutoff (currently set at 10)? How does this impact performance?
*   What is the computational cost (training time and memory usage) of Doc2VecC as document size increases, compared to methods like Doc2Vec?
