- Proposal of Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings enforced via a corruption model that yields data-dependent regularization.
- Core idea builds on Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, emphasizing simplicity, efficiency, and improved performance.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reasons for acceptance: novelty of the combined approach, strong theoretical analysis (corruption as regularization), and empirical effectiveness.
- Theoretical contribution: Section 3 derives how corruption acts as data-dependent regularization and justifies performance gains.
- Empirical results: competitive or superior performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.
- Efficiency and scalability: notable test-time inference speedups and reduced parameter footprint compared to Paragraph Vectors.
- Ablation study: demonstrates the importance of the corruption model to the method’s effectiveness.
- Evaluation limitations: experiments are not exhaustive and lack stronger, more recent baselines (e.g., Transformer-based models).
- t-SNE visualizations are overused and offer limited analytical value; space could be better spent on deeper analyses.
- Corruption rate (q) analysis is insufficient—needs systematic study across datasets and tasks to determine dataset dependence and optimal values.
- Effect of initialization: missing comparison between GloVe-initialized and randomly initialized word embeddings to quantify pre-training contribution.
- Questions for authors: how the corruption-based regularization differs from/complements standard regularizers (L1/L2, dropout).
- Questions for authors: performance and retraining cost on dynamic vocabularies (social media/news); how often retraining is needed and computational expense.
- Questions for authors: quantify parameter/memory reduction from the corruption model for large-scale training.
- Questions for authors: potential adaptations for handling longer documents without losing efficiency.
- Questions for authors: rationale for the chosen negative sampling strategy and whether alternative sampling methods were explored.
