## Review of MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings, coupled with a corruption model designed to regularize the learned embeddings. The model aims to improve upon existing methods by being efficient to train and test, and by producing high-quality document representations for tasks like sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. The authors demonstrate competitive performance on these tasks, highlighting the simplicity and speed of their approach.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonably well-motivated and clearly written approach, the technical novelty is limited, and the empirical validation, though positive, is not sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance. The reported improvements, while statistically significant, are incremental, and the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate generalizability beyond the tested tasks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of representing documents as an average of word embeddings is not entirely new (see related works discussed in the paper). The “corruption” mechanism, while a valid regularization technique, builds upon established concepts like dropout and denoising autoencoders. The primary contribution seems to lie in the specific combination of these techniques, and the insight that this combination implicitly provides a data-dependent regularization. 

However, the paper lacks a thorough exploration of why this particular combination is so effective. The explanation regarding the second-order Taylor expansion and resulting regularization is mathematically detailed, but its practical impact and connection to the observed performance gains aren’t fully elucidated. 

Furthermore, the experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, is limited to three tasks. While the results are encouraging, they aren’t sufficient to demonstrate the general applicability of Doc2VecC. Stronger claims of superiority require evaluation on a wider range of datasets and tasks, particularly those with different characteristics and complexities.  The reported performance improvements are also relatively modest, particularly on the semantic relatedness task where LSTM-based methods still outperform Doc2VecC.

**Additional Feedback:**

This is a generally well-written paper with a clear presentation of ideas. To strengthen the work, I suggest the following:

*   **Expand the empirical evaluation:**  Include experiments on more diverse tasks, such as topic modeling, question answering, or text summarization. This will help to establish the generalizability of Doc2VecC.
*   **Ablation studies:** Perform a more detailed analysis of the corruption mechanism. What is the optimal corruption rate? How does the performance change with different corruption strategies? This will help understand the impact of this component.
*   **Comparison to stronger baselines:**  Compare Doc2VecC to more recent and powerful document representation methods. Specifically, a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates the final hidden states would provide a stronger baseline.
*   **Analysis of learned embeddings:**  Provide a more in-depth analysis of the learned word embeddings. How do they differ from those produced by Word2Vec? Are the embeddings truly capturing the desired characteristics (e.g., upweighting informative words)?
*   **Clarify the RNN-LM experiment:** The paper mentions a supervised RNN-LM. Was this model trained as a standard language model (predicting the next word) or was it trained to directly minimize the classification error? Also, was the final hidden state or an average of hidden states used as the document representation?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the corruption mechanism and its connection to the observed improvements in performance? A more intuitive explanation of the regularization effect would be helpful.
2.  You mention that Doc2VecC does not rely on subsampling frequent words. Can you quantify the improvement gained by removing this step from Word2Vec+AVG?
3.  What is the computational cost of the Taylor expansion approximation used in the corruption mechanism? Does it significantly impact the training time?
4.  In the RNN-LM experiment, was the model trained as a language model or to minimize classification error, and was the final hidden state or the average of hidden states used as the representation?
