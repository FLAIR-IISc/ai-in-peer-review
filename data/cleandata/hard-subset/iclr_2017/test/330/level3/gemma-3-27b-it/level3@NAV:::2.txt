## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework based on averaging word embeddings with a novel data-dependent regularization technique. The core idea – upweighting rare/informative words and driving common word embeddings towards zero – is well-motivated, stemming from the observation that linear operations on word embeddings can preserve semantic meaning. The authors demonstrate competitive results, achieving lowest error rates on sentiment analysis and document classification compared to several baseline methods, while also highlighting the efficiency of their approach.

I recommend **Reject** at this time. While the paper presents a sensible approach and promising results, the technical contribution feels incremental. The core components – averaging word embeddings and regularization – are not novel in themselves. The strength of the paper lies in its simplicity and speed, but this is not sufficient for acceptance at a competitive conference. The results, while good, are shown on only two tasks. This makes it difficult to assess the generalizability and true superiority of Doc2VecC.

The paper successfully demonstrates the motivation and places itself within the existing literature, accurately referencing relevant works. The experiments are clearly described, and the improvements over baselines are reported comprehensively. However, the empirical evaluation is a key weakness. Expanding the evaluation to a wider range of tasks (e.g., topic modeling, information retrieval) would significantly strengthen the claims of superior performance. 

Here are some additional points for the authors to consider:

*   **RNN-LM Clarification:** The description of the RNN-LM baseline is unclear. Was it trained as a traditional language model (predicting the next word), or was it trained to directly minimize the classification error? Furthermore, which representation was used for the document – the final hidden state, or an average of hidden states across the sequence?
*   **Bidirectional LSTM Baseline:** The authors should compare Doc2VecC against a bidirectional LSTM that concatenates the final hidden states. This is a widely-used and strong baseline for document representation, and a comparison is crucial to understand where Doc2VecC truly stands.
*   **Ablation Study:** An ablation study investigating the contribution of the corruption mechanism would be valuable. What performance difference is observed without the corruption step? This would better isolate the impact of the proposed regularization.



I would like to understand the design choices behind the corruption rate *q*. Is there a sensitivity analysis on this parameter? How does the choice of *q* affect the learned embeddings and downstream task performance? And, relatedly, could the authors elaborate on the choice of using the average of word embeddings as opposed to a weighted average (e.g., using TF-IDF)?
