- Decision: Accept — recommendation to accept based on effectiveness and efficiency.
- Contribution: Proposes Doc2VecC — document representations by averaging word embeddings with a corruption (dropout) training scheme.
- Key innovation: Corruption model randomly drops words to amplify informative word embeddings and suppress non-discriminative ones.
- Theoretical justification: Authors relate corruption effect to a second-order Taylor expansion, providing a theoretical basis for the regularization.
- Empirical performance: Strong results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness, often matching or outperforming baselines.
- Baseline comparisons: Evaluated against BoW, Denoising Autoencoders, Word2Vec, Doc2Vec, and Skip-thought Vectors.
- Efficiency: Significant training and test-time speed improvements reported.
- Visualization: Figure 3 shows qualitatively meaningful document representations from Doc2VecC.
- Novelty caveat: Averaging embeddings and dropout-like corruption are not entirely novel; combination is effective but needs clearer distinction from prior work.
- Request for clarity: Explain how the specific corruption scheme improves beyond standard dropout.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: Claim of insensitivity to corruption rate q — ask for empirical ablation to support this.
- Additional hyperparams: Suggest exploring sensitivity to embedding dimensionality and other hyperparameters.
- Computational details: Ask for precise training times (per epoch/total) and hardware details to aid reproducibility.
- Error analysis: Recommend a brief error analysis to identify failure modes and categories where Doc2VecC struggles.
- Question on regularization: How does corruption-induced data-dependent regularization differ from direct L1/L2 regularization on embeddings?
- Question on short texts: Why does Doc2VecC underperform LSTM-based methods on short sentences; any hypotheses?
- Request experiment: Please present results varying q to demonstrate robustness.
- Bias concerns: Ask about potential biases from pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) and possible mitigation strategies.
