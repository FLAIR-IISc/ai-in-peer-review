This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple and efficient method for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, learned jointly with a corruption mechanism – essentially, random word dropout – during training. The authors demonstrate strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while highlighting the model's efficiency in generating document vectors, particularly compared to methods requiring inference at test time.

**Decision:** Accept. Despite limited methodological novelty, the simplicity, efficiency, and strong empirical results justify acceptance. The paper presents a compelling approach that addresses a practical challenge in document representation learning – efficient generation of document vectors – without sacrificing performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem it addresses: creating effective document representations efficiently. Existing methods like Paragraph Vectors suffer from scalability issues due to the growing number of parameters with corpus size, and expensive inference for unseen documents. Doc2VecC elegantly sidesteps these issues by leveraging a simple averaging mechanism coupled with a corruption model. The authors convincingly demonstrate that this approach isn't merely a shortcut, but actively encourages the learning of meaningful word embeddings conducive to effective document representation.

The empirical evaluation is thorough. The comparisons against several baselines, including BoW, DEA, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, and Skip-thought Vectors, are well-executed. The results consistently show that Doc2VecC matches or surpasses the performance of state-of-the-art methods across the three evaluated tasks. The reported training and inference times underscore the model’s efficiency, a key advantage highlighted by the authors. The ablation study with the data-dependent regularization (Table 3) is a valuable inclusion, providing insight into the contribution of the corruption mechanism. The word analogy results (Table 5) further validate the quality of the learned word embeddings. 

While the individual components – averaging word embeddings and dropout – are not novel in themselves, their combination within this framework, and the demonstration of its effectiveness, constitutes a valuable contribution. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, acknowledging related approaches and clearly differentiating their contributions.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the “corruption” as dropout:** While the authors allude to the connection, explicitly framing the corruption mechanism as input-layer dropout would strengthen the presentation and connect it to a well-understood technique. This could also open up a discussion on the optimal dropout rate and its impact on performance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate, q. It would be beneficial to include a brief sensitivity analysis demonstrating this claim, perhaps with a small plot showing performance variation with different q values.
*   **Negative Sampling Details:** Briefly elaborate on the parameters used for negative sampling (e.g., number of negative samples). This is a crucial parameter in Word2Vec-style models and should be mentioned for reproducibility.
*   **Computational Resources:** While the paper mentions the CPU used for experimentation, including details about RAM would be helpful.
*   **Qualitative Examples:** Provide a few qualitative examples of documents and their corresponding vector representations. This could help illustrate the model's ability to capture semantic meaning and provide further intuition.
*   **Impact of Pre-trained Embeddings:** The authors mention using pre-trained GloVe vectors for the word analogy task. Discussing the impact of initializing with these vectors versus learning them from scratch for the downstream tasks would be valuable. Does pre-training consistently improve performance, or is the benefit task-dependent?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper emphasizes the efficiency of test-time document vector generation. How does this efficiency scale with document length? Are there any practical limitations in terms of memory usage for very long documents?
2.  You mention that Doc2VecC "intrinsically minimizes" a data-dependent regularization. Could you elaborate on the connection between the corruption mechanism and regularization in more detail? Specifically, how does the regularization prevent overfitting, and how does it impact the interpretability of the learned embeddings?
3.  The Skip-thought vectors perform poorly on the IMDB dataset. Is this solely attributable to the document length, or could other factors, such as differences in the datasets used for pre-training, contribute to the observed performance gap?
4.  In the word analogy experiments, Paragraph Vectors performed poorly. Is this a known limitation of Paragraph Vectors, or could it be related to the specific experimental setup?
