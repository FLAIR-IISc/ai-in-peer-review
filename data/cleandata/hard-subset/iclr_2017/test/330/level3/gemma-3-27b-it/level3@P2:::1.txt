This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of word embeddings, enforced during learning through a corruption model that introduces a data-dependent regularization, favoring informative words and suppressing common ones. The approach builds upon existing work like Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors but offers improvements in efficiency and performance.

**Decision: Accept**

The primary reasons for accepting this paper are the novelty of the combined approach, its theoretical grounding (the detailed analysis of the corruption as regularization in Section 3), and its demonstrated empirical effectiveness, despite some limitations in the evaluation. The method appears to be a valuable contribution to the field of document representation learning, offering a compelling trade-off between simplicity, efficiency, and performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively tackles the problem of generating meaningful document representations. While averaging word embeddings is a simple concept, the authors convincingly argue and demonstrate that enforcing this during the learning process, coupled with the corruption mechanism, yields significantly better results than existing methods. The theoretical analysis in Section 3 is particularly strong. The derivation showing how the corruption acts as a data-dependent regularization is insightful and provides a solid justification for the observed improvements. This is not merely an empirical observation; the authors explain *why* their method works.

The experimental results, while not exhaustive, are promising. The authors compare Doc2VecC against a variety of baselines on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, consistently achieving competitive or superior performance. The efficiency gains, especially in test time inference, are also noteworthy. The detailed comparison with Paragraph Vectors, highlighting the scalability advantage of Doc2VecC, is particularly valuable. The ablation study, demonstrating the importance of the corruption model, further strengthens the claims.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several areas could be improved. The experimental evaluation could be expanded. The baselines, while reasonable, are relatively “soft” – some more recent and stronger baselines (e.g., Transformers-based models) could be included to provide a more challenging comparison.  While the authors acknowledge this in light of other reviewer comments, a stronger benchmark would be beneficial.

The reliance on t-SNE visualizations feels excessive given the limited analytical value they provide. While visually appealing, these plots don’t offer strong evidence of superior performance compared to quantitative metrics. Space dedicated to these visualizations could be better used for more in-depth analysis of the results, such as error analysis or ablation studies on different corruption rates.

The impact of the corruption rate (q) is briefly mentioned but not thoroughly explored. A more systematic investigation of the optimal corruption rate for different datasets and tasks would be valuable. Is the optimal ‘q’ dataset-dependent? Are there scenarios where a lower or higher rate is more effective?

The paper mentions using pre-trained GloVe vectors. It would be helpful to include a comparison of Doc2VecC’s performance when initialized with GloVe versus randomly initialized word embeddings. This would shed light on the contribution of the pre-trained embeddings versus the learning process of Doc2VecC.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper states the corruption model introduces a data-dependent regularization. How does this regularization differ from, or complement, other regularization techniques commonly used in neural networks (e.g., L1/L2 regularization, dropout)?
2.  The experiments focus on relatively static datasets (IMDB, Wikipedia). How does Doc2VecC perform on more dynamic datasets where the vocabulary and distribution of words change over time (e.g., social media data, news streams)? Would retraining be frequently required, and if so, how computationally expensive is this process?
3.  You mention a reduction in parameters due to the corruption model. Can you quantify this parameter reduction and its impact on memory usage during training, particularly for large-scale datasets?
4.  The comparison to Skip-thought Vectors suggests performance is hindered by paragraph length. Do you have any insights into how Doc2VecC might be adapted to better handle longer documents without sacrificing efficiency?
5.  Can you elaborate on the choice of negative sampling in the loss function? Are there alternative sampling strategies that might further improve performance, and if so, have you explored them?
