## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that leverages a skip-gram style objective alongside a global context vector regularizer and dropout. The core idea – representing documents as the average of word embeddings and incorporating a corruption mechanism – is well-motivated by the observed success of averaging word vectors for capturing semantic meaning. The authors clearly articulate how the corruption model functions as a data-dependent regularization, suppressing uninformative words and promoting those with greater discriminatory power. Section 3 provides a detailed and appreciated analysis of this regularization effect and the theoretical basis behind it. 

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While no single component of Doc2VecC is groundbreaking in isolation, the novel combination and the insightful analysis of its behavior make it a valuable contribution. The method appears efficient and likely to be useful for researchers and practitioners in document representation learning.

My assessment is based on addressing the three key questions: 1) The paper tackles the problem of learning effective document representations from text. 2) The approach is well-motivated, building upon existing work like Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors and addressing their limitations, particularly regarding parameter size and test-time efficiency. 3) The paper largely supports its claims, demonstrating competitive performance on sentiment analysis and document classification tasks. However, the empirical evaluation could be strengthened (see feedback below).

**Additional Feedback:**

The empirical evaluation is somewhat limited. While the reported results are promising, the tasks (sentiment analysis, k-way classification) are relatively standard. Exploring tasks that require more nuanced semantic understanding, such as question answering or text summarization, would provide a more robust evaluation. The comparison to baselines, as noted by other reviewers, relies on “soft” baselines; including stronger, more recent approaches, especially those leveraging transformers, would be beneficial. I also share the concern about the heavy reliance on t-SNE visualizations. While visually appealing, they don't offer quantitative insights. Deeper analysis, like examining the learned word embeddings for specific semantic relationships, would be more compelling. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The corruption mechanism (randomly removing words) seems crucial. Have you experimented with different corruption strategies, such as masking based on POS tags or TF-IDF scores, and if so, how did they compare to the current random approach?
2.  You mention that Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization" than Word2Vec+IDF. Could you quantify this regularization effect more precisely, perhaps by comparing the L2 norms of the learned word embeddings?
3.  The paper focuses on averaging word embeddings. Have you considered weighting the embeddings based on TF-IDF or other relevance metrics *during* training (not just as a post-processing step like Word2Vec+IDF), and how would that fit into the corruption framework?
