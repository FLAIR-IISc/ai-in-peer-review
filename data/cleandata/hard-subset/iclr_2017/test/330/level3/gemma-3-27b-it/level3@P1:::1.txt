## Review of MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel approach to learning document representations. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of word embeddings, incorporating a corruption model during training that acts as a data-dependent regularizer. This regularization favors informative words while suppressing less discriminative ones. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while also being computationally efficient. The paper is well-written and the motivation for the approach is clear.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed method, Doc2VecC, is a compelling combination of established techniques – averaging word embeddings and regularization – presented in a novel and insightful manner. The theoretical analysis in Section 3, detailing the regularization properties of the corruption model, is particularly strong and contributes significantly to understanding *why* the method works. The demonstrated efficiency of the model is also a significant benefit.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The strength of this paper lies in its principled approach to document representation learning. While averaging word embeddings isn't a new concept, the authors convincingly argue for enforcing this as a property *during* training, rather than as a post-processing step. The corruption model adds a critical component, offering a clever form of regularization that addresses the common problem of frequent, uninformative words dominating document representations. 

The detailed mathematical analysis in Section 3 clearly explains how the corruption mechanism acts as a data-dependent regularizer, providing valuable insight into the model’s behavior. This level of theoretical grounding elevates the work beyond a purely empirical investigation.

The experimental results, while not groundbreaking, demonstrate that Doc2VecC consistently matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The authors perform comparisons across several tasks, providing a reasonable level of validation. The speed and efficiency of the training process is also a notable advantage.

**Additional Feedback:**

While I recommend acceptance, the paper could benefit from several improvements. The experimental evaluation, while adequate, feels somewhat limited. Expanding the benchmark to include more diverse datasets and tasks would strengthen the paper.  Specifically, exploring document similarity or retrieval tasks would be beneficial.

The presentation of results, particularly the reliance on t-SNE visualizations (Figures 3 & 4), feels disproportionate. While t-SNE can provide qualitative insights, it’s easily influenced by parameter settings and doesn't provide quantitative evidence of performance. The space dedicated to these figures could be better used for more rigorous empirical analysis, such as ablation studies examining the impact of different corruption rates.

The comparison to baseline methods is also a point of concern. Several reviewers have noted this, and I agree. The baselines, while standard, could be considered relatively weak in some cases. A more thorough comparison against stronger, more recent methods would further solidify the claims of the paper.  For example, transformer-based methods are now commonplace in document representation learning and should be included for comparison.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The corruption rate *q* is mentioned as not being sensitive to performance. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of this sensitivity, perhaps with a figure showing performance as a function of *q*?
2.  The paper mentions a vocabulary cutoff. How sensitive are the results to this cutoff? Does a smaller cutoff lead to more significant regularization, and at what point does it begin to degrade performance?
3.  You mention that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on the subsampling of frequent words used in Word2Vec+AVG. Does removing the subsampling trick from Word2Vec+AVG completely eliminate its benefits, or are there other advantages to this technique?
4.  Could you elaborate on the practical considerations for choosing the embedding dimensionality *h*? Are there guidelines or heuristics for selecting an appropriate value for a given dataset?
5.  While you mention the efficiency of Doc2VecC, a more detailed computational complexity analysis comparing it to Paragraph Vectors and other methods would be valuable.
