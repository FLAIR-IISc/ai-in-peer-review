This paper proposes Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple yet effective framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings, coupled with a corruption model that acts as a data-dependent regularizer, favoring informative words and suppressing common ones. The authors demonstrate strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, often outperforming state-of-the-art methods while maintaining impressive training and inference speed.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a conceptually sound and practically efficient approach, the technical novelty is limited, and the empirical validation, though strong on the presented tasks, does not fully convince me of its general superiority.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of Doc2VecC lies in its simplicity and efficiency. The method is easily implemented and scales well to large datasets. The data-dependent regularization is a clever addition, and the empirical results on sentiment analysis and document classification are promising. However, many of the underlying concepts—averaging word embeddings for document representation and data-dependent regularization—have precedents in the literature. The corruption model, while effective, builds upon established dropout techniques. 

My main concern stems from the limited scope of evaluation. The paper focuses on three tasks, and while the results are compelling within those contexts, it's unclear whether the observed performance gains generalize to other tasks or datasets. The authors claim to outperform state-of-the-art methods, but a more comprehensive evaluation is needed to substantiate this claim. Specifically, the comparisons to Skip-thought Vectors feel limited, as it's mentioned this model wasn't retrained specifically for this evaluation.

Furthermore, the comparisons to existing methods, while extensive, don't always include the strongest current baselines. A direct comparison against a more powerful bidirectional LSTM with concatenated hidden states, which is a common and competitive approach for document representation learning, is missing.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed method. However, a more thorough discussion of the related work, specifically highlighting the differences and advantages of Doc2VecC over existing approaches, would be beneficial. 

Regarding the experiments, providing more details about the hyperparameter tuning process would enhance reproducibility. For instance, what search strategy was used, and what range of values were explored for each hyperparameter? It would also be helpful to include standard deviations or confidence intervals for the reported results to assess the statistical significance of the performance gains.

The discussion of the data-dependent regularization is insightful, but it could be strengthened by providing a more intuitive explanation of *why* it works so effectively. Is it merely suppressing stop words, or is there a more nuanced effect on the learned representations?

Finally, a minor point: the numerous references throughout the paper, while appreciated, could be formatted more consistently.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions an RNN-LM baseline. Was this RNN-LM trained as a standard language model, or was it trained to directly minimize classification error on the downstream tasks? And importantly, what document representation was extracted from the RNN-LM – was it the final hidden state, or an average of hidden states across the entire sequence?
2.  Given the strong performance on the presented tasks, could the authors provide an ablation study, perhaps removing the corruption model to isolate its contribution? This would help to quantify the benefits of this particular component.
3.  The authors state Doc2VecC significantly outperforms Word2Vec. Is this performance differential consistent across different embedding dimensions? Providing results for a wider range of embedding sizes would be beneficial.
4.  To address the concern about generalizability, could the authors perform evaluations on additional document representation tasks, such as question answering or text summarization? This would strengthen the claim of Doc2VecC's general superiority.
5.  How does the performance of Doc2VecC change with variations in the corruption rate ‘q’? Is there an optimal range for ‘q’ that yields the best results?
