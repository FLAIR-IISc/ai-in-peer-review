- Decision: Reject — primary reason is insufficient convincing empirical evidence for significant improvement over strong baselines.
- Inconsistent and marginal improvements — reported gains are often very small (e.g., <1%) and sometimes hard to interpret or inconsistent.
- Lack of statistical significance testing — no p-values or tests to show observed differences aren’t due to chance.
- Dataset selection & justification concerns — dataset choices feel arbitrary and some highlighted wins (e.g., SICK) have limited impact due to dataset properties.
- Complexity vs. benefit — corruption mechanism adds complexity but paper doesn’t show it yields benefits that outweigh simpler alternatives like Word2Vec+IDF.
- Word analogy results mixed — word embedding claims supported, but explanation for paragraph vectors’ underperformance is weak and needs deeper analysis.
- Ablation studies missing — need experiments isolating the corruption model’s contribution and the effect of the corruption rate q.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity underreported — insufficient detail on ranges explored and selection criteria for hyperparameters.
- Error analysis lacking — qualitative examples of where Doc2VecC succeeds or fails would provide useful insight.
- Corruption-as-regularization explanation unclear — theoretical justification (Taylor expansion to regularization) is dense and needs a clearer, intuitive explanation.
- Need comparison to more recent baselines — include newer competitive document embedding methods.
- Replication details inadequate — provide more experimental setup details (library versions, hardware) despite available code.
- Questions for authors requested — supply statistical tests, sensitivity analysis for q, qualitative success/failure examples, computational cost comparisons, and clarification of the unbiased corruption role.
