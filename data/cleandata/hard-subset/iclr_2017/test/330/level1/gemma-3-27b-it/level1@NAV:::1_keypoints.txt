- Decision: Accept — paper presents a novel, effective document representation method with strong empirical evidence.
- Proposal: Doc2VecC represents documents as the average of word embeddings trained with a random-word "corruption" mechanism.
- Corruption role: Randomly removing words acts as a data-dependent regularizer, favoring informative words and suppressing common, less-discriminative ones.
- Empirical results: Matches or exceeds state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.
- Efficiency: Offers significant training and inference speed and scalability advantages over Paragraph Vectors.
- Motivation/literature: Well-motivated and clearly situated relative to BoW, Word2Vec, and Paragraph Vectors, addressing their scalability and test-time issues.
- Evaluation: Thorough comparisons to comprehensive baselines and multiple datasets; includes ablation study on the corruption model.
- Theory: Provides a clear mathematical derivation showing the corruption induces data-dependent regularization.
- Missing intuition: Lacks a more intuitive, conceptual explanation of why corruption improves representations beyond the formal derivation.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: The choice and sensitivity of the corruption rate q is only briefly discussed and needs more analysis.
- Reproducibility detail: Experimental hardware specs (CPU model, RAM, etc.) are not provided and should be included.
- Subsampling question: Paper notes good performance without Word2Vec-style subsampling but could better explain why subsampling isn’t needed and what that implies about representation quality.
- Questions for authors: request elaboration on corruption intuition, sensitivity analysis for q, hardware specifications, and explanation for not needing subsampling.
