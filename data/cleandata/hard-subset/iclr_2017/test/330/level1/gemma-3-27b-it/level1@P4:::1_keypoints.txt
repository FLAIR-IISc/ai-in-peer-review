- Paper proposes Doc2VecC: document vectors via averaged word embeddings with a corruption-based data-dependent regularizer; claims state-of-the-art accuracy and computational efficiency.
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient justification for claimed gains and weaknesses in experiments and clarity.
- Core innovation (corruption model) is not convincingly demonstrated to be the primary cause of improved results.
- Missing ablation study: no results reported for Doc2VecC without the corruption step.
- Claim that corruption induces stronger regularization on common-word embeddings is asserted but not quantified or causally shown.
- Table 3’s small-norm words are shown but it’s unclear whether this effect stems from corruption or from averaging itself.
- Experimental setup/reporting is inconsistent; hyperparameter tuning and implementation details are often unclear.
- Skip-thought vectors were not retrained on the same datasets, introducing potential comparison bias.
- Claim of being “much faster” is insufficiently supported—timing comparisons are limited to a small set of models in Table 2.
- The assertion that the model “reduces to Denoising Autoencoders” without context words lacks elaboration and experimental validation.
- Performance claims are vague (“similar or even better”) and reported improvements are often small and not shown to be statistically significant.
- The claim that corruption obviates subsampling of frequent words is weak and not convincingly demonstrated.
- Request to simplify and better explain the mathematical derivation of corruption-as-regularization and why that corruption form is effective.
- Need ablation studies across corruption rates (q) and a report of Doc2VecC’s sensitivity to hyperparameters (embedding size, learning rate, q, etc.).
- Ask for statistical significance testing for all reported performance comparisons.
- Recommend comparison to simpler baselines such as averaging pre-trained embeddings (e.g., GloVe) without training.
- Suggest providing qualitative examples showing where Doc2VecC captures semantics better than alternatives, and discussing limitations (vocabulary sensitivity, averaging vs richer composition).
- Questions for authors requested: performance without corruption; sensitivity to corruption rate q; detailed hyperparameter tuning for baselines; rationale for not retraining Skip-thoughts; more comprehensive timing/compute costs; and specifics of how corruption-based regularization differs from subsampling.
