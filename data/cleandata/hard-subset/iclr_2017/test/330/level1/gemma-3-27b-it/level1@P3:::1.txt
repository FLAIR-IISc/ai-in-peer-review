This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that leverages the simplicity of averaging word embeddings while incorporating a “corruption” mechanism for regularization. The core idea – that averaging word embeddings *can* be a powerful document representation if learned correctly – is insightful and well-motivated. The authors argue that their corruption model effectively regularizes the learning process, favoring informative words and suppressing less useful ones. The experimental results demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves competitive or superior performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks. The efficiency claims are also compelling, particularly concerning test time inference.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a promising approach and demonstrates solid experimental results, the presentation lacks sufficient detail regarding the implementation and analysis of the "corruption" mechanism. Specifically, the claimed connection between the corruption process and the resulting data-dependent regularization feels somewhat hand-wavy and lacks a truly convincing justification. The empirical benefits are shown, but the *why* behind them remains somewhat unclear, reducing confidence in the novelty and robustness of the method.

The paper’s primary strength lies in its empirical performance. The results consistently show Doc2VecC matching or exceeding the performance of more complex models on diverse tasks. The speed advantage, especially at test time, is also significant. The ablation study focusing on the corruption mechanism (Table 3) provides *some* evidence of its effectiveness, showing that it dampens the embeddings of common words. However, this is a limited analysis.  A more thorough ablation study, exploring different corruption rates (q) and their impact on performance, would strengthen the claims. Moreover, the derivation of the data-dependent regularization (Section 3.1) is mathematically involved but relies on a Taylor expansion. While common, the implications and potential limitations of this approximation aren’t thoroughly discussed. The impact of this approximation on the effectiveness of the regularization isn't explored. 

The related work section is reasonably comprehensive, covering relevant approaches to document representation learning. However, the comparison to Paragraph Vectors feels somewhat superficial. The authors highlight the parameter growth issue with Paragraph Vectors, but don’t fully explore the architectural differences and the potential impact of learning a dedicated document vector versus relying solely on averaging word embeddings. The analysis of Skip-thought Vectors' performance is also somewhat brief, simply attributing the poorer results to paragraph length. A deeper investigation of why Doc2VecC performs better on longer documents would be valuable.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

*   **Clarify the corruption mechanism:** Provide more intuition behind the choice of the specific corruption method (randomly removing words with probability *q* and rescaling). Why is this approach effective? Are there alternative corruption strategies that were considered and why were they rejected?
*   **Detailed Ablation Study:** Conduct a more extensive ablation study focusing on the corruption rate (*q*). Plot performance metrics (e.g., accuracy, F1-score) as a function of *q*. This will provide a clearer understanding of the optimal corruption level and the sensitivity of the model to this hyperparameter.
*   **Analysis of Regularization:** Provide a more rigorous analysis of the data-dependent regularization term. How does the magnitude of the regularization vary with word frequency and importance? Can this regularization be interpreted in terms of established regularization techniques (e.g., L1/L2 regularization)?
*   **Comparison to Paragraph Vectors:** A more in-depth comparison to Paragraph Vectors, discussing the trade-offs between learning a dedicated document vector and averaging word embeddings.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of Doc2VecC to other hyperparameters, such as embedding dimension and learning rate.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct an error analysis to identify common failure cases of Doc2VecC. This could provide insights into the limitations of the model and potential areas for improvement.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  The derivation of the data-dependent regularization relies on a Taylor expansion. What is the impact of the order of the expansion on the accuracy of the regularization term? Have you investigated higher-order terms?
2.  You mention that the corruption rate *q* does not significantly affect performance. Can you provide specific results from your experiments showing the performance variation with different values of *q*?
3.  What is the computational cost of the corruption step during training? Does it significantly impact the overall training time?
4.  Can you provide more details on the hardware and software configuration used for your experiments? This will help with reproducibility.
5.  You state Doc2VecC is more robust to common words. Do you see any cases where this robustness hinders performance (e.g., in tasks requiring fine-grained sentiment analysis)?
