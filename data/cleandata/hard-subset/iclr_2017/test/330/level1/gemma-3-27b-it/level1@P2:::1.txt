The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings with a corruption mechanism to favor informative words and suppress common ones. The authors claim their method is efficient, scalable, and achieves state-of-the-art performance on several text understanding tasks.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a conceptually simple and potentially promising approach, the experimental validation lacks sufficient detail and comparative strength to justify acceptance at a venue like ICLR. The improvements reported, while sometimes statistically significant, are often marginal and lack convincing justification given the amount of hyperparameter tuning typically required for state-of-the-art results. The claims of significantly outperforming other methods need stronger evidence.

The approach is well-motivated by observations regarding the compositional nature of language and the limitations of existing document representation techniques like bag-of-words and Paragraph Vectors. The connection to regularization through the corruption mechanism is a sensible idea, and the claimed efficiency benefits are attractive. The paper is well-placed within the literature, referencing relevant work on word embeddings, document representations, and regularization techniques.

However, the support for the claims is weak in several areas. First, the experimental setup lacks crucial details. For example, the specific hyperparameter tuning process for each baseline algorithm is not described. This makes it difficult to assess whether the comparisons are fair. Secondly, the improvement in performance over strong baselines is often small (e.g., a few percentage points), and it's unclear if these differences are statistically significant, especially given the multiple comparisons being made. The ablation study regarding the corruption model is also weak. Finally, the discussion of the word analogy task is somewhat unconvincing, merely stating the improvement without much analysis of *why* Doc2VecC performs better. While the authors make a claim regarding Paragraph Vectors relying more on document vectors than word similarities, this requires deeper investigation. 

Furthermore, the paper feels slightly repetitive in its explanation of the corruption mechanism and its benefits. The mathematical derivations, while present, are not fully leveraged to provide clear insight into the behavior of the model.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for all reported results to demonstrate that the improvements are not due to random chance.
*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Include a comprehensive description of the hyperparameters used for all algorithms, including the search space and selection criteria. This is essential for reproducibility and fair comparison.
*   **Ablation Study:**  Conduct a more rigorous ablation study to understand the contribution of each component of Doc2VecC, including the corruption rate. Vary the corruption rate (q) and report results.
*   **Error Analysis:**  Perform an error analysis on the classification tasks to identify the types of documents where Doc2VecC excels or struggles.
*   **Computational Cost:**  While the paper mentions efficiency, provide more detailed computational cost measurements (e.g., training time, memory usage) for different dataset sizes.
*   **Word Analogy Analysis**: Offer a more nuanced discussion of the word analogy results. Provide examples of analogies where Doc2VecC succeeds and fails and analyze the underlying reasons.
*   **Explain Choice of t-SNE**: Justify the choice of t-SNE for visualizing document vectors. Are there alternatives that might provide clearer insights?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide the statistical significance of the improvements reported in the tables?
*   What specific strategies were used to tune the hyperparameters of the baseline algorithms?
*   How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of corruption rate (q)?
*   Could you provide examples of specific word analogies where Doc2VecC performs better than Word2Vec, and explain why?
*   What is the computational complexity of training and generating document representations with Doc2VecC compared to the baselines, especially as the vocabulary size increases?
