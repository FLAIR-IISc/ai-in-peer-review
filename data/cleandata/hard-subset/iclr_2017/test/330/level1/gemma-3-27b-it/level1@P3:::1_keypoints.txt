- Decision: Reject — promising approach with solid empirical results but insufficient analysis of the corruption mechanism and its justification.
- Proposal: Doc2VecC uses averaged word embeddings with a random-word “corruption” regularizer to learn document representations.
- Insight: Averaging word embeddings can be powerful if learned and regularized correctly; this is well-motivated.
- Main claim: Corruption favors informative words and suppresses common/less-useful words, yielding data-dependent regularization.
- Empirical strength: Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.
- Efficiency: Significant speed advantage, particularly at test-time inference.
- Presentation weakness: Implementation and analysis of the corruption mechanism lack sufficient detail and convincing justification.
- Theoretical weakness: Connection between corruption and data-dependent regularization is hand-wavy; the Taylor-expansion approximation and its limitations are not fully explored.
- Ablation critique: Table 3 offers limited evidence; more thorough exploration of corruption rates (q) and their impact is needed.
- Sensitivity missing: No detailed analysis of sensitivity to other hyperparameters (embedding dim, learning rate).
- Comparison gaps: Comparison to Paragraph Vectors is superficial; trade-offs between learned document vectors and averaged embeddings are underexplored.
- Skip-thought analysis: Attribution of its poorer performance merely to paragraph length is insufficient; deeper investigation is needed.
- Recommended experiments: More extensive ablation on q (performance vs q plots), analysis of regularization magnitude versus word frequency/importance, and error analysis to find failure modes.
- Reproducibility: Provide details on hardware/software used for experiments.
- Question 1: How does the Taylor expansion order affect the accuracy of the derived regularization term; were higher-order terms investigated?
- Question 2: Provide experimental results showing performance variation across different corruption rates q.
- Question 3: What is the computational cost of the corruption step during training and its impact on overall training time?
- Question 4: What hardware and software configurations were used for the experiments?
- Question 5: Are there cases where robustness to common words hurts performance (e.g., fine-grained sentiment tasks)?
