This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings, incorporating a “corruption” mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer. The core idea is to learn word embeddings such that averaging them yields meaningful document representations. The paper claims Doc2VecC outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while also being computationally efficient.

**Reject.** The paper, while presenting a conceptually simple and potentially useful idea, lacks sufficient justification for its performance gains and contains several weaknesses in experimental evaluation and clarity. The core innovation – the corruption model – is not convincingly demonstrated to be the primary driver of the improved results, and the experimental setup, while comprehensive, doesn’t definitively isolate the impact of Doc2VecC’s components.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clear ablation studies. The paper mentions the corruption mechanism as a key contribution and suggests it acts as a data-dependent regularizer, but doesn't present results comparing Doc2VecC *without* the corruption step to the full model. Without this comparison, it’s impossible to determine the impact of this feature. Additionally, the paper states that Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization toward embeddings of common words," yet doesn't quantify or demonstrate this regularization effect. While Table 3 shows words with small norms, it’s not clear if this is a direct consequence of the corruption or inherent to the averaging approach.

Another reason for rejection stems from inconsistencies in the experimental setup and reporting. The paper compares to numerous baselines, which is commendable, but the hyperparameter tuning and implementation details aren't always clearly explained. For instance, the Skip-thought vectors model isn’t retrained, which introduces a potential bias. Moreover, there is a repeated claim that the method is "much faster" without offering a comprehensive timing comparison beyond Table 2, which only reports training and inference times for a limited set of models. The claim about "reducing to Denoising Autoencoders" when removing context words needs further elaboration and experimental validation. The phrase "similar or even better performance" is vague, and the specific improvements are often small and not statistically significant. The argument that Doc2VecC does not "rely on" subsampling frequent words because of the regularization is weak; subsampling is a common practice for similar methods, and the authors do not convincingly demonstrate that the corruption model entirely eliminates the need for it.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the Corruption Mechanism:** The mathematical derivation of the corruption as regularization is complex and could be simplified. The explanation of *why* this specific form of corruption is effective remains somewhat unclear.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Crucially, include results without the corruption component and with different corruption rates (q) to demonstrate its effectiveness.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons to confirm the observed differences are not due to chance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of Doc2VecC’s performance to different hyperparameters (embedding dimension, learning rate, corruption rate, etc.).
*   **Comparison to Simpler Baselines:** Compare against a simple average of pre-trained (e.g., GloVe) word embeddings *without* any training to establish a stronger baseline.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide examples of documents where Doc2VecC's representation captures semantic meaning particularly well, and contrast with representations generated by other methods.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:**  Acknowledge limitations, such as potential sensitivity to vocabulary size or the choice of averaging versus more sophisticated composition methods.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  What is the performance of Doc2VecC *without* the corruption mechanism?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of the corruption rate (q)?
3.  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for each baseline?
4.  Why was the Skip-thought vectors model not retrained on the datasets used in the experiments?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed timing comparison, including the computational cost of embedding generation for a large number of documents?
6.  What specific aspects of the regularization introduced by the corruption mechanism lead to the observed performance improvements, compared to methods like subsampling frequent words?
