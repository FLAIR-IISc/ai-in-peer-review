## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper proposes Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient document representation learning framework. The core idea is to represent a document as the average of word embeddings, coupled with a "corruption" mechanism during training. This corruption randomly removes words, acting as a data-dependent regularizer, favoring informative words and suppressing common, less-discriminative ones. The authors demonstrate strong performance on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, often matching or exceeding state-of-the-art results, while boasting significant training and inference efficiency.

**Decision: Accept**

The key reasons for acceptance are the novelty of the approach and the strong empirical results. Doc2VecC presents a simple yet effective way to learn document representations, directly addressing limitations of existing methods like Paragraph Vectors in terms of scalability and test-time efficiency. The consistent outperformance of existing methods across various tasks is a strong indicator of the method's efficacy.

The paper is well-motivated, clearly placing itself within the existing literature on document representation learning, including BoW, Word2Vec, and Paragraph Vectors. The authors effectively highlight the drawbacks of previous approaches and position Doc2VecC as a viable and efficient alternative. The empirical evaluation is thorough, comparing against a comprehensive set of baselines and demonstrating improvements across multiple datasets and tasks. The ablation study regarding the corruption model provides additional insights into its importance. The mathematical derivation of the data-dependent regularization is also clearly explained.

However, a few points could improve the paper. While the benefits of the corruption mechanism are shown empirically, a more intuitive explanation of *why* it works so well could be helpful. Further, the choice of the corruption rate *q* is briefly mentioned, but a more detailed discussion of how this parameter was chosen or its sensitivity would be beneficial. Finally, the authors mention that the experiments were conducted on a desktop machine, but more details about the hardware specifications could aid reproducibility. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind why the corruption mechanism leads to improved document representations, beyond the formal derivation of the regularization term? Does it encourage the model to learn more robust or generalizable word embeddings?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the corruption rate *q*? Were different values explored, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Could you provide more details about the hardware specifications used for the experiments (CPU model, RAM, etc.) to improve reproducibility?
4.  The paper shows that Doc2VecC performs well even *without* the subsampling trick used by Word2Vec. Can you provide more insight on *why* it doesn’t need it, and what that says about the quality of representations it produces?
