This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework that averages word embeddings, coupled with a corruption model to regularize the embeddings. The core idea – that a meaningful document representation can be formed by averaging word embeddings during learning – is well-motivated by observations about the compositionality of word embeddings (e.g., "king" - "man" + "woman" ≈ "queen"). The paper claims Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec and matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods on several downstream tasks (sentiment analysis, document classification, semantic relatedness), while being computationally efficient.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of convincing empirical evidence supporting the claim of significant performance improvements over strong baselines, especially considering the amount of experimentation performed. While the paper *reports* outperforming baselines, the differences are often marginal and, in some cases, the presented results are difficult to interpret or seem inconsistent. The improvements reported do not consistently justify the added complexity of the proposed method compared to simpler approaches like Word2Vec + IDF.

The supporting arguments for this decision are as follows:

1. **Inconsistent and Marginal Improvements:** The reported performance gains are frequently small, often within a few percentage points. For instance, in the document classification task (Table 5), the improvements are often less than 1%. It’s unclear if these differences are statistically significant and if they truly represent a substantial advance.  The gains on semantic relatedness are more pronounced, but the comparison is against older baselines and skip-thought vectors which are noted to have limitations on longer text.
2. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The paper does not provide any statistical significance testing (e.g., p-values) to support the claims of outperformance. Without this information, it’s impossible to determine whether the observed differences are due to the method itself or random chance.
3. **Dataset Selection & Justification:** The choice of datasets and experimental setup, while standard, feels somewhat arbitrary. The strong performance on SICK, for example, is highlighted, but the limitations of the dataset (length of sentences) are acknowledged, diminishing the impact of the result.
4. **Complexity vs. Benefit:** Doc2VecC introduces a corruption mechanism, adding complexity to the training process. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that this complexity translates into substantial benefits that outweigh the increased computational overhead or implementation difficulty compared to simpler models.
5. **Word Analogy Results:** The claims regarding improved word embeddings are supported by the word analogy task, however the explanation for the paragraph vectors' underperformance seems weak ("Paragraph vectors relies mostly on the unique document vectors..."). A more detailed analysis is needed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be highly valuable to include ablation studies to understand the contribution of the corruption model. How much of the performance gain is attributable to the corruption mechanism, and how does the corruption rate (q) affect performance?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper briefly mentions hyperparameter tuning, but doesn't provide details about the range of values explored or the criteria used for selection. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the results.
*   **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis would be beneficial. Examining specific examples where Doc2VecC performs well and poorly could provide insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model.
*   **Clarify "Corruption as Data-Dependent Regularization":** While the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism is presented, it is quite dense and difficult to follow. A more intuitive explanation would be helpful. The connection between the Taylor expansion and the resulting regularization could be clarified.
*   **Comparison to More Recent Baselines:** Consider including more recent and competitive document embedding methods in the comparison, beyond those mentioned.
*   **Replication Details:** While code is available, providing a more detailed description of the experimental setup (e.g., specific library versions, hardware specifications) would improve reproducibility.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) to demonstrate the significance of the performance improvements reported in the tables?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of Doc2VecC to the choice of the corruption rate (q)? Can you provide a graph or table showing the performance as a function of q?
3.  Could you provide a few examples where Doc2VecC outperforms the baselines and a few examples where it fails, with a qualitative explanation of why?
4.  How does the computational cost of training and testing Doc2VecC compare to the baselines, specifically in terms of training time and memory usage?
5.  In the explanation of the corruption mechanism as regularization, could you clarify the role of the unbiased corruption in leading to the observed benefits? Specifically, how does enforcing  µx = x affect the regularization term?
