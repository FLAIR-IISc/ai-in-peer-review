- Proposes Doc2VecC: average word embeddings with corruption to emphasize informative words and suppress commons.
- Claims efficiency, scalability, and state-of-the-art performance on multiple text tasks.
- Recommendation: Reject due to insufficient experimental validation and weak comparative strength.
- Reported improvements are often marginal and may require heavy hyperparameter tuning to reproduce.
- Experimental setup lacks crucial details, especially hyperparameter tuning for baselines, making comparisons unclear.
- No statistical significance testing reported to support claimed performance gains.
- Ablation study on the corruption mechanism is weak and does not thoroughly assess component contributions.
- Word analogy results are stated without in-depth analysis of why Doc2VecC outperforms alternatives.
- Claim that Paragraph Vectors rely more on document vectors than word similarities needs deeper investigation.
- Exposition is repetitive regarding the corruption mechanism and its benefits.
- Mathematical derivations are present but not used to clearly explain model behavior.
- Request: provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for all results.
- Request: include comprehensive hyperparameter details, search spaces, and selection criteria for all methods.
- Request: perform a more rigorous ablation study, varying corruption rate (q) and reporting effects.
- Request: conduct error analysis on classification tasks to identify strengths and weaknesses by document type.
- Request: report detailed computational costs (training time, memory) across dataset sizes.
- Request: give nuanced word analogy analysis with examples of successes and failures and underlying reasons.
- Request: justify the choice of t-SNE for visualization or consider alternative visualization methods.
- Questions for authors: provide statistical significance of table improvements and describe baseline hyperparameter tuning strategies.
- Questions: how sensitive is performance to corruption rate q, can you give example analogies where Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec, and what is the computational complexity relative to baselines as vocabulary grows?
