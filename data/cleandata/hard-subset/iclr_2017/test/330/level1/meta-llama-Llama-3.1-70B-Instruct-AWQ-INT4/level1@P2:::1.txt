**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a novel document representation learning framework, Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC). This framework represents each document as a simple average of word embeddings, ensuring that the representation captures the semantic meanings of the document during learning. A corruption model is introduced, which randomly removes words from a document during learning, favoring informative or rare words while forcing the embeddings of common and non-discriminative ones to be close to zero. The authors claim that Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than Word2Vec and outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation learning algorithms in sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.

**Decision**

I decide to **accept** this paper, with the main reasons being:

1. The paper proposes a novel and efficient document representation learning framework that addresses the limitations of existing methods.
2. The authors provide extensive experimental evaluations, demonstrating the effectiveness of Doc2VecC in various tasks and comparing it to several state-of-the-art baselines.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of the limitations of existing document representation learning methods, such as Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors. The proposed corruption model is a key innovation that enables Doc2VecC to learn more informative word embeddings and document representations.

The experimental evaluations are thorough and well-designed, covering a range of tasks and datasets. The authors provide detailed comparisons with several state-of-the-art baselines, demonstrating the superiority of Doc2VecC in many cases. The results are also supported by visualizations, such as the t-SNE plots, which help to illustrate the quality of the learned document representations.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. Provide more theoretical analysis of the corruption model and its effects on the learned word embeddings and document representations.
2. Consider adding more ablation studies to evaluate the impact of different components of the Doc2VecC framework, such as the corruption rate and the word embedding dimensionality.
3. Provide more details on the computational resources required to train and test the Doc2VecC model, as well as the time complexity of the algorithm.
4. Consider adding more comparisons with other state-of-the-art document representation learning methods, such as those based on deep neural networks.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more insights into the choice of the corruption rate (q) and its impact on the learned word embeddings and document representations?
2. How does the Doc2VecC framework handle out-of-vocabulary words, and are there any plans to extend the model to handle such cases?
3. Can you provide more details on the training procedure, including the optimization algorithm, batch size, and number of iterations?
4. Are there any plans to apply the Doc2VecC framework to other natural language processing tasks, such as machine translation or question answering?
