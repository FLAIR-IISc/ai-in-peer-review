- Paper proposes Doc2VecC: document represented by average word embeddings with a corruption model that drops words to favor informative/rare words and push common words' embeddings toward zero.  
- Authors claim Doc2VecC yields better word embeddings than Word2Vec and outperforms several state-of-the-art document representation methods on sentiment analysis, classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Decision: accept, primarily because the method is novel, efficient, and addresses limitations of existing approaches.  
- Strong experimental evaluation showing effectiveness across multiple tasks and comparisons to baselines.  
- Paper is well-motivated with clear explanation of limitations of Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors.  
- The corruption model is a key innovation enabling more informative word and document representations.  
- Results are supported by visualizations (t-SNE) illustrating representation quality.  
- Suggestion: provide more theoretical analysis of the corruption model and its effects.  
- Suggestion: add ablation studies to measure impact of components like corruption rate and embedding dimensionality.  
- Suggestion: provide details on computational resources, training time, and time complexity.  
- Suggestion: include additional comparisons with other state-of-the-art methods, especially deep neural approaches.  
- Question: please clarify choice of corruption rate q and its impact on learned embeddings and representations.  
- Question: how does Doc2VecC handle out-of-vocabulary words and are there plans to extend for OOV handling?  
- Question: provide more training details (optimization algorithm, batch size, number of iterations).  
- Question: are there plans to apply Doc2VecC to other NLP tasks like machine translation or question answering?
