Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for document representation learning. Its central idea is to learn word embeddings such that the average of a document’s word vectors serves as an effective document representation. During training, Doc2VecC augments a CBoW-style predictor with a global document context obtained by masking (corrupting) words from the document’s bag-of-words, and shows that this unbiased corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words toward zero while preserving informative words. The method decouples model size from the number of documents, enables very fast training and inference, and outperforms strong baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, DAE, Paragraph Vectors) on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, and produces competitive sentence embeddings on SICK. It also yields substantially better word analogies than Word2Vec. The method is simple, scalable, and easy to deploy.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical performance with a notably simple and scalable architecture; superior or competitive results across diverse tasks, and substantial speed advantages at both train and test time.
- Clear, well-motivated approach with a useful theoretical analysis showing the corruption-induced, data-dependent regularization that explains why simple averaging works and why frequent stopwords are suppressed.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: Learning document representations that are both high-quality and efficient. The paper aims to reconcile the simplicity and scalability of Word2Vec with the task effectiveness of document encoders like Paragraph Vectors, while enabling cheap inference for unseen documents.

2) Motivation and placement in literature: The motivation is grounded in empirical semantics of linear operations on word embeddings and the desire to avoid per-document parameters and expensive test-time inference. The method is positioned relative to Word2Vec (local context only), Paragraph Vectors (adds per-document parameters), Denoising Autoencoders, and more recent sentence encoders (Skip-Thought). The proposed corruption mechanism is related to dropout/mDA and is leveraged here to shape word embeddings so that their averages encode global semantics.

3) Do results support claims?
- Accuracy: On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches or outperforms Paragraph Vector and clearly beats BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF; on Wikipedia multi-class classification it consistently yields lower error than all baselines across dimensions; on SICK it is competitive, albeit slightly behind LSTM/Skip-Thought (reasonable given sentence length). Word analogy results show consistently higher accuracy than Word2Vec across subtasks, which supports the claim that the corruption regularization yields better word embeddings. The qualitative analysis (norms of frequent vs rare words) aligns with the theory.
- Efficiency: Training/inference timing is carefully reported and strongly supports the efficiency claims. Notably, inference for new documents is several orders faster than Paragraph Vectors.
- Rigor: The second-order Taylor expansion around the corruption mean is a standard tool to reveal the regularization effect; the resulting term is interpretable and matches the observed behavior.

4) Novelty and significance: While averaging word embeddings is common, enforcing that averaging works via a training objective that includes a corrupted global context is a neat and impactful idea. The data-dependent regularization explanation is insightful and practically useful. The method offers a compelling accuracy/efficiency trade-off valuable to large-scale applications.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all items affect my decision)
- Clarify negative sampling details. You state Pv is uniform; Word2Vec typically uses unigram^0.75. Please specify the distribution and number of negatives used for Doc2VecC and baselines, and report sensitivity to this choice to ensure fairness.
- Ablations: Include explicit ablations on (i) no corruption, (ii) corruption rate q (e.g., 0.5–0.95), (iii) no local context (global-only), (iv) global context without scaling by 1/T, and (v) using the uncorrupted average during training. This will isolate the contribution of each component.
- Baseline tuning: Provide full hyperparameter settings for Word2Vec (window, subsampling t, negatives, learning rate/epochs) and Paragraph Vector (PV-DM vs PV-DBOW, concatenation, window) and ensure they are well-tuned. PV often benefits from DBOW and increased epochs; please confirm you used strong variants.
- Fairness of “using test data” protocol: While common in unsupervised representation learning, please also emphasize the protocol where only train+unlabeled are used and present both sets of numbers side-by-side.
- Regularization analysis: Strengthen the empirical support by plotting embedding norm vs. word frequency for different methods, and showing that Doc2VecC downweights common stopwords while preserving sentiment/label-indicative terms.
- Wikipedia dataset: Provide more details and release the split (category list, selection criteria, exact counts) for reproducibility beyond the code. Consider evaluating on standard benchmarks (e.g., 20 Newsgroups, R8/R52, AG News) to ease comparison.
- SICK setup: You fine-tune embeddings on SICK without labels. Please clarify whether any vocabulary expansion or OOV handling could advantage Doc2VecC relative to Skip-Thought’s vocabulary expansion trick, and report with/without fine-tuning.
- Theoretical clarity: In the expansion, make explicit the assumptions leading to the diagonal covariance and the sign of the regularization term. A brief discussion on when σ(1−σ) tends to zero and the implications for rare but predictive words would improve readability.
- Implementation details: Specify tokenization, lowercasing, min-count, window size, learning rate schedule, epochs, and whether you use subsampling (t) for all methods. Report variance over multiple runs.

Questions for the authors
- What negative sampling distribution and number of negatives did you use for Doc2VecC and for Word2Vec/PV? How sensitive are your results to using unigram^0.75 vs uniform?
- How does performance change without the corruption (q=0)? With different q values? What is the empirical sweet spot and why?
- Does removing the local context (i.e., training only with the corrupted global context) significantly hurt performance? Conversely, how far can you go with only local context plus average-at-test?
- For IMDB, what are the exact hyperparameters for each method, and did you train all methods for the same number of epochs with early stopping on validation?
- On the word analogy task, what accounts for the large gap vs Word2Vec—better negative sampling, regularization, or training epochs? Can you equalize those to isolate the corruption effect?
- Can you provide a correlation plot of embedding norm vs frequency and examples where Doc2VecC preserves rare yet predictive words relative to Word2Vec/PV?
- For PV, did you evaluate PV-DBOW and PV-DM+DBOW combined, and did you use inference tricks (e.g., more inference steps) at test time?

Overall, this is a strong and practical contribution: simple, fast, and effective, with a clear theoretical intuition and solid empirical validation. I recommend acceptance.
