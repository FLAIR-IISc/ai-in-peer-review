- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings trained with word-level corruption/dropout to enforce compositionality and shrink common word embeddings.
- Corruption acts as an unbiased dropout inducing a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses non-informative words while preserving rare/informative ones.
- Provides a second-order Taylor expansion analysis to explain the regularization effect on embeddings.
- Decouples model size from corpus size, trains nearly as fast as Word2Vec, and enables extremely fast test-time inference via simple averaging.
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance on IMDB (sentiment), Wikipedia (classification), SICK (semantic relatedness), and word analogy benchmarks.
- Decision: Accept.
- Primary reason 1: Novel, elegant corruption-as-regularization mechanism with clear theoretical motivation targeting compositional averaging.
- Primary reason 2: Strong, broad empirical evidence, efficiency gains, and improved embeddings over Word2Vec, matching or outperforming state of the art on multiple tasks.
- Motivational placement is solid, bridging Word2Vec compositionality and Paragraph Vector global context while relating to denoising autoencoders and dropout-as-regularization literature.
- Taylor analysis aligns with observed behavior (reduced norms for stopwords, improved analogy scores).
- Experiments are diverse and consistent; code is provided and speed comparisons show practical advantages.
- Design choices (averaging, corruption, negative sampling) produce a practical method with favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-offs.
- Request ablations: no corruption, corruption without local context, varying corruption rate q, and different negative sampling distributions.
- Ask to clarify fairness: avoid training on test data, report strict no-leakage results and statistical significance across runs.
- Request sensitivity analyses for embedding size and context window, and equal-dimension/runtime comparisons to Skip-Thought and Paragraph Vector.
- Ask for details on frequent-word subsampling and its interaction with the regularizer, including results without subsampling.
- Request clarification on memory footprint and wall-clock training/inference times on common hardware, plus seed variance reporting.
- Question on OOV handling and potential integration of subword modeling.
- For SICK, ask whether fine-tuning on target vocabulary without labels changes results and to show with/without comparisons.
