- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn document vectors as the average of corrupted word embeddings using a CBOW-style objective and negative sampling.  
- Uses unbiased dropout-like corruption on bag-of-words to speed training and induce a data-dependent regularizer that downweights frequent, non-informative words.  
- Provides a Taylor-expansion analysis linking corruption to adaptive regularization that explains small norms for stopwords.  
- Shows strong empirical performance on IMDB, Wikipedia-100, SICK, and word analogy tasks with large efficiency gains and simple test-time encoding.  
- Decision: Accept — due to strong, consistent empirical results and clear motivation/analysis.  
- Tackles a timely problem: scalable, unsupervised document representation learning that preserves semantics.  
- Well-motivated placement: builds on Word2Vec/Paragraph Vector and connects to dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders.  
- Empirical claims supported: matches/outperforms baselines on long-document tasks, competitive on sentence tasks, and improves word embeddings.  
- Theoretical claims supported by a second-order expansion analysis, despite approximations (diagonal Hessian, independence assumptions).  
- Methodology is standard and sound (CBOW-like with negative sampling); experiments include useful ablations and public code.  
- Strength — Simplicity/practicality: single-pass averaging at test time; model size depends only on vocabulary.  
- Strength — Scalability: training speed comparable to Word2Vec and much faster than Paragraph Vector and RNN encoders.  
- Strength — Insightful analysis: connects corruption to adaptive regularization and empirically validates embedding norm shrinkage.  
- Strength — Broad utility: effective for long documents and competitive for sentence-level tasks.  
- Strength — Reproducibility: code provided and sensible dataset choices.  
- Weakness — Missing comparisons to newer/competitive baselines (e.g., fastText, SIF/Sent2Vec).  
- Weakness — Limited ablations on corruption rate q, length-normalization, and local vs global context contributions.  
- Weakness — Analytical derivation relies on approximations and a uniform negative-sampling assumption that may not match implementation.  
- Weakness — Potential confusion on IMDB train/test splits; stricter split results should be emphasized.  
- Suggested improvements: provide full training details (negatives, distribution, window, subsampling, learning rates, epochs).  
- Suggested improvements: add quantitative ablations for corruption/no-corruption, varying q, removing local context, and length-normalization choices.  
- Suggested improvements: include supervised/unsupervised baselines like fastText and SIF/Sent2Vec, report variance across runs and more datasets for robustness.  
- Suggested improvements: clarify whether implementation matches analytic assumptions (negative-sampling distribution) and fix minor editing/formatting issues.  
- Key author questions: what negative-sampling distribution/num negatives used, sensitivity to q and local context removal, window/subsampling settings, strict IMDB-split results, skip-gram variant performance, short-sentence comparisons to LSTM/Skip-thought, subword experiments, and robustness of per-word norm shrinkage.
