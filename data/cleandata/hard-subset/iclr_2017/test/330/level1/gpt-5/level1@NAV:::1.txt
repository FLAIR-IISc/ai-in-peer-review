Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for document representation that learns to represent a document as the average of its word embeddings while training with a corruption process. The key idea is to enforce compositionality during learning and to use unbiased dropout on document words to induce a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of common, non-informative words toward zero while preserving informative/rare words. The authors provide a second-order Taylor analysis explaining the regularization effect, and demonstrate strong empirical performance on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia), semantic relatedness (SICK), and word analogy benchmarks. The approach decouples model size from corpus size, trains very fast (near Word2Vec), and provides extremely fast test-time inference by simple averaging.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) A novel yet elegant corruption-as-regularization mechanism with clear theoretical motivation that directly targets compositional document averaging; (2) Strong, broad empirical evidence with meaningful efficiency gains and improved word embeddings over Word2Vec, while outperforming or matching state of the art on multiple tasks.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement are solid: the work bridges Word2Vec compositionality and Paragraph Vector’s global context, while addressing PV’s scaling/inference drawbacks. It is well connected to denoising autoencoders and dropout-as-adaptive-regularization literature.
- The Taylor expansion makes the regularization mechanism concrete and aligns with observed behavior (embedding norms of stopwords reduced; improved analogy scores).
- Experiments are diverse and consistent: improved results on IMDB and Wikipedia, competitive SICK performance, substantial uplift on analogy tasks; speed comparisons show clear practical advantages; code is provided.
- The design choices (averaging, corruption, negative sampling) lead to a highly practical method with favorable accuracy–efficiency trade-offs.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve, not all tied to the decision)
- Please add ablations isolating: (i) no corruption, (ii) corruption but no local context, (iii) varying corruption rate q, and (iv) different negative sampling distributions (uniform vs unigram^0.75).
- Clarify fairness choices: some settings learn representations on all data including test; report results strictly without test leakage, and provide statistical significance across multiple runs.
- Report sensitivity to embedding size and context window; include equal-dimensional and equal-runtime comparisons to Skip-Thought and PV.
- Provide details on subsampling of frequent words and its interaction with your regularizer; do results hold without subsampling?
- Clarify memory footprint and training/inference wall-clock on common hardware; report seed variance.
- How does Doc2VecC handle OOV words, and could subword modeling be integrated?
- For SICK, does fine-tuning on target vocabulary (without labels) materially change results? Can you show with/without?
