Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple and efficient framework for learning document representations by averaging word embeddings learned under a corruption model. The key ideas are: (i) use a CBOW-style predictive objective where the global document vector is the average of (corrupted) word embeddings, thereby training embeddings so that simple averaging yields meaningful document representations; (ii) apply unbiased dropout-like corruption to the document bag-of-words during training, which both accelerates learning and induces a data-dependent regularizer that suppresses frequent, non-informative words while preserving informative ones; and (iii) leverage negative sampling for scalable training. The authors provide a Taylor-expansion analysis to justify the data-dependent regularization effect and demonstrate strong empirical results on sentiment analysis (IMDB), document classification (Wikipedia-100), semantic relatedness (SICK), and word analogy, along with substantial efficiency gains and a very simple test-time representation.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong and consistent empirical performance with a very simple, scalable method that is easy to reproduce and deploy; (2) Clear motivation and analysis showing the corruption induces a useful data-dependent regularizer that explains the performance gains over naïve averaging and Paragraph Vector baselines.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled? Efficient, general-purpose, unsupervised document representation learning that preserves semantic content and scales to large corpora. This is a timely and important problem.

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. It builds on Word2Vec/Paragraph Vector and prior observations that linear composition of word embeddings captures semantics. The key novelty is to enforce during training that an average-of-embeddings is predictive, and to introduce a corruption mechanism that both speeds training and yields a principled regularizer. The links to dropout/marginalized denoising autoencoders are apt; the paper’s “global context via corrupted average” sits between Word2Vec and PV-DM/PV-DBOW with an elegant twist. Related work is largely well covered, though adding fastText and more recent averaging-based sentence/paragraph methods would strengthen the placement.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Empirical: The method matches or outperforms strong baselines on IMDB and Wikipedia-100 with markedly lower computational cost and simple test-time encoding, and is competitive on SICK. The word-analogy improvements (notably large on full data) substantiate the claim that the corruption yields better word embeddings. Timing comparisons and PV inference costs are convincing.
- Theoretical: The second-order expansion around the unbiased corruption mean provides a clear, interpretable regularization term that penalizes frequent, non-informative words while diminishing penalties when a word contributes to confident predictions. While approximate (diagonal Hessian, independence assumptions, and analysis around a negative-sampling surrogate), it gives a compelling mechanism that aligns with empirical observations (e.g., stopwords have small norms).
- Rigor and correctness: The methodology is standard and sound (CBOW-like with negative sampling). The analysis is carefully argued. Experiments are reasonably thorough, include ablations on corruption effects (qualitative), and code is provided. Some details (negative sampling distribution, number of negatives, window sizes) could be more explicit, but are likely in the code.

Strengths
- Simplicity and practicality: single-pass averaging at test time; model size depends only on vocabulary.
- Scalability: comparable to Word2Vec; clear wall-clock advantages over PV and RNN-based encoders.
- Insightful analysis: connects corruption to adaptive, data-dependent regularization; empirically validates by inspecting small-norm embeddings.
- Broad utility: works well for long documents (IMDB, Wikipedia); competitive for sentence relatedness.
- Reproducibility: public code; sensible preprocessing and dataset choices.

Weaknesses/concerns (non-blocking)
- Comparisons could include more recent and highly competitive baselines (e.g., fastText for supervised classification; unsupervised SIF/Sent2Vec-like averaging methods), although some of these are contemporaneous.
- Limited ablations: More quantitative analysis of the effect of corruption rate q, document-length normalization choices, and the contribution of local vs global context would strengthen the evidence.
- The analytical derivation relies on approximations and uses a uniform negative-sampling distribution in the exposition; clarifying whether the implemented training matches the analysis would help.
- The IMDB setting that allows using test data for representation learning (unsupervised) is reported; the paper also reports the stricter split, but highlighting and standardizing on the latter would avoid confusion.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect my recommendation)
- Provide full training details: number of negative samples, negative-sampling distribution (unigram^0.75 vs uniform), window size k, subsampling threshold, learning rates, epochs, and optimization details.
- Add ablations: (i) no corruption vs corruption; (ii) varying q; (iii) remove local context (pure global objective) to quantify its role; (iv) length normalization choices (1/T vs other scalings).
- Fairness/coverage: Include fastText (supervised) on classification and discuss its relation; for unsupervised comparisons, consider SIF/Sent2Vec if feasible, or at least discuss.
- Robustness: Report variance across runs; include additional datasets (e.g., AG News, Yelp, 20NG) to broaden evidence.
- Clarify analysis vs practice: If a different negative-sampling distribution is used in code, discuss implications for the regularization derivation.
- Editing: Fix reference formatting glitches and minor typos; improve figure/table captions with precise settings.

Questions for the authors
- What negative-sampling distribution and number of negatives are used in practice? Does it match the analysis (uniform) or standard unigram^0.75? How sensitive are results to this choice?
- How does performance vary with corruption rate q and with removing the local context term? Can you report a small ablation table?
- What window sizes and subsampling thresholds were used, and how sensitive are results to them?
- For IMDB, when training only on train+unlabeled (no test), what are the exact numbers for all methods, and do conclusions remain?
- Can you provide results for a Skip-gram-style variant of Doc2VecC, as suggested in the text?
- How does Doc2VecC perform on short-sentence datasets compared to LSTM/Skip-thought when controlling for training data (e.g., pretrain size matched)?
- Did you try subword information (as in fastText) to mitigate OOV and rare words; does it interact with the corruption regularizer?
- Is the per-word norm shrinkage effect robust across corpora and vocab sizes? Any quantitative metrics beyond the qualitative table?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, elegant, and practically impactful contribution that meets ICLR’s bar.
