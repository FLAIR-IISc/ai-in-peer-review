- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn document vectors as the average of (corrupted) word embeddings trained jointly with a CBOW-style predictor.
- Corruption (masking with unbiased scaling) gives faster training by updating fewer words and induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks frequent non-informative word embeddings.
- Provides a second-order Taylor analysis connecting the corruption to a variance-weighted diagonal Hessian penalty, similar to dropout/mDAE.
- Empirically matches or outperforms PV, Word2Vec+post-processing, SDA, and Skip-thought on several tasks while being much faster at train and test time.
- Also yields improved word embeddings on analogy tasks, suggesting benefits beyond document representations.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for accept: simple/scalable/effective method; novel analysis showing IDF-like regularization from corruption; strong empirical results and efficiency gains.
- Methodology/novelty: no per-document parameters, corruption applied to global document average (not local context), pushing document average to capture complementary signal.
- Theoretical motivation: Taylor expansion derivation is sound and explains selective shrinkage of frequent stopwords while preserving predictive rare words.
- IMDB sentiment: matches/exceeds PV and beats BoW/Word2Vec+IDF with large test-time efficiency gains and useful analysis of subsampling vs learned shrinkage.
- Word analogies: substantial gains over Word2Vec, indicating better word embeddings.
- Wikipedia classification: consistent gains across sizes and clear qualitative clustering (t-SNE).
- SICK relatedness: competitive but slightly behind LSTM/Skip-thought; plausible trade-off between long-document robustness and sequence modeling.
- Efficiency: parameter size independent of corpus size and simple averaging at test time yield clear practical speed/memory advantages.
- Clarify whether x is binary BoW or token-count based and whether averaging is over tokens or types, since this affects theory and implementation.
- Negative sampling: using uniform Pv vs. unigram^(3/4) may affect fairness; request results with standard negative sampling or justification for choice.
- Ablations/sensitivity requested: include q=0 (no corruption), vary q/report performance vs throughput, remove local context to isolate contributions, and test different 1/T normalizations.
- Baselines: compare to fastText-style classifiers and SIF-weighted averages (Arora et al.) which are strong simple baselines.
- Dimensionality fairness: control for Skip-thought’s much larger dimensionality by projecting or scaling Doc2VecC to rule out capacity effects.
- Statistics/reproducibility: report standard deviations over runs and full training hyperparameters (window, negatives, learning rates, subsampling); code link appreciated.
- Word analogy setup: specify training corpora, preprocessing, negative sampling, and whether subword or position weights were used.
- Visualizations: t-SNE is illustrative but add quantitative clustering/retrieval metrics for stronger evidence.
- Questions for authors: clarify x binary vs count, negative sampling k/window consistency across baselines, sensitivity to q and rescaling choice, effect of regularizer on function words (POS analysis), IMDB training split consistency and variances, SICK fine-tuning protocol and effect of disallowing adaptation, throughput/tokens-per-second and memory footprints, and potential benefits of subword features for OOV/morphology.
