- Proposes Doc2VecC: learn word embeddings so the average of word vectors is an effective document representation.  
- Uses corruption (masking words in bag-of-words) to create a global document context during training.  
- Shows corruption induces a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent, non-informative words.  
- Decouples model size from number of documents, enabling very fast training and inference.  
- Empirically outperforms or matches strong baselines (BoW, Word2Vec+IDF, DAE, Paragraph Vectors) on IMDB and Wikipedia, and is competitive on SICK.  
- Produces substantially better word analogy results than Word2Vec.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: strong empirical performance with a simple, scalable architecture and large speed advantages.  
- Key reason: clear theoretical analysis explaining corruption-induced regularization and why averaging works.  
- Problem tackled: aim to obtain high-quality, efficient document representations without per-document parameters.  
- Motivation/placement: grounds approach in linear semantics of embeddings and positions it relative to Word2Vec, Paragraph Vectors, DAEs, and sentence encoders.  
- Corruption mechanism relates to dropout/mDA and is used to shape embeddings so averages encode global semantics.  
- Accuracy: matches/outperforms Paragraph Vector on IMDB, lower error on Wikipedia, competitive on SICK, and better analogy performance.  
- Efficiency: training and inference timing strongly support claimed speedups; inference is orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors.  
- Rigor: uses second-order Taylor expansion to reveal and interpret the regularization effect.  
- Novelty/significance: simple but impactful idea to enforce averaging via corrupted global context and data-dependent regularization.
