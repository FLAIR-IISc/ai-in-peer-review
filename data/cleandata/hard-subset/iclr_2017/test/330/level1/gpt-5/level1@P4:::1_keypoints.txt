- Doc2VecC represents documents as the average of word embeddings and trains with a CBOW-style objective conditioning on local context plus a corrupted-average global document vector.
- Unbiased corruption (dropout on bag-of-words) is analyzed via a second-order Taylor expansion and argued to induce a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent/non-informative words.
- The method is claimed to be simple, highly efficient (training billions of tokens/hour), test-time cheap (just averaging), and competitive or superior to several baselines on IMDB, Wikipedia classification, SICK, and word analogy tasks.
- Paper emphasizes scalability and decoupling model size from corpus size as practical benefits.
- Recommendation: Accept — method is simple, well-motivated, empirically strong, and offers useful corruption-as-regularization insight.
- Clarity: core idea, objective, and corruption scheme are clearly described with equations and code provided, though some typos, inconsistent notation, and disorganized references remain.
- Technical soundness: straightforward CBOW extension; corruption/Taylor expansion analysis is reasonable and consistent with dropout/marginalized DAE literature; training/inference (negative sampling) and efficiency claims are justified.
- Novelty: averaging is known, but the specific corrupted-average training and adaptive regularization that suppresses frequent words is a meaningful, practical contribution that avoids document-specific parameters.
- Empirical support: Doc2VecC matches/exceeds Paragraph Vectors and other baselines on IMDB, Wiki, SICK, and produces stronger word embeddings on analogy tasks, supporting the paper’s claims.
- Baselines/fairness concerns: clarify negative sampling distribution and hyperparameters, include PV-DBOW, and add SIF/weighted-averaging baselines for stronger comparisons.
- Ablation requests: isolate effects of (a) global context without corruption, (b) varying corruption rate q (including q=0), (c) subsampling of frequent words, and quantify removing local context.
- Analysis requests: report embedding norm distributions by word frequency to validate regularization, and include confidence intervals or statistical significance for key comparisons.
- Presentation fixes: correct notation inconsistencies and typos, fix tables/references, and provide detailed training hyperparameters (window size, negatives, learning rates, epochs, vocab thresholds, hardware).
- Clarify whether unsupervised representations use test documents per task and state/justify this prominently.
- Scope/limitations: discuss ignoring word order and consider n-gram embeddings (fastText-style) as an extension; relate the corruption analysis more directly to denoising/marginalized DAEs.
- Questions for authors: which negative sampling distribution was used and sensitivity to it; sensitivity of results to corruption rate q with ablation curves; performance of (i) global average without corruption, (ii) corruption without local context, (iii) local context only; effects of subsampling on comparisons; detailed analogy training settings and breakdown by semantic/syntactic categories and dimensions; supervised fine-tuning on SICK to compare ceilings; throughput (tokens/sec) and memory footprint vs PV and Word2Vec; and evaluation on other long-document datasets (Yelp/Amazon) for robustness.
