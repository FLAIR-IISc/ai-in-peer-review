1. Summary of claimed contributions
- The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a document representation learning framework where a document vector is simply the average of its word embeddings. During training, the model uses a CBOW-style objective that conditions on both local context words and a global document vector formed by averaging a corrupted subset of the document’s words.
- A key component is unbiased corruption (dropout on the document bag-of-words), which the authors analyze via a second-order Taylor expansion to show it induces a data-dependent regularizer: embeddings of frequent/non-informative words are shrunk toward zero, while informative/rare words are less penalized when they contribute to confident predictions.
- Empirically, the method aims to be simple and efficient (training on billions of tokens per hour; fast test-time inference via averaging), while matching or improving over state-of-the-art methods (BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, Paragraph Vectors, Skip-thoughts) on IMDB sentiment, Wikipedia document classification, and SICK semantic relatedness; and producing superior word embeddings to Word2Vec on word analogy tasks.
- The paper emphasizes scalability, decoupling model size from corpus size, and practical benefits in speed and representation quality.

2. Decision
Accept. The method is simple, well-motivated, and empirically strong with clear efficiency advantages; the corruption-as-regularization analysis offers a useful explanation for its behavior.

3. Supporting arguments
- Clarity: The core idea and model are clearly described. The training objective, corruption scheme, and the intuition behind the regularization are explained with equations and a derivation. The empirical setup across tasks is broadly understandable, and a code link is provided for reproducibility. Some presentation issues (typos, inconsistent notation in equations, and disorganized references) detract slightly but do not obscure the main content.
- Technical soundness: The model is a straightforward extension of CBOW with a global corrupted-average context. The unbiased corruption and the Taylor expansion argument are standard tools and reasonably applied to show an expected objective equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. While approximate, the analysis is consistent with prior work on dropout/marginalized DAE and convincingly justifies the observed shrinkage of frequent words. The training/inference procedures are standard (negative sampling), and claims about test-time efficiency follow directly from the averaging construction.
- Novelty: Averaging word embeddings to represent documents is known, and global-context CBOW variants have been explored, but this work’s specific combination—training embeddings so that a corruption-averaged document vector is directly predictive, and showing that corruption induces an adaptive, data-dependent regularization that suppresses frequent words—is a meaningful and practical contribution. The approach contrasts with Paragraph Vectors by avoiding document-specific parameters and expensive test-time inference.
- Empirical support: On IMDB, Doc2VecC matches or exceeds Paragraph Vectors and outperforms BoW, DAE, Word2Vec+IDF, and Skip-thoughts, with notably better efficiency. On Wikipedia document classification, Doc2VecC achieves lower error than baselines across embedding sizes; visualization suggests better cluster separation. On SICK, Doc2VecC is close to strong LSTM/Skip-thought baselines despite using a much simpler architecture and being faster. On word analogies, Doc2VecC embeddings outperform Word2Vec notably. The empirical results broadly support the paper’s claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Baselines and fairness:
  - Clarify negative sampling distributions and hyperparameters for all methods. Word2Vec typically uses unigram^3/4; the text mentions uniform Pv in the derivation—please specify what was used in practice.
  - Include PV-DBOW results, given its strong performance and different capacity trade-offs from PV-DM.
  - Consider adding SIF/weighted averaging baselines (e.g., inverse word frequency with common component removal) for a stronger averaging baseline comparison.
- Ablations:
  - Provide ablations isolating the contributions of (a) global context without corruption, (b) corruption rate q (including q=0), (c) subsampling of frequent words. This would more directly support the regularization story and performance gains.
  - Report performance when removing local context (connecting to DAE), to quantify the gain from combining local+global.
- Analysis:
  - Add statistics on the distribution of embedding norms by word frequency to empirically validate the data-dependent regularization.
  - Report confidence intervals or statistical significance for key comparisons, especially where improvements are modest.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Fix notation inconsistencies (e.g., 1/T U x̃ vs 1T Ux̃), table numbering, and typos. Clean up the references.
  - Provide more details on training: window size, number of negatives, learning rates, epochs, vocabulary thresholds, and hardware.
  - Clarify whether unsupervised representation learning uses test documents; when it does, state this prominently for each task and justify as standard practice.
- Scope:
  - Discuss limitations: averaging ignores word order; consider adding n-gram embeddings (as in bag-of-tricks/fastText-style) as a possible extension.
  - Briefly relate to denoising autoencoders/marginalized DAEs more directly, since the corruption analysis is closely connected.

5. Questions for the authors
- Negative sampling: What distribution did you use in practice (uniform vs unigram^3/4)? Did you observe sensitivity to this choice, and is the analogy performance gain robust across choices?
- Corruption rate: How sensitive are results to q? Can you provide a small ablation curve on IMDB/Wiki/SICK and on analogy performance?
- Ablation of components: What is the performance of (i) global average without corruption, (ii) corruption without local context, (iii) local context only (standard CBOW), all with identical training data/compute?
- Frequent word handling: You mention subsampling frequent words was applied. How much of the gain over Word2Vec+IDF remains if subsampling is disabled for both? Conversely, what happens if subsampling is enabled only for Word2Vec+IDF?
- Analogy training data: Please provide exact numbers for vocabulary size, window size, negatives, epochs, and training time for the analogy experiments. Are the improvements uniform across semantic and syntactic categories, and do they persist at higher dimensions (e.g., 300)?
- SICK comparison: The LSTM baselines in Tai et al. (2015) are supervised on SICK. Since Doc2VecC is unsupervised with a shallow scoring model, can you add a supervised fine-tuning variant on SICK to clarify the ceiling for your approach?
- Efficiency: Can you report throughput (tokens/sec) and memory footprint, and compare to PV and Word2Vec on the same hardware?
- Generalization: Have you tested Doc2VecC on other long-document datasets (e.g., Yelp reviews, Amazon reviews) to confirm robustness across domains?
