- Introduces Doc2VecC: documents represented as averaged word embeddings with a corruption model for regularization.  
- Corruption model provides data-dependent regularization that downweights common/non-informative words and emphasizes rare/informative ones.  
- Simple model architecture that is computationally efficient for training and fast at inference (billions of words/hour on one machine).  
- Empirical results show Doc2VecC outperforms or matches state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Produces superior word embeddings for analogy tasks and can generate representations for unseen documents quickly.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: novelty and simplicity of the corruption mechanism, and strong empirical rigor.  
- Builds on Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, addressing scalability and inference inefficiencies.  
- Experiments are thorough, cover multiple datasets/tasks, and code is provided for reproducibility.  
- Efficiency makes the method practical for large-scale applications.  
- Suggestion: clarify/simplify the theoretical derivation of the regularization effect.  
- Suggestion: include comparisons with recent transformer-based models (e.g., BERT) for a more comprehensive evaluation.  
- Suggestion: add ablation studies to isolate the impact of the corruption mechanism versus simple averaging.  
- Question: How does Doc2VecC perform on fine-grained semantic tasks (e.g., QA, summarization) versus transformers?  
- Question: Can the corruption probability q be dynamically adjusted during training to improve performance?
