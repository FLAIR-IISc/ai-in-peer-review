

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a novel document representation learning framework that represents documents as simple averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption mechanism. The authors claim that this approach efficiently captures semantic meanings of documents during training while providing significant advantages in scalability and test-time efficiency over existing methods like Paragraph Vectors. They demonstrate that Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec, matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, and can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine. The core innovation appears to be the corruption mechanism that introduces a data-dependent regularization favoring informative words while suppressing common, non-discriminative ones.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. The key reasons are: (1) The core idea of representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings is not novel, having been previously proposed by Huang et al. (2012), and the paper fails to sufficiently differentiate its approach; (2) The claimed advantages (particularly in efficiency) are not convincingly demonstrated against appropriate baselines.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the corruption mechanism applied to document representation learning. However, the novelty claim is weak because Huang et al. (2012) already proposed using simple averaging of word embeddings for document representation. The paper acknowledges this work but doesn't clearly articulate how Doc2VecC's corruption mechanism provides a meaningful improvement beyond the simple averaging approach.

Regarding efficiency claims, the paper states Doc2VecC "can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine," but this is also true for Word2Vec, which is the baseline model the authors claim to improve upon. The paper doesn't provide a clear comparison of training times between Doc2VecC and Word2Vec for the same document representation task, making the efficiency claim unsubstantiated.

The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism (Section 3.1) is overly complex and doesn't provide clear intuition about why the mechanism improves performance. The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training," but the empirical evidence doesn't convincingly demonstrate that this is the case, as the improvements could be due to the averaging approach itself rather than the corruption mechanism.

The experimental results show marginal improvements over baselines, but the paper fails to isolate the contribution of the corruption mechanism. For instance, it's unclear whether the improvements come from the corruption mechanism or simply from using a different averaging approach. The comparison with Word2Vec+IDF (which applies post-processing to word embeddings) doesn't address whether Doc2VecC's improvements are due to the corruption mechanism or the averaging approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:
- Clearly differentiate Doc2VecC from Huang et al. (2012) by demonstrating how the corruption mechanism provides specific, measurable advantages that weren't possible with their approach.
- Provide a more detailed comparison of training times between Doc2VecC and Word2Vec for the same document representation task, including the same corpus size and hardware.
- Simplify the theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism and provide more intuitive explanations of how it improves performance.
- Include experiments that isolate the effect of the corruption mechanism by comparing Doc2VecC to a simple averaging approach without corruption (i.e., using standard Word2Vec embeddings).
- Clarify the relationship between Doc2VecC and Denoising Autoencoders, as the paper states Doc2VecC "reduces to DEA if the local context words are removed," but the relationship isn't clearly explained.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does Doc2VecC specifically differ from Huang et al. (2012), which also proposed representing documents as averages of word embeddings? What specific improvements does the corruption mechanism bring beyond their approach?

2. The paper claims Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed," but DEA is a different model that uses reconstruction error. How does this relate to the corruption mechanism in Doc2VecC, and what specific advantages does the corruption mechanism provide over the standard DEA approach?

3. The paper states that Doc2VecC "can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine," but this is also true for Word2Vec. Can you provide a more detailed comparison of training times between Doc2VecC and Word2Vec for the same document representation task, including the same corpus size and hardware configuration?

4. In the sentiment analysis results (Table 1), Doc2VecC achieves similar performance to Paragraph Vectors. Given that Paragraph Vectors have more parameters and are more expensive at test time, why would a practitioner choose Doc2VecC over Paragraph Vectors? What specific advantages does Doc2VecC offer over Paragraph Vectors beyond the claimed efficiency gains?
