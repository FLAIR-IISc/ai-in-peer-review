

# Review of "Doc2VecC: Document Vector through Corruption"

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective framework for document representation learning. The authors claim that representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings can capture semantic meaning when combined with a corruption-based regularization mechanism. This approach avoids the parameter growth issues of Paragraph Vectors while maintaining efficiency in both training and inference. The method is motivated by the observation that linear combinations of word embeddings preserve semantic regularities, and the corruption model (randomly removing words during training) introduces data-dependent regularization that suppresses common, non-discriminative words while favoring informative ones. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC outperforms several baselines on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly faster to train and test.

## 2. Decision
Reject.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's core claim—that Doc2VecC outperforms Paragraph Vectors (PV) while being more efficient—is undermined by several critical issues:

First, the comparison to Paragraph Vectors is incomplete. The authors compare only to PV-Distributed Memory (PV-DM), which is known to be less efficient than PV-Distributed Bag of Words (PV-DBOW) as noted in the original PV paper (Le & Mikolov, 2014). PV-DBOW is specifically designed to avoid the parameter growth issue of PV-DM and typically outperforms it on many tasks. Omitting PV-DBOW from the comparison is a serious flaw, as the authors claim their method "matches or outperforms the state-of-the-art," but PV-DBOW is the more efficient and commonly used variant of Paragraph Vectors.

Second, the sentiment analysis experiment uses the test set for representation learning, which constitutes data leakage. The paper states: "we test the various representation learning algorithms under two settings: one follows the same protocol proposed in (Mesnil et al., 2014), where representation is learned using all the available data, including the test set." This is not standard practice in machine learning and inflates performance metrics. Proper evaluation should use a separate validation set for hyperparameter tuning and reserve the test set for final evaluation.

Third, the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is overly complex and not particularly insightful. The authors derive a regularization term from Taylor expansion, but this does not clearly demonstrate why their corruption mechanism is superior to standard techniques like subsampling frequent words (which is already used in Word2Vec and would be needed for Word2Vec+IDF to work well).

Fourth, the claim that Doc2VecC "naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training" is not convincingly supported. The paper shows that Doc2VecC outperforms Word2Vec+IDF, but fails to demonstrate that the document representations themselves are better than those produced by PV or other methods.

## 4. Additional feedback
- The authors should include PV-DBOW in their comparison, as it is the more efficient variant of Paragraph Vectors and would provide a fairer comparison.
- The experimental setup should follow standard machine learning practices with proper train/validation/test splits.
- The paper would benefit from ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the corruption mechanism versus simple averaging of word embeddings.
- The regularization effect should be more clearly explained and compared to standard techniques like subsampling frequent words.
- The paper claims Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization," but the evidence provided is weak. A more thorough analysis of how the corruption model differs from standard regularization techniques would strengthen the contribution.

## 5. Questions for authors
- Why was PV-DBOW omitted from the comparison? How does Doc2VecC compare to PV-DBOW, which is the more efficient variant of Paragraph Vectors?
- What evidence do you have that the document representations produced by Doc2VecC are semantically meaningful beyond the accuracy on specific tasks?
- How does the corruption model specifically contribute to performance improvements, and how does it differ from standard techniques like dropout or subsampling?
- What is the impact of the corruption rate q on performance, and how should it be tuned in practice?
- Why does the paper claim Doc2VecC "naturally exerts a stronger regularization" when standard word embedding techniques already use subsampling to handle frequent words, which serves a similar purpose?
