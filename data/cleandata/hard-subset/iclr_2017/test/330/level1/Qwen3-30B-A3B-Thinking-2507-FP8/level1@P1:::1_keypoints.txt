- Introduces Doc2VecC: represent documents as averages of word embeddings with a corruption model that randomly removes words during training for data-dependent regularization.  
- Regularization favors informative/rare words and suppresses common, non-discriminative words via the corruption process.  
- Claims Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness while being much more efficient.  
- Generates document vectors for unseen documents nearly instantly and can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine.  
- Recommends acceptance based on theoretical soundness, empirical validation, and strong efficiency gains.  
- Addresses limitations of Paragraph Vectors: model complexity scales with corpus size and inference for unseen docs is expensive; Doc2VecC depends only on vocabulary size.  
- Provides strong theoretical justification: Taylor expansion shows corruption introduces a regularizer that penalizes common-word embeddings; connected to dropout and denoising autoencoders.  
- Empirical results are comprehensive: large improvements on IMDB, state-of-the-art on Wikipedia classification, and strong SemEval performance; outperforms Skip-thought in training time.  
- Demonstrates via analysis (Table 3) that Doc2VecC suppresses stop-word embeddings while preserving informative words.  
- Suggest clearer explanation or visualization of how corruption specifically improves averaging-based document representations.  
- Recommend stronger comparisons to both PV-DM and PV-DBOW Paragraph Vector variants.  
- Suggest expanding the connection to Denoising Autoencoders to clarify relationships to prior work.  
- Request a hyperparameter sensitivity analysis for the corruption rate q (used q=0.9) to show robustness.  
- Question: How does Doc2VecC’s training-time enforcement of averaged semantics differ from post-hoc weighted averages like Word2Vec+IDF?  
- Question: How does the modulation term σ(1−σ) help distinguish common-but-informative vs common-uninformative words?  
- Question: Why do LSTM methods outperform on short-text SICK despite Doc2VecC’s strengths for longer paragraphs, and can Doc2VecC be adapted for short text?
