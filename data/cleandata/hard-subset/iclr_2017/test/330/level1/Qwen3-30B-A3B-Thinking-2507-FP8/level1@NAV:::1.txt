

This paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective document representation learning framework that represents documents as averages of word embeddings, enhanced by a corruption model that introduces data-dependent regularization. The authors convincingly argue that this approach captures semantic meanings more effectively than existing methods while being significantly more efficient. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC outperforms or matches state-of-the-art methods across sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while training and inference are substantially faster than alternatives like Paragraph Vectors.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature, addressing clear limitations of existing document representation methods; (2) The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting all claims, including theoretical analysis of the corruption mechanism as regularization, comprehensive experiments across multiple tasks, and convincing efficiency comparisons.

The paper's strengths include a clear theoretical justification for the corruption model, well-designed experiments with appropriate baselines, and compelling efficiency metrics. The ablation study in Table 3 effectively demonstrates how the corruption model suppresses embeddings of common, non-discriminative words, which is a novel contribution to the field.

For improvement, I suggest clarifying the relationship between Doc2VecC and other recent work on document representation (e.g., BERT-based approaches that emerged after this paper's submission). Additionally, the authors could provide more insight into why Skip-thought vectors performed poorly on IMDB (Section 4.2), as this seems like a significant outlier that deserves deeper analysis.

I have two specific questions for clarification: (1) How would the corruption rate q affect performance when applied to different document lengths? (2) Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of how the corruption model's regularization differs from standard word subsampling techniques used in Word2Vec?
