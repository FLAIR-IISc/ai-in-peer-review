

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of claims
The paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a framework for efficient document representation learning. The authors claim that by representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings during training, with a corruption mechanism that randomly removes words from documents, their method produces high-quality document representations. The corruption mechanism serves as data-dependent regularization, favoring informative/rare words while suppressing common non-discriminative words. The authors claim Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient to train and test than alternatives like Paragraph Vectors.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution with a novel corruption mechanism that enables a simple averaging approach to document representation. The empirical results are comprehensive and consistently show improvements over baselines.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors effectively position their work in relation to Word2Vec and Paragraph Vectors, clearly articulating the limitations of existing approaches (high model complexity, expensive inference for unseen documents) that their method addresses. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularization is sound, with a Taylor expansion analysis showing how the corruption introduces a specific regularization effect on word embeddings.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and scientifically rigorous. The authors test on multiple standard benchmarks (IMDB sentiment analysis, Wikipedia document classification, SemEval semantic relatedness) across various task types. Their results consistently show Doc2VecC outperforming or matching state-of-the-art methods while being significantly more efficient. The ablation study in Table 3 clearly demonstrates the regularization effect of the corruption mechanism on word embeddings, showing that Doc2VecC effectively suppresses embeddings of common non-discriminative words.

The paper also provides important efficiency metrics, showing that Doc2VecC can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine, and generates document representations at test time orders of magnitude faster than alternatives like Paragraph Vectors.

## 4. Additional feedback
- The authors could provide a more detailed sensitivity analysis for the corruption rate q, as it's mentioned that they used q=0.9 throughout experiments but don't discuss how varying q affects performance.
- The comparison with Skip-thought Vectors could be strengthened by training Skip-thought on the same dataset rather than using a pre-trained model, to ensure a fair comparison.
- The relationship to Huang et al. (2012), which proposed using average word embeddings for document representation, could be clarified more explicitly. The authors should better distinguish their corruption-based regularization from that earlier work.
- The paper could better explain why the corruption mechanism works particularly well for document representation, as opposed to just word representation.

## 5. Questions for authors
- The paper shows in Table 3 that Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words, but how does this regularization specifically affect the semantic meaning of the document representation? Could the authors provide examples of documents where this leads to meaningful improvements?
- The paper claims Doc2VecC is faster to train than Skip-thought Vectors (2 hours vs. 2 weeks), but how does the training time scale with corpus size compared to other methods? Does Doc2VecC maintain its efficiency advantage as the corpus grows?
- The paper states Doc2VecC "matches or out-performs the state-of-the-art" but doesn't explicitly identify which specific task or dataset shows the most significant improvement. Could the authors highlight which results are most compelling?
