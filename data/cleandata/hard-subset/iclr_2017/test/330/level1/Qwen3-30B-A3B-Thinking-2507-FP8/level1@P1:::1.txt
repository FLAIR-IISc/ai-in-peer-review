

# Peer Review for "Document Vector through Corruption"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces Doc2VecC, a simple yet effective framework for document representation learning. It proposes representing documents as simple averages of word embeddings, with a key innovation being a corruption model that randomly removes words during training to introduce data-dependent regularization. This regularization mechanism favors informative or rare words while suppressing embeddings of common, non-discriminative words. The authors claim that Doc2VecC matches or outperforms state-of-the-art document representation methods on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly more efficient in both training and test time. The paper demonstrates that Doc2VecC can be trained on billions of words per hour on a single machine and generates document representations for unseen documents in near-instant time.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a compelling contribution that is both theoretically sound and empirically validated. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach is well-motivated by the limitations of existing methods and the simplicity of the Word2Vec framework; (2) the empirical results across multiple tasks demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines while maintaining exceptional efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in document representation learning. The authors correctly identify two key limitations of Paragraph Vectors (Le & Mikolov, 2014): (1) the model complexity grows with the size of the training corpus, and (2) generating representations for unseen documents is computationally expensive. Doc2VecC elegantly circumvents both issues by using a simple averaging mechanism that depends only on the vocabulary size, not the corpus size.

The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularization is particularly strong. The authors provide a detailed Taylor expansion analysis showing how the corruption model introduces a regularization term that specifically penalizes embeddings of common words while allowing critical words to maintain strong representations. This explanation is well-placed in the literature, drawing appropriate connections to dropout training and denoising autoencoders.

The empirical results are comprehensive and convincing. Doc2VecC outperforms all baselines on the IMDB sentiment analysis task (beating BoW by 15%), achieves state-of-the-art results on the Wikipedia document classification task, and significantly outperforms the competition-winning solutions on the SemEval semantic relatedness task. The efficiency gains are equally impressive, with Doc2VecC training in hours compared to days for comparable methods like Skip-thought Vectors.

The paper also provides a thoughtful analysis of why the corruption model works effectively, particularly in Table 3 where they demonstrate that Doc2VecC successfully suppresses embeddings of common stop words while preserving meaningful representations for rare, informative words.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. The paper could provide a clearer explanation of why the corruption model specifically enhances the averaging operation. While the regularization effect is well-explained, it would be helpful to include a small case study or visualization showing how the corruption mechanism leads to better document representations through the averaging process.

2. The comparison with Paragraph Vectors could be strengthened. The paper notes that Paragraph Vectors performs surprisingly poorly on the word analogy task, but it would be beneficial to explicitly compare with both PV-DM and PV-DBOW versions of Paragraph Vectors, as these are the two standard variants.

3. The paper mentions that Doc2VecC "reduces to Denoising Autoencoders (DEA) if the local context words are removed." This is an important connection that could be developed more thoroughly, as it would help readers understand the relationship between Doc2VecC and existing work on denoising.

4. The paper could provide more detail about the hyperparameter sensitivity to the corruption rate q. While the authors mention they used q=0.9 throughout, it would be helpful to see a small sensitivity analysis showing how performance changes with different corruption rates.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that "Doc2VecC naturally enforces document semantics to be captured by averaged word embeddings during training." How does this differ from previous approaches that use weighted averages of word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec+IDF)? What specific mechanism in Doc2VecC ensures that the averaging operation captures semantics, beyond what could be achieved with post-hoc weighting?

2. In Table 3, the authors show that Doc2VecC suppresses embeddings of common words. However, they also note that "the regularization is modulated by σw,c,x(1−σw,c,x), which is small if σw,c,x → 1 or 0." Could the authors elaborate on how this modulation helps the model distinguish between words that are common but informative versus words that are common and uninformative?

3. The paper mentions that "Doc2VecC matches or out-performs the state-of-the-art in generating high-quality document representations." For the semantic relatedness task, the authors report that Doc2VecC slightly underperforms LSTM-based methods. Given the paper's claim that Doc2VecC is better for longer paragraphs, could the authors provide more insight into why LSTM-based methods outperform on the SICK dataset (which contains relatively short sentences), and whether there's a way to adapt Doc2VecC to handle short text better?
