## Review of "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

This paper presents Doc2VecC, a novel and efficient document representation learning framework. The core idea is to represent documents as the average of word embeddings, coupled with a “corruption” model that regularizes the embeddings, favoring informative words and suppressing common ones. The authors demonstrate that Doc2VecC achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being significantly faster to train and test than many existing methods. The simplicity and efficiency of the approach are particularly appealing.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a promising approach and strong empirical results, the explanation of *why* the corruption model works as a data-dependent regularization feels overly complex and relies heavily on second-order Taylor expansions. The justification for this expansion and its practical impact on the learning process isn’t entirely convincing and feels somewhat hand-wavy. The benefit of this detailed derivation isn’t immediately clear, and the paper might be stronger by focusing on the empirical benefits rather than a complex theoretical justification.

The supporting arguments for this decision stem from the aforementioned concerns regarding the regularization explanation. While the authors *show* that the corruption model improves performance, the detailed mathematical derivation doesn’t fully illuminate *how* it achieves this. The connection between the Taylor expansion, the variance matrix, and the resulting regularization effect feels tenuous and requires further clarification. Additionally, while the experimental results are comprehensive, the ablation studies focusing specifically on the corruption component could be more detailed. How does performance change with different corruption rates (q)? What is the impact of removing the corruption step entirely?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more intuitive explanation of the corruption model’s effect. Perhaps visualizing the learned embeddings before and after corruption could be helpful.
*   The authors mention that Doc2VecC doesn't rely on subsampling frequent words, unlike some baselines. It would be interesting to see a direct comparison where subsampling is *disabled* for those baselines to isolate the effect of the corruption model.
*   The choice of hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimensionality, corruption rate) is briefly mentioned. A more detailed discussion of hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
*   **Question for Authors:** Could you elaborate on the practical implications of the second-order Taylor expansion? Does it significantly impact computational cost during training? If so, are there potential approximations that could simplify the process without sacrificing performance?
*   **Question for Authors:** The paper highlights the efficiency gains of Doc2VecC. Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the computational cost (e.g., training time, memory usage) compared to other methods, particularly Paragraph Vectors?
