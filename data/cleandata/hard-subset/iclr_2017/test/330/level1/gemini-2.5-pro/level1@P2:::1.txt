### **Summary**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an elegant, efficient, and highly effective framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The novelty lies in how these embeddings are learned: within a Word2Vec-like CBoW architecture, the model predicts target words using both a local context window and a global document context. This global context is derived from a "corrupted" version of the document, where words are randomly dropped out. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption scheme is approximately equivalent to adding a data-dependent regularizer to the objective function. This regularizer has the desirable property of penalizing the embeddings of frequent, non-informative words while preserving the magnitude of embeddings for rare or discriminative words. The paper presents strong empirical results across a range of tasks—sentiment analysis, document classification, word analogy, and semantic relatedness—demonstrating that Doc2VecC often matches or surpasses state-of-the-art models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought vectors, while being significantly more efficient to train and apply to new documents.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Insight:** The paper presents a simple yet powerful idea—using document corruption as a training signal—and provides a clear theoretical justification that connects this practical technique to a principled, data-dependent regularization scheme. This provides valuable insight into *why* simply averaging word vectors can be made to work so well.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support and Practicality:** The claims are backed by extensive experiments on multiple, diverse benchmarks against strong baselines. The method not only achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance but also offers significant advantages in computational efficiency and scalability, making it a highly practical and attractive method for real-world applications.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

First, the proposed method elegantly addresses the primary drawbacks of its closest predecessor, Paragraph Vectors (PV). By eschewing unique vectors for each training document, Doc2VecC's complexity is dependent only on vocabulary size, not corpus size. Furthermore, generating a representation for an unseen document is a trivial and fast operation (averaging word vectors), in stark contrast to the expensive inference step required by PV. This makes Doc2VecC far more scalable and practical.

Second, the theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a major contribution. Rather than presenting the corruption model as a mere heuristic, the authors use a Taylor expansion to show its equivalence to a regularizer. This regularizer insightfully penalizes frequent words more heavily but diminishes the penalty for words that are crucial for making confident predictions. This provides a principled explanation for the model's ability to down-weight common stop words and focus on semantically meaningful terms, a claim further supported by the qualitative analysis in Table 3.

Third, the empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing.
*   On document-level tasks like sentiment analysis (IMDB) and classification (Wikipedia), Doc2VecC is shown to be a top performer, outperforming PV and Skip-thought vectors. The t-SNE visualizations provide a clear qualitative illustration of the superior class separability of Doc2VecC's representations.
*   A particularly impressive result is the performance on the word analogy task. The fact that Doc2VecC's word embeddings are substantially better than those from Word2Vec suggests that learning to compose document representations provides a richer, more effective training signal for the word embeddings themselves. This is a significant finding.
*   The performance on the SICK dataset is reasonable and the authors provide a plausible hypothesis for why recurrent models might have an edge on very short sentences, while their model excels on longer documents. This nuanced discussion adds credibility to the analysis.
*   Finally, the efficiency results (Table 2) are dramatic and clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the model in terms of both training and, especially, inference time.

The paper is well-written, the method is clearly explained, and the provision of code enhances reproducibility. Overall, Doc2VecC represents a significant step forward in unsupervised document representation learning, combining simplicity, theoretical elegance, strong performance, and practical efficiency.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   The title of the submitted paper appears to have a typo: "MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION". This should almost certainly be "DOCUMENT REPRESENTATIONS THROUGH CORRUPTION" or similar.
*   The theoretical justification in Section 3.1 relies on a second-order Taylor approximation. The experiments use a high corruption probability (`q=0.9`), which might stretch the validity of an expansion around the mean (`x`). A brief discussion on the limitations or robustness of this approximation would strengthen the paper. For instance, does the approximation hold better for smaller `q`, and how does that affect performance?
*   The paper hypothesizes that the performance gap between Doc2VecC and Skip-thought vectors is due to document length. This is an excellent point. It could be made much stronger by including a brief analysis that plots error rates against document length for both models on a dataset like IMDB.
*   An ablation study could further solidify the paper's claims. For example, since the model is presented as an extension of a Denoising Autoencoder (by adding local context), an experiment comparing Doc2VecC directly against a version of itself without the local context term (`Uct`) would explicitly quantify the benefit of this component.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  The improvement of Doc2VecC word vectors over standard Word2Vec vectors on the analogy task is impressive. What is your intuition for why enforcing compositional document-level objectives leads to qualitatively better word-level semantics, compared to just using a local context window?
2.  In Section 4.2, you state that Doc2VecC does not rely on the frequent-word subsampling trick used by Word2Vec. Did you run the main experiments for Doc2VecC *without* this subsampling? Showing that performance remains high without this heuristic would be a very strong testament to the effectiveness of the proposed corruption-based regularization.
3.  Regarding the Taylor approximation, how sensitive is the model's performance to the corruption rate `q`? You mention the model is "not sensitive" and you use `q=0.9` for efficiency, but it would be interesting to see a plot of performance vs. `q` to understand the trade-offs.
