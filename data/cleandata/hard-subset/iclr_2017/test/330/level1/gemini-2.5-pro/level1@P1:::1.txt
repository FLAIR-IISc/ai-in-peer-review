### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an efficient and effective framework for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent each document as a simple average of the embeddings of its constituent words. Unlike prior work that applies averaging as a post-processing step, Doc2VecC enforces this compositional property directly during training. The model's key innovation is a corruption mechanism where a random subset of words is used to form the document's "global context" vector when predicting a target word. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer. This regularizer is claimed to suppress the embeddings of frequent but non-informative words while preserving the signal from rarer, more discriminative ones.

The paper backs these claims with a comprehensive set of experiments. On sentiment analysis, document classification, and word analogy tasks, Doc2VecC is shown to match or significantly outperform strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors (Doc2Vec), Denoising Autoencoders, and averaged Word2Vec embeddings. A particularly strong result is that the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are superior to those from Word2Vec on the standard word analogy task. The method is also demonstrated to be highly efficient, with training and inference times that are orders of magnitude faster than competing high-performance models like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Elegant Simplicity and Efficiency:** The paper presents a simple, well-motivated model that directly addresses the primary drawbacks of its closest predecessor, Paragraph Vectors. By representing the document vector compositionally instead of learning a unique vector for each document, Doc2VecC's complexity is independent of the training corpus size, and inference for new documents is extremely fast (a simple average) rather than a costly optimization procedure. This represents a significant practical advancement.
2.  **Strong Theoretical Grounding and Empirical Support:** The connection drawn between the corruption process and an explicit data-dependent regularization term (Section 3.1) provides a solid theoretical foundation for the method's design. This theoretical insight is convincingly supported by comprehensive experiments across multiple benchmarks, which not only demonstrate state-of-the-art performance but also include qualitative analyses (Table 3) that directly validate the regularizer's claimed effect on word embeddings.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are numerous. The proposed model, Doc2VecC, is a clever evolution of the CBoW architecture. It elegantly sidesteps the scalability issues of Paragraph Vectors while retaining the core idea of using both local and global context for prediction.

The theoretical analysis in Section 3.1 is a standout feature. By using a Taylor expansion, the authors demonstrate that their stochastic corruption scheme is not just an ad-hoc trick but approximates a well-defined regularizer. This regularizer penalizes word vectors based on their frequency and their utility in making confident predictions, providing a principled explanation for why the model learns to down-weight common stop words.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing.
*   **Downstream Tasks:** On the IMDB sentiment analysis and Wikipedia classification tasks, Doc2VecC achieves superior or competitive results compared to all baselines, including the more complex Paragraph Vectors. The t-SNE visualizations in Figure 3 provide a clear qualitative illustration of the learned representations' quality.
*   **Word Embedding Quality:** The results on the word analogy task are particularly impressive. The finding that Doc2VecC produces significantly better word embeddings than the standard Word2Vec model is a powerful testament to the benefits of its training objective. It suggests that forcing word vectors to compose into meaningful document vectors provides a richer training signal than the local context window alone.
*   **Efficiency:** The timing experiments in Table 2 clearly demonstrate the practical advantages of the method. The inference speedup over Paragraph Vectors is dramatic (7 seconds vs. ~4.5 minutes on the IMDB test set), making Doc2VecC far more suitable for real-world applications where new documents must be processed quickly.

The authors are also intellectually honest, acknowledging where their model underperforms slightly (e.g., against LSTMs on the short-sentence SICK dataset) and offering a plausible hypothesis regarding the model's differing strengths for long vs. short texts.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Clarity on Subsampling:** The last paragraph of Section 4.2 states that the frequent word subsampling trick from Mikolov et al. (2013) was applied for all reported numbers. However, it also claims that Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick". This is slightly confusing. It would be beneficial to clarify if Doc2VecC's performance was measured *without* this trick to fully support the claim, or to rephrase the claim to state that the model's built-in regularization is complementary to or enhanced by subsampling.
*   **Ablation on Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper states that `q=0.9` was used throughout the experiments and that the model is not sensitive to this choice. While this is acceptable, a small plot or table showing performance on a validation set for a few different values of `q` (e.g., 0.5, 0.7, 0.9) would make this claim more concrete and add to the paper's rigor.
*   **Hypothesis on Document Length:** The hypothesis that Doc2VecC excels at long documents while LSTMs excel at short ones is very interesting. While a full experiment is beyond the scope of this paper, it could be briefly explored by, for instance, reporting the performance on the IMDB dataset on subsets of reviews binned by length. This would provide preliminary evidence for an interesting avenue of future work.
*   **Title:** The title in the provided text is truncated ("MENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"). Please ensure the full title is correct in the final version.

### Questions for the Authors

*   In the objective function (Eq. 2), the global context term `(1/T) * U*x̃` is normalized by `T`, the original document length. Was there a specific reason for this choice over normalizing by the number of non-zero elements in the corrupted vector `x̃`?
*   Could you elaborate on your intuition for why enforcing compositional document representations so dramatically improves performance on the word-level analogy task? Is the primary mechanism that the global document context provides a more diverse and robust signal for learning word semantics compared to the constraints of the local CBoW window alone?
*   In the Denoising Autoencoder baseline, could you briefly clarify how negative sampling was employed with a Kullback-Leibler divergence reconstruction error? Typically, negative sampling is associated with log-likelihood objectives.
