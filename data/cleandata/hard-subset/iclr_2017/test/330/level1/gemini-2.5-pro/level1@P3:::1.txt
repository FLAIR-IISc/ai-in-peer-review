### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), an elegant and highly efficient method for learning distributed representations of documents. The core idea is to represent a document as a simple average of its constituent word embeddings. The novelty lies in the learning procedure: the word embeddings are trained in a Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) style framework to predict a target word from both its local context (neighboring words) and a global context. Crucially, this global context is derived from an average of word embeddings from a *corrupted* version of the document, where words are randomly dropped. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis, framing this corruption as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, non-informative words. The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments that this simple architecture achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, while being orders of magnitude faster to train and use for inference than competing methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors. A notable contribution is that the word embeddings learned by Doc2VecC are themselves superior to those from the standard Word2Vec model on word analogy tasks.

### **Decision: Accept**

My decision is to **accept** this paper. The key reasons are:

1.  **Significant practical value:** The proposed method is remarkably simple, scalable, and efficient. It addresses the key drawbacks of Paragraph Vectors (model size dependency on corpus size and slow inference) while achieving superior or comparable performance. This combination of high performance and high efficiency makes it a very strong and practical contribution.
2.  **Novel and well-motivated approach:** The central idea of using a corrupted document average as a global training context is novel. The authors provide a solid theoretical justification for this "corruption" mechanism, linking it to adaptive regularization. This provides a principled explanation for why a simple average of word embeddings, learned this way, can be so effective, moving beyond heuristic approaches like post-hoc IDF weighting.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

First, the **simplicity and efficiency** of Doc2VecC cannot be overstated. By decoupling the model size from the number of documents and relying on a simple averaging operation for inference, the authors present a method that is practically superior to Paragraph Vectors, which requires storing a unique vector for each training document and performing a costly inference step for new documents. The reported training and testing times in Table 2 provide clear evidence of this substantial advantage. This makes the method highly applicable to large-scale, real-world systems.

Second, the **empirical results are strong and comprehensive**. The authors evaluate their model on a diverse set of tasks (sentiment, topic classification, semantic relatedness) and consistently show that Doc2VecC performs at or near the top. It outperforms Paragraph Vectors on both the IMDB and Wikipedia classification tasks. The analysis of its performance relative to Skip-thought Vectors—excelling on long documents (IMDB) while being slightly behind on short sentences (SICK)—is insightful and highlights a key practical difference between the modeling approaches.

Third, the **theoretical justification in Section 3.1 is a key contribution**. While the Taylor expansion is an approximation, it provides a clear intuition for why the corruption scheme works. It effectively learns to down-weight the norms of frequent, non-discriminative words (e.g., stop words, as shown in Table 3), achieving a similar goal to TF-IDF but in a more integrated, data-driven manner during training. This analysis elevates the method from a clever heuristic to a well-motivated technique.

Finally, the finding that **Doc2VecC produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec** (Table 4) is a significant result in its own right. It suggests that forcing word vectors to be compositionally effective for representing document-level semantics also improves their representation of local, word-level semantics. This adds another layer of value to the proposed framework.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

1.  **Contradiction regarding subsampling:** There is a confusing contradiction in Section 4.2. The paper claims that Doc2VecC "does not rely on this trick [subsampling of frequent words]" because its regularizer handles frequent words naturally. However, it immediately follows by stating, "for all the numbers reported, we applied the trick". This significantly weakens the claim. To make a convincing case, you should report the performance of Doc2VecC *without* subsampling and show that the performance drop is minimal compared to the drop for Word2Vec+AVG.
2.  **Ablation Study on Corruption:** The paper's main technical contribution is the corruption mechanism. A direct ablation study would make its impact clearer. The paper would be strengthened by including results for a variant that uses the full, uncorrupted document average as the global context. This would directly isolate and quantify the benefit of the corruption itself.
3.  **Sensitivity to Corruption Rate `q`:** The paper states that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" and fixes it at 0.9. While this is acceptable for brevity, a plot showing performance (e.g., IMDB accuracy) as a function of `q` (e.g., from 0.0 to 0.95) would provide much richer information and add confidence to this claim.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify some points in the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you please provide the results for Doc2VecC on the IMDB sentiment task without using the frequent word subsampling technique? This would be crucial evidence to support the claim that the implicit regularization from corruption makes this pre-processing step redundant.
2.  The paper argues that Paragraph Vectors relies heavily on its unique document vectors, leading to poor word-level representations (as seen in the word analogy task). Given that Doc2VecC has no such unique document vectors, have you analyzed whether the local CBoW context or the global corrupted document context contributes more to the learning objective during training?
3.  Have you experimented with a model that uses the full, uncorrupted average of word embeddings as the global context? How does this baseline compare to the proposed Doc2VecC, and how does it compare to a simple Word2Vec+AVG baseline? This would help to precisely disentangle the benefits of having a global context from the benefits of corrupting it.
