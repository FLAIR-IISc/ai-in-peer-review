- Proposes Doc2VecC: represent documents as the average of word embeddings trained end-to-end with a modified CBoW that uses a corrupted-sample global context.  
- Corruption: global context is formed by averaging embeddings of a randomly sampled subset of document words, termed "corruption."  
- Theoretical claim: corruption is equivalent to a data-dependent regularizer that shrinks embeddings of frequent non-discriminative words while preserving informative ones.  
- Practical advantage: training time independent of corpus size and very fast inference for unseen documents (simple averaging).  
- Empirical claim: matches or surpasses Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thoughts on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness tasks.  
- Word-embedding quality: produces substantially better word vectors than Word2Vec on word analogy tasks.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: novel/simple technically sound method, strong theoretical justification, and efficient with strong empirical support.  
- Well-motivated: addresses Paragraph Vectors’ scalability and slow inference by decoupling complexity from document count.  
- Strong empirical results: consistent performance gains on Wikipedia classification and competitive IMDB sentiment results; t-SNE shows better class separation.  
- Theoretical support validated: empirical evidence (e.g., suppression of stop-word embeddings) supports the regularizer analysis.  
- Clarity and reproducibility: paper is well-written and code is provided.  
- Minor presentation issues: typographical/table numbering errors need proofreading and correction.  
- Suggested ablation: evaluate Doc2VecC without the local context (pure DAE variant) to quantify the CBoW objective’s contribution.  
- Suggested theoretical clarification: comment on the validity of the second-order Taylor expansion approximation and the magnitude of higher-order terms.  
- Questions for authors: clarify interplay between frequency and prediction-uncertainty in the regularizer (e.g., frequent but predictive vs. non-discriminative words).  
- Questions for authors: provide empirical sensitivity analysis for corruption rate q (set to 0.9) across tasks.  
- Questions for authors: detail fine-tuning on SICK—which parameters are updated, whether corruption is used during fine-tuning, and how much fine-tuning improves results.
