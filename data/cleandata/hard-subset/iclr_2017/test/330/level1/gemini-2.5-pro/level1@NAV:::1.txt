This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a simple, elegant, and highly efficient framework for learning document representations. The core idea is to represent a document as an average of its word embeddings, a constraint that is enforced during training. The key contribution is a corruption mechanism that randomly drops words from the document’s global context. The authors provide a compelling theoretical analysis framing this corruption as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, non-informative words. The paper claims that this simple architecture matches or outperforms state-of-the-art models on various NLP tasks while being significantly faster to train and use for inference.

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are: 1) The proposed method addresses critical and practical limitations of prior work—namely, the scalability and inference-time inefficiency of models like Paragraph Vectors. 2) The claims of high performance and efficiency are convincingly supported by comprehensive experiments across a diverse range of standard benchmarks.

The supporting arguments for this decision are strong. Doc2VecC’s simplicity is its greatest strength; by avoiding the need to learn unique vectors for each document, it scales with vocabulary size rather than corpus size and makes inference for unseen documents trivial and fast. The empirical evaluation is thorough, demonstrating superior or competitive performance against strong baselines, including Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors, on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness. The finding that Doc2VecC also produces better word embeddings than Word2Vec, as shown in the word analogy task, is a significant and impressive result. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism adds depth to the paper, providing a clear intuition for why the model works so well.

To help improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions. The claim that the model is "not sensitive to the corruption rate q" is a strong one; providing a small sensitivity analysis (e.g., a table or plot showing performance for a few different values of q) would make this more concrete. The theoretical analysis based on the Taylor expansion is insightful, but it is an approximation. A brief discussion on the validity or potential limitations of this approximation in the context of the model would be welcome.

Finally, I have a couple of questions for the authors that would strengthen my assessment:
1.  The paper argues that the implicit regularization from corruption makes the common practice of subsampling frequent words unnecessary. Could you provide results for Doc2VecC trained with and without this subsampling step to directly ablate the effect of your regularizer versus this heuristic?
2.  How does the model perform when the global context is constructed from *all* words in the document (i.e., q=0)? This would serve as a crucial baseline to quantify the performance gain attributable directly to the corruption mechanism.
