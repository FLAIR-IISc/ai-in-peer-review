- Introduces Doc2VecC: average of word embeddings with training via CBoW using a corrupted-document global context.  
- The corruption mechanism is theoretically framed as a data-dependent regularizer that downweights common, non-informative words.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Major practical value: simple, scalable, and efficient; decouples model size from corpus size and enables fast inference vs. Paragraph Vectors.  
- Novelty: using a corrupted document average as global context with solid theoretical motivation.  
- Empirical strength: state-of-the-art or competitive results on sentiment, document classification, and semantic relatedness across diverse tasks.  
- Efficiency evidence: training and inference are orders of magnitude faster than Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors (Table 2).  
- Performance nuance: excels on long documents (IMDB) and is slightly behind on short-sentence tasks (SICK), highlighting practical differences to Skip-thought.  
- Theoretical contribution: Taylor expansion analysis explains how corruption implicitly down-weights frequent words, akin to TF-IDF but learned during training.  
- Additional empirical finding: Doc2VecC yields better word embeddings than Word2Vec on analogy tasks.  
- Improvement suggestion 1: Clarify or remove contradiction about subsampling—report results without subsampling to validate the regularizer claim.  
- Improvement suggestion 2: Include an ablation comparing corrupted vs. uncorrupted document averages to isolate the corruption benefit.  
- Improvement suggestion 3: Provide sensitivity analysis for corruption rate q (e.g., plot performance across q values).  
- Questions for authors: provide IMDB results without subsampling; analyze relative contributions of local CBoW vs. global corrupted context during training; report results for using an uncorrupted global average baseline.
