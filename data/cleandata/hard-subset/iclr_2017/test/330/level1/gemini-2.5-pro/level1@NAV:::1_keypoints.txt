- Proposes Doc2VecC, a simple, elegant, and highly efficient framework for learning document representations.  
- Represents a document as the average of its word embeddings, enforced during training.  
- Introduces a corruption mechanism that randomly drops words from the document’s global context.  
- Provides theoretical analysis framing corruption as a data-dependent regularizer that penalizes common, non-informative words.  
- Claims matching or outperforming state-of-the-art models on various NLP tasks while being much faster to train and infer.  
- Recommends acceptance, citing practical impact on scalability and inference-time inefficiency of prior methods like Paragraph Vectors.  
- Argues the method addresses scalability by scaling with vocabulary size rather than corpus size and enabling trivial inference for unseen documents.  
- Empirical evaluation is thorough and diverse, supporting high performance and efficiency across standard benchmarks.  
- Demonstrates superior or competitive performance versus Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought on sentiment, classification, and semantic relatedness.  
- Shows Doc2VecC yields better word embeddings than Word2Vec on word analogy tasks.  
- Theoretical justification via corruption enhances understanding and provides clear intuition for the method’s effectiveness.  
- Suggests adding a sensitivity analysis for the corruption rate q to substantiate the claim of insensitivity.  
- Recommends discussing validity and potential limitations of the Taylor expansion approximation used in the theoretical analysis.  
- Requests ablation: results with and without subsampling frequent words to separate implicit regularization from the heuristic.  
- Requests baseline: performance when global context uses all words (q=0) to quantify gain from the corruption mechanism.
