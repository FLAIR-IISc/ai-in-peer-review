### Review of "DOCUMENTS THROUGH CORRUPTION"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces Document Vector through Corruption (Doc2VecC), a novel and efficient framework for learning distributed representations of documents. The core contribution is a simple yet powerful model architecture that represents each document as an average of the embeddings of its constituent words. Unlike prior methods that treat document vector generation as a post-processing step on pre-trained word embeddings, Doc2VecC is trained end-to-end to ensure that the learned word embeddings are optimized for this averaging composition.

The key mechanism is a modification of the Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) architecture. To predict a target word, the model uses not only the local context of surrounding words but also a global context vector representing the entire document. This global context is constructed by averaging the embeddings of a randomly sampled subset of words from the document—a process the authors term "corruption." The paper provides a theoretical analysis showing that this corruption process is equivalent to imposing a data-dependent regularizer on the word embeddings. This regularizer penalizes the embeddings of frequent, non-discriminative words, pushing their norms towards zero, while preserving the norms of rare or informative words.

The authors claim that Doc2VecC is highly efficient, with training time independent of the number of documents in the corpus and fast inference for unseen documents. They support these claims with extensive experiments on sentiment analysis, document classification, and semantic relatedness tasks, demonstrating that Doc2VecC matches or surpasses state-of-the-art methods like Paragraph Vectors and Skip-thought Vectors. Furthermore, the paper shows that the word embeddings produced by Doc2VecC are of significantly higher quality than those from the standard Word2Vec model, as evaluated on the word analogy task.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novelty and Technical Soundness:** The paper proposes a simple and elegant method that effectively combines ideas from word embedding models (Word2Vec) and denoising autoencoders. The theoretical justification for the corruption mechanism as a data-dependent regularizer provides a strong technical foundation for the model's design and empirical success.
2.  **Strong Empirical Support and Efficiency:** The claims are backed by comprehensive experiments across a diverse set of tasks. The method not only achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance but also offers significant practical advantages in terms of training and inference speed over comparable models like Paragraph Vectors, making it a highly practical contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The decision to accept is based on the following strengths:

*   **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly identifies the limitations of Paragraph Vectors (scalability with corpus size, slow inference) and proposes a direct and effective solution. By representing documents as an average of word vectors, the model's complexity is decoupled from the number of documents, and inference becomes a simple, fast averaging operation.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. On the Wikipedia document classification task (Table 5), Doc2VecC demonstrates a consistent and significant performance advantage over all baselines, including Paragraph Vectors. On the IMDB sentiment analysis task (Table 1), it achieves performance on par with the state-of-the-art. The t-SNE visualizations (Figure 3) provide qualitative evidence that corroborates the quantitative results, showing better class separation for Doc2VecC.
*   **Improved Word Embeddings:** A particularly strong result is the substantial improvement in word embedding quality over Word2Vec on the word analogy task (Table 4). This shows that the benefits of the proposed learning framework are not confined to the document level but also enhance the underlying word representations themselves. This is a significant finding, as it suggests the model learns more meaningful compositional semantics.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** The analysis in Section 3.1, which frames the corruption mechanism as a regularizer, is a key strength. It provides a clear intuition for *why* the method works, moving beyond a purely empirical demonstration. The empirical validation in Table 3, showing that Doc2VecC suppresses the embeddings of common stop words, directly supports this theoretical insight.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, the method is explained clearly, and the inclusion of a link to the source code promotes reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Minor Presentation Issues:** The manuscript contains some typographical errors in table numbering. In Section 4.4, the text refers to "Table 5" for classification results, which is correct. However, in Section 4.5, the text refers to "Table 6," but the table itself is not numbered. Similarly, the text in Section 4.3 refers to a detailed breakdown in "table 5," which seems to be part of the unnumbered table that should be Table 4. Careful proofreading to correct these references would improve clarity.
*   **Ablation Study:** The paper argues that Doc2VecC improves upon Denoising Autoencoders (DAE) by including the local context prediction task. An ablation study that explicitly evaluates a version of Doc2VecC without the local context term (i.e., a pure DAE-style model within the same framework) would empirically quantify the contribution of the CBoW-style objective and strengthen this argument.
*   **Discussion on Approximation:** The theoretical analysis relies on a second-order Taylor expansion. While this is a standard technique, a brief comment on the validity of this approximation in the model's context (e.g., are higher-order terms expected to be small?) would add to the rigor of the analysis.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   In the analysis of the regularizer R(uj) in Section 3.1, the penalty on a word's embedding is proportional to its frequency but is also modulated by the prediction uncertainty term σ(1-σ). Could you elaborate on the interplay between these two factors? For instance, would a frequent but highly predictive word (e.g., "excellent" in a positive review) be penalized less than a frequent but non-discriminative word (e.g., "the")?
*   The paper states that the model is not sensitive to the corruption rate `q`, which was set to 0.9 for all experiments. Could you provide some empirical evidence to support this claim, perhaps by showing performance on one of the key tasks for a few different values of `q`?
*   In the semantic relatedness task (Section 4.5), the model is fine-tuned on the SICK dataset. Could you clarify the exact procedure for this? Specifically, which parameters are updated during this stage, and is the corruption mechanism still employed? How much does this fine-tuning step contribute to the final performance?
