

1. Summary:
The paper introduces a novel approach called the "neural statistician" that learns to compute summary statistics of datasets in an unsupervised manner. The authors extend variational autoencoders to model a hierarchical generative process over datasets, where each dataset is treated as a collection of i.i.d. samples from an underlying distribution. The key innovation is a statistic network that takes a dataset as input and outputs a vector of summary statistics (context) that specifies a generative model for that dataset. The approach enables efficient learning from new datasets for unsupervised and supervised tasks, including clustering datasets, transferring generative models, selecting representative samples, and classifying unseen classes. The paper makes a significant contribution by formalizing dataset statistics learning as a neural network problem, which has not been explored in depth before.

2. Decision:
Accept

Key reasons:
- The paper makes a novel contribution to the literature by formalizing dataset statistics learning as a neural network problem.
- The approach is theoretically sound, well-motivated, and well-placed in the literature with clear connections to existing work.

3. Supporting arguments:
The paper's theoretical foundation is strong, with clear derivations of the variational lower bound for the hierarchical generative model. The authors effectively connect their work to transfer learning, few-shot learning, and variational inference literature, positioning their contribution precisely. The model architecture is well-explained, and the authors provide pseudocode for key algorithms.

The experimental results across multiple datasets (1D synthetic, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, YouTube Faces) are convincing and demonstrate the approach's effectiveness. The 1D experiment shows the model can cluster datasets by distribution family, while the spatial MNIST experiment demonstrates its ability to handle complex spatial structures. The Omniglot results show competitive few-shot classification performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, and the YouTube Faces experiment demonstrates the model's ability to generate realistic faces from a small number of input images.

The paper's claim that the approach is "unsupervised" is well-supported, as the model learns statistics without requiring labels for the datasets. The authors also provide evidence for the approach's data efficiency, parameter efficiency, and few-shot learning capabilities. The comparison with related work is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the contribution.

4. Additional feedback:
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of how the statistic network's capacity relates to the complexity of the underlying distributions. While the authors mention that the sample mean provides significant representational power, a more quantitative analysis would strengthen this claim.
- The authors could provide more insight into the dataset size requirements for the approach to be effective. The statement that the method is "dataset hungry" is valid, but it would be helpful to have a more detailed analysis of how the number of datasets affects performance.
- The comparison with other few-shot learning approaches could be strengthened by including more detailed ablation studies on the components of the proposed model.

5. Questions for authors:
- How does the performance of the neural statistician scale with the number of datasets in the training set? Is there a minimum number of datasets required for the approach to be effective?
- In the YouTube Faces experiment, the authors mention that the model can generate faces of previously unseen people. How does the model handle cases where the input dataset has very few examples (e.g., only 1-2 frames) for a person?
- The paper mentions that the model can "select representative samples of datasets." Could the authors provide more details on the specific applications where this capability would be particularly valuable?
