The author introduces a type of variational autoencoder (VAE) that models a dataset-level latent variable. The idea is clearly motivated and well-described. In my opinion, the paper's greatest contribution lies in moving beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs and introducing more interesting structures in the deep learning community.

Notes:
- It's not clear to me why it should be called a "statistician." Summary statistics are not the only conceivable way to summarize a dataset with an approximate backward learning neural network. One could consider a maximum likelihood approach, etc. In general, it seemed that the paper could have been clearer if it had refrained from coining new terms like "statistic network" and had stuck to a more precise "approximate backward."
- The experiments are good, and I appreciate the response to my question about "one-shot generation." I still feel that the language needs clarification, especially towards the end of page 6. My understanding of Figure 5 is as follows: take an input set, calculate the approximate backward on the reference vector, then generate the samples given by the forward model from the approximate backward. I would like clarification on the following:
(A) Are the data points generated from the dependent vector z forward model or taken from the approximate backward?
(B) I agree that the samples are of high quality, but this is not a quantitative statement. The advantage of VAEs over GANs is that we have natural ways to calculate log- probabilities. By that end, "one-shot generation" is a "reasonable" way to calculate log p (x | c) (where c is the sample taken from the approximate backward) or log p (x) for a hold-out dataset. I doubt that the log-probability performance of these networks will be as impressive as a vanilla VAE without the reference latent variable. I still see no reason not to include it.
