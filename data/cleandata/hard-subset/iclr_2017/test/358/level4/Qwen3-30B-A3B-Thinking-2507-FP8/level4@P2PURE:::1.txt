

This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model wherein the lower level represents points within datasets, and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The core approach employs a "double" variational bound, with a higher-level latent variable describing entire datasets and a lower-level latent variable describing individual examples. Hierarchical modeling represents an important and under-explored area in deep learning with significant potential impact.  

Pros:  
  - The few-shot learning results appear promising, though I am not an expert in this domain.  
  - The concept of applying a "double" variational bound within a hierarchical generative framework is clearly articulated and appears broadly applicable.  

Questions:  
  - Does the training of the statistic network utilize minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples)?  
  - If not, would using minibatches yield an unbiased estimator of the full gradient (as would be obtained with all examples)? Specifically, consider a scenario where the statistic network must identify whether *any* example in a dataset possesses a specific feature to characterize the dataset (as illustrated in the right-hand graphical model of Figure 1). If trained on minibatches, the statistic network would be unable to capture this dataset-level characteristic, as any single minibatch would omit examples from the full dataset. Training the statistic network on minibatches rather than the complete dataset would thus constrain the model's expressive capacity.  

Suggestions:  
  - Exploring hierarchical forecasting applications (e.g., electricity or sales prediction) could provide a compelling and practical demonstration of this model's utility.
