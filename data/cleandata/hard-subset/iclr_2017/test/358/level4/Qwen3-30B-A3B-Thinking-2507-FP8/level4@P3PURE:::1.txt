

This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model where the lower level represents points within datasets, and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The core innovation employs a "double" variational bound, with a higher-level latent variable describing entire datasets and a lower-level latent variable describing individual examples. Hierarchical modeling represents an important and under-explored problem in deep learning literature, and I believe the proposed approach addresses this gap with significant potential impact.  

**Pros:**  
- The few-shot learning results appear promising, though I am not an expert in this domain.  
- The conceptual framework of the "double" variational bound is clearly articulated and appears broadly applicable across hierarchical generative modeling tasks.  

**Questions:**  
- During training of the statistic network, are minibatches (subsets of examples) employed?  
- If not, does training on minibatches yield an unbiased estimator of the full gradient (relative to using all examples in a dataset)? For instance, if the statistic network must identify whether *any* example in a dataset exhibits a specific feature to characterize the dataset (as suggested by the graphical model in Figure 1), minibatch training would prevent this capability. A minibatch might omit critical examples, thereby limiting the model’s ability to capture dataset-wide features. Consequently, using minibatches instead of full-dataset training appears to constrain the model’s expressive power.  

**Suggestions:**  
- Hierarchical forecasting applications (e.g., electricity demand or sales prediction) represent a promising and practical use case for this modeling framework.
