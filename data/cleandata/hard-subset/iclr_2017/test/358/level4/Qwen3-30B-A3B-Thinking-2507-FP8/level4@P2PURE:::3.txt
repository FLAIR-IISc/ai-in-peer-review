

The authors introduce a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that models dataset-level latent variables. The motivation and description are clear, and the most significant contribution lies in moving beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs to introduce more complex structures to the deep learning community.  

Comments:  
- The rationale for calling this a "statistician" is unclear. Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only viable approach for dataset summarization via neural networks (e.g., maximum likelihood could be used). The paper would be more precise if it avoided coining terms like "statistic network" and instead used "approximate posterior" consistently.  
- The experiments are well-executed, and I appreciate the response regarding "one-shot generation." However, the description at the end of page 6 requires clarification. My understanding of Figure 5 is as follows: Given an input set, the approximate posterior over the context vector is computed, and samples are generated from the forward model using draws from this posterior. I request clarification on:  
  (a) Are the data-point-dependent context vectors \(z\) generated by the forward model or drawn from the approximate posterior?  
  (b) While the generated samples are high-quality, this claim lacks quantification. A key advantage of VAEs over GANs is the ability to compute log-probabilities. To properly evaluate "one-shot generation," the paper should report \(\log p(x \mid c)\) (where \(c\) is sampled from the approximate posterior) or \(\log p(x)\) for held-out datasets. I expect that the log-probability performance of this model relative to a vanilla VAE (without the context latent variable) would be compelling, and including this metric is essential.
