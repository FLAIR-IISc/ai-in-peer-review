

The paper introduces a novel variant of variational autoencoders (VAEs) that incorporates dataset-level latent variables, advancing beyond traditional VAE structures to model richer hierarchical dependencies. The core idea is well-motivated and clearly articulated, representing the paper’s most significant contribution: extending deep generative modeling beyond simplistic graphical structures to embrace more expressive, dataset-level representations. This conceptual shift holds considerable promise for the deep learning community.

However, several points require clarification to strengthen the paper’s technical precision and reproducibility. First, the term "statistician" is misleading and should be replaced. The model learns an approximate posterior over summary statistics, but this is not uniquely tied to "statistics" (e.g., maximum likelihood approaches could also summarize datasets via neural networks). The authors should adopt standard terminology—replacing "statistic network" with "approximate posterior network" or similar—and avoid introducing unnecessary jargon. This would align the work with established VAE literature and prevent confusion about the model’s fundamental mechanism.

Regarding experiments, the description of "one-shot generation" (Section 4.2, Page 6) requires refinement. The current phrasing suggests ambiguity about the generation process. Specifically:  
(a) Are the context vectors \( \mathbf{z} \) (referred to as "data point dependent vectors" in Figure 5) sampled from the approximate posterior or directly used as inputs to the forward model? The figure caption and text imply \( \mathbf{z} \) is sampled from the posterior, but this needs explicit confirmation to avoid misinterpretation.  
(b) While Figure 5 demonstrates high-quality samples, the paper lacks quantitative validation. A key advantage of VAEs over GANs is their ability to compute log-likelihoods (\( \log p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{c}) \)), which provide a rigorous metric for generative performance. The authors should report \( \log p(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{c}) \) for held-out datasets (where \( \mathbf{c} \) is sampled from the approximate posterior) and compare it to a standard VAE without dataset-level latent variables. This would objectively demonstrate the model’s advantage and address the reviewer’s prior query about "one-shot generation" performance. The absence of such metrics weakens the empirical claims despite the qualitative appeal of the samples.

The experiments themselves are well-executed and respond thoughtfully to reviewer feedback. The ablation studies (Table 2) effectively illustrate the necessity of the dataset-level latent variable. However, the evaluation of "one-shot generation" remains superficial without log-likelihood scores. Including these would transform a qualitative observation into a compelling quantitative result, directly leveraging the VAE framework’s strengths. The authors’ hesitation to report these metrics is unexplained, as the methodology naturally supports them.

Minor suggestions to enhance clarity:  
- On Page 3, the phrase "dataset-level latent variables" could be specified as "dataset-level latent representations" to avoid confusion with per-dataset parameters.  
- The distinction between "context vector" (\( \mathbf{c} \)) and \( \mathbf{z} \) (the latent variable) should be consistently maintained; in Figure 5, \( \mathbf{z} \) appears to represent \( \mathbf{c} \), but this is not explicitly stated.  
- The claim that "the model generalizes to unseen datasets" (Page 7) should be tempered with evidence; the current results only cover the training datasets, not truly novel ones.

Overall, this work presents a valuable conceptual advance in generative modeling. With minor revisions to terminology, clearer exposition of the generation procedure, and the addition of log-likelihood metrics for quantitative validation, the paper would significantly strengthen its contribution. The core idea—modeling dataset-level structure via hierarchical VAEs—is promising and merits publication, provided the above concerns are addressed. The authors are encouraged to clarify the terminology, refine the description of Figure 5, and include log-likelihood results to fully realize the paper’s potential.
