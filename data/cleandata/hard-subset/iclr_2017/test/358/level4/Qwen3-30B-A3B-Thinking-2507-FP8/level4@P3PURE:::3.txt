

The authors introduce a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that models dataset-level latent variables. The motivation and description are clear, and the paper's most significant contribution lies in moving beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs to introduce more complex structures to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- The term "statistician" is misleading and should be revised. Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only viable approach to dataset summarization via neural networks (e.g., maximum likelihood methods are equally plausible). The paper would be clearer if it avoided coining the term "statistic network" and instead consistently used "approximate posterior" to accurately describe the model component.
- The experiments are well-executed, and I appreciate the authors' response to my query about "one-shot generation." However, the description at the end of page 6 requires clarification regarding Figure 5. My understanding is as follows: for an input set, the approximate posterior over the context vector is computed, then samples from this posterior are used as inputs to the forward model for generation. I seek clarification on:
  (a) Are the data-dependent vectors \( z \) generated by the forward model or directly sampled from the approximate posterior?
  (b) While the sample quality is high, this claim lacks quantification. The key advantage of VAEs over GANs is the availability of natural log-probability computations. To properly evaluate "one-shot generation," it would be essential to report log \( p(x \mid c) \) (where \( c \) is sampled from the approximate posterior) or log \( p(x) \) for held-out datasets. Reporting these metrics would provide a rigorous comparison to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable and would likely demonstrate significant performance gains. I see no reason not to include this analysis.
