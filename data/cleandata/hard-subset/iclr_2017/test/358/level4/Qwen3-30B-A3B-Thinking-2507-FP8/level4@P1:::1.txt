

# Review of "Neural Statistician"

This paper introduces a novel approach called the "neural statistician," which extends variational autoencoders to learn summary statistics of datasets in an unsupervised manner. The authors propose a hierarchical generative model where each dataset is represented by a context variable c, and the model learns to compute statistics that encapsulate a generative model for each dataset. This enables efficient learning from new datasets for both unsupervised and supervised tasks, with demonstrated capabilities in clustering datasets, transferring generative models, selecting representative samples, and few-shot classification.

The paper makes a compelling contribution by addressing the underexplored problem of modeling datasets as the fundamental units of learning rather than individual data points. The authors present a well-motivated extension of variational autoencoders that effectively captures the hierarchical structure of data across multiple related datasets, with clear theoretical foundations and strong empirical results across multiple domains including synthetic data, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, and YouTube Faces.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The core technical contribution of the "statistic network" lacks sufficient theoretical justification and empirical validation regarding its training procedure.
2. The paper does not adequately address a critical limitation in the approach: the model's reliance on multiple datasets for meaningful statistics, which significantly limits its applicability to real-world scenarios where only a few datasets may be available.

**Supporting arguments:**

The paper claims to solve the problem of learning summary statistics of datasets without supervision, which is a significant and valuable contribution to the field. The hierarchical generative model they propose is well-structured, with the context variable c capturing dataset-level information and the latent variables z capturing example-level information. The model's advantages—unsupervised learning, data efficiency, parameter efficiency, and few-shot learning capability—are well-articulated and seem plausible.

However, the paper fails to adequately address a critical concern about the training of the statistic network. The draft review correctly questions whether the statistic network is trained on minibatches of examples within datasets or on entire datasets. The paper states they use "minibatches consisting of a subsample of datasets," but this needs to be clarified as it's crucial for understanding the model's capabilities. If the statistic network is trained on entire datasets (as the paper suggests), then the concern about minibatches is misplaced. However, the paper's description of the training procedure is ambiguous, and the lack of clarity undermines confidence in the model's design.

More importantly, the paper does not sufficiently address the fundamental limitation that the method requires a large number of small, related datasets to be effective. The authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion ("the method is dataset hungry"), but they fail to provide empirical evidence that the model actually requires many datasets to learn useful representations. The experimental results are primarily conducted with datasets containing 5-50 samples each, but the paper doesn't investigate how performance changes with varying numbers of datasets. Without such analysis, it's impossible to assess whether the method is truly practical or if the reported results are simply artifacts of the specific dataset configurations used.

The few-shot learning results are promising but lack proper comparison to the state-of-the-art. While the paper compares to matching networks and other few-shot methods, it doesn't clearly demonstrate why their approach is superior or when it should be preferred. The authors note that their model performs worse than matching networks for 20-way tasks, yet they don't explain why their method is still valuable despite this limitation.

**Additional feedback to improve the paper:**

1. Clarify the training procedure for the statistic network: Explicitly state whether the network is trained on entire datasets (all examples) or on subsets of examples within datasets. This is critical for understanding the model's capabilities and limitations.

2. Conduct experiments to demonstrate the dataset size requirement: Show how the model's performance changes as the number of datasets varies (e.g., 10, 50, 100, 500 datasets). This will provide crucial evidence for the dataset-hungry limitation.

3. Provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art few-shot learning methods: Explain why the neural statistician approach is preferable for certain applications, even if it doesn't outperform matching networks in all cases.

4. Address the practical applicability: Discuss realistic scenarios where the "dataset-hungry" requirement is satisfied, and provide evidence that the method works well in those settings.

**Questions for the authors:**

1. The paper states that "we use minibatches consisting of a subsample of datasets," but it's unclear whether each dataset in the minibatch is fully used (all examples) or partially used (subsampled examples). Please clarify this point.

2. Can you provide evidence showing how the model's performance changes as the number of datasets used for training varies (e.g., from 10 to 500 datasets)? This would help establish the dataset size requirements more concretely.

3. The paper claims the model can handle "datasets of any size," but the experiments use fixed sizes (5-50 examples). How does the model perform when the dataset size varies significantly across different datasets in the training set?
