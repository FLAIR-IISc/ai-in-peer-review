

This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model where the lower level models individual data points within datasets, and the higher level models unordered collections of datasets. The core contribution is a "double" variational bound framework, where a higher-level latent variable characterizes entire datasets, while a lower-level latent variable describes individual examples. Hierarchical modeling represents an important and impactful research direction that remains significantly underexplored in deep learning.  

**Strengths:**  
- The few-shot learning results appear promising, though I am not a specialist in this domain.  
- The proposal of a "double" variational bound for hierarchical generative modeling is clearly articulated and seems broadly applicable across diverse tasks.  

**Key Questions:**  
1. During training of the statistic network, are minibatches (subsets of examples) employed?  
2. If minibatches are not used, does the full-batch approach yield an unbiased gradient estimator? Specifically, consider a scenario where the statistic network must identify a dataset-wide characteristic (e.g., "this dataset contains at least one outlier"), as suggested by the graphical model in Figure 1. If minibatches are used, the network would fail to learn such global features, as any single minibatch would omit examples from the full dataset. Training on minibatches thus risks severely limiting the model’s expressive capacity for capturing dataset-level patterns.  

**Suggestions for Future Work:**  
Hierarchical forecasting applications—such as electricity or sales demand prediction—could provide a compelling, practical use case for this framework, demonstrating its utility beyond classification tasks.
