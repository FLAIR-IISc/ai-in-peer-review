

The authors introduce a novel approach called the "Neural Statistician" that extends variational autoencoders to learn summary statistics of datasets in an unsupervised manner. The paper proposes a model that treats datasets as the fundamental objects of interest, rather than individual datapoints, and learns to produce summary statistics that capture generative models for each dataset. This enables efficient learning from new datasets for both unsupervised and supervised tasks, including clustering datasets, transferring generative models, selecting representative samples, and classifying unseen classes. The paper provides a thorough theoretical foundation for the approach, with experiments across multiple domains including synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, and YouTube faces.

I recommend rejection of this paper. The primary reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper uses misleading terminology that obscures the actual contribution, and (2) it fails to provide quantitative evidence for the claimed benefits of the approach, particularly for the one-shot learning results.

First, the paper's terminology is problematic. The authors coin terms like "neural statistician" and "statistic network" that are confusing and not aligned with standard statistical or machine learning terminology. A "statistic" in statistics is a function of the data (e.g., mean, variance), not a network that outputs a statistic. The paper's approach is essentially learning an approximate posterior over a context variable that summarizes the dataset, which is a standard variational inference technique. The authors' choice of terminology obscures the fact that their model is an extension of existing VAEs with a hierarchical structure, rather than a fundamentally new concept. This misleading terminology makes it difficult for readers to understand the actual contribution.

Second, the paper lacks proper quantitative evaluation for the one-shot learning claims. In Figure 5, the authors show samples generated from the model conditioned on a few examples, but they do not provide any quantitative metrics to demonstrate that these samples are better than those produced by a standard VAE. As I noted in my comments, a proper evaluation would involve reporting log p(x | c) or log p(x) for held-out datasets, and comparing these values to a baseline VAE without the context variable. Without these metrics, it's impossible to assess whether the claimed benefits of the approach are real or merely perceptual. The paper's claim that the model "can generalize even to new datasets" is unsubstantiated by quantitative evidence.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
1. Replace misleading terms like "neural statistician" and "statistic network" with more standard terminology, such as "context encoder" or "dataset summarizer."
2. Include quantitative metrics for the one-shot learning results, such as log likelihoods on held-out datasets.
3. Clarify the generation process in Figure 5: specify whether the z vectors are generated from the forward model (p(z|c)) or taken from the approximate posterior (q(z|x,c)).
4. Compare the log likelihoods of the proposed model against a baseline VAE without the context variable to demonstrate the improvement claimed in the paper.

To clarify my understanding, I would like to ask:
(a) In Figure 5, are the z vectors used for generating the samples generated from the forward model (p(z|c)) or taken from the approximate posterior (q(z|x,c))? This is important for understanding how the samples are generated.
(b) Could the authors provide log likelihood scores (log p(x) or log p(x|c)) for the one-shot generation results to quantify the quality of the generated samples compared to a baseline VAE?
