

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper introduces the "neural statistician," a novel approach to learning summary statistics of datasets in an unsupervised manner. The authors propose extending variational autoencoders to model datasets as the primary objects of interest, rather than individual data points. The core innovation is a "statistic network" that takes a dataset as input and outputs a vector of summary statistics, which can then be used to model the generative process of that dataset. The approach enables efficient learning from new datasets for both unsupervised and supervised tasks, including clustering datasets, transferring generative models, selecting representative samples, and classifying unseen classes. The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness across multiple domains, including synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, and YouTube Faces, with promising few-shot learning results.

## Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the literature on hierarchical modeling and dataset representation, with strong experimental validation of the approach.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses an important gap in machine learning: the lack of principled methods for learning representations of datasets rather than individual data points. The neural statistician framework is well-motivated by the observation that efficient learning requires understanding similarities among datasets, and the authors successfully formalize this through a hierarchical generative model.

The key strength of the paper is the elegant extension of variational autoencoders to operate on datasets, with a "double" variational bound that handles both the dataset-level context and the point-level latent variables. The statistic network's ability to learn meaningful representations without supervision is particularly impressive. The experimental results are comprehensive and well-executed, with the few-shot learning results on Omniglot being especially compelling. The model achieves competitive performance against specialized few-shot learning methods while requiring weaker supervision (only datasets, not class labels).

The paper also makes a valuable contribution to the literature by clearly articulating the relationship between their approach and transfer learning, one-shot learning, and other related work. The theoretical foundation is sound, with the hierarchical generative model clearly justified through the variational lower bound.

## Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. Clarify the training procedure for the statistic network. The paper mentions using "minibatches consisting of a subsample of datasets" but does not explicitly state whether the statistic network is trained on minibatches of examples within a dataset (which would be problematic, as the draft review correctly points out). Clarifying this would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

2. Provide more detailed analysis of how the model handles varying dataset sizes. The paper mentions "sample dropout" as a technique to handle varying dataset sizes, but the rationale and impact of this approach could be better explained.

3. Include a more thorough comparison with related work in multiple-instance learning, which shares some conceptual similarities with the authors' approach.

## Questions for Authors
1. When training the statistic network, do you use minibatches of examples within a dataset (e.g., subsampling from the dataset), or do you process entire datasets at once? If you use minibatches within datasets, how does this affect the model's ability to capture dataset-wide characteristics?

2. The paper states that "the humble sample mean already gives the statistic network a great deal of representational power." Can you elaborate on the theoretical guarantees or empirical evidence supporting this claim, particularly regarding the ability to approximate arbitrary moments of a distribution?

3. In the YouTube Faces experiment, you mention using "shared variance parameters across all datapoints" to improve training stability. How does this architectural choice affect the model's ability to capture the variability within individual datasets versus across datasets?

The neural statistician represents a significant step toward developing machine learning systems that can effectively reuse knowledge across datasets, and I believe this work will be valuable to the community. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and makes a compelling case for the importance of dataset-level representations. With the clarification of the training procedure for the statistic network, the paper would be even stronger.
