

This paper introduces a hierarchical generative framework where the lower level represents individual data points within datasets, and the higher level models unordered collections of datasets. The core approach employs a "double" variational bound, with higher-level latent variables characterizing entire datasets and lower-level latent variables describing individual examples.  

Hierarchical modeling constitutes a significant and impactful research direction that remains underexplored within deep learning literature.  

Pros:  
  - The few-shot learning results appear promising, though I lack expertise in this domain.  
  - The concept of a "double" variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is well-motivated and appears broadly applicable.  

Questions:  
  - Does training the statistic network utilize minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples)?  
  - If not, does minibatch training yield an unbiased gradient estimator for the full dataset? For instance, if the statistic network aims to identify whether *any* example in a dataset possesses a specific feature (as suggested by the graphical model in Figure 1, right panel), minibatch training would prevent learning this global dataset characteristic—since a minibatch inherently omits examples from the full dataset. This approach may thus constrain the model's expressive capacity.  

Suggestions:  
  - Exploring hierarchical forecasting applications (e.g., electricity demand or sales prediction) could provide a compelling and practical demonstration of this framework.
