The authors present a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that introduces dataset-level (context) latent variables. The idea is clearly motivated and well described. In my view, the most significant contribution is pushing beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs and bringing richer latent-variable structures to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- Terminology: I am not convinced by the use of the term “statistician.” Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only way to summarize a dataset with a neural network; a maximum likelihood approach, among others, is also conceivable. Overall, the paper would be clearer if it avoided coining new terms like “statistic network” and instead used the more precise “approximate posterior.”
- Experiments and “one-shot generation”: The experiments are nicely executed, and I appreciate the response to my question about “one-shot generation.” However, the language—particularly at the end of page 6—still needs clarification. My current understanding of Figure 5 is as follows: given an input set, you compute the approximate posterior over the context vector, then generate from the forward model using samples from that approximate posterior. I would like clarification on:
  (a) Are the data-point-specific latent variables z drawn from the forward (generative) model or from the approximate posterior?
  (b) While the samples look high quality, that assessment is qualitative. One advantage of VAEs over GANs is the ability to compute log-probabilities. A principled way to quantify “one-shot generation” performance would be to report log p(x | c) (with c sampled from the approximate posterior) or log p(x) on held-out datasets. I suspect the log-probability performance of these models, relative to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable, will be strong. I do not see a reason not to include these results.
