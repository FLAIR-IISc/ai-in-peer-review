This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model with two latent levels: a dataset-level variable that characterizes entire datasets (treated as unordered sets) and an example-level variable that describes individual points within each dataset. The central idea is a “double” variational bound, with a higher-level latent variable for datasets and a lower-level latent variable for examples.

Hierarchical modeling is an important, high-impact problem that remains under-explored in the deep learning literature.

Pros:
- The few-shot learning results appear strong, though I am not an expert in this area.
- The use of a “double” variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is clearly presented and seems broadly applicable.

Questions:
- When training the statistic network (the component that aggregates examples to produce the dataset-level representation), do you use minibatches, i.e., subsets of the examples from each dataset?
- If so, does this produce an unbiased estimator of the gradient you would obtain if you used all examples in the dataset? For instance, consider a case where the statistic network needs to detect whether any example in the dataset exhibits a particular feature, which aligns with the graphical model on the right side of Figure 1. If the statistic network is trained on minibatches, many minibatches will omit the informative examples, preventing it from learning this “any-of” characterization. More generally, training the statistic network on subsets rather than full datasets seems likely to limit the effective expressive power of the model.

Suggestions:
- Hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity demand or retail sales) could be an interesting and practical application for this type of model.
