The authors present a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that introduces dataset-level latent variables. The idea is well motivated and clearly described. In my view, the paper’s most significant contribution is pushing beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs and bringing richer model structures to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- Terminology: It is not clear why this component should be called a “statistician.” Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is only one possible way to summarize a dataset with a neural network; one could also consider maximum likelihood approaches, among others. Overall, the paper would be clearer if it avoided coining new terms such as “statistic network” and instead used the more precise “approximate posterior.”
- Experiments: The experiments are solid, and I appreciate the response to my question about “one-shot generation.” That said, the language still needs clarification, especially at the end of page 6. My reading of Figure 5 is: given an input set, compute the approximate posterior over the context vector, then generate from the forward model using samples from that approximate posterior. I would like clarification on:
  (a) For the data-point–specific vectors z, are they sampled from the forward model or taken from the approximate posterior?
  (b) While the samples appear high quality, that is a qualitative assessment. A key advantage of VAEs over GANs is the ability to compute log probabilities. A principled way to evaluate “one-shot generation” would be to report log p(x | c) (with c sampled from the approximate posterior) or log p(x) on held-out datasets. I suspect these models will show strong log-probability performance relative to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable. I see no reason not to include such results.
