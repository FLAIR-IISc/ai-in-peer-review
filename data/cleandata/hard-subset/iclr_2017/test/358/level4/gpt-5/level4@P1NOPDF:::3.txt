The authors propose a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that introduces dataset-level latent variables. The idea is well motivated and clearly presented. In my view, the paper’s primary contribution is moving beyond the relatively simple graphical-model structure of standard VAEs and bringing richer latent-variable structures to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- The term “statistician” is not well justified. Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only way to summarize a dataset with a neural network; one could also take a maximum-likelihood approach, among others. Overall, the paper would be clearer if it avoided coining new terms like “statistic network” and instead used established terminology such as “approximate posterior” for the variational distribution.
- The experiments are solid, and I appreciate the response to my question about “one-shot generation.” However, the language remains ambiguous, particularly at the end of page 6. My reading of Figure 5 is: given an input set, you infer an approximate posterior over the context vector; you then draw samples from that posterior and generate from the forward model conditioned on those samples. Please clarify the following:
  (a) For each data point, are the data-point–specific latent vectors z drawn from the generative model (conditional on the context) or taken from the per-data-point approximate posterior?
  (b) While the generated samples look high quality, that assessment is qualitative. A key advantage of VAEs over GANs is the ability to compute or estimate log probabilities. A principled evaluation of “one-shot generation” would report log p(x | c), with c sampled from the inferred posterior, or log p(x) on held-out datasets. I suspect these models would perform well relative to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable, which makes it all the more compelling to include such quantitative results.
