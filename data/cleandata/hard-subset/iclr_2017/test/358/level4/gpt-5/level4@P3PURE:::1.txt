This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model in which the lower level models points within datasets, and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The core idea is to introduce a “double” variational bound: a dataset-level latent variable captures properties of entire datasets, while an example-level latent variable captures individual instances.

Hierarchical modeling is an important and high-impact problem that remains under-explored in the deep learning literature.

Pros:
- The few-shot learning results are compelling, though I am not an expert in this area.
- The idea of using a “double” variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is clearly presented and appears broadly applicable.

Questions:
- When training the statistic network, are minibatches (i.e., subsets of the examples) used?
- If so, does training with minibatches yield an unbiased estimator of the gradient one would obtain using all examples in a dataset? For instance, suppose the statistic network needs to detect whether any example in the dataset exhibits a particular feature and use that as the dataset characterization—this seems consistent with the graphical model on the right side of Figure 1. If the statistic network is trained on minibatches, it may fail to learn such “any-of” characterizations because a given minibatch may omit the critical examples. More broadly, training the statistic network on minibatches (as opposed to all examples in the dataset) seems likely to limit the expressive power of the model.

Suggestions:
- Hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity demand or retail sales) could be an interesting and practical application domain for this type of model.
