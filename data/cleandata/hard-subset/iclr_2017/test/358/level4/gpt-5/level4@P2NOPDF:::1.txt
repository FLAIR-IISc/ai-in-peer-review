This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model in which the lower level models individual data points within datasets, and the higher level models collections of datasets treated as unordered sets. The central idea is a “double” variational bound: a dataset-level latent variable captures properties of entire datasets, while an example-level latent variable explains individual samples.

Hierarchical modeling is important and has high potential impact, yet remains under-explored in deep learning. This work is a valuable step in that direction.

Pros:
- The few-shot learning results are encouraging, though I am not a specialist in that subfield.
- The use of a double variational bound in a hierarchical generative framework is clearly presented and appears broadly applicable.

Questions and clarifications:
- When training the statistic network, do you use minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples from each dataset)?
- If minibatches are used, is the resulting gradient an unbiased estimator of the gradient computed using all examples in the dataset? Consider a case where the statistic network needs to detect whether any example in the dataset exhibits a particular feature—an interpretation consistent with the graphical model on the right side of Figure 1. If training relies on minibatches, some batches may omit the relevant examples, potentially preventing the statistic network from learning such “any-instance” characterizations. More generally, does minibatch training limit the expressive power of the statistic network relative to using the full set of examples per dataset, and how do you mitigate this (e.g., via aggregation schemes, importance weighting, or batch construction strategies)?

Suggestions:
- Hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity demand or retail sales across aggregation levels) seems like a promising and practical application for this model.
