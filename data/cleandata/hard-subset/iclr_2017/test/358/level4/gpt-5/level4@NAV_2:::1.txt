1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a hierarchical generative framework that models datasets (unordered sets of points) via a dataset-level latent “context” variable and a per-example latent variable, yielding a nested variational objective (“double” variational bound). A statistic network maps an exchangeable set of inputs to a posterior over the dataset context, enabling the model to learn dataset-level summaries in an unsupervised manner. The approach is presented as a general extension of VAEs that jointly captures within-dataset and across-dataset structure. Empirically, the method is applied to multiple tasks—dataset clustering, few-shot transfer/generation, representative subset selection, and few-shot classification—showing compelling qualitative and quantitative results. Few-shot learning performance appears strong (though I am not a specialist in that subarea), and the method is clearly explained and seems broadly applicable.

2. Decision
Accept, primarily because:
- The hierarchical “double” variational formulation is clearly motivated, well integrated with the literature on VAEs, and broadly useful for modeling sets of datasets.
- The experimental results on few-shot learning and related tasks are promising and substantiate the paper’s central claims.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: Hierarchical modeling across datasets remains relatively under-explored in deep learning, and this work addresses it head-on with a principled and general construction. The connection to transfer learning via shared priors/parameters is well motivated.
- Technical soundness: The nested variational bound and the use of an exchangeable statistic network are presented cleanly; the modeling and inference details are consistent with standard VAE methodology.
- Empirical support: Demonstrations across synthetic 1-D distributions, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces align with the intended use cases (dataset clustering, transfer/generation, representative selection, and few-shot classification). While I am not an expert in few-shot evaluation, the reported performance looks competitive and the qualitative samples are persuasive.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify the statistic network training protocol: Spell out precisely whether minibatches refer to subsampling datasets only, subsampling points within each dataset, or both, and how this interacts with the exchangeable pooling choice.
- Discuss estimator properties: If subsampling points within a dataset is used during training, analyze whether the resulting gradient estimator is unbiased relative to using the full set, and under what conditions (e.g., with mean pooling versus other pooling operators).
- Expressivity of pooling: Consider discussing pooling choices beyond the sample mean (e.g., max, log-sum-exp, learned attention) and whether certain dataset-level properties (e.g., “existence” of rare features) are representable under your current pooling.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Brief ablations on sample size per dataset, pooling operator, and the impact of “sample dropout” would strengthen the empirical story.
- Potential application: Hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity demand or retail sales across related groups) may be a practical arena where dataset-level contexts could improve data and parameter efficiency.

Questions for the authors
- When training the statistic network, do you use minibatches consisting of subsets of examples from each dataset (in addition to minibatching across datasets)? Please detail the exact batching scheme (tensor shapes, per-dataset subsampling, and any variability across experiments).
- If you do subsample points within a dataset during training, is the resulting gradient estimator unbiased with respect to the objective that would use the full dataset? More concretely, suppose the optimal statistic requires detecting whether any example in the set exhibits a certain feature (an “existence” property consistent with the graphical model on the right of Fig. 1). With mean pooling and within-dataset subsampling, such a statistic could be missed if minibatches omit those rare examples. How do you mitigate this potential loss in expressive power (e.g., via pooling choice, larger per-set sample sizes during training, or other architectural changes)?
