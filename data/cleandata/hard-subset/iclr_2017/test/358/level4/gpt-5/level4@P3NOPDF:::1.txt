Summary:
The paper proposes a hierarchical generative model in which a dataset-level latent variable captures shared structure across examples, and an example-level latent variable captures instance-specific variation. Learning proceeds via a “double” variational bound: an outer bound for the dataset-level latent and an inner bound for the example-level latent. The model treats datasets as unordered sets and aims to learn statistics that generalize across tasks. This framework is applied to few-shot learning.

Strengths:
- The paper tackles hierarchical modeling, an important and relatively under-explored area in deep learning with high potential impact.
- The use of a double variational bound is clearly presented and appears broadly applicable to meta-learning and multi-task settings.
- The few-shot learning results are promising (though I am not a domain expert), suggesting the approach can capture meaningful dataset-level structure.

Questions and concerns:
- Training of the statistic network: Do you train it using all examples from each dataset, or do you rely on minibatches/subsets? Please clarify the training protocol and how gradients are computed with respect to the dataset-level objective.
- Unbiasedness of minibatch gradients: If the statistic network is intended to summarize an entire dataset, under what conditions do minibatches provide an unbiased (or acceptably biased) estimator of the gradient one would obtain using all examples? A formal argument or citation would be very helpful.
- Expressivity under minibatching: Some dataset-level summaries are inherently “existential” (e.g., detecting whether any example exhibits a particular feature), which aligns with the graphical model shown in Figure 1 (right). If the statistic network only sees subsets, it may miss rare-but-decisive examples, limiting its ability to learn such summaries. How does your architecture and training scheme mitigate this? For instance:
  - Do you employ permutation-invariant pooling that can represent max/any-type statistics, or attention mechanisms that emphasize rare examples?
  - Do you vary the subset size or use curriculum strategies to increase coverage?
  - Have you empirically compared training with full datasets versus minibatches, or measured performance as a function of context size/subset size?
- Practical implications: What is the computational cost of using all examples per dataset during training, and how does it scale? If you rely on minibatching for efficiency, what trade-offs do you observe in bias/variance and downstream accuracy?
- Implementation details: Please clarify how permutation invariance over examples is enforced in the statistic network and whether the same network is used at training and test time when the number of examples per dataset may vary.

Suggestions:
- Provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of when minibatch-based training of the statistic network yields unbiased or low-bias gradient estimates relative to using full datasets. At minimum, include an ablation varying subset size and demonstrating the model’s ability to learn “any-example” (max-like) statistics.
- Report sensitivity analyses on:
  - Number of context examples used to form the dataset-level summary.
  - The architecture of the statistic network (e.g., mean vs. max pooling vs. attention).
  - The variance of the estimator as minibatch size changes.
- Consider discussing or demonstrating an application to hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity demand or retail sales across product/store hierarchies). This seems a natural and practical fit, and could broaden the paper’s impact.
- Expand on the scope of applicability: beyond few-shot classification, where might the double variational bound be most advantageous (e.g., structured prediction across tasks, domain adaptation)?

Clarity and presentation:
- The core idea and derivation of the double variational bound are well explained and accessible.
- Please ensure that Figure references (e.g., Figure 1, right) are clearly tied to the text where existential statistics and minibatching are discussed.
- Include more detail in the methods section on how datasets are sampled, how many examples are used to compute the dataset-level latent per training step, and whether this differs at test time.

Overall assessment:
This is a timely and well-motivated contribution to hierarchical generative modeling. The proposed double variational bound and the notion of learning dataset-level statistics are compelling, and the few-shot results are encouraging. My primary concern is the reliance on minibatches when training the statistic network and the resulting implications for unbiasedness and expressivity, especially for “any-example” type features. Addressing these questions through theory or targeted experiments would significantly strengthen the paper. If clarified, I believe the work could have substantial impact and broad applicability.
