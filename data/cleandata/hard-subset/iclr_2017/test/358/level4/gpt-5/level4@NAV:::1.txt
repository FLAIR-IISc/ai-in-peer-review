This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model in which the lower level represents points within datasets, and the upper level models unordered sets of datasets. The core idea is to employ a “double” variational bound: a higher-level latent variable captures datasets, while a lower-level latent variable accounts for individual examples.

Hierarchical modeling is an important, high-impact direction, and I believe it remains underexplored in the deep learning literature.

Pros:
  - The few-shot learning results are strong, though I am not a specialist in that area.
  - The use of a “double” variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is clearly explained and appears broadly applicable.

Questions:
  - When training the statistic network, do you use minibatches (i.e., subsets of the examples)?
  - If not, would using minibatches provide an unbiased estimate of the full-data gradient (as if all examples were used)? For instance, consider a case where the statistic network needs to detect whether any example in a dataset exhibits a particular feature and use that as the dataset’s characterization. This seems consistent with the graphical model on the right side of Figure 1. If the statistic network is trained with minibatches, it may be unable to learn this characterization because a minibatch could omit the critical examples. Using minibatches (instead of all examples in the dataset) to train the statistic network seems likely to restrict the model’s expressive power.

Suggestions:
  - Hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity or sales) could be an interesting and practical application for this type of model.
