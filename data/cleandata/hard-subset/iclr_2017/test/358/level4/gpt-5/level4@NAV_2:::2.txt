1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a method for learning from sets of examples by extending a variational autoencoder with a dataset-level latent “context” that summarizes each set. This hierarchical model learns, without supervision, to compute set-level representations that parameterize a generative model for the underlying dataset. Empirically, the approach exhibits “learning to learn” behavior, enabling the model to infer distributions from only a few examples.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) the approach is conceptually clean, unsupervised, and well-executed, and (ii) the empirical results on few-shot settings are strong given the simplicity of the method. The paper is clearly written and enjoyable to read.

3. Supporting arguments
- Relative to recent one-/few-shot learning work, the method is simpler and grounded in unsupervised representation learning rather than task-specific supervision, yet delivers competitive performance.
- The experiments are thoughtfully chosen and convincingly demonstrate the intended capabilities, particularly few-shot generalization.
- The spatial MNIST construction is an interesting and potentially useful toy benchmark to probe set-level inference.

4. Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not decision-critical) and questions for the authors
- The title/terminology feels overstated: calling the model a “neural statistician” seems broader than what is actually demonstrated (it learns to form summary statistics, rather than performing statistical analysis in a general sense).
- In spatial MNIST (Fig. 4), the input sets look quite dense; one might expect the model to identify the underlying distribution with substantially fewer points. Also, the red-highlighted points do not appear to correspond to the “representative” samples described in the text.
- Will you release code?

Questions:
- How sensitive is performance on spatial MNIST to the number of samples per set? Can you report results showing how quickly the model identifies the distribution as sample count decreases?
- In Fig. 4, could you clarify exactly how the highlighted points are selected and why they should be considered representative under your divergence-based criterion?
- Do you plan to provide code and pretrained models to facilitate reproducibility and adoption?
