Summary
The paper proposes a hierarchical generative model that treats datasets (unordered sets of datapoints) as first-class objects. It introduces a “double” variational formulation with a higher-level latent variable (context) that models dataset-level structure and a lower-level latent variable that models individual examples. The approach extends the variational autoencoder with a statistic network that maps sets to a distribution over contexts, enabling unsupervised learning of dataset-level summary statistics. The method is clearly presented and appears broadly applicable, with promising few-shot learning results.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper tackles an important, under-explored problem—learning representations of datasets—and proposes a clear, well-motivated hierarchical generative approach.
- The “double” variational bound and statistic network are presented cleanly and seem widely applicable; the empirical results for few-shot learning look strong.

Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: Learning dataset-level representations is a natural and impactful extension of representation learning, yet comparatively under-studied in deep learning. The model is well situated in the literature as a hierarchical extension of VAEs, with clear connections to transfer learning and one/few-shot learning.
- Approach and motivation: The hierarchical construction, with a dataset-level context and an example-level latent, is intuitive and aligns with the goal of modeling sets of i.i.d. samples. The statistic network respects exchangeability via pooling, which is appropriate for unordered sets. The “double” variational lower bound is a straightforward but elegant extension of standard VAE training.
- Empirical support: The few-shot learning results look good (I am not a specialist in this sub-area), and the framework seems capable of capturing dataset-level structure. The examples suggest the approach can generalize across datasets and support few-shot tasks.

Primary concern (clarity and rigor): Training the statistic network on minibatches of examples from a dataset may limit what the statistic network can learn and may affect the unbiasedness of the gradient estimate with respect to the full-dataset objective. For instance, if the statistic network needs to capture the presence of a rare feature that occurs in any example in the set (consistent with the graphical model on the right of Figure 1), minibatches that omit those rare examples prevent learning such a characterization. This could limit expressive power and raises the question of whether minibatch training yields an unbiased estimator of the gradient one would obtain using all examples in a dataset.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Clarify the training procedure for the statistic network:
  - Do you use all examples in each dataset to compute q(c|D), or do you use minibatches (subsets) of the dataset?
  - If you use minibatches, please discuss whether the resulting gradient is unbiased for the full-dataset objective, and under what assumptions. If it is biased, characterize the bias and its practical implications.
  - Provide an ablation on minibatch size within a dataset and its effect on performance, to quantify the trade-off between computational efficiency and expressive power.
- Expressivity of pooling: If the statistic network must capture “any”-type features (e.g., existence of a rare pattern), simple mean pooling can be limiting under minibatching. Consider discussing or experimenting with pooling operators that can capture such properties (e.g., max pooling or learned attention mechanisms), and report their impact.
- Practical use case: Hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity or sales forecasting with coherent aggregation across levels) could be an interesting and practical domain for this model; a brief discussion of applicability and required adaptations would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
- Statistic network training:
  - Are minibatches used at the within-dataset level when computing q(c|D)? If so, how are they formed and how many examples per set are used?
  - Does minibatch training yield an unbiased estimator of the gradient of the full-dataset objective? If not, can you provide a theoretical or empirical analysis of the bias?
  - How would the statistic network learn an “existence”-type feature (presence of any example with a property) if minibatches frequently exclude rare instances?
- Pooling and exchangeability:
  - Which pooling operator(s) did you use by default, and did you explore alternatives (e.g., max, sum, learned attention)? How sensitive are results to this choice, especially under minibatching?
- Empirical analysis:
  - Can you include an ablation showing the effect of the number of examples per dataset used during training of q(c|D) on downstream few-shot performance?
  - If computationally feasible, can you compare training with full sets versus minibatched subsets for the statistic network and report the performance gap, if any?
