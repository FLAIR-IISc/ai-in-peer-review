This paper proposes a novel method for learning to predict distributions from sets of data points, building upon the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) framework. The authors introduce a hierarchical extension of the VAE, where the top layer comprises an abstract context unit that summarizes a dataset. Experimental results demonstrate the method's ability to "learn to learn" by acquiring the capacity to learn distributions from a small number of examples.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the literature on one- or few-shot learning. The proposed method is conceptually simple and elegant, and its performance is impressive. Compared to recent papers on one-shot learning, the authors' approach is notable for its simplicity and reliance on unsupervised representation learning. The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read.

However, the title of the paper seems somewhat exaggerated in relation to the content. The proposed method does not appear to share significant connections with statistical inference, unless the term "statistician" is interpreted broadly as "someone who devises statistical models."

The experimental design is well-chosen, and the few-shot learning results appear robust given the simplicity of the method. The spatial MNIST dataset is an interesting contribution and may serve as a useful toy benchmark for future research. Nevertheless, the inputs in Figure 4 seem relatively dense, suggesting that the method should be able to recognize the distribution with fewer samples. Additionally, the red points in Figure 4 do not appear to correspond to meaningful points, as claimed in the text.

It would be beneficial for the authors to release the code accompanying this paper to facilitate further research and experimentation.
