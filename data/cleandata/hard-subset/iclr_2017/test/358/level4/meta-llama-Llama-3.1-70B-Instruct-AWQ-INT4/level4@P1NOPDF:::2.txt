This paper proposes a method for learning to make predictions from sets of data points. The approach is a hierarchical extension of the Variational Autoencoder (VAE), where the top layer comprises an abstract context unit that summarizes a dataset. The experiments demonstrate that the method can "learn to learn" by acquiring the ability to learn distributions from small numbers of examples.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the literature on one- or few-shot learning. The method is conceptually simple and elegant, and its performance is impressive. Compared to other recent papers on one-shot learning, the proposed method is simpler and relies on unsupervised representation learning. The paper is well-written and a pleasure to read.

However, the title of the paper seems overly ambitious in relation to the actual contribution. The proposed method does not appear to have much in common with statistical reasoning, unless one interprets this as "someone who thinks up statistics."

The experiments are well-designed, and the few-shot learning results seem robust given the simplicity of the method. The spatial MNIST dataset is an interesting addition and could serve as a useful toy benchmark. However, the inputs in Figure 4 appear to be quite dense; it is surprising that the method requires so many samples to recognize the distribution. Additionally, the red points in Figure 4 do not seem to correspond to meaningful points, as claimed in the text.

Finally, will the authors make the code publicly available?
