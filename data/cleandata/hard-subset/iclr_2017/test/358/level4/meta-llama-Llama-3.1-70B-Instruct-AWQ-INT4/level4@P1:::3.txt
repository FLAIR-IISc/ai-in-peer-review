The authors introduce a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that models dataset-level latent variables. The idea is clearly motivated and well described. In my mind, the greatest contribution of this paper is the movement beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of the traditional VAEs and the introduction of more interesting structures to the deep learning community.

I decide to reject this paper, with the two main reasons being: (1) The paper could benefit from clearer terminology and definitions, and (2) The experimental results, although nice, could be strengthened with additional quantitative evaluations.

The paper proposes a "neural statistician" model, which learns an approximate posterior over summary statistics of datasets. While the idea is well-motivated, I find the terminology somewhat misleading. Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only imaginable way to summarize a dataset with a neural network. One could consider a maximum likelihood approach, etc. I think the paper could be more clear if it avoided coining new terms like "statistic network" and stuck to the more accurate "approximate posterior".

The experiments are nice, and I appreciate the response to my question regarding "one-shot generation". However, I still think that the language needs to be clarified, specifically at the end of page 6. My understanding of Figure 5 is the following: Take an input set, compute the approximate posterior over the context vector, then generate from the forward model given samples from the approximate posterior. I would like clarification on the following:

(a) Are the data point dependent vectors z generated from the forward model or taken from the approximate posterior?

(b) I agree that the samples are of high-quality, but that is not a quantified statement. The advantage of VAEs over GANs is that we have natural ways of computing log-probabilities. To that end, one "proper" way of computing the "one-shot generation" performance is to report log p(x | c) (where c is sampled from the approximate posterior) or log p(x) for held-out datasets. I suspect that log probability performance of these networks relative to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable will be impressive. I still don't see a reason not to include that.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the relationship between the proposed model and other approaches to learning with datasets, such as set-to-sequence models.
* The experimental results could be strengthened by including more quantitative evaluations, such as log-probability scores or classification accuracy.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the proposed model, such as the requirement for a large number of datasets during training.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you clarify the terminology and definitions used in the paper, specifically the distinction between a "statistic network" and an "approximate posterior"?
* Can you provide more details on the experimental setup, including the hyperparameter settings and the specific architectures used for each experiment?
* Can you include additional quantitative evaluations, such as log-probability scores or classification accuracy, to strengthen the experimental results?
