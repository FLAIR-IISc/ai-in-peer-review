The authors propose a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that incorporates dataset-level latent variables, presenting a well-motivated idea. The key contribution of this paper lies in its departure from the traditional VAE's relatively simple graphical model structure and the introduction of more complex structures to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- The term "statistician" is unclear in this context. Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only method to summarize a dataset using a neural network; alternative approaches, such as maximum likelihood estimation, could also be considered. To enhance clarity, the authors may want to refrain from introducing new terms like "statistic network" and instead use the more accurate term "approximate posterior".
- The experiments are well-executed, and the authors' response to the question regarding "one shot generation" is appreciated. However, the language at the end of page 6 requires further clarification. Based on Figure 5, it appears that the process involves computing the approximate posterior over the context vector from an input set and then generating samples from the forward model using samples from the approximate posterior. To provide further insight:
(a) Could the authors clarify whether the data point-dependent vectors z are generated from the forward model or taken from the approximate posterior?
(b) Although the generated samples are of high quality, this assessment is qualitative. Given the advantage of VAEs over GANs in providing natural methods for computing log-probabilities, a more quantitative evaluation of "one shot generation" performance could be achieved by reporting log p(x | c) (where c is sampled from the approximate posterior) or log p(x) for held-out datasets. It is likely that the log probability performance of these networks relative to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable would be impressive, making it worthwhile to include this information.
