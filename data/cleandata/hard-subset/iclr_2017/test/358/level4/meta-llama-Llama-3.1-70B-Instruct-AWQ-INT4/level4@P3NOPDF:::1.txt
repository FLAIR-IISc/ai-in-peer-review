This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model that employs a novel approach to modeling complex data structures. The model consists of two levels: a lower level that focuses on individual points within datasets, and a higher level that models unordered sets of datasets. The core idea is to utilize a "double" variational bound, where a higher-level latent variable describes the datasets, and a lower-level latent variable describes individual examples within those datasets.

Hierarchical modeling is a crucial and high-impact problem that has not been extensively explored in the deep learning literature. This paper's contribution to this area is significant, and its approach has the potential to make a substantial impact.

The paper has several strengths. Firstly, the few-shot learning results presented in the paper appear promising, although a more in-depth evaluation by an expert in this area would be beneficial. Secondly, the idea of using a "double" variational bound in a hierarchical generative model is well-presented and seems widely applicable. This approach could be adapted to various domains and problems, making it a valuable contribution to the field.

However, there are some questions and concerns that need to be addressed. When training the statistic network, it is unclear whether minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples) are used. If not, using minibatches would likely result in an unbiased estimator of the full gradient, but it may limit the expressive power of the model. For instance, if the statistic network aims to identify a specific feature present in any example within the dataset, training on minibatches would prevent it from learning this characterization. This is because a given minibatch may not contain all the examples from the dataset, and therefore, the network would not be able to capture this information. To further improve the paper, the authors could consider exploring additional applications and use cases for their proposed model. One potential direction could be hierarchical forecasting, such as predicting electricity consumption or sales. This would not only demonstrate the practicality of the model but also provide a more comprehensive understanding of its capabilities and limitations.

In conclusion, this paper presents a novel hierarchical generative model that has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of deep learning. While there are some questions and concerns that need to be addressed, the paper's strengths and contributions make it a valuable addition to the literature.
