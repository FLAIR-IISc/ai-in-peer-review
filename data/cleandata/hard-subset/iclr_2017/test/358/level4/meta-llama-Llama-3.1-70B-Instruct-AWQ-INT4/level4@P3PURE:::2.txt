This paper proposes a method for learning to predict distributions from sets of data points. The approach is a hierarchical extension of the Variational Autoencoder (VAE), where the top layer comprises an abstract context unit that summarizes a dataset. The experiments demonstrate that the method can "learn to learn" by acquiring the ability to learn distributions from small numbers of examples.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the literature on one- or few-shot learning. The method is conceptually simple and elegant, and its performance appears to be satisfactory. Compared to other recent papers on one-shot learning, the proposed method is simpler and is based on unsupervised representation learning. The paper is clearly written and a pleasure to read.

However, the title of the paper seems overly grandiose relative to the actual contribution. The proposed method does not appear to have much in common with a statistician, unless the term is used loosely to refer to "someone who thinks up statistics".

The experiments are well-chosen, and the few-shot learning results seem robust given the simplicity of the method. The spatial MNIST dataset is interesting and could potentially serve as a good toy benchmark. Nevertheless, the inputs in Figure 4 appear to be quite dense; it is unclear why the method requires so many samples to recognize the distribution. Additionally, the red points in Figure 4 do not seem to correspond to meaningful points as claimed in the text.

Finally, it would be beneficial if the authors could release the code to facilitate further research and experimentation.
