This paper presents a method for learning to summarize and represent datasets, enabling efficient learning from new datasets for both unsupervised and supervised tasks. The proposed approach, called the neural statistician, is an extension of the variational autoencoder that can learn to compute statistics of datasets in an unsupervised fashion. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the method on various tasks, including clustering datasets, transferring generative models to new datasets, selecting representative samples of datasets, and classifying previously unseen classes.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons. Firstly, the approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, providing a novel and elegant solution to the problem of learning from datasets. Secondly, the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims, demonstrating the effectiveness of the method on a range of tasks and datasets.

The paper's contribution is significant, as it provides a new perspective on how to model datasets and learn from them. The proposed method is conceptually simple and easy to implement, making it a valuable addition to the literature on one- or few-shot learning. The paper is clearly written and a pleasure to read, with well-chosen experiments and solid results.

One potential criticism of the paper is that the name "neural statistician" may be overly grandiose relative to what was done. However, this is a minor point, and the paper's content and contributions are strong enough to stand on their own merit.

The experiments are well chosen, and the few-shot learning results seem pretty solid given the simplicity of the method. The spatial MNIST dataset is interesting and might make a good toy benchmark. However, the inputs in Figure 4 seem pretty dense, and it would be interesting to see how the method performs with fewer samples. Additionally, the red points in Figure 4 do not seem to correspond to meaningful points as claimed in the text.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors release the code to facilitate reproducibility and further experimentation. Additionally, it would be interesting to see how the method performs on more complex datasets and tasks, and how it compares to other state-of-the-art methods.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more details on how the statistic network is designed and implemented?
* How do you choose the number of stochastic layers and the dimensionality of the latent variables?
* Can you provide more information on the computational resources required to train the model, and how it scales to larger datasets?
* Have you explored other applications of the neural statistician, such as transfer learning or meta-learning?
* Can you provide more insights on how the method performs with imbalanced datasets or datasets with varying sample sizes?
