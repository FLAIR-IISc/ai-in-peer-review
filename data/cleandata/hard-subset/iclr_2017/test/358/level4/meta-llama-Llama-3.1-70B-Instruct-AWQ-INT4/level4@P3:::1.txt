This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model where the lower level consists of points within datasets and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The basic idea is to use a "double" variational bound where a higher level latent variable describes datasets and a lower level latent variable describes individual examples.

Hierarchical modeling is an important and high impact problem, and I think that it's under-explored in the Deep Learning literature.

The paper tackles the specific question of how to efficiently learn representations of datasets in an unsupervised manner, which is a crucial problem in machine learning. The approach is well-motivated, and the authors provide a clear explanation of how their model, called the neural statistician, can be used for various tasks such as clustering, transferring generative models, selecting representative samples, and classifying previously unseen classes.

The paper supports its claims through a series of experiments on various datasets, including synthetic 1-D distributions, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the neural statistician in learning useful representations of datasets and performing few-shot learning tasks.

Based on the results and the quality of the paper, I decide to accept this paper.

The key reasons for this choice are the originality and impact of the proposed approach, as well as the thorough evaluation of the model on various tasks and datasets.

The paper provides a clear and well-motivated explanation of the proposed approach, and the experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the model in learning useful representations of datasets.

However, I do have some questions and suggestions to help improve the paper.

When training the statistic network, are minibatches (i.e., subsets of the examples) used? If not, does using minibatches actually give you an unbiased estimator of the full gradient (if you had used all examples)? For example, what if the statistic network wants to pull out if any example from the dataset has a certain feature and treat that as the characterization. If your statistic network is trained on minibatches, it won't be able to learn this characterization, because a given minibatch will be missing some of the examples from the dataset. Using minibatches (as opposed to using all examples in the dataset) to train the statistic network seems like it would limit the expressive power of the model.

Additionally, hierarchical forecasting (electricity/sales) could be an interesting and practical use case for this type of model. It would be great to see the authors explore this application in future work.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of machine learning. With some minor revisions to address the questions and suggestions above, it has the potential to be an even more impactful and influential work.
