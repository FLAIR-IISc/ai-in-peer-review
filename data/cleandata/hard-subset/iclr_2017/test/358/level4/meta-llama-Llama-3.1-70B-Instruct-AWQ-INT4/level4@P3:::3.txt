**Summary of the Paper**

The authors propose a novel extension of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that models dataset-level latent variables, which they refer to as a "neural statistician." The key idea is to learn a representation of a dataset that can be used for various tasks, such as clustering, transferring generative models, selecting representative samples, and classifying previously unseen classes. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on several datasets, including synthetic data, MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with one key reason being the introduction of a novel and well-motivated approach to modeling dataset-level latent variables. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their ideas, and the experimental results are convincing.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the extension of the VAE to model dataset-level latent variables, which is a natural and important direction for the field. The authors provide a thorough explanation of their approach, including the use of a "statistic network" to learn an approximate posterior over summary statistics. The experimental results are impressive, demonstrating the ability of the model to learn meaningful representations of datasets and perform various tasks.

**Additional Feedback**

While the paper is well-written, there are a few areas that could be improved:

* The term "statistician" might be misleading, as it implies a specific approach to summarizing datasets. The authors could consider using more accurate and descriptive language, such as "approximate posterior" instead of "statistic network."
* The language in the paper could be clarified, particularly in the experimental section. For example, it is not entirely clear how the "one-shot generation" results were obtained, and the authors could provide more details on the procedure.
* The authors could consider including additional quantitative evaluations of their model's performance, such as log-probability metrics, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of its strengths and weaknesses.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on the procedure for obtaining the "one-shot generation" results, particularly with regards to the generation of data point-dependent vectors z?
* Would you consider including additional quantitative evaluations of your model's performance, such as log-probability metrics?
* How do you envision the "neural statistician" being used in practice, particularly in scenarios where the number of datasets is limited?
