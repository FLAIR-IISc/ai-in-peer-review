This paper proposes a method for learning to predict from sets of data points, introducing a hierarchical variant of the Variational Autoencoder (VAE). The top layer of this hierarchy consists of an abstract context unit that summarizes a dataset. The experimental results demonstrate the method's ability to "learn to learn" by acquiring the capacity to learn distributions from a small number of examples.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the literature on one- or few-shot learning. The method is conceptually simple and elegant, and it appears to perform well. Compared to recent papers on one-shot learning, the proposed method is simpler and relies on unsupervised representation learning. The paper is well-written and enjoyable to read.

However, the title of the paper seems overly ambitious in relation to the actual contribution. The proposed method does not appear to have much in common with a statistician, unless the term is used loosely to refer to "someone who thinks up statistics."

The experiments are well-designed, and the few-shot learning results appear robust given the simplicity of the method. The spatial MNIST dataset is an interesting contribution and may serve as a useful toy benchmark. Nevertheless, the inputs in Figure 4 seem surprisingly dense; it is expected that the method should be able to recognize the distribution with fewer samples. Additionally, the red points in Figure 4 do not appear to correspond to meaningful points as claimed in the text.

Will the authors consider releasing the code to facilitate further research and experimentation?
