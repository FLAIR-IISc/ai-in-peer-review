The authors introduce a variant of the variational autoencoder (VAE) that models dataset-level latent variables. The idea is clearly motivated and well described. In my opinion, the greatest contribution of this paper lies in its extension of the traditional VAE's relatively simple graphical model structure, introducing more complex and interesting structures to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- I find the term "statistician" unclear. Learning an approximate posterior over summary statistics is not the only conceivable way to summarize a dataset using a neural network; alternative approaches, such as maximum likelihood estimation, could also be considered. The paper could benefit from avoiding the introduction of new terms like "statistic network" and instead using more accurate descriptions, such as "approximate posterior".
- The experiments are well-conducted, and I appreciate the authors' response to my question regarding "one shot generation". However, I believe the language used in this context requires clarification, particularly at the end of page 6. Based on my understanding of Figure 5, the process involves the following steps: taking an input set, computing the approximate posterior over the context vector, and then generating samples from the forward model using samples from the approximate posterior. I would like clarification on two points:
  (a) Are the data point-dependent vectors z generated from the forward model or taken from the approximate posterior?
  (b) Although the samples appear to be of high quality, this assessment is subjective. A key advantage of VAEs over GANs is the ability to compute log-probabilities in a principled manner. Therefore, a more quantitative evaluation of "one shot generation" performance could be achieved by reporting log p(x | c) (where c is sampled from the approximate posterior) or log p(x) for held-out datasets. I suspect that the log probability performance of these networks, relative to a vanilla VAE without the context latent variable, will be impressive, and I see no reason not to include this analysis.
