This paper presents a method for learning to predict things from sets of data points. The method is a hierarchical version of the VAE, where the top layer consists of an abstract context unit that summarizes a dataset. Experiments show that the method is able to "learn to learn" by acquiring the ability to learn distributions from small numbers of examples.

Overall, this paper is a nice addition to the literature on one- or few-shot learning. The method is conceptually simple and elegant, and seems to perform well. Compared to other recent papers on one-shot learning, the proposed method is simpler, and is based on unsupervised representation learning. The paper is clearly written and a pleasure to read.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key reasons for this choice:**

1. The paper presents a novel and conceptually simple method for learning to predict things from sets of data points.
2. The method is based on unsupervised representation learning and performs well on few-shot learning tasks.

**Supporting arguments for the reasons:**

1. The paper's extension of the VAE to a hierarchical version with an abstract context unit is a natural and intuitive way to model datasets.
2. The experiments demonstrate the method's ability to learn distributions from small numbers of examples, which is a key aspect of few-shot learning.
3. The paper's comparison to other recent papers on one-shot learning highlights the simplicity and elegance of the proposed method.

**Additional feedback:**

1. The name of the paper is overly grandiose relative to what was done; the proposed method doesn't seem to have much in common with a statistician, unless one means by that "someone who thinks up statistics".
2. The experiments are well chosen, and the few-shot learning results seem pretty solid given the simplicity of the method.
3. The spatial MNIST dataset is interesting and might make a good toy benchmark. However, the inputs in Figure 4 seem pretty dense; shouldn't the method be able to recognize the distribution with fewer samples?
4. The red points in Figure 4 don't seem to correspond to meaningful points as was claimed in the text.
5. Will the authors release the code?

**Questions for the authors:**

1. Can you provide more details on how the abstract context unit is learned and how it summarizes a dataset?
2. How does the method perform on more complex datasets, such as those with multiple modes or non-linear relationships?
3. Can you provide more insights on why the method performs well on few-shot learning tasks, despite being based on unsupervised representation learning?
