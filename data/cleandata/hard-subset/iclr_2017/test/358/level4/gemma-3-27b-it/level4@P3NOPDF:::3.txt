## Review of “Variational Autoencoders for Dataset-Level Modeling”

This paper introduces a novel extension to variational autoencoders (VAEs) by incorporating dataset-level latent variables, effectively modeling an entire dataset with a single latent vector. The motivation for this approach is clear and compelling, addressing a limitation of traditional VAEs which primarily focus on instance-level modeling. The authors successfully move beyond the standard VAE graphical model structure, introducing a more sophisticated framework that could be valuable to the deep learning community. This expansion of architectural possibilities represents a significant contribution.

However, while the core idea is strong, certain aspects of the presentation and experimental evaluation could be improved. 

Specifically, the terminology used throughout the paper warrants reconsideration. The designation of the network learning the approximate posterior over summary statistics as a “statistician” feels unnecessarily restrictive. Learning an approximate posterior is not the sole method for summarizing a dataset with a neural network; maximum likelihood estimation and other approaches are equally viable. The paper would benefit from greater clarity if it avoided coining new terms like “statistic network” and consistently employed the more precise and universally understood term “approximate posterior.”

The experimental results are promising, and I appreciate the authors’ response to my prior question concerning “one-shot generation.” Nevertheless, the explanation surrounding this evaluation, particularly at the end of page 6, requires further clarification. My understanding of Figure 5 is that for a given input set, the approximate posterior over the context vector is computed, and samples from this posterior are then used to generate data via the forward model. To ensure complete understanding, I request clarification on the following:

(a) Are the data point-dependent latent vectors *z* generated *from* the forward model, conditioned on the context vector *c*, or are they directly sampled *from* the approximate posterior *q(c|X)*?

(b) While the generated samples presented are visually impressive, the assessment of generation quality remains subjective. A key advantage of VAEs over generative adversarial networks (GANs) is the ability to naturally compute log-probabilities. Therefore, a more rigorous evaluation of “one-shot generation” performance would involve reporting metrics such as log *p(x|c)* (where *c* is sampled from the approximate posterior) or log *p(x)* for held-out datasets. I anticipate that the log-probability performance of this model, relative to a standard VAE without the context latent variable, will demonstrate a substantial improvement. I strongly encourage the authors to include such quantitative results. Omitting these metrics weakens the quantitative support for the claims of improved generative capabilities.

In summary, this paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable extension to VAEs. The core idea of dataset-level modeling is well-motivated and the initial experiments are encouraging. Addressing the concerns regarding terminology and providing a more quantitative evaluation of generation quality, particularly through log-probability measurements, would significantly strengthen the paper and its contribution to the field.
