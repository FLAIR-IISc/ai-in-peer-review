## Review of “Hierarchical Generative Modeling for Few-Shot Learning”

This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model designed to represent data at multiple levels of abstraction. The lower level models individual data points, while the higher level models unordered sets of datasets, effectively learning a distribution over datasets themselves. The core innovation lies in the proposal of a “double” variational bound, leveraging a higher-level latent variable to describe entire datasets and a lower-level latent variable to characterize individual examples within those datasets. 

Hierarchical modeling is a crucial area, and I believe it remains comparatively under-explored within the deep learning community. The potential for impact is significant, and this work makes a valuable contribution by offering a principled approach to tackling this challenge.

**Strengths:**

*   **Promising Few-Shot Learning Results:** The experimental results in the few-shot learning domain are encouraging. While I do not possess specialized expertise in this particular application, the reported performance demonstrates the model’s potential.
*   **Novel and Well-Presented Approach:** The concept of employing a “double” variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is clearly articulated and appears broadly applicable to other problem settings. The paper presents the framework in a digestible manner, and the graphical model in Figure 1 effectively illustrates the relationships between the latent variables. 
*   **Potential for Generalization:** The framework’s focus on learning a distribution over datasets, rather than individual examples, suggests a capacity for generalization and adaptation to new, unseen datasets.

**Questions & Concerns:**

My primary concern centers on the training procedure for the statistic network, which aggregates information across the examples within a dataset. Specifically, I am interested in understanding whether training utilizes mini-batches or the entire dataset at once. If mini-batches are employed, it is unclear whether this yields an unbiased estimator of the full gradient. 

Consider a scenario where the statistic network seeks to identify a critical characteristic present in *any* example within a given dataset. This characteristic, if identified, would fundamentally define that dataset’s representation. However, if the statistic network is trained on mini-batches, it’s possible that such a characterizing example may be absent from a given batch, preventing the network from learning this crucial feature. This limitation appears to directly contradict the intent of the graphical model depicted in Figure 1, which suggests a holistic dataset representation. Utilizing mini-batches instead of the complete dataset during statistic network training may therefore constrain the expressive power of the model and its ability to capture subtle, but important, dataset-level characteristics. Could the authors elaborate on this design choice and perhaps explore the impact of full-dataset training versus mini-batch training?

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Explore Practical Applications Beyond Few-Shot Learning:** While the few-shot learning results are positive, the proposed hierarchical framework has broader potential. I suggest the authors consider showcasing the model’s capabilities in other domains. Hierarchical forecasting problems, such as those encountered in electricity demand prediction or sales forecasting, would provide a compelling and practical demonstration of the model’s utility. These applications naturally lend themselves to hierarchical representations, where higher levels capture long-term trends and lower levels model short-term fluctuations.
*   **Further Discussion of Computational Complexity:** The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of both training and inference, particularly as the dataset size increases. A comparative analysis with other hierarchical modeling approaches, focusing on computational cost, would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Performing ablation studies focusing on the impact of the "double" variational bound and the statistic network architecture would help to solidify the contribution of each component and provide a better understanding of the model’s behavior.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a novel and potentially impactful approach to hierarchical generative modeling. The “double” variational bound is a clever idea, and the initial results are promising. Addressing the questions raised regarding the statistic network training procedure and expanding the application scope would significantly strengthen the work. Despite the concerns, I believe this paper represents a valuable contribution to the field and warrants acceptance.
