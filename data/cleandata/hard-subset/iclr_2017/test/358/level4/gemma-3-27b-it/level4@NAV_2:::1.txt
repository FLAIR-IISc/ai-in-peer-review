This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model where the lower level consists of points within datasets and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The basic idea is to use a "double" variational bound where a higher-level latent variable describes datasets and a lower-level latent variable describes individual examples. 
Hierarchical modeling is an important and high-impact problem, and I think it's under-explored in the Deep Learning literature.  

I recommend **rejecting** the paper. While the proposed approach is interesting and the results demonstrate potential, concerns regarding the training methodology and clarity of experimental design prevent me from recommending acceptance at this time. Specifically, the potential bias introduced by minibatching during statistic network training raises questions about the model’s full expressive capacity.

The core argument for rejection centers on the training procedure of the statistic network. The paper describes training this network using minibatches of datasets. However, it does not fully address the implications of this choice for the learned representations. The statistic network aims to capture properties of an entire dataset, but training on minibatches may introduce a bias towards features present in those specific subsets, potentially hindering the network's ability to learn a truly representative summary of the full dataset. This concern is particularly relevant given the architecture depicted in Figure 1, which suggests the statistic network could, in principle, learn to identify critical features present in *any* example within a dataset.  Minibatching might prevent learning these globally important characteristics. 

Furthermore, the paper lacks a detailed discussion of how the choice of batch size impacts the performance and robustness of the model. Establishing the consistency of results across different batch sizes or demonstrating that the current approach mitigates the potential bias would significantly strengthen the work. Although the experimental results are promising, the validity of these results is contingent on the proper training of the statistic network.

Additional feedback, aiming to improve the paper, includes the following:

*   **Clarify Minibatching:** Explicitly state whether full datasets or minibatches are used when training the statistic network. If minibatches are used, provide a justification for this choice and discuss its potential impact on the learned representations. Quantify any observed differences in performance with varying batch sizes.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more rigorous theoretical justification for the choice of the sample mean as the pooling operation within the statistic network. While its representational power is mentioned, a deeper analysis would be beneficial.
*   **Experimentation with Pooling:** Explore alternative pooling strategies beyond the sample mean, and evaluate their impact on performance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the model, such as the skip connections and multiple stochastic layers.
*   **Practical Applications:** The suggestion of hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity or sales data) is relevant. Exploring and presenting results on such a practical application would further demonstrate the value of this work.
*   **Algorithm 4:** It is unclear how the KL divergence is computed in algorithm 4 - what is the distribution q(C|x;φ) exactly? Is it the posterior from a single forward pass through q(c|D;φ) with x treated as a single element dataset?



I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following questions:

*   Regarding the training of the statistic network: Are full datasets or minibatches used? If minibatches are used, what is the batch size and how was it chosen?
*   Have you investigated the impact of different pooling operations on the performance of the statistic network?
*   Could you elaborate on the theoretical justification for choosing the sample mean as the pooling operation? What are the limitations of this choice?
*   How does the model perform when presented with datasets of significantly varying sizes? Does the performance degrade, and if so, are there any mitigation strategies?
