This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model with two levels: the lower level models individual data points, and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The core idea is to employ a “double” variational bound, utilizing a higher-level latent variable to represent datasets and a lower-level latent variable to represent individual examples within those datasets. 

Hierarchical modeling is a significant and potentially impactful area that, in my opinion, remains relatively underexplored within the deep learning literature.

Pros:
  -The few-shot learning results appear promising, although I lack specialized expertise in this domain.
  -The concept of a “double” variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is clearly articulated and appears broadly applicable.

Questions:
  -During training of the statistic network, are minibatches used (i.e., are updates performed on subsets of the examples)?
  -If not, does the use of minibatches provide an unbiased estimate of the full gradient (as would be obtained using all examples)? Specifically, consider a scenario where the statistic network aims to identify the presence of a particular feature in *any* example from a dataset as a defining characteristic. This aligns with the graphical model depicted on the right side of Figure 1. Training the statistic network on minibatches might prevent it from learning such a characterization, as a given minibatch may not include all relevant examples. Consequently, employing minibatches – as opposed to utilizing the complete dataset – could limit the expressive capacity of the model.

Suggestions:
  -Investigating hierarchical forecasting tasks, such as those found in electricity or sales prediction, could represent an interesting and practical application of this model.
