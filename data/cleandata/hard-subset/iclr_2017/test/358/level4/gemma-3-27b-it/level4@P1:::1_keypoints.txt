- Proposes a hierarchical generative model ("neural statistician") with a double variational bound: dataset-level latents and example-level latents.  
- Hierarchical modeling is important and under-explored in deep learning.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason for rejection: experimental results are not sufficiently convincing and training of the statistic network raises concerns that limit expressiveness.  
- Paper motivation and literature positioning (transfer learning, topic models, VAEs) are clear and promising.  
- Results on synthetic 1D distributions and spatial MNIST are reasonable but not compelling.  
- Few-shot results on Omniglot and YouTube Faces are interesting but need more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art and deeper analysis of gains.  
- Primary technical concern: unclear minibatch procedure—uncertainty whether the statistic network sees all datapoints per dataset or only subsamples, which can bias gradient estimates.  
- Risk that minibatch-based training of the statistic network misses rare but important datapoints, reducing model expressiveness.  
- Paper needs more ablation studies (e.g., effect of skip connections and pooling methods).  
- Choice of sample mean pooling seems arbitrary; alternative pooling strategies (max, geometric mean, etc.) should be explored.  
- Related work discussion should deepen comparisons to multiple-instance learning (MIL).  
- Notation should be clarified and made more consistent (use subscripts for indices, superscripts for versions).  
- Questions for authors: (1) Does a minibatch include all datapoints per selected dataset or individual datapoints across datasets?  
- Questions for authors: (2) If minibatches subsample datapoints, how is gradient bias addressed and what mitigation strategies were considered?  
- Questions for authors: (3) Can you provide more detailed few-shot comparisons to SOTA with confidence intervals or standard deviations?  
- Questions for authors: (4) What is the computational cost of training (especially the statistic network) compared to other few-shot approaches?
