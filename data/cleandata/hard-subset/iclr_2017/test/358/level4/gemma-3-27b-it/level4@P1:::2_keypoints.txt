- Proposes a hierarchical VAE with a top-level context variable to learn dataset-level representations.  
- Demonstrates ability to “learn to learn” and model distributions from few examples (few-shot learning).  
- Method is conceptually simple, elegant, unsupervised, and performs well compared to recent one-shot work.  
- Paper is clearly written and pleasant to read.  
- Title “Neural Statistician” may be overstated; connection to formal statistics is tenuous unless broadly interpreted.  
- Experiments are well-chosen and show versatility across 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, and YouTube Faces.  
- Spatial MNIST is a useful toy benchmark addition.  
- Few-shot learning results are promising given the method’s simplicity.  
- Sampled representations in Figure 4 look dense; question whether fewer samples can capture the distribution.  
- Minor figure issue: red points in Figure 4 do not clearly align with text-highlighted points.  
- Asks whether the authors will release their code.  
- Suggest clarifying the statistical parallels (e.g., can the context vector be a sufficient statistic?).  
- Recommend ablation studies to quantify contributions of components like skip connections and instance pooling.  
- Request discussion of sensitivity to hyperparameters (latent dimensionality, learning rate, etc.).  
- Recommend direct comparisons to simpler few-shot baselines (e.g., nearest neighbor).  
- Suggest clarifying how performance scales with dataset/sample size and stating limitations.  
- Question: Was sample mean pooling compared to other pooling methods (max, geometric mean)?  
- Question: Why use a shared variance for observation likelihood in YouTube Faces, and were alternatives explored?  
- Question: How were hyperparameters selected for few-shot experiments and were they fixed across datasets?  
- Question: What is the computational cost of training and inference compared to other few-shot approaches?
