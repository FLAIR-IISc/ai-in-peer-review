This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model where the lower level consists of points within datasets and the higher level models unordered sets of datasets. The basic idea is to use a “double” variational bound where a higher-level latent variable describes datasets and a lower-level latent variable describes individual examples. 
Hierarchical modeling is an important and high-impact problem, and I believe it is under-explored in the Deep Learning literature.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea, but the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advance, and I have concerns regarding the training procedure of the statistic network that limit its potential expressiveness.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of learning representations of datasets – “neural statistician” – is promising. The paper clearly articulates the motivation and positions the work within the relevant literature (transfer learning, topic models, variational autoencoders). The results on the synthetic 1D distributions and spatial MNIST are reasonable demonstrations of the model's ability to learn representations, but they are not particularly compelling. The most significant results are presented for the Omniglot and YouTube Faces datasets, specifically regarding few-shot learning. While the few-shot learning results are presented, a more thorough comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods, and a more detailed analysis of the performance gains, would be necessary to justify acceptance. 

My primary concern lies in the training procedure of the statistic network. The paper states that minibatches consisting of a subsample of *datasets* are used. However, it's unclear whether the statistic network sees all datapoints *within* each dataset in a minibatch. If not, this introduces a bias in the gradient estimation. The statistic network aims to summarize the entire dataset, but if trained on minibatches of datapoints within each dataset, it may fail to capture characteristics that depend on the entire dataset’s composition. For example, if a single datapoint in a dataset possesses a rare feature crucial for characterization, the statistic network may miss it if that datapoint is not present in the minibatch. This limitation on the expressive power of the model is a significant issue.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The paper would benefit from more detailed ablation studies. For example, the effect of the skip connections (Section 3.3) and the different pooling methods in the statistic network (Section 3.4) could be investigated more thoroughly.
*   The choice of the sample mean as the pooling method in the statistic network seems somewhat arbitrary. Exploring other pooling strategies (e.g., max pooling, geometric mean) and discussing their potential impact would strengthen the paper.
*   In the related work section, the connections to multiple-instance learning (MIL) could be explored in more detail. Although the authors mention MIL, a deeper discussion of how this work differs from existing MIL approaches would be valuable.
*   The notation could be slightly improved for clarity. For example, consistently using subscripts for indices and superscripts for versions would aid readability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  During training of the statistic network, does a minibatch contain all data points within each selected dataset, or is the minibatch constructed from individual data points across multiple datasets?
2.  If minibatches of individual data points within datasets are used, how do the authors address the potential bias introduced in the gradient estimation? Have they considered any strategies to mitigate this bias, such as using a larger batch size or incorporating a mechanism to ensure that all data points are considered over time?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the few-shot learning performance to state-of-the-art methods, including confidence intervals or standard deviations to indicate the robustness of the results?
4.  What is the computational cost of training the model, particularly the statistic network, compared to other few-shot learning approaches?
