This paper presents a novel approach to learning distributions from limited data, framed as a hierarchical Variational Autoencoder (VAE). The core idea is to learn an abstract, dataset-level context representation that summarizes the information contained within a set of data points, enabling efficient learning with few examples – effectively “learning to learn.” The experiments demonstrate promising results in few-shot learning scenarios, showcasing the method’s ability to generalize from small datasets.

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the growing literature on one- and few-shot learning. The proposed method is conceptually clear, elegant in its simplicity, and demonstrably effective. Compared to recent work in this area, it offers a compelling advantage through its reliance on unsupervised representation learning, avoiding the complexities often associated with meta-learning or explicit metric learning approaches. The paper is exceptionally well-written and a pleasure to read.

However, the title feels somewhat overstated. While the work demonstrates an ability to quickly adapt to new distributions, the connection to the broader concept of a “statistician” feels tenuous, bordering on metaphorical. It evokes a practitioner of statistical inference, whereas the method focuses more on representation learning for rapid distribution estimation.

The experimental evaluation is well-designed and appropriate for the claims made. The few-shot learning results are particularly encouraging, given the method’s relative simplicity. The introduction of the spatial MNIST dataset as a benchmark is a valuable contribution, offering a visually intuitive and potentially useful evaluation setting. 

I do have a couple of specific observations regarding the experiments. The density of samples used in Figure 4 to represent the learned distributions appears rather high. A key benefit of methods designed for few-shot learning should be the ability to accurately represent distributions with *truly* limited data. It would be beneficial to see results demonstrating performance with even sparser sample sets. Additionally, the correspondence between the red points in Figure 4 and the “meaningful points” referenced in the text is unclear. Clarification or a revision of the figure's caption would be helpful.

Finally, the potential impact of this work would be significantly increased by the public release of the code. Making the implementation available would facilitate reproducibility and encourage further research in this promising direction. I strongly encourage the authors to consider doing so.
