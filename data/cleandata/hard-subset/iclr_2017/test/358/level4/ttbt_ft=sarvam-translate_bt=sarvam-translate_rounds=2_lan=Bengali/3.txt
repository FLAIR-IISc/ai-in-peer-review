The authors have introduced a different type of variational autoencoder (VAE) that models latent variables at the dataset level. The concept is clearly inspired and well-described. In my opinion, the biggest contribution of this paper is moving away from the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs and introducing a more appealing structure in the deep learning community.

Comments:

- It's not clear to me why it should be called "statisticsian." A heuristic backward learning with a neural network on concise statistics is not the only conceivable way to summarize a dataset. One could consider a maximum likelihood method. Generally, it seemed that the paper could have been clearer if it had avoided creating new terms like "statistics network" and emphasized more accurate "heuristic backward learning."
- I appreciate the tests, and the response to my question regarding "one-shot generation." I still think clarity of language is needed, especially at the end of page 6. My understanding of Figure 5 is as follows: take an input set, compute the heuristic backward on the reference vector, then generate a sample from the forward model given the heuristic backward. I want clarity on the following:
(a) Are the data points generated from the forward model dependent vectors z or taken from the heuristic backward?
(b) I agree that the samples are of high quality, but this is not a quantitative statement. The advantage of VAEs over GANs is that we have a natural way to calculate log-probability. To that end, a "correct" way to calculate "one-shot generation" performance is log p (x | c) (where c is the sample taken from the heuristic backward) or log p (x) reported for the hold-out dataset. I doubt that the log-probability performance of these networks will be as impressive as vanilla VAEs without the reference latent variable. I still see no reason not to include it.
