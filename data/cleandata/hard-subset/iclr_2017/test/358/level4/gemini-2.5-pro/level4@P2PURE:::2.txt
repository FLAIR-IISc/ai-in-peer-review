This paper introduces a method for learning from sets of data points, framed as a hierarchical Variational Autoencoder (VAE). The model's key component is a top-level latent variable, an "abstract context unit," that captures a summary of an entire dataset. The experiments demonstrate that this architecture facilitates "learning to learn," enabling the model to infer distributions from a small number of examples.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the literature on one- and few-shot learning. The proposed method is conceptually simple, elegant, and appears to perform well. A key advantage over other recent one-shot learning approaches is the model's relative simplicity and its foundation in unsupervised representation learning. The manuscript is clearly written and a pleasure to read.

The title of the paper seems overly ambitious relative to the contributions. The connection to the role of a "statistician" is tenuous; the model's function seems more aligned with defining or inferring a statistic rather than emulating the complex reasoning of a statistician.

The experiments are well-chosen, and the few-shot learning results are solid, especially given the method's simplicity. The proposed spatial MNIST dataset is an interesting contribution that could serve as a useful toy benchmark. However, a question regarding the results on this dataset: the input samples shown in Figure 4 appear quite dense. Is it expected that the model requires this many samples to recognize the distribution, or could it succeed with fewer? A minor point regarding Figure 4: the text claims the red points correspond to meaningful locations, but their placement in the figure does not seem to support this.

Finally, do the authors intend to release the code for this work?
