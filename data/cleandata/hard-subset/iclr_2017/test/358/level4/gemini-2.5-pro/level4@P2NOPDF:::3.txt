This paper introduces a variant of the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) that incorporates dataset-level latent variables, allowing the model to capture shared structure within a collection of data points. The core idea is clearly motivated, well-described, and represents a valuable contribution. A key strength of this work is its extension of the standard VAE graphical model, introducing a more complex and interesting hierarchical structure that is highly relevant to the deep learning community.

**Comments:**

While the proposed model is compelling, the clarity of the presentation could be improved by adhering more closely to established terminology. The introduction of new terms like "statistician" and "statistic network" may be unnecessary. The function of this network is to learn an approximate posterior distribution over a set of summary statistics (the context vector `c`). This is just one of several possible methods for summarizing a dataset with a neural network; for instance, one could also envision a maximum likelihood approach. The paper would be more precise and easier to situate within existing literature if it described this component using standard VAE terminology, such as an "inference network for the approximate posterior" of the global latent variables.

The experimental results are promising, and the authors' clarification regarding the "one-shot generation" task is appreciated. However, the description of this experiment, particularly at the end of page 6, could be more explicit. As I understand it, the procedure for generating the samples in Figure 5 is as follows: first, an input set is used to compute the approximate posterior over the context vector `c`; second, samples are generated from the forward model conditioned on samples of `c` drawn from this approximate posterior. To fully understand this process, further clarification is needed on one point: are the per-instance latent vectors `z` also sampled from their approximate posterior `q(z|x,c)`, or are they drawn from the prior `p(z)`? This is a critical detail for interpreting the generation results.

Furthermore, while the generated samples appear to be of high quality, this assessment is currently qualitative. A major advantage of the VAE framework over alternatives like Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) is the ability to perform quantitative evaluation using log-probabilities. The claims regarding "one-shot generation" would be substantially strengthened by a quantitative analysis. A robust evaluation would involve reporting the log-likelihood of held-out data. Specifically, the authors could report `log p(x | c)` on a test set, where `c` is inferred from a separate context set from the same underlying data distribution. Alternatively, one could compute the marginal log-likelihood `log p(x)` for entire held-out datasets. This form of evaluation would provide a principled way to measure the model's ability to generalize from a small context set. I suspect that a comparison against a standard VAE (without the global context variable `c`) on this metric would demonstrate a significant performance improvement and provide compelling evidence for the benefits of the proposed model. Including such an analysis seems straightforward and would make the paper's contributions more concrete.
