My apologies for the late submission of this review; technical issues with the OpenReview platform prevented me from posting it sooner.

This paper presents a method for learning from sets of data points, framed as a hierarchical Variational Autoencoder (VAE). In this model, the top layer consists of an abstract context unit that summarizes a dataset. Experiments demonstrate that the method can "learn to learn" by acquiring the ability to infer distributions from a small number of examples.

Overall, this paper is a valuable contribution to the literature on one- and few-shot learning. The proposed method is conceptually simple and elegant, and it appears to perform well. Compared to other recent approaches in one-shot learning, this method is notable for its relative simplicity and its foundation in unsupervised representation learning. The paper is clearly written and was a pleasure to review.

The title of the paper seems overly grandiose relative to the method's actual capabilities. The proposed model does not appear to have much in common with a human statistician, unless the term is interpreted loosely as "an entity that devises statistics."

The experiments are well-chosen, and the few-shot learning results are solid, particularly given the method's simplicity. The spatial MNIST dataset is an interesting contribution and could serve as a useful toy benchmark for future work. A question arises from Figure 4: the input samples appear quite dense. Given the apparent simplicity of the underlying distribution, one might expect the model to recognize it with fewer samples. Could the authors comment on this? Additionally, a minor point regarding Figure 4: the red points do not seem to correspond to the "meaningful points" as claimed in the text.

Will the authors be releasing the code for this work?
