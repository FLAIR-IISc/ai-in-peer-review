This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model with two levels of stochasticity. The higher level models a distribution over unordered sets of data (i.e., datasets), while the lower level models the distribution of individual data points within a given dataset. The core technical contribution is a "double" variational bound, which introduces a higher-level latent variable to represent dataset properties and a lower-level latent variable for individual examples.

The problem of hierarchical modeling is important and has high potential impact, yet it remains relatively under-explored within the Deep Learning literature. This work presents a novel and principled approach to this problem.

**Strengths:**
- The model demonstrates promising results on few-shot learning tasks, although I must note that this is not my primary area of expertise.
- The proposed "double" variational bound is a well-presented and seemingly generalizable technique for hierarchical generative models.

**Questions and Concerns:**
My main concern relates to the training of the statistic network. It is unclear whether minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples from a dataset) are used during this training phase. If they are, this raises a question about whether this provides an unbiased estimator of the gradient with respect to the full dataset.

For instance, consider a scenario where the statistic network needs to characterize a dataset by detecting the presence of a specific feature within *any* of its examples. This type of global characterization seems consistent with the graphical model presented on the right side of Figure 1. However, if the statistic network is trained on minibatches, it may fail to learn such characterizations, as any given minibatch is likely to be missing the specific examples required to compute the statistic. Consequently, using minibatches to train the statistic network, as opposed to using all examples in the dataset, appears to fundamentally limit the expressive power of the model. I would appreciate clarification from the authors on this point.

**Suggestions:**
- The authors might consider exploring hierarchical forecasting (e.g., for electricity or sales data) as another interesting and practical application for this type of model.
