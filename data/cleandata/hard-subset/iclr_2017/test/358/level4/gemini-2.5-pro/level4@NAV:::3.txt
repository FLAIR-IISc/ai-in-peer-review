This work presents an extension of the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework designed to incorporate and model latent variables at the dataset level. The motivation for this approach is compelling, and the methodology is lucidly explained. In my view, the paper's most significant contribution is its departure from the conventional, simpler graphical model of VAEs, introducing a more complex and expressive structure that could be of great interest to the deep learning community.

Comments:
- The choice of the term "statistician" is somewhat questionable. While the network learns an approximate posterior over summary statistics, this is not the sole method for summarizing a dataset; for instance, a maximum likelihood approach could also be employed. The paper's clarity might be improved by adhering to established terminology, such as "approximate posterior," rather than introducing new terms like "statistic network," which may be overly specific.

- The experimental results are well-executed, and I thank the authors for their clarification on the "one-shot generation" aspect. However, the description, particularly at the conclusion of page 6, could benefit from further refinement for clarity. My interpretation of the procedure for Figure 5 is that an input set is used to infer the approximate posterior of the context vector, and samples are then drawn from this posterior and passed to the generative model to produce outputs. I request clarification on the following points:
(a) Are the instance-specific latent vectors, z, sampled from the generative model's prior or inferred via the approximate posterior during this generation process?
(b) While the generated samples appear to be of high fidelity, this observation is qualitative. A key strength of VAEs over generative adversarial networks (GANs) is the ability to perform tractable likelihood estimation. A more rigorous evaluation of the "one-shot generation" capability would involve reporting quantitative metrics such as the log-likelihood of held-out data conditioned on an inferred context vector (log p(x|c)) or the marginal log-likelihood (log p(x)). I anticipate that a comparison of these log-likelihoods against a baseline VAE that lacks the dataset-level context variable would strongly support the authors' claims. Given this, the absence of such a quantitative analysis is notable.
