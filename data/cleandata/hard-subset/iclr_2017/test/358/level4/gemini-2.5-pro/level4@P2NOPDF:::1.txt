### Review

This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model that operates on two levels: a lower level that models individual data points within a dataset, and a higher level that models the distribution over unordered sets of these datasets. The central idea is the application of a nested or "double" variational bound, which involves a high-level latent variable to represent an entire dataset and low-level latent variables for individual examples within it. The work addresses the important and relatively under-explored problem of hierarchical modeling within the deep learning paradigm.

### Strengths

*   The proposed framework, centered on a nested variational bound for hierarchical structures, is presented clearly. The concept is elegant and appears to be broadly applicable to other problems involving nested or grouped data.
*   The empirical results on few-shot learning tasks appear strong and competitive. While this is not my primary area of expertise, the reported performance gains are notable.

### Questions and Points for Clarification

My main concern relates to the training of the statistic network and its potential limitations.

A key point requiring clarification is the training procedure for the statistic network. Specifically, is this network trained using minibatches of examples from a given dataset, or does it process the entire dataset at once to compute the parameters of the approximate posterior for the dataset-level latent variable?

If minibatches are used, there is a concern about whether this yields an unbiased estimate of the gradient. More importantly, the use of minibatches may fundamentally limit the expressive power of the statistic network. For instance, consider a scenario where the essential characteristic of a dataset is the presence or absence of a specific feature in *any* of its examples (e.g., an existential quantifier). A statistic network designed to capture this would need to see the entire dataset to make a correct inference. As the graphical model in Figure 1 (right) suggests, the statistic network's role is to aggregate information across the whole dataset. If trained on minibatches, the network would be unable to learn such global, set-level properties, as any given minibatch might not contain the critical example(s).

This seems to be a significant potential limitation, as it would constrain the model to learning statistics that are decomposable over examples (like means or variances) rather than more complex, holistic dataset properties. The authors' clarification on this implementation detail and its theoretical implications would be highly valuable.

### Suggestions

The proposed hierarchical structure could be highly relevant for other domains. One promising application area is hierarchical time-series forecasting, such as predicting electricity consumption or sales figures, where data is naturally grouped by region, store, or product line. Exploring such practical use cases could further demonstrate the model's utility.
