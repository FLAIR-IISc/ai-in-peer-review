This paper introduces a hierarchical generative model that operates on two levels: a higher level that models a distribution over unordered sets of data (i.e., datasets), and a lower level that models the distribution of data points within a given set. The core idea is to employ a "double" variational bound, utilizing a high-level latent variable to describe entire datasets and low-level latent variables for individual examples. Hierarchical modeling is an important and impactful problem that is relatively under-explored in the deep learning literature, and this work is a valuable contribution.

**Pros:**
- The results on few-shot learning are promising, although this is not my primary area of expertise.
- The concept of using a "double" variational bound in a hierarchical generative model is well-presented, intuitive, and seems widely applicable.

**Questions and Concerns:**
My main concern relates to the training of the statistic network.
- Are minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples from a dataset) used when training the statistic network?
- If so, it is not clear that this procedure yields an unbiased estimator of the full gradient (i.e., the gradient computed using all examples in the dataset). For example, the statistic network might need to learn a characterization based on the presence of a specific feature in *any* example within the dataset, which seems consistent with the graphical model in Figure 1 (right). A statistic network trained on minibatches would be unable to learn such a characterization, as any given minibatch may not contain the example with the relevant feature. Using minibatches to train the statistic network, as opposed to processing the entire dataset, appears to fundamentally limit the model's expressive power. I would appreciate the authors' clarification on this training detail and its implications.

**Suggestions:**
- The authors might consider hierarchical forecasting (e.g., for electricity or sales data) as another interesting and practical application for this type of model.
