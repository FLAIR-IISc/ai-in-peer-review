This paper introduces a hierarchical generative framework that operates on two levels: a lower level modeling individual data points and a higher level modeling unordered collections of datasets. The core of this approach is a "double" variational bound, which utilizes a higher-level latent variable to describe entire datasets and a lower-level latent variable for specific examples within them.
Hierarchical modeling is a significant and impactful problem that, in my view, has not received sufficient attention within the Deep Learning community.

Pros:
- The few-shot learning results appear promising, although this is not my primary area of expertise.
- The concept of employing a "double" variational bound within a hierarchical generative model is clearly articulated and seems to have broad applicability.

Questions:
- A key question concerns the training of the statistic network: is it performed using minibatches (i.e., subsets of examples from a given dataset)?
- If so, it is unclear whether this approach yields an unbiased estimate of the full gradient that would be obtained by using the entire dataset. For instance, consider a scenario where the statistic network's function is to characterize a dataset based on the presence of a specific feature in *any* of its examples, a behavior consistent with the graphical model depicted in Figure 1 (right). If the statistic network is trained on minibatches, it would be unable to learn this characterization, as any given minibatch might not contain the example with the crucial feature. This suggests that employing minibatches for the statistic network, as opposed to the full set of examples, could potentially constrain the model's expressive capacity.

Suggestions:
- The authors might consider exploring hierarchical forecasting (for domains like electricity or sales) as a compelling and practical application for this type of model.
