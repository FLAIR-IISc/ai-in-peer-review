The authors introduce a type of variational autoencoder (VAE) that models dataset-level latent variables. The idea is clearly motivated and well-described. In my opinion, the paper's greatest contribution lies in moving beyond the relatively simple graphical model structure of traditional VAEs and introducing more interesting structures in the deep learning community.

Notes:
- It's not clear to me why it should be called "statistician." A heuristic backward learning on summary statistics is not the only conceivable way to summarize the dataset with a neural network. One might consider a maximum likelihood approach, etc. In general, it seemed that the paper could have been clearer if it had avoided coining new terms like "statistic network" and had stuck to a more precise "heuristic backward."
- The experiments are good, and I appreciate the response to my question about "one-shot generation." I still feel that language needs to be clarified, especially at the end of page 6. My understanding of Figure 5 is as follows: take an input set, calculate the heuristic backward on the reference vector, then generate the given samples from the forward model from the heuristic backward. I would like clarification on the following:
(a) Are the data point dependent vectors z generated from the forward model or taken from the heuristic backward?
(b) I agree that the samples are of high quality, but this is not a quantitative statement. The advantage of VAEs over GANs is that we have natural ways to calculate log-probabilities. By that end, "one-shot generation" is a "reasonable" way to calculate the performance log p(x | c) (where c is the sample taken from the heuristic backward) or log p(x) for a hold-out dataset. I doubt that the log-probability performance of these networks will be as impressive as that of a vanilla VAE without the reference latent variable. I still see no reason not to include it.
