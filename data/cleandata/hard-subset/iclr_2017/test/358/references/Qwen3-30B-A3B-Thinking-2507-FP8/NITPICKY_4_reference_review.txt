

# Review of "Neural Statistician"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a novel "neural statistician" approach that learns summary statistics of datasets (unordered collections of vectors) using neural networks. The key contribution is a statistic network that outputs summary statistics (mean and variance specifying a Gaussian distribution in a latent context space) for a given dataset. The authors claim their approach is unsupervised, data-efficient, parameter-efficient, and capable of few-shot learning, with experiments demonstrating these properties on synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes an important contribution to the problem of learning representations of datasets, but I must recommend rejection due to a fundamental contradiction in the paper's core claim and several technical inconsistencies.

The most critical issue is the contradiction between the paper's unsupervised claim and its experimental methodology. The authors explicitly state: "we do not need labels for the datasets, nor even information on whether two datasets represent the same or different classes." However, in the OMNIGLOT experiment, they state: "We constructed datasets by splitting each class into datasets of size 5." This means they used class information to create the datasets, which directly contradicts their unsupervised claim. Similarly, in the YouTube Faces experiment, they state "The validation and test sets contain 100 unique people each," meaning they used person identity information to create the datasets. This is a form of supervision that undermines the paper's main claim of being unsupervised.

This contradiction is severe enough to warrant rejection, as the unsupervised nature is central to the paper's contribution. The paper's claim of "we only ask at training time that we are given datasets" is misleading because the datasets themselves are constructed using class/identity information.

In addition to this major flaw, I identified several technical inconsistencies that would confuse readers and undermine the paper's credibility:

1. Multiple equation typos: In Equation (3), "p(z|c; θ)" should be "p(z|c; θ)" (repeated variable). In Equation (4), "x∈d" should be "x∈D". In Equation (5), "L−1∏ i=1" should be "∏_{i=1}^{L-1}". In Equation (10), "L−1∑ i=1 DKL" should be "∑_{i=1}^{L-1} DKL".

2. Inconsistent notation: The paper uses "c" for both the context variable and the number of datasets (e.g., "c for the context" vs. "c ∈ R^l" where c is a context vector, and "c" as a scalar in "c ∈ R^l").

3. Missing methodological details: The paper states "we always use a batch size of 16" without justifying this choice, which is important for reproducibility. Similarly, the choice of "set size of 5 frames" for YouTube Faces isn't explained.

4. Ambiguous experimental details: The OMNIGLOT experiment mentions "We sampled a binarization of each image for each epoch" without explaining what this means or how it was implemented.

These technical inconsistencies, while minor individually, collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail that undermines the paper's credibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify the unsupervised claim**: The paper must either revise its unsupervised claim to acknowledge the use of class/identity information in dataset construction, or redesign the experiments to truly be unsupervised (e.g., using synthetic data with no class labels for dataset construction). The current experiments don't support the unsupervised claim.

2. **Fix technical typos**: Correct all equation typos and inconsistencies in notation to prevent reader confusion.

3. **Justify experimental choices**: Provide justification for batch size, set size, and binarization methods.

4. **Expand discussion of pooling function**: In Section 3.4, the authors claim "the humble sample mean already gives the statistic network a great deal of representational power" but don't discuss limitations of the sample mean approach or when other pooling functions might be preferable.

5. **Clarify relationship to related work**: The paper mentions "Set2Seq" as related work but doesn't explain how the neural statistician differs in terms of unsupervised vs. supervised learning.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the OMNIGLOT experiment, which explicitly constructs datasets by splitting "each class into datasets of size 5," align with your claim that "we do not need labels for the datasets, nor even information on whether two datasets represent the same or different classes"?

2. In the YouTube Faces experiment, you use person identity to create datasets ("The validation and test sets contain 100 unique people each"). How does this fit with your unsupervised claim?

3. In Section 3.4, you state "We use the sample mean for all experiments." What are the limitations of using the sample mean as the pooling function, and when might alternative pooling functions be preferable?

4. In the OMNIGLOT experiment, you mention "We sampled a binarization of each image for each epoch." Could you provide more detail on how this binarization was performed?

The paper presents an interesting idea with potential applications, but the fundamental contradiction in the unsupervised claim and the technical inconsistencies prevent me from recommending acceptance. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper.
