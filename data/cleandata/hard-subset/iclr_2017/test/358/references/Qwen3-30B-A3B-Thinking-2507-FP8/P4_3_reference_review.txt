

# Review for "Neural Statistician"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces the "Neural Statistician," a method for learning summary statistics of datasets (unordered collections of vectors) using neural networks. The approach formulates a hierarchical generative model where each dataset is assumed to be drawn from a distribution parameterized by a context variable c. The key contribution is a statistic network that takes a dataset as input and outputs a context vector c, which can then be used to generate new data points from the same distribution. The authors claim their approach is unsupervised, data-efficient, parameter-efficient, and capable of few-shot learning. The method is evaluated on synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, and YouTube Faces datasets.

## 2. Decision

Reject

Key reasons: The paper lacks sufficient novelty compared to recent work, and the experimental results do not convincingly demonstrate the claimed advantages.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Lack of Novelty:** The core idea of learning to summarize datasets using neural networks is very similar to recent work by Vinyals et al. (2016a) on "Set to Sequence" and Rezende et al. (2016) on "One-shot Generalization in Deep Generative Models." The Neural Statistician appears to be a specific instantiation of these approaches rather than a fundamentally new method. The paper acknowledges these works but fails to clearly differentiate its contribution. The statistic network described in Section 3.4 is functionally similar to the set-encoding components in Vinyals et al. (2016a), and the hierarchical generative model with context variables is directly inspired by Rezende et al. (2016).

**Insufficient Experimental Evidence:** The experimental results do not provide strong evidence for the claimed advantages. For example, in the Omniglot experiment (Section 5.3), the Neural Statistician performs worse than matching networks (Vinyals et al., 2016b) for 20-way tasks, which is a significant limitation for a method claiming to be effective for few-shot learning. The paper claims "parameter efficiency" as an advantage but provides no comparison of the number of parameters used versus other methods. The results on spatial MNIST (Section 5.2) show the model can handle complex arrangements but don't demonstrate why this is superior to existing approaches for this task.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The paper should more clearly articulate how the Neural Statistician differs from Vinyals et al. (2016a) and Rezende et al. (2016) in terms of both theoretical contribution and empirical performance.
- The claim of "parameter efficiency" needs quantitative support through direct comparisons of parameter counts with competing methods.
- The paper should provide more detailed analysis of why the model performs better for 5-way tasks but worse for 20-way tasks, and whether this limitation is inherent to the approach or can be addressed.
- The paper could benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the approach, particularly regarding the "dataset hungry" requirement mentioned in the conclusion.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does the Neural Statistician fundamentally differ from the Set2Seq approach of Vinyals et al. (2016a) in terms of the underlying model architecture and training procedure? What specific improvements does the proposed method offer?

2. Can the authors provide a quantitative comparison of the number of parameters used in the Neural Statistician versus other few-shot learning methods (e.g., matching networks, memory-augmented networks) that achieve similar performance on the Omniglot dataset?

3. The paper states that the Neural Statistician is "parameter efficient" but provides no evidence for this claim. Could the authors clarify what metric they are using to measure parameter efficiency and provide specific numbers comparing their method to alternatives?

4. In the Omniglot experiment, the Neural Statistician performs worse than matching networks for 20-way tasks. Is this a fundamental limitation of the approach, or could it be addressed through architectural modifications or additional training techniques?
