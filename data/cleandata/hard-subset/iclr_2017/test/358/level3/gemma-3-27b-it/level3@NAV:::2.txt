## Review of “Neural Statistician”

This paper introduces a hierarchical variational autoencoder (VAE) with a novel “statistic network” designed to learn representations of datasets, effectively capturing summary statistics that can be used for tasks like clustering, transfer learning, and few-shot learning. The approach is conceptually elegant and aims to move beyond point-wise modeling towards a dataset-centric view. The authors demonstrate the model's ability to learn useful representations in a variety of settings, including synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, Omniglot, and YouTube Faces. The writing is clear, and the paper is enjoyable to read.

**Reject** with a recommendation for revision. While the work is promising and the results are encouraging, the title and framing of the work as a “neural statistician” feels overly ambitious and potentially misleading. The method provides a way to *learn* statistics, but doesn’t necessarily achieve the broad scope implied by the title. The core concept is valuable, but the presentation needs to be toned down.

The strength of the paper lies in its ability to perform few-shot learning. The results on Omniglot, in particular, are solid, and the experiments are well-chosen to demonstrate the method's capabilities. The approach is also notably simpler than many recent one-shot learning methods, which is a significant benefit. The extension to YouTube Faces, while preliminary, further illustrates the potential of the approach. However, the Spatial MNIST experiment raises some concerns. The inputs appear denser than one would expect, and it's unclear if the model can effectively infer distributions from truly sparse samples. The claim that the red points in Figure 4 represent “meaningful” points isn’t fully supported by the visual evidence.

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors: 1) Reconsider the title and framing – a more modest description of the method would be more appropriate. 2) Provide more detailed analysis of the Spatial MNIST experiment, perhaps with visualizations using sparser sample distributions. 3) Clarify which points in Figure 4 are considered "meaningful" and justify this claim. 4) Include ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., the skip-connections).

Finally, will the authors be releasing the code used for these experiments? Access to the code would greatly facilitate further research and validation of the approach.
