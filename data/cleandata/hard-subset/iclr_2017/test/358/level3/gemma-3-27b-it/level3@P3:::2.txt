This paper proposes a hierarchical variational autoencoder (VAE) with a novel “statistic network” designed to summarize datasets and learn a generative model for each. The core idea is compelling: by operating on datasets rather than individual data points, the model learns to efficiently transfer knowledge between related tasks and, crucially, exhibits strong few-shot learning capabilities. The approach is conceptually simple, elegant, and avoids the complexity of many recent one-shot learning methods. The experimental results are well-chosen and demonstrate solid performance, particularly on the spatial MNIST and OMNIGLOT datasets. The writing is clear and enjoyable, making the paper a pleasure to read.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. Its contributions are significant, presenting a novel and effective approach to few-shot learning grounded in a theoretically sound framework (VAE) and demonstrating strong empirical performance. The elegance and simplicity of the proposed method, alongside its good results, make it a valuable addition to the literature.

The strength of this work lies in its elegant framing of the problem. By explicitly modeling datasets and learning summary statistics, the authors sidestep some of the challenges of traditional one-shot learning. The theoretical motivation, extending the VAE framework, is well-explained and provides a strong foundation for the method. The experimental validation, across multiple datasets (synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces), convincingly demonstrates the model's effectiveness. The results on OMNIGLOT, in particular, showcase the few-shot learning ability and compare favorably to existing approaches. The ablation studies and careful choice of baselines strengthen the claims made. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of their approach (dataset hungry, limited improvement with larger datasets at test time), which further underscores the rigor of the work. 

However, there are a few points that could be addressed to further improve the paper. The title, “Neural Statistician,” feels overly grandiose and potentially misleading. While the concept is interesting, framing the model as a complete “statistician” might set expectations too high and doesn’t fully capture the method’s reliance on a generative modeling framework. A more descriptive title might be more appropriate.

Regarding the experiments, Figure 4, showing the representative subset selection on spatial MNIST, is a little puzzling. The red points marking the “meaningful points” don't seem to align with the clusters of high-density regions visible in the point cloud. It would be helpful to clarify how these points were selected and why they are considered meaningful. Also, the density of points in the inputs to Figure 4 appears higher than described in the text—the model seems to recognize distributions with fewer samples than the stated 50. A more visually representative example would strengthen the results presented.

Finally, and this is a minor point, it would be beneficial if the authors could release the code used for the experiments. This would facilitate reproducibility and allow other researchers to build upon this promising work.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The statistic network employs a simple mean for instance pooling. Did you experiment with other pooling methods (e.g., geometric mean, max pooling) and if so, what were the results? Would different pooling methods lead to differing performance or robustness?
2.  The paper mentions that the model is “dataset hungry.” Could you quantify this requirement? Roughly, how many datasets are needed to achieve good performance, and how does performance scale with the number of datasets?
3.  In the OMNIGLOT experiments, you mention using ‘sample dropout’ and find it beneficial. Can you elaborate on the rationale behind this technique and how it prevents overfitting?
4. The title frames the method as learning “statistics.” While the context vector *can* be interpreted as a statistic, the model learns it through a generative process. How do you see the relationship between the learned context vector and traditional statistical summaries (e.g. mean, variance)?
