## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission

This paper proposes a novel neural network architecture, termed a “neural statistician,” designed to learn representations of datasets, rather than individual data points. The core idea is to extend the variational autoencoder framework to model datasets hierarchically, with a higher-level latent variable capturing dataset-level statistics and a lower-level latent variable representing individual examples within each dataset. This allows the model to learn a generative model for each dataset in an unsupervised fashion, facilitating tasks like clustering, transfer learning, and few-shot classification. The work is well-motivated, addresses an important problem in machine learning, and demonstrates promising results on several benchmark datasets.

**Decision: Accept**

My primary reason for acceptance is the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach. Learning representations of datasets is a critical challenge, and the neural statistician offers a principled and flexible framework for tackling this problem.  The extension of the VAE to this hierarchical setting is well-reasoned and clearly presented, with a solid theoretical foundation. Secondly, the empirical results, particularly the few-shot learning performance, are encouraging and demonstrate the practical utility of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of representing datasets and positions the neural statistician within the broader landscape of machine learning research. It draws connections to existing work in topic modeling, transfer learning, and multiple-instance learning, effectively highlighting the novelty of its contribution. The mathematical formulation of the model, based on a double variational bound, is rigorous and well-explained, allowing for a clear understanding of the underlying principles. 

The experimental results presented across a variety of tasks – including synthetic 1D distributions, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces – provide compelling evidence for the effectiveness of the model. While the results on YouTube Faces are presented as a "proof of concept," the generated samples demonstrate the model's ability to capture meaningful dataset-level information. The few-shot learning results on OMNIGLOT are particularly noteworthy, showing competitive performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods. 

The authors appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their work, such as the potential for dataset hunger and the challenge of bridging the gap between fast adaptation and slow training. These acknowledgements demonstrate a thoughtful and realistic assessment of the current state of the research.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is generally well-written and clearly organized, but some minor improvements could further enhance its clarity. For example, a more detailed discussion of the choice of network architectures (e.g., the number of layers, the activation functions) for each task would be beneficial. Additionally, a more thorough ablation study could help to isolate the contributions of different components of the model, such as the skip-connections and the sample dropout.

Regarding the minibatch training of the statistic network, I am curious about the impact of this approach on the ability to learn dataset-wide features. While minibatches provide computational efficiency, it is possible that they prevent the model from capturing relationships between all data points within a single dataset.  Could the authors elaborate on whether using full datasets during training (even for a subset of experiments) results in improved performance, particularly for tasks requiring a global understanding of the data?  Furthermore, how does the choice of pooling layer (specifically, the sample mean) affect the model's ability to capture different types of dataset-level statistics?  Would other pooling methods, such as the maximum or a weighted average, lead to different representations?

I agree with the authors that exploring applications beyond those presented in the paper would be a valuable direction for future work. Hierarchical forecasting, where the goal is to predict future values based on historical patterns across multiple time series, could be an interesting test case for the neural statistician.

In summary, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of unsupervised learning. The proposed neural statistician offers a novel and promising approach to learning representations of datasets, with potential applications in a wide range of domains. While some questions remain regarding the optimal training procedures and the limitations of the model, the overall quality of the work and the encouraging empirical results warrant acceptance.
