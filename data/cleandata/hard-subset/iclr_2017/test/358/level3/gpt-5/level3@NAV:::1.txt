Summary: The paper tackles the problem of modeling datasets (unordered sets of points) rather than individual datapoints by introducing a hierarchical generative model with a dataset-level latent (context c) and per-example latents (z). The core technical contribution is a “double” variational bound: an ELBO that nests a dataset-level variational term over c with standard per-example terms over z, amortized via a statistic network that is permutation-invariant over sets. The approach is clearly presented, principled, and broadly applicable. Empirically, the model (Neural Statistician) supports few-shot adaptation, clustering of datasets, dataset summarization, and classification on Omniglot, spatial MNIST, and YouTube Faces; few-shot results are strong (though I am not a specialist in that subarea).

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) A timely, well-motivated formulation of hierarchical modeling over sets, with a clean and general variational treatment. (2) Promising few-shot results and diverse qualitative demonstrations that substantiate the paper’s claims.

Supporting arguments: The paper addresses an important and under-explored problem—learning unsupervised representations of datasets—and situates it well relative to VAEs, topic models, and few-shot learning. The derivation of the double ELBO is sound and the architecture respects exchangeability. The idea is likely reusable beyond the specific instantiations here. Empirical support spans synthetic and real data; while not SOTA on 20-way few-shot, results are competitive on 5-way and compelling qualitatively. My main concern is whether training the statistic network with minibatches of examples per dataset yields an unbiased estimator of the full-data gradient, given the nonlinearity of q(c|D) and the use of sample means. This has implications for learning dataset-wide features.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper):
- Clarify training: Do you subsample examples within each dataset when forming q(c|D)? If so, is the gradient unbiased, and how does variance scale with set size? Consider control variates or stratified sampling across within-dataset heterogeneity.
- Expressiveness of pooling: Mean pooling may miss “existential” features (e.g., any example has attribute). Exploring max/log-sum-exp/attention-based pooling or learned set encoders could help.
- Provide ablations: effect of pooling choice, number of stochastic layers, skip connections, and sample-dropout.
- Calibration and uncertainty: evaluate how posterior over c narrows with more shots.
- Practical application: hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity or retail sales across related series) seems a natural fit.

Questions for authors:
1) Are minibatches used over examples within datasets for the statistic network? If yes, is the resulting gradient estimator unbiased for the full ELBO?  
2) How sensitive are results to the pooling operator and to dataset size mismatch between train and test?  
3) Can the model capture “any-instance” features, and would alternative pooling/attention address this?  
4) Please provide computational cost per dataset and scaling with set size.
