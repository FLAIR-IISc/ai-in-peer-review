Summary: This paper proposes a hierarchical VAE with a dataset-level latent “context” inferred by a statistic network that pools over sets, yielding an exchangeable, amortized posterior over dataset summaries. The model “learns to learn” in an unsupervised manner: from few examples it infers a distribution over instances and supports tasks such as few-shot generation and classification, clustering datasets, and selecting representative subsets. The approach is conceptually simple and elegant relative to many recent one-shot methods, and the paper is clearly written and enjoyable to read.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) A well-motivated, general framework for modeling sets/datasets that advances few-shot learning with an unsupervised, simple architecture; (2) Strong, well-chosen empirical results across synthetic, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces that substantiate the claims.

Supporting arguments: The problem—learning representations of datasets rather than points—is clearly posed, and the approach is well situated within transfer learning, topic models, and one-shot learning. The hierarchical VAE with a top-layer context and an exchangeable statistic network is principled and easy to extend. The experiments convincingly demonstrate: clustering by distribution family; conditional generation from small support sets; competitive few-shot classification (especially 5-way); and sensible subset selection. The methodology and variational objectives are sound, and the empirical protocol (data splits, augmentations, and comparisons to contemporary baselines) supports the claims. The work is parameter- and data-efficient in the sense articulated, though it rightly notes dataset-hungriness.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper):
- The title/“neural statistician” label feels grandiose; it risks overclaiming relative to learned summary statistics.
- In spatial MNIST, Fig. 4 inputs appear denser than expected for “few-shot”; please clarify sampling and whether the model can recognize distributions with fewer points.
- Nitpick: the red points in Fig. 4 do not seem to correspond to the “meaningful” points described—please double-check visualization.
- Provide ablations: pooling choice, c dimensionality, effect of skip connections, and calibration of q(c|D) vs set size. Include conditional log-likelihoods or held-out predictive metrics to quantify gains.
- Will you release code and pretrained models? Reproducibility would substantially increase impact.

Questions for authors:
- How does performance scale with |D| at test time when training used smaller sets (calibration of q(c|D))?
- Any analysis on sample complexity: how many points are needed to accurately recover the context on spatial MNIST and OMNIGLOT?
- Sensitivity to pooling operator and to the dimension of c?
- Can conditioning on all classes during few-shot classification narrow the 20-way gap vs matching networks?
- Computational footprint (training/inference) compared to standard VAEs and matching networks?
