This paper proposes a novel approach to learning representations of datasets, rather than individual datapoints, using a hierarchical variational autoencoder (VAE) with a top-layer context unit that summarizes a dataset. The method is conceptually simple, elegant, and unsupervised, allowing it to acquire the ability to infer distributions from small numbers of examples, a phenomenon known as few-shot learning. The paper is clearly written and enjoyable to read, making it a pleasure to follow the authors' line of reasoning.

The approach is well-motivated, drawing inspiration from the idea of "learning to learn" and the need to model datasets, rather than individual datapoints. The authors provide a thorough overview of the related work, highlighting the key differences between their approach and existing methods. The technical aspects of the paper are sound, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their model and its components.

The empirical performance of the method is good, with the authors presenting a range of experiments that demonstrate its effectiveness in various tasks, including clustering, generative modeling, and few-shot learning. The experiments are well chosen, and the results are solid, providing strong evidence for the method's capabilities. The use of the Spatial MNIST dataset as a toy benchmark is particularly useful, as it allows for a clear visualization of the method's performance.

However, I do have some minor issues with the paper. The title and name of the method, "neural statistician," are somewhat grandiose and misleading. While the method does learn to summarize datasets, it is not a statistician in the classical sense. Additionally, the Spatial MNIST dataset, while useful, may not be the most challenging benchmark, and the model's performance on this task may not generalize to more complex datasets.

I also have a minor nitpick regarding Figure 4, where the red points do not appear to correspond to the meaningful points claimed in the text. However, this is a minor issue and does not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

Overall, I believe that this paper makes a strong contribution to the field of machine learning, and I would recommend accepting it for publication. However, I do have one question for the authors: do they plan to release the code for their method, and if so, when can we expect it to be available?
