This paper proposes a hierarchical generative model for learning representations of datasets, which is a crucial but under-explored problem in deep learning. The model, referred to as a neural statistician, uses a "double" variational bound to capture the structure within datasets and across datasets. The core idea is to use a higher-level latent variable to describe datasets and a lower-level latent variable to describe individual examples within a dataset.

The paper presents a well-motivated approach, building upon the variational autoencoder (VAE) framework, and extends it to model datasets instead of individual datapoints. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on various tasks, including clustering, transfer learning, and few-shot learning.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to tackle complex datasets with internal structure, such as images, and learn meaningful representations. The few-shot learning results, in particular, look promising, and the authors provide a thorough comparison with other state-of-the-art methods.

The "double" variational bound idea is well-presented and appears widely applicable to other problems in deep learning. The authors provide a clear explanation of their approach, and the mathematical derivations are sound.

However, there are some concerns regarding the training of the statistic network. Specifically, it is unclear whether minibatches are used when training the statistic network and whether they provide an unbiased estimator of the full gradient. If minibatches are used, it is possible that they may prevent the model from learning dataset-wide features, limiting its expressiveness.

To further improve the paper, the authors could consider exploring other applications of their approach, such as hierarchical forecasting (e.g., electricity or sales). This could provide additional insights into the strengths and limitations of their method.

In conclusion, this paper presents a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, and its ideas and techniques have the potential to be widely applicable. With some minor clarifications and additional experiments, the paper could be even stronger.
