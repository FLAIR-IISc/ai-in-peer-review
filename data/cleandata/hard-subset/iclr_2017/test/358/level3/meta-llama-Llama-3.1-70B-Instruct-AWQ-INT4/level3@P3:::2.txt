This paper proposes a hierarchical VAE with a top-layer context unit that summarizes a dataset to predict from sets of points. The method effectively "learns to learn," acquiring the ability to infer distributions from small numbers of examples (few-shot learning). The approach is conceptually simple, elegant, unsupervised, and simpler than many recent one-shot methods. 

I accept this paper. The key reason for this choice is that the paper supports its claims with well-chosen experiments that demonstrate solid few-shot learning results. The authors provide a clear and enjoyable read, making it easy to follow their argument. 

The approach is well-motivated, building upon existing work in variational autoencoders and transfer learning. The authors provide a thorough discussion of related work, highlighting the key differences between their approach and others. The method is also well-placed in the literature, as it addresses the important problem of few-shot learning.

The paper's empirical performance is good, with experiments that demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. The authors evaluate their approach on several datasets, including synthetic 1-D distributions, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces. The results show that the method can learn to cluster datasets, transfer generative models to new datasets, select representative samples of datasets, and classify previously unseen classes.

One potential weakness of the paper is its overly grandiose title, which may be misleading. The authors refer to their model as a "neural statistician," which may imply a level of sophistication that is not entirely justified. However, this is a minor quibble, and the paper's content is generally well-matched to its title.

The spatial MNIST dataset is a useful toy benchmark, but the inputs in Figure 4 appear denser than expected. It is possible that the model might be expected to recognize distributions with fewer samples. Additionally, the red points in Figure 4 do not appear to correspond to the meaningful points claimed in the text. However, these are minor issues that do not detract from the overall quality of the paper.

To improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors consider releasing their code. This would allow other researchers to build upon their work and verify their results. Additionally, the authors may want to consider evaluating their approach on more challenging datasets, such as those with complex internal structure or high-dimensional data.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of few-shot learning. The authors' approach is well-motivated, well-executed, and well-evaluated, making it a valuable addition to the literature.
