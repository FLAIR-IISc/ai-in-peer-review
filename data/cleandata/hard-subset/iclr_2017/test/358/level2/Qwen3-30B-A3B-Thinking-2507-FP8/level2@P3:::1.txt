

The paper introduces a "neural statistician," a neural network framework that learns to compute summary statistics of datasets in an unsupervised manner. By modeling datasets as the primary objects rather than individual datapoints, the approach extends variational autoencoders to produce context vectors that specify generative models for each dataset. This enables efficient learning for unsupervised tasks (clustering datasets by distribution family, dataset summarization) and supervised tasks (few-shot classification, generative model transfer), with demonstrated applications across synthetic data, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces.

The paper should be accepted. The work makes a clear contribution to the problem of dataset-level representation learning, and the approach is well-motivated with strong theoretical grounding in variational inference and transfer learning. The experimental results robustly support the claims across diverse domains, demonstrating the method's versatility and effectiveness.

The key strength lies in the elegant extension of VAEs to model datasets as the primary objects, with the statistic network providing principled unsupervised statistics learning. The connection to transfer learning (parameter-transfer approach) and few-shot learning is well-justified and positioned within relevant literature. The experiments comprehensively validate the approach: synthetic data demonstrates clustering by distribution family; spatial MNIST shows handling complex spatial structures; OMNIGLOT achieves competitive few-shot classification; and YouTube Faces demonstrates generative adaptation to new individuals. The unsupervised nature of the approach—requiring only datasets as input without labels—is particularly valuable, as it avoids the need for supervision on dataset-level labels or class information.

The paper would benefit from three clarifications. First, the relationship to recent set representation methods (e.g., Vinyals et al.'s Set2Seq) should be more explicitly contrasted beyond the related work section to highlight the unique contribution of unsupervised statistics learning versus supervised set-to-sequence mapping. Second, the computational requirements for training—particularly the need for multiple datasets (a dataset-hungry approach)—should be discussed in more depth, including how this might limit applicability in low-data regimes. Third, the paper could elaborate on the trade-offs between the statistic network's simplicity (using sample mean) and potential improvements from more sophisticated pooling mechanisms.

The authors should address these points in the revision. Additionally, the following questions would help clarify the work: (1) How does the instance encoder's polynomial approximation capability (mentioned in Section 3.4) specifically enable the statistic network to approximate moments, and are there theoretical bounds on the approximation error? (2) In the YouTube Faces experiment, the shared variance parameterization in the Gaussian likelihood improves training stability—how does this choice affect the model's ability to capture facial variability compared to a per-datapoint variance?
