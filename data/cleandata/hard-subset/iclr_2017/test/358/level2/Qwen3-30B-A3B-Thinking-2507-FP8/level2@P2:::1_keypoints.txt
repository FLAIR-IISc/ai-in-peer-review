Paper introduces the "neural statistician": a VAE extension that models whole datasets via a statistic network producing context vectors.  
Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
Main contribution: Shifts focus from datapoints to datasets, offering a novel, well-motivated framework for dataset-level representation learning.  
Methodology: Sound variational extension with clear lower bounds and useful skip connections to handle complex dataset structure.  
Experimental breadth: Validated on synthetic 1D, spatial MNIST, OMNIGLOT, and YouTube Faces with ablations and thorough implementation details.  
Key results: Demonstrates dataset clustering, representative sample selection, personalized generative modeling, and competitive few-shot OMNIGLOT (82.2% 5-way 5-shot).  
Strength: Strong theoretical grounding and versatility across domains; well-placed in literature on transfer and set-based learning.  
Weakness — "unsupervised" claim: Ambiguous—few-shot experiments use labeled datasets; clarify supervision level and compare to fully unsupervised baselines.  
Weakness — parameter efficiency: Claim lacks quantitative evidence; provide parameter counts versus VAEs/matching networks.  
Weakness — dataset hunger: Not quantified; add experiments showing performance vs. number of training datasets.  
Weakness — few-shot 20-way gap: Attribution to conditioning is untested; run ablation conditioning on all classes.  
Question: How does statistic network performance change with varying dataset sizes (e.g., 5 vs. 50 samples)?  
Question: Can the context vector be interpreted as a class embedding, and why doesn’t it reach SOTA on tasks like mini-ImageNet?  
Question: Does sample mean pooling have theoretical guarantees to approximate moments, and what are its limitations?  
Overall: Strong, well-executed paper with minor addressable issues; recommend acceptance.
