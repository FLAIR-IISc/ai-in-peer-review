This paper introduces a broadly applicable modeling framework for natural language understanding tasks involving two related textual inputs – for example, a question and a supporting document. The proposed approach leverages soft attention mechanisms to establish alignments between tokens in the two input texts. These alignments, expressed as query-result pairs, are then processed by a comparison function to generate representations, which are subsequently aggregated using a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a unified vector representation used for prediction. The work demonstrates the effectiveness of this overall modeling strategy and provides a thorough empirical investigation of the comparison function’s design.

This research is particularly relevant given the current challenges in natural language understanding and the emerging potential of representation learning techniques to address them. The presented approach is both conceptually clear and practically effective, achieving strong performance on benchmark tasks. While the contribution builds upon prior work (including that of the authors and Parikh et al.), it offers enough novel insights to warrant strong recommendation for acceptance.

Specific points for consideration:

- The current model implementation, particularly for longer sequences (excluding SNLI), exhibits insensitivity to word order. While acknowledged by the authors, this limitation and its potential impact on performance could benefit from explicit discussion in the introduction or discussion sections.
- The attention mechanism appears more aligned with the general/bilinear approach described by Luong et al. (2015) than with earlier Bahdanau-style attention. Updating the citations to reflect this connection would be beneficial.
- Given the potential for overfitting due to the number of parameters in the NTN component, have you explored using a reduced-dimensionality output layer (e.g., an *llm* tensor with input dimension *l* and output dimension *m*)?
- The SubMultNN component bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy presented in the Lili Mou paper cited. This similarity should be acknowledged.
- Could you clarify the rationale for using identical preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer texts in step (1)? Different weighting schemes might be optimal for each input.
