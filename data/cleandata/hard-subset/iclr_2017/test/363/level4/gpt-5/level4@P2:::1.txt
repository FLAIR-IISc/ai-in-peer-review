Summary and contributions
- The paper presents a general compare-aggregate architecture for sequence matching, targeting tasks such as machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment.
- It focuses on the choice of word-level comparison functions within an attention-based framework and CNN aggregation. Six options are evaluated: neural network (NN), neural tensor network (NTN), Euclidean+cosine (EUCCOS), element-wise subtraction (SUB), element-wise multiplication (MULT), and SUB+MULT followed by NN (SUBMULT+NN).
- Experiments across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) show that simple element-wise operations, especially SUB and MULT (and their combination), often outperform more complex comparators. The model achieves strong or state-of-the-art results on several QA benchmarks and competitive results on SNLI.
- Ablations suggest both preprocessing gates and attention help, with attention particularly helpful when sequence lengths differ substantially.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty: The compare-aggregate paradigm and attention-based word-level matching are well-established; the main contribution is a systematic comparison of simple element-wise functions versus neural comparators.
2) Insufficient analysis/insight: While empirical gains are solid, the paper offers limited qualitative or theoretical understanding of why SUB/MULT work better, and the evaluation lacks stronger controls and significance testing.

Supporting arguments
- Prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., Wang & Jiang, He & Lin) already exemplifies compare-aggregate designs, attention-guided alignment, and distance/similarity-based matching. The architectural differences here (one-directional attention, CNN for aggregation, gating-based preprocessing) appear incremental engineering choices rather than conceptual advances.
- The central claim—that element-wise operations outperform NN/NTN—would benefit from stronger evidence: controlled comparisons where all other components (attention, aggregation, depth, parameters) are held constant; statistical significance tests; and analyses isolating where these operations help (e.g., lexical overlap vs. paraphrase vs. negation).
- Some implementation decisions limit interpretability: embeddings are not fine-tuned, and preprocessing is order-insensitive for three tasks, which may underutilize context and confound comparisons with order-sensitive baselines.
- Baseline comparisons rely on reported numbers rather than unified re-implementations under identical conditions, making cross-paper gains harder to attribute solely to the comparison function.
- On SNLI, performance is competitive but not state-of-the-art; for QA tasks, gains are stronger but may hinge on dataset characteristics (e.g., lexical overlap), reinforcing the need for deeper analysis.

Suggestions to improve the paper (not central to the accept/reject decision)
- Provide a deeper qualitative and error analysis: contrast cases where SUB/MULT shine or fail (e.g., synonymy, antonymy, compositional semantics, long-distance dependencies, negation/quantifiers).
- Add statistical significance testing for all reported gains and include learning curves and variance across runs.
- Strengthen controlled studies: reimplement a strong compare-aggregate baseline and vary only the comparison function; match parameter counts across comparators; report parameter efficiency and computational cost (FLOPs, latency, memory).
- Explore richer aggregation and attention: bidirectional or multi-hop attention, pooling alternatives, and order-sensitive preprocessing across all tasks to check whether element-wise advantages persist.
- Analyze sensitivity to hyperparameters (dropout, CNN window sizes, dimensionality) and to embedding choices (fine-tuning vs. fixed; alternative pretrained embeddings).
- Clarify where the model underperforms and how it might integrate with more recent advances (e.g., self-attention encoders, pretrained contextual embeddings), even if outside the current scope.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are the results to the attention mechanism (one-directional vs. bidirectional) and to CNN aggregation choices (window sizes, number of filters, depth)?  
2) Did you control for parameter count across comparison functions (e.g., NN/NTN vs. SUB/MULT)? If so, do the conclusions still hold?  
3) Why were embeddings not fine-tuned? Does fine-tuning change the relative ranking of comparison functions?  
4) For SUBMULT+NN, how much of the gain comes from SUB/MULT vs. the subsequent NN layer? What happens with SUB+MULT concatenation without an NN?  
5) Can you provide statistical significance tests for improvements on each dataset?  
6) On SNLI you add LSTM-based preprocessing; why not apply order-sensitive preprocessing to the QA tasks as well to test whether element-wise comparisons still dominate?  
7) Do the element-wise methods retain their advantage on sentence pairs with minimal lexical overlap (e.g., paraphrases), or where inference depends on compositional structure rather than token-level alignment?  
8) What is the runtime/memory profile across comparison functions, and how does this trade off with accuracy in practice?

Overall, the paper offers a useful empirical study and a strong, simple baseline, but the novelty and depth of analysis are not yet sufficient for acceptance at ICLR.
