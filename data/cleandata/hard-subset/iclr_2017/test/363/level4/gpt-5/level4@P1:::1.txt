Summary
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences, applicable to machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment. The model aligns each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted representation of the other, compares the paired vectors using a chosen comparison function, and aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN for final prediction. The main contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions—ranging from neural network and tensor-based layers to simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN). Across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), the authors find that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication (and especially their combination with a small NN) often outperform more complex neural comparison modules. The paper also includes ablations showing the importance of the preprocessing (gating) and attention layers.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty: The overall architecture closely follows existing compare-aggregate paradigms (e.g., decomposable attention, pairwise interaction/CNN models). The principal contribution is an empirical comparison of comparison functions, which feels incremental.
- Insufficient analysis/insight: The paper lacks qualitative and diagnostic analyses explaining when and why subtraction/multiplication succeed or fail, and provides limited theoretical motivation beyond intuition.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses an important and well-scoped problem—matching text sequences—and is appropriately positioned with respect to attention-based and compare-aggregate literature. However, the architectural choices (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation, order-insensitive preprocessing for most tasks) are only lightly justified and appear to be engineering alternatives rather than conceptual advances.
- Claims and evidence: The empirical results are strong for three QA datasets and competitive on SNLI, and the cross-task evaluation is a plus. The finding that SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) outperform standard NN/NTN is interesting and practically useful. However:
  - The study stops short of explaining why simple element-wise functions work so well (e.g., sensitivity to embedding scaling, dimensionality, normalization).
  - The ablations are limited; for instance, the role of updating embeddings is unexplored, and the choice of CNN aggregation versus simpler pooling is not systematically compared.
  - Statistical significance and robustness (e.g., variance across runs) are not discussed.
- Relation to prior work: The framework is very close to the Decomposable Attention model (Parikh et al., 2016) and pairwise word interaction/CubeCNN (He & Lin, 2016), which also compare at the token level and then aggregate. The paper differentiates mainly through the set of comparison functions evaluated and the use of CNN for aggregation, which is a modest variation.
- Rigor and clarity: Core formulas and components are clearly described, and the code release is appreciated. Some implementation decisions (e.g., freezing GloVe embeddings) may bias results and are not analyzed. For SNLI, LSTM-based preprocessing is added, making the “general” architecture task-specific in ways that are not fully controlled.

Additional feedback and questions to improve the paper (not central to the accept/reject decision)
- Provide qualitative analysis: Show case studies illustrating which comparison functions trigger on which linguistic phenomena (e.g., synonymy vs. lexical overlap, negation, paraphrase, multiword expressions). Visualize SUB and MULT dimensions to interpret what they capture.
- Justify aggregation choice: Compare CNN aggregation with the summation/concatenation used in Decomposable Attention and with simple pooling or an RNN aggregator, controlling for parameter count.
- Attention directionality: Evaluate two-directional attention and discuss trade-offs. The current one-directional choice may limit symmetry for certain tasks (e.g., SNLI).
- Preprocessing layer: Since it is largely order-insensitive (except SNLI), analyze the effect of adding contextualization (e.g., BiLSTM) across all tasks, and report compute/accuracy trade-offs.
- Embeddings: Report results with fine-tuned embeddings versus frozen, and with normalization. SUB/MULT can be sensitive to scale; controlling this would strengthen conclusions.
- Fairness and tuning: Clarify hyperparameter search per dataset and per comparison function to ensure fair comparisons. Report variances over multiple runs and significance tests.
- Complexity and efficiency: Provide training/inference time and memory comparisons among comparison functions and aggregators.
- Error analysis: Include confusion patterns or error types across tasks, especially where element-wise methods underperform NN/NTN or EUCCOS.
- Broader applicability: Discuss extension to multi-sentence or multi-hop settings, and to multi-task learning as suggested in the conclusion. Are the findings consistent on other benchmarks (e.g., Quora paraphrase, SQuAD subsets)?

Specific questions for the authors
- How sensitive are SUB/MULT and SUBMULT+NN to embedding dimensionality, scaling, and normalization? Did you try layer normalization or learned scaling before comparison?
- Why choose one-directional attention? What happens with bi-directional alignment (as in Parikh et al.) under matched parameter budgets?
- Why CNN for aggregation? Do simpler pooling strategies (max/mean) or an RNN provide similar or better performance?
- Do the results hold when embeddings are fine-tuned? If not, what changes?
- Are improvements statistically significant across runs? Please report mean ± std over multiple seeds.
- For SNLI, you add LSTM in preprocessing, unlike other tasks. How much of the gain comes from this addition versus the comparison function itself?
- Can you share an analysis where EUCCOS wins (e.g., on MovieQA) and explain the characteristics of such cases?

Overall, the paper presents a clean and useful empirical finding—that simple element-wise comparison functions are strong baselines within compare-aggregate architectures across tasks. With deeper analysis and stronger differentiation from prior models, this work would be more compelling.
