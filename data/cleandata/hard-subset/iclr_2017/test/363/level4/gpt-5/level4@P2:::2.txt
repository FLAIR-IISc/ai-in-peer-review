Summary and contributions
- The paper presents a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences: it performs word-level alignment via attention, compares each word with an attention-weighted counterpart, and aggregates the comparison results with a CNN for prediction.
- The central contribution is a systematic study of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The authors argue and empirically show that simple element-wise operations (especially subtraction and multiplication, optionally followed by a small NN) are strong performers.
- The model is evaluated across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), showing competitive or state-of-the-art results. Ablations suggest both the preprocessing gate and attention are beneficial, with their relative importance varying by task.
- The work positions itself among recent compare-aggregate approaches and broadens evidence that shared architectures can work across multiple sequence matching problems.

Decision
Accept. The paper offers a clear, well-motivated framework with strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks, and a useful, systematic examination of comparison functions that will be valuable to practitioners.

Key reasons
1) Strong, consistent empirical performance on multiple datasets with a simple, interpretable comparison strategy (element-wise operations). 2) A thorough, cross-task evaluation and ablations that substantively inform model design choices within the compare-aggregate paradigm.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Matching two sequences for semantic relatedness lies at the core of NLI, QA, and MC. The paper proposes an effective word-level matching approach with attention and CNN aggregation and focuses on the underexplored choice of comparison function.
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The paper engages well with prior compare-aggregate work (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction models) and argues for studying comparison functions that better reflect similarity measurements. While the overall framework is not novel, the systematic comparison of comparison functions and cross-task breadth add value.
- Evidence supporting claims: Results compare favorably to strong baselines across four datasets. The finding that SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) outperform NN/NTN in several settings is convincing and actionable. Ablations indicate the importance of both preprocessing gates and attention. That said, statistical significance and more details on hyperparameter tuning and fairness of comparisons would further strengthen the evidence.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the acceptance decision)
- Clarity and presentation
  - Fix typo: page 4, line 5: “including a some” → “including some.”
  - Figure 2 is difficult to read (especially in print). Please increase resolution, use larger fonts, and add clearer legends/colorbars.
  - Briefly summarize why a one-directional attention and CNN aggregation were chosen over the more common bidirectional attention and pooling/FFN aggregation, and discuss observed trade-offs.
- Experimental details and rigor
  - Report statistical significance for key gains over the strongest baselines and over NN/NTN comparison functions.
  - Provide more detail on hyperparameters per dataset (e.g., learning rate, batch size for all tasks, dropout rates), early stopping criteria, and number of runs/variance.
  - Discuss computational efficiency (training/inference time, memory) and the impact of freezing vs. fine-tuning word embeddings. A small study on embedding fine-tuning would be informative.
  - Clarify baseline comparability: since many baselines are taken from prior work, comment on differences in preprocessing, embeddings, or candidate sets that may affect comparability.
- Analysis
  - Expand the ablation results: show full performance without preprocessing, without attention, and with both removed for each dataset to quantify each component’s contribution.
  - Provide per-task error analyses or qualitative examples highlighting when element-wise functions fail versus NN/NTN.
  - Discuss the order-insensitive preprocessing choice (except for SNLI) and whether lightweight contextualization (e.g., small CNN or shallow BiRNN) materially changes outcomes.

Questions for the authors
- Preprocessing and attention: Could you report complete ablation tables (per dataset) for removing preprocessing gates, removing attention, and removing both? How sensitive are the results to the gating dimensionality l?
- Comparison functions: Did you try normalizing vectors before MULT/SUB (e.g., L2 normalization)? How does dimensionality of tj affect CNN aggregation capacity? Any overfitting differences between parameter-free (SUB/MULT) and parameterized (NN/NTN) functions?
- Aggregation: Why CNN over simple pooling or RNN aggregation in your final design? Did you compare to bidirectional attention with symmetric aggregation?
- Training details: Were results averaged over multiple runs? If so, please report mean/std. Did you examine the effect of fine-tuning embeddings?
- Baselines and fairness: For datasets where you outperform SOTA, are embeddings and candidate pools comparable to those used by the baselines? Any re-implementations to equalize setups?
- Efficiency: What are training/inference times and peak memory usage for the largest dataset? How does complexity scale with sequence length, especially for MovieQA?
- SNLI specificity: You mention incorporating LSTM for SNLI preprocessing. How much does this change contribute relative to purely order-insensitive preprocessing?
- Figure 2: Could you provide higher-resolution visualizations and, if possible, quantitative proxies (e.g., saliency scores) to support the qualitative claims?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed study that provides practical guidance on comparison functions within a widely used architecture, with strong results across multiple tasks.
