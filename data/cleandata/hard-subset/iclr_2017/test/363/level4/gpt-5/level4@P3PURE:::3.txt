This paper presents a general modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks involving two textual inputs (e.g., a question and a source text) that can be aligned. The method first uses soft attention to derive token-level alignments between the texts, then applies a comparison function that consumes the resulting alignments—represented as pairs of attention queries and attention outputs—to build comparison-based representations. These are aggregated with a CNN into a single vector from which the final prediction is computed. The paper positions this as an overall modeling strategy that performs well in practice and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component.

The work is timely. Problems of this kind are central open challenges in NLP and are only now becoming tractable with representation learning methods. The approach is general, straightforward, and reasonable, and the results are strong. While the contribution borders on incremental relative to the authors’ earlier work and that of Parikh et al., it nonetheless advances the area in several substantive ways. I strongly recommend acceptance.

Comments and suggestions:
- For tasks with longer sequences (all but SNLI), the model, as implemented, is not sensitive to word order. Although it is empirically competitive, this insensitivity likely imposes a strong upper bound on performance. The paper does acknowledge this, but it seems salient enough to merit a brief mention in the introduction or discussion.
- If I understand correctly, the attention mechanism is closer to the general/bilinear formulation of Luong et al. (2015) than to the earlier Bahdanau et al. model. Please cite Luong et al. (or another directly relevant reference for that specific strategy).
- Because the NTN has many parameters and risks overfitting, did you try a version with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (i.e., an l×l×m tensor)?
- It would be helpful to note that SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper you cite.
- Is there a reason you use the same parameters for preprocessing the question and the answer in (1)? These inputs may benefit from different weighting schemes.
