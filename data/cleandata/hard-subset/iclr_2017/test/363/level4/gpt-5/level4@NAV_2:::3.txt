1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching two text sequences. It first aligns tokens across sequences using soft attention, compares each token with its attention-weighted counterpart via a suite of comparison functions, and then aggregates the comparison outputs with a CNN to produce a final representation for prediction. The work systematically studies six comparison functions (including NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, and element-wise SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) and evaluates the framework across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). Empirically, the approach is broadly effective, and simple element-wise comparison functions often outperform standard neural and tensor alternatives, yielding competitive or state-of-the-art results.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong, cross-task empirical validation of a simple, general modeling strategy for sequence matching.
- Clear, focused analysis demonstrating that lightweight element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex NN/NTN baselines.

3. Supporting arguments
- Timeliness and relevance: Sequence matching with learned representations underpins many core NLU tasks; this paper provides a clear, reusable blueprint that works well across QA and NLI settings.
- Empirical strength: The model achieves best or highly competitive results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and solid performance on SNLI, despite using relatively simple components.
- Insightful analysis: The head-to-head evaluation of comparison functions is valuable; the finding that SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) generally dominate NN/NTN offers practical guidance for future work.
- Scope and simplicity: The architecture is straightforward to implement and adapt, yet delivers robust gains, which offsets its incremental nature relative to prior compare-aggregate and decomposable attention models.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Word order sensitivity: For datasets with longer sequences (all but SNLI), the preprocessing is order-insensitive. While the model remains competitive, this likely limits the attainable ceiling; consider flagging this more prominently in the introduction or discussion.
- Attention variant and citation: The attention mechanism appears closer to the “general/bilinear” scoring of Luong et al. (2015) than to the original Bahdanau formulation. Please cite the most relevant source and clarify the exact form used.
- Neural tensor network capacity: Given the large parameter count and overfitting risk in NTN, consider reporting results for a reduced-output version (e.g., l×l×m with m < l) or other low-rank/parameter-sharing variants.
- Relation to prior matching strategies: SUBMULT+NN closely resembles the sentence-level matching heuristics in Mou et al. (2016). It would help to more explicitly position your word-level formulation relative to that work.
- Parameter tying in preprocessing: Equation (1) appears to share parameters between question and answer; these inputs may merit different gating. Consider discussing whether untied parameters improve performance.

Questions for the authors
- Attention formulation: Which exact attention scoring function did you use (e.g., Luong general/bilinear)? Can you include the explicit form and an appropriate citation?
- NTN ablations: Did you experiment with lower-dimensional or reduced-rank NTNs (smaller output dimension m) to mitigate overfitting? If so, how did they compare?
- Preprocessing parameter sharing: Did you try untied preprocessing parameters for Q and A in Eq. (1)? If yes, what was the effect on performance; if not, what motivated tying?
- Order sensitivity: Beyond SNLI, did you test an order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., LSTM/GRU) on the other datasets, and if so, how did results trade off against compute? Any quantitative evidence supporting the claim that order-insensitivity is the main limiting factor?
- Relation to Mou et al.: Can you elaborate on how SUBMULT+NN at the word level differs from the heuristic matching used by Mou et al. (2016), and clarify the novelty of your formulation and findings in that context?
