Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences. It uses attention to align words across two sequences, compares each word with an attention-weighted counterpart via a chosen comparison function, and aggregates the resulting comparison vectors with a CNN for prediction. The authors systematically study six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), highlighting that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and their combination) often outperform more expressive neural alternatives. The model is evaluated on four datasets spanning different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. The work provides practical guidance on selecting comparison functions and demonstrates that a single, simple architecture transfers well across tasks.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical performance across diverse benchmarks with a simple, general architecture. (2) A clear, well-executed ablation and comparison study that yields actionable insights about comparison functions.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and scope: The paper tackles sequence matching, central to QA and NLI. It frames the task as word-level alignment (via attention), local comparison, and global aggregation—an increasingly standard but still active design space.
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well-situated relative to prior compare-aggregate work (e.g., Parikh et al., match-LSTM, pairwise interaction models). Its main novel contribution is the systematic evaluation of comparison functions, showing that element-wise operations are a robust middle ground between overly flexible (NN/NTN) and overly compressed (cosine/Euclidean) similarity measures.
- Empirical support: Results are strong across four datasets, with clear wins on three (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and competitive performance on SNLI. Ablations indicate both preprocessing gates and attention matter, and the analysis sensibly relates their contributions to sequence-length asymmetries. The consistent advantage of SUBMULT+NN supports the central claim. The methodology is straightforward and reproducible, with code availability noted.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Word order sensitivity: For the longer-sequence tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA), the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. While performance is competitive, this likely limits the ceiling. Consider emphasizing this limitation earlier (intro/discussion) and, if possible, adding experiments with lightweight order-aware encoders (e.g., small CNN/RNN) to quantify the trade-off.
- Attention formulation and citations: The attention implementation appears closer to the bilinear/general form of Luong et al. (2015) than to Bahdanau et al. Please clarify this choice and ensure the most appropriate citation and discussion of differences.
- Neural tensor network capacity: NTN can overfit due to parameter count. Did you try reduced-rank/factorized versions or smaller output dimension (llm tensor with m < l)? Reporting whether such variants narrow the gap would strengthen the comparison.
- Relation to prior heuristic matching: SUB and MULT, and especially their combination with a shallow NN (SUBMULT+NN), resemble heuristic matching features used at the sentence level by Mou et al. Please clarify the relationship and novelty at the word-level with attention-weighted alignment, and articulate conceptual differences.
- Shared preprocessing parameters: In Eq. (1), do Q and A share preprocessing parameters (Wi, Wu, bi, bu)? If so, what motivated this choice? Questions and answers (or premise and hypothesis) might benefit from task-specific weighting. A brief comparison of shared vs. separate parameters would be informative.
- One-directional attention: You use one-directional attention (A attends to Q). Did you try bidirectional or iterative alignment? Even a small experiment on one dataset could quantify the gains vs. cost.
- Embedding training: You freeze GloVe and zero-initialize OOVs. Did you assess fine-tuning embeddings or using subword models to mitigate OOVs? A short sensitivity analysis would help practitioners.
- Hyperparameters and efficiency: Please provide more detail on CNN filter sizes/counts, window widths, and runtime/memory cost relative to baselines. Reporting variance over multiple runs and basic significance testing would strengthen the empirical claims.
- Error analysis: A brief qualitative/error analysis (e.g., cases needing compositional or discourse reasoning) would help delineate when order-insensitive preprocessing suffices and where it fails.

Overall, this is a timely and practical contribution: a clean, broadly applicable compare-aggregate architecture with a well-argued study showing that simple element-wise comparison functions are strong choices. The empirical breadth and clarity of findings warrant acceptance.
