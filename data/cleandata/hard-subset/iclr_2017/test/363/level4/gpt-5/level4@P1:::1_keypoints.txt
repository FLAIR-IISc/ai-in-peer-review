- Proposes a general compare-aggregate framework that aligns tokens via attention, compares token pairs with one of six functions, and aggregates comparison vectors with a CNN.  
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) often outperform more complex NN/NTN comparison modules across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Ablation results indicate preprocessing (gating) and attention layers are important for performance.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary reason: Limited novelty—architecture closely follows existing compare-aggregate models (e.g., Decomposable Attention, pairwise interaction/CubeCNN) and mainly provides an empirical comparison of comparison functions.  
- Secondary reason: Insufficient analysis and theoretical insight explaining why element-wise functions work better.  
- Architectural choices (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation, largely order-insensitive preprocessing) are lightly justified and appear engineering-focused rather than conceptual.  
- Empirical results are strong on QA datasets and competitive on SNLI, and cross-task evaluation is a plus.  
- Study does not explain sensitivity factors (embedding scaling, dimensionality, normalization) that might underlie SUB/MULT success.  
- Ablations are limited: role of updating/fine-tuning embeddings and choice of CNN vs. pooling/RNN aggregators are not systematically compared.  
- Statistical significance and robustness (variance across runs, mean±std) are not reported.  
- Relation to prior work: framework is very similar to Decomposable Attention and pairwise word interaction models; novelty mainly in the set of comparison functions and CNN aggregator.  
- Rigor/clarity: formulas and components are clear and code release is appreciated, but some implementation choices (e.g., freezing GloVe) may bias results.  
- SNLI uses additional LSTM preprocessing, making the “general” architecture task-specific and not fully controlled.  
- Request qualitative analysis: show case studies and visualize SUB/MULT dimensions to interpret which linguistic phenomena each captures.  
- Suggest comparing CNN aggregation to summation/concatenation, simple pooling, and RNN aggregators while controlling for parameter count.  
- Recommend evaluating bidirectional attention and discussing trade-offs versus the current one-directional choice.  
- Advise testing contextual preprocessing (BiLSTM) across tasks and reporting compute/accuracy trade-offs.  
- Ask for experiments with fine-tuned embeddings, normalization, and learned scaling to test sensitivity of SUB/MULT.  
- Call for fair hyperparameter tuning per dataset/function, reporting variances and significance tests across multiple runs.  
- Request training/inference time and memory comparisons among comparison functions and aggregators.  
- Ask for error analysis showing confusion patterns and cases where element-wise methods underperform NN/NTN.  
- Suggest testing broader applicability (multi-sentence, multi-hop, other benchmarks like Quora/SQuAD) and consistency of findings.  
- Specific methodological questions: sensitivity to embedding dimensionality/scaling, effect of bidirectional attention, justification for CNN aggregator, impact of fine-tuning embeddings, statistical significance across seeds, effect of LSTM preprocessing on SNLI, and characteristics of cases where EUCCOS wins.
