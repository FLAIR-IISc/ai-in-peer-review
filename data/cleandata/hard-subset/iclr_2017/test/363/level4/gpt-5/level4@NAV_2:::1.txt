1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces a compare-aggregate framework for NLP tasks that require semantic matching between text sequences (e.g., question answering and textual entailment). The model applies attention to obtain aligned representations and then performs word-level comparison via element-wise operations, followed by aggregation with a convolutional neural network. The central focus is an empirical study of comparison functions for matching vectors; element-wise subtraction and multiplication are shown to be particularly effective, delivering strong performance across four datasets.

2. Decision
Reject, primarily due to limited novelty. While the empirical results are solid, the approach feels incremental and lacks deeper insight into why the simple element-wise comparison functions outperform more complex alternatives.

3. Supporting arguments
- The overall architecture largely follows established compare-aggregate paradigms; the main novelty is the systematic comparison of matching functions. Although useful, this contribution is modest for a top-tier venue.
- The paper’s key empirical finding—that element-wise subtraction/multiplication generally work better—is compelling but not sufficiently analyzed. Without qualitative examination across diverse sentence phenomena, it is difficult to assess the generality and underlying reasons for these gains.
- The model relies on attention over LSTM-based representations and aggregates via CNN, which are standard choices; the paper would benefit from stronger motivation for these specific components relative to alternatives.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Please add qualitative analyses illustrating when subtraction vs. multiplication succeeds or fails (e.g., synonymy/paraphrase, contradiction/antonymy, negation, numeric comparisons, multi-hop reasoning). This would substantiate the central claim and provide actionable guidance.
- Consider a finer-grained breakdown by sentence types or linguistic phenomena to show whether certain comparison functions specialize for particular cases.
- Clarify the rationale for using CNN for aggregation and discuss how results change with other aggregators (e.g., RNNs, self-attention, pooling).
- Elaborate on the role of the attention mechanism and LSTM outputs in enabling effective comparisons, and provide sensitivity analyses where feasible.

Questions for the authors
- Can you provide concrete qualitative examples comparing subtraction and multiplication (and other baselines) on varied sentence relations (e.g., paraphrase, contradiction, negation, entailment with quantifiers or numbers)?
- How sensitive are the results to the attention formulation and the use of LSTM-generated context (versus simpler or order-insensitive preprocessing) in the comparison step?
- Why choose CNN for aggregation over alternatives, and how do the findings change with different aggregators?
- Do the observed advantages of element-wise operations persist when sentence lengths, lexical overlap, or domain shift vary substantially across datasets?
