Summary
The paper proposes a general compare–aggregate framework for matching text sequences across diverse NLP tasks (machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment). The model first applies a gating-based preprocessing step to weight word importance, then uses one-directional attention to align each token in one sequence with an attention-weighted representation of the other. A comparison function is applied to each aligned pair, and the resulting sequence of comparison vectors is aggregated by a CNN to produce a final representation for classification. The main contribution is a systematic, cross-task evaluation of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN). The authors find that simple element-wise operations—especially the combination of subtraction and multiplication (SUBMULT+NN)—consistently outperform standard neural network and tensor network comparators. The approach performs at or near state of the art on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and is competitive on SNLI. Ablations indicate the importance of both the gating-based preprocessing and the attention layer.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Strong, cross-task empirical performance with a simple, modular architecture, along with informative ablation studies.
- A clear, useful analysis demonstrating that element-wise comparison functions are robust and often superior to more complex NN/NTN comparators.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper targets a central question in NLP sequence-pair modeling: how best to compare two sequences by aligning and comparing their constituent units, and which word-level comparison function is most effective.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated within the compare–aggregate family (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction models). The architectural differences (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation, gating-based preprocessing) are reasonable, and the focus on comparison functions fills a gap in prior work, which typically defaulted to NN-based comparators without systematic evaluation.
- Evidence and rigor: Results across four datasets with different task formulations support the claims. The paper compares multiple comparison functions, reports competitive-to-SOTA results, and includes ablations showing the roles of attention and preprocessing. While the model is order-insensitive in three of the four tasks (except SNLI, where an LSTM is added), empirical performance remains strong, suggesting the word-by-word matching and aggregation are doing meaningful work. The experimental setup is described with implementation details, and the code is released, aiding reproducibility. A few aspects (variance across runs, baseline parity) could be clarified, but overall the evidence is convincing.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Word order sensitivity:
  - The model is largely order-insensitive on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA. This is an important limitation; consider highlighting it earlier (introduction) and quantifying its impact (e.g., try an order-aware variant or report a small experiment showing the potential gain).
- Attention mechanism citation:
  - Your attention is closer to the general/bilinear form (Luong et al., 2015) than to the Bahdanau formulation. Please cite Luong et al. explicitly (or an equivalent reference) and clarify the scoring function.
- Comparison function capacity and overfitting:
  - The NTN likely has many parameters. Did you try reducing its output dimensionality (e.g., l×l×m with m < l), low-rank factorization, or strong regularization? Reporting such attempts would strengthen the conclusion that simpler element-wise functions are preferable.
- Relation to prior element-wise matching:
  - SUB and MULT are related to heuristics used by Mou et al. (2016) and others at the sentence level. SUBMULT+NN also resembles common element-wise feature concatenations used in sentence-pair models. Please clarify the novelty: is it primarily the systematic evaluation at the word level within a compare–aggregate pipeline, or are there new insights about how to combine these operations?
- Parameter sharing in preprocessing:
  - Equation (1) appears to use the same parameters to preprocess Q and A. What is the rationale for tying them? Did you try untied parameters (separate gates for question vs. answer/hypothesis) or partial sharing, and how did that affect performance?
- Aggregation details:
  - Please specify CNN filter widths, number of filters, pooling strategy, and whether you tried alternative aggregators (e.g., BiLSTM/GRU or self-attention). A brief sensitivity analysis would help readers understand this design choice.
- Training details and fairness:
  - Baselines are reported from prior work. Where possible, discuss whether differences in preprocessing, embeddings (you freeze GloVe), candidate pool setups (especially for InsuranceQA/WikiQA), and training objectives could affect comparability. Did you assess performance variance across multiple random seeds or test statistical significance?
- Embedding fine-tuning:
  - You freeze GloVe embeddings. Did you try fine-tuning or partial fine-tuning (e.g., top layers only)? A brief note on whether that helps would be useful.
- Efficiency and scalability:
  - Provide a short discussion of runtime/memory trade-offs, especially the O(Q×A) attention and the overhead of NTN vs. element-wise comparators. This would further justify the recommendation for simple functions.
- SNLI variant:
  - For SNLI, you incorporate an LSTM in preprocessing. Please clarify the exact modification and its impact relative to the purely order-insensitive variant, and discuss how this compares to Parikh et al.’s decomposable attention setup.
- Bidirectional attention:
  - You use one-directional attention (A attends to Q). Did you try symmetric/bidirectional attention, and if so, what was the effect?
- MovieQA tri-partite matching:
  - For MovieQA, you match Q–P and A–P independently. Did you explore conditioning A–P matching on Q or using joint/bidirectional attention among all three sequences? Even a brief negative result would be informative.
- Visualizations:
  - Figure 2 is helpful. Please add a short description of how activations are normalized and selected (e.g., max-over-time across filters) to aid interpretation.

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed study that advances our understanding of word-level comparison in sequence matching. The paper’s empirical breadth and simple, effective recommendations (element-wise subtraction and multiplication) will be valuable to practitioners. I recommend acceptance.
