This paper proposes a compare–aggregate architecture for NLP tasks that require semantic comparison of text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The model first applies attention to LSTM encodings of the input sequences, then performs element-wise comparison operations on the attention-weighted representations, and finally uses a convolutional neural network to aggregate the resulting features.

The main contribution is the systematic examination of the comparison step. The authors evaluate several matching functions and show that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally deliver better performance across four datasets.

The primary weakness is limited novelty: the approach feels incremental. The paper would be strengthened by a qualitative analysis illustrating how subtraction, multiplication, and other comparison functions behave on different types of sentence pairs, helping to explain when and why each function is most effective.
