Summary
The paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for pairwise sentence matching. It performs word-level matching between two sequences using several comparison functions, then aggregates these local alignments with convolutional neural networks to produce a final decision. The authors compare six comparison functions and evaluate the approach on four datasets, reporting extensive empirical results against a range of published baselines. The paper is generally clear and well organized.

Strengths
- Clear, modular architecture: separating word-level comparison from aggregation makes the method easy to understand and potentially reusable across tasks.
- Thorough empirical evaluation: comparing multiple comparison functions across four datasets provides a useful and systematic study.
- Generally strong writing quality and presentation of the main ideas.

Questions and concerns
- Contribution of preprocessing and attention: The current presentation does not isolate the impact of the preprocessing and attention components. Please quantify their contributions with ablation studies. Specifically:
  - Report results without the preprocessing step.
  - Report results without the attention mechanism.
  - If possible, also show results with each component added independently to clarify interactions.
- Interpretation of comparison functions: The paper evaluates six comparison functions, but it would be helpful to include a brief analysis explaining when and why specific functions work better (e.g., across datasets or task types). Even qualitative insights or representative examples would improve the reader’s understanding of the design space.

Presentation and clarity
- Figure 2 is difficult to read, especially in printed form. Please increase the resolution and font sizes, improve line thickness and contrast, and ensure color choices are distinguishable when printed in grayscale.
- Minor typo: page 4, line 5 — “including a some” should be “including some.”

Suggestions for improvement
- Add an ablation table quantifying the effect of preprocessing and attention, ideally across at least one representative dataset per task to manage space.
- Provide a short discussion section interpreting performance differences among the six comparison functions (e.g., which are most robust, which are sensitive to domain or sequence length).
- If space permits, include a small error analysis highlighting typical failure cases; this would help readers and practitioners understand limitations and guide future improvements.
- Consider noting any computational trade-offs (e.g., runtime or parameter counts) introduced by the attention mechanism or by the more complex comparison functions, even if only in the appendix.

Overall assessment
This is a solid and well-written paper that proposes a simple yet effective architecture and backs it with a comprehensive empirical study. Addressing the ablation questions around preprocessing and attention, improving the readability of Figure 2, and adding a brief interpretability discussion for the comparison functions would strengthen the paper and make its contributions clearer and more actionable.
