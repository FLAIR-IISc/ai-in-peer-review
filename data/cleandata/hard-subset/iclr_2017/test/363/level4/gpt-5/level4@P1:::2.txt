1. Summary of the paper’s contributions
- The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences. It performs word-level comparison between an attended representation of one sequence and the tokens of the other, followed by aggregation via a CNN.
- It systematically studies six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with particular focus on simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and their combination), and evaluates them across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- The main empirical finding is that element-wise comparison functions (especially SUBMULT+NN) can outperform standard neural-network and neural-tensor comparison modules, achieving strong or state-of-the-art results on three QA datasets and competitive performance on SNLI.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Broad, convincing empirical evaluation across diverse tasks demonstrates the generality and effectiveness of the compare-aggregate paradigm.
- The systematic analysis of comparison functions yields clear, actionable insights (simple element-wise operations work best), which is likely to be useful to the community.

3. Supporting arguments
- The paper is clear and well organized, with a clean architecture and a careful comparison of six comparison functions. The focus on the comparison function itself is original and informative, bridging the gap between highly flexible but opaque NN/NTN modules and overly compressed similarity scores (Euclidean/cosine).
- Results are strong: the model achieves top performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and very competitive results on SNLI. This breadth suggests the framework is robust across problem settings (answer selection, ranking, and textual entailment).
- The ablation discussion indicates both the preprocessing (gating) and the attention mechanism materially contribute to performance; the observations about their relative importance across tasks with different length disparities are valuable.
- Using a simple CNN for aggregation keeps the architecture efficient while still capturing local patterns in the comparison vectors.
- The code release further increases the paper’s value for reproducibility and adoption.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Page 4, line 5: “including a some” → “including some.”
  - Figure 2 is difficult to read, especially in print. Please increase resolution, font size, and contrast; add clear axis labels and colorbars. Consider restructuring the visualization to better highlight which positions in the plot contribute to the final decision, and annotate examples for readability.
- Ablations and analysis:
  - You highlight the importance of preprocessing and attention. Please report explicit ablation numbers per dataset (no preprocessing, no attention, both) to quantify their contributions, ideally in a single table for easy comparison.
  - Given the choice of a CNN aggregator, a brief sensitivity analysis over kernel sizes and number of filters would help justify this design versus simpler aggregation (e.g., sum/concat as in prior work).
- Methodological details:
  - You fix word embeddings to pretrained GloVe. A short justification for not fine-tuning them, and a note on performance impact if you do fine-tune, would be helpful for practitioners.
  - For SNLI, you incorporate LSTMs in preprocessing; for the other tasks, preprocessing is order-insensitive. A short comparison showing how much context-sensitive preprocessing helps (beyond attention) would strengthen the generality claim.
- Related work:
  - Consider clarifying the relationship to bidirectional attention models and recent pairwise-interaction CNNs, and explicitly contrasting your one-directional attention and CNN aggregation choices with alternatives.

5. Questions for the authors
- Preprocessing and attention:
  - Could you provide per-dataset ablation results removing the preprocessing (gating) and/or attention layers? How much does each contribute to final accuracy/MAP/MRR?
  - For tasks with large length imbalance (e.g., MovieQA, InsuranceQA), can you quantify the gain from attention versus tasks with more balanced lengths (WikiQA, SNLI)?
- Comparison functions:
  - SUB and MULT perform best overall. How sensitive are these findings to the dimensionality of embeddings and to fine-tuning vs. freezing embeddings?
  - Have you tried normalizing vectors before comparison (e.g., layer norm or L2 norm)? Does normalization change the relative ordering of comparison functions?
- Aggregation:
  - Why a one-layer CNN over alternatives (RNN, self-attention, or simple pooling)? How sensitive are results to kernel widths and number of filters?
- Efficiency:
  - Can you report parameter counts and training/inference speed for NN/NTN vs. SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN to substantiate the “simpler is better” claim from a computational perspective?
- Generalization:
  - The attention is one-directional. Have you evaluated a bidirectional version, and if so, how does it compare in accuracy and efficiency?
  - Do the conclusions hold when incorporating stronger contextual encoders (e.g., LSTMs across all tasks), or do element-wise functions remain preferable?

Overall, this is a clear, well-executed study with useful insights that are likely to inform future work on sequence matching.
