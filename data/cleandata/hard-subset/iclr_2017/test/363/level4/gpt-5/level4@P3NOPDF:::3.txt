Summary
The paper proposes a general modeling framework for natural language understanding tasks involving two aligned textual inputs (e.g., question–context, premise–hypothesis). The method first applies soft attention to derive token-level alignments, then uses a comparison function over aligned pairs (attention queries and attended results) to produce local comparison vectors. These are aggregated with a CNN into a single representation from which predictions are made. The paper positions this as both a broadly applicable recipe and provides a careful empirical analysis of the comparison component.

Strengths
- Timely and relevant: Paired-text understanding remains a central challenge in NLP, and representation learning methods are just beginning to tackle it effectively.
- Clear, general approach: The architecture is straightforward, modular, and appears easy to adapt across tasks.
- Strong empirical results: Despite its simplicity in some components, the model is competitive on multiple benchmarks.
- Informative analysis: The focused study of the comparison function is valuable and yields actionable insights about what matters in this class of models.

Weaknesses and concerns
- Limited sensitivity to word order for long sequences: In all tasks except SNLI, the model—as instantiated—appears order-insensitive. While results are competitive, this imposes a clear ceiling on performance and interpretability. The paper does note this limitation; given its importance, it merits brief, explicit mention in the introduction or discussion, along with a pointer to potential remedies.
- Positioning relative to prior work: The contributions border on incremental relative to the authors’ earlier work and that of Parikh et al. Nevertheless, the paper adds enough substance—particularly in the analysis—to warrant acceptance. Still, a sharper articulation of novelty and differences would strengthen the paper.

Specific comments and suggestions
- Attention mechanism citation: The attention formulation seems closer to Luong et al. (2015) “general/bilinear” attention than to Bahdanau et al. Please cite Luong et al. (or another directly relevant reference) and clarify the exact scoring function used.
- Comparison function capacity and overfitting: The NTN variant risks overfitting due to its parameter count. Did you evaluate a reduced-capacity NTN with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (i.e., an l×l×m tensor with m < l), or low-rank/factorized parameterizations? Reporting such results would contextualize the trade-off between expressiveness and generalization.
- Relation to prior matching architectures: The SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper you cite. Please acknowledge this connection explicitly and clarify the distinctions (e.g., feature construction, parameter sharing, or training setup).
- Parameter sharing in preprocessing: You appear to use the same parameters to preprocess question and answer sequences in the initial encoding stage. Given potential asymmetries (e.g., questions vs. contexts may demand different emphasis), what motivated this choice? Did you try untied encoders or lightweight adapter layers to let each side specialize? Even a brief comparison would be informative.
- Order sensitivity: Beyond noting the limitation, a short ablation or discussion on whether alternative aggregation (e.g., order-aware CNN kernels, RNNs, or self-attention over comparison vectors) affects performance on longer sequences would be valuable, even if left for future work.

Clarity and presentation
- The paper is generally well written and easy to follow. The modular decomposition—alignment, comparison, aggregation—provides a clear mental model and should aid reproducibility.
- Since the empirical analysis centers on the comparison module, consider adding a concise summary table or figure that synthesizes its variants, capacities, and observed behaviors across tasks. This would make the takeaways crisper for readers looking to reuse the component.

Overall assessment
This paper offers a practical, well-motivated framework for paired-text understanding, with competitive results and a useful deep dive into the comparison stage. While some elements are incremental and the order-insensitivity on longer sequences is a meaningful limitation, the contributions—especially the empirical analysis and the clear modeling recipe—are substantial. With minor revisions to better position the work relative to prior attention mechanisms and related matching functions, and with a brief acknowledgment of the order-sensitivity issue in the introduction, the paper will be stronger.

Recommendation: Accept.
