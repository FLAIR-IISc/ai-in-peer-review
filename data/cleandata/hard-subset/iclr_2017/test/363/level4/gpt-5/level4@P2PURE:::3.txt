The paper proposes a general modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks that involve two textual inputs (e.g., a question and a source text) that can be aligned. The method first applies soft attention to align tokens across the two texts. A comparison function then uses the resulting alignments—represented as pairs of attention queries and their attended results—to produce representations, which are aggregated by a CNN into a single vector from which the final output is computed. The paper presents both this overall strategy and a detailed empirical analysis of the model’s comparison component.

This work is timely: such language understanding problems remain a major open challenge in NLP and are only now becoming tractable with representation learning methods. The approach is general, straightforward, and reasonable, and the experiments show that it can perform well. While the contribution borders on incremental relative to the authors’ earlier work and that of Parikh et al., it adds enough substantive value that I strongly recommend acceptance.

Detail:
- For the longer-sequence settings (everything except SNLI), the model is not sensitive to word order. It is empirically competitive, but this limitation likely imposes a strong upper bound on performance. The paper acknowledges this, but it seems important enough to warrant a brief mention in the introduction or discussion.
- If I understand correctly, the attention mechanism is closer to the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than to the earlier Bahdanau formulation. You should cite the former (or another directly relevant reference for that strategy).
- Since the NTN has many parameters and is prone to overfitting, did you try a variant with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (i.e., an l × l × m tensor)?
- It would be helpful to note that SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper you cite.
- Is there a reason to share parameters for preprocessing the question and the answer in (1)? These inputs may need different features to be weighted more heavily.
