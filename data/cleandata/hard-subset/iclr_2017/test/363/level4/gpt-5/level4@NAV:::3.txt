Review- The paper proposes a general modeling framework for natural language understanding tasks with two distinct textual inputs (e.g., a question and a source passage) that can be aligned. The method first applies soft attention to obtain alignments between tokens across the two texts, then a comparison module uses these alignments (treated as pairs of attention queries and their attended results) to produce representations that are aggregated by a CNN into a single vector from which the output is computed. The paper presents this both as a broadly applicable modeling recipe that can work well and as a detailed empirical study of the model’s comparison component.

This work is timely. Language understanding tasks of this sort remain a major open challenge in NLP and are just becoming amenable to representation learning approaches. The proposed framework is general, straightforward, and sensible, and it delivers strong results. While the contribution borders on incremental (relative to the authors’ prior work or that of Parikh et al.), it offers enough substantive additions that I would strongly recommend acceptance.

Detail:
- As implemented for tasks with longer sequences (all except SNLI), the model is insensitive to word order. It is empirically competitive, but this invariance likely imposes a strong upper bound on performance. The paper does state this, but it seems important enough to warrant a brief mention in the introduction or discussion.
- If I understand correctly, your attention mechanism aligns more closely with the general/bilinear formulation of Luong et al. (2015) than with the earlier Bahdanau approach. You should probably cite the former (or another directly relevant reference for that strategy).
- Since the NTN is prone to overfitting due to its large parameter count, did you try a version with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (i.e., an l l m tensor)?
- You might note that SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper you cite.
- Is there a reason you share the same preprocessing parameters for the question and answer in (1)? These inputs may need different features to be emphasized.
