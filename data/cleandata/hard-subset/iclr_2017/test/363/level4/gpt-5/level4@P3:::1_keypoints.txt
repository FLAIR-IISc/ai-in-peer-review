- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework: gating-based preprocessing, one-directional attention, comparison functions, and CNN aggregation.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Finds simple element-wise functions, especially SUBMULT+NN, are often best across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Ablations indicate preprocessing and attention are important components.  
- Paper is clearly written, broadly applicable, and offers useful empirical guidance.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline) due to limited technical novelty.  
- Main novelty concern: architecture and element-wise heuristics have strong precedents; contribution is largely empirical.  
- Analysis gap: lacks explanation of when and why element-wise operations succeed.  
- Missing statistical rigor: no multiple runs, confidence intervals, or significance testing.  
- Uncontrolled capacity: comparison functions differ in output dimensionality and parameter counts, potentially biasing results.  
- Design choices may limit fairness/generalizability: fixed GloVe, order-insensitive preprocessing in most tasks, and one-directional attention.  
- Baseline comparisons may be confounded because baselines were not re-implemented under identical settings.  
- Suggestion: run multiple seeds and report significance intervals for key comparisons.  
- Suggestion: equalize output dimensionality/parameter counts and report FLOPs/training time to control for capacity and compute.  
- Suggestion: provide theoretical/empirical analysis explaining why SUB and MULT work (symmetry, gradient behavior, metric connections).  
- Suggestion: add qualitative/error analyses and visualizations of attention and comparison activations.  
- Suggestion: evaluate alternative aggregators (RNN/LSTM) and bi-directional attention under identical settings.  
- Suggestion: test sensitivity to contextual encoders and fine-tuning embeddings across all datasets.  
- Suggestion: improve references/formatting and release exact hyperparameters and code for reproducibility.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to parameterization, multiple seeds/statistical significance, effect of fine-tuning embeddings, per-phenomenon breakdowns, projecting EUCCOS to match capacity, computational trade-offs, impact of bi-directional attention, role of contextual encoders for long contexts, and direct matched comparison to Parikh et al.
