Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences that (a) aligns words in one sequence to an attention-weighted representation of the other, (b) compares them with a chosen word-level comparison function, and (c) aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN for classification. The design is simple, modular, and task-agnostic.
- The core contribution is a careful, cross-task evaluation of six comparison functions: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN. A key empirical finding is that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN) consistently match or outperform more expressive neural comparison layers across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- The model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on three QA-style datasets and strong performance on SNLI, suggesting the effectiveness and generality of the compare-aggregate paradigm. The authors also provide ablation analyses and release code.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Strong empirical validation across four datasets, with clear performance gains over prior work on several tasks.
2) The systematic comparison of word-level matching functions is insightful and practically useful, showing that simple, parameter-free operations can be superior to heavier neural modules.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses sequence matching for tasks such as machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment, where fine-grained alignment and comparison between two sequences are crucial.
- Is the approach well motivated and situated in the literature? Yes. The work builds on the compare-aggregate line (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM) and motivates a focused study on the comparison function itself—an aspect often underexplored. The relation to prior architectures is clearly acknowledged, with differences (CNN aggregation, one-directional attention, systematic function study) articulated.
- Do the results support the claims? The multi-dataset evaluation, ablations (preprocessing and attention), and consistent findings about SUB/MULT vs. NN/NTN support the central claims. The reported numbers indicate competitive to state-of-the-art results. While the novelty is incremental architecturally, the breadth of evaluation and the clarity of the empirical message make the contribution solid.

Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Please fix the typo: page 4, line 5: “including a some” → “including some.”
  - Figure 2 is hard to read in print; increase resolution and font sizes, and consider adding captions that explain the key takeaways directly on the figure.
  - For readability, provide a concise schematic for the MovieQA variant (plot–question, plot–answer comparisons, and CNN aggregation).
- Quantitative details:
  - Ablations: You state the preprocessing (gating) and attention layers matter differently depending on sequence length mismatch. Please provide the exact deltas per dataset for removing each component, and report whether differences are statistically significant.
  - Parameter counts and compute: Report the number of parameters and training/inference times for each comparison function (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) to substantiate claims about simplicity/efficiency of element-wise methods.
  - Hyperparameters: Summarize per-dataset hyperparameters, early stopping criteria, and the number of runs/variance (mean ± std) to assess robustness.
- Methodological questions:
  - Preprocessing gates: Are gates shared across positions or conditioned per token only by linear transforms? Have you tried contextual gating (e.g., BiLSTM) uniformly across tasks to measure the trade-off between order-insensitive and contextualized preprocessing?
  - Attention directionality: Have you tried bi-directional attention (both Q→A and A→Q), as in decomposable attention or bidaf-style models? Does SUB/MULT still dominate under bi-directional attention?
  - Normalization: Since SUB/MULT relate to Euclidean/cosine, what is the effect of L2-normalizing embeddings before comparison? Does this change the relative ranking of functions?
  - Embedding fine-tuning: Word embeddings are fixed. Do SUB/MULT retain their advantage when embeddings are fine-tuned? Please provide a small study on SNLI or WikiQA.
  - Aggregation choice: Why a single-layer CNN instead of simple pooling (as in decomposable attention) or an RNN? An ablation comparing CNN vs. sum/mean pooling vs. BiLSTM would strengthen the aggregation choice.
  - Task-specific variants: For MovieQA, the plot is long. How do you handle very long contexts (truncation, sliding windows)? Any sensitivity analysis w.r.t. plot length?
  - Generalization: Do the simple comparison functions continue to excel when using stronger contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM or CNN encoders) for both sequences on all datasets, not only SNLI?
- Positioning and novelty:
  - Please sharpen the comparison to Parikh et al. (2016): emphasize that your main novelty is a thorough cross-task study of comparison functions and show where CNN aggregation helps or is neutral.
  - Consider adding a brief discussion on why element-wise operations may regularize better than NN/NTN for word-level matching (e.g., inductive bias toward similarity) and how this interacts with data size.

Overall assessment
This paper offers a clear, well-executed empirical study demonstrating that simple, interpretable word-level comparison functions within a compare-aggregate framework can deliver strong performance across diverse sequence matching tasks. The work is timely, practically relevant, and supported by comprehensive experiments. With minor presentation improvements and additional ablations/significance reporting, it will make a valuable contribution.
