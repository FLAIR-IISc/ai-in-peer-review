- Proposes a modular compare-aggregate framework: attention-based alignment, word-level comparison, CNN aggregation for classification.
- Core contribution: systematic cross-task evaluation of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).
- Key empirical finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and SUBMULT+NN match or outperform more expressive neural comparison layers across datasets.
- Achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on three QA datasets and strong performance on SNLI.
- Provides ablation analyses and releases code.
- Decision: Accept.
- Primary reasons for acceptance: strong multi-dataset empirical validation and an insightful, practical comparison showing simplicity can beat heavier modules.
- Problem addressed: sequence matching for machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment requiring fine-grained alignment and comparison.
- Motivation and related work: builds on compare-aggregate literature (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM) and focuses on the underexplored comparison function.
- Results support claims via multi-dataset evaluation and ablations showing SUB/MULT advantages over NN/NTN.
- Novelty is incremental architecturally but valuable due to breadth of evaluation and clear empirical message.
- Presentation fixes requested: correct typo on page 4 and improve Figure 2 resolution, fonts, and captions.
- Suggestion: add a concise schematic for the MovieQA variant (plot–question/answer and CNN aggregation).
- Quantitative requests: report exact ablation deltas per dataset and statistical significance for removing components.
- Efficiency request: provide parameter counts and training/inference times for each comparison function to substantiate simplicity/efficiency claims.
- Reproducibility request: summarize per-dataset hyperparameters, early stopping criteria, number of runs, and mean ± std variance.
- Methodological questions: clarify whether preprocessing gates are shared or token-conditioned and whether contextual gating (e.g., BiLSTM) was tried.
- Ask about attention directionality: evaluate bi-directional attention and whether SUB/MULT still dominate under it.
- Ask about normalization: test effect of L2-normalizing embeddings on function rankings.
- Ask about embedding fine-tuning: whether SUB/MULT advantages hold when embeddings are fine-tuned (suggest SNLI or WikiQA study).
- Aggregation choice: justify single-layer CNN vs. pooling or RNN and request ablation comparing CNN/sum/mean/BiLSTM.
- MovieQA specifics: explain handling of long plots (truncation, sliding windows) and sensitivity to plot length.
- Generalization question: do simple comparison functions remain superior with stronger contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/CNN) across datasets?
- Positioning: sharpen comparison to Parikh et al. (2016) and clarify where CNN aggregation helps or is neutral.
- Theorize why element-wise ops may regularize better than NN/NTN (inductive bias toward similarity) and interaction with data size.
- Overall assessment: clear, well-executed empirical study showing simple interpretable comparison functions work well across diverse tasks; minor presentation and additional ablation/significance reporting would strengthen the paper.
