The paper proposes a general framework for natural language understanding tasks with two aligned textual inputs (e.g., a question and a passage). The method first uses soft attention to align tokens across the two texts. For each token, the model forms a pair consisting of the attention query and its attended counterpart, and a comparison function maps these aligned pairs to vector representations. A CNN then aggregates these token-level comparisons into a single vector used for prediction. The paper presents this as a general modeling strategy that performs well across tasks, and it includes a careful empirical analysis of the comparison module.

This work is timely. These alignment-based language understanding problems remain a central challenge in NLP and are only recently becoming tractable with representation learning. The proposed approach is clear, reasonable, and empirically strong. While the contribution is somewhat incremental relative to the authors’ prior work and to Parikh et al., it nonetheless offers enough substantive advances—both in modeling and analysis—that I strongly recommend acceptance.

Details:
- For tasks with longer inputs (all except SNLI), the model as implemented is insensitive to word order. Although it remains empirically competitive, this insensitivity likely imposes a tight upper bound on performance. The paper notes this limitation, but it seems important enough to merit a brief mention in the introduction or discussion, to frame the results and guide future extensions.
- If I understand correctly, the attention mechanism is closer to the general/multiplicative (bilinear) attention of Luong et al. (2015) than to the additive mechanism of Bahdanau et al. You should explicitly cite Luong et al. (or another directly relevant source) for this attention strategy.
- Given the overfitting risk of the NTN due to its parameter count, did you try a variant with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (i.e., an l×l×m tensor)? This would reduce parameters and might preserve expressivity while improving generalization.
- It would be helpful to note that SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper you cite. The conceptual overlap is worth acknowledging to position your contribution relative to prior matching architectures.
- Is there a specific reason to share parameters for preprocessing the question and the answer in equation (1)? These inputs can have different roles and may benefit from different feature weightings. A brief justification (or an ablation comparing shared vs. separate parameters) would clarify this design choice.
