- Introduces a compare-aggregate framework using attention to align representations, element-wise word-level comparisons, and CNN aggregation for semantic matching tasks.  
- Empirical focus: systematic study of comparison functions, finding element-wise subtraction and multiplication perform best across four datasets.  
- Decision: Reject due to limited novelty and insufficient insight into why simple element-wise functions outperform complex alternatives.  
- Architecture largely follows existing compare-aggregate paradigms; the novelty (systematic matching-function comparison) is modest for a top-tier venue.  
- Main empirical finding is compelling but under-analyzed; lacks qualitative examination to explain generality or causes of gains.  
- Uses standard components (attention over LSTM, CNN aggregation) without strong motivation for these specific choices.  
- Recommend adding qualitative analyses showing when subtraction vs. multiplication succeed or fail (e.g., synonymy, antonymy, negation, numeric comparisons, multi-hop).  
- Suggest finer-grained breakdowns by sentence types or linguistic phenomena to test specialization of comparison functions.  
- Ask authors to clarify rationale for CNN aggregation and report results with alternative aggregators (RNNs, self-attention, pooling).  
- Request elaboration on the role of attention and LSTM outputs and sensitivity analyses for these components.  
- Question: provide concrete qualitative examples comparing subtraction, multiplication, and baselines across varied sentence relations.  
- Question: how sensitive are results to the attention formulation and use of LSTM context versus simpler/order-insensitive preprocessing?  
- Question: why choose CNN for aggregation, and how do findings change with different aggregators?  
- Question: do element-wise advantages hold under variations in sentence length, lexical overlap, or domain shift?
