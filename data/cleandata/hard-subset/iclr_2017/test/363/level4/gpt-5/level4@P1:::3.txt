Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences across multiple NLP tasks. The model first computes soft attention to align tokens between two sequences, compares each token with its attention-weighted counterpart using a chosen comparison function, and then aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN to produce a final representation for prediction. The key contribution is a systematic empirical study of six comparison functions—NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN—demonstrating that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and their combination) often outperform standard neural network and tensor-based comparators. The approach is evaluated on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results and highlighting the generality of the compare-aggregate framework.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper offers a clear, general modeling strategy with strong empirical results across diverse tasks, and provides a careful, well-executed analysis of the comparison component that is both practically useful and of broad interest.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Sequence matching underlies a wide range of tasks (machine comprehension, answer selection, textual entailment). Prior work often compresses sequences into single vectors or focuses on a specific task. This paper argues for word-level comparison with aggregation and systematically investigates the comparison function, a previously underexplored component.
- Placement in literature: The work is close to match-LSTM (Wang & Jiang), decomposable attention (Parikh et al.), and pairwise interaction models (He & Lin), but extends them by (i) consolidating a general compare-aggregate framework evaluated across four tasks and (ii) providing a thorough head-to-head evaluation of comparison functions, including underused element-wise formulations.
- Empirical support: Results are strong on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and competitive on SNLI. The ablations are informative: both preprocessing and attention matter, with attention more critical when sequence lengths differ substantially (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), and preprocessing more impactful when lengths are closer (WikiQA, SNLI). Crucially, simple, parameter-light comparison functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often outperform NN/NTN, supporting the claim that preserving element-wise similarity information is advantageous. The findings are consistent across tasks, increasing confidence in generality.
- Scope and limitations: For tasks with longer sequences (all but SNLI), the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Despite competitive performance, this imposes a likely ceiling on achievable accuracy. The paper acknowledges this; surfacing it earlier (e.g., in the introduction) would better contextualize results. Overall, novelty is incremental relative to closely related work, but the cross-task validation and systematic comparator analysis provide sufficient new insights to merit acceptance.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not central to the decision)
- Word order sensitivity: Since the preprocessing for longer sequences is order-insensitive, consider briefly discussing this limitation in the introduction or discussion, and clarifying whether order-aware preprocessing (e.g., LSTM/GRU) was tried for the QA tasks.
- Attention formulation and citation: The attention mechanism appears closer to Luong et al. (bilinear/general) than to Bahdanau et al. Please cite Luong et al. (2015) or another directly relevant source for this specific formulation.
- Neural tensor network capacity: Given NTN’s large parameter count and overfitting risk, did you try a reduced output dimension m (i.e., an l×l×m tensor) to decouple input and output sizes?
- Relation to prior element-wise matching: SubMultNN is very similar to the heuristic matching used for sentence-level comparison in Mou et al. (2016). A brief clarification of the relationship would help delineate novelty at the word-level matching stage.
- Parameter sharing in preprocessing: Equation (1) appears to share preprocessing parameters between question and answer. Is there a reason not to use separate parameters, given that the two roles may require different gating (e.g., downweighting stop words in questions vs. answers)?

Questions for the authors
- Did you experiment with order-aware preprocessing (e.g., LSTM/GRU) on the longer-sequence tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA)? If so, how did performance and efficiency trade off? If not, could you comment on expected gains?
- For the NTN comparator, did you evaluate a smaller output dimension m to mitigate overfitting? If yes, how did it compare to SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN?
- What motivated sharing (or not sharing) preprocessing parameters between questions and answers in Eq. (1)? Did separate parameter sets help?
- Could you clarify the precise attention scoring function and confirm it follows Luong’s general/bilinear form?
- Can you provide parameter counts and training times across the six comparison functions to contextualize performance vs. efficiency?

Assessment against key questions
1) Problem: Matching and comparing text sequences to assess semantic relatedness for QA and entailment tasks.
2) Motivation and placement: Well-motivated; builds on and extends recent compare-aggregate work with broader validation and a focused study of comparison functions.
3) Support for claims: Yes. Results across four datasets, with ablations, support the efficacy of the framework and the superiority of element-wise comparison functions in many settings. Limitations (order insensitivity) are acknowledged.
