- Paper proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for sequence matching across QA and entailment tasks.  
- Focuses on choice of word-level comparison functions within attention-based alignment and CNN aggregation.  
- Evaluates six comparators: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN.  
- Experiments on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI show simple element-wise ops (SUB, MULT, SUB+MULT) often outperform complex comparators.  
- Achieves strong or state-of-the-art results on several QA benchmarks and competitive results on SNLI.  
- Ablation studies indicate preprocessing gates and attention help; attention especially benefits when sequence lengths differ.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Key reason 1: Limited novelty — compare-aggregate and attention-based matching are well-established; contribution is mainly a systematic comparison of comparators.  
- Key reason 2: Insufficient analysis/insight — lacks qualitative/theoretical explanation for why SUB/MULT work and lacks stronger controls and significance testing.  
- Supporting: Prior work already uses similar designs; architectural changes here are incremental engineering choices.  
- Supporting: Need controlled comparisons holding attention, aggregation, depth, and parameters constant, plus significance testing and targeted analyses.  
- Supporting: Implementation choices (fixed embeddings, order-insensitive preprocessing on some tasks) may limit interpretability and confound results.  
- Supporting: Baseline comparisons use reported numbers rather than unified reimplementations, making attribution of gains uncertain.  
- Supporting: SNLI results are competitive but not SOTA; QA gains may be dataset-dependent (e.g., lexical overlap).  
- Suggested improvements: add deeper qualitative/error analysis on where SUB/MULT succeed or fail (synonymy, antonymy, negation, compositionality).  
- Suggested improvements: include statistical significance, variance across runs, and learning curves.  
- Suggested improvements: run controlled studies with unified baselines, matched parameter counts, and report computational cost and parameter efficiency.  
- Suggested improvements: explore richer attention/aggregation (bidirectional/multi-hop attention, order-sensitive preprocessing) and hyperparameter/embedding sensitivity analyses.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity to attention direction and CNN aggregation choices?  
- Questions for authors: were comparator parameter counts controlled, and do conclusions hold when matched?  
- Questions for authors: why were embeddings not fine-tuned and does fine-tuning alter comparator rankings?  
- Questions for authors: how much gain in SUBMULT+NN comes from SUB/MULT vs. the NN layer; what about simple concatenation?  
- Questions for authors: provide statistical significance for dataset improvements?  
- Questions for authors: why add LSTM preprocessing only for SNLI and not QA tasks to test order-sensitivity?  
- Questions for authors: do element-wise methods hold up on low-overlap/paraphrase pairs or compositional inference?  
- Questions for authors: what are runtime and memory profiles across comparison functions and accuracy trade-offs?  
- Overall: useful empirical study and strong baseline but lacks sufficient novelty and analysis for acceptance.
