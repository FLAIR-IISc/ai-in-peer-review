This paper proposes a compare–aggregate model for NLP tasks that require semantic comparison of text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The core architecture applies attention over LSTM encodings of the sequences, performs an element-wise comparison on the attentive representations, and then uses a convolutional neural network to aggregate the results.

The main contribution is the systematic evaluation of different comparison functions for matching text sequences. In particular, the paper shows that element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally yield better performance than alternatives across four datasets. This empirical finding is clearly presented and supported by results.

However, the work feels incremental and offers limited novelty. The overall framework—attention over LSTMs followed by element-wise comparison and CNN-based aggregation—is a relatively straightforward composition of known components. While the empirical comparisons are useful, the paper would be stronger with deeper analysis.

In particular, a qualitative evaluation of the comparison functions would add insight. It would be helpful to see how subtraction, multiplication, and other comparison operators behave on varied types of sentence pairs (e.g., paraphrases vs. contradictions, lexically similar vs. syntactically divergent cases). Such analysis could clarify why certain operators work better and under what conditions, and would make the contribution more informative beyond reporting aggregate accuracies.
