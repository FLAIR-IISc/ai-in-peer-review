This paper proposes a general framework for natural language understanding tasks that involve two textual inputs that can be aligned (e.g., question–passage). The method first computes soft attention to align tokens across the two texts. A comparison module then consumes the aligned pairs (the attention query and its attended counterpart) to produce local matching representations, which are subsequently aggregated with a CNN into a single vector used for prediction. The paper presents the overall modeling strategy and provides a careful empirical study of the comparison component.

The work is timely. Pairwise language understanding remains a central challenge in NLP and has only recently become tractable with representation learning. The proposed approach is straightforward, sensible, and shown to be effective. While the contribution is somewhat incremental relative to prior work (including the authors’ earlier work and Parikh et al.), the paper adds sufficient empirical depth and modeling clarity to warrant acceptance.

Specific comments and suggestions:
- Word order sensitivity: For tasks with longer sequences (all except SNLI), the model as instantiated is order-insensitive. Although it performs competitively, this limitation likely caps its achievable performance. The paper notes this, but given its importance, it deserves a brief, explicit mention in the introduction or discussion, along with a short rationale for when order-insensitive models are expected to work well and when they may fail.
- Attention citation: The attention mechanism appears closer to the bilinear/general form of Luong et al. (2015) than to the additive formulation of Bahdanau et al. Please cite the former (or an equivalently direct reference) to better situate the method.
- NTN capacity control: Given the Neural Tensor Network’s high parameter count and overfitting risk, did you try a version with input dimension l but a smaller number of tensor slices m (i.e., an l × l × m tensor, with m < l)? Alternatively, a low-rank factorization or weight tying could offer similar expressivity with fewer parameters. Reporting such variants would strengthen the analysis of the comparison module.
- Relation to prior matching functions: The SubMultNN comparison function closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper you cite. Please acknowledge this connection explicitly and clarify what is novel in your formulation or training setup.
- Parameter sharing in preprocessing: In Equation (1), you appear to share parameters for preprocessing the two inputs (question and answer/passage). Is there a reason to tie these weights? The two inputs may emphasize different lexical and syntactic cues; relaxing parameter sharing, or at least reporting a comparison, could be informative.

Overall assessment: A clear, well-executed paper that articulates a practical alignment–comparison–aggregation recipe and backs it with solid empirical analysis, especially of the comparison layer. Although the high-level design aligns closely with recent work, the careful study and strong results justify publication. I recommend acceptance.
