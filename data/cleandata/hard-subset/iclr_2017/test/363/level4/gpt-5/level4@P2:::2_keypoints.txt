- Presents a compare-aggregate framework that aligns words via attention, compares word pairs, and aggregates comparisons with a CNN for prediction.
- Main contribution: systematic study of six word-level comparison functions, showing element-wise SUB and MULT (optionally with a small NN) are strong.
- Evaluated on four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) with competitive or state-of-the-art results.
- Ablations show preprocessing gates and attention improve performance, with importance varying by task.
- Positions work within compare-aggregate literature and demonstrates cross-task applicability of the architecture.
- Decision: Accept — clear framework, strong empirical results, and useful study of comparison functions.
- Key reason 1: Strong, consistent empirical performance using simple, interpretable element-wise comparisons.
- Key reason 2: Thorough cross-task evaluation and ablations that inform design choices in compare-aggregate models.
- Problem framed: effective word-level matching with attention and CNN aggregation, focusing on underexplored comparison function choice.
- Literature placement: engages prior work (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise interaction) and adds systematic comparison function analysis.
- Evidence: favorable results vs. strong baselines; SUB/MULT often outperform NN/NTN; ablations support component importance.
- Need for stronger evidence: request for statistical significance, more hyperparameter and tuning details, and fairness of comparisons.
- Presentation feedback: fix typo, improve Figure 2 resolution/legibility, and justify one-directional attention plus CNN aggregation choices.
- Experimental rigor: report statistical significance, per-dataset hyperparameters, early stopping, run counts/variance, and embedding fine-tuning effects.
- Efficiency: discuss training/inference time, memory, and impact of freezing vs. fine-tuning embeddings.
- Baseline comparability: clarify preprocessing, embeddings, and candidate set differences with prior work.
- Analysis requests: fuller ablations (no preprocessing, no attention, both removed) and per-task error analyses/qualitative examples.
- Contextualization question: assess whether lightweight contextualization (small CNN/shallow BiRNN) changes outcomes compared to order-insensitive preprocessing.
- Specific questions on preprocessing: sensitivity to gating dimensionality and full ablation tables per dataset.
- Comparison-function questions: effect of normalizing vectors, effect of tj dimensionality on CNN capacity, and overfitting differences between parameter-free and parameterized functions.
- Aggregation questions: why CNN over pooling/RNN, and comparisons to bidirectional attention with symmetric aggregation.
- Training details questions: were results averaged over runs; report mean/std; effect of fine-tuning embeddings.
- Baselines/fairness questions: confirm comparable embeddings and candidate pools or re-implementations to equalize setups.
- Efficiency questions: provide training/inference times, peak memory for largest dataset, and scaling with sequence length (MovieQA).
- SNLI-specific question: quantify the contribution of LSTM preprocessing relative to order-insensitive preprocessing.
- Figure 2 request: provide higher-resolution visuals and quantitative proxies (e.g., saliency scores) to support qualitative claims.
