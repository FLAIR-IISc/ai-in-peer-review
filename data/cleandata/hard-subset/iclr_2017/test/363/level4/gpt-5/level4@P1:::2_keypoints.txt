- Proposes a general compare-aggregate framework that compares attended representations at the word level then aggregates via a CNN.  
- Systematically studies six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT, especially SUBMULT+NN) outperform NN/NTN modules and achieve strong or SOTA results on three QA datasets and competitive SNLI performance.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason for acceptance: broad, convincing empirical evaluation showing generality and effectiveness of the compare-aggregate paradigm.  
- Key reason for acceptance: clear, actionable insight that simple element-wise operations work best, valuable for the community.  
- Paper is clear, well organized, with a clean architecture and focused comparison of comparison functions.  
- Results demonstrate robustness across tasks (answer selection, ranking, textual entailment) with top performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA and competitive SNLI.  
- Ablations indicate preprocessing (gating) and attention materially contribute; their relative importance varies with sequence length disparity.  
- Using a simple CNN for aggregation provides efficiency while capturing local patterns.  
- Code release improves reproducibility and adoption.  
- Presentation issues: minor typo and Figure 2 needs higher resolution, larger fonts, clearer labels, and better annotations for readability.  
- Ablation/analysis requests: provide per-dataset ablation numbers (no preprocessing, no attention, both) and a sensitivity analysis of CNN kernel sizes and filter counts versus simpler aggregators.  
- Method details to add: justify fixing GloVe embeddings and report impact of fine-tuning; compare LSTM preprocessing vs order-insensitive preprocessing across tasks.  
- Related work: clarify relation to bidirectional attention models and pairwise-interaction CNNs, and contrast one-directional attention/CNN choices with alternatives.  
- Questions for authors include: per-dataset ablations for preprocessing/attention; quantified attention gains for length-imbalanced tasks; sensitivity of SUB/MULT to embedding dimensionality and fine-tuning; effect of vector normalization; rationale and sensitivity for one-layer CNN vs RNN/self-attention/pooling; parameter counts and speed comparisons; impact of bidirectional attention; and whether conclusions hold with stronger contextual encoders.
