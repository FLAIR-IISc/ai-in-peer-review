Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences, targeting tasks such as textual entailment and question answering (answer selection and machine comprehension). The model first applies a lightweight gating-based preprocessing to word embeddings, computes one-directional attention to align tokens across the two sequences, compares each aligned pair with a chosen comparison function, and aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN to produce the final decision. The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions—standard feed-forward (NN), neural tensor network (NTN), Euclidean+cosine (EUCCOS), and three element-wise options (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN). Across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), the paper finds that simple element-wise functions, especially the combination of subtraction and multiplication (SUBMULT+NN), consistently perform competitively or best, often outperforming NN/NTN-based comparison layers. The paper also includes ablations indicating the importance of preprocessing and attention. The work is clearly written, broadly applicable, and provides useful empirical guidance on designing comparison functions.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
1) The technical novelty is limited. The overall architecture—attention-based compare-aggregate—is well established in prior work; the main contribution is an empirical comparison of comparison functions and a modest architectural choice of CNN for aggregation.
2) The analysis, while useful, stops short of explaining when and why element-wise operations work best. The empirical study lacks significance testing, parameter/computation controls across comparison functions, and deeper qualitative or theoretical insights.

Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper tackles the general problem of matching text sequences by aligning and comparing token-level representations and aggregating the matches to make a decision. This is central to entailment and QA tasks.
- Motivation and placement: The motivation is sound—sequence-level pooling can miss fine-grained correspondences; compare-aggregate remedies this by word-level alignment and comparison. The paper correctly situates itself among attention-based models (e.g., Rocktäschel et al., Wang & Jiang’s match-LSTM, Parikh et al.’s decomposable attention, He & Lin’s pairwise word interaction), and highlights the underexplored role of the comparison function. That said, the architectural template and even the idea of simple element-wise heuristics have precedents (e.g., cosine/dot product, element-wise operators), so the novelty chiefly lies in breadth of empirical evaluation.
- Evidence for claims: The experimental scope is a strength: four datasets spanning different tasks, with consistent implementation details, and ablations on attention and preprocessing. Results are generally strong (best on three datasets; competitive on SNLI). However:
  • No statistical significance or variability (e.g., multiple runs with different seeds), making it hard to judge robustness of small gains.
  • Comparisons among NN/NTN vs element-wise functions do not control for parameter count and output dimensionality. For example, EUCCOS produces 2-D vectors while others produce l-D vectors, which could bias downstream CNN capacity.
  • Some design choices may limit generality or fairness: fixed (non-updated) GloVe embeddings, order-insensitive preprocessing in three of four tasks, and one-directional attention.
  • Baselines are taken from the literature without re-implementation; differences in preprocessing, embeddings, or training regimes may confound comparisons.

Overall, the paper provides a helpful empirical message—that simple element-wise comparisons are strong baselines in compare-aggregate architectures—but does not yet meet the novelty and analytical depth expected for acceptance.

Suggestions to improve (not necessarily part of the decision)
- Provide statistical rigor: multiple runs, confidence intervals, and significance tests for key comparisons, especially where differences are small.
- Control for capacity and compute across comparison functions (e.g., match output dimensionality and total parameter counts; report FLOPs and training time). Include a variant where EUCCOS outputs are projected to l dimensions for a fair comparison.
- Offer deeper analysis of why SUB and MULT work: e.g., symmetry/antisymmetry properties, invariances, gradient behavior, and connections to bilinear forms or distance metrics.
- Add qualitative and error analyses: per-phenomenon breakdowns (negation, paraphrase, lexical overlap, length disparity) and visualizations of attention and comparison activations across successful and failed cases.
- Strengthen architectural comparisons: evaluate alternative aggregators (e.g., RNN/LSTM vs CNN) under identical settings; explore bi-directional attention.
- Report sensitivity to using contextual encoders (LSTM/GRU) in preprocessing on all datasets, not only SNLI; assess the effect of fine-tuning embeddings.
- Clarify and clean up references and formatting; ensure code includes exact hyperparameters and data preprocessing for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are the conclusions to parameterization? If you equalize the output dimension and parameter count across comparison functions, do SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN still win?
2) Did you run multiple seeds? Are the improvements statistically significant, particularly on SNLI and WikiQA?
3) Why were embeddings kept fixed? Does fine-tuning change the relative ranking of comparison functions?
4) Can you provide per-phenomenon breakdowns on SNLI and WikiQA (e.g., contradiction vs entailment, lexical overlap buckets) to illustrate where element-wise methods help?
5) For EUCCOS, if you follow the 2-D vector with a learned projection to l dimensions (or a small MLP) to balance capacity, how does it compare?
6) What is the computational trade-off between comparison functions (training time, inference latency)? Are element-wise functions faster as well as better?
7) How crucial is one-directional attention? Does making attention bi-directional alter the ranking of comparison functions?
8) For tasks with long contexts (MovieQA), does adding contextual encoders in preprocessing materially change performance or the relative merits of comparison functions?
9) Could you compare directly to Parikh et al. under matched settings (same embeddings, optimizer, training budget) to isolate the contribution of the comparison function and CNN aggregator?

I appreciate the clear exposition and the cross-task evaluation. With stronger analysis and tighter experimental controls, this work could offer a more compelling, generalizable insight into word-level comparison design within compare-aggregate architectures.
