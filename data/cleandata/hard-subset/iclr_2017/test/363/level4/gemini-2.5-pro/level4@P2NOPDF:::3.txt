This paper proposes a general modeling framework for natural language understanding tasks that involve two distinct textual inputs which can be aligned, such as a question and a source document. In this framework, soft attention is first employed to compute alignments between the tokens of the two texts. Next, a comparison function operates on the resulting alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and their corresponding attended vectors) to produce a set of comparison representations. These representations are then aggregated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) into a single fixed-size vector, from which the final output is computed. The paper presents this as a versatile modeling strategy and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the model's comparison component, demonstrating its effectiveness.

This work is timely and addresses a significant open problem in NLP: language understanding tasks involving multiple textual inputs, which are becoming increasingly addressable with modern representation learning methods. The proposed framework is general, intuitive, and effective, achieving strong empirical results. While the work shares similarities with the authors' previous research and the work of Parikh et al. (2016), and could be considered borderline incremental, it introduces several substantial contributions. For these reasons, I strongly recommend acceptance.

**Detailed Comments and Questions:**

*   The model, as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (i.e., all tasks except SNLI), does not explicitly model word order within the aggregated representations. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, the insensitivity to word order likely imposes a strong upper bound on performance for many tasks. Given its significance, this point may warrant a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections for added clarity.

*   The attention mechanism described appears more closely related to the general/bilinear attention strategy from Luong et al. (2015) than the original formulation by Bahdanau et al. (2014). The authors should consider citing the former, or another more directly relevant reference, for this specific attention variant.

*   The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) component introduces a large number of parameters, which may pose a risk of overfitting. Did the authors experiment with a more constrained version of the NTN? For instance, one could use an NTN with an input dimension `l` and a smaller output dimension `m < l` (resulting in a tensor of size `l x l x m`) to reduce model capacity.

*   The "SubMultNN" comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy proposed in Mou et al. (2016), which is already cited. It would be beneficial to explicitly note this similarity in the text.

*   In Equation (1), the same feed-forward network (with shared parameters) is used to preprocess both the question and the source text. Is there a specific reason for this design choice? One might argue that the two inputs serve different roles and could benefit from separate, non-shared projections to capture their distinct semantic properties.
