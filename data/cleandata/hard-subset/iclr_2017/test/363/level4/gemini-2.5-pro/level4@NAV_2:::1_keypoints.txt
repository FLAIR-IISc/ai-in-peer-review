- Presents a "compare-aggregate" model that aligns sequences with attention, compares word pairs with six functions, and aggregates comparisons with a CNN for final prediction.  
- Primary contribution is a systematic empirical evaluation of six comparison functions (including NN, NTN, element-wise SUB/MULT), tested on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Key empirical finding: simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT, SUB+MULT+NN) often match or outperform more complex parameterized comparison functions.  
- Paper closely follows prior "compare-aggregate" work (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016) and is architecturally incremental (CNN aggregation vs. summation, one-directional attention).  
- Reviewer recommends rejection based on (1) high incremental novelty and (2) lack of deeper analysis explaining why simple functions work better.  
- Analysis in the paper is mostly quantitative and does not provide satisfying qualitative or theoretical insight into the success of SUB/MULT.  
- Suggested improvement: include qualitative examples showing where SUB/MULT succeed/fail versus NN/NTN to reveal handling of negation, antonymy, paraphrase, etc.  
- Suggested improvement: visualize intermediate comparison vectors (tj) for different functions to illustrate how word-pair matches are represented.  
- Suggested improvement: provide error analysis of the best model (SUBMULT+NN) to identify systematic failure modes across tasks.  
- Question: need more justification/analysis of the inductive biases introduced by element-wise operations and why they are well-suited for semantic matching.  
- Question: clarify whether LSTM-based order-sensitive preprocessing (used only for SNLI) was tested on other datasets and how that affects the claim that word-by-word comparison dominates.
