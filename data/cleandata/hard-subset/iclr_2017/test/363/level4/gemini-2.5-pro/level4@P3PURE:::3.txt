The paper presents a general modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks that involve two distinct, alignable text inputs, such as a question and a source document. The approach first uses soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts. A subsequent comparison function then utilizes these alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and their corresponding attended representations) to generate comparison vectors. Finally, these vectors are aggregated by a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a single representation from which the final output is computed. The paper presents this framework as a general and effective modeling strategy, and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the model's comparison component.

This work is timely, addressing a major open challenge in NLP: language understanding tasks involving multiple texts, which are now becoming tractable with representation learning methods. The proposed approach is general, straightforward, and reasonable, and the paper demonstrates that it can achieve strong empirical results. While the work has some overlap with prior art (e.g., the authors' own previous work and that of Parikh et al.), its contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance. I therefore strongly recommend that the paper be accepted.

**Detailed Comments:**

*   The model, as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (i.e., all tasks except SNLI), is not sensitive to word order. While empirically competitive, this insensitivity places a strong upper bound on its performance. The authors acknowledge this, but the limitation is significant enough that it should be mentioned more prominently, perhaps in the introduction or discussion.
*   The attention strategy appears to be more closely related to the general/bilinear alignment model of Luong et al. (2015) than to the work of Bahdanau et al. (2014). The authors should consider citing the former, as it seems to be a more direct and relevant reference.
*   The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) component has a large number of parameters, which introduces a risk of overfitting. Have the authors experimented with a variant that projects to a smaller output dimension `m` (i.e., using an `l x l x m` tensor) to mitigate this risk?
*   The SubMultNN comparison function is very similar to the sentence-level matching strategy proposed in Mou et al. (the paper by Lili Mou cited in the manuscript). This similarity should be explicitly noted.
*   In Equation (1), the same preprocessing parameters are used for both the question and the source text. Is there a specific reason for this design choice? It seems plausible that the two inputs might benefit from distinct transformations, as they could require different features to be weighted highly.
