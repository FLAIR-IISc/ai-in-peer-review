This paper introduces a general framework for natural language understanding tasks that involve aligning two distinct textual inputs, such as a question and a source text. The proposed approach first employs soft attention to compute alignments between the tokens of the two texts. A comparison function then operates on these alignments (represented as attention query-result pairs) to generate local comparison vectors. Finally, these vectors are aggregated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) into a single representation used for the final prediction. The paper makes two main contributions: it presents this modular architecture as a general and effective modeling strategy, and it provides a detailed empirical analysis of various choices for the crucial comparison component.

This work is timely. Understanding problems of this nature represent a major open challenge in NLP, and they are just beginning to be effectively addressed with representation learning methods. The authors present a general, intuitive approach and demonstrate that it can achieve strong results. While the work borders on incremental relative to previous research (including the authors' own and that of Parikh et al.), it makes several substantial contributions. For these reasons, I strongly recommend acceptance.

Detailed points:

-   The model, particularly as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), is insensitive to word order within each input text. While the model is empirically competitive, this design choice inherently limits its potential performance on tasks where word order is critical. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but its significance seems to warrant a brief mention in the introduction or concluding discussion.

-   The attention mechanism appears more closely related to the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than the earlier work of Bahdanau et al. (2015). The authors should consider citing the former or another more directly relevant reference for this attention variant.

-   Given that the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has a large number of parameters and is thus prone to overfitting, did the authors experiment with a rank-reduced version? For instance, one could use an input dimension `l` and a smaller output dimension `m`, resulting in a tensor of size `l x m x l`.

-   The authors may want to note that the SubMultNN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy proposed in the cited work by Mou et al.

-   In Equation (1), the same preprocessing parameters are used for both the question and the answer. Is there a specific reason for this parameter sharing? Since the two inputs have different roles (e.g., one is a query, the other is a context), they might benefit from separate, independently learned transformations.
