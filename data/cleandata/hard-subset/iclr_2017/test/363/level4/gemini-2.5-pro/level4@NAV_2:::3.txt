This paper introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of sequence matching tasks in natural language processing. The proposed model first uses an attention mechanism to create aligned, contextualized representations between the two input sequences at the word level. These word-level representations are then matched using one of several comparison functions, and the resulting comparison vectors are aggregated by a Convolutional Neural Network to produce a final prediction. The primary contributions are twofold: demonstrating the effectiveness of this general architecture across four distinct datasets, and providing a systematic empirical evaluation of different comparison functions, revealing that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches.

I recommend a strong ACCEPT for this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) The work provides a comprehensive empirical validation of the "compare-aggregate" architecture across multiple diverse tasks, demonstrating its general applicability. (2) The focused analysis and novel findings regarding the efficacy of different word-level comparison functions represent a valuable and practical contribution to the field.

This work is well-motivated, addressing the critical and timely problem of sequence matching which is fundamental to many NLP tasks like question answering and textual entailment. While the overall framework builds upon recent work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), the paper makes two significant contributions that justify its acceptance. First, by successfully applying a unified model to four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), it offers compelling evidence that "compare-aggregate" is a robust and generalizable strategy, a claim not substantiated by prior, more task-specific studies. Second, the paper's main technical contribution—the systematic investigation of comparison functions—is novel and insightful. The finding that simpler, parameter-free functions like element-wise subtraction and multiplication can be more effective than standard, fully-parameterized neural network layers is an important result that could guide future model design. These contributions are substantial enough to move the work beyond being merely incremental.

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I would also appreciate the authors' clarification on some of these during the rebuttal period.
*   The preprocessing layer described in Section 2.2 is order-insensitive for most tasks evaluated. While the paper acknowledges this and the results are strong, this property likely imposes a ceiling on performance. A brief mention of this limitation in the introduction or discussion could better frame the contribution.
*   The attention mechanism in Equation 2 appears to follow the general formulation from Luong et al. (2015) more closely than the original formulation from Bahdanau et al. (2014). Citing the more directly relevant work would be appropriate.
*   The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) comparison function has a large number of parameters (O(l^3)), making it prone to overfitting. Did the authors experiment with a lower-rank version, where the output dimension is smaller than the input dimension `l`, to mitigate this?
*   The SUBMULT+NN comparison function is conceptually similar to the sentence-level matching approach in Mou et al. (2016). It might be worth acknowledging this connection in the text.
*   In Equation 1, the same parameters (Wi, Wu, etc.) are used for preprocessing both the question (Q) and the answer (A). Is there a specific reason for this parameter sharing? It seems plausible that the two sequences might benefit from distinct transformations to highlight different types of information.
