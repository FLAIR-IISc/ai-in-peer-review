- Paper tackles sequence matching with a compare-aggregate model and systematically evaluates six comparison functions across four datasets.  
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT, and their combination) often outperform more complex parameterized functions.  
- Decision: Weak Reject.  
- Primary reason 1: Contribution seen as incremental since the architecture closely follows recent compare-aggregate work (e.g., Decomposable Attention).  
- Primary reason 2: Paper lacks deeper analysis or theoretical intuition explaining why element-wise operations work so well.  
- Prior work concern: Element-wise operations were used in Mou et al. (2016), reducing novelty.  
- Evidence critique: Visualization is limited and does not explain specific linguistic phenomena captured by SUB/MULT (e.g., antonyms, paraphrase handling).  
- Suggested improvement: Add qualitative/error analysis showing cases where SUB/MULT succeed or fail relative to NN/NTN to provide intuition.  
- Architectural question: Justify the use of a CNN for aggregation and consider ablations with LSTM or pooling methods.  
- Noted surprising result: Order-insensitive preprocessing worked for three of four tasks; recommend further exploration since it challenges assumptions about sequence order importance.  
- Questions for authors requested: provide intuition for SUB/MULT effectiveness, clarify novelty relative to Mou et al., and explain why ignoring word order in preprocessing is not detrimental.
