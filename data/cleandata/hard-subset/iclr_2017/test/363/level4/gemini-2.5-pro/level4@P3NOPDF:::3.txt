This paper introduces a general framework for natural language understanding tasks that involve reasoning over two distinct text inputs, such as a question and a context paragraph. The proposed model operates in three stages. First, it employs a soft attention mechanism to compute alignments between the tokens of the two texts. Second, a comparison function takes these alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and their corresponding weighted context vectors) to produce a set of comparison features. Finally, a convolutional neural network (CNN) aggregates these features across the entire sequence into a fixed-size vector representation, which is then used for the final prediction. The authors present this as a general and effective modeling strategy and provide a detailed empirical analysis of different choices for the comparison function.

This is a timely and valuable contribution. The class of language understanding problems addressed here represents a major challenge for NLP, and this work demonstrates a general, intuitive, and effective representation learning approach that achieves strong empirical results. While the overall architecture shares conceptual similarities with prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016, and the authors' own previous work), the paper makes several substantial contributions that warrant acceptance. In particular, the systematic exploration and empirical analysis of the comparison component is a significant strength, providing valuable insights for future research in this area. For these reasons, I strongly recommend acceptance.

Below are some detailed comments and questions for the authors:

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The model, particularly as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (i.e., all tasks except SNLI), processes the comparison vectors as an unordered set. While the model is empirically competitive, this design choice makes it insensitive to word order, which likely places a fundamental upper bound on its performance for more complex reasoning tasks. The authors are transparent about this limitation, but given its significance, it would be beneficial to briefly mention it in the introduction or the main discussion section to properly frame the model's capabilities and scope.

*   **Attention Mechanism Citation:** The attention mechanism described appears to be an instance of the general/bilinear dot-product attention, which is more directly related to the work of Luong et al. (2015) than the additive attention of Bahdanau et al. (2014). It would be appropriate to cite the Luong et al. paper or other relevant work that introduced this specific attention formulation.

*   **Relation to Prior Work (SubMultNN):** The proposed `SubMultNN` comparison function, which combines element-wise subtraction and multiplication, bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy described in Mou et al. (2015), which the paper already cites. It would be beneficial to explicitly acknowledge this similarity in the text.

*   **NTN Parameterization:** The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) is a powerful but highly parameterized comparison function, which can increase the risk of overfitting. Did the authors experiment with any parameter-reduction techniques for the NTN? For instance, reducing the number of tensor slices (the output dimensionality of the tensor layer) could offer a better trade-off between model capacity and generalization.

*   **Parameter Tying in Preprocessing:** Equation (1) appears to use the same MLP (`F`) for preprocessing both input texts (e.g., the question and the context). Was there a specific reason for this parameter sharing? Given that the two inputs often have different characteristics and roles (e.g., a query vs. a document), using separate, untied MLPs for each might allow the model to learn more specialized representations and could potentially improve performance. It would be interesting to know if this alternative was explored.
