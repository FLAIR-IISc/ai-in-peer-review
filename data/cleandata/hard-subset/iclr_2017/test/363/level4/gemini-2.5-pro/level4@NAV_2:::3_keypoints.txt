- Introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching that aligns words with attention, compares word pairs, and aggregates via CNN for final prediction.  
- Evaluates the architecture across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), demonstrating its generality and robustness.  
- Provides a systematic empirical comparison of multiple word-level comparison functions.  
- Finds that simple, parameter-free element-wise operations (e.g., subtraction, multiplication) often outperform more complex neural comparison functions.  
- Strong ACCEPT recommendation based on comprehensive validation and novel empirical findings.  
- Well-motivated work addressing a fundamental NLP problem (question answering, entailment).  
- Builds on prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016) but extends by showing cross-task generality and insightful comparison-function analysis.  
- Limitation: preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for most evaluated tasks, which may impose a performance ceiling and should be acknowledged.  
- Citation issue: attention mechanism resembles Luong et al. (2015) more than Bahdanau et al. (2014); cite the more relevant work.  
- NTN comparison function is high-parameter (O(l^3)) and prone to overfitting; consider or report lower-rank variants.  
- SUBMULT+NN is conceptually similar to sentence-level matching in Mou et al. (2016); acknowledge this connection.  
- Question about shared preprocessing parameters for Q and A (Wi, Wu, etc.): clarify rationale or consider separate transformations.
