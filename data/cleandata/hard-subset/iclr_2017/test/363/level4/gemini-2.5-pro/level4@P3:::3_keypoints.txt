- Presents a "compare-aggregate" framework: attention-align words, apply word-level comparison functions, aggregate matching vectors with a CNN for final prediction.
- Two main contributions: (1) demonstrates generality/effectiveness across diverse sequence-matching tasks; (2) systematic empirical evaluation of six comparison functions showing simple element-wise ops often outperform complex models.
- Decision: strongly recommend acceptance — a well-executed, valuable empirical paper.
- A single coherent architecture achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, supporting the framework's generality.
- Ablation/analysis of comparison functions is a core scientific contribution; SUBMULT+NN performs best while parameter-free SUB and MULT are highly competitive.
- Findings challenge the assumption that more complex, parameter-heavy components (e.g., NN, NTN) necessarily yield better performance.
- Evaluation across four diverse tasks (large-context QA, large-pool answer selection, answer ranking, sentence-pair classification) is a major strength validating broad applicability.
- State-of-the-art results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA are particularly impressive.
- Architectural limitation: preprocessing layer is largely insensitive to word order, which may cap performance on syntax-sensitive tasks and should be discussed.
- Attention description should cite Luong et al. (2015) for the bilinear/general attention implementation rather than Bahdanau et al. (2014).
- Note similarity to Mou et al. (2016): SUB and MULT are previously used and SUBMULT+NN resembles their sentence-level matching.
- Concern about NTN: very large parameter count (O(l^3)) likely causes overfitting; ask whether lower-rank/simplified NTN variants were tried.
- Question about shared preprocessing parameters (Wi, Wu, biases) for both sequences—untied transformations for Q and A might be beneficial; request justification.
