This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The core idea is to perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then aggregate these comparison signals using a Convolutional Neural Network. The main contributions are twofold: first, the paper demonstrates the effectiveness and generality of this framework by applying a single model architecture to four different tasks (answer selection, machine comprehension, and textual entailment), achieving strong results. Second, it provides a systematic investigation into six different comparison functions, revealing the surprising efficacy of simple, element-wise operations over more complex neural network-based functions.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  The work provides strong, multi-task empirical evidence for the generalizability of the compare-aggregate paradigm, which is a valuable contribution to the field.
2.  The systematic analysis of comparison functions yields an insightful and practical result: that simple element-wise functions can outperform more complex, parameterized models like standard neural networks for this sub-task.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper's claim of generality is well-supported by its comprehensive evaluation. By testing the same core architecture on four distinct datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), the authors show that their model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across the board. This contrasts favorably with many prior works that propose architectures tailored to a single task, and it strengthens the argument that compare-aggregate is a powerful and broadly applicable framework for sequence matching problems.

Furthermore, the focused investigation into the comparison function `f` is a key strength. The paper methodically evaluates six alternatives, ranging from complex, learnable functions (NN, NTN) to a simple, fixed function (EUCCOS) and novel element-wise operations (SUB, MULT). The finding that SUBMULT+NN performs best overall, and that even non-parametric functions like SUB and MULT are highly competitive (Table 3), is a significant result. This provides a clear and useful takeaway for practitioners and challenges the common default of using a full feedforward network for such interactions. The ablation study in Table 4 also effectively demonstrates the utility of the preprocessing and attention components of the model.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for my decision.
*   The paper is well-written and clear. A minor typo was noted on page 4: "including a some" should be "including some".
*   The discussion on the order-insensitive preprocessing layer is interesting. While the current results are strong, a brief discussion on the potential trade-offs between this efficient approach and a more context-aware (but computationally expensive) RNN-based preprocessor for tasks other than SNLI would be insightful.
*   Figure 2 is difficult to read, especially in a printed version. Please consider increasing the resolution or using a more distinct color map to improve its clarity and impact.

**Questions for Authors:**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following:
*   The ablation study in Table 4 is very helpful. It shows the results of removing the preprocessing and attention layers separately. Could you also provide results for a baseline model that has neither the preprocessing layer nor the attention layer? This would establish a clearer baseline for the "compare-aggregate" components alone.
*   A major finding is that simpler comparison functions are highly effective. Could you provide a brief analysis of the parameter counts and computational overhead introduced by each of the six comparison functions? This would further bolster the argument for using simpler, element-wise methods.
