### **Summary**

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language tasks that involve matching two text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The proposed model first uses an attention mechanism to create aligned, context-aware representations for each word in one sequence relative to the other. It then applies a comparison function at the word level to produce a sequence of matching vectors. Finally, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) aggregates these vectors to produce a final representation for classification or ranking. The authors make two primary contributions: first, they demonstrate the general effectiveness of this framework by achieving strong or state-of-the-art results across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). Second, they provide a systematic empirical analysis of six different comparison functions, revealing the surprising effectiveness of simple, parameter-free functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication over more complex, parameterized neural network approaches.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend **Accepting** this paper.

1.  The paper successfully establishes the "compare-aggregate" architecture as a general and powerful framework for a wide variety of sequence matching tasks. This claim is substantiated by strong empirical performance across four distinct and challenging benchmarks, moving the architecture beyond a task-specific solution to a more broadly applicable modeling principle.
2.  The systematic investigation into different comparison functions provides a valuable and non-obvious insight for the community. The finding that simple, element-wise operations can outperform heavily parameterized functions like Neural Networks and Neural Tensor Networks is an important contribution that can inform future model design, suggesting that carefully chosen architectural priors can be more effective than brute-force learning.

### **Supporting Arguments**

This work is well-motivated and timely, addressing the important problem of semantic matching between text sequences. While the overall framework builds upon recent ideas seen in papers like Parikh et al. (2016), this paper's contributions are substantial and distinct. Its key strength lies in its breadth and systematic analysis.

First, by applying a unified architecture to four different tasks—spanning machine comprehension, answer selection, answer ranking, and textual entailment—the authors provide compelling evidence for the framework's generality. This cross-task validation is a significant step forward from previous works that typically focus on one or two tasks. The model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on all datasets, demonstrating its practical effectiveness.

Second, the paper's core technical contribution is the focused ablation study on the comparison function. This is not merely an architectural detail; it is a critical investigation into *how* two word-level representations should be compared for semantic matching. The authors test a range of functions from the highly specific (Euclidean+Cosine) to the highly general (NN, NTN), and find that a middle ground—element-wise subtraction and multiplication—works best. This result is valuable because it suggests that preserving dimensional information (unlike Euclidean distance) while using simple, well-motivated similarity-like operations (like subtraction and multiplication) is a powerful inductive bias for these tasks. This challenges the common assumption that adding more learnable parameters to every component is always the optimal strategy.

The potential criticism of this work is that it may seem incremental. However, I believe the focus on systematically evaluating a key model component and rigorously testing the framework's generality across multiple tasks provides a clear and significant contribution that moves the field forward. The paper shifts the focus from proposing yet another novel architecture to understanding and refining an emerging, successful one.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Order Insensitivity:** The model, particularly as implemented for the longer-sequence QA tasks, is largely insensitive to word order within the preprocessing layer. While the results are excellent regardless, this is a significant architectural choice that limits the model's theoretical capacity for tasks requiring complex sequential reasoning. It would be beneficial to briefly acknowledge this limitation in the introduction or discussion section.
*   **Attention Citation:** The attention mechanism described in Equation (2) appears to be a form of bilinear or "general" attention, which is more directly related to the work of Luong et al. (2015) than the original formulation in Bahdanau et al. (2014). Citing the former would be more precise.
*   **Connection to Mou et al. (2016):** It may be worth explicitly noting that the SUBMULT+NN comparison function is conceptually similar to the sentence-level matching strategy used in the work by Mou et al. (2016), which you cite in the related work section. This helps to better situate your contribution within the literature.

### **Questions for the Authors**

Your response to these questions would help clarify my understanding of the work.

*   **NTN Regularization:** The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) comparison function has a very large number of parameters (O(l³)), which makes it prone to overfitting. Did you experiment with a lower-rank or factorized version of the tensor, for instance by projecting the output to a smaller dimension *m < l*? This could potentially regularize the NTN and improve its performance.
*   **Shared Preprocessing Parameters:** In Equation (1), the same weight matrices (Wi, Wu) and biases are used to preprocess both input sequences (e.g., the question Q and the answer A). Was there a specific motivation for sharing these parameters? Given that the sequences often have different roles (e.g., premise vs. hypothesis), it seems plausible that they might benefit from separate, non-shared transformations.
