- Presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework performing word-level comparisons followed by CNN aggregation for sequence matching tasks.
- Main contributions: (1) applies a single architecture to four tasks with strong results; (2) systematically evaluates six comparison functions, finding simple element-wise ops often outperform complex ones.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Reason 1: Strong multi-task empirical evidence demonstrating the framework's generality across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.
- Reason 2: Insightful finding that simple element-wise comparison functions can be more effective than parameterized neural functions for this sub-task.
- Supporting evidence: Same core model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance across datasets, contrasting with task-specific prior work.
- Supporting evidence: Evaluation of six comparison functions shows SUBMULT+NN best overall and non-parametric SUB and MULT highly competitive.
- Ablation study (Table 4) effectively demonstrates the value of preprocessing and attention components.
- Minor writing issue: typo on page 4 ("including a some" → "including some").
- Suggestion: Discuss trade-offs between the order-insensitive preprocessing layer and more context-aware (but costlier) RNN-based preprocessors for tasks beyond SNLI.
- Suggestion: Improve Figure 2 resolution/color map for better readability in print.
- Question 1 for authors: Please report results for a baseline model without both preprocessing and attention to clarify the standalone "compare-aggregate" contribution.
- Question 2 for authors: Provide parameter counts and computational overhead for each of the six comparison functions to support the claim favoring simpler element-wise methods.
