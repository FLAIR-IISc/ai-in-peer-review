- Paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching: word-level comparison (after attention) followed by CNN aggregation.  
- Primary contribution is a systematic empirical evaluation across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strong empirical results: the framework achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance across tasks with minimal task-specific tuning.  
- Key finding: simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex learned functions (NN, NTN).  
- Best-performing variant: SUBMULT+NN yields the strongest overall results; SUB and MULT are highly competitive and top on WikiQA.  
- Main strength is the thorough, well-executed empirical analysis rather than novel model components.  
- Ablation study shows the preprocessing and attention layers are important to performance.  
- Suggestion: add qualitative analysis showing examples where SUB/MULT succeed and NN/EUCCOS fail to explain why.  
- Suggestion: analyze the gated preprocessing layer (e.g., visualize gate values to see if stop words get low gates).  
- Suggestion: report parameter counts and computational complexity to highlight efficiency benefits of parameter-free comparisons.  
- Suggestion: explicitly state which preprocessing (LSTM or not) was used for each of the three non-SNLI tasks for clarity.
