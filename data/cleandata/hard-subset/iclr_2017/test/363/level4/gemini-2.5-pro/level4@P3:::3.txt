### Review Summary

This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language sequence matching tasks, such as question answering and textual entailment. The model first uses an attention mechanism to create aligned vector representations for words in the two sequences. It then applies a comparison function at the word level to produce a sequence of matching vectors. Finally, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) aggregates these matching vectors to make a final prediction. The two main contributions of this work are: (1) demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of this framework by achieving strong results across four different and diverse datasets, and (2) providing a systematic empirical evaluation of six different comparison functions, revealing that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based functions.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Strongly recommend acceptance.

This is a well-executed and valuable empirical paper that makes two significant contributions to an important area of NLP.
1.  **Generality and Effectiveness:** The paper convincingly shows that a single, coherent "compare-aggregate" architecture can achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across four distinct sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). This provides strong evidence for the general applicability of this modeling paradigm.
2.  **Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The systematic ablation study of the comparison function is a key strength. It yields the non-obvious and highly useful finding that simple, parameter-efficient functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication outperform more complex, heavily-parameterized functions like standard neural networks or neural tensor networks. This insight is valuable for practitioners and researchers building future models for similar tasks.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

While the high-level "compare-aggregate" structure has appeared in recent work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), this paper moves beyond an incremental contribution by providing a broad, multi-task validation and a deep-dive into a critical model component.

The evaluation on four datasets is a major strength. These tasks are not trivial variations of one another; they encompass machine comprehension with a large context (MovieQA), answer selection from a large pool (InsuranceQA), answer ranking (WikiQA), and sentence-pair classification (SNLI). The fact that the proposed model performs so well across these varied settings validates the authors' claim that this is a general and effective framework. The results, particularly the state-of-the-art performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, are impressive.

The core scientific contribution lies in the analysis of the comparison functions. The paper methodically tests six options, ranging from general function approximators (NN, NTN) to well-motivated similarity metrics (EUCCOS) and a middle ground of element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The discovery that the SUBMULT+NN variant performs best, and that even parameter-free functions like SUB and MULT are highly competitive, is a significant finding. It challenges the default assumption that more complex, learnable components are always superior and provides a clear, practical recommendation for designing similar models. This kind of careful, focused empirical analysis is extremely valuable to the research community.

### Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision. I would also be interested in the authors' responses to the questions.

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The model, particularly in its preprocessing layer for the QA tasks, is largely insensitive to word order. While the empirical results are clearly strong, this is a notable architectural limitation that places an upper bound on performance for tasks where syntax and word order are critical. The authors do mention this, but it seems salient enough to warrant a brief discussion of its potential implications in the introduction or conclusion.
*   **Attention Citation:** In Section 2.2, the attention mechanism described appears to be a direct implementation of the 'general' or 'bilinear' attention from Luong et al. (2015), rather than the mechanism from Bahdanau et al. (2014) which is conditioned on the previous decoder state. Citing the Luong et al. paper would be more precise.
*   **Similarity to Mou et al. (2016):** The authors correctly cite Mou et al. for prior use of the SUB and MULT functions. For completeness, it may also be worth noting that the proposed SUBMULT+NN function is very similar in spirit to the sentence-level matching strategy used in that same work.
*   **Question on NTN:** The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has a very large number of parameters (O(l^3)), making it prone to overfitting, which might explain its relatively poor performance. Did you experiment with a lower-rank or simplified version of the NTN, for instance one that projects to a smaller output dimension *m* (using a tensor of size l x l x m)? This could provide a more controlled comparison against the other functions.
*   **Question on Shared Preprocessing Parameters:** In Equation (1), the same weight matrices (Wi, Wu) and biases are used to preprocess both input sequences (Q and A). Is there a specific reason for this parameter sharing? It seems plausible that the two sequences (e.g., a question vs. a candidate answer) might have different characteristics and could benefit from separate, un-tied preprocessing transformations. Could you comment on this design choice?
