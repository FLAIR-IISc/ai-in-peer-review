This paper presents a general modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks that involve aligning two distinct textual inputs, such as a question and a source document. The approach first uses soft attention to align tokens between the two texts. A subsequent comparison function then processes these alignments—represented as pairs of attention queries and results—to generate representations. These representations are aggregated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) into a single vector to produce the final output. The paper presents this as an effective general modeling strategy and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the model's comparison component.

This work is timely, addressing a major open challenge in NLP: language understanding tasks that require aligning two texts, which are now becoming tractable with representation learning methods. The authors propose a straightforward and sensible general approach and demonstrate its ability to achieve strong empirical results. While the work is arguably incremental when compared to the authors' previous work or that of Parikh et al., its contributions are substantial enough to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

Detailed points:
- The model, particularly as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (i.e., all tasks except SNLI), is insensitive to word order. Although the model is empirically competitive, this insensitivity imposes a hard ceiling on its potential performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it is significant enough that it should be briefly mentioned in the introduction or discussion.

- The attention mechanism appears to be more closely related to the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than the earlier work of Bahdanau et al. (2015). The authors should consider citing the former or another more directly relevant reference for this strategy.

- The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has a large number of parameters, which creates a risk of overfitting. Have the authors experimented with a version that projects the inputs to a smaller dimension, e.g., using a tensor of size l x l x m where m < l?

- The authors should consider noting the strong resemblance between their SubMultNN component and the sentence-level matching strategy proposed in the cited work by Mou et al.

- What is the rationale for using shared parameters to preprocess both the question and the answer in Equation (1)? It seems plausible that the two inputs would benefit from separate parameterizations, as they might require different features to be emphasized.
