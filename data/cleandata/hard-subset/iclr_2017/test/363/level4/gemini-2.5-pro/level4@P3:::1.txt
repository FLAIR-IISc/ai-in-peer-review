### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, a fundamental problem in many NLP tasks such as question answering and textual entailment. The model first performs a word-level comparison between two sequences (after an attention step) and then aggregates the results using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction. The primary contribution of this work is a systematic and extensive empirical evaluation of this framework across four different datasets. Specifically, the authors rigorously compare six different comparison functions, ranging from standard neural networks to simple distance metrics. The key finding is that comparison functions based on simple element-wise operations, namely subtraction and multiplication, are highly effective and often outperform more complex, parameterized alternatives like neural networks or neural tensor networks.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Strong and Generalizable Empirical Results:** The paper provides a convincing demonstration that the "compare-aggregate" framework is a powerful and versatile approach for sequence matching. By achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across four diverse and challenging datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), the authors substantiate their claim about the framework's general effectiveness.
2.  **Clear and Actionable Findings on Comparison Functions:** The systematic study of different comparison functions yields a valuable and non-obvious insight: simple, parameter-free element-wise operations can be more effective than more complex, learned functions for word-level matching. This is a useful finding that can guide future research and practical applications in this domain.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The main strength of this paper lies in its thorough and well-executed empirical evaluation. While the individual components of the model (attention, CNNs) are not novel, the contribution is in the systematic analysis, generalization, and the insights drawn from it.

The authors motivate the work by noting that previous "compare-aggregate" models were often tested on only one or two tasks and did not deeply investigate the core comparison function. This paper directly addresses these gaps. The choice of four datasets, spanning machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment, provides strong evidence for the model's robustness and broad applicability. The fact that the same general architecture, with minimal task-specific tuning, performs so well is a significant result.

The core of the paper is the analysis of comparison functions. The authors hypothesize that functions like element-wise subtraction (SUB) and multiplication (MULT) offer a good compromise between overly-restrictive metrics (EUCCOS) and overly-general learned functions (NN, NTN). The results in Table 3 strongly support this hypothesis. Across the board, SUBMULT+NN performs best, and the simpler SUB and MULT functions are themselves highly competitive, even achieving the best results on the WikiQA dataset. This finding challenges the common assumption that more parameters and complexity are always better, providing a clear, practical takeaway for the community. The ablation study further reinforces the design choices, demonstrating the utility of both the preprocessing and attention layers.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

*   **Qualitative Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The current analysis is primarily quantitative. A qualitative analysis would significantly strengthen the paper's claims. For instance, could you provide examples of sentence pairs where the element-wise functions succeed while others (e.g., EUCCOS or NN) fail? This could offer deeper intuition into *why* these functions work well. What kinds of semantic relationships (e.g., synonymy, antonymy, relatedness) are best captured by SUB and MULT?
*   **Analysis of the Preprocessing Layer:** The gated preprocessing layer (Eq. 1) is shown to be important in the ablation study. It would be helpful to include some analysis of what this layer learns. For example, does it learn to assign low gate values to stop words, as hypothesized? Visualizing the gate values for a few examples could provide valuable insight.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** A key advantage of the SUB, MULT, and EUCCOS functions is that they are parameter-free or have very few parameters compared to NN and NTN. This makes them computationally more efficient. A brief discussion of the parameter counts and computational complexity of the different comparison functions would be a valuable addition and would further bolster the argument for using simpler element-wise operations.
*   **Clarity on LSTM Usage:** The paper states that LSTM was incorporated into the preprocessing for the SNLI task but not for others to avoid high computational costs. This is a reasonable choice, but it would be good to explicitly state which preprocessing formulation was used for each of the other three tasks in the main text for clarity.

Thank you for this solid piece of empirical work. Your findings on comparison functions are particularly interesting and useful for the NLP community.
