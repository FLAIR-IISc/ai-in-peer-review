This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language inference and sentence similarity tasks. The model first decomposes the problem into a word-level matching step, where various comparison functions are applied to contextualized word representations. The resulting comparison vectors are then aggregated using a convolutional neural network to produce a final classification. The authors conduct a systematic study of six different comparison functions and provide an extensive empirical evaluation on four benchmark datasets, comparing their results against numerous published baselines.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and easy to follow. A major strength is the comprehensive experimental evaluation. The systematic comparison of different comparison functions is particularly valuable and provides useful insights into what types of word-level interactions are most effective for these tasks. The results presented are strong and demonstrate the competitiveness of the proposed framework.

**Weaknesses and Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The contribution of the initial preprocessing and attention step is not entirely clear. To better understand the model's components, an ablation study is needed. Could the authors provide results for the model without this step? This would help quantify its impact on overall performance.
*   The paper could be strengthened by a more in-depth analysis of *why* certain comparison functions outperform others on specific tasks. While the empirical results are well-presented, adding more intuition or qualitative analysis would provide a deeper understanding.

**Minor Comments:**
