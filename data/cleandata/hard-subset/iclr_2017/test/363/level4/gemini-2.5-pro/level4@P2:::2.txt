### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The core idea is to first perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then aggregate these comparison signals using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction. The primary contributions of this work are twofold. First, it demonstrates the broad applicability and strong performance of this framework by evaluating it on four different datasets spanning tasks from question answering and machine comprehension to textual entailment. Second, and more significantly, the paper conducts a systematic and insightful analysis of six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations. The authors find, compellingly, that simple, parameter-efficient functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex and parameter-heavy alternatives like standard neural networks or neural tensor networks.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that I recommend for acceptance. The key reasons for my decision are:
1.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides extensive experimental evidence across four distinct and challenging benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results, convincingly demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of the proposed "compare-aggregate" framework.
2.  **Valuable Investigation of Comparison Functions:** The systematic evaluation of different word-level comparison functions is a significant contribution. The finding that simpler, element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) can be more effective than more complex neural models is an important and practical insight for the research community, suggesting that carefully chosen, well-motivated architectural components can be superior to more generic, black-box approaches.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The authors correctly identify a trend in recent literature towards "compare-aggregate" models but note that previous work has often been task-specific and has not deeply investigated the crucial comparison component. This paper successfully addresses both limitations.

The strength of the empirical results provides robust support for the paper's claims. By testing on four datasets with different characteristics—answer selection from a pool (InsuranceQA), answer ranking (WikiQA), entailment classification (SNLI), and comprehension involving a third context document (MovieQA)—the authors make a powerful case for the framework's versatility. The fact that the same core architecture, with minor task-specific adaptations, performs at the state-of-the-art level across these diverse problems is a major strength.

The central contribution, in my view, is the methodical analysis of the comparison function `f(a, h)`. The paper thoughtfully categorizes the options, from highly flexible, parameterized models (NN, NTN) to a highly constrained, low-dimensional one (EUCCOS). The exploration of the middle ground with element-wise SUB and MULT is insightful. These functions are intuitive, as they are closely related to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity, respectively, yet they preserve the high-dimensional information from the word embeddings. The results in Table 3 clearly show that these simpler functions are not just adequate but are often superior, with the SUBMULT+NN variant achieving the best overall performance. This is a valuable lesson in model design.

Finally, the inclusion of an ablation study (Table 4) that analyzes the impact of the preprocessing and attention layers strengthens the paper's conclusions by demonstrating that each component of the proposed model contributes positively to the final performance.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   **Figure 2:** The visualization in Figure 2, while a good idea for providing qualitative insight, is difficult to read due to its low resolution, particularly when printed. Please consider regenerating this figure at a higher quality and perhaps using a more distinct color map or clearer labels to highlight the key takeaways.
*   **Discussion on Preprocessing Layer:** The paper notes that the order-insensitive preprocessing layer works well, which is an interesting finding. A brief discussion on the trade-offs between this simpler approach and more computationally expensive, order-sensitive methods like LSTMs (which were only used for SNLI) would be beneficial. It would be helpful to hypothesize when the simpler approach is sufficient versus when a more complex sequential encoder is necessary.
*   **Attention Mechanism:** The attention mechanism is one-directional (from sequence A to sequence Q). It would be interesting to see a short discussion on this design choice, especially in relation to other work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016) that uses a bi-directional attention mechanism.
*   **Minor Typo:** On page 4, in the first sentence of Section 3.1, the phrase "including a some" should be corrected to "including some".

**Questions for Clarification:**
*   The ablation study in Table 4 effectively demonstrates the utility of the preprocessing and attention layers. Could you elaborate on your finding that the relative importance of these layers seems to vary by task? For instance, why might the attention layer be more crucial for tasks with large length differences (MovieQA/InsuranceQA) while the preprocessing layer is more important for others (WikiQA/SNLI)?
*   Could you provide some insight into the computational complexity and training time differences between the various comparison functions? One of the appealing aspects of SUB, MULT, and EUCCOS is their apparent simplicity and lower parameter count compared to NN and NTN. Quantifying this benefit in terms of training speed or model size would further strengthen the paper's conclusions.
