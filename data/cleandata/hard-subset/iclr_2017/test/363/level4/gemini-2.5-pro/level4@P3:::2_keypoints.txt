- Presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework applied to multiple sequence-matching tasks, showing a single unified model works across tasks.  
- Main contributions: demonstrating model versatility on four datasets and systematically evaluating six word-level comparison functions.  
- Central empirical finding: simple, parameter-free element-wise subtraction and multiplication (and SUBMULT+NN) often outperform more complex parameterized comparison functions (NN, NTN).  
- Strong empirical validation: state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Insight: preserving dimensional information via element-wise comparisons is a crucial inductive bias for similarity tasks.  
- Paper is well-written and situates the work within existing literature.  
- Recommendation to present ablation results (preprocessing and attention removal) in a dedicated table to quantify component contributions.  
- Request to regenerate Figure 2 at higher resolution for clearer qualitative interpretation.  
- Suggest briefly discussing trade-offs of the current order-insensitive preprocessing versus more context-aware preprocessors (CNN/BiLSTM).  
- Minor typo: change "including a some uncommon ones" to "including some uncommon ones".  
- Question: explain why NTN underperforms—overfitting from many parameters or unsuitable inductive bias?  
- Question: clarify why word embeddings were not updated—design choice or fine-tuning worsened performance?
