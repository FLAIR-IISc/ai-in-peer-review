This paper presents a general approach to modeling natural language understanding problems involving two distinct textual inputs that can be aligned in some way, such as a question and a source text. The approach utilizes soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts. A comparison function then uses the resulting alignments, represented as pairs of attention queries and attention results, to derive representations that are aggregated by a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a single vector from which an output can be computed. The paper not only presents this as an overall modeling strategy that can be made to work quite well but also offers a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

This work is timely, as language understanding problems of this kind are a major open issue in natural language processing (NLP) and are just at the threshold of being addressable with representation learning methods. The work presents a general approach that is straightforward and reasonable, and demonstrates that it can yield good results. Although the work may be considered incremental relative to the authors' earlier work or that of Parikh et al., it contributes in enough substantial ways that I would strongly recommend acceptance.

However, there are a few details that warrant discussion. Firstly, the model, at least as implemented for problems with longer sequences (everything but SNLI), is not sensitive to word order. While it is empirically competitive, this insensitivity places a strong upper bound on its performance. The paper does acknowledge this limitation, but it seems salient enough to warrant a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections.

Secondly, the attention strategy appears to be more closely based on the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than on the earlier Bahdanau work. It would be beneficial to cite the former or some other more directly relevant reference for that strategy.

Thirdly, since the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) risks overfitting due to its large number of parameters, it would be interesting to know whether the authors tried using a version with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (resulting in an l x l x m tensor).

Additionally, it is worth noting that the SubMultNN model bears a strong resemblance to the strategy for sentence-level matching in the Lili Mou paper cited by the authors.

Lastly, the authors use the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1). However, these could potentially require different things to be weighted highly, and it would be beneficial to explore this possibility.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of natural language understanding, and its strengths outweigh its limitations.
