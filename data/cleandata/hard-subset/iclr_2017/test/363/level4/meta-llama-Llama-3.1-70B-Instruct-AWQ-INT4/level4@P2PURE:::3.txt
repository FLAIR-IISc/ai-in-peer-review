The paper proposes a general framework for modeling natural language understanding problems involving two distinct textual inputs, such as a question and a source text, that can be aligned in some way. This approach utilizes soft attention to establish alignments between the tokens of the two texts, followed by a comparison function that leverages these alignments, represented as pairs of attention queries and attention results, to derive representations. These representations are then aggregated by a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a single vector, from which an output can be computed. The paper presents this overall modeling strategy as effective and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

This work is timely, as language understanding problems of this kind are a significant open issue in NLP, and are just beginning to be addressable with representation learning methods. The proposed approach is straightforward and reasonable, yielding good results. Although the work may be considered incremental relative to the authors' earlier work or that of Parikh et al., it contributes substantially enough to warrant strong recommendation for acceptance.

Several details are worth noting:

- The model, as implemented for problems with longer sequences (all except SNLI), is insensitive to word order. While empirically competitive, this insensitivity imposes a strong upper bound on its performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it is significant enough to merit a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections.

- The attention strategy appears to be more closely based on the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. '15 than on the earlier Bahdanau work. A citation to the former or a more directly relevant reference for this strategy would be appropriate.

- Given the risk of overfitting due to the large number of parameters in the Neural Tensor Network (NTN), it would be useful to explore a version with an input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (resulting in an llm tensor).

- The SubMultNN model bears a strong resemblance to the strategy for sentence-level matching in the Lili Mou paper cited by the authors. This similarity should be acknowledged.

- The use of the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1) may not be optimal, as these could require different weights.
