This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for NLP tasks that require semantically comparing text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The model's basic framework involves applying a convolutional neural network (aggregation) after an element-wise operation (comparison) over the attentive outputs of the LSTMs. The comparison component is the paper's main focus, where several different methods for matching text sequences are compared, and element-wise subtraction/multiplication operations are shown to achieve generally better performance on four different datasets.

I decide to **accept** this paper with two key reasons for this choice. First, the paper presents a thorough evaluation of different comparison functions for matching text sequences, which is a valuable contribution to the field of NLP. Second, the experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model on four different datasets, showing its generalizability and competitiveness with state-of-the-art models.

The supporting arguments for the reasons of my decision are as follows. The paper's comparison of different comparison functions, including element-wise subtraction/multiplication operations, neural networks, and neural tensor networks, provides a comprehensive understanding of their strengths and weaknesses. The experimental results, which show that element-wise subtraction/multiplication operations can achieve better performance than more complex models, are particularly noteworthy. Additionally, the paper's evaluation on four different datasets demonstrates the model's ability to generalize across different tasks and datasets.

However, I also have some additional feedback to help improve the paper. While the paper's experimental results are thorough, a qualitative evaluation of how different comparison functions perform on varied kinds of sentences would be more interesting and provide deeper insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each method. Furthermore, the paper's discussion of the model's limitations and potential future work could be more detailed and specific.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper and provide additional evidence for my assessment are: (1) Can the authors provide more details on how they selected the four datasets used in the evaluation, and whether they considered other datasets or tasks? (2) How do the authors plan to extend their model to handle more complex tasks or datasets, such as multi-task learning or tasks with longer sequences? (3) Can the authors provide more insights into why element-wise subtraction/multiplication operations performed better than more complex models, and whether this is due to the specific characteristics of the datasets or tasks used in the evaluation?
