This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for NLP tasks that require semantically comparing text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment.

The paper claims to contribute to the field of natural language processing by presenting a general "compare-aggregate" framework that is effective for a wide range of sequence matching problems. Specifically, the authors argue that their model, which follows this framework, achieves state-of-the-art performance on four different datasets, namely MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. Furthermore, they claim that their systematic evaluation of different comparison functions shows that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions.

I accept this paper. The key reasons for my decision are the thorough evaluation of the proposed model on multiple datasets and the systematic comparison of different comparison functions. The authors provide a clear and well-motivated approach to sequence matching problems, and their results are well-supported by the experimental evaluation.

The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their model and the comparison functions they evaluate. The experimental evaluation is thorough, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the results. The paper also includes a good discussion of related work and clearly positions the proposed approach within the context of existing research.

One potential weakness of the paper is that the proposed model is not particularly innovative. The "compare-aggregate" framework is not new, and the authors' contribution is primarily in evaluating and comparing different comparison functions within this framework. However, the authors do provide a thorough and well-motivated evaluation of their approach, and the results are interesting and well-supported.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors provide a more qualitative evaluation of the comparison functions they evaluate. For example, they could provide an analysis of how different comparison functions perform on different types of sentences or how they capture different aspects of semantic similarity. This would provide a more nuanced understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of each comparison function and would make the paper more interesting and impactful.

Additionally, I would like the authors to answer the following questions:

* Can you provide more details on how you selected the specific comparison functions you evaluated? Were there other comparison functions that you considered but did not evaluate, and if so, why?
* How do you think the results of your evaluation would change if you used a different aggregation method, such as a recurrent neural network instead of a convolutional neural network?
* Can you provide more analysis of the results on the SNLI dataset? Why do you think the proposed model did not achieve state-of-the-art performance on this dataset, despite achieving strong results on the other datasets?
