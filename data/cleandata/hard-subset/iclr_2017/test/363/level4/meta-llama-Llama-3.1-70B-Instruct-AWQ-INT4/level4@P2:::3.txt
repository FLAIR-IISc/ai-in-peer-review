**Summary of the Paper**

The paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which is effective for various natural language processing tasks, including question answering and textual entailment. The framework consists of four layers: preprocessing, attention, comparison, and aggregation. The authors propose six different comparison functions, including neural network, neural tensor network, Euclidean distance, cosine similarity, subtraction, and multiplication. They evaluate their model on four datasets, showing that the framework achieves competitive performance, and some simple comparison functions can outperform more complex ones.

**Decision**

Based on the evaluation of the paper, I recommend acceptance. The work is timely, and the approach is straightforward and reasonable. The paper contributes substantially to the field by presenting a general framework and analyzing the comparison component of the model.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper presents a well-motivated approach to natural language understanding problems with two distinct textual inputs. The framework is general and can be applied to various tasks. The authors provide a thorough empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model, which is a key contribution of the paper. The results show that the framework achieves competitive performance, and some simple comparison functions can outperform more complex ones.

**Additional Feedback**

1. The model is not sensitive to word order, which places a strong upper bound on its performance. This should be mentioned in the introduction or discussion sections.
2. The attention strategy is based on the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. '15, which should be cited.
3. The authors should consider using a version of the neural tensor network with a smaller output dimension to reduce the risk of overfitting.
4. The SubMultNN comparison function is similar to the strategy for sentence-level matching in the Lili Mou paper, which should be noted.
5. The authors should consider using different parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1).

**Questions for the Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the limitation of the model not being sensitive to word order?
2. Can the authors provide more details on why they chose to use the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1)?
3. How do the authors think their framework can be extended to other natural language understanding tasks?
