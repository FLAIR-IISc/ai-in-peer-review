The paper proposes a general approach to modeling natural language understanding problems involving two distinct textual inputs that can be aligned, such as a question and a source text. This approach employs soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts, followed by a comparison function that utilizes these alignments to produce representations. These representations are then aggregated into a single vector using a convolutional neural network (CNN), from which an output can be computed. The paper not only presents this overall modeling strategy, demonstrating its effectiveness, but also provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

This work is timely, as language understanding problems of this kind are a major open issue in NLP, and are now becoming addressable with representation learning methods. The proposed approach is straightforward and reasonable, yielding good results. Although the work builds upon earlier research, including the authors' own previous work and that of Parikh et al., it contributes substantially enough to warrant strong consideration for acceptance.

However, there are a few points worth noting. Firstly, the model, as implemented for problems with longer sequences (all datasets except SNLI), appears to be insensitive to word order. While this approach is empirically competitive, this insensitivity may limit its performance. Although the paper acknowledges this limitation, it would be beneficial to mention it briefly in the introduction or discussion sections to provide context.

Regarding the attention strategy, it appears to be more closely related to the general/bilinear approach of Luong et al. (2015) than the earlier work by Bahdanau. A citation to Luong et al. (2015) or a more relevant reference for this strategy would be appropriate.

Additionally, considering the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has a large number of parameters, which may lead to overfitting, it would be interesting to explore a version with a smaller output dimension (i.e., an l x l x m tensor). The authors may want to discuss whether they experimented with such a configuration.

Furthermore, the SubMultNN model bears a resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou paper cited by the authors. A brief acknowledgement of this similarity would be helpful.

Finally, the authors may want to justify their decision to use the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1). It is possible that these two inputs may require different weights, and exploring this aspect could lead to further improvements.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of natural language understanding, and with some revisions to address these points, it has the potential to make an even more significant impact.
