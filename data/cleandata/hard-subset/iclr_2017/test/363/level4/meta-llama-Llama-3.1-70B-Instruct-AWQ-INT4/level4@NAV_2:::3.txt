The paper presents a general approach to modeling for natural language understanding problems with two distinct textual inputs that can be aligned in some way. In the approach, soft attention is first used to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts, then a comparison function uses the resulting alignments to derive representations that are aggregated by CNN into a single vector from which an output can be computed. The paper both presents this as an overall modeling strategy that can be made to work quite well, and offers a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

I recommend accepting this paper, with the decision based on two key reasons. First, the paper presents a timely and relevant contribution to the field of natural language processing, addressing a major open issue in NLP. Second, the work is well-motivated, well-placed in the literature, and demonstrates a clear understanding of the problem domain.

The paper supports its claims through a thorough evaluation of the proposed model on four different datasets, representing different tasks. The results show that the model achieves competitive performance on these tasks, and that the comparison component of the model is effective in capturing the semantic similarity between the input texts.

Some supporting arguments for the decision include the fact that the paper presents a general approach that can be applied to different tasks, and that the model is able to capture the semantic similarity between the input texts through the comparison component. Additionally, the paper provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component, which is a key contribution of the work.

To further improve the paper, I suggest considering the following points:

* The model's insensitivity to word order may be a limitation, and it would be worth discussing this in more detail in the introduction or discussion sections.
* The attention strategy used in the paper is based on the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. '15, and it would be worth citing this work explicitly.
* It would be worth exploring the use of a version of the NTN with a smaller output dimension to reduce the risk of overfitting.
* The SubMultNN comparison function bears some resemblance to the strategy used in the Lili Mou paper, and it would be worth noting this explicitly.
* It would be worth considering the use of different parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1), as these may require different things to be weighted highly.

Some questions I would like the authors to clarify include:

* Can the authors provide more detail on why they chose to use the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1)?
* How do the authors plan to address the limitation of the model's insensitivity to word order in future work?
* Can the authors provide more detail on the computational resources required to train the model, and how this might impact its scalability to larger datasets?
