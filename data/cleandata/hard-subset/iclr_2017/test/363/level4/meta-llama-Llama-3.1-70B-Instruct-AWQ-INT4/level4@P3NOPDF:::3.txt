The paper presents a general approach to modeling natural language understanding problems that involve two distinct textual inputs, such as a question and a source text, which can be aligned in some way. This approach leverages soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts, followed by a comparison function that utilizes the resulting alignments, represented as pairs of attention queries and attention results, to derive representations. These representations are then aggregated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) into a single vector, from which an output can be computed. The paper not only presents this overall modeling strategy, demonstrating its potential to achieve good results, but also provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

This work is timely, as language understanding problems of this kind are a major open issue in Natural Language Processing (NLP) and are just beginning to be addressable with representation learning methods. The proposed approach is straightforward and reasonable, making it a valuable contribution to the field. Although the work may be considered incremental relative to the authors' earlier work or that of Parikh et al., it contributes in substantial ways, warranting strong consideration for acceptance.

One notable aspect of the model, at least as implemented for problems with longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), is its insensitivity to word order. While the model is empirically competitive, this limitation places a strong upper bound on its performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it is salient enough to warrant a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections to provide context for readers.

Regarding the attention strategy, it appears to be more closely based on the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than on the earlier work of Bahdanau et al. A citation to Luong et al. or another relevant reference for this strategy would be appropriate to provide proper attribution.

The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) component of the model risks overfitting due to its large number of parameters. It would be interesting to know whether the authors experimented with a version of NTN that uses a smaller output dimension (e.g., an l x l x m tensor), which could help mitigate this issue.

The SubMultNN comparison function bears a resemblance to the strategy for sentence-level matching presented in the work of Lili Mou, which is cited in the paper. Acknowledging this similarity could provide additional context for readers and help clarify the novelty of the proposed approach.

In Equation (1), the same parameters are used for preprocessing the question and answer. However, these two inputs may require different weighting schemes, and it would be beneficial to explore the use of separate parameters for each input to potentially improve the model's performance.

Overall, the paper presents a valuable contribution to the field of natural language understanding, and its empirical analysis provides useful insights into the comparison component of the model. With some minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, this work has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
