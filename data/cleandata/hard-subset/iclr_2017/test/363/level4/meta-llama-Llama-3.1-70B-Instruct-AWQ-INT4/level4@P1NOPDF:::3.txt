The paper presents a general approach to modeling natural language understanding problems involving two distinct textual inputs, such as a question and a source text, that can be aligned in some way. This approach leverages soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts and then employs a comparison function to compute representations from the resulting alignments, represented as pairs of attention queries and attention results. These representations are then aggregated into a single vector using a convolutional neural network (CNN), from which the output can be computed. The paper not only introduces this overall modeling strategy, demonstrating its effectiveness, but also provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

This work is timely, addressing a major open issue in NLP: language understanding problems that are just beginning to be tackled using representation learning methods. The proposed approach is straightforward and reasonable, yielding good results. While the work may be considered incremental relative to the authors' earlier work or that of Parikh et al., it contributes substantially enough to warrant strong recommendation for acceptance.

In terms of details, a few points are worth noting:

- The model, as implemented for problems with longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), appears to be insensitive to word order. Although it achieves competitive results empirically, this insensitivity imposes a strong upper bound on its performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it seems significant enough to merit a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections.

- The attention strategy employed in this work appears to be more closely related to the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than to the earlier work of Bahdanau. It would be beneficial to cite the former or another more directly relevant reference for this strategy.

- Given that the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) risks overfitting due to its large number of parameters, it would be interesting to explore a version with an input dimension of l and a smaller output dimension m (resulting in an l x l x m tensor). Did the authors consider this possibility?

- The SubMultNN model bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy presented in the Lili Mou paper cited by the authors. It would be helpful to acknowledge this similarity.

- The authors use the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in equation (1). However, these might require different weights. Is there a specific reason for using the same parameters in this case?
