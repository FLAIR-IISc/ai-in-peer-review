This paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework that performs word-level matching followed by aggregation with convolutional neural networks. It compares six different comparison functions and evaluates them on four datasets. Extensive experimental results have been reported and compared against various published baselines.

The paper is well written overall.

A few detailed comments:
* page 4, line 5: "including a some" -> "including some"
* What's the benefit of the preprocessing and attention step? Can you provide the results without it?
* Figure 2 is hard to read, especially when printed in hard copy. Please enhance the quality.

Decision: Accept

Reasons: The paper presents a well-motivated and well-executed study on the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching problems. The authors provide a thorough evaluation of six different comparison functions on four datasets, and the results are carefully analyzed and compared against various published baselines.

Supporting arguments:
* The paper tackles a specific and well-defined problem in natural language processing, namely sequence matching.
* The authors provide a clear and well-motivated explanation of the compare-aggregate framework and its application to sequence matching problems.
* The evaluation is thorough and well-executed, with a careful analysis of the results and a comparison against various published baselines.
* The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the technical details.

Additional feedback:
* It would be helpful to provide more insight into the benefits of the preprocessing and attention step. While the authors mention that these steps are important, it would be useful to see some quantitative results that demonstrate their effectiveness.
* The visualization in Figure 2 is difficult to read, especially when printed in hard copy. It would be helpful to enhance the quality of the figure or to provide a clearer explanation of what the visualization is intended to show.

Questions for the authors:
* Can you provide more insight into the benefits of the preprocessing and attention step? Are there any specific challenges or limitations that these steps are intended to address?
* How do you think the compare-aggregate framework could be extended or modified to address other sequence matching problems? Are there any specific applications or domains that you think would be particularly well-suited to this framework?
