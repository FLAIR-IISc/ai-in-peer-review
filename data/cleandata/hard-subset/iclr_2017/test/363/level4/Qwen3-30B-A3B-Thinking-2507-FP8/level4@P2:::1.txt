

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, including question answering and textual entailment. The authors systematically evaluate different comparison functions for matching word-level representations between sequences, demonstrating that simple element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication) outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.

Decision: Reject

Key reasons for rejection: 1) The work is largely incremental with limited novelty, as the overall framework closely follows prior "compare-aggregate" approaches (Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016), and the element-wise comparison functions they evaluate have been previously used (Mou et al., 2016). 2) The empirical improvements are marginal (typically 0.3-1.5% across datasets) and lack sufficient theoretical justification for why these simple operations work better for semantic matching.

Supporting arguments: The paper's main contribution is the systematic evaluation of comparison functions within an established framework. While this empirical investigation is useful, it doesn't constitute a significant advance. The authors claim element-wise operations "work better," but fail to provide a compelling theoretical explanation for why these simple operations outperform more expressive neural network-based comparisons for semantic matching. The marginal improvements observed (e.g., SUBMULT+NN achieving 0.3-1.5% better accuracy than baselines) are not sufficiently strong to justify publication, especially when compared to more substantial advances in the field.

The paper also lacks a detailed analysis of how different comparison functions perform across different types of sequence matching tasks. For instance, why do element-wise operations work better for question answering tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) but not as well for textual entailment (SNLI)? Without such analysis, it's difficult to understand the broader implications of their findings.

Additional feedback: The paper would be significantly strengthened by providing a more detailed qualitative analysis of why element-wise operations work better for specific sequence matching tasks. For example, showing examples where subtraction/multiplication successfully capture semantic relationships that neural network-based approaches miss would add valuable insight. The authors should also clarify whether their observed improvements are statistically significant (the paper lacks proper statistical testing). Furthermore, a more thorough comparison with He & Lin (2016), who evaluated cosine similarity and Euclidean distance but not element-wise operations, would better position their work.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper claims element-wise operations work better for semantic matching, but provides no theoretical justification. Can you elaborate on why these simple operations might be better suited for measuring semantic similarity between word representations in sequence matching tasks?

2. The paper reports marginal improvements (0.3-1.5%) over baselines. Are these differences statistically significant? If not, how do you justify the significance of these results?

3. The authors note that their model outperforms previous work despite using an order-insensitive preprocessing layer. Can you provide more insight into why word-by-word comparison is so effective in these tasks, even without contextual information?

4. The paper evaluates six comparison functions but doesn't explain why certain functions work better for specific tasks. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how different comparison functions perform across the four datasets, particularly for the different task types (question answering vs. textual entailment)?
