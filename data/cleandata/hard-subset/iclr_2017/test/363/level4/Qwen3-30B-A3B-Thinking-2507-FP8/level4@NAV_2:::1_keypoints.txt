- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework for text sequence matching and systematically evaluates six comparison functions (including SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).
- Empirical evaluation across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) shows element-wise methods perform strongly, with SUBMULT+NN often best.
- Authors claim simple element-wise comparison can outperform neural network-based alternatives while remaining broadly applicable.
- Reviewer recommends rejection due to insufficient novelty; primary contribution is seen as incremental within an established framework.
- Prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016) already validates compare-aggregate approaches, weakening novelty claims.
- Paper lacks clear articulation of how the specific comparison functions advance beyond existing literature.
- Empirical rigor is strong: diverse datasets, thorough ablations, and consistent performance gains reported.
- Paper does not address whether element-wise functions resolve prior limitations (e.g., efficiency, interpretability, task adaptability) or provide theoretical justification beyond experiments.
- Claim of framework generality is not fully substantiated—evaluation limited to four text datasets without exploring non-textual sequence matching.
- Suggestions to improve: explicitly contrast with prior work, provide qualitative analysis of SUB/MULT on specific sentence types (negation, idioms), and clarify generality to non-text tasks.
- Reviewer asks for clarification: (1) What specific limitations of prior compare-aggregate models does this work address to justify focusing on element-wise functions?
- Reviewer asks for clarification: (2) How do the element-wise functions improve interpretability or efficiency compared to neural alternatives, beyond accuracy gains?
