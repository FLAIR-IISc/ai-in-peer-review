

This paper introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which performs word-level matching followed by aggregation using convolutional neural networks. The authors focus on evaluating different comparison functions for matching word vectors across four distinct NLP tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. They demonstrate that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple datasets, making a significant contribution to understanding the role of comparison functions in sequence matching.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple datasets, revealing that element-wise operations can be more effective than standard neural approaches. The empirical analysis is comprehensive and well-executed, providing clear evidence for the effectiveness of their framework.

The paper's key strengths include its thorough evaluation across four diverse datasets, which demonstrates the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework beyond previous single-task studies. The systematic comparison of six different comparison functions provides valuable insights into the design of sequence matching models. The authors' finding that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural approaches challenges conventional wisdom and offers practical guidance for future work.

The paper would be strengthened by addressing a few points: First, the word order insensitivity of the model (particularly for longer sequences) is a notable limitation that should be acknowledged more prominently in the introduction or discussion. Second, the attention strategy appears to be based on Luong et al. (2015) rather than Bahdanau et al. (2014), so the paper should cite the more relevant reference. Third, the authors might consider exploring whether the neural tensor network could be simplified by reducing the output dimension to prevent potential overfitting. Fourth, the SubMult+NN comparison function bears strong resemblance to the approach in Mou et al. (2016) for sentence-level matching, which should be explicitly acknowledged. Finally, using the same parameters for preprocessing both the question and answer might be suboptimal, as these sequences may require different weighting schemes.

To clarify my understanding, could the authors explain why they chose to use the same preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer sequences? Additionally, how did they determine the optimal dimensionality for the comparison functions, particularly for SUB and MULT? Lastly, could you elaborate on the specific advantages of using a CNN for aggregation rather than simpler methods like summation or concatenation, especially given the word-order insensitivity of the model?
