- Paper systematically investigates the "compare-aggregate" framework and finds simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) often outperform neural-network based comparisons across four datasets.  
- Presents a unified model that applies various comparison functions to word-level attention outputs and aggregates via CNN, evaluating six comparison mechanisms on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Key contribution: empirical validation of the framework’s broad applicability and identification of parameter-efficient element-wise operations as effective alternatives to complex neural comparisons.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Comprehensive validation: four-dataset evaluation (QA and entailment) with strong InsuranceQA results and ablation studies showing attention and preprocessing layers are critical.  
- Novel, actionable insight: SUB/MULT (no extra parameters) often match or exceed NN/NTN, challenging the need for complex architectures.  
- Clear literature contribution: fills a gap by systematically comparing comparison functions and contrasting EUCCOS with neural variants.  
- Suggested improvement: add qualitative analysis (e.g., attention visualizations) to explain why element-wise ops succeed.  
- Suggested improvement: clarify SNLI performance and how model simplicity impacts entailment versus QA tasks.  
- Questions for authors: why does EUCCOS reach near-SOTA on MovieQA—task-specific reasons?  
- Question for authors: why does SUB (retaining dimensionality) outperform summed measures like EUCCOS—does preserved dimensionality retain critical information?
