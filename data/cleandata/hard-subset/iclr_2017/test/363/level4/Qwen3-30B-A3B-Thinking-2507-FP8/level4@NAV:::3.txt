

The paper introduces a general modeling framework for natural language understanding tasks involving two distinct textual inputs (e.g., a question and source text) that can be aligned. The approach first employs soft attention to establish token-level alignments between the two texts, then utilizes a comparison function on these alignments (represented as query-result pairs) to derive representations. These representations are aggregated via CNN into a single vector for output computation. The paper presents this as a viable overall strategy with strong empirical performance, complemented by a detailed analysis of the comparison component.  

This work is timely, addressing a significant open challenge in NLP where such language understanding problems are nearing resolution through representation learning. The proposed approach is straightforward, reasonable, and demonstrates competitive results. While the contribution borders on incremental relative to prior work (e.g., the authors' earlier studies or Parikh et al.), it provides substantial enough advances to warrant strong recommendation for acceptance.  

Details:  
- The model’s implementation for longer sequences (excluding SNLI) is insensitive to word order, which empirically yields competitive results but imposes a strict performance ceiling. Though acknowledged, this limitation merits brief inclusion in the introduction or discussion.  
- The attention strategy aligns more closely with Luong et al. (2015)’s bilinear approach than Bahdanau et al.’s method; a citation to Luong et al. or a more directly relevant reference should be added.  
- Given NTN’s risk of overfitting due to high parameter count, consider testing a variant with input dimension *l* and reduced output dimension *m* (i.e., an *l* × *m* tensor).  
- SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-matching strategy in the Lili Mou et al. paper cited.  
- Using identical parameters for preprocessing question and answer inputs in (1) may be suboptimal, as they likely require distinct weighting priorities.
