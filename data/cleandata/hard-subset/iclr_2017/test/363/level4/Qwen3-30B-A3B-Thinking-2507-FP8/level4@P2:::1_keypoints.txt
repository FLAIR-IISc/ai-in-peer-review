- Proposal: A "compare-aggregate" framework evaluating different comparison functions, claiming element-wise ops (subtraction, multiplication) outperform complex functions across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI.

- Decision: Reject.

- Reason 1: Largely incremental with limited novelty; framework and element-wise operations have prior art (Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016; Mou et al., 2016).

- Reason 2: Empirical improvements are marginal (0.3–1.5%) and lack theoretical justification for why simple ops work better.

- Main contribution: Systematic empirical evaluation of comparison functions within an established framework, not a substantial methodological advance.

- Critique: No compelling theoretical explanation for why simple element-wise operations outperform more expressive neural comparison functions.

- Weakness: Insufficient analysis of varying performance across task types (question answering vs. textual entailment), e.g., why gains appear in QA but not SNLI.

- Suggestion: Include qualitative examples showing cases where subtraction/multiplication capture semantic relations missed by neural methods.

- Suggestion: Report statistical significance tests to validate the reported marginal improvements.

- Suggestion: Provide a more thorough comparison with He & Lin (2016), who evaluated other similarity measures.

- Question: Can authors provide a theoretical justification for why element-wise operations measure semantic similarity effectively?

- Question: Are the reported 0.3–1.5% improvements statistically significant, and if not, how are they justified?

- Question: Why does word-by-word comparison work well despite using an order-insensitive preprocessing layer and limited contextual information?

- Question: Can the authors present a detailed analysis of how each comparison function performs across the four datasets and task types?
