

**Summary of Contributions**  
This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, such as textual entailment and question answering. The authors propose a model that first compares word-level representations from two sequences using various comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations), then aggregates the comparison results using a CNN. The key contribution lies in systematically evaluating six different comparison functions across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), demonstrating that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper establishes that the compare-aggregate framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, not just limited to specific domains.

**Decision**  
Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution by demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence matching tasks and providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions that reveals surprising insights about the effectiveness of simple element-wise operations. The empirical results are strong, and the findings have clear implications for future work in sequence matching.

**Key Reasons for Acceptance**  
1. **Generalizability**: The authors convincingly demonstrate that their compare-aggregate framework is effective across four distinct sequence matching tasks (three question answering and one textual entailment), addressing a key limitation in prior work that typically tested models on only one or two tasks.  
2. **Systematic Evaluation**: The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis of six comparison functions, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), showing that these simple approaches outperform more complex neural network-based methods across multiple datasets. This systematic comparison addresses an important gap in the literature.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's contributions are significant for two reasons. First, it establishes the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework beyond specific tasks, which is crucial for understanding the broader utility of this approach in sequence matching. Second, the empirical findings about comparison functions challenge conventional assumptions about the need for complex neural network-based comparisons, suggesting that simpler, more efficient operations can be equally or more effective. The experiments are well-designed, with thorough ablation studies showing the importance of different model components (e.g., attention and preprocessing layers) across tasks with varying sequence length differences. The results consistently show that the authors' framework achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance across all four datasets.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
1. The paper should more prominently discuss the limitation of order insensitivity in the preprocessing layer, as this places an important constraint on the model's capabilities. This limitation should be addressed in the introduction or related work to set appropriate expectations for readers.

2. The authors should clarify why they use identical parameters for preprocessing both sequences (equation 1). Different weighting schemes might be needed for the two sequences, and this could be an important consideration for future work.

3. The attention mechanism is described as "one-directional," but the paper doesn't clearly explain what this means in the context of the model. The authors should clarify whether this refers to directionality of attention (e.g., from Q to A versus bidirectional) or another aspect.

4. While the paper notes that SUBMULT+NN performs best, it could provide more intuition about why this combination works well, beyond simply stating it combines SUB and MULT.

**Questions for the Authors**  
1. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, which is an important limitation. How does this affect the model's ability to handle tasks where word order is critical (e.g., grammatical structures), and what trade-offs does this represent between performance and computational efficiency?

2. The authors note that their model achieves competitive results on SNLI but not state-of-the-art. What are the main limitations of their approach in this task, and how might these be addressed?

3. The paper demonstrates that element-wise operations like SUB and MULT work well, but doesn't provide much intuition about why. Could the authors elaborate on the semantic meaning of these operations and how they relate to the underlying sequence matching problem?

4. The authors use the same parameters for preprocessing both sequences (equation 1). Is there a theoretical or empirical basis for this decision, or would using different parameters for the two sequences yield better results?
