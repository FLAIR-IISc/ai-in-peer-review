

This paper introduces a compare-aggregate framework for sentence matching, leveraging word-level comparisons followed by convolutional aggregation. The authors systematically evaluate six distinct comparison functions across four benchmark datasets, reporting comprehensive results against state-of-the-art baselines. The work is well-structured, clearly motivated, and makes a solid contribution to the field of semantic matching.  

The paper is generally well-written, with logical flow and sufficient technical detail. However, three specific points require clarification or minor revision:  

1.  **Typo and phrasing**: On page 4, line 5, "including a some" should be corrected to "including some."  

2.  **Preprocessing and attention justification**: The preprocessing step and attention mechanism are integral to the framework, yet their specific contribution is not clearly articulated. The authors should provide ablation results demonstrating the impact of these components—specifically, performance comparisons *with* and *without* preprocessing and attention—to justify their inclusion. Without this, it remains unclear whether these steps provide marginal gains or are essential for the model’s effectiveness.  

3.  **Figure 2 readability**: Figure 2 (showing comparison function results) suffers from poor legibility in printed form, particularly due to small text and low-contrast elements. The authors should enhance the figure’s resolution, increase font sizes, and ensure sufficient contrast for clarity in both digital and hard-copy formats.  

The experimental evaluation is thorough, with results consistently supporting the proposed approach over baselines. The choice of comparison functions is well-justified, and the aggregation via CNNs provides a coherent mechanism for combining word-level matches. The four-dataset coverage (including SNLI, MultiNLI, etc.) strengthens the empirical claims.  

One minor suggestion: While the paper discusses "word-level matching," it could briefly clarify how the framework handles out-of-vocabulary words or varying sentence lengths during the comparison phase, as this is a common practical concern.  

Overall, this is a strong submission that advances the state of sentence matching. The core idea is sound, the experiments are rigorous, and the writing is clear. Addressing the minor points above—particularly the ablation study for preprocessing/attention and Figure 2’s readability—would significantly strengthen the paper.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The work makes a valuable contribution with clear methodology, thorough evaluation, and high-quality writing. The requested revisions are straightforward and do not undermine the paper’s core merits.
