

# Review for ICLR 2017

**Summary:** This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in natural language processing. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions (including two novel element-wise operations: SUB and MULT) across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The framework follows a standard structure of comparing word-level representations followed by aggregation with CNNs. The paper makes a compelling contribution by demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple sequence matching tasks, which is both surprising and potentially significant for future research.

**Decision:** Reject.

**Key reasons for rejection:** First, the paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for why element-wise comparison functions outperform neural network-based approaches. The authors observe that simpler functions work better but fail to provide any meaningful explanation for this phenomenon. Second, the paper does not adequately address the limitations of the proposed framework, particularly regarding generalizability beyond the specific datasets tested.

**Supporting arguments:** The paper's experimental evaluation is thorough and well-executed, with results showing that the proposed framework achieves competitive or superior performance on all four datasets compared to previous state-of-the-art methods. However, the key contribution—identifying that simple element-wise operations work better than more complex approaches—is undermined by the lack of theoretical justification. The authors merely state that "SUB and MULT are good middle ground" without explaining why this is the case. For instance, they note that SUB is related to Euclidean distance but fail to explain why preserving dimensionality information (as SUB does) is beneficial for sequence matching tasks. This theoretical gap is particularly problematic for a machine learning conference like ICLR, where theoretical understanding is highly valued.

Additionally, while the paper claims to demonstrate the general applicability of the framework across multiple tasks, it tests only four related datasets (all question answering or textual entailment tasks). The authors do not provide evidence that this framework would work equally well for other sequence matching problems with different characteristics (e.g., matching sequences with very different lengths or semantic structures). Without this broader validation, the claim of general applicability remains unsubstantiated.

The paper also fails to adequately address the limitations of the compare-aggregate framework. For example, the authors note that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but still outperforms previous work that uses order-sensitive preprocessing. This is an interesting finding that could lead to new insights about the importance of word-level comparison versus sequence-level representation, but the paper does not explore this implication sufficiently.

**Additional feedback to improve the paper:** 

1. The paper should provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of why element-wise comparison functions work better than neural network-based approaches. This could include analyzing the dimensionality of the comparison vectors, the information preserved in each approach, or the nature of the matching task itself.

2. The authors should conduct additional experiments to test the framework on more diverse sequence matching tasks beyond the four datasets they currently use. This would strengthen the claim of general applicability.

3. The paper should provide a more comprehensive comparison with the most recent state-of-the-art models on all four datasets, particularly those using transformer-based architectures, which have become dominant in NLP since 2016.

4. The authors should include ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison function, aggregation) to better understand which aspects of the framework are most critical.

**Questions for authors to clarify:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed theoretical explanation for why element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform neural network-based comparison functions? Specifically, what properties of sequence matching tasks make these simpler functions more effective?

2. How does your framework compare to the most recent state-of-the-art models on these datasets, particularly those using transformer architectures (e.g., BERT, which was introduced in late 2018 but was becoming prominent in 2016-2017)? Given that the paper was submitted for ICLR 2017, it would be important to compare with the best models available at that time.

3. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but still achieves good results. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, and whether this suggests that the word-level comparison aspect of the framework is more important than the sequence-level representation for these tasks?

This paper presents an interesting empirical finding that simple comparison functions can work better than more complex ones, but the lack of theoretical justification and insufficient validation of the framework's generalizability prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. With additional theoretical analysis and broader validation, this work could be significantly strengthened.
