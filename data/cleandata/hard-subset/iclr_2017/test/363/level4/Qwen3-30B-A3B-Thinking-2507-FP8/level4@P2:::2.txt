

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences that performs word-level matching followed by aggregation using convolutional neural networks. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations SUB and MULT) across four diverse NLP tasks: textual entailment (SNLI) and three question answering tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA). The key contribution is demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple sequence matching problems.

Decision: Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature by demonstrating the general applicability of the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple tasks and providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions that is lacking in prior work.

Supporting arguments: The paper effectively addresses two important limitations in existing sequence matching research: (1) most prior models are tested on only one or two tasks, while the authors show the framework's effectiveness across four different tasks; (2) previous work has not systematically evaluated comparison functions, whereas the authors compare six different approaches including novel element-wise operations. The results are compelling, showing that simple comparison functions (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) consistently outperform more complex neural network approaches across all four datasets. The ablation studies further strengthen the claims by demonstrating the importance of key components like the attention layer and preprocessing layer.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:
- The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, but it's unclear why this works better than order-sensitive approaches. A more detailed explanation would help readers understand this finding.
- The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer does not consider context information, yet the model still outperforms previous work with context-aware preprocessing. This is a significant finding that deserves more discussion.
- The ablation study in Section 3.3 is valuable, but it would be even stronger with results showing the impact of removing each component individually (preprocessing, attention, comparison function) rather than just removing them separately.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper claims the preprocessing and attention steps are important, but the ablation study only shows results when removing these layers separately. Could you provide results for removing both layers simultaneously to better understand their combined contribution?
2. The paper states that element-wise comparison functions work better than neural network-based approaches despite being less flexible. Is there a theoretical explanation for this counterintuitive finding, or is it simply an empirical observation?
3. For the MovieQA task, the paper mentions that the model can outperform previous work even without considering word order. Given that this is a question answering task where context matters, why does the order-insensitive approach still work well?

The paper is well-written with clear organization and a strong contribution to the field. The experimental results are comprehensive and well-supported, with thorough evaluation across multiple datasets and comparison against relevant baselines. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides valuable insights that will inform future research on sequence matching problems.

The minor issues noted (typo on page 4, line 5; Figure 2 readability) can be easily addressed in the camera-ready version. The paper makes a solid contribution to the field with well-supported claims, and the additional clarity from addressing the questions above would make it even stronger. The results are scientifically rigorous and the framework is general enough to be applied to many sequence matching problems beyond those tested in the paper.
