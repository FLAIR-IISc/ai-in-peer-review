

The paper introduces a general approach for natural language understanding tasks involving two aligned textual inputs (e.g., a question and source text). The method first employs soft attention to derive token-level alignments between the two texts, then applies a comparison function to the resulting alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and values). These comparisons are aggregated via CNN into a single vector from which the output is derived. The work presents this as a broadly applicable modeling strategy that achieves strong empirical results, accompanied by a detailed analysis of the comparison component.

This work is timely, addressing a major open challenge in NLP where representation learning methods are now approaching viability for such tasks. The proposed approach is straightforward, reasonable, and empirically effective. While the contribution borders on incremental relative to prior work (e.g., the authors' earlier work or Parikh et al.), it offers sufficient substantial advances to warrant strong acceptance.

Key points for the authors:  
- The model (as implemented for longer sequences, excluding SNLI) is insensitive to word order. Though this limitation is acknowledged, it imposes a strong upper bound on performance and merits brief mention in the introduction or discussion.  
- The attention mechanism appears closer to the general/bilinear strategy of Luong et al. (2015) than to Bahdanau et al., and the paper should cite Luong et al. (or a more directly relevant reference for this strategy).  
- Given NTN’s risk of overfitting due to its large parameter count, it would be valuable to investigate a variant with input dimension *l* and reduced output dimension *m* (i.e., an *l*×*l*×*m* tensor).  
- SubMultNN resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou et al. paper cited. This similarity should be noted.  
- The use of identical parameters for preprocessing both the question and answer in Equation (1) is questionable; these components may require distinct weighting schemes.
