

This paper introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which performs word-level matching followed by aggregation using convolutional neural networks. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for matching word vectors, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), and demonstrate their effectiveness across four diverse NLP datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) representing question answering and textual entailment tasks. The paper makes a valuable contribution by showing that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches, addressing a gap in the literature where previous studies have not systematically evaluated comparison functions across multiple tasks.

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the sequence matching literature by demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks and providing a thorough evaluation of comparison functions, showing that simple element-wise operations can outperform standard neural network approaches.

The paper is well-motivated, addressing two key limitations in previous work: (1) previous models were tested on only one or two tasks, while this work validates the framework across four diverse tasks, and (2) previous studies did not systematically evaluate comparison functions, whereas this work provides a comprehensive analysis of six different comparison functions. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with thorough comparisons against multiple baselines across four different datasets using appropriate evaluation metrics. The finding that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network approaches is both novel and significant for the field.

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. To further strengthen the contribution, the authors should:
- Provide experimental results without the preprocessing and attention layers to quantify their specific contribution to the overall performance
- Improve the quality of Figure 2 for better readability in printed form
- Clarify the relationship between the SUB/MULT comparison functions and their corresponding distance/similarity measures (e.g., how SUB relates to Euclidean distance and MULT relates to cosine similarity)
- Include a more detailed discussion of why the element-wise comparison functions outperform neural network approaches, potentially with some qualitative examples

The following questions would help clarify the paper's contribution:
1. How does the performance of the model change when the attention mechanism is removed, particularly for the shorter-sequence matching tasks (WikiQA and SNLI) where the preprocessing layer appears to be more important?
2. What is the computational cost of the element-wise comparison functions compared to neural network-based approaches, and how does this impact scalability for very long sequences?
3. Could you provide more insight into why the element-wise functions perform better than neural network approaches, particularly regarding the nature of semantic matching at the word level?
