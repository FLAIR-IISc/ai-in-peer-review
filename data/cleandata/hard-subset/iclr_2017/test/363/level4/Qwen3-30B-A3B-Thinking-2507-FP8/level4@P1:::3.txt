

The paper introduces a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks, systematically evaluating six comparison functions at the word level. It demonstrates the framework's effectiveness across four distinct datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), showing that simple element-wise operations (SUB: subtraction, MULT: multiplication) often outperform standard neural network-based comparisons. The work makes a clear contribution by empirically validating the framework's broad applicability and rigorously analyzing comparison functions—addressing a key gap in prior literature.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a strong contribution through its systematic evaluation of comparison functions across diverse tasks, and the results are scientifically rigorous.  

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Broad applicability and empirical rigor**: The authors test their framework on four distinct tasks (three QA and one entailment), significantly expanding beyond the single-task focus of prior "compare-aggregate" work. This validates the framework’s generality, a critical claim the paper substantiates with consistent performance gains.  
2. **Novel comparison function analysis**: The empirical comparison of six functions—including the underexplored SUB/MULT operations—provides actionable insights. The finding that element-wise operations (which require no learnable parameters) outperform neural networks on multiple datasets is both surprising and valuable, directly addressing the paper’s core hypothesis.  
3. **Scientifically sound evaluation**: Results are presented with appropriate metrics (accuracy, MAP, MRR), baselines are well-chosen and state-of-the-art, and ablation studies clearly isolate the impact of key components (e.g., attention vs. preprocessing layers). The visualizations in Section 3.4 further strengthen the interpretability of results.  

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
- **Clarify the word-order limitation**: The paper notes the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, which limits performance for tasks where word order matters (e.g., MovieQA). While acknowledged, this limitation warrants explicit discussion in the introduction or related work to contextualize the framework’s scope.  
- **Standardize comparison function notation**: Use consistent notation (e.g., "SUB" not "Sub," "SUBMULT+NN" not "SubMultNN") throughout for clarity.  
- **Refine NTN parameter discussion**: The NTN’s high parameter count risks overfitting. Consider briefly addressing whether a dimensionality reduction (e.g., output dimension *m* < *l*) was explored, as this could improve generalization.  
- **Cite Luong-style attention correctly**: The attention mechanism aligns more closely with Luong et al. (2015) than Bahdanau et al. (2014). Add a citation to Luong et al. (2015) when describing the attention layer.  
- **Address preprocessing symmetry**: Using identical parameters for question and answer preprocessing (Eq. 1) may not suit task-specific weighting needs (e.g., questions vs. answers in QA). Discuss whether this could be a limitation or if symmetric parameters were empirically justified.  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states SUB and MULT outperform neural networks on WikiQA and MovieQA. Could you elaborate on *why* these simple functions generalize better than parameter-rich alternatives? Is it due to reduced overfitting, or do they better align with the semantic similarity task?  
2. For the MovieQA task, the model matches question/plot and answer/plot pairs separately before aggregation (Section 3.1). How does this design compare to a unified matching approach (e.g., matching question-answer directly), and why was the two-stage method chosen?  
3. The ablation study (Table 4) shows attention is critical for long-sequence tasks (MovieQA/InsuranceQA) while preprocessing matters more for shorter sequences (WikiQA/SNLI). Is this pattern consistent across all comparison functions, or does it vary by function type (e.g., SUB vs. NN)?  

This paper makes a meaningful contribution to sequence matching by rigorously testing a generalizable framework and revealing counterintuitive insights about comparison functions. The empirical analysis is thorough, and the results are compelling across diverse tasks. With minor clarifications on limitations and notation, it will be a valuable reference for future work.
