- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework performing word-level comparisons followed by CNN aggregation.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including novel element-wise SUB and MULT, across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Finds simple element-wise comparison functions achieve state-of-the-art or competitive results, challenging need for complex neural architectures.  
- Experimental design includes comprehensive baselines and ablation studies.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Major limitation: No statistical significance tests (p-values or confidence intervals) for claims about comparison-function superiority.  
- Major limitation: Ablation study omits results for removing the preprocessing layer, undermining claims about its necessity.  
- Example concern: Small reported differences (e.g., WikiQA MULT 0.65 MAP vs NN 0.64) lack significance testing.  
- Unclear generalization: Paper does not explain how the same model structure adapts to tasks with different sequence structures (e.g., MovieQA’s three sequences vs SNLI’s two).  
- Suggested improvement: Add statistical validation (t-tests or confidence intervals) for all performance comparisons.  
- Suggested improvement: Complete ablation by reporting results when preprocessing layer is removed.  
- Suggested improvement: Improve Figure 2 readability (larger axis labels, higher resolution).  
- Suggested improvement: Clarify how the framework handles differing sequence structures across tasks.  
- Question for authors: Why do element-wise functions (SUB/MULT) outperform NN/NTN—reduced overfitting or a fundamental property of sequence matching?  
- Question for authors: How does order-insensitive gated preprocessing align with order-sensitive tasks like MovieQA?  
- Question for authors: Is the SUBMULT+NN combination strictly better than SUB/MULT alone—provide statistical evidence.
