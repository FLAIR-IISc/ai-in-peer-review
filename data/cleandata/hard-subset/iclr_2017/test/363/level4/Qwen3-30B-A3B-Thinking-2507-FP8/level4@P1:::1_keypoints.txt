- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching and evaluates it on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- Systematically compares six word-level comparison functions and finds simple element-wise operations (subtraction, multiplication) often outperform complex neural comparators.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main critique: work is incremental and lacks sufficient novelty over prior works (Wang & Jiang 2016b; He & Lin 2016; Parikh et al. 2016).
- Evaluation is not seen as substantially advancing the field beyond He & Lin (2016), who explored similar comparison functions.
- Lacks theoretical justification for why element-wise operations outperform neural network-based comparison functions.
- Empirical results are inconsistent: different comparison functions perform best on different datasets, undermining the claim that element-wise ops are universally best.
- Claim of a “general” framework is weak: testing on four datasets is insufficient and the paper fails to analyze limitations or boundary conditions.
- Recommended improvements: clearly differentiate from prior work, provide theoretical analysis, include qualitative examples explaining successes/failures, and discuss framework limitations and generalization.
- Questions for authors: How does this work meaningfully differ from prior compare-aggregate frameworks? How do you reconcile the contradictory dataset-specific best functions with your broad claim? Can you provide theoretical insight into why element-wise operations work better?
