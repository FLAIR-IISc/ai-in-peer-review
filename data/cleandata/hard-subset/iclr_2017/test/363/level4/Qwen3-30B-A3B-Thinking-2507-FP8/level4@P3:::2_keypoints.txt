- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching with six comparison functions (including novel SUB and MULT).
- Evaluates the framework on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) using CNN aggregation.
- Empirical finding: simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex neural comparisons.
- Experimental evaluation is thorough and shows competitive or superior performance versus prior methods.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary reason for rejection: lacks theoretical explanation for why element-wise functions outperform neural-network-based comparisons.
- Secondary reason: limited evidence of generalizability — only four related QA/entailment datasets tested.
- Paper insufficiently explores implications or limitations (e.g., why order-insensitive preprocessing still works well).
- Suggested improvement: provide detailed theoretical analysis of SUB/MULT and information preserved by different comparisons.
- Suggested improvement: test on more diverse sequence-matching tasks to support general applicability.
- Suggested improvement: compare comprehensively to recent state-of-the-art models, especially transformer-based approaches.
- Suggested improvement: include ablation studies isolating preprocessing, attention, comparison function, and aggregation contributions.
- Questions for authors: explain theoretically why SUB/MULT work better; compare to contemporaneous SOTA (transformers/BERT); clarify why order-insensitive preprocessing performs well.
