

The paper introduces a general approach for modeling natural language understanding tasks involving two distinct textual inputs (e.g., a question and source text) that can be aligned. The method first employs soft attention to establish token-level alignments between the two texts, then applies a comparison function to the resulting alignments (represented as attention query-result pairs) to derive representations. These representations are aggregated via CNN into a single vector for output computation. The work presents this as a broadly applicable modeling strategy yielding strong performance, alongside a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component.

This work is timely, addressing a major open challenge in NLP where such language understanding tasks are now becoming tractable via representation learning. The proposed approach is straightforward, reasonable, and empirically effective. While bordering on incremental relative to the authors' prior work or Parikh et al.'s approach, it contributes sufficiently substantively to warrant strong recommendation for acceptance.

Details:
- The model, as implemented for longer-sequence tasks (all except SNLI), is insensitive to word order. Though empirically competitive, this insensitivity imposes a strong upper bound on performance. The paper acknowledges this but warrants a brief mention in the introduction or discussion.
- The attention strategy aligns more closely with Luong et al. (2015)’s general/bilinear approach than with Bahdanau et al.’s work; the authors should cite Luong et al. (or a more directly relevant reference).
- Given NTN’s risk of overfitting due to high parameter count, the authors should consider testing a variant with input dimension *l* and reduced output dimension *m* (i.e., an *l*×*l*×*m* tensor).
- SubMultNN resembles the sentence-level matching strategy in the Lili Mou et al. paper cited; this similarity should be noted.
- The use of identical parameters for preprocessing questions and answers in Equation (1) appears unjustified; these may require different weighting schemes.
