

This paper proposes a general architecture for natural language understanding tasks involving two aligned textual inputs (e.g., question and passage), leveraging soft attention for alignment followed by a comparison function and CNN-based aggregation. The approach demonstrates strong empirical performance across multiple benchmarks, including the Stanford Natural Language Inference (SNLI) dataset. The work is timely, addressing a critical challenge in NLP where representation learning is beginning to yield meaningful progress. While the core methodology builds on prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), it offers a coherent, well-motivated framework with substantial empirical validation, making a meaningful contribution to the field. I strongly recommend acceptance.

The paper’s greatest strength lies in its clear articulation of a flexible modeling strategy that effectively handles diverse NLU tasks. The use of attention to derive token-level alignments, followed by a comparison function (e.g., NTN, SubMult) and CNN aggregation, provides a principled way to fuse information from two inputs. The empirical analysis thoroughly evaluates the impact of different comparison functions, offering valuable insights into their relative strengths and limitations. This level of detail significantly enhances the paper’s contribution beyond a mere implementation.

That said, three key limitations warrant attention. First, the model’s insensitivity to word order—particularly for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI)—is a fundamental architectural constraint. The CNN aggregation step discards sequential information, placing a hard upper bound on performance for tasks where ordering is critical (e.g., reasoning over temporal or structural relationships). While the paper acknowledges this empirically, it should explicitly state this limitation in the introduction or discussion to set appropriate expectations for readers. This is not merely a minor detail but a significant constraint of the approach.

Second, the attention mechanism closely follows Luong et al.’s (2015) bilinear strategy rather than Bahdanau et al.’s (2015) additive approach. The paper should cite Luong et al. as the more relevant reference for the attention formulation used, rather than implying a closer relationship to Bahdanau. This correction would improve technical precision and align the work with established literature.

Third, the choice of the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) for comparison raises a potential overfitting concern due to its large parameter count. The authors might consider exploring dimensionality reduction for NTN (e.g., using input dimension *l* and output dimension *m* with *m* < *l*, forming an *l*×*l*×*m* tensor) to mitigate this risk. While the paper does not report overfitting symptoms, a brief mention of such a strategy or its omission would strengthen the methodology section.

Additionally, two minor points require clarification. The SubMultNN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-matching strategy in Mou et al.’s (2015) *Lili Mou* paper. The authors should acknowledge this similarity to avoid potential confusion about novelty. Finally, the shared parameters for preprocessing both inputs (question and passage) in Equation (1) seem questionable. Given that the two inputs may require distinct feature weighting (e.g., questions often emphasize content words while passages rely on context), separate encoders or distinct parameter sets for each input could improve performance. The paper should address this design choice.

Despite these points, the paper’s contribution is substantial. It provides a robust, generalizable framework that achieves competitive results across multiple tasks, with a clear empirical analysis that guides future work. The limitations noted are architectural and well-understood, not methodological flaws, and the proposed solution remains highly effective for the target tasks. The work’s clarity, thorough evaluation, and relevance to ongoing NLP challenges make it a strong fit for ICLR. The minor revisions suggested above would further elevate the paper’s quality without altering its core contribution. I therefore strongly recommend acceptance.
