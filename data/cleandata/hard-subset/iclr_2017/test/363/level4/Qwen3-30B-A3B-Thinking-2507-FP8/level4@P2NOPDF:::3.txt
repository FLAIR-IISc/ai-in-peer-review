

This paper proposes a general framework for natural language understanding tasks involving two aligned textual inputs (e.g., questions and passages). The approach first employs soft attention to establish token-level alignments between the inputs, then utilizes a comparison function on these alignments (represented as query-response pairs) to generate context-aware representations. These representations are processed by convolutional neural network (CNN) layers to produce a single vector for downstream prediction. The authors present this as a versatile modeling strategy achieving strong empirical results, complemented by a detailed analysis of the comparison component.

The work addresses a timely and significant challenge in NLP: modeling complex semantic relationships between text pairs, which has become tractable through modern representation learning. The proposed framework is conceptually straightforward and empirically effective, demonstrating competitive performance across multiple benchmarks. While the method builds upon prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), it makes substantial contributions through its general architecture and comprehensive empirical validation. I strongly recommend acceptance, as the incremental advances are meaningful and well-justified.

That said, several points warrant clarification or elaboration:

1.  **Word Order Sensitivity:** The model, particularly for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), appears insensitive to word order. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, its significance as a performance ceiling merits explicit discussion in the introduction or related work section. This is a critical characteristic for tasks where sequential structure matters.

2.  **Attention Mechanism Attribution:** The attention strategy aligns more closely with Luong et al. (2015) (bilinear) than Bahdanau et al. (2014). The authors should cite Luong et al. (2015) as the more directly relevant foundation for their attention mechanism.

3.  **NTN Overfitting Mitigation:** Given the NTN's high parameter count, the authors should address whether they explored dimensionality reduction (e.g., using an input dimension `l` and smaller output dimension `m`, resulting in an `l x l x m` tensor) to mitigate overfitting risk, especially on smaller datasets.

4.  **SubMultNN Similarity:** The SubMultNN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-matching strategy in Mou et al. (2015). The authors should explicitly acknowledge this similarity and clarify how their implementation differs or extends the prior work.

5.  **Asymmetric Preprocessing:** The use of identical parameters to preprocess both the question and passage (Equation 1) seems questionable. These inputs likely require different feature weighting (e.g., questions may prioritize specific question words, while passages require broader context). The authors should justify this design choice or discuss potential benefits of asymmetric preprocessing.

The paper's core contribution—providing a flexible, effective, and analytically grounded framework for text-pair modeling—is compelling. The empirical results are solid, and the analysis of the comparison function adds value. Addressing the points above would strengthen the presentation and clarify the model's scope and novelty. The work is well-positioned to advance the field of text-pair understanding, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
