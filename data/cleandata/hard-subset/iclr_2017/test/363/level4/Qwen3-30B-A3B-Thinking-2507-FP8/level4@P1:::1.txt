

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in natural language processing, including question answering and textual entailment. The authors demonstrate that this framework is effective across four distinct datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), each representing different sequence matching problems. A key contribution lies in their systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations. The authors find that simple element-wise operations (specifically subtraction and multiplication) consistently outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple tasks, which is a significant empirical finding for the field.

**Decision**

Reject.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper makes a reasonable contribution by demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence matching tasks. However, the work is primarily incremental and lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core idea of using a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching builds directly on prior work by Wang & Jiang (2016b), He & Lin (2016), and Parikh et al. (2016), without providing a compelling justification for why this specific instantiation constitutes a meaningful advancement. While the authors do systematically evaluate different comparison functions, this evaluation does not substantially advance the field beyond what was already suggested by He & Lin (2016), who also explored similar comparison functions including cosine similarity and Euclidean distance.

The paper's most significant limitation is the lack of a clear theoretical foundation or novel insight explaining why element-wise operations work better than neural network-based approaches. The empirical results show that different comparison functions perform best on different datasets (e.g., SUBMULT+NN on InsuranceQA, EUCCOS on MovieQA), yet the authors claim element-wise operations "work the best across the different datasets" without adequately addressing these discrepancies. This lack of theoretical justification weakens the broader impact of their findings.

Furthermore, the paper does not sufficiently demonstrate why their framework represents a "general" approach to sequence matching. While they test on four datasets, these represent only a small subset of sequence matching problems, and the paper does not provide a thorough analysis of the framework's limitations or boundary conditions.

**Additional Feedback**

To strengthen future work, the authors should:
1. Provide a more compelling argument for novelty by clearly differentiating their approach from prior work in the literature.
2. Include a theoretical analysis explaining why element-wise operations might be effective for sequence matching, rather than relying solely on empirical results.
3. Conduct a more detailed qualitative analysis of when and why different comparison functions succeed or fail, including specific examples from the datasets.
4. Discuss the limitations of their framework and provide a more comprehensive analysis of how their approach might generalize to other sequence matching tasks.

**Questions for Authors**

1. How does your framework specifically differ from the compare-aggregate frameworks proposed by Wang & Jiang (2016b), He & Lin (2016), and Parikh et al. (2016) in a way that constitutes a significant advancement over prior work?

2. The paper claims that element-wise operations "work the best across the different datasets," yet Table 3 shows that different comparison functions achieve the best performance on different datasets (e.g., EUCCOS on MovieQA, SUBMULT+NN on InsuranceQA). How do you reconcile these differing results with your general claim?

3. The paper does not provide a theoretical justification for why element-wise operations might outperform neural network-based comparison functions. Can you offer any insights into the underlying mechanisms that might explain these empirical results?
