- Introduces a compare-aggregate framework evaluating six word-level comparison functions across sequence-matching tasks.  
- Evaluates the framework on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform neural network–based comparison functions.  
- Empirically validates the framework’s broad applicability beyond prior single-task work.  
- Decision: Accept — strong contribution and rigorous experimental results.  
- Broad applicability and empirical rigor: tests across diverse tasks with consistent performance gains.  
- Novel comparison-function analysis: highlights value of underexplored SUB/MULT operations.  
- Scientific evaluation: uses appropriate metrics, strong baselines, ablation studies, and helpful visualizations.  
- Clarify limitation: preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, which may hurt word-order–sensitive tasks (e.g., MovieQA).  
- Standardize notation for comparison functions (e.g., use "SUB" consistently).  
- Discuss NTN parameter count and whether dimensionality reduction was tried to mitigate overfitting.  
- Cite Luong et al. (2015) for the attention mechanism rather than only Bahdanau et al. (2014).  
- Address symmetric preprocessing parameters for question and answer and whether asymmetry was considered or justified.  
- Question: Why do SUB and MULT generalize better — reduced overfitting or better alignment with semantic similarity?  
- Question: Why use two-stage matching (question/plot and answer/plot) for MovieQA instead of unified question-answer matching?  
- Question: Is the observed pattern (attention critical for long sequences, preprocessing for short) consistent across all comparison functions or does it vary?
