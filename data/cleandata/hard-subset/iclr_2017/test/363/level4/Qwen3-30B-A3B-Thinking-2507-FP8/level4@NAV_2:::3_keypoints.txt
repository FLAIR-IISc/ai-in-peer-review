- Introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework: word-level matching followed by CNN-based aggregation.  
- Evaluates different comparison functions across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Main finding: simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT) outperform more complex neural comparison functions.  
- Comprehensive empirical analysis showing the framework’s effectiveness and practical guidance for sequence matching.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Strength: thorough evaluation across diverse datasets demonstrating general applicability beyond single-task studies.  
- Strength: systematic comparison of six comparison functions yields useful design insights.  
- Strength: challenges conventional wisdom by showing simpler element-wise ops can beat complex neural approaches.  
- Limitation: model exhibits word-order insensitivity, especially for longer sequences, which should be emphasized.  
- Citation concern: attention implementation aligns with Luong et al. (2015) and should cite the appropriate prior work (vs. Bahdanau et al.).  
- Suggestion: consider reducing neural tensor network output dimensionality to avoid potential overfitting.  
- Suggestion: explicitly acknowledge similarity of SubMult+NN to Mou et al. (2016).  
- Suggestion: using identical preprocessing parameters for question and answer may be suboptimal and needs justification.  
- Questions for authors: why use the same preprocessing for question and answer?  
- Questions for authors: how were optimal dimensionalities chosen for the comparison functions (esp. SUB and MULT)?  
- Questions for authors: why use a CNN for aggregation instead of simpler methods (e.g., summation/concatenation) given word-order insensitivity?
