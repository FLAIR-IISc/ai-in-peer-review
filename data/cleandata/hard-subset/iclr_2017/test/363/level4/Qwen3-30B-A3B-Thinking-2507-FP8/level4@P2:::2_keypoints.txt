- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework that does word-level matching followed by CNN-based aggregation.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including novel element-wise operators SUB and MULT.  
- Experiments cover four diverse tasks: SNLI (textual entailment), MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA (question answering).  
- Main contribution: simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often outperform more complex neural-network-based approaches.  
- Decision: Accept — paper provides a valuable, general framework and a systematic evaluation missing in prior work.  
- Addresses two gaps: prior models were tested on few tasks, and comparison functions were not systematically evaluated before.  
- Results are compelling and consistent across datasets; ablation studies show attention and preprocessing layers are important.  
- Ask for clearer explanation why order-insensitive preprocessing outperforms order-sensitive methods.  
- Ask for discussion on why context-unaware preprocessing still beats context-aware preprocessing.  
- Suggest stronger ablation: report effects of removing each component individually and removing preprocessing+attention jointly.  
- Specific questions for authors: provide joint-removal ablation; offer theoretical intuition for element-wise superiority; explain MovieQA success despite order-insensitivity.  
- Minor fixes: correct typo (page 4, line 5) and improve Figure 2 readability for camera-ready.
