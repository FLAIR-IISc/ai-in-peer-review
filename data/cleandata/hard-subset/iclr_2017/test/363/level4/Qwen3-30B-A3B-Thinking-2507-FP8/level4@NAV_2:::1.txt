

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching tasks, including question answering and textual entailment. It systematically evaluates six comparison functions—three novel element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) alongside standard neural approaches—and demonstrates that element-wise methods achieve strong performance across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The authors show that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can outperform neural network-based alternatives while maintaining broad applicability across tasks.

Reject. The work presents a well-executed empirical study but lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance. The core contribution—applying element-wise comparison functions within an established compare-aggregate framework—is incremental, as the framework itself has been previously validated in multiple studies (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016). The paper fails to clearly articulate how the specific comparison functions or their systematic evaluation advance beyond existing work in the literature.

The empirical rigor is strong: the model is evaluated on four distinct datasets with thorough ablation studies, and the results consistently show that element-wise functions (particularly SUBMULT+NN) achieve state-of-the-art or competitive performance. However, the paper does not sufficiently differentiate its contribution from prior compare-aggregate models. For instance, while Parikh et al. (2016) and He & Lin (2016) also explore comparison functions, this paper’s evaluation of element-wise operations does not address whether these functions solve a previously unaddressed limitation (e.g., computational efficiency, interpretability, or task-specific adaptability) or provide theoretical justification beyond empirical observation. The claim that "this general framework is effective on many sequence matching problems" is not substantiated beyond demonstrating performance on four datasets, without addressing whether the framework’s generality is truly novel.

To strengthen the paper, the authors should: (1) more explicitly contrast their comparison function evaluation with prior work (e.g., why element-wise operations were overlooked before), (2) provide qualitative analysis of how SUB/MULT behave on specific sentence types (e.g., negation, idioms), and (3) clarify whether the framework’s generality extends to non-textual sequence matching tasks. The current focus on empirical results without deeper theoretical or methodological justification limits the paper’s contribution.

Please clarify: (1) What specific limitations of prior compare-aggregate models does this work address that justify the focus on element-wise comparison functions? (2) How do the element-wise functions improve interpretability or efficiency compared to neural network alternatives, beyond accuracy gains?
