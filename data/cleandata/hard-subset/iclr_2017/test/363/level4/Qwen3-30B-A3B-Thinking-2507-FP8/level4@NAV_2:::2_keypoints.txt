- Introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for word-level text sequence matching followed by CNN-based aggregation.
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including novel element-wise operations SUB and MULT.
- Demonstrates effectiveness across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) covering QA and textual entailment.
- Shows simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network approaches.
- Addresses gaps in prior work by validating across multiple tasks rather than one or two.
- Provides a thorough, scientifically rigorous experimental comparison against multiple baselines with appropriate metrics.
- Paper is well-written, clearly organized, and makes a significant contribution to sequence matching literature.
- Recommendation: provide experimental ablations without preprocessing and attention to quantify their contributions.
- Suggestion: improve Figure 2 quality for printed readability.
- Request clarification on how SUB and MULT relate to classical distance/similarity measures (e.g., SUB↔Euclidean, MULT↔cosine).
- Recommend a deeper discussion (and qualitative examples) explaining why element-wise functions outperform neural-network approaches.
- Questions to address: effect of removing attention, computational cost and scalability of element-wise vs. neural approaches, and further insight into why element-wise functions excel at word-level semantic matching.
