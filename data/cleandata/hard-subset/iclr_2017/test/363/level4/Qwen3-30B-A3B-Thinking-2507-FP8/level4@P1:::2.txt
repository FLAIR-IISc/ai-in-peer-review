

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching that performs word-level comparisons followed by aggregation using convolutional neural networks. It systematically evaluates six comparison functions—including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT)—across four diverse NLP tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The authors demonstrate that simple element-wise comparison functions achieve state-of-the-art or competitive results on all datasets, challenging the assumption that complex neural networks are necessary for word-level matching. The experimental design is thorough, with comprehensive baselines and ablation studies.

**Decision: Reject**  
The key limitations are: (1) the paper fails to provide statistical significance tests for its claims about comparison function superiority, and (2) the ablation study omits results for removing the preprocessing layer, undermining the argument about its necessity.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The core claim—that element-wise functions like SUB and MULT outperform neural networks (NN/NTN) across datasets—lacks statistical validation. The paper reports accuracy/MAP/MRR scores but does not include p-values or confidence intervals for comparisons (e.g., SUB vs. NN on WikiQA). Without this, the assertion that "simple comparison functions work better" remains anecdotal. For instance, on WikiQA, MULT achieves 0.65 MAP versus NN’s 0.64, but the paper does not state whether this difference is statistically significant.  

Additionally, Section 3.3 states the authors "remove these two layers separately" (preprocessing and attention), yet Table 4 only reports ablation results for the attention layer. The preprocessing layer’s contribution is never quantified (e.g., removing it entirely), making it impossible to assess its claimed importance. The paper notes that preprocessing is "order-insensitive" but argues it still outperforms order-sensitive baselines—this conclusion requires ablation evidence, which is missing.  

Finally, while the framework is applied to four tasks, the paper does not clarify why the same model structure generalizes across them. For example, MovieQA involves three sequences (question, plot, answer), while SNLI uses two. The authors do not explain how the framework adapts to this structural difference, weakening the "general framework" claim.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
1. **Statistical validation**: Include t-tests or confidence intervals for all performance comparisons (e.g., SUB vs. NN on each dataset) to substantiate claims of superiority.  
2. **Complete ablation study**: Report results for removing the preprocessing layer (not just attention) to validate its contribution.  
3. **Figure 2 readability**: Enhance visual clarity (e.g., larger axis labels, higher resolution) for printed copies.  
4. **Clarify framework adaptability**: Explain how the model handles tasks with differing sequence structures (e.g., MovieQA’s three sequences vs. SNLI’s two).  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. Why do element-wise functions (SUB/MULT) outperform NN/NTN on all datasets despite their simplicity? Is this due to reduced overfitting, or does it reflect a fundamental property of sequence matching tasks?  
2. The preprocessing layer uses gates but is order-insensitive. How does this align with the task requirements for MovieQA (where plot word order matters)?  
3. In Table 3, the "SUBMULT+NN" variant achieves the best results. Is this combination strictly better than SUB/MULT alone, or is it redundant? Please provide statistical evidence.  

This work has potential but requires stronger empirical rigor to support its core claims. Addressing these points would significantly strengthen the contribution.
