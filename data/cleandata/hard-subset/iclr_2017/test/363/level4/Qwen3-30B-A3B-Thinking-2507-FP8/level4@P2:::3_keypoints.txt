- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching using various comparison functions and CNN-based aggregation.  
- Evaluated on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI; simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex neural alternatives.  
- Decision: Accept — paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating broad applicability and insights into comparison functions.  
- Primary contribution: systematic evaluation of six comparison functions within a consistent framework across multiple tasks.  
- Empirical highlight: SUBMULT+NN achieves state-of-the-art on three datasets; EUCCOS and element-wise methods perform well on MovieQA and WikiQA.  
- Provides practical insights about when preprocessing vs. attention layers are most beneficial.  
- Limitation: model is largely insensitive to word order for longer sequences and this should be more prominently discussed.  
- Methodology clarification: the attention mechanism aligns more with Luong et al. (2015) bilinear attention than Bahdanau’s and should be cited/clarified.  
- Model complexity concern: the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has many parameters; report results with dimensionality reduction to address overfitting risk.  
- Literature positioning: SUBMULT+NN resembles Mou et al. (2016)’s sentence-level matching—clarify the relationship.  
- Preprocessing design: using identical preprocessing parameters for questions and answers may be suboptimal; consider task-specific settings.  
- Question for authors: why do simple, parameter-free comparison functions consistently outperform more complex neural approaches?  
- Question for authors: why is the attention layer especially important for tasks with large sequence length differences (e.g., MovieQA)?  
- Question for authors: given order-insensitive preprocessing, which aspects of the comparison layer compensate for lost word order information?  
- Question for authors: why do parameter-free element-wise comparisons work particularly well on WikiQA relative to other tasks?
