

**Summary:**  
This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, addressing fundamental NLP tasks like question answering and textual entailment. The authors systematically investigate various comparison functions for matching word-level representations between sequences, using a CNN-based aggregation mechanism. They evaluate their approach across four distinct datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural network-based alternatives. The work makes a meaningful contribution by empirically validating the framework's broad applicability across multiple sequence matching tasks and providing a thorough analysis of comparison functions, which has been underexplored in prior work.

**Decision:**  
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by demonstrating the effectiveness of a general compare-aggregate framework across multiple NLP tasks and providing valuable insights into comparison functions that can improve sequence matching models.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
The paper's primary contribution lies in its systematic evaluation of comparison functions within a consistent framework. While prior work has used similar architectures (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), this paper advances the field by: (1) testing the framework across four diverse datasets representing different tasks, proving its broad applicability beyond single-task specialization; and (2) rigorously comparing six different comparison functions, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that outperform standard neural network approaches. The empirical results are compelling, showing consistent improvements across multiple benchmarks. For instance, the SUBMULT+NN combination achieves state-of-the-art results on three of the four datasets, while simple comparison functions like EUCCOS and element-wise operations perform surprisingly well on MovieQA and WikiQA. The authors also provide valuable insights about when specific components (preprocessing vs. attention layers) are most beneficial, which is practically useful for future work.

**Additional Feedback:**  
The paper would benefit from the following improvements to strengthen its contribution and clarity:

1. The model's insensitivity to word order (for longer sequences) is a notable limitation that should be more prominently discussed in the introduction or related work. While the authors acknowledge this, it's a critical constraint on the model's theoretical scope that deserves more emphasis.

2. The attention mechanism is more closely aligned with Luong et al.'s (2015) general bilinear approach than with Bahdanau's attention. Please clarify and cite this more directly in the methodology section.

3. The neural tensor network (NTN) component has a large number of parameters. It would be valuable to report results using a dimensionality reduction approach (e.g., reducing input dimension l to a smaller output dimension m) to address potential overfitting concerns.

4. The SUBMULT+NN comparison function bears strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy in Mou et al. (2016). Please clarify the relationship between these approaches to properly position your work in the literature.

5. Using identical preprocessing parameters for questions and answers may be suboptimal, as these sequences often require different weighting strategies. Consider exploring task-specific preprocessing parameters for the two input sequences.

**Questions for Authors:**  
To help clarify the paper and ensure confidence in the assessment, please address the following:

1. How do you explain the consistent superiority of simple comparison functions (like SUB and MULT) over more complex neural network approaches, especially given that they don't require additional parameters to learn?

2. In the ablation study, you note that the attention layer is particularly important for tasks with large sequence length differences (like MovieQA). Could you elaborate on why this is the case and whether this insight could inform future model design for similar tasks?

3. The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Given that the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing, could you discuss what specific aspects of the comparison layer might compensate for the loss of word order information?

4. For the WikiQA dataset, the element-wise comparison functions (which don't require parameters) achieve the best results. Could you provide more insight into why parameter-free functions work particularly well for this task compared to others?
