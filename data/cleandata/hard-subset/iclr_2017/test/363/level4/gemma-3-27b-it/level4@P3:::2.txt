## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, focusing on a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions within this framework. The authors implement their model and evaluate it on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), demonstrating competitive performance against established baselines. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and presents a thorough empirical evaluation.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP, provides a well-defined and justified approach, and supports its claims with compelling experimental results. The systematic comparison of comparison functions is a particularly valuable contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper effectively tackles the problem of sequence matching, a fundamental task in various NLP applications. The “compare-aggregate” framework is a widely adopted approach, but the authors rightly identify a gap in systematic evaluation of the core component – the comparison function. Their hypothesis that the choice of comparison function significantly impacts performance is convincingly demonstrated across four different datasets. 

The experimental setup is robust. The use of four diverse datasets provides strong evidence for the generalizability of the proposed approach. The inclusion of multiple baselines allows for a fair comparison, and the reported results consistently show the proposed model achieving competitive, and often state-of-the-art, performance. The ablation study (Table 4) further strengthens the conclusions, highlighting the importance of individual components. The availability of the code is a positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

While the paper is generally well-written, there are some areas for potential improvement. 

*   **Preprocessing & Attention Analysis:** The authors briefly mention the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers in the analysis section. Providing results *without* these layers (even briefly) would further solidify this claim and allow for a more direct assessment of their contribution.
*   **Figure Quality:** As noted in the draft, Figure 2 is difficult to interpret, especially in printed form. Increasing the font size, using clearer visual cues to highlight important areas, and potentially providing a color version would greatly improve its readability and impact.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** A brief discussion of the limitations of the approach would add nuance to the paper. For example, the authors might address potential issues with scalability to very long sequences or computational cost.
*   **Comparison Function Intuition:** Expanding on the intuition behind why the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform well would be beneficial. Is it capturing specific types of semantic relationships that the other functions miss?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA. Did you experiment with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., using an RNN) for these tasks, and if so, how did they compare to the current approach?
2.  In the experiments, the learning rate and batch size were tuned for WikiQA, but not for the other datasets. Was this due to computational constraints, or did preliminary tuning suggest that the default parameters were sufficient for those datasets?
3.  The paper effectively demonstrates that element-wise operations can be competitive comparison functions. Could you elaborate on the specific types of semantic relationships these operations appear to capture effectively, and why they might be advantageous in the context of sequence matching?
4.  You mention future work on multi-task learning. Could you share some initial thoughts on how you envision adapting this model for a multi-task setting, and what challenges you anticipate?
