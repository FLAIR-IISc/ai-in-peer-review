This paper presents a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, a common task in NLP applications like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The authors propose a general model utilizing attention mechanisms followed by a comparison function and CNN-based aggregation. A key contribution lies in a systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions, demonstrating that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex approaches like neural networks and neural tensor networks. The model is evaluated on four diverse datasets, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses an important problem with a well-motivated and empirically sound approach. The systematic comparison of comparison functions is a significant contribution, and the strong performance across multiple datasets justifies acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully demonstrates the effectiveness of the “compare-aggregate” framework. The authors rightly point out the limitations of previous work – a lack of cross-dataset evaluation and insufficient attention to the choice of comparison function. By addressing both these points, the paper provides valuable insights into designing effective sequence matching models. The experimental results are compelling, showing that simpler comparison functions can be surprisingly effective, challenging common assumptions in the field. The ablation studies and analysis further strengthen the findings. The clear presentation and thorough experimental setup also contribute to the paper’s overall quality. The code availability is a positive aspect.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several points could be improved. 

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The observation that the model (except for SNLI) is not sensitive to word order is important and well-noted. However, briefly discussing the potential implications and limitations of this design choice in the introduction or discussion would strengthen the paper. Perhaps future work could explore incorporating order-sensitive mechanisms.
*   **Attention Mechanism Citation:** Correctly identifying the Luong et al. (2015) attention mechanism (or a similarly relevant reference) would improve the precision of the literature review.
*   **NTN Parameterization:** Investigating a reduced-dimensionality output for the NTN (llm tensor) to mitigate potential overfitting would be a worthwhile extension and could provide further insights into the performance of different comparison functions.
*   **Related Work Connection:** Acknowledging the similarities between SUBMULT+NN and the approach in the Lili Mou et al. paper would demonstrate a comprehensive understanding of the related literature.
*   **Preprocessing Parameters:** Exploring separate parameters for preprocessing the question and answer sequences (as in Equation 1) could potentially improve performance by allowing for task-specific weighting.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper shows excellent performance despite the word order insensitivity for longer sequences. Could you elaborate on why you believe the element-wise comparison functions are less affected by this limitation compared to more complex, parameter-rich functions like neural networks?
2.  You mention the potential for preprocessing layers to be order-sensitive in future work. Have you experimented with simpler order-sensitive features (e.g., position embeddings) in conjunction with the current framework, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the ablation studies, could you provide further detail on the impact of removing *both* the preprocessing and attention layers simultaneously? Would this lead to a significant performance drop, or would it be additive to the individual effects?
4.  You note that the best comparison function differs slightly across datasets. Are there any dataset characteristics that you believe correlate with the optimal choice of comparison function?
5.  Could you expand on the selection of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality *l* of the hidden layers? Was this value determined through experimentation, or was it based on prior work or computational constraints?
