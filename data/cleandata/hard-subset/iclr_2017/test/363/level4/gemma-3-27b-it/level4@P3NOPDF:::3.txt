This paper introduces a general modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks involving two distinct textual inputs capable of alignment – such as question answering and natural language inference. The core of the method utilizes soft attention to establish alignments between tokens in the two texts. These alignments, represented as query-result pairs, are then processed by a learned comparison function. The resulting representations are aggregated via a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a single vector, which is finally used to compute the task-specific output. The work demonstrates the efficacy of this modeling strategy and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison function component.

This research is particularly timely. Understanding relationships between paired texts is a critical challenge in NLP, and representation learning methods are only now beginning to offer promising solutions. The proposed approach is conceptually straightforward, well-motivated, and achieves strong performance. While the work builds upon prior research, including that of Parikh et al., it presents enough substantial contributions to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

Here are some specific points for the authors to consider:

**Strengths:** The generality of the approach is a significant strength. The modular design, with a focus on the learnable comparison function, allows for flexible adaptation to various tasks. The empirical analysis is thorough, and the results demonstrate competitive performance across several benchmarks.

**Weaknesses & Suggestions:**

* **Word Order Insensitivity:** The model, as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (all except SNLI), appears insensitive to word order. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, its potential impact on performance is significant enough to warrant a brief discussion in the introduction or discussion sections. Exploring methods to incorporate word order information could be a valuable direction for future work.
* **Attention Mechanism Citation:** The attention mechanism employed appears more closely aligned with the general/bilinear approach described in Luong et al. (2015) than with the earlier Bahdanau et al. (2015) work. I recommend citing Luong et al. (or another directly relevant reference) to accurately reflect the methodological basis of the attention mechanism.
* **NTN Parameter Efficiency:** The NTN component, due to its parameter count, carries a risk of overfitting. Did the authors investigate employing a lower-dimensional output layer (e.g., mapping to a dimension *m* < *l* from an input dimension *l*, resulting in an *llm* tensor) to potentially mitigate this risk and improve generalization?
* **SubMultNN Relationship:** The SubMultNN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy presented in the Lili Mou et al. paper cited. Acknowledging this connection would strengthen the related work section.
* **Preprocessing Parameter Sharing:** In section (1) of the paper, the same parameters are used for preprocessing the question and answer texts. Given the differing importance of various features in each input (e.g., keywords in the question versus entities in the answer), it is unclear why a shared preprocessing approach is optimal. Investigating task-specific preprocessing weighting schemes could potentially improve performance.



**Overall:**

This paper presents a well-conceived and effectively implemented approach to a challenging problem in NLP. The contributions are substantial, the experiments are thorough, and the writing is clear. The identified points above are minor suggestions for improvement and do not detract from the overall quality of the work. I believe this paper is a valuable addition to the literature and recommend it for acceptance to ICLR 2017.

* Bahdanau, D., et al. "Neural Machine Translation by Jointly Learning to Align and Translate." *ICLR 2015*.
