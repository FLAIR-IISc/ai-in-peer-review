This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for NLP tasks requiring semantic comparison of text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The core framework involves applying a convolutional neural network (aggregation) following element-wise operations (comparison) performed on attentive LSTM outputs. A key contribution is the comparative analysis of various methods for matching text sequences, demonstrating that element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally outperform other approaches across four different datasets.

**Reject.** While the work is sound and presents a thorough empirical evaluation, the contributions are incremental and lack substantial novelty. The paper primarily explores existing techniques within a well-established framework rather than proposing a fundamentally new approach.

The paper effectively tackles the problem of sequence matching by systematically evaluating different comparison functions within a compare-aggregate framework. The approach is well-motivated, building upon prior work in attention mechanisms and pairwise word interaction models (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016). The empirical evaluation across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) is comprehensive and demonstrates the effectiveness of the proposed model, particularly the surprising result that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. The results are clearly presented and the code availability is a positive aspect.

To further strengthen the work, I suggest the following: 
*   A more detailed analysis of *why* the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform so well would be valuable. Are there specific linguistic properties of the datasets that favor these operations?
*   Exploring the impact of different embedding sizes and CNN architectures could provide further insights into the model's sensitivity to these hyperparameters.
*   While the ablation studies are helpful, a more granular analysis of the contributions of the preprocessing and attention layers would be beneficial.
*   Consider including a qualitative error analysis. Examining specific examples where the model fails could reveal underlying limitations and suggest avenues for future research.

I have the following questions for the authors:
*   Could you elaborate on the intuition behind why element-wise operations are effective? Does this relate to capturing specific types of semantic relationships (e.g., differences or commonalities) between words?
*   The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Have you experimented with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., LSTMs or GRUs) and, if so, what were the results?
*   For the datasets where the attention layer appears more crucial, can you provide examples of attention weights to illustrate what the model is attending to?
*   How does the performance of the model change with different sizes of the word embeddings?
