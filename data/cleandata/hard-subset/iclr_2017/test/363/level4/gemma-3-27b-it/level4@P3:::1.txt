## Review of “A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES”

This paper presents a compare-aggregate model for sequence matching tasks, including question answering and textual entailment. The core idea is to perform word-level matching followed by aggregation using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions – including element-wise subtraction and multiplication – demonstrating that these simpler operations often outperform more complex methods like neural networks and neural tensor networks across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI).

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation, the contribution feels incremental and lacks substantial novelty to warrant acceptance at a highly competitive venue like ICLR. The core framework of compare-aggregate is well-established, and the primary contribution – the comparison of comparison functions – doesn't represent a significant conceptual advance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully demonstrates that simple element-wise operations can be competitive, and even superior, to more complex comparison functions within the compare-aggregate framework. The empirical evaluation across four datasets is a strength, providing reasonable support for the claim that the proposed approach is generally effective. The ablation studies (Table 4) are also helpful in understanding the relative importance of different components of the model. However, the observed improvements, while statistically significant in some cases, are often marginal. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *why* element-wise operations are so effective; it primarily shows *that* they are.

Furthermore, the novelty of exploring element-wise operations as comparison functions is limited, as acknowledged by the authors referencing Mou et al. (2016). While this work systematically evaluates these functions, the core idea isn't groundbreaking. The comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods isn’t always comprehensive; for instance, the baselines for WikiQA are somewhat dated. The paper also lacks a deeper analysis of *where* the model succeeds and fails. A more nuanced understanding of the types of sequence matching problems where element-wise operations excel would strengthen the contribution.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Motivation:** While the introduction highlights the importance of sequence matching, the motivation for focusing specifically on the comparison function is not fully compelling. A stronger argument could be made regarding the limitations of existing comparison functions and why element-wise operations represent a promising alternative.
*   **Analysis:** The results section would benefit from a more detailed analysis of the learned parameters in the comparison functions. For example, visualizing the weights learned in the NN and NTN comparisons could provide insights into their behavior and why they are less effective than the element-wise operations.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** As noted in the original draft, a qualitative analysis would be valuable. Providing examples of sentence pairs where the different comparison functions succeed or fail, and explaining *why*, would enhance understanding.
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** Expand the ablation study to include variations in the pre-processing and attention layers. How do these components interact with the different comparison functions?
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Ensure the baselines are up-to-date and representative of the current state-of-the-art for each dataset.
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Explicitly discuss the limitations of the approach, such as its potential sensitivity to word embedding quality or its inability to capture complex semantic relationships beyond element-wise interactions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind the success of element-wise subtraction and multiplication? What specific properties of these operations make them well-suited for sequence matching?
2.  The paper mentions that preprocessing is order-insensitive. Did you explore incorporating order information (e.g., using RNNs) into the preprocessing layer and, if so, what were the results?
3.  The qualitative analysis is missing. Can you provide examples of sentence pairs where the different comparison functions yield significantly different results and explain the reasoning behind these differences?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embedding spaces?
5.  The CNN aggregation layer seems relatively simple. Have you explored more sophisticated aggregation mechanisms (e.g., attention-based aggregation) and, if so, how did they compare to the CNN?
