The paper presents a general approach to modeling natural language understanding problems involving two distinct textual inputs – such as a question and a source text – that possess some form of alignment. The approach first employs soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts. A comparison function then leverages these alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and results) to generate representations, which are subsequently aggregated by a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a single vector used for output computation. The paper demonstrates this as a viable overall modeling strategy and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component.

This work is timely. Language understanding problems of this type remain a major open challenge in NLP, and are only now becoming addressable with representation learning methods. The work presents a straightforward and reasonable general approach that yields strong results. While the contribution borders on incremental – relative to the authors’ prior work or that of Parikh et al. – it is substantial enough to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

Detail: 
- The model, at least as implemented for problems with longer sequences (all except SNLI), is insensitive to word order. While empirically competitive, this insensitivity imposes a strong upper bound on potential performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but its significance merits a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections.
- If I understand correctly, the attention strategy is more closely aligned with the general/bilinear approach of Luong et al. (2015) than with the earlier Bahdanau work. The authors should consider citing the former (or another more directly relevant reference for that attention strategy).
- Given the risk of overfitting due to the large number of parameters in the NTN, did the authors explore a version with input dimension *l* and a smaller output dimension *m*, resulting in an *llm* tensor?
- The SubMultNN component bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy outlined in the Lili Mou paper cited by the authors. This similarity should be noted.
- Is there a specific rationale for using the same parameters for preprocessing the question and answer in (1)? Different weighting schemes may be more appropriate for each input.
