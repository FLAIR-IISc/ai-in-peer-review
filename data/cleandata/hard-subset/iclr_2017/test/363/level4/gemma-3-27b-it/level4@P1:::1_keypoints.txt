- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework combining attention, element-wise comparison, and CNN aggregation for sequence comparison tasks.
- Main contribution is an empirical comparison of different comparison functions, showing element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform alternatives.
- Decision: Reject — judged incremental rather than a significant conceptual advance.
- Limited novelty: framework largely builds on prior “compare-aggregate” work rather than introducing a new paradigm.
- Incremental improvement: findings are empirical observations without strong theoretical justification for why element-wise ops work best.
- Lack of deep analysis: paper doesn’t sufficiently investigate when and why particular comparison functions succeed or fail.
- Positive: paper is well-written and clearly explains methodology and experiments.
- Positive: extensive evaluation on four datasets strengthens empirical validity.
- Positive: authors provide code, improving reproducibility.
- Suggestion: include detailed qualitative analysis with example cases where subtraction/multiplication excel or fail.
- Suggestion: better justify why element-wise operations are a “good compromise” and relate this to dataset/task properties.
- Suggestion: expand discussion of the preprocessing layer and its order-insensitivity and impact across tasks.
- Suggestion: discuss computational costs of comparison functions (e.g., NTN) and how cost influenced method choices.
- Questions for authors: provide concrete examples where element-wise ops outperform neural/tensor networks and explain why.
- Questions for authors: report sensitivity to hyperparameters (embedding dimensionality, CNN filter sizes) and consider other element-wise operations.
