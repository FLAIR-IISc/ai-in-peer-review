- Proposes a “compare-aggregate” framework for text sequence matching with focus on systematically evaluating different comparison functions.
- Implements the model and evaluates on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), showing competitive or state-of-the-art performance.
- Well-motivated, clearly written paper with thorough empirical evaluation.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Key claim: choice of comparison function significantly affects performance, supported by experiments across datasets.
- Experimental setup is robust: diverse datasets, multiple baselines, and reproducible code release.
- Ablation study (Table 4) highlights importance of individual components.
- Suggestion: report results without preprocessing and attention layers to quantify their contributions.
- Suggestion: improve Figure 2 readability (larger fonts, clearer cues, color).
- Suggestion: add a discussion of limitations (e.g., scalability to long sequences, computational cost).
- Suggestion: expand intuition for why element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) perform well and what semantic relations they capture.
- Question: did you try order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., RNN) for MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and how did it compare?
- Question: why were learning rate and batch size tuned only for WikiQA—computational constraints or sufficiency of defaults?
- Question: please elaborate on the semantic relationships captured by element-wise comparison functions.
- Question: any initial ideas and anticipated challenges for adapting the model to multi-task learning?
