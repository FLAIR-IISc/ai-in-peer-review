This paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework that performs word-level matching followed by aggregation with convolutional neural networks. It compares six different comparison functions and evaluates them on four datasets. Extensive experimental results have been reported and compared against various published baselines.

The paper is well-written overall and presents a thorough investigation of the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles an important problem in NLP, and the approach is well-motivated and empirically validated. The results demonstrate that the proposed model, utilizing a simple yet effective comparison function, achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance across diverse tasks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Clear Problem Definition:** The paper clearly defines the problem of sequence matching, outlining its importance in several NLP tasks like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The motivation for focusing on the comparison function within the compare-aggregate framework is also well articulated.
2. **Strong Empirical Validation:** The authors provide extensive experimental results on four different datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – demonstrating the generalizability of their approach. The comparison with existing baselines is comprehensive and shows that the proposed model performs favorably.  The ablation study further supports the contributions, showing the importance of key components.
3. **Novelty in Comparison Function Exploration:** While the compare-aggregate framework itself is not entirely new, the systematic evaluation of different comparison functions, particularly the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), is a significant contribution. The finding that these simple functions outperform more complex alternatives like neural tensor networks is noteworthy and provides valuable insights.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   Page 4, line 5: "including a some" should be "including some".
*   The paper could benefit from a discussion of the computational complexity of the different comparison functions. Understanding the trade-offs between performance and efficiency would be valuable.
*   The visualization in Figure 2 is somewhat difficult to interpret, particularly in a printed format. Increasing the font size or using a clearer visual encoding would enhance its readability.
*   While the ablation study shows the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers, it would be helpful to see a more detailed analysis of their individual contributions. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of the dimensionality `d` for the word embeddings? Did you experiment with different values, and if so, how did they affect performance?
*   The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for MovieQA and InsuranceQA. Could you explain why you believe this is sufficient for these tasks, and whether you considered incorporating positional information?
*   The results show SUBMULT+NN often performs best.  Could you analyze the learned weights of the final NN layer in SUBMULT+NN? Does this reveal anything about how the SUB and MULT components are being combined?
*   You mention that the comparison functions based on element-wise operations are not widely used. Do you have any hypotheses as to why this might be the case, despite their strong performance?
