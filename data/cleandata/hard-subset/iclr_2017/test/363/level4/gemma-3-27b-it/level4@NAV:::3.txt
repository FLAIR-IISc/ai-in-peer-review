The paper introduces a broadly applicable modeling framework for natural language understanding tasks involving two related text inputs – for example, a question and a supporting document. The approach utilizes soft attention to establish token-level alignments between the inputs. These alignments, represented as query-result pairs, are then processed by a comparison function to generate representations that are aggregated via a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a unified vector for output computation. The paper effectively demonstrates the viability of this modeling strategy and provides a thorough empirical investigation of the comparison function’s design.

This work is particularly relevant given the current state of the field. Problems requiring understanding relationships between texts are central to NLP and are becoming increasingly tractable through representation learning. The proposed approach is both conceptually simple and effective, delivering strong performance. While the contribution builds upon prior work (including that of the authors and Parikh et al.), it contains sufficient novelty and impact to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

Specific points for consideration:

- The current model implementation, particularly for longer sequences (excluding SNLI), exhibits a lack of sensitivity to word order. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, its potential impact on performance is significant and could benefit from explicit discussion in the introduction or conclusion.
- The attention mechanism appears more aligned with the general/bilinear approach detailed in Luong et al. (2015) than with earlier Bahdanau-style attention. A citation of Luong et al. (or a similarly relevant reference) would be appropriate.
- Given the potential for overfitting due to the NTN’s parameter count, were lower-dimensional output configurations (e.g., an *llm* tensor with reduced output dimension *m*) explored?
- The SubMultNN component bears a strong resemblance to the sentence matching strategy presented in the Lili Mou paper cited; this similarity should be acknowledged.
- Could you clarify the rationale behind using identical preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer in step (1)? Different weighting schemes may be optimal for each input.
