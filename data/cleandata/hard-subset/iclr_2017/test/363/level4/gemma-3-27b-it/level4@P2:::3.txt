This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching tasks, a common requirement in many NLP problems like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The model employs an attention mechanism to align words between two sequences, followed by a comparison function to represent their relationship, and finally aggregates these representations using a CNN for prediction. A key contribution lies in a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions, demonstrating that element-wise operations can outperform more complex methods like neural networks and neural tensor networks.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a sound approach to a well-motivated problem and demonstrates strong empirical results across multiple datasets. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions is a particularly valuable contribution, offering insights into effective strategies for sequence matching.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The proposed “compare-aggregate” framework is a logical and widely used approach for sequence matching. The paper’s contribution isn’t necessarily a novel framework *per se*, but rather a thorough investigation and strong empirical validation of this framework across diverse tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). This broad evaluation strengthens the claim that the approach is generally effective. The key finding – that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) perform competitively, and often outperform, more complex models – is insightful and potentially impactful. These simpler functions are computationally efficient and require fewer parameters, making them attractive alternatives. The ablation studies and visualizations in Section 3 further support these findings, providing evidence for the model's behavior. The code release is also a positive aspect, enabling reproducibility and further research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not affecting the decision):**

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The model, particularly for longer sequences, doesn’t inherently consider word order. While acknowledged, emphasizing this limitation more prominently (e.g., in the introduction or discussion) would provide a more balanced view of the model's capabilities. Investigating methods to incorporate word order information could be a direction for future work.
*   **Attention Mechanism Citation:** The attention mechanism appears more aligned with Luong et al. (2015) than Bahdanau et al. (2014). Updating the citation to reflect this would improve accuracy.
*   **NTN Parameter Reduction:** Given the potential for overfitting with the neural tensor network (NTN), exploring a reduced-dimensionality output layer (llm tensor) could be beneficial and warrant a brief mention in the experimental analysis.
*   **Connection to Prior Work (Mou et al.):** The SubMultNN function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy in Mou et al. (2016). Explicitly acknowledging this connection would strengthen the related work section.
*   **Preprocessing Layer Parameterization:** Investigating task-specific parameters for the preprocessing layer (Equation 1) – allowing different weighting of words in the question versus the answer – could potentially improve performance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the computational cost differences between the various comparison functions? A table summarizing the number of parameters for each function would be helpful.
*   The results show consistently strong performance with the SUB and MULT comparison functions. Do you have any intuition as to *why* these simple element-wise operations are so effective? Is it related to capturing specific types of semantic relationships?
*   You mention that the attention layer is more crucial for tasks with larger sequence length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA). Can you provide any examples of attention weights to illustrate this phenomenon and demonstrate what the model is focusing on?
*   You mention that the preprocessing layer is order insensitive. Have you considered incorporating positional embeddings or other methods to inject some degree of word order information into this layer, and if so, did this impact performance?
