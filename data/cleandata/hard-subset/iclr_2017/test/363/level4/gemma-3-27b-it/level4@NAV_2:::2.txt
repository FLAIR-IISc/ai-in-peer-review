This paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework that performs word-level matching followed by aggregation with convolutional neural networks. It compares six different comparison functions and evaluates them on four datasets. Extensive experimental results have been reported and compared against various published baselines.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a thorough investigation of comparison functions within a well-established framework for sequence matching, demonstrating strong performance across multiple datasets and outperforming existing baselines on several. The systematic evaluation and insightful analysis justify acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The paper tackles a significant problem in NLP – sequence matching – which is crucial for tasks like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The “compare-aggregate” framework is well-motivated, building upon prior work (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016) and identifying a key gap in the existing literature: a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions. The experiments convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed model, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The ablation studies (Table 4) further support the contributions, confirming the importance of both the preprocessing and attention layers. The results clearly support the claims made regarding the efficacy of the framework and the surprisingly strong performance of element-wise comparison functions.

**Additional Feedback:**
The paper is generally well-written, but a few minor edits could improve clarity. For instance, on page 4, line 5, “including a some” should be “including some.” The visualizations in Figure 2 are somewhat difficult to interpret, especially in printed form; enhancing their clarity would be beneficial. It would be valuable to understand the impact of removing the preprocessing and attention steps; providing results without these components would further illuminate their contribution.  Considering the strong performance achieved, exploring the impact of different embedding sizes (d) beyond the current setting could be a direction for future work. It may also be useful to analyze the learned attention weights to better understand *what* the model is attending to when making its predictions.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  Could you provide the results of the experiments without the preprocessing and attention layers to quantify their impact on performance?
2.  Figure 2 is difficult to interpret. Could you provide a higher-resolution version with clearer labeling or consider a different visualization approach?
3.  The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of element-wise operations as comparison functions. Do these functions exhibit any specific advantages in terms of computational efficiency compared to more complex neural network-based approaches?
