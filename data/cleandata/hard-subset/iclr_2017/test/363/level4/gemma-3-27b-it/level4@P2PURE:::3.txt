The paper introduces a general modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks involving two aligned textual inputs, such as question answering and reading comprehension. The method first employs soft attention to establish alignments between the tokens of the two texts. A comparison function then leverages these alignments – represented as query-result pairs – to generate representations, which are subsequently aggregated by a convolutional neural network (CNN) into a single vector used for output computation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this modeling strategy and provide a thorough empirical analysis of the comparison component.

This work is particularly relevant, as language understanding problems of this type represent a significant open challenge in NLP and are now becoming tractable with representation learning techniques. The proposed approach is both conceptually simple and well-justified, and the results demonstrate its strong performance. While the work builds upon prior research (including that of the authors and Parikh et al.), it makes enough substantive contributions to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

Specific points for consideration:

- The model, as implemented for longer sequence problems (excluding SNLI), appears insensitive to word order. Although the paper acknowledges this limitation, its potential impact on performance is significant enough to merit a brief discussion in the introduction or conclusion.
- The attention mechanism appears more aligned with the general/bilinear approach of Luong et al. (2015) than with the earlier work of Bahdanau et al. – citing the former (or a similarly relevant reference) would be appropriate.
- Given the potential for overfitting due to the large number of parameters in the NTN, did the authors explore a version with a reduced output dimension (e.g., transforming an *ll* input to an *llm* tensor)?
- It would be beneficial to acknowledge the similarity between the SubMultNN and the sentence-level matching strategy presented in the Lili Mou paper cited.
- Could you elaborate on the rationale for using identical preprocessing parameters for the question and answer in step (1)? Different weighting schemes may be optimal for each input.
