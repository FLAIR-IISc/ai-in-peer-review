The paper presents a general approach to modeling for natural language understanding problems with two distinct textual inputs (such as a question and a source text) that can be aligned in some way. In this approach, soft attention is first used to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts, then a comparison function uses the resulting alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and attention results) to derive representations that are aggregated by a CNN into a single vector from which an output can be computed. The paper demonstrates that this approach is effective and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

I recommend accepting this paper. The work is timely, as language understanding problems involving comparing two sequences are a central challenge in NLP and are becoming increasingly addressable with representation learning methods. The proposed approach is straightforward, reasonable, and yields strong results across diverse tasks. While potentially incremental, the work’s contributions are substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

Specifically, the paper successfully demonstrates the effectiveness of a “compare-aggregate” framework across four different datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – showcasing its generality. Furthermore, the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions reveals that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), often overlooked, can outperform standard neural network and neural tensor network approaches. This finding is a significant contribution and provides valuable insights for future research.

Here are some suggestions to further improve the paper:

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The model, particularly for longer sequences, does not consider word order. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, briefly discussing its potential impact on performance in the introduction or discussion section would be beneficial.
*   **Attention Mechanism Reference:** The attention strategy appears to be more closely aligned with the general/bilinear approach proposed by Luong et al. (2015) than the earlier Bahdanau et al. (2014) work. Consider citing Luong et al. or another directly relevant reference.
*   **NTN Parameter Efficiency:** Given the potential for overfitting with the Neural Tensor Network (NTN), did you explore a version with a reduced output dimension *m* (resulting in an *llm* tensor) to improve parameter efficiency?
*   **Connection to Previous Work:** The SubMultNN comparison function bears resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy in the work by Mou et al. (2016). Acknowledging this connection would strengthen the related work section.
*   **Preprocessing Layer Parameters:** Is there a rationale for using the same parameters for preprocessing both the question and answer in Equation (1)? Different weighting might be appropriate for each sequence. 

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of dimensionality *l* for the hidden layers? Was this determined through experimentation, or is it a standard value?
*   The paper highlights the importance of the attention and preprocessing layers. Could you provide more details on the sensitivity of the results to the hyperparameters of these layers (e.g., learning rate, dropout)?
*   For the datasets where you achieved state-of-the-art results, how statistically significant are the improvements over the baselines? Providing confidence intervals or p-values would strengthen the claims.
