This paper presents a compare-aggregate model for sequence matching, a common requirement in NLP tasks such as question answering and textual entailment. The model employs an attention mechanism followed by element-wise operations for comparison, and convolutional neural networks for aggregation. The core contribution lies in a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions, demonstrating that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication consistently outperform more complex methods—including standard neural networks and neural tensor networks—across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI).

**Reject.** While the paper demonstrates solid empirical results and a thorough evaluation, the work feels incremental and lacks substantial novelty. The compare-aggregate framework itself is well-established in the literature, and the primary contribution—the observation that element-wise operations can be effective comparison functions—feels intuitive and lacks deep theoretical justification. 

The paper's supporting arguments are largely empirical. The authors convincingly show that their model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on the chosen datasets. The ablation studies help to demonstrate the importance of the individual components (preprocessing and attention). However, the paper doesn't offer a strong *explanation* for why element-wise operations are so effective. Why do these simple functions outperform more expressive models? The empirical success is not fully coupled with insights into the underlying mechanisms. The claim that the framework is "effective for a wide range of sequence matching problems" is supported by the four datasets, but this is a limited scope and doesn't guarantee generalizability.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Qualitative Analysis:** A qualitative analysis of *what* kinds of sentence pairs are best handled by each comparison function would be highly valuable. Illustrating specific examples where subtraction or multiplication excels—or fails—would provide a more nuanced understanding of their strengths and weaknesses.
*   **Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis on the datasets could reveal patterns in the types of errors the model makes, potentially guiding future research.
*   **Theoretical Motivation:**  Exploring potential theoretical reasons for the success of element-wise operations (e.g., relating them to specific properties of word embeddings or semantic similarity) would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discussing the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices would improve reproducibility and provide further insights into the model’s behavior.
*  **Comparison to Stronger Baselines**: While the reported baselines are relevant, including comparison to more recent and stronger models, where available, would further contextualize the contribution.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a more intuitive explanation for why element-wise subtraction and multiplication perform so well as comparison functions? Are there specific properties of word embeddings or semantic relationships that make these operations particularly effective?
*   Could you provide specific examples of sentence pairs where the element-wise operations significantly outperform the neural network or neural tensor network comparison functions?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality of the word embeddings and the architecture of the CNN aggregator?
*   Were there any surprising patterns in the errors the model made? If so, what do these patterns suggest about the limitations of the current approach?
