The paper presents a general approach to modeling natural language understanding problems involving two distinct textual inputs – such as a question and a source text – where some form of alignment between the texts is possible. The approach first uses soft attention to derive alignments between the tokens of the two texts, then employs a comparison function that leverages these alignments (represented as pairs of attention queries and results) to generate representations. These representations are aggregated by a CNN into a single vector, from which an output is computed. The paper demonstrates this as a viable overall modeling strategy and offers a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component.

This work is timely. Language understanding problems of this type represent a major open challenge in NLP, and are only now becoming addressable with representation learning methods. The paper presents a general and reasonable approach that achieves strong results. While the work is somewhat incremental (relative to the authors’ previous work or that of Parikh et al.), it makes enough substantial contributions to warrant a strong recommendation for acceptance.

Details:

- The model, at least in its implementation for problems with longer sequences (all except SNLI), is insensitive to word order. While empirically competitive, this insensitivity inherently limits its potential performance. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but it seems significant enough to merit a brief discussion in the introduction or conclusion.

- If I understand correctly, your attention strategy is more closely based on the general/bilinear approach of Luong et al. (2015) than on the earlier Bahdanau work. You should consider citing the former (or another more directly relevant reference for that strategy).

- Given that the NTN has a large number of parameters and thus risks overfitting, did you explore using a version with an input dimension *l* and a smaller output dimension *m*, resulting in an *l*x*l*x*m* tensor?

- It would be helpful to note that SubMultNN closely resembles the sentence-level matching strategy described in the Lili Mou paper you cite.

- Is there a specific reason for using the same preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer in step (1)? These texts might benefit from different weighting schemes during preprocessing.
