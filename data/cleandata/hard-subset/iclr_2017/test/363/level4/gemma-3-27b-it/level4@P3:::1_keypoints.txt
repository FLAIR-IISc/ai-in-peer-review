- Proposes a compare-aggregate model that does word-level matching followed by CNN aggregation for sequence matching tasks.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including element-wise subtraction and multiplication.  
- Empirical results across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) show simple element-wise operations often outperform more complex functions (NN, NTN).  
- Ablation studies (Table 4) help identify component importance.  
- Decision: Reject — contribution considered incremental with limited novelty for a top venue.  
- Improvements shown are often marginal, even if sometimes statistically significant.  
- Paper demonstrates that simple ops work, but fails to explain why they are effective (lacks theoretical/insightful analysis).  
- Novelty is limited given prior work (e.g., Mou et al., 2016) and the paper primarily offers systematic comparison rather than a new concept.  
- Baseline comparisons are sometimes outdated (notably WikiQA) and not fully comprehensive.  
- Lacks deeper analysis of failure/success cases and qualitative examples illustrating differences between comparison functions.  
- Suggested improvements: stronger motivation for focusing on comparison functions and clearer limitations discussion (e.g., sensitivity to embeddings, inability to capture complex semantics).  
- Suggested analyses: visualize learned weights for NN/NTN, provide qualitative sentence-pair examples, and expand ablations to preprocessing and attention layers.  
- Suggest experimenting with order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., RNNs), different embedding choices, and alternative aggregation mechanisms (e.g., attention-based aggregation).  
- Questions for authors: intuition for why subtraction/multiplication succeed, results of incorporating order information, examples showing divergent behavior, sensitivity to embedding choice, and comparisons with more sophisticated aggregation methods.
