This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for natural language processing tasks requiring semantic comparison of text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. 
The core framework applies a convolutional neural network (aggregation) following an element-wise operation (comparison) performed on the attentive outputs of LSTMs. 
A key contribution of this work is a comparative analysis of different methods for matching text sequences, demonstrating that element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally outperform other approaches across four datasets.
However, a potential weakness is that this work represents an incremental improvement rather than a substantial innovation. A qualitative evaluation of how subtraction, multiplication, and other comparison functions perform on diverse sentence types would be particularly insightful.

The model is well-motivated, building on existing LSTM-based attentive models by explicitly focusing on the comparison stage. The authors clearly articulate the rationale behind exploring different comparison functions, and the experimental results are presented comprehensively. The choice of datasets – including Stanford Natural Language Inference (SNLI), Multi-Genre Natural Language Inference (MultiNLI), Question Matching Dataset (QMD), and Stanford Question Answering Dataset (SQuAD) – provides a good evaluation across varied tasks and data characteristics. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each component of the proposed model.

The comparison of subtraction and multiplication as comparison functions is particularly compelling. While both outperform alternatives, the consistent advantage of these two operations warrants further investigation. It would be beneficial to understand *why* these operations are so effective; is it due to their sensitivity to differences in magnitude or sign, or some other factor? 

Regarding potential improvements, while the quantitative results are strong, a more in-depth qualitative analysis would significantly strengthen the paper. Examining specific examples where subtraction excels versus those where multiplication is superior could reveal valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. For example, are there cases where negation or subtle semantic differences are better captured by one operation versus the other? This analysis could also explore the impact of sentence length and complexity on the performance of different comparison functions.

Furthermore, the paper could briefly discuss the computational cost of the different comparison operations. Although likely minimal, acknowledging potential trade-offs between performance and efficiency would be helpful.

Overall, this is a solid contribution with clear benefits in the area of semantic text comparison. While the work is not groundbreaking, the thorough empirical evaluation and insightful comparison of different methods make it a worthwhile addition to the literature. The suggested qualitative analysis would significantly enhance the paper's impact and provide a more nuanced understanding of the proposed approach. 

**Confidence in this review:** 8/10. I believe the paper is well-executed and the results are reliable, but the incremental nature of the work prevents a higher confidence score.
