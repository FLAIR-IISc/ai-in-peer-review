This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for NLP tasks requiring semantic comparison of text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The core framework involves applying a convolutional neural network (aggregation) following an element-wise operation (comparison) on the attentive outputs of LSTMs. The paper’s key contribution lies in systematically comparing several different methods for matching text sequences, demonstrating that element-wise subtraction and multiplication operations generally outperform others across four datasets. 

**Decision: Reject**

My primary reason for rejection is that, while technically sound, the work presents incremental improvements rather than a significant advance. The core idea of a compare-aggregate framework is well-established in the literature, and the novelty of this work resides primarily in the empirical comparison of different comparison functions. While valuable, this comparison does not represent a substantial conceptual breakthrough.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The “compare-aggregate” framework is not novel, as acknowledged by the authors themselves, citing several prior works (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016). The paper builds upon this existing framework rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.
2. **Incremental Improvement:** While the finding that element-wise operations perform well is interesting, it’s presented as an empirical observation rather than a theoretically motivated discovery. The authors posit reasons for this performance, but further investigation into *why* these operations succeed would strengthen the contribution.
3. **Lack of Deep Analysis:** While the results demonstrate performance gains, there’s limited in-depth analysis of *when* and *why* certain comparison functions work better than others. The authors mention the importance of semantic similarity but don’t fully explore how the different comparison functions capture or fail to capture semantic relationships. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experiments.
*   The extensive evaluation on four datasets is commendable and strengthens the empirical validity of the findings.
*   The code availability is a positive aspect.
*   Consider adding a more detailed qualitative analysis. Examining specific examples where subtraction/multiplication excel (or fail) compared to other methods could provide valuable insights. Specifically, analyzing cases where the element-wise operations capture nuanced semantic relationships that other methods miss would be beneficial.
*   Could the authors elaborate on why they believe element-wise operations work so well, beyond simply stating they’re a “good compromise?” Is there a connection to specific properties of the datasets or tasks?
*   The preprocessing layer is briefly discussed. More details about its impact, particularly its order-insensitivity, and whether this is a beneficial property for all tasks, would be helpful.
*   Could the authors discuss the computational cost of different comparison functions, especially NTN, and whether this influenced their choice to favor simpler operations?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide examples of input sequences where the element-wise subtraction or multiplication functions demonstrably outperform the neural network or tensor network approaches, and explain *why* this difference occurs?
*   What is the intuition behind using element-wise operations as a “good compromise” between flexible neural networks and restrictive similarity measures?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality of the word embeddings and the size of the convolutional filters?
*   Have you considered exploring other element-wise operations beyond subtraction and multiplication?
