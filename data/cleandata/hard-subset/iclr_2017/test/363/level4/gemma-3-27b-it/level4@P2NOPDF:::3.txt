This paper introduces a broadly applicable modeling approach for natural language understanding tasks involving two related text inputs, such as question answering or textual entailment. The core idea is to first utilize soft attention mechanisms to establish alignments between the tokens of the two input texts. These alignments – represented as query-result pairs – are then fed into a comparison function to generate contextualized representations. Finally, a convolutional neural network (CNN) aggregates these representations into a single vector used for downstream output computation. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this general framework and provide a thorough empirical analysis focusing specifically on the comparison function component.

This work is particularly relevant given the current state of NLP research. Tasks requiring understanding of relationships between texts remain a significant challenge, and representation learning techniques are only now beginning to offer promising solutions. The proposed approach is both conceptually simple and practically effective, achieving strong performance on several benchmark datasets. While the contribution builds upon prior work (including the authors’ own and that of Parikh et al.), it offers enough novel insights and substantial improvements to warrant strong recommendation for acceptance.

Here are some specific points for the authors to consider:

* **Word Order Insensitivity:** The model, as implemented for tasks with longer sequences (excluding SNLI), appears insensitive to word order. While the paper acknowledges this limitation, its potential impact on overall performance is significant and should be briefly discussed in the introduction or discussion sections to provide broader context. 

* **Attention Mechanism Citation:** The attention mechanism employed seems more aligned with the general/bilinear approach described in Luong et al. (2015) than with the earlier work of Bahdanau et al. (2015). Updating the citations to reflect this closer relationship would be beneficial.

* **NTN Parameterization:** Given the potential for overfitting due to the large parameter count in the NTN component, did the authors explore using a reduced dimensionality output layer (e.g., mapping from input dimension *l* to a smaller output dimension *m*, resulting in an *llm* tensor)? This could potentially improve generalization.

* **SubMultNN Similarity:** The SubMultNN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy presented in the Lili Mou paper cited in the work. Highlighting this connection would be useful for readers familiar with that approach.

* **Preprocessing Parameter Sharing:** In step (1) of the process, the authors apply the same parameters for preprocessing both the question and the answer text. Given that these inputs may require different weighting of features for optimal performance, could the authors explain the rationale for this shared parameterization or explore the potential benefits of independent preprocessing configurations?
