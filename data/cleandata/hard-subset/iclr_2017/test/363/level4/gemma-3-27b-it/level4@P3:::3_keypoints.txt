- Decision: Accept — well-motivated, empirically sound paper addressing an important problem.
- Proposed model: "compare-aggregate" framework using attention, comparison functions, and CNN-based aggregation.
- Key contribution: systematic evaluation of six comparison functions across datasets.
- Main finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex models (NNs, NTN).
- Empirical validation: competitive or state-of-the-art results on four diverse datasets.
- Ablation studies and analysis strengthen the empirical claims.
- Code release is a positive aspect for reproducibility.
- Concern: model is largely insensitive to word order (except SNLI); implications should be discussed.
- Suggestion: discuss incorporating order-sensitive mechanisms or future work exploring them.
- Citation fix: correctly cite Luong et al. (2015) or equivalent for the attention mechanism.
- NTN suggestion: consider reduced-dimensionality NTN outputs to mitigate overfitting and clarify performance differences.
- Related work: acknowledge similarity between SUBMULT+NN and Lili Mou et al.’s approach.
- Preprocessing: explore separate preprocessing parameters for question and answer sequences.
- Q1 for authors: why are element-wise comparisons less impacted by word-order insensitivity than parameter-rich functions?
- Q2 for authors: were simple order-sensitive features (e.g., position embeddings) tried with this framework, and what were the results?
- Q3 for authors: what is the combined effect of removing both preprocessing and attention layers versus removing each individually?
- Q4 for authors: are dataset characteristics correlated with the optimal comparison function choice?
- Q5 for authors: how were hyperparameters (especially hidden dimensionality l) chosen — tuned experimentally or fixed from prior work/computation limits?
