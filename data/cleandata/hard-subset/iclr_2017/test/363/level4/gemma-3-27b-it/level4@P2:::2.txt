This paper introduces a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching, employing word-level matching followed by aggregation using convolutional neural networks. The core contribution lies in a systematic comparison of six different comparison functions – including element-wise operations often overlooked in prior work – across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The authors demonstrate strong performance, often surpassing existing state-of-the-art results, and provide ablation studies to support their design choices.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated approach to a fundamental NLP problem – sequence matching. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions is particularly valuable and the strong empirical results across multiple datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed framework.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles the important problem of sequence matching, a core component of numerous NLP tasks like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The proposed compare-aggregate framework is a logical extension of recent work in this area (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016), and the authors clearly position their contribution within the existing literature. The motivation for exploring diverse comparison functions – moving beyond standard neural networks to investigate element-wise operations – is well-reasoned. The empirical validation is robust; the model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on four different datasets, demonstrating its generalizability. The ablation studies (Table 4) provide further insight into the importance of different components of the framework. The code release is also a significant positive aspect.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Preprocessing & Attention:** While the paper presents the model overview, a more detailed justification for the preprocessing and attention layers would be beneficial. The ablation study hints at their importance, but further analysis exploring *why* these components contribute to performance would be valuable.
*   **Figure 2 Clarity:** The visualization in Figure 2 is difficult to interpret, especially in printed form. Consider increasing the font size, using a clearer color scheme, or employing a different visualization technique to better highlight the relevant patterns.
*   **Minor Typo:** Page 4, line 5: "including a some" should read "including some."
*   **Comparison Function Analysis:** A deeper dive into the characteristics of the datasets that favor certain comparison functions (e.g., why SUB and MULT perform well on WikiQA but not as well on SNLI) would strengthen the analysis.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the design choices behind the preprocessing layer? Specifically, how does the use of gates in LSTM and GRU influence the model’s performance, and why did you opt for an order-insensitive approach?
*   Given the importance of the attention layer, did you experiment with different attention mechanisms beyond the standard one described in Section 2.2? If so, what were the results?
*   Regarding Figure 2, can you provide a higher-resolution version or an alternative visualization that clearly illustrates the CNN's focus on matched word representations?
*   For the datasets where your model doesn't achieve state-of-the-art results, what are the limitations of your approach compared to the best-performing baselines?
