This paper presents a general approach for modeling natural language understanding problems with two different text inputs (e.g., a question and a source text) that can be aligned in some way. In this method, mild attention is first used to create an alignment between the tokens of the two texts, then a comparison function uses the resulting alignments (presented as attention queries and attention results) to generate a representation that is combined by CNN into a single vector, from which an output can be computed. This paper presents it as an overall modeling technique that can work quite well and also provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison element of the model.

This work is timely. Such language understanding problems are a major unsolved issue in NLP, and are on the verge of being solved through representation learning methods. This work presents a simple and reasonable approach, and shows that it can give good results. This work is close to (compared to their previous work or Parikh et al.), but it contributes in a significant way that I would strongly recommend adopting.

Details:
- The model, as implemented for at least long sequence problems (everything except SNLI), is not sensitive to word order. It is empirically competitive, but this insensitivity places a strong upper limit on its performance. The paper clarifies this, but it seems important enough to mention briefly in the introduction or discussion section.
- If I understand correctly, your attention strategy is based on Lung et al.'s '15 general/quadratic strategy, rather than the previous Bahdanou work. You should probably cite your previous one (or any other reference more directly relevant to that strategy).
- Since there is a risk of overfitting due to the large number of parameters in the NNTN, have you tried using a version with input dimension l and small output dimension m (thus an llm tensor)?
- You should probably mention that SubMultNN looks a lot like the sentence-level matching strategy in Lily Ma's paper.
- Is there any reason to use the same parameters for pre-processing the question and answer in (1)? Different things may have to be given more importance for these.
