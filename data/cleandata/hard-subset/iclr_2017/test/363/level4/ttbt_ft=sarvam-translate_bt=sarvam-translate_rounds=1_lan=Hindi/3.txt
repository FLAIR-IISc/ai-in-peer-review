This paper presents a general approach for modeling natural language understanding problems with two different text inputs (e.g., a question and a source text) that can be aligned in some way. In this approach, soft attention is first used to obtain alignment between the tokens of the two texts, then a comparison function obtains a representation using the resulting alignment (represented as a pair of attention queries and attention results) which is aggregated into a single vector by CNN to calculate an output. This paper presents it as a holistic modeling strategy that can be built to work well, and provides a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component of the model.

This work is timely. Such language understanding problems are a major open issue in NLP, and are on the verge of being addressed by representation learning methods. This work presents a general approach that is straightforward and reasonable, and shows that it can yield good results. This work is on the edge of incremental (relative to their previous work or Parikh et al.), but it contributes in ways that I would strongly recommend acceptance.

Details:
- The model has been implemented for problems with at least long sequences (everything except SNTL), not sensitive to word order. It is empirically competitive, but its insensitivity places a strong upper limit on its performance. The paper clarifies this, but it seems important enough to justify a brief mention in the introduction or discussion sections.
- If I understand correctly, your attention strategy is more closely based on the general/binary strategy of Luong et al. '15 than the previous adversarial work. You should probably cite the former (or some other more directly relevant reference to that strategy).
- Since the NTN runs the risk of overfitting due to its large number of parameters, did you try using a version with input dimension l and a smaller output dimension m (hence an llm tensor)?
- You should probably note that SubMultNN looks like a sentence-level matching strategy in your mentioned Lily Mou paper.
- Is there any reason why you use the same parameters to preprocess the question and answer in (1)? They may need to be heavily weighted differently.
