## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a comprehensive study of a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, a crucial task in NLP. The authors explore different comparison functions within this framework, evaluating their performance across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of this general framework and, surprisingly, finding that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and provides a thorough experimental evaluation.

**Reject.** While the work is solid and presents interesting findings, the results, while positive, are not groundbreaking enough to warrant acceptance at a competitive AI conference. The core idea of exploring different comparison functions within a compare-aggregate framework is valuable, but the improvements over existing state-of-the-art methods are not substantial enough to represent a significant advance in the field. The performance gains, while consistent, are often marginal and don't fully justify the extensive experimentation.

The paper effectively demonstrates the viability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks. The systematic evaluation of six comparison functions is a strong point, and the finding that simple element-wise operations can be competitive (or even superior) to more complex models is a valuable insight. The ablation studies and visualizations further support the claims made in the paper. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous work and position their contribution within the existing literature. The code availability is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* these simple comparison functions perform well. Is it simply a regularization effect, or is there a specific property of these datasets that makes element-wise operations particularly suitable? Furthermore, the paper doesn’t explore the interaction between the preprocessing layer and the comparison function in detail. It would be beneficial to understand how the choice of preprocessing impacts the performance of different comparison functions. 

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on the potential reasons why the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) perform so well? Do you have any hypotheses regarding the underlying mechanisms at play?
*   How sensitive is the performance to the dimensionality of the word embeddings (d)? Did you experiment with different embedding sizes, and if so, how did it affect the results?
*   You mention the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive. Did you explore incorporating order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., using LSTMs) and how did that impact the results, particularly on tasks where word order is crucial?
*   The CNN aggregation layer is relatively simple. Did you explore more sophisticated aggregation methods (e.g., attention-based aggregation) and how did they compare to the CNN approach?
