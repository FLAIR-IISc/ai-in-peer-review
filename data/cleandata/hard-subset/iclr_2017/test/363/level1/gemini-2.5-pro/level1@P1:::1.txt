### **Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes and evaluates a general neural network framework, termed "compare-aggregate," for the task of matching text sequences. This framework is relevant to a wide range of NLP problems, including textual entailment, answer selection, and machine comprehension. The model first performs fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then aggregates these local comparison results using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction.

The main contributions of the work are twofold. First, it demonstrates the general effectiveness of this architectural paradigm by applying a single, consistent model to four different and challenging datasets (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and MovieQA), achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all of them. Second, and more importantly, the paper conducts a systematic investigation into the core "comparison function" used for matching word-level representations. It evaluates six different functions, ranging from complex, parameterized models like Neural Networks (NN) and Neural Tensor Networks (NTN) to simpler, more direct functions based on cosine/Euclidean similarity and element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication). The key finding is that the simpler, element-wise functions (SUB and MULT) consistently outperform their more complex counterparts, suggesting they are a highly effective way to capture local semantic similarity.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a strong empirical paper with clear and valuable contributions. The primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation and Generality:** The paper's strength lies in its comprehensive evaluation across four distinct tasks. By showing that a single, well-designed architecture excels in different settings, the authors provide compelling evidence for the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework. This cross-task validation is a significant contribution beyond papers that introduce a model for a single task.
2.  **Novel and Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The systematic study of the comparison function `f` is the paper's core novelty. The finding that simple, non-parametric, element-wise operations like subtraction and multiplication are more effective than heavily parameterized functions like NN or NTN is both non-obvious and impactful. It provides valuable architectural guidance for future work on sequence matching and suggests that preserving dimensional information about vector differences/similarities is crucial.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the claims are strongly supported by the experimental results.

The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature at the time. While several concurrent works were exploring similar "compare-aggregate" ideas (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016), this paper is unique in its focus on systematically analyzing the comparison component and validating the overall architecture's robustness across a diverse set of problems. The model architecture itself is a sensible and effective combination of existing components (gated preprocessing, attention, CNN aggregation), but the careful study of how to best implement the "compare" step is what sets this work apart.

The results presented in Table 3 are convincing. The proposed model, especially with the `SUBMULT+NN` comparison function, establishes a new state of the art on three of the four datasets and is highly competitive on the fourth (SNLI). More importantly, the table clearly shows the superiority of `SUB`, `MULT`, and `SUBMULT+NN` over `NN` and `NTN`. This empirical result directly supports the central claim of the paper. The ablation study in Table 4 further strengthens the paper by demonstrating the positive contribution of the attention and preprocessing layers, validating the key components of the overall design.

In summary, this paper provides both a strong, general-purpose model for sequence matching and a valuable insight into what makes such models work well. It is a solid piece of empirical research that would be of great interest to the NLP and representation learning communities.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Order-Insensitive Preprocessing:** The preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. While the results are excellent regardless, it would be beneficial to include a brief discussion on the potential limitations of this choice. For tasks where word order and syntax are paramount, this could be a drawback. The paper mentions that LSTM was incorporated for the SNLI task, but this is not detailed. Clarifying how this was done and perhaps showing a direct comparison with the order-insensitive version on SNLI would make the paper more self-contained and reproducible.
*   **One-Directional Attention:** The model uses a one-directional attention mechanism (sequence A attends to sequence Q). This is a reasonable design choice, but it differs from other contemporary models that use symmetric, two-directional attention. A short justification for this choice (e.g., efficiency, or empirical findings that it was sufficient) would be helpful for the reader.
*   **Visualization Analysis:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a nice addition to provide intuition. However, the explanation is slightly high-level. It could be improved by being more specific about what the visualized values represent (e.g., activations of specific CNN filters across the sequence) and what a high activation in that context implies about the features the model has learned to detect.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  In Section 2.2, you state, "In our experiments, we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task." Could you please elaborate on how the LSTM was integrated into the preprocessing formula? Was this change critical for the strong performance on SNLI compared to the order-insensitive version?
2.  The `SUBMULT+NN` function is the top performer. This suggests that combining the "difference" signal (`SUB`) and the "similarity/agreement" signal (`MULT`) is powerful. Did you perform any analysis to see if one of these two components contributes more than the other across the different tasks?
3.  The performance of `EUCCOS` is surprisingly strong on MovieQA, nearly matching the best model despite producing only a 2-dimensional comparison vector. Do you have a hypothesis for why this very explicit and low-dimensional similarity signal is so effective for that specific task, which involves matching against a long plot synopsis?
