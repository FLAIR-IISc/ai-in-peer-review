- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework that performs word-level comparisons then aggregates with a CNN for text matching.  
- Applies a single unified model to four diverse benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) achieving SOTA or competitive results.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions and finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex parameterized alternatives.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: Demonstrates broad applicability of the architecture across multiple tasks with strong empirical results.  
- Key reason: Provides actionable insight that simple element-wise comparison functions are highly effective.  
- Broad empirical evaluation and ablations (including attention and preprocessing) support the generalization claim.  
- Preserving embedding dimensionality via SUB/MULT is more effective than collapsing embeddings into low-dim scores or complex transforms.  
- Suggest deeper analysis of the gated preprocessing layer (e.g., gate values vs. stop/content words or TF-IDF).  
- Recommend more direct comparison/ablation against Parikh et al. (2016), e.g., one-directional vs. two-directional attention.  
- Ask for discussion of parameter counts and overfitting/optimization issues for heavy models like NTN.  
- Suggest improved visualizations, especially of the attention matrix G, to show pre-comparison alignments.  
- Questions: Have you tried symmetric two-directional attention and its effect (e.g., on SNLI)?  
- Questions: For MovieQA, did you try alternative ways to combine (Question, Plot) and (Answer, Plot) signals (gating or separate CNNs)?  
- Questions: Any experiments with an order-sensitive preprocessor (LSTM/GRU) on shorter-sequence tasks like SNLI and WikiQA?
