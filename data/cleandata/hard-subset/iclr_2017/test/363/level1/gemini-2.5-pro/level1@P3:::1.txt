### Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents and empirically validates a general "compare-aggregate" framework for a wide range of text sequence matching tasks. The proposed model first performs a fine-grained, word-level comparison between two sequences and then aggregates these local comparison results using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction. The primary contributions of this work are twofold. First, it demonstrates the broad applicability and effectiveness of this architectural paradigm by applying a single, unified model to four distinct and challenging benchmarks: three different question-answering tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one textual entailment task (SNLI). The model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all four datasets. Second, the paper provides a systematic investigation into the crucial "comparison function" component of the framework. It evaluates six different functions, ranging from complex neural networks to simple similarity metrics, and reveals the surprising effectiveness of simple, element-wise algebraic operations (subtraction and multiplication), which often outperform more heavily parameterized alternatives.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The paper provides a compelling and timely synthesis of the emerging "compare-aggregate" paradigm. By successfully applying a single architecture to four diverse tasks, it convincingly demonstrates that this is a powerful and general-purpose framework for text matching, moving beyond the single-task focus of prior work.
2.  The systematic empirical analysis of different comparison functions offers a clear and valuable insight for the research community. The finding that simple, element-wise operations are highly effective is a non-obvious result that provides concrete, actionable guidance for future model design in this area.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The strength of this paper lies in its rigorous and broad empirical evaluation, which strongly supports its central claims.

First, the generalization claim is well-supported by the choice of tasks and the results. While previous works like Parikh et al. (2016) and Wang & Jiang (2016b) introduced similar models, they were typically evaluated on one or two tasks. This paper's application of a unified model to SNLI, WikiQA, InsuranceQA, and MovieQA—tasks with different input structures, sequence lengths, and objectives—is a significant step forward. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on three of these diverse benchmarks (and near SOTA on SNLI) with the same core architecture is strong evidence for the framework's robustness and general applicability. The ablation studies in Table 4 further strengthen the paper by demonstrating the importance of the attention and preprocessing components within this framework.

Second, the investigation into comparison functions is a focused and impactful contribution. The authors categorize and test a sensible range of functions, from the geometrically intuitive (`EUCCOS`) to the highly flexible (`NN`, `NTN`), correctly identifying the element-wise `SUB` and `MULT` operations as an interesting middle ground. The results in Table 3 clearly show that these simple, parameter-free (or lightly-parameterized in the `SUBMULT+NN` case) functions are remarkably powerful. For instance, on WikiQA, `SUB` and `MULT` outperform the much more complex `NN` and `NTN` variants. This finding is significant because it suggests that preserving the dimensional structure of the word embeddings while capturing their difference or interaction is more effective than collapsing them into a low-dimensional similarity score or transforming them with a complex, potentially hard-to-optimize function. This provides a valuable lesson on architectural inductive biases for text matching.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Analysis of the Preprocessing Layer:** The gated preprocessing layer (Eq. 1) is an interesting, order-insensitive mechanism for weighting words. The hypothesis that it learns to down-weight stop words is plausible but not verified. An analysis of the learned gate values for different word types (e.g., stop words vs. content words, or correlation with TF-IDF) would provide deeper insight into what this component contributes and solidify its motivation.
*   **Comparison with Parikh et al. (2016):** The model is conceptually very similar to the Decomposable Attention model. The paper mentions key differences (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation), but the impact of these specific choices is not fully explored. A more direct discussion or even a small ablation (e.g., comparing one-directional vs. two-directional attention) would help disentangle the sources of performance gains and more clearly position the work relative to this important baseline.
*   **Model Complexity:** The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the parameter counts of the different comparison functions. The Neural Tensor Network (`NTN`) is extremely parameter-heavy (O(l³)), and its relatively poor performance could be due to overfitting or optimization challenges on smaller datasets. Mentioning this would add nuance to the results and further bolster the argument for the efficiency and effectiveness of the simpler functions.
*   **Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a good first step but could be more informative. It confirms that the model focuses on matching words, which is expected. A visualization of the attention matrix `G` from Eq. (2) for a representative example would be highly valuable, as it would show the explicit word-level alignments the model computes *before* the comparison step.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The attention mechanism is one-directional, aligning words in sequence A to an attention-weighted representation of sequence Q. Have you experimented with a symmetric, two-directional attention mechanism, as seen in related work? Would you expect this to improve performance, particularly on a symmetric task like SNLI where the premise and hypothesis roles are distinct but syntactically similar?
*   For the MovieQA task, the comparison vectors for the (Question, Plot) and (Answer, Plot) pairs are concatenated before the aggregation step (`tk,j = [ tqj, tak,j ]`). Did you explore other methods for integrating these two matching signals, such as a gating mechanism or aggregating them through separate CNNs before a final combination?
*   The paper notes that an order-sensitive preprocessor like an RNN was omitted for computational reasons. Have you since run experiments with an LSTM/GRU preprocessor, particularly on the shorter-sequence tasks like SNLI and WikiQA? It would be interesting to know how much contextualization at this early stage helps, in addition to the word-level matching.
