- Presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences applicable to QA and entailment.
- Main contribution: systematic empirical study of six word-level comparison functions within the framework.
- Evaluated on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- Demonstrates broad effectiveness and achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance.
- Key finding: simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex parameterized functions.
- Recommendation: Accept.
- Strength: focused analysis of the often-overlooked comparison function component.
- Strength: clear motivation and comprehensive experiments supporting generalizability across tasks.
- Conclusions are well-supported by empirical results and outperform strong baselines.
- Suggestion: expand discussion/justification of the order-insensitive preprocessing layer for QA where word order may matter.
- Suggestion: provide more analysis on why element-wise operations capture semantic similarity effectively.
- Questions for authors: impact of adding an LSTM to the preprocessor for SNLI and whether it was tried on other tasks.
- Questions for authors: relative importance of SUB vs. MULT in the SUBMULT+NN function across tasks.
- Questions for authors: provide parameter counts for models using different comparison functions to contextualize performance vs. complexity trade-offs.
