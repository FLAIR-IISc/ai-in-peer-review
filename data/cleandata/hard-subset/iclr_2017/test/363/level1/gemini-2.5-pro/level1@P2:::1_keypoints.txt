- Proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework performing word-level comparisons and CNN aggregation instead of single-vector sequence encoding.  
- Main contribution: systematic evaluation of six comparison functions for word-level matching, from complex parameterized models to simple element-wise operations.  
- Key finding: simple, parameter-free element-wise functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often outperform more complex learned functions (NN, NTN).  
- Aggregates comparison vectors via a CNN and validates the approach across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Empirical results: state-of-the-art on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA and very competitive on SNLI, demonstrating robustness and generality.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is clear, simple, powerful, and generalizable with strong empirical evidence.  
- Supporting argument: strong empirical validation by applying one architecture to four diverse tasks with appropriate baselines and metrics.  
- Supporting argument: insightful, surprising analysis showing element-wise comparisons provide a better inductive bias than projecting into new spaces.  
- Supporting argument: paper is well-written, modular (Preprocessing, Attention, Comparison, Aggregation), includes ablation studies and released code for reproducibility.  
- Feedback: preprocessing layer is mostly order-insensitive for three tasks; authors should justify design or test LSTM preprocessing more broadly.  
- Feedback: visualization (Fig.2) is useful but needs deeper quantitative analysis of attention weights (matrix G).  
- Feedback: paper should discuss rationale for one-directional attention (A attends to Q) versus bi-directional alternatives.  
- Question: why define SUB as element-wise squared difference instead of simple difference or absolute difference—have you tried those?  
- Question: MovieQA design choice—matching Q and Ak independently to P then concatenating vs. hierarchical question-aware plot representation?  
- Question: are simpler comparison functions a general principle for sequence matching, or are there scenarios where NN/NTN would outperform them?
