- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching and evaluates a single core model across four NLP tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Systematically investigates six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Finds simpler element-wise functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often outperform complex learned functions (NN, NTN).  
- Achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across the four datasets, demonstrating broad applicability and robustness.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Strong, broad empirical validation across diverse tasks strengthens the claim of a generally effective architecture.  
- Key reason 2: Novel, actionable insight that parameter-free element-wise comparisons can be more effective than complex parameterized comparators.  
- Supporting evidence: Detailed experiments and ablations show SUBMULT+NN as the best overall and SUB/MULT frequently beating NN/NTN.  
- Ablation study demonstrates the importance of preprocessing and attention layers; code release improves reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Explicitly describe and ablate the LSTM preprocessing used for SNLI to isolate its impact from the comparison function.  
- Suggestion: Provide a short theoretical/intuitive discussion on why SUB and MULT work well (difference and per-dimension agreement explanations).  
- Suggestion: Clarify Figure 2’s visualization and explain axes and how it relates to attention/focus.  
- Suggestion: Better position the work relative to Parikh et al. (2016), framing SUB/MULT as more effective alternatives to their feedforward compare step.  
- Questions for authors: clarify LSTM integration and its performance impact; provide intuition for superiority of element-wise operations; explain why attention matters more for long sequences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) than for short ones (WikiQA, SNLI).
