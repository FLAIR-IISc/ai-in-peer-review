### **Summary**

The paper proposes a general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The core idea is to move beyond encoding entire sequences into single vectors and instead perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences. The resulting comparison vectors are then aggregated by a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction. The primary contribution of this work is a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions that can be used at the word level. The authors test six functions, ranging from complex, parameterized models like a standard Neural Network (NN) and a Neural Tensor Network (NTN) to simpler, intuitive functions based on Euclidean distance, cosine similarity, and element-wise subtraction (SUB) and multiplication (MULT). The authors validate their framework's generality and performance across four different datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The results show that their model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across all tasks. A key and valuable finding is that the simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations often outperform their more complex, parameterized counterparts.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:
1.  The paper presents a clear, simple, and powerful framework for a fundamental NLP problem and demonstrates its effectiveness and generalizability with strong empirical results across four diverse and challenging benchmarks.
2.  The systematic study of comparison functions provides a significant and non-obvious insight: simple, parameter-free element-wise operations can serve as a more effective inductive bias for word-level matching than more complex, learned functions. This is a valuable contribution for practitioners and researchers working on similar problems.

### **Supporting Arguments**

**1. Strong Empirical Validation and Generality:** The main claim of the paper is that the "compare-aggregate" framework is a generally effective approach for sequence matching. This claim is convincingly supported by applying the same core model architecture to four different tasks. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and very competitive results on SNLI (Table 3). This is a strong testament to the model's robustness and generality, especially compared to previous works which were often tailored to a single task.

**2. Novel and Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The paper's most significant contribution is its focused investigation into the comparison function `f`. While prior work in this area often defaulted to using a standard feed-forward network, this paper systematically compares it against alternatives. The results are surprising and impactful. The element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN) consistently outperform the more heavily parameterized NN and NTN options. This suggests that explicitly modeling similarity and dissimilarity along the original embedding dimensions (as SUB and MULT do) is a powerful mechanism that might be lost when projecting into a new space with a generic NN. This finding is well-supported by the results in Table 3 and provides a clear and actionable insight for future research in this area.

**3. Clarity and Sound Methodology:** The paper is well-written and the proposed model is explained with clarity. The architecture is broken down into logical components (Preprocessing, Attention, Comparison, Aggregation), making it easy to follow. The experimental setup is sound, with appropriate baselines and evaluation metrics for each dataset. The ablation study (Table 4), which demonstrates the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers, further strengthens the paper's conclusions. The authors' decision to release their code also adds to the paper's value by ensuring reproducibility.

### **Additional Feedback**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   **Preprocessing Layer:** The paper notes that the preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive" for three of the four tasks. While the results are excellent regardless, it seems like a missed opportunity. Since an LSTM-based preprocessor was used for SNLI, it would strengthen the paper to either apply it to the other tasks as well (even if only reported in an appendix) or to provide a clearer justification for why this design choice was made.
*   **Analysis and Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 2 provides a helpful, qualitative glimpse into the model's behavior. However, the analysis could be deepened. For example, a quantitative analysis of the attention weights (matrix G from Eq. 2) could reveal what types of words in sequence Q are deemed important for matching words in sequence A.
*   **Discussion on One-Directional Attention:** The model uses one-directional attention (A attends to Q), which differs from some contemporary models like Parikh et al. (2016) that use two-directional attention. A brief discussion on the motivation for this choice (e.g., efficiency, empirical performance, task suitability) would be a welcome addition.

### **Questions for the Authors**
*   The comparison function SUB is defined as `(aj - hj) * (aj - hj)`, which is the element-wise square of the difference. This is closely related to the squared Euclidean distance. Have you experimented with just the simple difference `(aj - hj)`? What is the intuition for squaring it element-wise rather than just taking the absolute difference?
*   For the MovieQA task, the question (Q) and a candidate answer (Ak) are independently matched against the plot (P), and the resulting comparison vectors are concatenated before the aggregation CNN. Could you elaborate on the intuition for this design? Have you considered a more hierarchical approach, such as first creating a question-aware plot representation before matching it with the answer?
*   Your results suggest that simpler comparison functions are often better. Do you believe this is a general principle for sequence matching, or is it specific to the tasks you chose? Could you speculate on a scenario or task where a more complex, learned comparison function like an NN or NTN might be superior?
