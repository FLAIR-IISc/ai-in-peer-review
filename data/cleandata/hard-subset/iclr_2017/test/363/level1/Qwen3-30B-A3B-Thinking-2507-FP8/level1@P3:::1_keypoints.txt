- Decision: Accept (with minor revisions).

- Paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching focusing on the comparison function.

- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (including novel element-wise SUB and MULT) across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).

- Main empirical claim: simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural approaches, with SUBMULT+NN often best.

- Demonstrates general applicability by outperforming or matching state-of-the-art on multiple tasks and datasets.

- Provides ablation studies on preprocessing and attention layers that give insight into component contributions.

- Paper is well-written and clearly organized with a thorough experimental setup.

- Major concern: no statistical significance testing reported for performance differences between comparison functions.

- Suggested addition: include statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) to validate reported gains.

- Suggested addition: deeper ablation comparing individual comparison components (e.g., SUB vs MULT vs SUBMULT+NN).

- Suggested addition: report computational efficiency (runtime/memory) to support claims that element-wise operations are more efficient.

- Suggested addition: provide more formal/theoretical justification for why SUB and MULT work well.

- Questions for authors: clarify the claim about parameter-free SUB/MULT vs parameterized SUBMULT+NN and their relative performance on WikiQA.

- Questions for authors: explain how the attention layer helps with large sequence length differences (e.g., MovieQA, InsuranceQA).

- Questions for authors: elaborate on why an order-insensitive preprocessing layer can outperform order-sensitive variants and the role of context.

- Questions for authors: clarify why SUB and MULT were emphasized over other simple functions like EUCCOS, given similar performance on MovieQA.
