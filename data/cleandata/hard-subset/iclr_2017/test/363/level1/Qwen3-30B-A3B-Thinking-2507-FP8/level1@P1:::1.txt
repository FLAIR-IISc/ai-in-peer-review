

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which has broad applicability across multiple NLP tasks including machine comprehension, answer selection, and textual entailment. The authors make two key contributions: (1) they demonstrate that their general framework achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across four different datasets representing diverse sequence matching tasks; (2) they systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper is well-structured, clearly motivated, and provides a strong empirical foundation for their claims. The authors make their code publicly available, which is a valuable contribution to the community.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating that a simple "compare-aggregate" framework with carefully chosen comparison functions can outperform more complex approaches across multiple NLP tasks. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides strong empirical evidence across four diverse datasets showing the general effectiveness of their framework; (2) their systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides novel insights that will inform future work in sequence matching.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper effectively addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. Previous "compare-aggregate" models were tested on only one or two tasks, and their comparison functions were not systematically evaluated. The authors convincingly demonstrate that their framework is effective across four different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), which significantly strengthens their claim about the general applicability of the approach.

The experimental design is robust. The authors carefully select appropriate baselines for each task and provide comprehensive results with clear evaluation metrics. The ablation study (Table 4) shows that the preprocessing and attention layers are important components of their model, and they provide insightful analysis about when each component is most beneficial. The finding that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches is particularly compelling and well-supported by their results.

The paper's contribution to understanding comparison functions is novel and valuable. While previous work has used cosine similarity or neural networks for comparison, this paper systematically evaluates these and introduces element-wise operations as a middle ground. The results showing that SUB and MULT work well without requiring additional parameters is significant, as it suggests that simpler, more interpretable models can sometimes outperform more complex ones.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, there are several areas where it could be improved:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper would benefit from a more thorough theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work well. Why do SUB and MULT capture semantic similarity better than neural networks for this specific task? A brief discussion of the mathematical properties that make these operations suitable for sequence matching would strengthen the paper.

2. **Ablation Study Depth**: The ablation study (Table 4) is somewhat superficial. A more detailed analysis of how different comparison functions perform on different types of sequence matching tasks (e.g., when sequences have large length differences vs. small differences) would provide deeper insights.

3. **Error Analysis**: The paper includes some visualization of attention patterns (Figure 2), but a more comprehensive error analysis would help understand when the model fails and why. This could guide future improvements.

4. **Implementation Details**: The paper states that word embeddings are initialized from GloVe and not updated, but it's unclear why this was chosen. A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., embedding dimensionality, hidden layer size) would be helpful for reproducibility.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet the model still outperforms previous work that uses order-sensitive preprocessing. Could you elaborate on why word-by-word comparison is so effective despite the loss of order information in the preprocessing step?

2. In Table 3, the SUBMULT+NN comparison function achieves the best results overall, but the element-wise functions SUB and MULT perform very well on different datasets. Could you provide more insight into why certain comparison functions work better on specific tasks (e.g., why MULT works best on WikiQA)?

3. The paper claims that element-wise operations "preserve some information about the original two vectors" compared to Euclidean distance or cosine similarity. Could you elaborate on exactly what information is preserved that proves useful for sequence matching, and how this relates to the semantic properties of the sequences?

4. The authors mention they did not re-implement baselines but took reported performance. For the SNLI dataset, the paper states their model achieves "very competitive performance" but doesn't specify how it compares to the state-of-the-art. Could you provide more specific comparative numbers with the top-performing models on SNLI?

Overall, this is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of sequence matching in NLP. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides valuable insights that will inform future work, and the empirical results across multiple datasets demonstrate the general effectiveness of the approach. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
