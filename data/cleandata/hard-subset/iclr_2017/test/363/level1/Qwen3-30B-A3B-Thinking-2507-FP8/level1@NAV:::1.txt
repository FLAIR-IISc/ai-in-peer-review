

This paper presents a compelling "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions across four diverse NLP datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors make a strong case that a general framework can effectively address multiple sequence matching problems, and their key contribution lies in the systematic comparison of different word-level matching functions, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that outperform more complex neural network approaches.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are: (1) The paper addresses a well-motivated gap in the literature by demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple NLP tasks, not just one or two as in prior work; (2) The empirical evaluation is thorough, with systematic comparison of six different comparison functions across four datasets, providing convincing evidence that simple element-wise operations can outperform standard neural network approaches.

The paper's contributions are significant: it shows that the general compare-aggregate structure is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, and it provides valuable insights about which comparison functions work best. The ablation studies further strengthen the claims by demonstrating the importance of different components (preprocessing vs. attention layers) depending on task characteristics.

For improvement, the authors could provide more detailed analysis of why element-wise operations work so well - is it due to computational efficiency, better information preservation, or something else? The paper mentions that SUB is related to Euclidean distance but doesn't explore this relationship in depth. Additionally, while the paper tests on four datasets, it would be valuable to include a more detailed comparison of the computational complexity of different comparison functions, as this has practical implications for deployment.

I would like to ask: Could the authors provide more insight into why SUB and MULT work so well compared to neural network approaches? Specifically, do they preserve more useful information than NN/NTN, and if so, what aspects of the information are most valuable for sequence matching tasks? This would help clarify the theoretical underpinnings of their empirical findings.
