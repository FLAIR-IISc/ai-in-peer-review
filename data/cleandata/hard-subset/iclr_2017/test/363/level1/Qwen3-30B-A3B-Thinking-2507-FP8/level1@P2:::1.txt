

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences across multiple NLP tasks. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations SUB and MULT) across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). They demonstrate that their framework outperforms previous state-of-the-art methods on three of the four datasets, with particularly strong results on InsuranceQA. The key contribution lies in showing that simple element-wise comparison functions can work better than more complex neural network-based approaches, which is a significant insight for the sequence matching community. The paper makes a strong case for the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence matching tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple datasets, addressing an important gap in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the sequence matching literature: previous work has either focused on a single task or failed to systematically evaluate different comparison functions. By testing their framework on four different datasets representing different sequence matching problems, the authors provide strong empirical evidence that the compare-aggregate framework is generalizable. Their finding that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches is particularly compelling and potentially influential.

The experimental design is robust, with careful ablation studies showing the importance of preprocessing and attention layers. The paper's results across four datasets provide convincing evidence that simple comparison functions can work better than more complex alternatives. For instance, the authors report that their SUB comparison function achieves state-of-the-art results on WikiQA without any parameters, while the element-wise functions outperform neural network-based approaches on InsuranceQA.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of previous work (testing on only one or two tasks, not systematically evaluating comparison functions) and position their contribution as addressing these limitations. The comparison to related work is thorough and appropriately contextualized.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper makes a strong contribution, I offer the following suggestions to enhance its impact:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why element-wise comparison functions work well. The authors hypothesize that these functions "preserve some information about the different dimensions of the original two vectors," but a more thorough analysis would strengthen this claim. For instance, the paper could include a visualization showing how the SUB and MULT operations capture different aspects of semantic similarity compared to Euclidean distance or cosine similarity.

2. The authors mention that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as the MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role. For sequence matching with smaller difference in length, such as the WikiQA and SNLI tasks, the pre-processing layer plays a more important role." This is an interesting observation that deserves more elaboration. I recommend including more concrete examples or visualizations of attention weights for different tasks to support this claim.

3. The paper should clarify the relationship between the authors' SUB/MULT comparison functions and standard neural network approaches. Specifically, they should discuss how these element-wise operations compare to neural network-based approaches in terms of parameter count, computational complexity, and flexibility. This would help readers understand the trade-offs between the different comparison functions.

4. The authors should briefly discuss the limitations of their approach. For instance, how might their framework scale to very long sequences? Are there specific types of semantic relationships that element-wise comparison functions might struggle to capture?

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper shows that element-wise comparison functions work well on three datasets but not on SNLI (where neural network-based functions perform better). What characteristics of the SNLI dataset might explain this difference, and how might the authors modify their approach to better handle this dataset?

2. The authors use GloVe embeddings without updating them during training. How might the performance change if they fine-tuned the embeddings? Is this a limitation of their approach, or is it a deliberate design choice to compare with previous work?

3. The paper mentions that their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet their model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Can the authors provide more insight into why word-level comparison might be more important than contextual information in these tasks, and whether this suggests that sequence matching problems might not require strong contextual modeling?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple datasets is particularly noteworthy, and the finding that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches is both surprising and significant. With minor revisions to address the points above, this paper would make a valuable contribution to ICLR 2017.
