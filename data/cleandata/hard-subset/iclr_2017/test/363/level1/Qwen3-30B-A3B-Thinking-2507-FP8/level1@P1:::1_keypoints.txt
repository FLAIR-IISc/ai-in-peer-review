- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences across multiple NLP tasks.  
- Shows state-of-the-art or competitive results on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions and finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex neural comparison methods.  
- Releases code publicly.  
- Decision: Accept — paper makes a significant contribution with strong empirical evidence.  
- Experimental design is robust with appropriate baselines, clear metrics, and useful ablation showing preprocessing and attention matter.  
- Novel finding: SUB and MULT perform well without extra parameters, implying simpler, more interpretable comparisons can be superior.  
- Suggests adding theoretical justification for why SUB and MULT capture semantic similarity better than neural networks.  
- Recommends deeper ablation analyses (e.g., performance by sequence length differences) for more insight.  
- Requests more comprehensive error analysis to diagnose failure modes.  
- Asks for clarification on implementation choices (fixed GloVe embeddings) and sensitivity to hyperparameters for reproducibility.  
- Question: Why is order-insensitive preprocessing effective compared to order-sensitive methods?  
- Question: Why do certain comparison functions perform better on specific tasks (e.g., MULT on WikiQA)?  
- Question: What exact information do element-wise operations preserve compared to Euclidean/cosine measures?  
- Question: Provide explicit comparative numbers on SNLI versus top-performing models, since baselines were not re-implemented.
