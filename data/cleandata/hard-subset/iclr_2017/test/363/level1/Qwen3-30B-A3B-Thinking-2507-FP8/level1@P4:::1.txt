

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching in NLP tasks, with a specific focus on evaluating different comparison functions for matching word-level representations between sequences. The authors introduce six comparison functions, including three novel element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), and evaluate their model across four distinct datasets representing different sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The key contribution is demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple datasets, challenging the assumption that more complex comparison functions are necessary for effective sequence matching.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to the sequence matching literature by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple tasks, with results that are well-supported by rigorous experiments.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by moving beyond task-specific models to demonstrate the general applicability of the "compare-aggregate" framework across diverse sequence matching problems. The authors make a compelling case that previous work has insufficiently explored the comparison function component of these models.

The experimental evaluation is robust and scientifically rigorous. The authors test their model on four different datasets with distinct characteristics (three question answering tasks and one textual entailment task), which provides strong evidence for the generalizability of their approach. They compare six different comparison functions, including three novel element-wise operations, against appropriate baselines. The results consistently show that some simple comparison functions (particularly SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple datasets, with the element-wise operations achieving the best performance on WikiQA and competitive results on others.

The paper provides a clear methodology for the "compare-aggregate" framework, with well-motivated choices for preprocessing, attention, comparison, and aggregation layers. The ablation studies further strengthen the claims by demonstrating the importance of different components of the model for different task characteristics.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why the element-wise comparison functions work so well. While the authors note that these functions preserve information about the original vectors (unlike Euclidean distance or cosine similarity), a deeper exploration of the specific properties of sequence matching tasks that make these operations effective would strengthen the contribution.
- The computational efficiency of the proposed approach compared to more complex neural network-based comparison functions could be better quantified, as this would be important for practical applications.
- The authors mention that their order-insensitive preprocessing layer still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive approaches, but this counterintuitive finding is not thoroughly explained. A more detailed discussion of this phenomenon would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper demonstrates that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) work well across multiple datasets. Can the authors provide more insight into the specific semantic properties of sequence matching tasks that make these operations particularly effective? What aspects of the word representations are most important for capturing the semantic relationships between sequences?

2. The authors note that for tasks with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), the attention layer plays a more important role, while for tasks with smaller length differences (WikiQA, SNLI), the preprocessing layer is more important. Could the authors elaborate on the specific characteristics of these tasks that lead to this difference in importance?

3. In Table 3, the SUBMULT+NN comparison function achieves the best performance on MovieQA, but the simpler SUB and MULT functions achieve better results on WikiQA. What specific properties of the WikiQA task might explain why the simpler element-wise functions outperform the combined approach?
