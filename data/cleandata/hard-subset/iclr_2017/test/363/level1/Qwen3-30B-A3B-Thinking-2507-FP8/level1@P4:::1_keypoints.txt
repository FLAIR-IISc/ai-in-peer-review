- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching and evaluates different comparison functions for word-level matching.  
- Introduces six comparison functions, including three novel element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Evaluates the model across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Shows simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural approaches across multiple datasets.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Addresses a gap by systematically exploring the comparison-function component rather than task-specific architectures.  
- Experimental evaluation is robust, comparing six functions and using ablation studies to validate component importance.  
- SUB and MULT achieve best performance on WikiQA and competitive results elsewhere; SUBMULT+NN performs best on MovieQA.  
- Suggests deeper analysis is needed on why element-wise functions work well and what semantic properties they capture.  
- Requests better quantification of computational efficiency versus complex comparison functions.  
- Notes the surprising finding that an order-insensitive preprocessing layer can outperform order-sensitive approaches and asks for more explanation.  
- Asks authors to clarify: why SUB/MULT are effective semantically, how task length differences affect attention vs. preprocessing importance, and why WikiQA favors simpler element-wise functions over SUBMULT+NN.
