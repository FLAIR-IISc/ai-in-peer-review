- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework and evaluates six comparison functions (including novel SUB and MULT) across four NLP datasets.  
- Shows element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural-network-based approaches.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art on three of four datasets, notably strong on InsuranceQA; SUB gets SOTA on WikiQA.  
- Fills a gap by systematically comparing comparison functions across diverse sequence-matching tasks (vs. prior single-task studies).  
- Uses robust experiments and ablation studies highlighting the roles of preprocessing and attention layers.  
- Key insight: simple element-wise operations can be more effective and influential for sequence matching than complex models.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Suggestion: Provide deeper analysis/visualizations explaining why SUB and MULT work well (vs. Euclidean/cosine).  
- Suggestion: Elaborate on when attention vs. preprocessing matters (e.g., long vs. short sequence length differences) with examples/visualizations.  
- Suggestion: Clarify how SUB/MULT compare to neural approaches in parameter count, computational complexity, and flexibility.  
- Suggestion: Discuss limitations (scalability to very long sequences, types of semantic relations element-wise ops may miss).  
- Question: Why do element-wise functions underperform on SNLI and how might the approach be adapted?  
- Question: What is the impact of fine-tuning GloVe embeddings vs keeping them fixed?  
- Question: Why does order-insensitive preprocessing still outperform order-sensitive methods, and what does this imply about the need for strong contextual modeling?
