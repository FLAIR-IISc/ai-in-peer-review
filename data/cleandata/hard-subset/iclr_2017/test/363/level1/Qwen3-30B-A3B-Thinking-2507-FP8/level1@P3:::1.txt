

# Peer Review: A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims

The paper introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, focusing on the comparison function used to match word-level representations between sequences. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions (including two novel element-wise operations: SUB and MULT) across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). They demonstrate that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches, and that their general framework effectively solves multiple sequence matching tasks. The authors make a compelling case that the comparison function is a critical component of sequence matching models that has been underexplored in prior work.

## 2. Decision

Accept (with minor revisions)

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes a solid contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions in the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple tasks, addressing a genuine gap in the literature. The key strengths include:

1. **Problem significance and motivation**: The paper correctly identifies the importance of sequence matching in NLP and the need for a general framework applicable across multiple tasks. The authors properly situate their work within the literature, clearly distinguishing their contributions from prior "compare-aggregate" models (Parikh et al., Wang & Jiang) that focused on single tasks.

2. **Methodological contribution**: The systematic evaluation of six comparison functions, including the novel element-wise SUB and MULT operations, is well-executed and provides valuable insights. The finding that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural approaches is significant and well-supported by the results.

3. **Empirical rigor**: The evaluation across four distinct datasets (three QA tasks and one textual entailment task) demonstrates the general applicability of the framework. The results consistently show that their model outperforms or matches state-of-the-art approaches, with the element-wise comparison functions (especially SUBMULT+NN) achieving the best results. The ablation studies on the preprocessing and attention layers provide useful insights into the framework's components.

4. **Clarity and organization**: The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the model architecture and comparison functions. The experimental setup is thorough, and the results are presented in an organized manner.

The main concern is the lack of statistical significance testing for the performance differences between comparison functions. While the paper reports absolute performance metrics, it doesn't provide evidence that the observed differences are statistically significant. The authors should add significance tests to strengthen their claims about the superiority of certain comparison functions.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Statistical significance**: Add statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests) to validate the performance differences between comparison functions and baselines. This is particularly important since the differences between SUBMULT+NN and other approaches are often small (e.g., 0.2-0.5% on WikiQA).

2. **Ablation study depth**: While the paper includes an ablation study on removing preprocessing and attention layers, it would be valuable to see an ablation study specifically on the contribution of each comparison function component (e.g., comparing SUB+MULT vs. SUBMULT+NN).

3. **Computational efficiency**: The paper mentions that element-wise operations are computationally efficient, but doesn't provide runtime or memory usage comparisons with neural network-based approaches. Adding this information would strengthen the practical case for using simpler comparison functions.

4. **Theoretical justification**: While the paper intuitively justifies SUB and MULT as a middle ground between Euclidean distance and neural networks, a more formal theoretical analysis of why these operations work well would add depth to the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset." However, the SUBMULT+NN function does require parameters (the linear layer after concatenating SUB and MULT results). Could you clarify this point and explain why the parameter-free SUB and MULT functions outperform the parameterized SUBMULT+NN on WikiQA?

2. The paper mentions that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as the MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role." Could you provide more details about how the attention layer specifically helps in these cases, and whether this is related to the attention mechanism's ability to handle sequence length mismatches?

3. The authors state that "our preprocessing layer is order-insensitive so that it will not take the context information into consideration during the comparison, but our model can still outperform the previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing layer." This is an interesting finding. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether you believe context information is truly less important for these sequence matching tasks than previously assumed?

4. In Table 3, the performance of the "EUCCOS" comparison function on MovieQA is 61.7%, which is nearly as good as the best model (62.5% with SUBMULT+NN). Given that EUCCOS is also a simple function (not requiring parameters), why did the authors choose to focus on SUB and MULT as the novel contributions rather than EUCCOS?
