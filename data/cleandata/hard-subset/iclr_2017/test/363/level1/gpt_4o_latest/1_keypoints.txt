- Presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching with focus on evaluating word-level comparison functions.  
- Evaluates the framework on four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Systematically compares six comparison functions, including element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and neural variants (SUBMULT+NN).  
- Finds simple element-wise comparison functions often outperform more complex neural network–based approaches.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across the evaluated datasets.  
- Highlights the importance of preprocessing and attention layers in model performance.  
- Releases code publicly to improve reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Primary reason 1: Demonstrates generalizability and novelty by applying the framework across diverse sequence-matching tasks.  
- Primary reason 2: Strong empirical rigor with thorough experiments, ablation studies, and comparisons to strong baselines.  
- Supporting point: Well-situated in literature, addressing gaps in generalizability and exploration of comparison functions.  
- Supporting point: Robust experimental results across multiple datasets strengthen claims of broad applicability.  
- Supporting point: Insights about preprocessing, attention, and the effectiveness of simple comparison functions are valuable for future work.  
- Suggestion: Provide clearer intuition and examples for the comparison functions (e.g., SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Suggestion: Analyze why certain comparison functions perform better on specific datasets (dataset-specific insights).  
- Suggestion: Include computational efficiency and scalability analysis comparing element-wise and neural comparison functions.  
- Suggestion: Discuss broader applicability to other sequence-matching tasks (e.g., dialogue, multi-turn QA).
