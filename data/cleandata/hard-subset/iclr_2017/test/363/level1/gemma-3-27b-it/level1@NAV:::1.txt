## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a comprehensive study of a “compare-aggregate” framework for text sequence matching, applied to four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within this framework, finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) surprisingly outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The work is well-motivated by the need for a generalizable sequence matching approach and a deeper understanding of effective comparison functions. 

**Reject.** While the paper presents interesting findings, the analysis feels somewhat superficial, and the performance gains, while consistent, aren’t dramatic enough to warrant strong acceptance. The core contribution – the superiority of simple element-wise operations – requires more robust justification and a deeper exploration of *why* these functions work so well.

The paper provides a clear problem definition and positions itself well within the existing literature on sequence matching, acknowledging previous “compare-aggregate” approaches and their limitations. The experimental setup is solid, utilizing four established datasets. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on three datasets and comparable performance on SNLI. The ablation studies provide some insight into the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. However, the claim that the element-wise operations are superior isn't thoroughly investigated; the discussion remains largely observational. The improved performance over more complex methods, while interesting, isn't fully explained. The visualization in Figure 2 is limited and doesn't provide strong support for the claims about CNN focusing on matched words.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the performance comparisons. Simply stating “outperforms” is insufficient.
*   **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis would be beneficial. Where does the model fail? Are there specific types of matching errors that the element-wise operations struggle with?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, especially regarding the comparison functions.
*   **Computational Cost:** Compare the computational cost of the different comparison functions. Simpler functions may be advantageous not only in performance but also in efficiency.
*   **Ablation on Comparison Function:** Perform ablation studies specifically focusing on individual elements within the SUB and MULT functions (e.g., removing the subtraction or multiplication).

**Questions for Authors:**

*   Can you provide a theoretical justification for why the element-wise operations perform so well? What properties of these functions make them particularly suitable for sequence matching?
*   Have you explored other element-wise operations or combinations of element-wise operations?
*   What is the impact of the embedding dimension (d) on the performance of the different comparison functions? Does performance vary with embedding size?
*   Could you provide more examples of visualization (like Figure 2) with detailed explanations of what the CNN is attending to, and how that relates to correct/incorrect predictions?
