This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, focusing on systematically evaluating different comparison functions at the word level. The authors test their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and find that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform standard neural network and neural tensor network-based comparison functions.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonable approach and empirical results, the contributions feel incremental and lack sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core idea of exploring different comparison functions within the compare-aggregate framework, while valid, doesn’t represent a significant advance. The performance improvements, while observed, are not substantial enough to justify a paradigm shift or introduce a truly novel approach.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the lack of a compelling theoretical justification for *why* the element-wise operations perform well. The paper acknowledges this, stating these functions are a “good compromise” but doesn't delve deeper into the underlying reasons. The authors suggest that these methods preserve information lost in other approaches, but this is presented as a hypothesis rather than a supported claim. Furthermore, the ablation studies (Table 4) lack detail and don’t sufficiently isolate the impact of the comparison function; changes in accuracy are reported but lack statistical significance testing.

A secondary concern relates to the experimental setup. The paper primarily relies on reporting accuracy/MAP/MRR scores without extensive analysis or error analysis. A more in-depth investigation of *where* the model succeeds and fails, particularly in relation to the chosen comparison function, would strengthen the claims. The reliance on pre-trained GloVe embeddings and the limited tuning of hyperparameters (only dropout is mentioned) also raise questions about the robustness of the results. The claim that the pre-processing layer is order-insensitive is surprising given its use of tanh and sigma, and warrants further explanation. The statement that CNN is used for aggregation is vague; details on the specific CNN architecture are missing.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not assessment):**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The most critical improvement would be to provide a more theoretical understanding of why element-wise operations are effective. Can this be related to properties of the embedding space? Is there a connection to semantic similarity measures?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform statistical significance testing on all reported results. Reporting p-values will lend more credibility to the observed improvements.
*   **Ablation Study Detail:** Expand the ablation study. Isolate the impact of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison function, aggregation) more clearly.  Consider ablating different elements *within* the comparison functions (e.g., removing the multiplication part of SUBMULT).
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct a detailed error analysis. What types of examples does the model struggle with? Do different comparison functions exhibit different error patterns?
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Provide a more thorough discussion of hyperparameter tuning. What range of values were explored for different parameters?
*   **CNN Architecture:** Clearly specify the architecture of the CNN layer used for aggregation (number of filters, kernel sizes, pooling layers, etc.).
*   **Order-Insensitivity:** Elaborate on why the preprocessing layer is considered order-insensitive despite the presence of non-linear activation functions applied element-wise.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Investigate performance against more recent and state-of-the-art models on each dataset, beyond the baselines already reported.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more theoretical justification for the observed effectiveness of the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as comparison functions? What properties of these operations might be particularly well-suited for sequence matching tasks?
2.  What range of hyperparameters was explored during training, and how were the final values selected? Did you perform any grid search or other optimization techniques?
3.  Can you elaborate on the CNN architecture used for aggregation? Specifically, what are the number of filters, kernel sizes, and pooling layers?
4.  Could you present some examples where the SUB and MULT functions succeed while other comparison methods fail, illustrating the specific benefits of these approaches?
5.  You mention the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Could you explain why the tanh and sigma functions, which are applied element-wise, do not introduce order dependence?
