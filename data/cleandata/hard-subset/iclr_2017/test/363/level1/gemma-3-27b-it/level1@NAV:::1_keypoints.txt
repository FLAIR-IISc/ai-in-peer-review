- Paper studies a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Authors evaluate six comparison functions and find simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) outperform more complex neural approaches.  
- Work is well-motivated and positioned within existing literature on sequence matching.  
- Experimental setup is solid and achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on three datasets, comparable on SNLI.  
- Ablation studies show preprocessing and attention layers matter, but analysis is limited.  
- Review recommends rejection: analysis is superficial and performance gains are not dramatic enough.  
- Core claim (superiority of element-wise ops) lacks robust justification and deeper explanation of why they work.  
- Visualization (Figure 2) is limited and does not convincingly show CNN focus on matched words.  
- Request to report statistical significance tests for performance comparisons.  
- Suggestion for more detailed error analysis to identify failure modes of the element-wise operations.  
- Ask for discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially for comparison functions.  
- Recommend comparing computational cost of different comparison functions (efficiency vs. performance).  
- Propose ablation specifically on SUB and MULT (e.g., removing subtraction or multiplication).  
- Questions for authors: provide theoretical justification for element-wise performance and explore other/combined element-wise operations.  
- Questions for authors: report impact of embedding dimension on comparison functions and provide more detailed visualizations linking attention to predictions.
