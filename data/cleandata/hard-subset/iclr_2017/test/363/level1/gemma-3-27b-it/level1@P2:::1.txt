## Review for ICLR 2017 - A Compare-Aggregate Model for Matching Text Sequences

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper presents a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, a common task in NLP applications like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The authors argue that this framework is broadly applicable and focus on systematically evaluating different comparison functions within it. They test their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and demonstrate that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as comparison functions often outperform more complex approaches like neural networks and neural tensor networks. The authors also provide code for reproducibility.

**2. Decision**

**Reject.** While the paper presents a solid empirical investigation, the contribution feels incremental and lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The key finding – that simple comparison functions can be effective – is intuitively plausible and doesn't represent a significant advancement in the field.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Limited Novelty:** The "compare-aggregate" framework itself is not new (acknowledged by the authors) and is well-established in the literature. The primary contribution revolves around the comparison of different comparison functions *within* this established framework. The finding that element-wise operations can perform well, while interesting, doesn't fundamentally alter our understanding of sequence matching or provide a breakthrough.
* **Insufficient Justification for Element-wise Success:** The paper suggests that element-wise operations provide a good “compromise” between highly flexible and highly restrictive comparison functions. However, the *why* behind this success isn't deeply explored. Is it due to regularization effects? Does it more effectively capture specific semantic relationships? The paper lacks a theoretical or in-depth analysis of *why* these functions work well.
* **Experimental Setup Concerns:** While the authors test on four datasets, the experimental setup feels somewhat superficial. The hyperparameters are largely standard, and the ablation study (Table 4) is limited. There is no attempt to thoroughly analyze the learned weights or activation patterns to understand *how* the comparison functions are being used. The claim that preprocessing is order insensitive seems counterintuitive and warrants more investigation - the authors state that this allows the model to outperform those with order-sensitive layers, but don't offer a concrete explanation.
* **Presentation of Results:** While the results are presented clearly, the lack of error analysis or qualitative examples diminishes their impact. Showing specific examples where the SUB/MULT functions excel or fail would provide valuable insight.

**4. Additional Feedback**

* **Related Work:** The related work section could be more focused and critical. The authors briefly mention various attention-based and Siamese network approaches but don't adequately contextualize their work within this broader landscape.
* **Ablation Study Expansion:** A more comprehensive ablation study is needed. Specifically, explore the impact of different embedding dimensions, the role of the attention mechanism, and the influence of the CNN aggregation layer.
* **Statistical Significance:** The paper should report statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief analysis of the hyperparameter sensitivity of the best performing comparison functions would be beneficial.
* **Theoretical Analysis:** The authors should attempt to provide some theoretical justification for the success of the element-wise comparison functions. Perhaps relating it to properties of the word embeddings or the nature of the sequence matching tasks.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The authors state their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive and that this allows the model to outperform models *with* order-sensitive layers. This seems counterintuitive. Can you elaborate on why this is the case? What specific benefit does the order-insensitivity provide?
2. You mention that the element-wise operations provide a "good compromise" between flexibility and restriction. Can you quantify this compromise in any way? For example, what is the parameter count difference between the SUB/MULT functions and the neural network-based comparison function?
3. Could you provide some specific examples of input pairs where the SUB/MULT functions perform significantly better or worse than the other comparison methods? Visualizing the attention weights or learned representations for these examples would be helpful.
4. What is the computational cost difference between the various comparison functions? Would the element-wise operations provide a significant speed-up in practical applications?
