This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, focusing on the comparison function used at the word level. The authors evaluate six different comparison functions on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform standard neural networks and neural tensor networks. The work demonstrates the effectiveness of the framework and highlights the importance of a well-chosen comparison function.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of sufficient novelty and depth in the empirical evaluation. While the paper identifies that element-wise operations can be effective, this observation feels more like an empirical finding than a significant theoretical contribution. Prior work (He & Lin, 2016) already explores cosine similarity, Euclidean distance, and dot product, which are conceptually close to the proposed element-wise functions, and the presented results do not convincingly demonstrate a substantial improvement over these existing methods, especially considering the simplicity of the proposed functions. Additionally, the experiments lack a rigorous statistical significance analysis, hindering the interpretation of the results.

Further, the analysis of the results is superficial. While the authors note some trends (e.g., attention layer importance for different sequence length differences), a more in-depth analysis of *why* these functions perform better, and under what conditions, is missing. The visualization in Figure 2 is limited and doesn't offer strong evidence for the stated hypothesis.

The paper’s contributions – demonstrating the framework’s effectiveness and identifying the superiority of element-wise operations – are incremental. The claim of novelty regarding the application of these operations feels weak given their conceptual connection to previously explored similarity metrics.



**Supporting Arguments:**

1.  **Limited Novelty:** The core idea of the “compare-aggregate” framework is well-established (citations to Wang & Jiang 2016b, He & Lin 2016, Parikh et al. 2016). The primary contribution is the exploration of different comparison functions, but the chosen functions are relatively simple and share similarities with existing approaches. The improvement over the prior art isn't substantial enough to constitute a significant advance.

2.  **Weak Empirical Justification:** The results, presented in Table 3, show only marginal improvements in some cases. A thorough statistical significance analysis (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) is absent, making it difficult to determine whether the observed differences are statistically meaningful. The ablation study (Table 4) is insufficient to fully understand the contribution of each component.

3.  **Superficial Analysis:** The discussion of results lacks depth. The authors identify trends but do not provide convincing explanations for *why* the element-wise operations work well. The visualization is insufficient to support the claims about the CNN’s focus.

4.  **Lack of Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** While baselines are provided, they don’t represent the absolute state-of-the-art for each task. Including more recent and powerful models would provide a more challenging and informative comparison.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Statistical Significance:**  Conduct a thorough statistical significance analysis for all reported results.
*   **Ablation Study:**  Expand the ablation study to include more variations (e.g., different preprocessing techniques, attention mechanisms, CNN architectures).
*   **Error Analysis:**  Perform an error analysis to identify specific cases where the model fails and to gain insights into the limitations of the proposed approach.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical justification for why element-wise operations are effective for sequence matching. Is there a connection to semantic similarity measures or other relevant concepts?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Include a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially for the comparison functions.
*   **More Robust Baselines:** Compare against stronger, more recent baselines for each dataset.
*   **Explain Visualization:** Expand upon the visualization in Figure 2, providing more detailed explanations and examples.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions He & Lin (2016) used cosine similarity, Euclidean distance and dot product. How do the results of SUB and MULT compare *specifically* to these metrics? Could you provide a more direct comparison in a table?
2.  Could you provide more details about the statistical significance of the reported results? Were any statistical tests performed? If so, what were the p-values?
3.  The visualization in Figure 2 is limited. Can you provide more examples and explain how the CNN’s focus relates to the correct answer selection?
4.  What is the computational cost of the different comparison functions? Do the element-wise operations offer any significant computational advantages?
5.  The claim is made that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but still important. Can you elaborate on how it contributes to performance despite not considering word order?
