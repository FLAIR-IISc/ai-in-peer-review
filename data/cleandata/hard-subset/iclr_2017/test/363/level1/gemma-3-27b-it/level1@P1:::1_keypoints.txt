- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework and empirically evaluates six comparison functions, claiming simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex methods across multiple datasets.
- Decision: Reject — judged to lack sufficient novelty and depth for ICLR.
- Limited novelty: framework is not new and the contribution is mainly an empirical comparison without strong theoretical justification.
- Incremental results: improvements are modest and the paper lacks detailed comparative and error analysis to substantiate "very effective" claims.
- Insufficient details & ablation: CNN aggregation architecture, hyperparameters, and ablation study are under-specified and do not isolate component contributions.
- Presentation issues: writing is sometimes repetitive and baseline descriptions are too brief to assess fairness of comparisons.
- Need theoretical motivation: explain why element-wise operations are appropriate for semantic similarity and relate to embedding properties.
- Request deeper analysis: provide error analysis, cases where simple functions outperform complex ones, and visualize attention weights.
- Hyperparameter sensitivity: perform more thorough hyperparameter search and report variances or confidence intervals.
- Expand ablation study: evaluate different preprocessing, attention mechanisms, and CNN architecture choices.
- Improve reproducibility: release full experimental details (hyperparams, optimizers, training procedures) and a well-documented codebase.
- Related work: strengthen discussion of similarity functions and metric learning literature.
- Q1: Justify choice of element-wise multiplication and subtraction and their semantic intuition.
- Q2: Provide detailed CNN aggregation architecture and how hyperparameters were chosen.
- Q3: Give examples of model failures and analyze causes.
- Q4: Report whether statistical significance tests were performed for performance differences.
- Q5: Detail computational cost and trade-offs among the different comparison functions.
