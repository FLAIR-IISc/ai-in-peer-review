Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper presents a general compare-aggregate architecture for text sequence matching that (i) preprocesses token embeddings with gating, (ii) attends from one sequence to another to produce aligned token representations, (iii) compares each token pair using a chosen comparison function, and (iv) aggregates the comparison sequence via a CNN for prediction.
- The main focus is a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and SUB+MULT+NN). The authors argue that simple, element-wise operations strike a good balance between flexibility and inductive bias for similarity, and empirically perform best.
- The model is evaluated across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), reportedly achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results. Ablations indicate the importance of attention and preprocessing, with relative importance varying by sequence length disparity.
- Code is made available.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty relative to prior “compare-aggregate” work (e.g., decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction models); the architecture and insights largely overlap with known approaches. The main contribution—systematic comparison of simple word-level comparison functions—is incremental and has been partially explored (e.g., element-wise difference/product).
- Empirical support, while broad, lacks rigor on comparability and analysis: reliance on reported baselines, sparse training details, and no statistical significance testing. The design choices (e.g., one-directional attention, order-insensitive preprocessing) are not sufficiently justified or contrasted against known stronger variants (e.g., bi-directional attention, RNN-based contextualization).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The paper targets the well-studied sequence matching problem (entailment, QA selection, machine comprehension) and clearly situates the approach in the compare-aggregate family. However, several key elements are prior art: attention-based alignment followed by token-level comparison and aggregation is central to Parikh et al. (2016), He & Lin (2016), Wang & Jiang (2016). The use of element-wise subtraction and multiplication as heuristic matching features appears in prior sentence pair modeling (e.g., Mou et al., 2016). The CNN aggregator is a minor variant versus summation/concatenation or RNN aggregation.
- Technical soundness: The model components are standard and sound. The attention and comparison formulations are clear. However:
  - The preprocessing “gate” is order-insensitive for three datasets, potentially discarding valuable context; using LSTMs only for SNLI makes cross-task comparisons less controlled.
  - The choice of one-directional attention is not justified against two-directional or iterative alignment which are known to help.
  - The EUCCOS vs. element-wise comparisons: the rationale is plausible, but there is no theoretical analysis; claims rest solely on empirical results.
  - Important training and architecture details are missing (e.g., CNN filter widths/counts, number of windows n, dropout rates per dataset, early stopping, seeds), hindering reproducibility and fair assessment.
- Empirical support:
  - The breadth (four datasets) is a strength. Ablations suggesting different roles of attention vs. gating by length disparity are interesting.
  - Comparisons rely on previously reported baselines; no unified reimplementation or controlled evaluation is provided. It is unclear if data preprocessing, embeddings, and candidate pool settings match prior work (especially for InsuranceQA and WikiQA), raising comparability concerns.
  - No statistical significance or variance across runs is reported. Given close numbers in these tasks, this is important.
  - Not updating embeddings differs from some baselines and may confound conclusions about the comparison function’s efficacy.
  - Visualization is descriptive but limited; no quantitative probing of where SUB/MULT helps or fails.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Provide full architectural and training details: CNN kernel sizes and counts, pooling, n, dropout schedules, learning rate and batch size per dataset, early stopping criteria, and random seed/variance across runs.
- Report statistical significance and multiple runs. Include wall-clock and parameter counts to support the claim that simple functions are not only effective but also efficient.
- Re-implement representative baselines within a common framework to ensure fair comparisons and ablations (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM), controlling for embeddings, preprocessing, and aggregation.
- Evaluate bi-directional attention and RNN-based contextual encoders uniformly across tasks to isolate the contribution of the comparison function.
- Extend analysis: compare aggregation choices (CNN vs. sum vs. RNN), test robustness to updating embeddings, and analyze performance by length, lexical overlap, and semantic phenomena. Include error analysis.
- Clarify MovieQA design (matching Q–P and A–P and concatenation): compare against alternatives (e.g., joint Q–A–P alignment, co-attention) to show that the aggregation and comparison choices drive gains.
- Discuss relationships to prior element-wise feature usage more explicitly and position the novelty (e.g., systematic cross-task study) clearly.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact CNN configurations (number of filters, kernel widths, pooling strategy) and n in r ∈ R^{nl} for each dataset?
- How many runs were averaged? Are improvements statistically significant? Please provide mean±std.
- Did you update word embeddings in any setting? If so, how did that affect the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- For SNLI, what was the LSTM configuration in preprocessing? Hidden size, number of layers, bidirectionality, and was attention still one-directional?
- Why choose one-directional attention? How does bi-directional or multi-hop attention affect results and the relative performance of comparison functions?
- For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, what were the candidate pool sizes and evaluation protocols? Are they identical to those used in the reported baselines?
- How sensitive are results to the hidden dimension l and to the choice of optimizer and learning rate, especially for NTN vs. SUB/MULT?
- In MovieQA, did you compare against models that jointly attend over Q and A when matching P? How does your method perform under that setting?
- Can you provide per-dataset training time and parameter counts for each comparison function to substantiate the “simple is better” claim on efficiency as well as accuracy?
