Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching two text sequences, instantiated with (i) a per-word gating “preprocessing” layer, (ii) unidirectional soft attention to align each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other, (iii) a word-level comparison function f(aj, hj) that combines a token vector with its aligned counterpart, and (iv) aggregation via a 1-layer CNN to yield a final representation for classification/ranking. The central contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions—NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN—highlighting that simple element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication), especially when combined and followed by a small NN, outperform standard feedforward and tensor networks across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The model attains SOTA or competitive results and includes ablations suggesting the importance of the gating and attention components. The work is clean, broadly applicable, and the code is released.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty over existing compare-aggregate architectures (notably Decomposable Attention; Parikh et al., 2016), with differences largely in the choice of aggregation (CNN) and an order-insensitive gating layer. The element-wise comparison operations have also been used in prior work.
- The empirical study on comparison functions is not sufficiently controlled: the dimensionality/capacity of the comparison outputs differs substantially across functions, confounding conclusions about “which comparison works better.”

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement. The overall framework closely mirrors prior compare-aggregate models (e.g., Parikh et al.’s alignment + per-token comparison + aggregation), and related attention-based matching models (match-LSTM, pairwise word interaction). Using a CNN for aggregation instead of summation/MLP and focusing on element-wise comparison functions are incremental. The element-wise SUB/MULT features have been used before at sentence or token levels (e.g., Mou et al., Tai et al.), and EUC/COS similarities are standard. The paper’s main novelty is a cross-task comparison-function study, which is valuable, but arguably more suitable as a short/empirical note without stronger methodological control.
- Methodological control. The comparison functions produce outputs of different sizes: EUCCOS yields 2-D vectors, SUB/MULT yield l-D, and SUBMULT+NN yields l-D after concatenation and projection; NN uses 2l input; NTN is highly parameterized. Since the aggregator is a fixed 1-layer CNN applied over these sequences of vectors, the capacity and signal available to the CNN differs across functions. Without normalizing these to a common dimensionality before aggregation (e.g., projecting each comparison output to a fixed size with an identical linear layer), it’s difficult to attribute performance differences to the comparison operation rather than to representational capacity or regularization effects.
- Training details and fairness. Word embeddings are frozen, which can both handicap models that benefit from fine-tuning (e.g., NN/NTN) and favor parameter-free element-wise ops; the choice should be justified and its impact studied. The NTN may be under-regularized or under-tuned relative to its parameter count. Hyperparameter tuning is minimal (dropout, and for WikiQA learning rate/batch size only), and statistical significance or variance across runs is not reported. Given the small differences often seen on these datasets, this weakens claims.
- Architectural choices. The preprocessing layer is order-insensitive (except for SNLI), effectively a gated linear transform per word. While efficient for long sequences, it forfeits contextualization that many prior works leveraged. The paper claims this suggests word-by-word matching dominates, but does not provide a controlled comparison to contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM/projection with identical parameter budgets). Unidirectional attention (A attends to Q) versus bi-attention is a design choice; reporting a bi-attention variant would help isolate the specific contributions.
- Experimental coverage. While the paper compares against strong baselines, it omits controls that would clarify the source of gains: (i) compare to Decomposable Attention with the same CNN aggregator; (ii) unify output dimensionality across comparison functions; (iii) a parameter-count table and training-time comparison; (iv) stronger contemporary baselines on SNLI (e.g., EBIM/TreeLSTM is cited but exact relative numbers and settings are unclear here). Ablations are mentioned but not fully detailed (e.g., per-dataset impacts and statistical significance).
- Interpretation. The conclusion that simple, element-wise comparison works broadly is plausible and consistent with prior findings, but given confounds (capacity, tuning, frozen embeddings), the evidence does not yet convincingly isolate the comparison function as the determining factor.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all tied to the decision)
- Control the dimensionality: add a fixed-size projection after each comparison function before CNN, or design all f to output the same size. Report parameter counts for each variant.
- Report multiple runs with mean±std and significance tests. Provide development-set tuning details and early stopping criteria.
- Evaluate with fine-tuned embeddings and, optionally, with contextual encoders (BiLSTM or CNN) in the preprocessing layer to test whether conclusions hold.
- Include a bi-attention variant and a Decomposable-Attention-with-CNN baseline to isolate the aggregation change.
- Provide error analysis and qualitative examples contrasting comparison functions, especially on paraphrase/synonym cases where element-wise ops might struggle without contextualization.
- Clarify long-context handling for MovieQA (plot truncation/windowing) and the effect of attention span.
- Consider adding more recent QA/NLI datasets or adversarial evaluations to stress-test the approach.

Questions for the authors
1) How did you ensure a fair capacity match across comparison functions? Did you try projecting all tj to a fixed dimensionality before the CNN and see if the ranking of comparison functions persists?
2) Why freeze GloVe embeddings? Do the conclusions (especially SUB/MULT superiority) hold when embeddings are fine-tuned? Please report both settings.
3) NTN is parameter-heavy. What regularization and tuning did you use for NTN/NN to avoid under/overfitting? Can you provide parameter counts and training curves?
4) For SNLI, you mention incorporating LSTM in preprocessing. What exact architecture differs from the other tasks, and how does this affect comparability of comparison functions across datasets?
5) Did you try bi-directional attention (both Q→A and A→Q) or a bi-attention flow? How does that change results and the relative ranking of f?
6) For MovieQA plots, how do you handle very long sequences (truncation, sliding windows)? What is the average effective context length attended to?
7) Can you report variance across runs and statistical significance of improvements, particularly where differences are small?
8) Can you share an analysis of cases where EUCCOS beats SUB/MULT and vice versa, to better understand when richer element-wise signals help?
