Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for text sequence matching that performs word-level alignment via attention, compares aligned vectors with a family of comparison functions, and aggregates with a CNN for prediction. The key contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with the finding that simple element-wise operators (SUB, MULT) and their combination outperform more complex neural layers across diverse tasks. The model is evaluated on four datasets spanning two problem classes (QA and NLI: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results, and includes ablations highlighting the roles of attention and preprocessing gates. The work is clearly written, broadly applicable, and accompanied by code.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Strong, consistent empirical evidence across four benchmarks showing that simple, well-justified comparison functions are competitive or superior to heavier architectures. (2) A clean, generalizable instantiation of the compare-aggregate paradigm with a careful, task-spanning analysis that will be useful to practitioners.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the core problem of sequence matching and frames it within a widely used paradigm, positioning the work well amid match-LSTM, decomposable attention, and pairwise interaction models. The focus on the under-explored choice of comparison function is timely and well motivated.
- Correctness and rigor: The architecture is standard and sound; experiments cover multiple datasets with clear task-specific adaptations and ablations isolating preprocessing vs attention contributions. The cross-dataset consistency of SUB/MULT-based functions provides credible support for the main claim.
- Scope and novelty: While architectural novelty is incremental relative to prior compare-aggregate models, the methodological contribution—systematic evaluation and a compelling negative result for more complex comparators—is valuable.

Additional feedback (to help improve, not decisive for acceptance)
- Report statistical significance and variance across runs; some gains are modest and could be noise.
- Provide more implementation specifics (CNN filter widths/pooling, parameter counts) and computational cost comparisons for fairness.
- Include a stronger error analysis and qualitative study beyond two visualizations.
- Clarify fairness of baseline comparisons given different embedding and training choices.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are results to embedding updates, fine-tuning, and different pre-trained embeddings?
- What are the exact CNN aggregation hyperparameters and their impact?
- Did you control for parameter count when comparing NN/NTN vs SUB/MULT?
- Are improvements statistically significant across multiple seeds?
- For SNLI, how was the LSTM integrated into preprocessing, and does order-sensitive preprocessing help on other tasks?
