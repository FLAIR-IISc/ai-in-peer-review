- Proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text pairs with gating preprocessing, unidirectional soft attention, per-word comparison functions, and a 1-layer CNN aggregator.  
- Systematically studies six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) and finds element-wise SUB/MULT (especially combined with a small NN) perform best across four datasets.  
- Achieves SOTA or competitive results with released code and ablations showing gating and attention matter.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main critique: Limited novelty over existing compare-aggregate work (e.g., Decomposable Attention); differences are incremental (CNN aggregator, order-insensitive gating).  
- Element-wise comparison operations have prior precedent and are not novel.  
- Empirical study is insufficiently controlled: comparison outputs differ in dimensionality/capacity, confounding performance comparisons.  
- Aggregator (fixed 1-layer CNN) receives unequal signal sizes from different f variants, so gains may reflect representational capacity rather than comparison type.  
- Training choices may bias results: frozen word embeddings favor parameter-free ops and may handicap NN/NTN variants.  
- NTN likely under-regularized/under-tuned relative to its parameter count; hyperparameter tuning is limited and variance/significance are not reported.  
- Preprocessing layer is order-insensitive and forgoes contextual encoders, so claims about word-by-word matching dominating are not adequately controlled.  
- Use of unidirectional attention (instead of bi-attention) is a design choice that should be evaluated against bi-attention variants.  
- Experimental omissions: no Decomposable-Attention-with-CNN baseline, no unified output dimensionality across f, no parameter-count or training-time comparisons, and unclear comparisons to strong SNLI baselines.  
- Ablations are mentioned but lack per-dataset detail and statistical significance.  
- Interpretation caution: evidence that element-wise ops are superior is confounded by capacity, tuning, and frozen embeddings.  
- Recommended fixes: project all comparison outputs to fixed size, report parameter counts, and run multiple seeds with mean±std and significance tests.  
- Suggested experiments: fine-tune embeddings, add contextual encoders (BiLSTM/CNN), evaluate bi-attention and Decomposable Attention + CNN baselines, and test on additional/adversarial datasets.  
- Ask for error analysis and qualitative examples showing when different comparison functions succeed or fail.  
- Request clarifications: handling of long contexts (MovieQA), exact SNLI preprocessing differences, regularization/tuning and parameter counts for NTN/NN, and whether fixed-capacity projections change ranking of comparison functions.
