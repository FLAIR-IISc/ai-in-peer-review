1. Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a simple, general compare-aggregate architecture for sequence matching (e.g., QA, answer selection, textual entailment): word-level attention to align two sequences, a word-by-word comparison layer, and a CNN aggregator for decision making.
- The main contribution is a systematic study of the comparison function f used at the word level. Six alternatives are evaluated: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN. A key empirical finding is that simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN) outperform standard neural network and tensor-based comparators across tasks.
- The model is evaluated on four datasets spanning three QA settings (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and entailment (SNLI). Results are competitive or SOTA on three datasets, with clear ablations indicating the importance of attention and the preprocessing gate. Code availability strengthens reproducibility.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical evidence that a simple, well-motivated compare-aggregate model with element-wise comparison functions is highly effective across diverse sequence matching tasks.
- Clear, actionable insight: for word-level matching, element-wise subtraction/multiplication are a robust middle ground between rigid distances and over-parameterized NN/NTN, with better accuracy and likely better sample efficiency.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- The paper addresses how to best compare two text sequences by (i) matching at the word level instead of compressing sequences to single vectors and (ii) choosing appropriate comparison functions for word-level alignment. This is central to many NLP tasks and timely in the context of attention-based models.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- The paper situates itself against prior compare-aggregate models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction), highlighting two gaps: limited cross-task evaluation and lack of systematic study of the comparison function. The motivation for element-wise SUB/MULT as preserving dimension-wise information while being closely tied to Euclidean/cosine similarity is sound.

Do the results support the claims rigorously?
- The model achieves SOTA or highly competitive results on 4 datasets and includes ablations that isolate the contribution of attention and preprocessing gates, supporting the claim that compare-aggregate and word-level matching drive performance.
- The comparative study across six functions makes a convincing case that SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN are strong choices; the observation that EUCCOS is quite competitive in some settings is also informative.
- Implementation details are given (fixed GloVe, Adamax, hidden size, dropout tuning), and the code is released, aiding reproducibility.

Caveats (do not overturn the main claims but temper the strength):
- Baselines are taken from reported numbers; no re-implementations or standardized training protocol across methods. Statistical significance, multiple runs, and variance are not discussed.
- The “general” framework is slightly adapted per task (e.g., LSTM added for SNLI), which blurs how strictly uniform the architecture is across tasks.
- For datasets with large candidate pools (e.g., InsuranceQA), training specifics about candidate sampling vs. full softmax are under-specified, which can affect fairness and complexity.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not central to the decision)
- Report mean and standard deviation across multiple seeds for all comparison functions; include significance tests to strengthen the conclusions about function superiority.
- Clarify training details for InsuranceQA and WikiQA: size of candidate sets during training/inference, negative sampling strategy (if any), and how multi-label cases (WikiQA) are handled when using a softmax over K answers.
- Provide more hyperparameter details for the CNN aggregator (filter widths, number of filters, pooling strategy) and a small sensitivity analysis; the aggregator design could influence which comparison functions look favorable.
- Explore bidirectional attention (A-to-Q and Q-to-A) and test whether it consistently improves or is unnecessary given the CNN aggregation; relate to Parikh et al.’s two-way attention.
- Evaluate fine-tuning embeddings vs. fixing GloVe to test whether the advantage of element-wise functions persists when embeddings adapt to the task.
- Include a brief computational analysis (speed/memory) comparing NN/NTN vs. SUB/MULT; efficiency could be a practical advantage of element-wise functions.
- Add targeted diagnostics: performance by length mismatch, lexical overlap, and adversarial examples with high overlap but incorrect labels to validate that improvements are not from shallow matching alone.
- Consider reporting calibration or pairwise ranking losses on WikiQA where multiple correct answers exist, or justify the chosen objective carefully.

Questions for the authors
- How are K candidate answers constructed during training for InsuranceQA and WikiQA? Do you use full candidate pools, sampled negatives, or per-question subsets? Please detail the sampling scheme and its effect on performance.
- For WikiQA (multi-answer), what is the exact training objective? Is it pointwise, pairwise, or listwise? If using the softmax in Eqn. 10, how is multi-label ground truth handled?
- What are the CNN aggregator hyperparameters (filter sizes, number of filters, pooling)? How sensitive are results to these choices across datasets?
- Did you run multiple seeds for each comparison function? Please report variance; are the observed gains of SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN consistent across runs?
- For SNLI, how exactly is the LSTM incorporated into Eqn. (1)? Is it applied before or after gating, and is the gating still used? How much does the LSTM change the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- Did you test bidirectional attention or alternative normalizations (e.g., scaling, temperature)? Any qualitative differences?
- What are the sequence truncation/padding policies across datasets (max Q/A/P lengths)? Do long-range interactions degrade the performance of element-wise functions?
- Have you tried fine-tuning word embeddings? If so, do the relative trends between comparison functions hold?
- Can you share error analyses separating lexical-overlap successes from cases requiring paraphrase or compositional reasoning? Where do SUB/MULT fail?

Overall, this paper offers a clear and useful empirical contribution with practical guidance on comparison functions for compare-aggregate models, backed by strong cross-task results.
