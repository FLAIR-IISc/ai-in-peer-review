Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles the general problem of matching two text sequences (e.g., question–answer, premise–hypothesis) and argues for a broadly applicable “compare-aggregate” framework. The model aligns (via attention) each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other, applies a word-level comparison function, and aggregates the per-token comparisons via a CNN for final prediction.
- The core contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, and three element-wise variants: SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with the finding that simple element-wise operators (especially SUB, MULT, and their combination with a light NN) consistently outperform standard NN/NTN comparators and are competitive with or better than distance-only comparators.
- The model is evaluated on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving SOTA on three and competitive performance on SNLI. Ablations indicate the relative importance of the attention and preprocessing (gating) layers depending on sequence length mismatch.
- The paper emphasizes generality, simplicity, and efficiency (no expensive cross-sentence RNNs during comparison for three of the four tasks), and releases code.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical evidence across four distinct tasks that isolates the role of the comparison function and yields a clear, practically useful insight: element-wise comparison operators are a robust, high-performing middle ground between over-parameterized NN/NTN and over-compressed distance metrics.
- The framework is simple, general, and effective, delivering new SOTA results on three tasks and competitive results on SNLI, with ablations that help explain when attention vs. preprocessing is most beneficial.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper clearly identifies the centrality of sequence matching for QA and NLI and situates the work among attention-based and compare-aggregate models (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction). It highlights a gap: prior work used comparison functions largely by default (NN/NTN) or ad hoc similarity measures, without a systematic cross-task study.
- Method: The architecture is well specified: (i) an order-insensitive gating layer to reweight word embeddings; (ii) a unidirectional attention that aligns A to Q; (iii) per-token comparison via one of six functions; (iv) a CNN aggregator. The choice to focus on the comparison unit is justified, with clear hypotheses (element-wise ops preserve dimension-wise information while biasing toward similarity).
- Empirical support: The model attains SOTA or strong results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, while being competitive on SNLI against stronger, more complex models. The ablations (removing attention/preprocessing) and the observation that attention is more critical when lengths differ (MovieQA/InsuranceQA) versus preprocessing when lengths are similar (WikiQA/SNLI) provide useful diagnostic insights. Visualizations further suggest the CNN aggregates local alignments in intuitive ways.
- Scientific rigor: The comparison space is well scoped (six comparators), all plugged into the same pipeline. Training details (optimizer, dropout, hidden sizes) are provided and code is released, supporting reproducibility.

Caveats and limitations (not blocking)
- Novelty is incremental: the compare-aggregate paradigm is established; the main novelty is comparative analysis of comparators and a simple CNN aggregator. However, the empirical insight and breadth of evaluation compensate.
- Some implementation details are underspecified (CNN filter widths, number of filters, pooling), and no statistical significance or variance across runs is reported.
- Baseline fairness: baselines are taken from reported numbers rather than re-implemented under a controlled setting; differences in preprocessing/embeddings could impact comparisons.
- Preprocessing gates are order-insensitive for three tasks; while the empirical results are strong, a direct comparison against order-sensitive encoders for those tasks would strengthen claims about the sufficiency of word-by-word matching.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report variance across multiple seeds and statistical significance for key comparisons, especially when differences are small (e.g., EUCCOS vs. element-wise variants).
- Provide full details of the CNN aggregator (filter widths, number of filters, pooling strategy, n in r ∈ Rnl), and a brief sensitivity analysis to these hyperparameters.
- Include parameter counts per comparator and learning curves to support the overfitting hypothesis for NN/NTN and the regularization effect of element-wise comparators.
- Clarify training objectives per task: for WikiQA (multiple correct answers), specify whether training is pointwise, pairwise, or listwise, and how Eq. 10’s softmax over K is adapted.
- Explore a bi-directional attention variant (align Q to A and A to Q) and briefly report whether it helps across tasks; this would better position your model relative to decomposable attention.
- Consider fine-tuning embeddings (with appropriate regularization) and report any gains; freezing embeddings may disadvantage NN/NTN comparators.
- Deepen the analysis of the gating layer: visualize gate values by POS/IDF, and compare to a simple IDF baseline to quantify its contribution beyond heuristic reweighting.
- Discuss efficiency: training/inference time per instance and memory footprint across comparators, to emphasize the practical appeal of the element-wise variants.

Questions for the authors
- WikiQA training: How exactly is the loss defined when multiple answers are correct? Is Eq. 10 applied over all candidates per question with multiple positives, or do you use pairwise/listwise objectives? How do you handle variable K during training?
- CNN aggregator: What filter widths and numbers are used? Max or k-max pooling? Are inputs padded/truncated, and how does this affect very long plots in MovieQA?
- Attention: Why the specific bilinear form (WgQ + bg)TA versus dot product? Did you try simpler/dot-product attention, and does it change the relative ranking of comparators?
- Embeddings: Did you try fine-tuning GloVe or using character/subword embeddings for OOVs? How sensitive are results to embedding dimension?
- Variance and significance: What is the average and standard deviation over multiple runs for each comparator and dataset?
- InsuranceQA/MovieQA setup: For InsuranceQA, what is the candidate pool size during training/evaluation? For MovieQA, are all five candidates used jointly in the softmax (Eq. 10), and do you perform any plot sentence selection or truncation?
- Gating layer: Can you provide evidence that the gates correlate with task-relevant words (e.g., high-IDF content words)? How does performance change if the gating layer is replaced by static IDF weighting?
- Bi-attention and encoders: Did you try adding a lightweight BiLSTM in preprocessing for the QA tasks, or a bi-directional attention as in decomposable attention? Does this change the comparative performance of SUB/MULT vs. NN/NTN?

Overall, this paper offers a clear, reproducible, and practically meaningful insight about comparison functions within a general compare-aggregate architecture, supported by strong cross-task results.
