- Presents a compare-aggregate architecture with gating preprocessing, one-directional attention, token-wise comparison functions, and CNN aggregation.
- Main contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclid+cos, SUB, MULT, SUB+MULT+NN), arguing element-wise ops work best.
- Evaluated on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI and claims state-of-the-art or competitive results; code released.
- Decision: Reject.
- Limited novelty: architecture largely overlaps prior compare-aggregate and pairwise interaction models.
- The core contribution (comparing simple word-level functions) is incremental and partly explored in earlier work.
- Empirical rigor concerns: reliance on reported baselines rather than unified reimplementation.
- Sparse training and architecture details hinder reproducibility (CNN kernels, filter counts, n, dropout, seeds, early stopping).
- No statistical significance testing or reporting of variance across runs.
- Design choices insufficiently justified: one-directional attention and order-insensitive preprocessing for most datasets.
- Preprocessing gate being order-insensitive on three datasets may discard contextual information; LSTMs used only for SNLI reduce cross-task control.
- No theoretical analysis for why element-wise comparisons outperform more complex functions; claims rest on empirical observation.
- Breadth across four datasets is a strength and ablations on attention vs. gating by length disparity are interesting.
- Comparability concerns: unclear if data preprocessing, embeddings, and candidate pool settings match prior baselines for QA datasets.
- Not updating embeddings in experiments may confound conclusions about comparison functions.
- Visualizations are descriptive but lack quantitative probing of when SUB/MULT help or fail.
- Recommended improvements: provide full architectural/training details, run multiple seeds and report stats, reimplement baselines in a common framework, test bi-directional/multi-hop attention and RNN encoders, compare aggregation methods, and add error analyses.
- Key questions for authors: exact CNN configs and n per dataset; number of runs and mean±std; whether embeddings were updated; SNLI LSTM details; why one-directional attention and effect of bi-directional/multi-hop; candidate pool/eval protocols for InsuranceQA/WikiQA; sensitivity to hidden size/optimizer; MovieQA joint Q–A–P alternatives; per-method training time and parameter counts.
