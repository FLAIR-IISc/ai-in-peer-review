**Summary of the paper**

This paper presents a general neural network framework, termed "compare-aggregate", for matching text sequences, a fundamental problem in many NLP tasks. The authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness by applying it to four different datasets across question answering (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and natural language inference (SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. The core contribution of the work is a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions used to match individual words or textual units between two sequences. The paper compares standard neural network-based functions (NN, NTN) with simpler similarity-based metrics (EUCCOS) and, most notably, with element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication). The key finding is that these simple, often parameter-free, element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex and heavily parameterized alternatives across various tasks.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:
1.  The paper provides strong and broad empirical evidence for the effectiveness and generality of the "compare-aggregate" architecture. By testing a single, coherent framework on four diverse tasks and achieving excellent performance, the work serves as a valuable validation of this modeling paradigm.
2.  The systematic study of comparison functions yields a clear, practical, and somewhat counter-intuitive insight: simple, element-wise operations for matching word representations are often superior to more complex learned functions. This is a useful contribution that can guide future architecture design in this area.

**Supporting arguments**

The main strength of this paper lies in its well-executed empirical study. While the overall "compare-aggregate" structure is inspired by recent work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), this paper is the first to explicitly test its generalizability across such a wide range of tasks. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three of the four datasets, which is a very strong result and speaks to the robustness of the proposed architecture.

The central contribution—the analysis of the comparison function—is well-motivated and impactful. The authors correctly identify that this component has not been systematically studied. Their hypothesis that element-wise functions like SUB and MULT offer a good compromise between overly-general functions (NN/NTN) and potentially information-losing ones (EUCCOS) is convincingly supported by the results in Table 3. Across all four datasets, SUB, MULT, or their combination (SUBMULT+NN) are the top-performing or near top-performing variants. This finding is significant because it suggests that explicitly modeling the difference and interaction between vector dimensions is more effective than learning an arbitrary transformation with a dense layer. It provides a simple but powerful architectural principle for other researchers to adopt.

The ablation study presented in Table 4 further strengthens the paper by demonstrating the utility of the other model components (preprocessing and attention), justifying the overall design. The analysis that attention is more critical for tasks with large length disparities (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) while the gating preprocessor is more important for shorter sequences (WikiQA, SNLI) is an interesting and plausible finding.

**Additional feedback for improvement**

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved for clarity and impact. These points are suggestions and do not detract from my overall positive assessment.

*   **Architectural Novelty:** The paper is very honest about its place in the literature, building on the compare-aggregate theme. However, it could be slightly clearer about its own architectural distinctions from prior work like Parikh et al. (2016). For example, a brief summary or small table comparing the attention mechanism (one-directional here vs. two-directional), aggregation layer (CNN vs. summation), and preprocessing could help readers quickly grasp the specific architectural choices and contributions of this work.
*   **Preprocessing Layer:** The motivation for the order-insensitive preprocessing layer (Eq. 1) is primarily computational efficiency. However, the authors note that an LSTM was incorporated for the SNLI task. This feels a bit ad-hoc. A more detailed justification for why this was done only for SNLI would be beneficial. Was this choice tuned on a development set? Did adding an LSTM to other tasks hurt performance? Clarifying this would make the design choices feel more principled.
*   **Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The analysis could be strengthened by including a comparison of the number of parameters for each comparison function variant. The fact that the best-performing functions (SUB, MULT) are parameter-free, while NN and NTN add a significant number of parameters, is a very strong argument in their favor that is not explicitly highlighted.
*   **Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a good idea, but the explanation is slightly vague. Clarifying precisely what is being visualized (e.g., the max-pooled values from the CNN output corresponding to each input comparison vector) would make the analysis more concrete and easier to interpret.

**Questions for the authors**

*   Could you elaborate on how the LSTM was integrated with the preprocessing layer for the SNLI task? Was this a critical component for achieving high performance on that specific dataset?
*   In your comparison experiments, did you consider a simpler baseline of just concatenating the attended vector and the context vector, i.e., `tj = [aj, hj]`, and feeding that directly to the CNN aggregator? This would help isolate the effect of the explicit comparison operations.
*   The SUBMULT+NN function combines the outputs of SUB and MULT before feeding them to an NN layer. Did you experiment with simply concatenating the two outputs and passing them to the CNN, without the extra NN layer? This could help determine if the added parameterized transformation is necessary.
