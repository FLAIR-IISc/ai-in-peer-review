This paper presents a valuable empirical study on the "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching. The authors make two main contributions: first, they demonstrate that this framework is a general and effective approach for a variety of sequence matching tasks by testing it across four different datasets for question answering and textual entailment. Second, and more importantly, they conduct a systematic investigation into the word-level comparison function, a critical but often overlooked component of these models. The key finding is that simple, parameter-free comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication can outperform more complex and heavily parameterized functions like standard neural networks and neural tensor networks.

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. My decision is based on two key strengths: 1) The paper provides a rigorous and focused empirical evaluation of a fundamental architectural component (the comparison function), leading to a practical and non-obvious conclusion. 2) The work convincingly demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed model by achieving strong, often state-of-the-art, results across four diverse and standard benchmarks.

The primary strength of this work lies in its systematic approach. While many papers propose novel end-to-end architectures, this paper instead isolates and analyzes a single, crucial stage of the matching process. The finding that simpler element-wise functions are highly effective is an important contribution that could inform the design of future, more efficient sequence matching models. The extensive experiments across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI provide robust evidence for the authors' claims, showing the model is not overtuned to a single task's idiosyncrasies. The paper is well-structured, clearly motivated, and positions itself well within the existing literature.

To further improve the paper, I would offer the following suggestions. The analysis could be strengthened by providing more intuition as to *why* the SUB and MULT functions perform so well. Do they better preserve specific dimensional information relevant to semantic similarity compared to the learned projections in NN/NTN models? Additionally, while the ablation study is mentioned, a brief discussion of its results in the main text would be beneficial.

Finally, I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1.  Could you comment on the computational efficiency of the different comparison functions? The simplicity of SUB/MULT suggests a potential advantage in training and inference time, which would be a strong practical argument in their favor.
2.  The aggregation layer uses a CNN. Could you briefly justify this choice over other common aggregation methods, such as an LSTM or simple max/mean pooling?
