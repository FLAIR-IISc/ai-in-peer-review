- Paper investigates the "compare-aggregate" framework across four tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and shows strong, often state-of-the-art, performance.
- Systematically compares six word-level comparison functions and finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) are highly effective, often outperforming more complex parameterized functions.
- Decision: Accept.
- Reason: Provides a well-executed empirical study analyzing the often-overlooked word-level comparison function across multiple datasets, offering clear practical insights.
- Reason: Demonstrates strong empirical results across diverse benchmarks, validating the framework and the effectiveness of element-wise comparison functions.
- The paper fills a literature gap by evaluating comparison functions across tasks rather than focusing on a single task, supporting claims of generality.
- Experimental setup is rigorous with strong baselines, standard metrics, and ablation studies isolating component contributions.
- Central finding: SUB captures fine-grained dimensional differences and MULT captures interactions, making them preferable to high-capacity generic transforms for word-level matching.
- Paper is well-written, clearly explained, and includes source code for reproducibility.
- Suggestion: Clarify the SNLI preprocessing implementation—specifically how the LSTM was incorporated and its performance impact versus an order-insensitive version.
- Suggestion: Discuss dimensionality differences of comparison outputs (e.g., EUCCOS 2-d vs SUB/MULT l-d) and whether the aggregation CNN bottlenecks higher-dimensional vectors or projection effects.
- Suggestion: Add deeper error analysis to show when SUB/MULT outperform NN and vice versa, especially for paraphrasing, negation, or antonymy cases.
- Question: Please elaborate on how the LSTM was integrated into the SNLI preprocessing layer and the performance difference compared to the order-insensitive version.
- Question: Why does the low-dimensional EUCCOS representation perform surprisingly well on MovieQA—any intuition for task-specific effectiveness?
- Question: Can the authors expand on why the attention layer matters more for tasks with large length differences while preprocessing matters more for tasks with smaller length differences?
