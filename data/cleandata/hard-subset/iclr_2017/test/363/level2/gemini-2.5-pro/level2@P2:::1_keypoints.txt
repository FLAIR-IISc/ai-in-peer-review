- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" neural framework for text-sequence matching, evaluated on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) with SOTA or competitive results.  
- Core contribution: systematic evaluation of comparison functions, showing simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex learned functions (NN, NTN).  
- Decision: Accept — strong empirical evidence and generality across diverse tasks.  
- Strength: First broad test of the compare-aggregate paradigm across multiple tasks, achieving state-of-the-art on three of four datasets.  
- Strength: Empirical finding that SUB, MULT, or SUBMULT+NN are top-performing variants, suggesting modeling per-dimension differences/interactions is effective.  
- Ablation study validates other components (preprocessing and attention) and shows attention matters more for long sequences, gating preprocessor for short sequences.  
- Suggestion: Clarify architectural novelty relative to prior work (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016) with a brief comparative summary/table of attention, aggregation, preprocessing choices.  
- Suggestion: Better justify the order-insensitive preprocessing layer and the ad-hoc use of an LSTM for SNLI; clarify if this was tuned on dev sets and effects on other tasks.  
- Suggestion: Report parameter counts for each comparison function variant to highlight the parameter-free advantage of SUB/MULT.  
- Suggestion: Clarify precisely what Figure 2 visualizes (e.g., whether shown values are max-pooled CNN outputs corresponding to comparison vectors).  
- Question: How was the LSTM integrated into the preprocessing layer for SNLI, and was it critical for performance on that dataset?  
- Question: Did you test the simple baseline of concatenating the attended vector and context vector ([aj, hj]) and feeding it directly to the CNN?  
- Question: For SUBMULT+NN, did you try concatenating SUB and MULT outputs and passing them to the CNN without the extra NN layer to see if the NN is necessary?
