- Empirical study of the "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching.  
- Two main contributions: (1) shows framework is general/effective across four QA and entailment datasets, (2) systematically investigates word-level comparison functions.  
- Key finding: simple, parameter-free element-wise SUB and MULT outperform complex parameterized functions (NN/NTN).  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Reason for accept: rigorous, focused empirical evaluation of a fundamental architectural component.  
- Reason for accept: strong generalizability with strong/often state-of-the-art results across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Primary strength: isolates and analyzes a single crucial stage, yielding practical, non-obvious conclusions for efficient model design.  
- Experiments are extensive and demonstrate the model is not overtuned to a single task.  
- Paper is well-structured, clearly motivated, and well-situated in the literature.  
- Suggestion: provide more intuition for why SUB/MULT perform well (e.g., what dimensional information they preserve).  
- Suggestion: include a brief discussion of ablation study results in the main text.  
- Question: comment on computational efficiency of comparison functions (training/inference advantages of SUB/MULT).  
- Question: justify use of CNN for aggregation versus alternatives like LSTM or max/mean pooling.
