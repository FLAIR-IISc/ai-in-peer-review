### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a general neural network framework, termed "compare-aggregate", for text sequence matching tasks. The core idea is to first perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then aggregate these comparison signals to make a final prediction. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they demonstrate the generality and effectiveness of their proposed model by applying it to four different and challenging datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) spanning question answering and natural language inference, achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across the board. Second, and more importantly, the paper conducts a systematic investigation into the comparison function, which is a critical component of the framework. It evaluates six different functions, from complex parametric models like Neural Networks (NN) and Neural Tensor Networks (NTN) to simple, intuitive functions based on element-wise subtraction (SUB) and multiplication (MULT). The key finding is that these simpler, element-wise comparison functions are remarkably effective and often outperform their more complex counterparts, providing a valuable insight for future model design in this area.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed and valuable empirical paper. The two main reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation of a General Framework:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a single, coherent "compare-aggregate" architecture can be effectively applied to a diverse set of sequence matching tasks. By achieving strong performance on four distinct benchmarks, the work provides compelling evidence for the power and generality of this modeling paradigm, helping to consolidate and generalize findings from several recent, task-specific papers.
2.  **Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The focused study on the word-level comparison function is a significant contribution. The finding that simple, lightly-parameterized functions based on element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) perform as well as or better than more complex, heavily-parameterized ones (NN, NTN) is an important and practical result. This analysis is well-motivated and provides clear guidance for practitioners and researchers working on similar problems.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing. Applying a single model architecture to four tasks with different characteristics (e.g., with/without a third context document, answer selection vs. ranking vs. classification) robustly supports the claim of the model's generality. The results, clearly presented in Table 3, speak for themselves, showing state-of-the-art performance on three of the four tasks and performance very close to the state-of-the-art on SNLI.

The core contribution—the systematic comparison of different `f` functions—is particularly well done. The authors motivate a spectrum of functions, from the highly flexible but potentially data-hungry NN and NTN, to the restrictive but semantically grounded EUCCOS, to the proposed middle-ground of SUB and MULT. This is a well-designed experiment, and the conclusion that SUBMULT+NN is the most consistent performer is a useful takeaway. The fact that simpler, parameter-free functions like SUB and MULT are also highly competitive is an important finding that questions the necessity of ever-increasing model complexity for this component.

The paper is also clearly written and easy to follow. The model architecture is explained logically, from preprocessing and attention to comparison and aggregation. The inclusion of an ablation study (Table 4) further strengthens the paper by demonstrating the utility of the attention and preprocessing layers. Finally, the authors' decision to make their code publicly available is commendable and enhances the potential impact and reproducibility of their work.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision. I would appreciate it if the authors could address them in their response.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Motivation for Preprocessing Layer:** The form of the preprocessing layer in Equation 1, while inspired by gating mechanisms, could benefit from further justification. It acts as a learned, order-insensitive feature transformation and weighting scheme. A bit more intuition on why this specific form (a product of a sigmoid gate and a tanh transformation) was chosen over simpler alternatives would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost Analysis:** The paper convincingly argues for simpler comparison functions based on performance, but an analysis of parameter count and computational cost would make the argument even stronger. Functions like SUB and MULT are essentially free, whereas NN adds O(l^2) parameters and NTN, as formulated, seems to add O(l^3) parameters. A small table or discussion comparing the parameter efficiency of the different comparison functions would be a valuable addition.
*   **Impact of One-Directional Attention:** The model uses a one-directional attention mechanism (sequence A attends to Q). This is a valid design choice, but it differs from other work that uses bi-directional attention. A brief discussion on the implications of this choice would be welcome. Does this impose an assumption of asymmetry that is always appropriate for these tasks?
*   **Clarity of Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a good idea, but its interpretation is not immediately obvious. It shows "max values in each dimension of the convolutional layer". It might be more intuitive to visualize the attention matrix `G` to directly show which words in the premise/passage are being attended to by words in the hypothesis/question.

**Questions:**

1.  Could you please clarify the implementation details and parameter count for the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) comparison function? The formula suggests a tensor T of size `l x l x l`. With `l=150`, this would be over 3 million parameters just for this layer, which seems exceptionally large. Was a low-rank approximation or a different formulation used in practice?
2.  For the SNLI task, the paper mentions that an LSTM was incorporated into the preprocessing layer to make it order-sensitive. Could you elaborate on how this was done? It would also be very informative to see an ablation study for SNLI that compares the performance with and without this LSTM-based preprocessing, to better isolate the contribution of modeling word order for that specific task.
3.  The experiments use fixed GloVe embeddings. Have you experimented with fine-tuning the word embeddings during training? Given the strong results already achieved, it would be interesting to know if this provides any further performance gains.
