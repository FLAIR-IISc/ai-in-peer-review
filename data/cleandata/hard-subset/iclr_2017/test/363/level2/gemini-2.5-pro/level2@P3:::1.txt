**Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a systematic investigation of the "compare-aggregate" architectural framework for natural language sequence matching. The work's primary contributions are twofold. First, it demonstrates the general effectiveness of this framework by applying a unified model architecture to four distinct tasks: three variants of question answering (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one natural language inference task (SNLI), achieving strong, often state-of-the-art, results. Second, and more central to the paper's thesis, it conducts a thorough empirical comparison of six different word-level comparison functions that serve as the core of the "compare" step. The key finding is that relatively simple, element-wise operations—specifically subtraction and multiplication—are highly effective and often outperform more complex, heavily parameterized functions like standard neural networks or neural tensor networks. This work provides valuable empirical evidence and architectural guidance for designing models for a wide range of semantic matching problems.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper provides a well-executed and valuable empirical study that systematically analyzes a crucial but often overlooked component—the word-level comparison function—within a popular and effective model class for sequence matching. This kind of ablation across multiple datasets is a significant contribution that offers clear insights for practitioners and researchers.
2.  The proposed model demonstrates strong empirical performance, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results across four challenging and diverse benchmarks. This validates the general applicability of the "compare-aggregate" framework and the specific effectiveness of the element-wise comparison functions highlighted in the work.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is well-motivated by identifying a clear gap in the existing literature. While several contemporary works employ a compare-aggregate strategy, they typically focus on a single task and do not systematically justify or evaluate their choice of comparison function. This work provides that systematic evaluation, and the breadth of its experiments across four datasets is a major strength that supports the claim of generality.

The experimental setup is rigorous and convincing. The authors compare against strong, relevant baselines for each dataset and report results using standard evaluation metrics. The ablation study mentioned in Section 3.3, which analyzes the impact of the preprocessing and attention layers, further strengthens the paper's conclusions by isolating the contribution of different model components.

The central finding—that simple element-wise functions like SUB and MULT are highly effective—is both interesting and practical. It suggests that for word-level similarity matching, capturing fine-grained dimensional differences (as SUB does) and interactions (as MULT does) is more beneficial than applying a generic, high-capacity transformation like a fully connected layer. This insight could guide the development of more efficient and effective models in the future. The paper is well-written, the model is clearly explained, and the provision of source code is commendable for reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Clarification on the Preprocessing Layer:** The paper states that for the SNLI task, an LSTM was incorporated into the otherwise order-insensitive preprocessing layer. It would be beneficial to provide more detail on this specific implementation. For instance, was a BiLSTM run over the inputs before the gating mechanism in Equation 1? Clarifying this and reporting the performance impact of this order-aware component versus the order-insensitive one on SNLI would provide a more complete picture, especially since this task is often considered more sensitive to word order and syntax than some QA tasks.
*   **Dimensionality of Comparison Vectors:** The comparison functions produce output vectors of different dimensionalities (e.g., EUCCOS produces a 2-dim vector, while SUB and MULT produce l-dim vectors). This difference in information capacity passed to the aggregation layer could be a confounding factor. While the paper notes that EUCCOS might "lose some useful information," a brief discussion on whether the aggregation CNN might be a bottleneck for the higher-dimensional comparison vectors could be insightful. For example, would projecting the l-dimensional outputs to a lower dimension before the CNN alter the relative performance of the functions?
*   **Deeper Analysis:** The visualization in Section 3.4 provides a useful glimpse into the model's behavior, showing a focus on matching keywords. To further enhance the analysis, an error analysis could be very valuable. For which types of examples do the simpler SUB/MULT functions outperform the more complex NN function, and vice-versa? Investigating examples involving paraphrasing, negation, or antonymy could provide deeper insights into the specific linguistic phenomena captured by each comparison method.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To aid this reviewer's understanding and potentially strengthen the final version of the paper, answers to the following questions would be helpful:

*   Could the authors please elaborate on the specific integration of the LSTM into the preprocessing layer for the SNLI task? What was the performance difference between the order-sensitive (with LSTM) and order-insensitive versions on this specific task?
*   The comparison function EUCCOS seems to perform surprisingly well on MovieQA despite its low dimensionality. Is there an intuition for why this highly compressed representation is so effective for that specific task compared to others?
*   The paper finds that the attention layer is more important for tasks with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), while the preprocessing layer is more important for tasks with smaller length differences (WikiQA, SNLI). This is an interesting finding. Could the authors expand on their hypothesis for why this might be the case?
