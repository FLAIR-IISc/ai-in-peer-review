- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" neural framework that performs word-level comparisons then aggregates them for sequence matching.  
- Demonstrates the model across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) with state-of-the-art or highly competitive results.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) to study their effectiveness.  
- Key finding: simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) are surprisingly effective and often outperform complex parametric functions.  
- SUBMULT+NN is identified as the most consistently strong performer across tasks.  
- Decision: Accept — judged a well-executed and valuable empirical paper.  
- Main reason 1: Strong empirical validation showing a single architecture generalizes across multiple sequence-matching tasks.  
- Main reason 2: Insightful, practical analysis of comparison functions that questions the need for complex parametrizations.  
- Strengths: thorough experiments, clear presentations (Table 3), ablation study (Table 4), and public code for reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Provide more motivation/intuition for the specific preprocessing gating layer used (Equation 1).  
- Suggestion: Include parameter-count and computational-cost analysis comparing comparison functions (e.g., SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN).  
- Suggestion: Discuss implications of using one-directional attention vs. bi-directional attention and any asymmetry assumptions.  
- Suggestion: Improve visualization clarity—consider showing attention matrix G rather than max-pooled convolution activations.  
- Question: Clarify NTN implementation and parameter count (tensor size and whether low-rank approximation was used).  
- Question: Explain the LSTM incorporation in SNLI preprocessing and provide an ablation comparing with/without that LSTM.  
- Question: Report whether word embeddings were fine-tuned during training and if that yields further gains.
