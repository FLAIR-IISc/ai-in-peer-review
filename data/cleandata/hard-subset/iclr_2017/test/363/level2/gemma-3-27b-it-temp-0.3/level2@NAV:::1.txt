## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, evaluating different comparison functions within this framework across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model and highlight that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex approaches like neural tensor networks. The work is well-motivated, addressing a core problem in NLP and building upon existing “compare-aggregate” models.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a solid empirical study, the novelty of the findings is limited. The core contribution – that simple comparison functions can be effective – feels somewhat intuitive and doesn't represent a significant breakthrough. The performance gains, while present, aren’t substantial enough to warrant acceptance given the existing body of work in sequence matching.

The paper provides a clear and comprehensive evaluation of the proposed framework. The experimental setup is well-defined, utilizing established datasets and baselines. The ablation studies (though brief) offer some insight into the importance of different components. The results consistently show the SUB and MULT comparison functions performing well, supporting the authors' claim. The inclusion of code is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper lacks a deeper analysis of *why* these simple functions work so well. Is it due to the specific characteristics of the datasets? Could the performance be further improved by combining these functions with more complex ones? The discussion section feels somewhat superficial, simply stating the results without delving into potential explanations. Additionally, the preprocessing step, while mentioned, isn’t thoroughly investigated or justified. The impact of different embedding choices is also not discussed. 

To improve the paper, I suggest:

*   **Deeper Analysis:** Explore the reasons behind the success of SUB and MULT. Conduct error analysis to identify cases where they fail and why.
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** A more comprehensive ablation study, varying preprocessing layers and embedding dimensions, would strengthen the findings.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a theoretical rationale for why element-wise operations might be effective for semantic similarity measurement.
*   **Comparison to Stronger Baselines:** Include more recent and sophisticated baselines, particularly those utilizing transformers, to provide a more robust comparison.
*   **Clarification on Preprocessing:** Explain the rationale behind the chosen preprocessing method and its potential impact on the results.



I encourage the authors to consider these points and resubmit a revised version. The work has potential, but requires further refinement to demonstrate a more substantial contribution to the field.
