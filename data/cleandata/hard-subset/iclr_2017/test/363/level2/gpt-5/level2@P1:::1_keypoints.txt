- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework: attention-weighted alignments, word-level comparison functions, and CNN aggregation for sequence matching.  
- Main contribution is a systematic study of six word-level comparison functions, finding element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, especially SUBMULT+NN) perform best.  
- Introduces a lightweight gating-based preprocessing layer and shows strong performance without heavy sequence encoders.  
- Achieves state-of-the-art results on three of four benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and competitive results on SNLI.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Empirical findings are robust across diverse datasets and give actionable guidance on comparison functions.  
- Key reason 2: The approach is simple, general, well-motivated, and ablations clarify when attention and preprocessing matter.  
- Problem/motivation: Addresses how to best compare aligned word representations, improving on default feed-forward comparisons.  
- Architecture clarity: Uses gating preprocessing, one-directional attention (A→Q), word-level compare, and CNN aggregation; scope focused on comparison functions.  
- Comparison-function coverage: Evaluates flexible (NN/NTN), fixed similarity (Euclidean+cosine), and element-wise (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) methods.  
- Insight: Connects SUB/MULT to Euclidean and cosine measures and shows simple element-wise ops can outperform complex parameterized layers.  
- Empirical support: Results and ablations across four datasets are convincing; visualizations aid interpretability.  
- Rigor/clarity: Training details and dimensions given; study isolates comparison-function effects by keeping other components fixed.  
- Limitation: Moderate novelty—many components known though not systematically compared at word-level across tasks.  
- Limitation: Baseline comparisons use reported numbers rather than unified re-implementations, risking fairness issues.  
- Limitation: No statistical significance testing and limited error analysis.  
- Limitation: Freezing embeddings could bias results; impact of fine-tuning not reported.  
- Limitation: Some implementation details (e.g., dimensionality for EUCCOS, parameter counts) are unclear.  
- Suggested clarification: Explicitly state dimensionalities per comparison function and CNN hyperparameters; provide parameter counts per variant.  
- Suggested experiments: Report mean/std over runs, significance tests, and sensitivity to seeds and hyperparameters.  
- Suggested baselines: Re-run strong baselines under the same preprocessing/embeddings for at least one dataset for fair comparison.  
- Suggested ablation: Test embedding fine-tuning to see if ranking of comparison functions changes.  
- Suggested analysis: Provide error typology (lexical vs compositional) and when SUB vs MULT helps most; test normalized cosine/Euclidean.  
- Architectural checks: Compare CNN aggregation to sum/max/RNN and test bi-directional attention to justify choices.  
- Preprocessing gate: Relate to highway/GLU layers and test context-sensitive gating for long sequences (e.g., MovieQA).  
- Efficiency reporting: Include training/inference time and memory to show parameter/compute advantages of simpler comparison functions.  
- Key author questions: Effects of embedding fine-tuning; EUCCOS dimensionality and CNN config; parameter counts and training times; dataset split/preprocessing parity; robustness to hyperparams and seeds; impact of bi-directional attention; MovieQA long-plot handling and sentence-level attention; multi-task/transfer experiments and consistency of best comparison functions.
