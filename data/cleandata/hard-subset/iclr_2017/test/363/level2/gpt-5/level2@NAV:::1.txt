1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences that performs word-level alignment via attention, compares aligned vectors with a family of comparison functions, and aggregates comparison results with a CNN. The core claims are: (a) this framework is broadly effective across disparate sequence-matching tasks; and (b) simple, element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) are strong or superior alternatives to standard NN/NTN comparators. The authors evaluate on four benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), report state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance, and provide ablations indicating the importance of preprocessing gates and attention. Code is released to support reproducibility.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper offers a clear, well-motivated empirical study across multiple tasks and demonstrates that simple, principled comparison functions can outperform more complex alternatives, yielding strong results and practical guidance.

3. Supporting arguments
- Specific problem: Matching text sequences for QA and NLI; the paper targets how to best compare aligned word vectors within a standard attention-based pipeline.
- Motivation and placement: Well grounded in the literature (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, CNN/RNN encoders). The focus on the comparison function fills a gap overlooked by prior work.
- Evidence: Results across four datasets with consistent trends; SUBMULT+NN performs best overall, and SUB/MULT/EUCCOS are often competitive. Ablations show attention vs. preprocessing contributions across length regimes. The architecture is simple and broadly applicable, and code availability strengthens reproducibility.
- Usefulness: The finding that element-wise operations are robust, parameter-efficient choices is actionable for practitioners designing pairwise models.
- Completeness: The model and training details are mostly sufficient; the framework is modular and clearly described.

4. Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not gating the decision)
- Provide statistical significance tests and confidence intervals; report variance over multiple runs.
- Ensure parameter-count parity when comparing NN/NTN vs. element-wise methods; discuss regularization and overfitting risks for NTN.
- Expand implementation details (CNN kernel sizes/widths, number of filters n, pooling strategy) and report training/inference speed and memory.
- Analyze error types per dataset; include qualitative examples explaining why SUB/MULT succeed.
- Discuss bidirectional attention and order-sensitive preprocessing effects more systematically; consider training embeddings and reporting the impact.
- Clarify baseline comparability (splits, preprocessing) and, where feasible, re-run key baselines under a shared setting.

Questions for the authors
- Did you enforce parameter-budget fairness across comparison functions? How sensitive are conclusions to model capacity and dropout?
- Are improvements statistically significant across multiple seeds?
- What are the exact CNN hyperparameters (filter widths, counts, pooling) and their sensitivity?
- How does training with updated embeddings vs. fixed GloVe affect the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- For MovieQA, how is very long context handled (truncation/stride), and does performance degrade with plot length?
- Would bidirectional attention or multi-head scoring alter the relative advantage of element-wise comparisons?
