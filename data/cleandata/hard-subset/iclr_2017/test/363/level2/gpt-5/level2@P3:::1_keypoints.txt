- Proposes a general compare-aggregate framework that aligns tokens with attention, compares tokens to attended representations, and aggregates comparisons via a CNN.  
- Systematically studies six comparison functions, highlighting simple element-wise operations (squared difference, product) and their combination.  
- Evaluated on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving SOTA on three and competitive SNLI results.  
- Ablation studies show attention and gating-based preprocessing are important.  
- Finding: simple element-wise comparators can outperform neural/tensor comparators.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Clear, general, and effective architecture with strong empirical performance across diverse benchmarks.  
- Key reason 2: Useful comparative analysis of comparison functions yielding actionable insights.  
- Problem/motivation: Well-motivated by prior work and targets an underexplored design choice (comparison function).  
- Novelty/contribution: Multi-dataset systematic evaluation of comparators and a simple CNN-based aggregation variant.  
- Empirical support: Competitive/superior results across datasets and ablations; code available for reproducibility.  
- Clarity: Model described with sufficient mathematical detail (gating, attention, comparators, CNN).  
- Clarification needed: Specify exact InsuranceQA evaluation setting (candidate pool sizes) and ensure comparability with baselines.  
- Clarification needed: Describe WikiQA preprocessing/candidate filtering used and alignment with prior work.  
- Reporting needed: Indicate whether results are averaged over multiple seeds and include statistical significance tests.  
- Capacity control concern: EUCLIDEAN+COSINE outputs 2D vs other higher-dim comparators—match downstream capacity or report parameter/FLOPs to ensure fair comparison.  
- Provide parameter counts and runtime comparisons for SUB+MULT+NN vs NN and NTN.  
- Architectural choices to justify/compare: one-directional attention (consider bi-directional/symmetric attention) and order-sensitive preprocessing inconsistencies (LSTM on SNLI only).  
- MovieQA scalability: Report truncation/windowing, max plot length processed, and memory/time complexity for long contexts.  
- Nice-to-have: Sensitivity analyses (embedding fine-tuning, CNN kernel widths, pooling, alternative aggregators like BiLSTM/self-attention).  
- Nice-to-have: Additional comparator baselines (bilinear, learned cosine) as intermediate options.  
- Nice-to-have: Deeper error analysis (lexical overlap vs paraphrase vs negation) to show where element-wise comparators help or fail.  
- Nice-to-have: Evaluate on additional reading comprehension/retrieval benchmarks to support generality.  
- Specific questions for authors include InsuranceQA protocol, capacity matching for EUCCOS, CNN aggregator hyperparameters, attention directionality effects, preprocessing consistency across tasks, MovieQA computational footprint, number of runs/variance, embedding fine-tuning effects, runtime/memory comparisons to baselines, and analyses explaining SUB/MULT advantages.
