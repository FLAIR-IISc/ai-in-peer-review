Summary
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences that (a) computes word-level alignments via attention, (b) compares each token in one sequence with an attention-weighted representation of the other, and (c) aggregates the sequence of local comparison results using a CNN for final prediction. The central focus is a systematic study of six comparison functions, including two simple but underexplored element-wise operations (squared difference and product) and their combination. The model is evaluated on four datasets spanning different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), showing strong results—state-of-the-art on three and competitive performance on SNLI. Ablations indicate the importance of the attention and preprocessing (gating) layers, and suggest that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform neural and tensor comparators.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A clear, general, and practically effective compare-aggregate architecture with strong empirical performance across diverse benchmarks; (2) a careful, well-motivated, and useful comparative analysis of comparison functions that yields actionable insights (element-wise operations work surprisingly well).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Matching sequences via word-level alignment and local comparison is well-motivated by recent literature (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM). The paper positions itself appropriately and targets a meaningful design choice (the comparison function) that has been underexplored.
- Novelty and contribution: While the overall framework is close to prior “compare-aggregate” models, the contributions are (i) a systematic, multi-dataset evaluation of comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise SUB, MULT, and SUB+MULT+NN) and (ii) a simple CNN-based aggregation variant. Demonstrating that element-wise operations consistently match or exceed more complex comparators is informative and broadly applicable.
- Empirical support: Reported results show the approach is competitive or superior to strong baselines across four datasets of different nature and length profiles. The ablation study assessing the roles of attention and preprocessing provides additional evidence that the architecture choices matter. Code availability supports reproducibility.
- Clarity: The model is described with sufficient mathematical detail, including the gating-based preprocessing, attention, comparison variants, and CNN aggregation.

Areas for improvement (aimed to help strengthen the work)
Essential clarifications and checks:
- Evaluation protocols and comparability:
  - InsuranceQA has multiple evaluation settings (e.g., fixed 500-candidate pool vs. all-pool). Please specify the exact setting used, candidate pool sizes at test time, and confirm consistency with cited baselines.
  - WikiQA preprocessing and candidate filtering often vary. Clarify whether the same candidate filtering as Yang et al. (2015) is used.
  - Report whether scores are averaged over multiple random seeds and include statistical significance (e.g., bootstrap or paired tests) to substantiate improvements.
- Capacity control across comparison functions:
  - The EUCLIDEAN+COSINE comparator outputs a 2D vector, while others yield l-dimensional representations or include additional parameters. To ensure a fair comparison, consider matching the downstream capacity (e.g., a learned projection to l dims for EUCCOS) or report parameter counts and FLOPs per variant.
  - For SUB+MULT+NN vs NN and NTN, provide parameter counts and runtime comparisons.
- Architectural design choices:
  - The attention is one-directional. Given the popularity of bi-directional or symmetric attention in related work, include a comparison or a brief justification for the chosen directionality.
  - SNLI uses an order-sensitive preprocessing (LSTM), while other tasks do not. This complicates cross-task conclusions about order-insensitive preprocessing. Provide an ablation on SNLI without LSTM preprocessing or, conversely, a variant with LSTM preprocessing on at least one QA dataset, to cleanly separate the effect of order sensitivity.
- Long-sequence handling (MovieQA):
  - Detail truncation/windowing strategies, maximum plot length processed, and memory/time complexity. The proposed approach could be challenged by very long contexts; reporting these details will contextualize the results.

Nice-to-have extensions:
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide results for fine-tuning vs freezing embeddings, different CNN kernel widths and pooling strategies, and alternative aggregators (e.g., BiLSTM, self-attentive pooling) to assess robustness.
- Additional comparator baselines: Include bilinear similarity or learned cosine (normalized dot product with scaling) as comparators; these often offer a middle ground between element-wise and MLP/tensor functions.
- Error analysis: Qualitative analysis beyond the provided visualizations (e.g., lexical overlap vs paraphrase vs negation) would clarify where element-wise comparators help or struggle.
- Broader datasets: If feasible, evaluate on an additional reading comprehension or retrieval benchmark to further support generality.

Questions for the authors
1. InsuranceQA evaluation: Which official setting was used (candidate pool size, sampling)? Are the compared baselines reported under the same protocol?
2. Capacity matching: How was capacity controlled when comparing EUCCOS (2D) to higher-dimensional comparators? Are downstream layers adjusted to equalize representational power?
3. Aggregation specifics: What kernel sizes, number of filters, and pooling strategies were used in the CNN aggregator? Were alternative aggregators (BiLSTM/self-attention) tried, and with what effect?
4. Attention directionality: Did a bi-directional attention variant (attend A to Q and Q to A) improve results? If not, any hypothesis why?
5. Preprocessing consistency: On SNLI, an LSTM is added. What is the performance of the purely order-insensitive preprocessing on SNLI? Conversely, does adding LSTM preprocessing to WikiQA/InsuranceQA change conclusions?
6. MovieQA scalability: What are the maximum plot length and computational footprint? Any hierarchical or chunked attention considered?
7. Stability and variance: How many runs per configuration were performed, and what are mean/std across seeds?
8. Embedding fine-tuning: Do element-wise comparators retain their advantage when word embeddings are fine-tuned?
9. Runtime: How do training/inference time and memory compare to Decomposable Attention and CubeCNN on shared datasets?
10. Interpretation: SUB and MULT resemble preserving per-dimension squared distance and alignment; any further analysis (e.g., probing which dimensions dominate or whether normalization helps) that could explain their advantage?

Overall, this work delivers a solid empirical study with clear practical takeaways. With clarifications on evaluation protocols and capacity control, plus modest additional analyses, the paper would provide even stronger and more generalizable evidence.
