- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework (attention-aligned word reps → per-word comparison → CNN aggregation) and evaluates six comparison functions across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI, finding element-wise ops (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often best.

- Decision: Accept (weak accept).

- Key reasons: clear, reproducible empirical finding that simple element-wise comparisons are robust; simple general compare-aggregate architecture yields SOTA or competitive results on four benchmarks.

- Motivation: well-grounded shift from sequence-level to word-level compare-aggregate, filling a gap by systematically studying comparison functions.

- Method: well-specified model (gated preprocessing, one-directional attention, chosen f for per-word comparison, CNN aggregation); comparison function choices cover a sensible spectrum and SUB/MULT have plausible links to distance/similarity.

- Empirical support: strong results across tasks; ablations show attention and preprocessing matter; code released for reproducibility.

- Significance/originality: incremental architectural novelty but valuable multi-task empirical study and practical guideline favoring element-wise comparisons; some overlap with prior element-wise uses acknowledged.

- Limitations/concerns: novelty mainly empirical; unclear parameter-control across comparison functions; no run variance or significance testing; baselines taken from literature without reimplementation; dataset protocol details (WikiQA/InsuranceQA) not fully specified; single-direction attention and CNN aggregator not compared to common alternatives, so conclusions may be architecture-dependent.

- Suggested improvements: report mean±std and significance tests; control/report parameter counts; add bilinear and multi-perspective cosine comparators; compare CNN to sum/avg pooling, LSTM/GRU, self-attention; clarify dataset protocols and attention normalization axis; specify CNN filter widths/filters and pooling; test embedding fine-tuning and sensitivity; expand related work on element-wise ops and bi-directional attention.

- Questions for authors: sensitivity to random seed and mean±std of rankings; whether parameter counts were controlled or matched; which WikiQA/InsuranceQA evaluation protocols/candidate pools were used; along which axis softmax is applied and whether bi-directional attention was tried; why CNN was chosen over other aggregators and whether alternatives were tested; results for bilinear and multi-perspective comparators; impact of fine-tuning embeddings on the element-wise advantage; could task-specific preprocessing differences (e.g., SNLI LSTM) affect comparison-function rankings?
