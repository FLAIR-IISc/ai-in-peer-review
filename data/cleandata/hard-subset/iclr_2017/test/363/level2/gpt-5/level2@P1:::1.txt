Summary and contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences. It first computes attention-weighted alignments between words of two sequences (Q and A), compares each aligned pair using a chosen comparison function, and then aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN for prediction. The main contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions at the word level—ranging from learned NN/NTN layers to fixed similarity measures (Euclidean + cosine) to simple element-wise operations (squared difference “SUB” and product “MULT”)—and the finding that element-wise operations, especially SUBMULT+NN, consistently perform very well across four benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The paper also explores a lightweight gating-based preprocessing layer and demonstrates strong, often state-of-the-art, performance without heavy sequence encoders for three of the four tasks.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The empirical findings are robust across diverse datasets and provide actionable guidance on comparison functions for sequence matching. (2) The approach is simple, general, and well-motivated by recent literature; the ablations give insight into when attention and preprocessing matter. Although the architectural novelty is incremental, the careful comparison and cross-task validation are valuable to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a core issue in pairwise text matching: how best to compare aligned word representations. Prior work often defaulted to generic feed-forward layers; this work identifies and justifies simpler, task-aligned alternatives that better capture similarity/relatedness. The placement in the literature (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise interactions) is appropriate and timely.
- Approach: The architecture is clear: gating-based preprocessing, one-directional attention from A to Q, word-level comparison, CNN aggregation. The focus on the comparison function is well scoped, with variants that span the spectrum from flexible (NN/NTN) to restrictive (EUCCOS) and mid-ground (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The connection between SUB/MULT and Euclidean/cosine is insightful.
- Empirical support: Results on four datasets are convincing. The model achieves SOTA on three tasks and competitive SNLI results. The ablation indicating differential importance of attention vs preprocessing across tasks of different sequence length imbalance is informative. Visualization examples help interpret aggregation behavior.
- Rigor and clarity: The training setup is described (GloVe init, Adamax, dropout tuning), and architectural dimensions are provided. The study isolates the effect of the comparison function while keeping the rest fixed, which strengthens the conclusions.
- Limitations: (i) Novelty is moderate; several components (compare-aggregate, element-wise difference/product) are known, though not systematically evaluated at word-level across tasks. (ii) Baseline comparisons rely on reported numbers rather than unified re-implementations; fairness and matching of preprocessing/splits are not fully guaranteed. (iii) No significance testing, and limited error analysis. (iv) Freezing embeddings may bias the relative strength of comparison functions; the impact of fine-tuning is not reported. (v) Some implementation details could be clearer (e.g., dimensionality l for EUCCOS vs other functions, parameter counts).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (for authors’ benefit; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify dimensionality: When using EUCCOS, tj is 2-D, whereas elsewhere l=150. Please explicitly state how l is set per comparison function and how CNN hyperparameters adapt. A small table of parameter counts per variant would help.
- Statistical rigor: Report mean/std over multiple runs and significance tests for key gains. This is particularly important given the simplicity of some comparison functions.
- Baselines and fairness: If feasible, re-run a few strong baselines under your preprocessing and embeddings to ensure apples-to-apples for at least one task (e.g., SNLI or WikiQA). Alternatively, discuss known differences (splits, tokenization).
- Embedding fine-tuning: Evaluate whether fine-tuning GloVe changes the ranking among comparison functions. This would strengthen the claim that element-wise operations are robust.
- Deeper analysis: Provide error typology (e.g., lexical vs compositional mismatches) and when SUB vs MULT contributes most. Consider reporting cosine/Euclidean on normalized vectors to disentangle scale effects.
- Architectural variants: Briefly compare CNN aggregation to a simple sum/max aggregate and to an RNN aggregator on at least one dataset to justify the CNN choice. Similarly, show a 2-way attention variant to quantify the trade-off with your 1-way attention.
- Preprocessing gate: Relate the gate to highway/GLU layers and report whether context-sensitive gating (e.g., via a shallow BiLSTM) yields gains on long sequences (MovieQA).
- Efficiency: Include training/inference time and memory footprint; highlight that the simpler comparison functions reduce parameters relative to NTN/NN.

Questions for the authors
1) Embeddings: Did you try fine-tuning word embeddings? If so, does the relative ranking of comparison functions change?
2) Dimensionality: For EUCCOS, tj ∈ R^2; what CNN configuration (number of filters/window sizes) did you use, and how did you set l for Eq. 10? Please clarify to avoid confusion with the earlier fixed l=150.
3) Parameterization: Can you report parameter counts per comparison function and training times? How much of the gains over NTN/NN could be due to regularization/overfitting?
4) Baselines: Were dataset splits and preprocessing strictly identical to those in the compared papers (especially WikiQA and InsuranceQA)? If not, can you provide a controlled comparison for at least one dataset?
5) Robustness: How sensitive are the results to dropout rate, learning rate, and CNN window sizes? Are the improvements consistent across multiple random seeds?
6) Attention directionality: Have you tested bi-directional attention (as in decomposable attention) within your framework? If so, does it change the conclusions about the best comparison function?
7) MovieQA specifics: How do you handle very long plots (truncation/selection)? Does the attention distribution concentrate on a few sentences, and could sentence-level attention further help?
8) Generalization: You suggest applicability across tasks. Did you attempt multi-task training or transfer (e.g., pretrain on SNLI, finetune on QA)? Do the best comparison functions remain the same?

Overall, this is a well-executed, practically useful study with clear takeaways on comparison functions for sequence matching. The simplicity, strong cross-task results, and analysis outweigh the limited architectural novelty.
