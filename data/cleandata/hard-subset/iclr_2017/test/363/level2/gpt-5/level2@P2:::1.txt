Summary and contributions
The paper studies sequence matching via a general compare-aggregate framework that (i) computes attention-aligned word representations between two sequences, (ii) compares each word with its attended counterpart using a chosen comparison function, and (iii) aggregates comparison vectors with a CNN for final prediction. The main contributions are: (1) a unified instantiation of this framework applicable across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) with strong results (SOTA or competitive), and (2) a systematic evaluation of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), showing that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and especially their combination followed by a small NN) consistently outperform standard NN/NTN and often even EUCLIDEAN/COSINE. The paper includes ablations on the preprocessing gates and attention, provides qualitative visualizations, and releases code.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) Clear, reproducible empirical finding that simple element-wise comparison functions are robustly strong across diverse sequence matching tasks. (2) A simple, general compare-aggregate architecture that achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on four benchmarks, strengthening the case for this design pattern.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation: The paper targets the core problem of matching two text sequences for tasks like QA and NLI. The motivation—to move from sequence-level comparison to word-level compare-aggregate—is well grounded in recent literature (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016; Wang & Jiang 2016b; He & Lin 2016). The specific focus on the comparison function is timely and fills a gap: prior works often default to feedforward layers or simple similarities without a systematic study. The paper is generally well-placed, though see notes below on related work coverage.

2) Method: The model is straightforward and well-specified: gating-based per-word preprocessing; one-directional attention from A to Q; word-level comparison via a chosen f; CNN aggregation. The choice of CNN for aggregation is reasonable, and one advantage is simplicity and speed. The comparison functions are thoughtfully chosen to span a spectrum from flexible (NN/NTN) to rigid (distance/similarity), with element-wise options as a middle ground. SUB and MULT relate neatly to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity while keeping per-dimension information—this is a convincing rationale.

3) Correctness and empirical support: Results across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI support the central claims: the compare-aggregate model is strong, and element-wise comparisons often win. The ablation that removes attention or preprocessing substantiates the importance of these layers and provides useful task-dependent insights (attention more crucial when sequence lengths differ greatly; preprocessing more helpful when lengths are closer). Code availability further supports reproducibility.

4) Significance and originality: The architectural novelty is incremental given prior compare-aggregate/NLI models, but the comprehensive, multi-task empirical study of comparison functions is valuable. The finding that SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) outperform NN/NTN across tasks is a useful, actionable guideline for the community. That said, some prior works have also used element-wise difference and product (e.g., Tai et al., Mou et al., and later ESIM-like models); the paper acknowledges some of this but could discuss overlaps more deeply.

5) Limitations and concerns:
- Novelty is mainly empirical rather than theoretical; the comparison functions studied are simple and some are known. Still, the cross-task validation adds weight.
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons could be strengthened: it is unclear if parameter counts are controlled across comparison functions; variance across random seeds and statistical significance are not reported; baselines are taken from literature without re-implementation, which can be noisy across settings.
- For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, dataset protocol details matter (e.g., candidate pool size, whether unanswerable questions are filtered in WikiQA). These details aren’t fully specified here.
- The choice of a single-direction attention and CNN aggregator is not compared against commonly used alternatives (bi-directional attention, RNN/FF aggregation), so conclusions about the comparison functions might be architecture-dependent.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs, and significance tests for gains among comparison functions and against baselines.
- Control (or report) parameter counts for NN/NTN vs element-wise variants to disentangle capacity from inductive bias.
- Add a bilinear comparison (a^T M h) and multi-perspective cosine (as in later BiMPM) to the comparison set; they offer a middle ground between rigid metrics and full NNs.
- Compare CNN aggregation to alternatives (sum/avg pooling as in Decomposable Attention, LSTM/GRU, self-attention) to test robustness of the conclusions about f.
- Clarify dataset-specific protocols: WikiQA evaluation (with/without unanswered questions), InsuranceQA candidate pool at test time, and negative sampling (if any).
- Report attention normalization axis and implementation details; specify CNN filter widths, number of filters, and pooling.
- Consider fine-tuning embeddings and reporting the impact; also test sensitivity to embedding choice and dimension.
- Expand related work to more explicitly position element-wise difference/product in prior models (e.g., Tai et al. 2015; Mou et al. 2016) and to discuss bi-directional attention models.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the outcomes to random seed? Please report mean±std of key results and of the ranking among comparison functions.
- Did you control for the number of parameters among NN/NTN vs SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN? If not, can you match capacity and see if conclusions hold?
- For WikiQA, which evaluation setting did you use (with or without unanswered questions)? For InsuranceQA, what candidate pool size was used at test time?
- Along which dimension is softmax applied in the attention equation, and did you try a bi-directional attention variant? Do the relative rankings of comparison functions change?
- Why choose CNN for aggregation over the sum/concat used by Parikh et al.? Did you test alternatives?
- Have you tried a bilinear comparator (a^T M h) or multi-perspective cosine? If so, how do they compare to SUB/MULT?
- How much do results change if word embeddings are fine-tuned? Does the advantage of element-wise operations persist?
- For SNLI, you add LSTM in preprocessing but not in other tasks. Could differing preprocessing across tasks influence the comparison-function ranking?
