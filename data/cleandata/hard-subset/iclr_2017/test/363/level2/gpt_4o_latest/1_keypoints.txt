- Proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework evaluated on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Systematically compares six comparison functions, including novel element-wise SUB and MULT.  
- Finds element-wise comparison functions often outperform neural network–based approaches for word-level matching.  
- Main contributions: (1) demonstrating framework effectiveness across diverse tasks; (2) identifying element-wise comparisons as particularly effective.  
- Achieves competitive or superior results compared to state-of-the-art baselines on four datasets.  
- Conducts ablation studies confirming the importance of preprocessing and attention layers.  
- Notes the importance of word-by-word comparison for tasks with large sequence length differences.  
- Provides code to enhance reproducibility.  
- Well-motivated study that fills a gap in systematic evaluation of comparison functions.  
- Experiments are thorough with clear baselines and appropriate metrics (accuracy, MAP, MRR).  
- Writing is generally clear, but mathematical formulations could use more explanation or visual aids.  
- Omits explicit discussion of limitations, such as computational efficiency of element-wise vs neural methods.  
- Suggests future work on multi-task learning but lacks preliminary experiments or detailed discussion of challenges.  
- Questions for authors: compare computational cost/scalability of SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN; impact of contextual embeddings (e.g., BERT); extension to tasks beyond sequence matching.  
- Recommendation: Accept.
