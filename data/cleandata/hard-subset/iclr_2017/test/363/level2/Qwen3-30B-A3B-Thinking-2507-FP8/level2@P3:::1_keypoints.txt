- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework focusing on word-level comparison functions for sequence matching.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions across four NLP tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Finds simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural comparison functions.  
- Element-wise comparisons achieve state-of-the-art or near-state-of-the-art on three of the four datasets.  
- Argues the framework is a unified solution for multiple sequence matching problems and fills a gap in prior work.  
- Experimental design is rigorous: diverse datasets, appropriate metrics (accuracy, MAP, MRR), and careful ablation studies.  
- Ablations show attention layer is important for long/unequal-length sequences and preprocessing helps for shorter sequences.  
- Implementation details are sufficient for reproducibility and code is made available.  
- Needs deeper theoretical analysis explaining why SUB and MULT capture semantic similarity better than other functions.  
- Should analyze and compare attention weight patterns across comparison functions (e.g., SUB vs NN) to explain performance differences.  
- Must clarify relation to prior work (e.g., Mou et al. 2016) and correct claims about the novelty of SUB/MULT usage.  
- Should discuss limitations: scalability to very long sequences, computational costs, and the effect of preprocessing being order-insensitive.  
- Recommend testing generalization to other sequence matching tasks and comparing parameter counts to rule out parameter-size confounders.
