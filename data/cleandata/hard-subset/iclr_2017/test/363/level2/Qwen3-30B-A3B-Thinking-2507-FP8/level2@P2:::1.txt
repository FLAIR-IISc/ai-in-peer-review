

Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

1. Summary: The paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks, focusing on systematically evaluating different comparison functions at the word level. The authors demonstrate that their approach achieves strong performance across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and find that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions.

2. Decision: Accept (weak accept). The paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating different comparison functions in a "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple sequence matching tasks, showing that simpler element-wise operations can be highly effective.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper's main strength is its comprehensive evaluation across four distinct datasets, which effectively demonstrates the general applicability of the "compare-aggregate" framework. The systematic comparison of six different comparison functions (including the novel element-wise SUB and MULT functions) is well-motivated and provides useful empirical insights for the community. The results are generally convincing, with the authors showing that their approach outperforms previous methods on three of the four datasets, and achieving competitive results on the fourth.

However, the paper has several limitations that prevent a stronger recommendation. First, the theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work well is weak - the paper observes that these functions perform well but doesn't provide a compelling explanation for why they might be better suited for sequence matching tasks than more complex neural network-based approaches. Second, the ablation study is limited to removing the preprocessing and attention layers, without exploring other architectural components. Third, the paper doesn't provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences, which is a standard expectation in NLP papers. Finally, while the authors claim their framework is general, they don't sufficiently address why their specific implementation would work better than other "compare-aggregate" approaches, which have been previously proposed in the literature.

4. Additional feedback: The paper would be significantly strengthened by:
- Providing a more thorough theoretical justification for why element-wise operations might work well for sequence matching, possibly by analyzing the properties of the comparison functions in relation to semantic similarity.
- Including statistical significance tests for the performance comparisons.
- Conducting a more comprehensive ablation study, including the impact of different aggregation methods (they use CNN but don't compare to other aggregation approaches).
- Discussing how their findings might generalize to other sequence matching tasks beyond the four they tested.
- Comparing more directly with recent advances in the field (e.g., the 2016-2017 work on transformers or more recent attention mechanisms).

5. Questions for authors:
- Why do you believe element-wise operations work better than neural network-based comparison functions for sequence matching? Is there a theoretical basis for this, or is it purely empirical?
- How do you explain the fact that the simplest comparison function (EUCCOS) performs so well on MovieQA, while the element-wise functions perform best on WikiQA? Is there a task-specific reason for this pattern?
- The paper mentions that their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet their model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Can you elaborate on why word-by-word comparison might be sufficient without contextual information?
- The paper claims that the "compare-aggregate" framework is general, but doesn't discuss how it would apply to tasks where sequence alignment is more complex (e.g., paraphrasing or translation). How would your framework extend to these scenarios?
