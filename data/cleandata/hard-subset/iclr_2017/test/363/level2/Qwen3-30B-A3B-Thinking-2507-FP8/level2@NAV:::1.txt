

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks, making two key claims: (1) this general framework is effective across multiple sequence matching problems (not limited to one or two tasks as previous studies), and (2) simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform standard neural network and neural tensor network approaches.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The authors provide strong empirical support for both claims through systematic evaluation across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). Their model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on three datasets, with element-wise comparison functions consistently outperforming more complex neural approaches. The experimental design is rigorous, with thorough ablation studies demonstrating the importance of both preprocessing and attention layers, and the authors provide sufficient implementation details for reproducibility.

The work makes a meaningful contribution to the field by addressing a clear gap in previous sequence matching research—most prior studies focused on a single task without systematically evaluating comparison functions. The finding that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural approaches is both surprising and practically valuable for future research.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend adding a more detailed theoretical explanation of why element-wise operations work better than neural network-based approaches despite being less expressive. The current explanation that these operations "preserve some information about the different dimensions" is somewhat vague and could be expanded with more concrete reasoning.

Additionally, the authors should clarify whether the observed performance gains are due to the comparison functions themselves or the specific model architecture. A comparison of model complexity and training time across different comparison functions would provide valuable insights for practitioners.

I would also appreciate seeing a more detailed analysis of the visualization results in Section 3.4. How do the attention patterns differ between the best-performing comparison functions, and what do these patterns reveal about why certain functions work better for specific tasks?

Overall, this is a well-executed study that makes a valuable contribution to sequence matching research, and I believe it would be a strong addition to the conference program.
