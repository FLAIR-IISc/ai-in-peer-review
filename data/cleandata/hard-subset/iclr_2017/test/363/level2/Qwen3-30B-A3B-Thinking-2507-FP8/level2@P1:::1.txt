

**Review**

This paper presents a "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, focusing on systematically evaluating different comparison functions at the word level. The authors propose a general model that can be applied across multiple sequence matching tasks, including question answering and textual entailment. Their key contribution is demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The paper is well-motivated, addresses a meaningful gap in the literature regarding comparison function design, and provides thorough empirical evaluation across diverse tasks.

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper for ICLR 2017. The primary reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides strong empirical evidence that a simple "compare-aggregate" framework with element-wise comparison functions generalizes well across multiple sequence matching tasks, and (2) the systematic comparison of six different comparison functions provides valuable insights for future work in this area.

The paper makes several strong contributions. First, by testing their model on four distinct datasets representing different sequence matching tasks, the authors convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of their framework. Second, their systematic evaluation of six comparison functions—particularly highlighting the effectiveness of SUB and MULT—provides important insights that could guide future model design. The results are clear and consistent, with the element-wise comparison functions outperforming more complex approaches on multiple datasets. The ablation study effectively demonstrates the importance of both the preprocessing and attention layers in their framework.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written. The model description is detailed enough to be reproducible, and the experiments are thorough. The authors have made their code available online, which is a significant plus for reproducibility.

For improvement, I suggest the following:

1. The preprocessing layer (Equation 1) is described but not thoroughly explained. The authors should clarify how the gate values work in practice and provide more intuition about why this specific form of preprocessing is beneficial. A brief discussion of how the gate values relate to attention weights would help readers understand the relationship between these components.

2. The paper mentions that SUB is "closely related to Euclidean distance" but doesn't elaborate on the specific relationship. A sentence or two explaining this connection would help readers better understand why SUB might work well.

3. The computational complexity and parameter count of each comparison function would be helpful for readers to understand the trade-offs between different approaches. For instance, how many parameters do SUB and MULT use compared to NN and NTN?

4. The paper states that "the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive" but doesn't clearly explain how this affects the model's ability to capture contextual information. A brief discussion of this trade-off would strengthen the paper.

5. The results show that EUCCOS performs well on MovieQA, but the paper doesn't discuss why this might be the case. A short analysis of the specific characteristics of MovieQA that might make Euclidean distance/cosine similarity more appropriate could provide additional insight.

To better understand the paper, I would like to ask the authors:

1. In the preprocessing step (Equation 1), how do the parameters Wi, Wu, bi, and bu get initialized? Does the model's performance depend significantly on these initializations?

2. For the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT), the paper states they "do not need parameters." However, the implementation (Equations 6-7) doesn't involve any learnable parameters. Could you clarify whether these functions truly have no parameters, and if so, how this impacts the model's capacity?

3. The paper mentions that the model outperforms previous work on InsuranceQA with "any comparison function." Could you provide more detail on whether the improvement is consistent across all comparison functions, or if some functions work particularly well on this dataset?

4. The authors state that "the attention layer plays a more important role" for tasks with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) compared to tasks with smaller differences (WikiQA, SNLI). Could you elaborate on how you measured this importance, and whether this pattern holds across different comparison functions?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a clear contribution to the sequence matching literature. The empirical evidence is compelling, and the insights about comparison functions will likely be valuable to future work in this area. The paper is well-written and makes a solid case for the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework with simple comparison functions.
