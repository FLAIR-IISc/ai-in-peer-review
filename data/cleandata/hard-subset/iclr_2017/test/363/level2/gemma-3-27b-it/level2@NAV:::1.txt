## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, evaluating various comparison functions within that framework across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors argue for the general effectiveness of this framework and highlight the surprisingly strong performance of element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) compared to more complex methods like neural networks and neural tensor networks. The paper is well-motivated, addressing a core problem in NLP and building upon existing “compare-aggregate” approaches.

I recommend **Acceptance** of this paper. The work provides a valuable empirical investigation into the effectiveness of different comparison functions, revealing a counter-intuitive finding that simpler methods can outperform more complex ones. This insight is useful for practitioners and researchers in sequence matching. The systematic evaluation across diverse datasets strengthens the findings and suggests the robustness of the proposed approach. 

The paper effectively establishes the problem, clearly outlines the proposed model and comparison functions, and presents a comprehensive experimental setup. The results are presented clearly and supported by ablation studies, demonstrating the importance of the attention and preprocessing layers. The inclusion of code availability is also a positive contribution to reproducibility. The comparison to relevant baselines on each dataset provides context for the reported performance.

However, the paper could be strengthened with some additions. While the results demonstrate *that* element-wise operations work well, it doesn't delve deeply into *why*. Further analysis exploring the learned representations or visualizing attention weights could provide more intuition. Additionally, the description of the preprocessing layer could be expanded. While it is mentioned as order-insensitive, discussing potential downsides of this design choice, and exploring order-sensitive alternatives, could be beneficial.  

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of *why* the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) perform so well? Are there specific characteristics of these datasets that favor these functions?
*   The preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Have you experimented with order-sensitive preprocessing techniques (e.g., using RNNs), and if so, what were the results?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, especially the embedding dimensionality and the CNN filter sizes? Were these parameters tuned specifically for each dataset, or were they kept consistent?
