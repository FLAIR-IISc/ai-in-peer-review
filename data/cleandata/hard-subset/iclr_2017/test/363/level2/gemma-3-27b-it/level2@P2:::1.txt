This paper presents a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, evaluating different comparison functions within this structure across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The core idea – systematically investigating various comparison functions beyond standard neural networks – is a valuable contribution, and the empirical results demonstrate that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex approaches.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP, proposes a well-motivated approach, and provides convincing empirical evidence supporting its claims. While not groundbreaking, the systematic evaluation of comparison functions is a significant and useful contribution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of sequence matching in several NLP tasks and justifies the need for a more thorough investigation of comparison functions within the “compare-aggregate” framework. The limitation of prior work – focusing on specific tasks and overlooking comparison function design – is well-explained.
* **Sound Methodology:** The “compare-aggregate” framework itself is well-established, and the paper builds upon it in a logical way. The selection of comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) represents a reasonable range of options, from complex to simple.  The use of four diverse datasets strengthens the generalizability of the findings.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results consistently show that SUB and MULT achieve competitive or superior performance across the datasets, often outperforming more complex models like NN and NTN. The ablation studies, while brief, provide some insights into the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. The analysis in section 3.4, using visualizations, adds credibility to the model’s behavior.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors mention making their code available, which is commendable and will facilitate further research.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Related Work:** While the related work section is adequate, it could be more comprehensive.  Specifically, recent advancements in attention mechanisms beyond what’s cited (e.g., self-attention, transformers) and other sequence matching architectures could be briefly discussed.
* **Ablation Studies:** Expand the ablation studies.  Beyond simply removing the preprocessing and attention layers, investigate the impact of different embedding sizes (d), CNN filter sizes, and the interaction between the different components.  A more detailed analysis would solidify the understanding of each module's contribution.
* **Statistical Significance:** While the paper presents performance numbers, statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) are not reported. Including these would strengthen the claims of superiority.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The paper mentions tuning dropout for WikiQA. Provide more details about the hyperparameter tuning process for *all* datasets. What search strategies were used? What ranges were explored?
* **Error Analysis:**  Include a qualitative error analysis.  Present some examples where the model fails and discuss potential reasons for those failures. This could provide insights for future improvements.
* **SNLI Performance:** The performance on SNLI is competitive but not state-of-the-art. Elaborate on why the model might not achieve the highest scores on this specific dataset, given its complexity.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the choice of element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), can you elaborate on the intuition behind their effectiveness?  Is there a connection to specific properties of semantic similarity that these operations capture?
2.  Could you provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process? Specifically, how did you select the learning rate, batch size, and other crucial parameters for each dataset?
3.  The paper mentions the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive. Have you experimented with order-sensitive preprocessing techniques (e.g., using LSTMs or GRUs) and, if so, how did they affect performance?
4.  What types of errors does the model frequently make, and what are the limitations of the approach? Providing concrete examples would be helpful.
