- Paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching and evaluates various comparison functions on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, finding element-wise SUB and MULT outperform NN/NTN.  
- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Main reason: contributions are incremental and not sufficiently novel.  
- Main reason: empirical justification for why simple element-wise ops work better is weak.  
- Analysis is shallow; paper reports results but lacks depth in explaining observed behaviors.  
- Ablation studies are limited and do not specifically target the comparison function.  
- Missing investigation of factors like activation functions and embedding dimensionality affecting comparison strategies.  
- Related work discussion is superficial, lacking deeper theoretical context on similarity measures.  
- Suggestion: include qualitative error analysis to reveal model limitations.  
- Suggestion: report statistical significance for performance differences (e.g., Table 3).  
- Suggestion: provide detailed hyperparameter tuning procedures (learning rates, batch sizes, dropout).  
- Suggestion: visualize attention weights to illustrate model behavior.  
- Suggestion: perform targeted ablation on comparison functions (e.g., different activations, other element-wise ops).  
- Suggestion: quantify computational cost differences between comparison functions.  
- Questions for authors: explain why SUB and MULT perform well, whether other element-wise ops were tried, sensitivity to embedding dimensionality, effects of order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., LSTMs), and hardware/software/training-time details.
