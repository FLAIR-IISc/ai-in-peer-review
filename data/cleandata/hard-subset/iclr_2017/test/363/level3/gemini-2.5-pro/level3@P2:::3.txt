### Summary of the Paper

This paper proposes a general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of natural language understanding tasks that require matching two text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The model first uses an attention mechanism to produce soft alignments between the words of the two sequences. It then applies a comparison function to each aligned word pair to generate a sequence of comparison vectors. Finally, a convolutional neural network (CNN) aggregates these vectors to make a final prediction. The primary contribution of the work is a systematic, empirical investigation into the design of the comparison function. The authors evaluate six different functions—ranging from complex Neural Networks and Neural Tensor Networks to simpler, parameter-free element-wise operations—across four challenging and diverse datasets. The results demonstrate that the general framework is highly effective, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive performance across all tasks, and that surprisingly simple comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex, heavily parameterized alternatives.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper provides a valuable and thorough empirical study of the comparison component within the popular compare-aggregate framework. The finding that simpler, less-parameterized comparison functions are highly effective is a practical and important contribution for the community.
2.  While the overall architecture is an incremental extension of recent work, the paper convincingly demonstrates the framework's generality and robustness by applying it successfully to four different sequence matching tasks, achieving strong results on each. This breadth of evaluation is a significant contribution that solidifies the utility of this modeling approach.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The main strength of this paper lies in its focused and systematic analysis of the word-level comparison function, a critical but often underexplored component of sequence matching models. By isolating and testing six different functional forms, the authors provide clear evidence that complex, high-capacity models like Neural Networks (NN) or Neural Tensor Networks (NTN) are not always the best choice. The success of the SUB and MULT functions, which are based on simple element-wise operations, is a compelling result. This provides a useful lesson in model design, suggesting that carefully chosen, well-motivated architectural priors can be more effective than simply increasing model parameterization.

Furthermore, the paper serves as an excellent validation of the compare-aggregate paradigm. While similar ideas have appeared in recent work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b), this paper is one of the first to demonstrate its effectiveness across such a wide range of tasks (three different QA settings and NLI). By showing that a single, unified architecture can achieve strong performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, the authors make a convincing case for its generality. This is a valuable contribution, as it helps consolidate a promising line of research and provides a strong baseline model for future work on these and similar problems.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions aimed at further strengthening the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Order Insensitivity:** The preprocessing layer described in Equation 1 is order-insensitive. While an LSTM is added for the SNLI task, this means that for the other three (longer sequence) tasks, the initial word representations do not incorporate contextual information from their neighbors. This is a significant architectural simplification that may limit the model's upper-bound performance on tasks where word order and local syntax are important. It would be beneficial to explicitly acknowledge and discuss this limitation in the paper.
*   **Neural Tensor Network (NTN) Capacity:** The NTN comparison function (Eq. 4) has O(l^3) parameters, which is very high for l=150. This makes it highly susceptible to overfitting and may explain its relatively poor performance. A common technique to manage this is to use a low-rank factorization of the tensor, which would dramatically reduce the parameter count. This could be a more competitive variant of the NTN baseline.
*   **Citation Nuances:**
    *   The proposed attention mechanism in Equation 2, which uses a learned bilinear term `(WgQ)TA`, appears more closely related to the "general" attention in Luong et al. (2015) than the additive attention in Bahdanau et al. (2014). Citing the more relevant work would improve precision.
    *   The SUBMULT+NN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy used in Mou et al. (2016), which also combines element-wise difference and product. Acknowledging this connection would better place your work in the literature.

### Questions for the Authors

I have one main question to help clarify a design choice in the model:
*   In Equation 1, the preprocessing layers for the two input sequences (Q and A) use shared parameters (Wi, Wu, bi, bu). Given that the sequences often have distinct roles (e.g., question vs. answer, premise vs. hypothesis), did you experiment with using separate, un-tied parameters for each sequence? It seems plausible that the two sequences might benefit from different gating and transformation logic.
