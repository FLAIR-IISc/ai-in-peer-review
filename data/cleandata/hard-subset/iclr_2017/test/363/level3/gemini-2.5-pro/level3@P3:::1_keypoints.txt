- Presents a "compare-aggregate" framework for matching two text sequences using attention, word-level comparisons, and CNN aggregation.  
- Systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Finds simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform complex parameterized functions (NN, NTN).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: Empirical finding that SUB and MULT are highly effective and practical for sequence matching.  
- Key reason: Single unified architecture achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across diverse tasks, showing robustness and generality.  
- Strength: Strong, systematic empirical study isolating the comparison function across multiple tasks.  
- Strength: Demonstrated generality and strong performance, notably on InsuranceQA where all variants outperform baselines.  
- Strength: Clear writing, reproducible experiments, and released code.  
- Strength: Actionable insight that element-wise operations capture differences and interactions efficiently and inform model design.  
- Weakness: Incremental novelty—architecture components (attention, CNN) are standard; main contribution is empirical analysis rather than a new technique.  
- Suggestion: Add qualitative analysis (e.g., examples and visualizations) to explain why SUB/MULT perform better.  
- Suggestion: Visualize pre-aggregation comparison vectors (t_j) across sequences rather than only aggregated CNN maps.  
- Suggestion: Discuss computational complexity and parameter efficiency of different comparison functions explicitly.  
- Question: Can the authors provide intuition on semantic properties captured by subtraction vs. multiplication?  
- Question: Was the CNN architecture adjusted for differing comparison vector dimensionalities, or kept fixed, possibly disadvantaging low-dimensional functions?  
- Question: Could SUB/MULT superiority be due to fewer parameters and better generalization—any analysis of performance vs. parameter count?
