- Paper studies sequence matching within the compare-aggregate framework and systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions.  
- Main finding: simple, parameter-free element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) consistently match or outperform complex parameterized functions (NN, NTN).  
- Evaluated across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Valuable large-scale empirical study highlighting a practical design choice favoring simple element-wise comparisons.  
- Key reason 2: Demonstrated generality and robustness by strong performance on multiple QA and NLI tasks.  
- Strength: Focused, systematic investigation isolating the comparison function clarifies an underexplored component of matching architectures.  
- Insight: Simpler element-wise comparisons provide a useful inductive bias that can outperform generic neural layers at the comparison stage.  
- Experimental validation is extensive and convincing, with ablations showing attention and preprocessing layers meaningfully contribute.  
- Paper is well-written and experiments are well-designed, increasing confidence in the results.  
- Suggested improvement: Provide more intuition and qualitative analysis explaining why SUB and MULT work so well.  
- Suggested improvement: Include qualitative visualizations of pre-aggregation comparison vectors (t_j) and attention weights rather than only post-aggregation CNN outputs.  
- Suggested improvement: Discuss the role of the order-insensitive preprocessing layer versus LSTM preprocessing and report comparisons across tasks.  
- Questions for authors: Expand on the intuition that SUB/MULT balance dimensionality retention and structural bias versus EUCCOS and NN/NTN.  
- Questions for authors: Clarify why the LSTM preprocessor was only used on SNLI and whether other preprocessor combinations were tested.  
- Questions for authors: Consider whether a three-way comparison architecture for MovieQA (q, p, a) was explored or how it might compare to the current two-way concatenation approach.
