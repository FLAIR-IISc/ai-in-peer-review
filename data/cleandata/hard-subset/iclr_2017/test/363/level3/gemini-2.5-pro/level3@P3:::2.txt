### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The core idea is to first perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then use a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to aggregate these comparison signals for a final decision. The main contribution of the work is a systematic exploration and evaluation of six different comparison functions, ranging from standard neural networks (NN, NTN) and similarity metrics (Euclidean, Cosine) to simpler, parameter-free, element-wise operations (Subtraction, Multiplication). The authors demonstrate the strength and generality of their approach through extensive experiments on four challenging datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across the board. A key finding is that simple element-wise comparison functions often outperform more complex, parameterized alternatives.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Valuable Systematic Study:** The paper provides a clear, systematic, and insightful empirical study of the comparison function, a critical but often overlooked component in sequence matching models. This analysis, which shows that simple element-wise operations can be highly effective, is a valuable contribution that can inform future research in this area.
2.  **Strong Empirical Results:** The claims are well-supported by strong and comprehensive experimental results across four diverse tasks. The proposed model consistently achieves state-of-the-art or very competitive performance against strong, published baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of the compare-aggregate framework combined with an appropriate comparison function.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper is well-motivated by identifying a gap in the existing literature. While several recent models have adopted a similar compare-aggregate structure, they have not systematically investigated the word-level comparison step. This paper fills that gap admirably by isolating this component and evaluating six distinct functional forms. The comparison between complex, learnable functions (NN, NTN) and simpler, intuitive ones (EUCCOS, SUB, MULT) provides a clear picture of the trade-offs involved. The finding that SUBMULT+NN is the best-performing model across tasks is a strong, actionable conclusion.

The empirical validation is thorough and convincing. By testing on three different question-answering tasks (with different structures) and one textual entailment task, the authors convincingly demonstrate the model's wide applicability. The performance gains, especially on the InsuranceQA and MovieQA datasets, are significant and clearly establish the model as a strong contender in the sequence matching space. The paper is well-written, the model is clearly explained, and the experiments are well-designed, making the results easy to understand and trust.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The paper is of high quality, and the following points are intended as suggestions for further improvement.

*   **Minor Typo:** On page 4, Section 2.4, line 5, "including a some" should be corrected to "including some".
*   **Figure 2 Readability:** Figure 2, which provides a qualitative analysis, is very difficult to read due to the small font size of the word labels. This is especially problematic for a printed version of the paper. I strongly recommend improving the quality of this figure, perhaps by using a vector graphics format, increasing the font size, or splitting the visualization into two larger figures.
*   **Ablation Study Presentation:** In Section 3.3, point (4), the authors mention an ablation study on the preprocessing and attention layers. While the summary of the findings is interesting, the paper would be significantly strengthened by presenting these results in a table. This would allow readers to quantitatively assess the contribution of each component on the different datasets, rather than relying solely on the textual description.

### **Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  As mentioned above, the text describes an ablation study on the preprocessing and attention layers. Could you please provide the concrete performance numbers (e.g., in a table) for the model run without the preprocessing layer, without the attention layer, and without both? This would provide clearer evidence for their importance.
2.  The paper notes that the order-insensitive preprocessing layer works surprisingly well on the QA tasks, and that an LSTM was only incorporated for the SNLI task. Have you experimented with adding the more context-aware LSTM preprocessing to the QA tasks? It would be interesting to see if this provides further gains or if, as you hypothesize, the word-by-word comparison is indeed the dominant factor in those tasks.
