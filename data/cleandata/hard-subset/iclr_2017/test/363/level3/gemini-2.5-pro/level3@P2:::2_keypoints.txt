- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework that performs fine-grained word-level comparisons then aggregates via a CNN for sequence matching.
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean, cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication).
- Experiments on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) show state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance.
- Key finding: simple, parameter-free element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex parameterized models.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reason: valuable, systematic study of the comparison component with strong empirical support across multiple tasks.
- Novelty: decouples and rigorously compares the comparison function within the compare-aggregate architecture, extending prior related work.
- Empirical setup is thorough and demonstrates the framework's generalizability and robustness against many specialized baselines.
- Paper strengths include clear writing and public release of code.
- Minor improvements suggested: fix a typo ("including a some"), regenerate Figure 2 at higher resolution for readability.
- Suggested addition: present quantitative ablation results for preprocessing and attention layers (e.g., in a table).
- Questions for authors: provide numerical ablation results for removed components and compare parameter counts/training times between simple and complex comparison functions.
