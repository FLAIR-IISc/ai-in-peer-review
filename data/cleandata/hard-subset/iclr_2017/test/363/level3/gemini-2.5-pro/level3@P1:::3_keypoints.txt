- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework: gated preprocessing → attention alignment → word-level comparison → CNN aggregation for sequence matching.  
- Core contribution is a systematic empirical analysis of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, etc.).  
- Finds simple, parameter-free element-wise operations (subtraction, multiplication) often outperform complex parameterized comparators like NTN.  
- Evaluates on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and achieves strong or state-of-the-art results on three.  
- Recommends acceptance based on the empirical study's value and the framework's strong cross-task performance.  
- Paper is timely, well-written, and offers practical guidance for choosing comparison functions in NLP models.  
- Architecture builds on prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016) but the focused analysis of the comparison function is a significant contribution.  
- Experimental validation is thorough and demonstrates general applicability across different sequence-matching tasks.  
- Limitation: preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for three QA tasks (gated bag-of-words), reducing ability to capture syntactic/sequential information.  
- Adding an LSTM for SNLI helped, indicating the need to incorporate sequential context in preprocessing for harder reasoning tasks.  
- NTN likely overfits due to huge parameter count (l x l x l); consider low-rank factorization or projection to smaller dimensions.  
- Suggest explicitly contrasting with Mou et al. (2016): they used SUB/MULT at sentence level, this work shows effectiveness at attention-aligned word level.  
- Main question for authors: did you try using separate (untied) preprocessing parameters for each input sequence (e.g., question vs. answer), and if so, what was the effect?
