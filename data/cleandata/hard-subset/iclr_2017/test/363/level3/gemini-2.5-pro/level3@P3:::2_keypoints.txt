- Presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework that does word-level comparisons then uses a CNN to aggregate signals for sequence matching.
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean, Cosine, Subtraction, Multiplication) and finds simple element-wise operations often outperform complex parameterized ones.
- Demonstrates strong, often state-of-the-art results across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), showing framework generality.
- Key finding: SUBMULT+NN (element-wise combinations plus NN) is the best-performing variant across tasks.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reason 1: Valuable, systematic empirical study isolating the often-overlooked word-level comparison component.
- Main reason 2: Strong, comprehensive experimental validation against solid baselines on diverse tasks.
- Supporting argument: Fills a gap by isolating and comparing comparison functions, clarifying trade-offs between simple and complex methods.
- Experiments are thorough and convincing, with significant gains especially on InsuranceQA and MovieQA.
- Paper is well-written, model clearly explained, and experiments well-designed.
- Minor typo noted: "including a some" → "including some" (p.4, Sec 2.4).
- Figure 2 is hard to read; recommend vector graphics, larger font, or splitting into two figures.
- Recommend presenting ablation study results in a table to quantify preprocessing and attention contributions across datasets.
- Questions for authors: provide concrete performance numbers for ablations (no preprocessing, no attention, neither) and clarify whether LSTM-based preprocessing was tried on QA tasks and its effect.
