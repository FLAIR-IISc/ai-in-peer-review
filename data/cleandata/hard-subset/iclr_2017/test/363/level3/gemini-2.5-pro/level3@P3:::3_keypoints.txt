- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework: attention-based alignment → per-pair comparison → CNN aggregation for sequence matching tasks.
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, etc.) with an empirical focus on the comparison component.
- Finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT) often outperform more complex, heavily parameterized comparison functions.
- Evaluated on four benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and achieves strong or state-of-the-art results.
- Decision: Accept.
- Main reason 1: The framework is effective and generalizes across diverse sequence-matching tasks.
- Main reason 2: Provides an insightful, practical analysis showing simplicity in comparison functions is often superior.
- Strengths: simplicity, strong empirical performance, clear ablation studies (preprocessing and attention shown important), and thorough evaluation.
- Novelty concern: The overall attend-compare-aggregate paradigm is known, making the work somewhat incremental, but the systematic f-function comparison and CNN aggregation justify publication.
- Limitation: Preprocessing layer is word-order insensitive (bag-of-words), potentially limiting performance on tasks needing fine syntactic reasoning.
- Citation refinements suggested: specify Luong et al. (2015) as the "general" bilinear attention variant and explicitly relate SUBMULT to Mou et al. (2016).
- NTN concern: Neural Tensor Network has O(l^3) parameters and may overfit; suggest noting this and exploring low-rank factorization.
- Question 1 for authors: Did you try separate preprocessing parameters for each input sequence (e.g., question vs. answer)?
- Question 2 for authors: Why does an order-insensitive preprocessing model perform well on QA—do these benchmarks rely mainly on keyword/local semantic matches rather than deep syntactic understanding?
