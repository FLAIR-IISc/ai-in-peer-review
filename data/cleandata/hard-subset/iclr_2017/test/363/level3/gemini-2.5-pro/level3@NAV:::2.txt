This paper presents a general “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, a fundamental problem in many NLP tasks such as question answering and textual entailment. The proposed model first performs word-level matching between two sequences and then uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to aggregate these local comparison results for a final decision. A significant contribution of this work is the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions, ranging from standard neural networks to simpler, element-wise operations. The authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness and generalizability through extensive experiments on four diverse datasets, achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results compared to published baselines.

**Decision:** Accept

This paper is a strong contribution that I recommend for acceptance. The primary reasons are: (1) it provides a convincing, cross-task validation of the "compare-aggregate" architecture, demonstrating its robustness and wide applicability, and (2) its systematic analysis of comparison functions offers a valuable and practical insight that simpler, element-wise functions can outperform more complex, parameterized alternatives.

**Supporting Arguments:**
The claims made by the authors are well-supported by rigorous empirical evidence. By evaluating their model on four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), they effectively address a limitation of prior work, which often focused on only one or two tasks. This multi-task evaluation strengthens their central claim about the framework's general utility. Furthermore, the investigation into different comparison functions is a core strength. The finding that element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUB, MULT) are highly effective is a noteworthy result that challenges the assumption that more complex functions like Neural Tensor Networks are always superior for such sub-tasks. The paper is also clearly written and well-structured, making its contributions easy to follow.

**Additional Feedback:**
The following points are intended to help improve the final version of the paper:
*   The visualization in Figure 2 is a great idea for providing intuition, but its current quality is poor. It is very difficult to read the text and discern details, especially in a black-and-white printout. I strongly suggest improving the resolution and clarity of this figure.
*   There is a minor typo on page 4, line 5: "including a some" should be corrected to "including some."

**Questions for the Authors:**
In Section 3.3, you mention that ablation experiments confirmed the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. Could you elaborate on the quantitative impact of removing these components? A more detailed breakdown showing the performance drop on each dataset when the preprocessing layer is removed versus when the attention layer is removed would further strengthen your claims and help readers understand the relative contribution of each component in different task settings.
