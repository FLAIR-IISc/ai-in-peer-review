This paper presents a general and effective “compare-aggregate” framework for natural language understanding tasks that require matching two text sequences, such as question answering and textual entailment. The proposed model uses a soft attention mechanism to align tokens, applies a comparison function to the aligned token pairs, and aggregates the results with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to produce a final output. A key contribution is the paper's systematic empirical analysis of six different comparison functions, which demonstrates that simple, element-wise operations can outperform more complex and parameter-heavy alternatives like Neural Tensor Networks.

I recommend accepting this paper. The work is timely, the proposed model is straightforward yet powerful, and it achieves strong results on four different benchmark datasets. While the overall framework is arguably incremental given prior work, the paper’s value lies in its generalization of the approach and its detailed, practical investigation into the comparison component, which provides a useful contribution to the field.

The primary strength of this paper is its thorough empirical validation. By testing the framework on four distinct tasks, the authors convincingly demonstrate its general applicability. The analysis of comparison functions is particularly insightful, showing that the non-parameteric SUB and MULT operations are highly effective. However, the work’s main limitation is that the model is insensitive to word order for longer sequences (all evaluated tasks except SNLI), which empirically limits its performance ceiling. This trade-off between simplicity and modeling power should be discussed more prominently. Despite this, the strong results validate the authors' claims about the effectiveness of their approach.

To further improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions. First, the attention mechanism used is closer to Luong et al. (2015)'s "general" scoring than the often-cited Bahdanau et al. (2014); making this distinction clearer would be helpful. Second, the Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has a large number of parameters; exploring a low-rank tensor variant could reduce the risk of overfitting. Finally, the similarity of the SubMultNN function to the sentence-level matching strategy from Mou et al. (2016) could be noted more explicitly.

I have one question for the authors: Could you explain the rationale for using identical preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer sequences? Given their potentially different structures and importance weighting, would using separate parameters for each sequence type provide any benefit?
