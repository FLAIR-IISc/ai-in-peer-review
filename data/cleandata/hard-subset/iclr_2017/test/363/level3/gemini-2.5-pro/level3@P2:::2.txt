### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, a fundamental problem in many NLP tasks such as question answering and textual entailment. The core idea is to first perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then aggregate these comparison signals using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction. The main contribution of this work is a systematic exploration and evaluation of six different comparison functions that can be used to match word-level vector representations. These functions range from complex, parameterized models like standard Neural Networks (NN) and Neural Tensor Networks (NTN) to simpler, parameter-free functions based on Euclidean distance, cosine similarity, and element-wise subtraction and multiplication. The authors conduct extensive experiments on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), demonstrating that their framework achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. Notably, they find that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations often outperform their more complex counterparts.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper provides a valuable and systematic study of the "comparison" component in the increasingly popular "compare-aggregate" architecture. The finding that simple, non-parametric, element-wise functions are highly effective is an important and practical contribution to the field.
2.  The claims are strongly supported by rigorous empirical evaluation across four different and challenging sequence matching tasks. The model demonstrates its generalizability by achieving state-of-the-art performance on three of the datasets and competitive performance on the fourth.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's primary strength lies in its focused and systematic analysis of a crucial architectural choice in models for sentence-pair modeling. While the overall "compare-aggregate" framework is related to prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), this paper is the first to decouple the comparison function and rigorously evaluate multiple alternatives side-by-side across a wide range of tasks.

The key insight—that simple functions like element-wise subtraction (SUB) and multiplication (MULT) can outperform more heavily parameterized functions like NN and NTN—is significant. This result provides a valuable lesson in model design, suggesting that for matching tasks, imposing a strong inductive bias through simple, similarity-focused operations can be more effective than relying on a generic, fully-learned transformation.

The experimental setup is thorough and convincing. By evaluating on four distinct datasets with different characteristics (answer selection, ranking, entailment, and machine comprehension), the authors convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of their framework and the robustness of their findings. The model's strong performance against a comprehensive set of published baselines, including many highly specialized models for each task, solidifies the paper's claims. The clarity of the writing and the public release of the code are also commendable and beneficial for the research community.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   The paper is very well-written. I noticed one minor typo on page 4: "including a some" should probably be "including some".
*   The visualization in Figure 2 is a nice idea to provide intuition about what the model learns. However, the image quality is low, making the text labels very difficult to read, especially in a black-and-white printout. I would strongly encourage the authors to regenerate this figure at a higher resolution for the final camera-ready version.
*   In Section 3.3, you discuss an ablation study where the preprocessing and attention layers were removed. This is an important analysis. The paper would be stronger if these results were presented quantitatively in a table (perhaps as part of Table 4), rather than just being summarized in the text. This would allow readers to more clearly see the specific impact of each component on each task.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if you could clarify the following points in your rebuttal:
1.  Could you provide the specific quantitative results from your ablation study on the preprocessing and attention layers? A table showing the performance drop on each dataset when these components are removed would greatly strengthen your claim about their importance.
2.  Your results show that the computationally cheaper, parameter-free comparison functions (SUB, MULT) are very effective. Could you comment on the practical differences in terms of parameter count and/or training time compared to the more complex NN and NTN variants? This would provide another compelling reason to favor the simpler methods you advocate for.
