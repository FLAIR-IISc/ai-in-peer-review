- Empirical study in the "compare-aggregate" framework for semantic text matching (QA, entailment).  
- Model: word-level attentive comparisons followed by CNN-based aggregation.  
- Main contribution: systematic comparison of six word-level comparison functions.  
- Key finding: simple parameter-free element-wise subtraction and multiplication often beat complex parameterized functions (NNs, neural tensor networks).  
- Strong empirical validation across four diverse datasets, showing generality and robustness.  
- Recommendation: Accept — focused and practically useful empirical contribution.  
- Strength: careful, systematic analysis of an under-explored component rather than proposing a radical new architecture.  
- Practical takeaway: simpler comparison operators can guide future model design.  
- Suggested improvement: add qualitative analysis (examples of comparison vectors for synonyms, paraphrases, contradictions).  
- Question 1: Need more intuition for why subtraction/multiplication work (semantic difference vs. shared attributes?).  
- Question 2: Was the CNN aggregator compared to alternatives (LSTM or pooling)?  
- Question 3: Why were LSTMs used in preprocessing only for the SNLI task?
