### Summary of the paper

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, a fundamental problem in many NLP tasks like question answering and textual entailment. The model first uses a gated preprocessing step on word embeddings, then employs a standard attention mechanism to align words from one sequence to the other, generating context-aware representations. The core contribution of the work lies in its systematic empirical analysis of the subsequent "comparison" step, where these aligned word representations are compared. The authors evaluate six different comparison functions, ranging from standard neural networks (NN) and neural tensor networks (NTN) to simpler, less common element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication). Finally, the resulting comparison vectors are aggregated using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to produce a final prediction. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and generality of their framework by achieving strong or state-of-the-art results on four distinct datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. A key finding is that simple, parameter-free comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex, parameterized alternatives.

### Decision and Key Reasons

I recommend **Acceptance** for this paper.

My decision is based on two primary reasons:
1.  The paper provides a valuable and systematic empirical study of various word-level comparison functions. This is a crucial component in many modern NLP architectures, yet it is often chosen without extensive justification. The finding that simpler element-wise operations are highly effective is an important and useful contribution to the community.
2.  The proposed framework, while building on existing ideas, is shown to be highly effective and generalizable, achieving strong performance across four different sequence matching tasks. This cross-task validation strengthens the paper's claims about the robustness of the "compare-aggregate" approach.

### Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper is timely, well-written, and addresses an important problem. While the overall "compare-aggregate" architecture is not entirely novel and bears resemblance to prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), the authors' primary contribution—the focused analysis of the comparison function `f`—is significant enough to warrant acceptance. This systematic evaluation provides practical guidance for future research in this area. The discovery that simple, non-parameterized functions like SUB and MULT can outperform complex, high-capacity models like NTN is a compelling result that encourages simpler, more interpretable model design.

The experimental validation is thorough. Testing on four datasets with different characteristics (answer selection, machine comprehension, textual entailment) convincingly demonstrates the general applicability of the framework. The model achieves state-of-the-art results on three of the four tasks, which provides strong evidence for its effectiveness.

However, the work is not without its limitations. The model's preprocessing layer (Equation 1) is order-insensitive for the three QA tasks, effectively treating the input sequences as a gated bag-of-words. While the results are strong, this design choice fundamentally limits the model's ability to capture syntactic or sequential information, which is crucial for many complex reasoning tasks. This limitation should be more explicitly discussed as a potential ceiling on the model's performance and an important direction for future work. Despite this, the paper's contributions in analyzing the comparison component and demonstrating the framework's broad utility are substantial.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Order Insensitivity:** As mentioned above, the order-insensitive nature of the preprocessing for most tasks is a major simplification. While you note that adding an LSTM for SNLI helped, I would suggest making this limitation more prominent in the introduction or discussion. It would strengthen the paper to acknowledge that while word-level matching is powerful, incorporating sequential context at the preprocessing stage is a key next step for this framework.
*   **NTN Capacity:** The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) comparison function has a very large number of parameters (l x l x l), which makes it prone to overfitting, especially with hidden dimensions of l=150. This might explain its relatively poor performance. It would be interesting to see if a lower-rank factorization of the tensor or a variant that projects to a smaller dimension before the tensor product would yield better results.
*   **Connection to Mou et al. (2016):** You correctly cite Mou et al. (2016) for the use of element-wise subtraction and multiplication. It might be worth explicitly noting that they applied these operations at the sentence-representation level, whereas your work demonstrates their effectiveness at the more granular, attention-aligned word level. This would help better position your contribution relative to theirs.

### Questions for the Authors

I have one main question to help clarify my understanding of the model's design choices:

1.  In the preprocessing layer (Equation 1), the same parameters (Wi, Wu, bi, bu) are used to process both input sequences (e.g., the question Q and the answer A). Have you experimented with using separate, un-tied sets of parameters for each sequence? Intuitively, the features that are important to "gate" in a question might be different from those in a candidate answer, and allowing the model to learn separate transformations could potentially improve performance.
