- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework: attention-based word-level comparison followed by CNN aggregation for sequence matching.
- Systematically evaluates six different word-level comparison functions.
- Tests the framework on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) covering QA, ranking, and entailment tasks.
- Finds simple, parameter-free element-wise operations (subtraction, multiplication) are highly effective and often outperform complex parametric functions.
- Shows the combined SUBMULT+NN comparison consistently performs at or near the top across tasks.
- Achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on three of the four datasets.
- Emphasizes that the paper’s primary contribution is a rigorous empirical analysis rather than novel architecture.
- Experimental setup and ablation studies are thorough, demonstrating the generalizability and importance of attention and preprocessing layers.
- Decision: Accept.
- Suggestion: add qualitative analysis visualizing comparison-layer outputs (t_j) for SUB vs MULT vs NN to explain why some functions work better.
- Suggestion: include an error analysis to identify failure modes (e.g., negation, antonyms, logical reasoning).
- Question: clarify why QA preprocessing is order-insensitive while SNLI uses an LSTM; report experiments and costs for order-sensitive preprocessors on QA.
- Question: did you try symmetric (two-directional) attention where both sequences attend to each other, and what was the impact?
- Question: why were GloVe embeddings kept fixed—computational reasons or was fine-tuning unhelpful?
