### Summary

This paper presents a general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language understanding tasks that require matching two sequences of text, such as question answering and textual entailment. The proposed model first uses a soft attention mechanism to align words in one sequence to the other, generating context-aware representations. It then applies a comparison function to each aligned pair of word representations. Finally, it uses a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to aggregate these local comparison results into a final representation for classification or ranking. The primary contribution of the work is a systematic empirical investigation into the comparison function itself. The authors evaluate six different comparison functions, from standard neural networks (NN) and neural tensor networks (NTN) to simpler, element-wise operations (SUB, MULT). Through experiments on four diverse benchmark datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), the paper demonstrates that its framework achieves strong or state-of-the-art results, and notably, that simple comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex, heavily parameterized alternatives.

### Decision and Justification

**Decision:** Accept

This is a solid, well-executed empirical paper that addresses an important problem in NLP. I recommend acceptance for two main reasons:

1.  **Effective and Generalizable Framework:** The paper successfully demonstrates that a single, straightforward architecture can achieve excellent performance across four distinct sequence matching tasks. This validation of the "compare-aggregate" paradigm's generality is a valuable contribution.
2.  **Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The core strength of the paper is its detailed analysis of the comparison component. The finding that simple, non-parametric, element-wise operations can be more effective than complex neural networks is an important and practical insight for the community. This kind of careful ablation and analysis is exactly what the field needs.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strengths lie in its simplicity, strong empirical results, and clear analysis. While the overall architectural paradigm is not entirely novel and bears similarity to prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), this work makes sufficient contributions to stand on its own. The authors are transparent about this relationship and correctly frame their contribution around the systematic evaluation of the comparison component and the demonstration of the framework's effectiveness across a wider range of tasks.

The empirical evaluation is thorough, spanning four datasets with different characteristics. The model's strong performance, particularly on the three QA datasets, underscores its robustness. The ablation study in Table 4, which shows the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers, further strengthens the paper's claims. The key takeaway—that simple, well-motivated comparison functions like `SUBMULT` are powerful—is a useful piece of knowledge that can guide future model design.

However, the work is borderline incremental. The three stages (attend, compare, aggregate) are a known recipe. But, the specific instantiation (e.g., using a CNN for aggregation) and, more importantly, the rigorous head-to-head comparison of different `f` functions provide enough novelty and insight to merit publication at ICLR.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper. They do not change my overall assessment.

*   **Acknowledge the Limitation of Word-Order Insensitivity:** The preprocessing layer in Equation 1 is order-insensitive. While an LSTM is added for the SNLI task, for the other three tasks (which often involve longer sequences), this means the initial word representations do not encode their position or context within the source sentence. This is a significant modeling choice that effectively makes the model a "bag-of-words" at the preprocessing stage, relying entirely on the attention and aggregation layers to infer structure. This limitation should be explicitly stated and discussed in the introduction or discussion, as it likely imposes an upper bound on the model's performance on tasks requiring nuanced syntactic understanding.
*   **Refining Citations and Connections:**
    *   The attention mechanism in Equation 2 is a form of bilinear attention, and the citation to Luong et al. (2015) is appropriate. It could be slightly more precise to specify that this corresponds to Luong's "general" scoring function to better distinguish it from other common attention variants.
    *   The paper correctly cites Mou et al. (2016) for using SUB and MULT. The similarity is even stronger, as Mou et al. also combine element-wise subtraction and multiplication for sentence-level matching. Explicitly noting this connection for the `SUBMULT` variant would better situate your work.
*   **Reducing NTN Capacity:** The Neural Tensor Network (NTN) has O(l^3) parameters, which is massive and prone to overfitting. When reporting its performance, it might be worth mentioning this high capacity as a potential reason for its underwhelming results. For future work, exploring a low-rank factorization of the tensor could be a way to use the NTN's expressive power with far fewer parameters.

### Questions for Authors

I would appreciate it if you could clarify the following points during the author response period:

1.  In Equation 1, the same preprocessing parameters (`Wi`, `Wu`, etc.) are used for both input sequences (e.g., the question `Q` and the answer `A`). Did you experiment with using separate sets of parameters for each input? It seems plausible that the types of information worth gating would differ between a question and an answer, and separate parameters could capture this.
2.  Following up on the word-order insensitivity point, could you elaborate on why you believe this simplified model still performs so well on the QA tasks? Does this suggest that these benchmarks can be solved largely by identifying keyword matches and local semantic similarity, which your model is well-suited to capture, rather than by deep syntactic reasoning?
