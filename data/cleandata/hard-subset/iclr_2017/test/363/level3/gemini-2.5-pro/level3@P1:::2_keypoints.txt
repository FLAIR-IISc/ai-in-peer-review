- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework that compares word-level representations and aggregates with a CNN for sequence matching tasks.  
- Demonstrates a single model applied to MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI with state-of-the-art or competitive results.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions and finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex parameterized functions (NN, NTN).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Thorough empirical validation across four diverse datasets supports the framework's generality and effectiveness.  
- Reason 2: Insightful analysis showing simple element-wise comparisons are strong and challenge the default use of complex layers.  
- Experiments show the unified architecture achieves SOTA on three of four datasets, suggesting low risk of task-specific overfitting.  
- The comparison-function study (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, etc.) is the paper's most novel and practically useful contribution.  
- Ablation studies confirm the utility of preprocessing and attention layers and support the model design choices.  
- Presentation is clear and situates the work well within related literature on Siamese, attentive, and compare-aggregate models.  
- Suggest expanding intuition in Section 3.3 on why attention helps for large length differences and preprocessing helps for similar-length sequences.  
- Recommend improving Figure 2 resolution (use vector graphic) for readability.  
- Minor typo: change "including a some uncommon ones" to "including some uncommon ones" on page 4, Section 2.3.  
- Question 1: Please elaborate why attention is more important for long vs. short sequence differences and why preprocessing matters more for similar-length sequences.  
- Question 2: Was bi-directional attention considered or tested, and if not, why choose one-directional attention over symmetric approaches?
