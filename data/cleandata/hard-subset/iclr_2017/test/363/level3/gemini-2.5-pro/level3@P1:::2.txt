### Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The core idea is to first perform fine-grained, word-level comparisons between two sequences and then aggregate these comparison signals using a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) to make a final prediction. The primary contributions of this work are twofold. First, it demonstrates the general effectiveness of this architectural pattern by applying a single model structure to four different and challenging datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. Second, and more importantly, the paper conducts a systematic investigation into the comparison function itself, evaluating six different methods for matching word-level vector representations. A key finding is that simple, parameter-free comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex, parameterized functions like standard neural networks or neural tensor networks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong empirical paper with clear and useful contributions. My decision to accept is based on two main reasons:

1.  **Thorough Empirical Validation:** The paper's primary claim—that the compare-aggregate framework is a general and effective solution for sequence matching—is convincingly supported by extensive experiments across four diverse tasks. The strong performance against established baselines validates the model's effectiveness and generality.
2.  **Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The systematic evaluation of different comparison functions provides a valuable insight for the community. The discovery that simpler element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) are highly effective is non-obvious and challenges the common default of using more complex neural network layers for such interactions. This finding is practical and could inform the design of future models for similar tasks.

### Supporting Arguments

The strength of this paper lies in its rigorous and well-executed experiments. By testing on datasets for machine comprehension (MovieQA), answer selection (InsuranceQA, WikiQA), and natural language inference (SNLI), the authors show that their proposed model is not overfitted to a single task's peculiarities. Achieving state-of-the-art results on three of the four datasets with a unified architecture is a significant accomplishment.

The investigation into the comparison function `f` is the most novel part of the paper. While prior works have used similar compare-aggregate structures, they often defaulted to a standard neural network layer for the comparison step. This paper explicitly questions that choice. The authors motivate and compare six different functions, ranging from the highly parameterized (NN, NTN) to the simple and well-justified (EUCCOS) and a middle ground based on element-wise operations (SUB, MULT). The strong performance of SUB and MULT, which preserve dimensional information unlike EUCCOS but are more constrained than NN/NTN, is a compelling result. This analysis provides a concrete, data-backed recommendation for researchers working on related problems.

The ablation studies presented in Table 4 further strengthen the paper's claims by demonstrating the utility of the preprocessing and attention layers. The overall presentation is clear, and the model is well-situated within the existing literature on Siamese, attentive, and compare-aggregate networks.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions aimed at improving the paper further and are not critical to my decision.

*   The analysis of the ablation study in Section 3.3 could be expanded. You note that attention is more important for tasks with large length differences, while preprocessing is more important for tasks with smaller ones. This is an interesting observation, and providing more intuition as to *why* this might be the case would strengthen the paper's analysis.
*   Figure 2 is a useful visualization, but its resolution is quite low, making it difficult to read the text labels, particularly in a printed version or when zoomed out. Providing a higher-quality vector graphic would improve the paper's presentation.
*   There is a minor typo on page 4, Section 2.3: "including a some uncommon ones" should probably be "including some uncommon ones".

### Questions for the Authors

Your responses to these questions will help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper.

1.  The paper includes an ablation study in Table 4 that removes the preprocessing and attention layers. You conclude that the attention layer seems more important for tasks like MovieQA and InsuranceQA, whereas the preprocessing layer is more critical for WikiQA and SNLI. Could you please elaborate on the intuition behind this finding? What specific properties of the attention mechanism make it better suited for sequences with large length differences, and what about the gated preprocessing layer makes it more impactful for sequences of more similar length?
2.  The attention mechanism used is one-directional, calculating attention over the first sequence (Q) for each word in the second sequence (A). This differs from some related work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016) that uses a symmetric, two-directional attention mechanism. Was a bi-directional attention mechanism considered or tested? If so, how did it perform? If not, what was the reasoning for choosing the one-directional approach?
