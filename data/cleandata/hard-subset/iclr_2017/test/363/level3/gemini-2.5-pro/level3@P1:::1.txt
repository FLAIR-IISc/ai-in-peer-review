### Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses the important and widespread problem of matching text sequences, a core component of tasks like question answering and textual entailment. The authors propose a model that fits within the general and effective "compare-aggregate" framework. In this paradigm, fine-grained, word-level comparisons are performed between two sequences first, and these comparison results are then aggregated to produce a final decision. The main contribution of this work is a systematic and thorough empirical evaluation of the comparison function itself, a critical but often overlooked component of this framework. The authors implement and test six different comparison functions—ranging from standard neural networks to simple Euclidean distance and cosine similarity—across four different and challenging datasets. The core finding is that simple, parameter-free, element-wise comparison functions (namely, subtraction and multiplication) consistently perform at or near the top, often outperforming more complex and heavily parameterized functions like neural networks or neural tensor networks. The presented model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on the MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI benchmarks, demonstrating the general effectiveness of the proposed architecture and the validity of their findings regarding comparison functions.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a valuable, large-scale empirical study on a crucial component of modern neural architectures for sequence matching. The finding that simpler, element-wise comparison functions outperform more complex, parameterized ones is a significant and practical contribution that can inform future model design.
2.  The authors convincingly demonstrate the generality and robustness of their proposed model and its underlying principles by achieving strong performance across four diverse tasks in question answering and natural language inference.

### Supporting Arguments

The primary strength of this paper lies in its focused and systematic investigation. While the "compare-aggregate" framework is not entirely novel, the community's understanding of which specific mechanisms are most effective within it is still developing. This paper provides clarity by isolating the comparison function and rigorously testing multiple variants. The conclusion that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication work best is compelling. This is an important result, as it suggests that models for semantic matching benefit more from a strong inductive bias (i.e., directly comparing vector dimensions) than from the raw representational power of a generic neural network layer at this stage of the architecture. This insight could lead to simpler, more efficient, and potentially more robust models for these tasks.

Furthermore, the experimental validation is extensive and convincing. Applying a single, general model to four distinct datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and achieving state-of-the-art results on three of them provides strong evidence for the authors' claims. This breadth demonstrates that the findings are not artifacts of a single task or dataset but are likely more fundamental principles for sequence matching. The ablation studies in Table 4 also effectively justify the inclusion of the attention and preprocessing layers, confirming that each component of the architecture contributes meaningfully to the final performance. The paper is well-written, the model is clearly explained, and the experiments are well-designed, making the work a solid contribution to the field.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the empirical results are strong, the paper could be improved by providing more intuition and qualitative analysis as to *why* the element-wise comparison functions work so well. The current analysis is limited to "what works best," but the paper would have a greater impact if it could shed light on the underlying reasons.

*   **Qualitative Analysis of Comparison Functions:** It would be highly beneficial to include a qualitative analysis of how the different comparison functions behave on specific examples. For instance, visualizing the comparison vectors `t_j` for SUB, MULT, and NN on a few carefully chosen sentence pairs could be very insightful. Does SUB excel at capturing semantic divergence or negation? Does MULT primarily highlight strong lexical overlap? How does a trained NN function learn to combine the vectors compared to these fixed operations? Such an analysis would elevate the paper from a report of empirical findings to a more profound exploration of semantic comparison.
*   **Deeper Dive into Preprocessing:** The use of an order-insensitive preprocessing layer for three of the four tasks is an interesting design choice that yields surprisingly strong results. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of this. Was an LSTM preprocessor tested on these tasks and found to be ineffective or computationally prohibitive? Exploring this further could provide insights into how much word-order information is truly necessary for these tasks when a powerful word-level comparison mechanism is in place.
*   **Improved Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a helpful start but visualizes the output of the CNN, which is a post-aggregation signal. To more directly support the paper's claims about word-level matching, it would be more informative to visualize the pre-aggregation comparison vectors (`t_j`) or the attention weights (`G`). This would more clearly show which specific word-level comparisons are being identified as important by the model.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  You motivate the element-wise SUB and MULT functions as a "good compromise" between the overly specific EUCCOS and the overly general NN/NTN. Could you expand on this intuition? Is the primary benefit that they retain the full dimensionality of the word embeddings (unlike EUCCOS) while still imposing a strong, non-learned structural bias for comparison (unlike NN/NTN)?
2.  The LSTM preprocessor was used only for the SNLI task. Could you clarify the rationale for this? Did you experiment with the order-insensitive preprocessor on SNLI or the LSTM preprocessor on tasks like WikiQA, and if so, what were the results? This would help clarify the role of sequential context in your framework across different tasks.
3.  For the MovieQA task, the model matches (Question, Plot) and (Answer, Plot) separately and concatenates the comparison vectors. This seems to miss any direct, trilateral interaction. Did you consider an architecture that performs a three-way comparison (e.g., using a function `f(q_i, p_j, a_k)`), and do you have any thoughts on how that might compare to your current approach?
