- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework: attention-based soft alignment, per-word comparison, CNN aggregation for sequence matching tasks.
- Main contribution is a systematic empirical study of six comparison functions, showing simpler element-wise functions often outperform complex parameterized ones.
- Evaluated across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results, demonstrating framework generality.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Thorough empirical analysis revealing practical value of low-parameter comparison functions.
- Key reason 2: Broad evaluation across tasks convincingly demonstrates robustness and utility of the unified architecture.
- Supporting argument: Isolating and testing comparison functions shows NNs/NTNs are not always best; SUB and MULT are strong, practically important findings.
- Suggestion: Preprocessing layer (Eq.1) is order-insensitive for three longer-sequence tasks and may limit performance; discuss this limitation.
- Suggestion: NTN has O(l^3) parameters and likely overfits; recommend low-rank tensor factorization as a competitive variant.
- Citation note: Attention in Eq.2 resembles Luong et al. (2015) "general" attention more than Bahdanau et al. (2014); cite accordingly.
- Citation note: SUBMULT+NN is similar to Mou et al. (2016)'s sentence-level matching; acknowledge this relation.
- Question for authors: Did you try untied (separate) preprocessing parameters for the two sequences, given their distinct roles (Q vs A, premise vs hypothesis)?
