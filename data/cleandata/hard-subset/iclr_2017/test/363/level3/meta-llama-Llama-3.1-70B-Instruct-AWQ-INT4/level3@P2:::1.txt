**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which is applicable to various natural language processing tasks such as question answering and textual entailment. The framework consists of four layers: preprocessing, attention, comparison, and aggregation. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions, including element-wise operations, neural networks, and neural tensor networks, on four different datasets. The results show that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on three datasets and competitive performance on the fourth dataset. The authors also find that element-wise comparison functions, particularly subtraction and multiplication, generally perform better than neural networks and neural tensor networks.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I decide to **accept** this paper with some minor revisions. The key reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper proposes a well-motivated and general framework for sequence matching tasks, which is a significant contribution to the field.
2. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of different comparison functions, providing valuable insights into their strengths and weaknesses.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's main contribution is the systematic comparison of different comparison functions, which is a crucial aspect of sequence matching tasks. The authors' finding that element-wise comparison functions, particularly subtraction and multiplication, generally perform better than neural networks and neural tensor networks is surprising and valuable. The paper's evaluation on four different datasets also demonstrates the framework's applicability to various tasks.

However, I do have some concerns about the paper's novelty and innovation. While the framework is well-motivated and the evaluation is thorough, the paper's contributions are largely incremental, building upon existing work in the field. The authors could have explored more novel comparison functions or architectures to further advance the state-of-the-art.

**Additional Feedback**

To improve the paper, I suggest the authors provide more qualitative analysis on how the comparison functions behave on varied sentence types. This could involve visualizing the attention weights, analyzing the errors made by different comparison functions, or providing examples of sentences where the framework succeeds or fails. This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the framework's strengths and weaknesses.

Additionally, the authors could consider exploring more novel comparison functions or architectures, such as using graph-based models or incorporating external knowledge sources. This would further advance the state-of-the-art and make the paper more impactful.
