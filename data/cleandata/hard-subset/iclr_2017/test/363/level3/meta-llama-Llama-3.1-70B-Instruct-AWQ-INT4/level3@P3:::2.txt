This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which consists of a word-level matching step followed by a convolutional neural network (CNN) based aggregation step. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for word-level matching and demonstrate the effectiveness of their framework on four different datasets, achieving state-of-the-art performance on three of them.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their framework and the comparison functions they evaluate. The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their findings.

The specific question tackled by the paper is how to effectively match text sequences, which is a fundamental problem in natural language processing. The authors' approach is well-motivated, as they note that existing methods for sequence matching often rely on simplistic representations of the input sequences, such as bag-of-words or sentence embeddings. The "compare-aggregate" framework is a natural extension of these approaches, as it allows for a more fine-grained comparison of the input sequences.

The paper supports its claims through extensive experiments on four different datasets, including MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The authors demonstrate that their framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on three of these datasets and competitive performance on the fourth. They also provide an ablation study to evaluate the importance of different components of their framework.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason for this choice: the paper presents a well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated framework for matching text sequences, which achieves state-of-the-art performance on multiple datasets.

The supporting arguments for this decision are:

* The paper presents a clear and concise explanation of the "compare-aggregate" framework and the comparison functions evaluated.
* The experimental results are thorough and well-presented, demonstrating the effectiveness of the framework on multiple datasets.
* The authors provide an ablation study to evaluate the importance of different components of their framework, which helps to build confidence in their results.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

* The authors mention that their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, but they do not provide a clear explanation of why this is the case. It would be helpful to clarify this point.
* The authors could provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process, including the ranges of values they searched over and the criteria they used to select the best hyperparameters.
* Figure 2 is difficult to read, especially in print. The authors could consider using a different visualization or providing a higher-resolution version of the figure.
* The authors mention that they did not re-implement the baseline models, but instead used the reported performance from the original papers. It would be helpful to clarify why they made this choice and whether they believe it had any impact on their results.

Questions to the authors:

* Can you provide more detail on the preprocessing layer and why it is order-insensitive?
* How did you select the hyperparameters for your framework, and what ranges of values did you search over?
* Can you provide a higher-resolution version of Figure 2 or consider using a different visualization?
* Why did you choose not to re-implement the baseline models, and do you believe this had any impact on your results?

Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated framework for matching text sequences. With some minor revisions to address the points mentioned above, it has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of natural language processing.
