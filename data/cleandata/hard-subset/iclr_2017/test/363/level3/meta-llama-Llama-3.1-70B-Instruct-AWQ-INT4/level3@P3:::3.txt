This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which is a fundamental problem in natural language processing. The framework consists of three main components: soft attention to align tokens, a comparison function on attention pairs, and CNN aggregation to produce outputs. The authors provide a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component, evaluating six different comparison functions on four different datasets. The results show that some simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can work better than standard neural network and neural tensor network.

I recommend accepting this paper. The work is timely and relevant, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the comparison component, which is a crucial part of the framework. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, and the experiments are well-designed and executed.

The main contribution of this paper is the systematic evaluation of different comparison functions, which is a valuable contribution to the field. The authors also show that their framework can be applied to different tasks, including question answering and textual entailment. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art models, and the authors provide a detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of their approach.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the model is insensitive to word order for longer sequences, which may limit its upper-bound performance. The authors acknowledge this limitation and note that it should be addressed in future work.

Some additional feedback to improve the paper:

* The authors may want to consider citing Luong et al. (2015) for their attention mechanism, as it appears to be closer to their approach than Bahdanau et al.
* The neural tensor network (NTN) comparison function may be prone to overfitting due to its large number of parameters. The authors may want to consider using a lower-dimensional variant of NTN or regularizing its parameters.
* The SubMultNN comparison function resembles the sentence-level matching strategy from Lili Mou et al. The authors may want to acknowledge this similarity and discuss the differences between their approach and Mou et al.'s.
* The authors use the same preprocessing parameters for the question and answer. They may want to consider using different parameters for the question and answer, as they may require different weighting.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on how you selected the hyperparameters for your model? Were they tuned using a development set or were they chosen based on prior knowledge?
* How do you plan to address the limitation of your model being insensitive to word order for longer sequences?
* Can you provide more details on the computational resources required to train your model? Was it trained on a single GPU or a cluster of machines?

Overall, this is a well-written and well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of natural language processing. I recommend accepting it for publication.
