**Summary**

This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for matching text sequences, which is a fundamental problem in many natural language processing tasks. The model consists of four layers: preprocessing, attention, comparison, and aggregation. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions, including element-wise subtraction and multiplication, and demonstrate that these simple functions can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. The model is tested on four different datasets, including MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, and achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on all of them.

**Decision**

I decide to accept this paper, with some minor revisions.

**Reasons for acceptance**

1. The paper tackles a well-defined problem in natural language processing, and the proposed model is well-motivated and clearly explained.
2. The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of different comparison functions, which is a key component of the model, and provide insights into their strengths and weaknesses.
3. The model is tested on multiple datasets, which demonstrates its generalizability and robustness.

**Supporting arguments**

* The paper provides a clear and concise overview of the related work in sequence matching, which helps to situate the proposed model in the context of existing research.
* The authors provide a detailed description of the model architecture and the comparison functions, which makes it easy to follow and understand the paper.
* The evaluation results are presented in a clear and organized manner, which makes it easy to compare the performance of different comparison functions and models.

**Additional feedback**

* To further improve the paper, I suggest that the authors provide more qualitative analysis of the comparison functions, such as visualizations or examples of how they behave on different sentence types.
* The authors could also consider providing more details on the preprocessing layer, such as the motivation behind using gate values to control the importance of different words.
* In the related work section, the authors could provide more discussion on how their model differs from existing compare-aggregate models, such as the match-LSTM model.

**Questions for the authors**

* Can you provide more details on how you selected the hyperparameters for the model, such as the dimensionality of the hidden layers and the learning rate?
* How do you plan to extend the model to multi-task learning, as mentioned in the conclusion?
* Can you provide more examples of how the comparison functions behave on different sentence types, such as sentences with different lengths or structures?
