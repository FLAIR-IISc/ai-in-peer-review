**Summary of the Paper**

The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which is a crucial task in many natural language processing (NLP) problems. The framework consists of three components: a soft attention mechanism to align tokens, a comparison function to compare attention pairs, and a convolutional neural network (CNN) to aggregate the comparison results. The authors provide a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component, evaluating six different comparison functions on four datasets representing different tasks. The results show that the proposed framework achieves state-of-the-art performance on several tasks and that some simple comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions.

**Decision**

Based on the evaluation of the paper, I recommend acceptance. The paper makes sufficient contributions to the field of NLP, despite being borderline incremental relative to prior work. The authors provide a timely and relevant work that addresses an important NLP problem and yields good results.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Effective framework**: The proposed "compare-aggregate" framework is effective in matching text sequences, as demonstrated by the state-of-the-art performance on several tasks.
2. **Detailed empirical analysis**: The authors provide a detailed empirical analysis of the comparison component, evaluating six different comparison functions on four datasets. This analysis is valuable for understanding the strengths and weaknesses of different comparison functions.
3. **Simple yet effective comparison functions**: The results show that some simple comparison functions, such as element-wise subtraction and multiplication, can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. This finding is important for practitioners who want to choose the most effective comparison function for their specific task.

**Additional Feedback**

1. **Insensitivity to word order**: For longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), the model is insensitive to word order, which empirically limits its upper-bound performance. This limitation should be noted in the introduction and discussion.
2. **Attention mechanism**: The attention mechanism appears closer to Luong et al. (2015)'s bilinear/general scoring than to Bahdanau et al. The authors should cite Luong or a more relevant reference.
3. **Neural tensor network**: NTN risks overfitting due to many parameters. The authors may consider trying an llm tensor variant (input dim l, smaller output dim m) to reduce capacity.
4. **SubMultNN**: SubMultNN resembles the sentence-level matching strategy from Lili Mou et al. The authors should note this similarity.
5. **Preprocessing parameters**: The authors use the same preprocessing parameters for question and answer. However, these may require different weighting, and the authors should consider using separate preprocessing parameters for question and answer.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how you chose the hyperparameters for the CNN aggregation component?
2. How did you select the specific comparison functions to evaluate in your empirical analysis?
3. Can you provide more insights on why some simple comparison functions outperformed more complex neural network-based comparison functions?
4. Have you considered applying your framework to other NLP tasks, such as machine translation or text summarization?
5. Can you provide more details on how you plan to extend your work to multi-task learning, as mentioned in the conclusion?
