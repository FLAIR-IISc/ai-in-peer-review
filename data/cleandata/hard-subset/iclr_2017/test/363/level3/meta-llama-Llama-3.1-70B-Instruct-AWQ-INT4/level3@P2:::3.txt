**Summary of the Paper**

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching two sequences of text, which is a fundamental problem in natural language processing (NLP). The framework consists of a preprocessing layer, an attention mechanism, a comparison function, and a convolutional neural network (CNN) aggregation layer. The authors evaluate their model on four different datasets, including MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, and achieve state-of-the-art performance on three of them. They also conduct a systematic analysis of different comparison functions and find that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper. The authors tackle an important problem in NLP and propose a general framework that can be applied to different tasks. While the framework is not entirely new, the authors provide a thorough empirical analysis of the comparison component, which is a key contribution of the paper. The results are also promising, with the model achieving state-of-the-art performance on three out of four datasets.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a clear overview of the "compare-aggregate" framework and its applications in NLP. They also discuss the limitations of previous work and how their model addresses these limitations.

The empirical analysis of the comparison component is thorough and well-executed. The authors evaluate six different comparison functions, including neural network-based comparison functions, element-wise operations, and a combination of both. They also conduct an ablation study to evaluate the importance of the preprocessing layer and the attention mechanism.

The results are promising, with the model achieving state-of-the-art performance on three out of four datasets. The authors also provide a detailed analysis of the results, including visualizations of the attention weights and the convolutional layer outputs.

**Additional Feedback**

One limitation of the model is that it is insensitive to word order for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI). This is noted in the paper, but it would be helpful to discuss this limitation further in the introduction and discussion sections.

The attention mechanism used in the paper is similar to Luong et al.'s (2015) bilinear/general scoring mechanism. It would be helpful to cite Luong et al. or a more relevant reference.

The neural tensor network (NTN) comparison function risks overfitting due to its many parameters. It would be helpful to try an lllm tensor variant (input dim l, smaller output dim m) to reduce capacity.

The SubMultNN comparison function resembles the sentence-level matching strategy from Lili Mou et al. It would be helpful to note this similarity.

It is not clear why the same preprocessing parameters are used for the question and answer. These may require different weighting, and it would be helpful to discuss this further in the paper.

**Questions for the Authors**

Can you discuss further the limitation of the model being insensitive to word order for longer sequences? How do you think this limitation could be addressed in future work?

Can you provide more details on the preprocessing layer and why the same parameters are used for the question and answer?

Can you discuss further the relationship between the SubMultNN comparison function and the sentence-level matching strategy from Lili Mou et al.? How do you think these two approaches could be combined or extended in future work?
