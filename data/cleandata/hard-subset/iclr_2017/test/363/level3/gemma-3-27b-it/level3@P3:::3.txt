## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper proposes a general “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching tasks, focusing on a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions between word-level embeddings. The model utilizes attention mechanisms followed by CNN aggregation for final classification and is evaluated across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The authors demonstrate competitive performance and highlight the effectiveness of element-wise comparison functions, specifically SUB and MULT, offering valuable insights into the design of sequence matching models.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a solid empirical study of a relevant and well-established framework (compare-aggregate) and contributes useful findings regarding the choice of comparison function. While not a groundbreaking departure from existing work, the systematic evaluation and performance gains justify acceptance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles a fundamental problem in NLP: sequence matching. The “compare-aggregate” approach is widely used, and the authors correctly identify the comparison function as a key, yet underexplored, component. Their systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions—NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN—is a significant contribution. The empirical results across four datasets consistently show that the simpler element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex functions like NN and NTN, offering a practical and potentially more efficient solution. The ablation studies further support the importance of the chosen components. The paper is well-written and clearly presents the methodology and results. The code release is also a positive aspect.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

1. **Word Order Insensitivity:** The paper acknowledges a limitation regarding word order insensitivity for longer sequences, impacting performance. This is a crucial point and should be prominently mentioned in the introduction and discussion sections, not just as an observation. Quantifying the impact of word order (perhaps through ablation or analysis of specific error cases) would further strengthen the analysis.

2. **Attention Mechanism Citation:** The attention mechanism used is more closely aligned with Luong et al. (2015)'s bilinear/general scoring function, rather than Bahdanau et al. (2014). Citing Luong et al. or a more representative reference would provide more accurate contextualization.

3. **NTN Parameter Capacity:** The authors correctly point out the risk of overfitting with the NTN due to its large number of parameters. Exploring a lower-dimensional tensor variant (input dim *l*, smaller output dim *m*) to reduce capacity would be a valuable addition, demonstrating awareness of practical implementation concerns.

4. **Connection to Mou et al.:** The SUB and MULT comparison functions closely resemble the sentence-level matching strategy presented in Mou et al. (2016). Explicitly noting this connection in the related work section would provide a more complete picture of the existing literature.

5. **Preprocessing Parameter Consistency:** A key question is why the same preprocessing parameters are used for both the question and answer/hypothesis sequences. It’s plausible that these sequences benefit from different weighting or regularization. Justifying this design choice, or experimenting with sequence-specific preprocessing parameters, would be valuable. For example, questions may benefit from more aggressive stop word removal than answers. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the word order insensitivity for longer sequences. Could you elaborate on the types of errors the model makes due to this limitation? Are there specific patterns in the error cases that suggest potential mitigation strategies (e.g., incorporating positional encodings)?

2.  You mention the potential for further exploration of the preprocessing layer. Were any alternative preprocessing methods considered (e.g., character-level embeddings, subword tokenization) and why were they ultimately not included in the final model?

3. The element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform surprisingly well. Do you have any intuition as to *why* these simple functions are so effective? Is it due to their robustness to noise or their ability to capture specific semantic relationships?

4.  Given that the SNLI results are competitive but not state-of-the-art, do you have any hypotheses about why the model performs comparatively better on the question answering tasks than on the textual entailment task? Is it related to the nature of the comparison function or other aspects of the task?
