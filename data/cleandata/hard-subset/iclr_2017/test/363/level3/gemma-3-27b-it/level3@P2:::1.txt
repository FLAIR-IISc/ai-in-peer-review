## Review for ICLR 2017 Submission: A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences, a common requirement in tasks such as machine comprehension, question answering, and textual entailment. The authors build upon existing compare-aggregate models by systematically evaluating six different comparison functions – including standard neural networks, neural tensor networks, Euclidean distance with cosine similarity, and, notably, element-wise subtraction and multiplication – within a consistent architecture. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their model across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), finding that the element-wise operations consistently perform well, often outperforming more complex methods. The code is publicly available, fostering reproducibility.

**2. Decision**

**Reject**. While the work is solid and the experiments are well-executed, the contribution feels incremental and doesn't represent a significant advance to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core idea of exploring different comparison functions within a compare-aggregate framework is reasonable, but the findings, while interesting, lack a strong theoretical justification or surprising novelty.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper successfully demonstrates that a simple compare-aggregate framework, using readily available components, can achieve competitive results on several standard benchmarks. The empirical evaluation is thorough, covering a diverse set of datasets. The finding that element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex comparison methods (like neural networks and tensor networks) is noteworthy. However, the *why* behind this observation remains largely unexplored. The authors posit that these functions better capture semantic similarity, but this is not rigorously demonstrated.

The paper builds upon a well-established line of work in sequence matching. While it provides a valuable empirical comparison, it doesn’t introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic approach or theoretical insight. The incremental nature of the contribution, coupled with the lack of strong justification for the observed performance differences, leads to the rejection decision. 

The introduction adequately contextualizes the work and clearly states the problem and contributions. The method section is well-organized and explains the architecture in detail. The experimental setup is described clearly, including the baselines used for comparison. The analysis of results is reasonable, and the ablation studies provide some insight into the importance of different components.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The following points are offered to help improve the paper, and are not directly influencing the rejection decision:

*   **Theoretical Motivation:** Elaborate on the potential reasons why element-wise operations work well. Is there a connection to feature independence or other properties of word embeddings that might explain this phenomenon? Investigating this further would elevate the paper beyond a purely empirical study.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Including a qualitative analysis of the comparison vectors produced by different comparison functions (e.g., NN, SUB, MULT) on a diverse set of sentence pairs would be highly valuable. Visualizing these vectors, or providing examples of where each comparison function succeeds and fails, would provide more intuition into their behavior. The paper mentions examples in the Results section, but they are limited to Table 1 and do not provide a detailed analysis.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the element-wise functions, could be beneficial. Are these functions more or less sensitive to learning rate, embedding dimensionality, or other hyperparameters?
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** While the ablation study is present, expanding it to include variations in the attention mechanism or CNN architecture could provide further insights into the contribution of each component.
*   **Connection to Existing Literature:** The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of related work, specifically highlighting the trade-offs between different comparison functions in other areas of machine learning (e.g., metric learning).

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind why element-wise subtraction and multiplication might be effective for capturing semantic similarity between word embeddings? Do you have any hypotheses about the properties of the word embeddings themselves that contribute to this performance?
2.  Could you provide examples of sentence pairs where each of the comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT) performs particularly well or poorly? Showing these examples would help us understand the strengths and weaknesses of each approach.
3.  Did you observe any significant differences in performance between the different datasets when using different comparison functions? If so, can you explain these differences based on the characteristics of the datasets?
4.  The paper focuses on comparing word-level embeddings. Have you considered applying these comparison functions at a sentence or phrase level, perhaps by pooling word embeddings? Could this potentially improve performance?
5.  What computational cost differences did you observe between the different comparison functions? Are the simpler functions (SUB, MULT) significantly faster to compute than the more complex ones (NN, NTN)?
