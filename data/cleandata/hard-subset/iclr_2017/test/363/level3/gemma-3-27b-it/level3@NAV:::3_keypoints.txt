- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework focusing on the impact of different comparison functions, evaluated on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Finds element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform standard neural networks and neural tensor networks.  
- Well-motivated extension of prior sequence-matching work, filling a gap in systematic comparison-function evaluation.  
- Strong, consistent empirical results across diverse tasks; ablation study supports component contributions; code released for reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept — contribution is somewhat incremental but the empirical analysis is valuable.  
- Limitation: Model shows insensitivity to word order for longer sequences; should be highlighted and addressed (e.g., positional embeddings).  
- Attention citation issue: the described attention is closer to Luong et al. (2015) bilinear scoring and should be cited accordingly.  
- NTN concern: large parameter count risks overfitting; consider lower-dimensional tensor variants.  
- Related-work note: SubMultNN resembles Lili Mou et al. (2016) sentence-level matching and should be acknowledged.  
- Preprocessing concern: same preprocessing parameters used for question and answer/hypothesis is unexplained; different preprocessing may be appropriate.  
- Question: Why do element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) perform so well compared to more complex neural methods?  
- Question: Have any strategies been tried to mitigate word-order insensitivity, and what were the results?  
- Question: Were preprocessing parameters or architectures tuned separately for question vs answer/hypothesis, and did that help?
