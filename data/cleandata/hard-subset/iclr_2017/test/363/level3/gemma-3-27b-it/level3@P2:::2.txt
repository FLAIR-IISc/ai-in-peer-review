## Review for ICLR 2017

This paper presents a compare-aggregate model for matching text sequences, focusing on a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions within this framework. The authors evaluate their model on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and demonstrate competitive, often state-of-the-art, performance.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper addresses a relevant problem in NLP, proposes a well-motivated approach, and supports its claims with thorough experimentation across diverse datasets. The systematic comparison of comparison functions is a significant contribution, revealing that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex methods.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly tackles the important problem of sequence matching, a core task in numerous NLP applications. The compare-aggregate framework is well-established (as the authors acknowledge), but this work distinguishes itself through a focused investigation of the crucial comparison function component. This is a valuable contribution, as previous studies have largely overlooked this aspect, often relying on standard neural network layers. 

The motivation for the study is strong. The authors correctly identify the limitations of previous work—namely, a lack of broad evaluation across tasks and insufficient attention to the comparison function itself. They hypothesize (and demonstrate) that the general framework is effective and that thoughtful design of the comparison function can lead to improved performance.

The experimental setup is comprehensive. The authors evaluate their model on four distinct datasets, each presenting unique challenges. They compare against relevant baselines, and the results consistently show that their approach achieves strong performance, often exceeding the state-of-the-art, particularly on InsuranceQA and WikiQA. The ablation study, while brief, provides some insight into the contribution of different components. The inclusion of code further strengthens the reproducibility of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is generally well-written, a few minor points could be improved. As noted, there is a minor typo on page 4 (“including a some”). Figure 2 is difficult to interpret, especially in black and white print. Increasing the font size, using clearer color schemes, and providing a more detailed caption would enhance its readability and impact. 

I am curious about the contribution of the preprocessing and attention layers. The authors mention their importance in the analysis of results but haven't provided detailed ablation results quantifying this. While the results demonstrate strong performance *with* these layers, it would be valuable to see results *without* them, to better understand the extent to which they contribute to the overall performance, and to justify their inclusion in the model architecture. Specifically, what is the performance drop when the attention mechanism is removed? Does removing the preprocessing layer significantly impact performance, and does this differ across the datasets?  

Furthermore, it would be useful to discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions. While the authors demonstrate that SUB and MULT perform well, are they significantly more efficient than NN or NTN? Understanding the trade-offs between performance and computational complexity would be beneficial.

Finally, could the authors elaborate on the choice of GloVe embeddings? Were other pre-trained embeddings considered, and if so, how did they affect the results?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Could you provide results for the model *without* the preprocessing and attention layers? This would help to quantify their contribution to the overall performance and justify their inclusion.
2. Can you comment on the computational cost (training time, inference time) of the different comparison functions? Are SUB and MULT significantly more efficient than NN or NTN?
3. Were other pre-trained word embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText) considered? If so, how did the results compare when using these different embeddings?
4. Could you clarify the specific parameters used for CNN aggregation (e.g. filter sizes, number of filters) and explain the rationale behind these choices?
