- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching and systematically evaluates different comparison functions.
- Decision: Accept.
- Demonstrates strong performance across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex methods like NTNs.
- Emphasizes that focusing on semantic similarity via element-wise ops is effective for word-level comparison.
- Provides cross-task validation, suggesting the framework’s general applicability to QA and entailment.
- Includes ablation studies and attention-weight visualizations to support analysis.
- Positions work within prior literature on sequence matching, attention, and compare-aggregate models.
- Experimental setup uses established datasets, strong baselines, clear reporting, and hyperparameters for reproducibility.
- Suggestion: discuss and address preprocessing layer’s insensitivity to word order for long sequences.
- Suggestion: cite Luong et al. (2015) or similar for the attention mechanism rather than Bahdanau et al. (2014).
- Suggestion: reduce NTN parameter count (e.g., lower-dimensional tensor) to avoid overfitting.
- Suggestion: acknowledge similarity between SUBMULT+NN and Mou et al. (2016) in related work.
- Suggestion: justify or explore differing preprocessing parameters for questions/premises vs answers/hypotheses.
- Suggestion: expand and detail ablation study results in the paper.
- Question: why is attention more crucial for tasks with large sequence-length differences while preprocessing matters more for smaller differences?
- Question: how would the model be adapted for multi-task learning and what challenges are expected?
- Question: why do SUB and MULT perform so well compared to complex methods—just semantic similarity or other reasons?
- Question: request specifics on ablation study metrics and the impact of removing preprocessing, attention, and individual comparison functions.
