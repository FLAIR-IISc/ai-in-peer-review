This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, focusing on a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions within this framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results. The key contribution lies in showing that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex approaches like neural networks and neural tensor networks.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses an important problem in NLP – sequence matching – with a well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated approach. The consistent performance gains achieved with the surprisingly effective element-wise comparison functions are a significant finding. While perhaps not revolutionary, the work provides valuable insights into the design of sequence matching models and offers a strong baseline for future research.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of sequence matching and its prevalence in various NLP tasks. The “compare-aggregate” framework is well-established, and the authors rightly identify the comparison function as a critical component deserving further investigation. The systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions, including the novel SUB and MULT, is a key strength. 

The experimental results are compelling. The model consistently performs well across all four datasets, and the superior performance of SUB and MULT compared to more complex functions is a notable observation. The ablation studies, while not extensive, provide some insight into the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. The authors also thoughtfully analyze the results, noting the impact of word order sensitivity for longer sequences, and the effectiveness of different layers across different tasks.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with clear explanations of the model architecture and experimental setup. The code availability is also a positive aspect, fostering reproducibility and further research. The related work section is comprehensive, placing the work in context with existing literature.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, here are some suggestions for improvement:

* **Word Order Insensitivity:** The observation regarding word order insensitivity for longer sequences is crucial. This limitation should be more prominently discussed in the introduction and conclusion. Perhaps exploring ways to incorporate word order information (e.g., using RNNs, positional embeddings) could be suggested as future work.
* **Attention Mechanism Reference:** The attention mechanism described is more aligned with Luong et al. (2015) than Bahdanau et al. (2014). Citing Luong or a more relevant reference would improve the accuracy of the related work section.
* **NTN Complexity:** The use of a full neural tensor network (NTN) might be overfitting, given the relatively limited data sizes. Consider experimenting with a lower-rank tensor (smaller output dimension *m* instead of *l*) to reduce the number of parameters.
* **Related Work – Mou et al.:** The SubMultNN comparison function bears a strong resemblance to the sentence-level matching strategy proposed by Mou et al. (2016). Explicitly acknowledging this connection would enhance the related work section.
* **Preprocessing Parameters:** The use of identical preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer sequences seems arbitrary. Investigating task-specific parameter tuning for the preprocessing layer might yield further performance improvements.
* **Visualization Detail:** The visualizations in Figure 2 are helpful, but could benefit from more detailed explanations. Specifying *which* dimensions of the convolutional layer are being visualized would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale for choosing the specific parameters used in the preprocessing layer (Equation 1) and why these parameters were kept constant across different tasks?
2.  Given the observation about word order insensitivity, have you considered any initial experiments with incorporating word order information (e.g., positional embeddings, RNNs) into the model, even if preliminary?
3.  Could you provide more details regarding the computational cost (training time, memory usage) of the different comparison functions, especially the NTN, given its potentially large parameter space?
4.  The paper focuses on word-level comparison.  Are there scenarios where a character-level comparison might be beneficial, and if so, how could this be integrated into the proposed framework?
