## Review for ICLR 2017 - A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences, focusing on a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions within this framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model on four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – achieving strong performance and highlighting the efficacy of element-wise operations as comparison functions.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper addresses an important problem in NLP – sequence matching – and offers a practical and well-supported contribution. While the compare-aggregate framework itself isn’t novel, the thorough analysis of different comparison functions and the consistent strong performance across multiple datasets justify acceptance. The paper is well-written and clearly presents its methodology and results. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core strength of this work lies in its empirical evaluation. The authors demonstrate that a seemingly simple approach – element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) – can outperform more complex methods like neural tensor networks (NTN) for the word-level comparison step. This is a significant finding, suggesting that focusing on semantic similarity, rather than solely relying on the representational power of complex networks, can be highly effective. The consistent performance across the four datasets (question answering and textual entailment) lends credence to the claim that the “compare-aggregate” framework is generally applicable to sequence matching problems.  The inclusion of ablation studies (mentioned in the paper, though not fully detailed in the review copy) and visualization of attention weights further strengthens the analysis.

The paper successfully positions itself within the existing literature. It acknowledges prior work on sequence matching, attention mechanisms, and compare-aggregate models, and clearly articulates how its contribution builds upon these foundations. The authors appropriately highlight the limitations of previous studies – the lack of cross-task validation and the insufficient attention given to the comparison function – and directly address these gaps in their work.

The experimental setup is robust. The use of established datasets and the comparison against strong baselines provide a fair assessment of the proposed model. The clear reporting of results and the inclusion of hyperparameters contribute to the reproducibility of the work.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

The following points are offered as suggestions for improvement and do not necessarily impact the acceptance decision.

* **Word Order Insensitivity:** The paper notes that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for longer sequences. This is a potential limitation that should be explicitly discussed in the introduction and conclusion. Exploring ways to incorporate word order information, even if it slightly increases complexity, could lead to further performance gains.
* **Attention Mechanism Reference:** The attention mechanism described appears more aligned with Luong et al. (2015)'s work on bilinear/general attention scoring than with Bahdanau et al. (2014).  Consider citing Luong et al. or a more relevant reference for the attention mechanism.
* **NTN Parameter Reduction:** The neural tensor network (NTN) has a large number of parameters, which could lead to overfitting. Exploring a lower-dimensional tensor variant (e.g., projecting to a smaller output dimension 'm') could mitigate this risk and potentially improve generalization.
* **Related Work - Mou et al.:** The SUBMULT+NN comparison function shares similarities with the sentence-level matching strategy proposed by Lili Mou et al. (2016). Acknowledging this connection in the related work section would provide a more complete contextualization of the contribution.
* **Preprocessing Parameters:**  The use of identical preprocessing parameters for both the question/premise and the answer/hypothesis might not be optimal. Justify the choice of using the same parameters, or consider exploring task-specific or sequence-specific parameter tuning.
* **Ablation Study Details:** While mentioned, the ablation study’s results are not fully detailed in the review copy. Expanding on this aspect would be valuable, clearly demonstrating the contribution of each component of the model.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper notes that the attention layer is more crucial for tasks with larger sequence length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) and the preprocessing layer for those with smaller differences (WikiQA, SNLI). Can you elaborate on *why* this is the case?  Is it related to the increased importance of contextual information in longer sequences?
2.  You mention the potential for future work on multi-task learning. How do you envision adapting the model to handle multiple tasks simultaneously, and what challenges do you anticipate?
3.  Regarding the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT), do you have any intuition as to *why* they perform so well compared to more complex methods? Is it simply a reflection of the importance of measuring semantic similarity directly, or are there other contributing factors?
4.  Could you elaborate on the specifics of the ablation studies? What metrics were used to evaluate the impact of removing the preprocessing layer, attention layer, and individual comparison functions?
