- Decision: Accept.  
- Main contribution: Systematic evaluation of comparison functions within a "compare-aggregate" sequence matching framework.  
- Key finding: Simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex neural/NTN approaches.  
- Empirical support: Competitive or state-of-the-art results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Strength: Thorough, well-motivated experiments and clear model/experimental descriptions.  
- Strength: Code release improves reproducibility.  
- Strength: Ablation studies and analysis identify role of preprocessing and attention layers.  
- Limitation: Word order insensitivity for longer sequences; needs more prominent discussion and mitigation strategies.  
- Suggestion: Explore incorporating word order (RNNs, positional embeddings) as future work.  
- Related-work correction: Attention description aligns more with Luong et al. (2015) than Bahdanau et al. (2014); cite accordingly.  
- Related-work note: SubMultNN resembles Mou et al. (2016); explicitly acknowledge this connection.  
- Model complexity concern: Full NTN may overfit on limited data; consider lower-rank/smaller-output NTNs to reduce parameters.  
- Preprocessing concern: Using identical preprocessing parameters for question and answer seems arbitrary; task-specific tuning may help.  
- Visualization feedback: Figure 2 visualizations need clearer explanations, including which convolution dimensions are shown.  
- Questions for authors: Rationale for fixed preprocessing parameters across tasks?  
- Questions for authors: Any preliminary experiments incorporating word order (positional embeddings, RNNs)?  
- Questions for authors: Computational cost and memory usage of different comparison functions, especially NTN?  
- Questions for authors: Potential for character-level comparison and how it could integrate into the framework?
