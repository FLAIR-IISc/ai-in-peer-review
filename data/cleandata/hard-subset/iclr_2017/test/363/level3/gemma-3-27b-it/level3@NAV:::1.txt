## Review of “A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES”

This paper proposes a compare-aggregate model for sequence matching tasks, leveraging convolutional neural networks to aggregate word-level comparisons made after applying attention over LSTM outputs. The core contribution lies in a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions, finding that element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally perform best across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The work builds upon the existing compare-aggregate framework but offers valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function itself.

**Reject.** While the paper is well-written and the experiments are reasonably thorough, the contribution feels incremental. The core idea of exploring different comparison functions within the compare-aggregate framework is sensible, but the novelty is limited. The observed superiority of subtraction and multiplication, while interesting, doesn’t represent a substantial breakthrough and feels more like an empirical observation than a theoretically driven advance. 

The paper adequately addresses the question of which comparison functions perform best within the given framework for a range of sequence matching tasks. The approach is well-motivated, situated within the existing literature on sequence matching and compare-aggregate models. The experimental setup appears sound, with comparison to established baselines on standard datasets. The results are presented clearly and generally support the claims made, demonstrating improved performance with subtraction and multiplication. However, the improvements aren’t substantial enough to warrant acceptance given the incremental nature of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

To strengthen the paper, I recommend including a qualitative analysis. Show examples of how subtraction, multiplication, and other comparison functions behave on varied sentence types, particularly cases where they succeed or fail. Visualizing the resulting comparison vectors would be very helpful. Furthermore, a discussion of *why* subtraction and multiplication work well would be beneficial. Is it related to capturing semantic differences or specific types of relationships between words? Adding a more theoretical justification, even speculative, could elevate the work. 

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that preprocessing is order-insensitive, yet demonstrates good performance on tasks where word order is important. Could you elaborate on why this seems to be the case? Does the CNN layer effectively capture some of the lost order information?
2.  You found SUB and MULT to be generally effective. Have you explored combining these functions in more complex ways, potentially with learned weights, beyond the SUBMULT+NN approach?
3.  The ablation study shows the importance of the attention and preprocessing layers. Could you provide more details on the specific configurations and hyperparameters used for these layers? How sensitive are the results to changes in these parameters?
