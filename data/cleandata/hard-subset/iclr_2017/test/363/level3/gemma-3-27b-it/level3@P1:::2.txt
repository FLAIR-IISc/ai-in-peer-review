## Review of “A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES”

This paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching, focusing on a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions at the word level. The framework involves comparing word embeddings, followed by aggregation using Convolutional Neural Networks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model across four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results. The thorough empirical evaluation and clear presentation make this a valuable contribution to the field.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper tackles a relevant and important problem in NLP – sequence matching – and presents a well-motivated approach. The systematic comparison of different comparison functions is a particularly strong point, revealing that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based methods. The experimental results convincingly support the claims, demonstrating strong performance across a variety of datasets.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work stems from the observed limitations of existing sequence matching models. While “compare-aggregate” frameworks have gained traction, prior studies often focused on task-specific models without systematically exploring the impact of the comparison function. This paper directly addresses this gap, providing a comprehensive analysis of six different comparison functions. The choice of datasets is also commendable, covering question answering and textual entailment tasks, demonstrating the general applicability of the proposed framework. 

The experimental setup is robust, including comparisons to strong baselines established in the literature for each dataset.  The reported results consistently demonstrate that the proposed model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance. Notably, the finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform well is counterintuitive and insightful, suggesting that carefully chosen simple functions can be highly effective for this task. The ablation studies (presented in Table 4) further support these conclusions.

The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The explanations of the different comparison functions are clear, and the description of the model architecture is detailed enough for replication.  The inclusion of code further enhances the reproducibility of the results.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong overall, a few minor improvements could further enhance its impact.

*   **Preprocessing & Attention Importance:** The paper mentions the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers through experimentation, but it would be valuable to *quantitatively* demonstrate their contribution.  Specifically, including results achieved *without* these layers would provide a clearer understanding of their impact on performance.  This would help establish a baseline and reinforce the rationale for their inclusion in the model.
*   **Figure 2 Clarity:** The visualizations in Figure 2 are difficult to interpret, particularly in printed form. Increasing the font size and using clearer visual cues to highlight the relevant activations would improve the readability and impact of this analysis.
*   **Discussion of Negative Results:** While the paper highlights the success of SUB and MULT, briefly discussing *why* the more complex neural network-based comparison functions performed relatively poorly could add valuable insight. Was it due to overfitting, gradient vanishing, or some other factor?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the different comparison functions, could be beneficial. Were certain functions more sensitive to learning rate or other hyperparameters?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide results for the experiments conducted without the preprocessing and attention layers? This would help to quantify their contribution to the overall performance.
2.  What considerations led to the selection of a CNN layer for aggregation, rather than other potential aggregation methods (e.g., attention mechanisms, recurrent networks)?
3.  The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Did you explore order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., using LSTMs/GRUs in the preprocessing step) and if so, how did their performance compare?
4.  Regarding the negative results with the NN and NTN comparison functions, do you have any hypotheses as to why they didn’t perform as well as the simpler element-wise operations? Were there any indications of overfitting or other issues during training?
