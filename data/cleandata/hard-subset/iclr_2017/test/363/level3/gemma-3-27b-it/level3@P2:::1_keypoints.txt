- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework evaluating six comparison functions across four datasets; code is publicly available.  
- Decision: Reject — solid experiments but contribution is incremental and lacks significant theoretical novelty.  
- Empirical finding: element-wise subtraction and multiplication consistently perform well, often outperforming more complex methods (NN, NTN).  
- Experiments are thorough with diverse datasets, clear setup, baselines, and ablation studies.  
- Major weakness: no rigorous theoretical explanation for why element-wise operations work better.  
- Paper builds on existing sequence-matching work and does not introduce a fundamentally new algorithmic approach.  
- Recommendation: provide stronger theoretical motivation linking SUB/MULT to embedding properties or feature independence.  
- Recommendation: include qualitative analyses/visualizations of comparison vectors and illustrative success/failure examples.  
- Recommendation: perform deeper hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, especially for element-wise functions.  
- Recommendation: expand ablation studies to test variations in attention mechanisms and CNN architectures.  
- Recommendation: better situate results within related work, discussing trade-offs of comparison functions (e.g., metric learning).  
- Question: what is the intuition/hypothesis for why element-wise subtraction and multiplication capture semantic similarity?  
- Question: can authors provide example sentence pairs where each comparison function (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT) excels or fails?  
- Question: were there dataset-specific differences in performance across comparison functions, and can these be explained?  
- Question: have the authors considered applying comparison functions at sentence/phrase level (pooled embeddings)?  
- Question: what are the computational cost differences between simpler functions (SUB, MULT) and more complex ones (NN, NTN)?
