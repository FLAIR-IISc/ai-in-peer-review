- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate sequence matching framework with attention + CNN aggregation and evaluates different word-level comparison functions.  
- Evaluated on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and shows competitive performance; code released.  
- Systematic comparison of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) is a core contribution.  
- Empirical finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex functions (NN, NTN).  
- Ablation studies support the importance of chosen components in the model.  
- Decision: Accept — solid empirical study and useful practical insights despite not being a major theoretical advance.  
- Limitation: model shows word order insensitivity for longer sequences; this should be highlighted and quantified.  
- Suggest correcting attention citation to Luong et al. (2015) rather than Bahdanau et al. (2014).  
- NTN has large parameter capacity and risk of overfitting; recommend exploring lower-dimensional tensor variants.  
- Note that SUB and MULT are similar to Mou et al. (2016) and this relation should be acknowledged.  
- Question design choice: why use identical preprocessing parameters for questions and answers/hypotheses; sequence-specific preprocessing might help.  
- Ask authors to analyze error types caused by word order insensitivity and potential fixes (e.g., positional encodings).  
- Request details on whether alternative preprocessing methods (character/subword embeddings) were considered.  
- Ask for intuition why SUB and MULT are so effective (robustness to noise or semantic capture).  
- Query why model is relatively stronger on QA tasks than on SNLI and whether task nature or comparison function explains this.
