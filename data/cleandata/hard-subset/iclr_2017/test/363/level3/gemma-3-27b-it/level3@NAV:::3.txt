## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences, focusing on the impact of different comparison functions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach across four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – and find that element-wise comparison functions outperform standard neural networks and neural tensor networks in many cases. The work is well-motivated, building upon prior research in sequence matching and identifying a gap in systematic comparison function evaluation.

**Decision: Accept** 

I recommend acceptance. While the contribution is somewhat incremental, the comprehensive empirical analysis of comparison functions is valuable and the model achieves strong results across multiple tasks. The paper addresses an important problem in NLP and offers practical insights into building effective sequence matching models.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the problem of comparing text sequences for tasks like question answering and textual entailment. The compare-aggregate framework is a well-established approach, and this work expands upon it with a detailed investigation into the comparison component – a crucial but often overlooked aspect. The empirical results are strong and consistent, showing the proposed model’s competitive performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods on diverse datasets. The ablation study (Table 4) further validates the contributions of individual components of the model. The code release is also a positive aspect, promoting reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The authors acknowledge the model’s insensitivity to word order for longer sequences, limiting performance. This should be explicitly discussed in the introduction and conclusion as a key limitation. Exploring methods to incorporate word order information (e.g., positional embeddings) could be a direction for future work.
*   **Attention Mechanism Citation:** The attention mechanism described is more aligned with Luong et al. (2015)’s bilinear/general scoring function, rather than Bahdanau et al. (2014).  The paper should cite Luong et al. or another more relevant reference.
*   **NTN Parameter Reduction:**  The authors acknowledge the risk of overfitting in the NTN due to its large number of parameters.  Exploring a lower-dimensional tensor variant (input dim *l*, smaller output dim *m*) could be beneficial.
*   **SubMultNN Similarity:** The SubMultNN comparison function resembles the sentence-level matching strategy proposed by Lili Mou et al. (2016). This similarity should be noted in the related work section.
*   **Preprocessing Parameters:** The authors use the same preprocessing parameters for both question and answer/hypothesis. It’s unclear why this is the case. Different weighting or pre-processing steps might be more appropriate for each sequence, given their distinct roles.



**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on why the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) perform so well, particularly in contrast to the more complex neural network-based methods? Is there a specific characteristic of the datasets that makes these simpler functions more effective?
2.  You mention the word order insensitivity as a limitation. Have you explored any initial strategies (even simple ones) to address this, and if so, what were the results?
3.  Regarding the preprocessing layer, did you experiment with different parameters or architectures tailored to the question and answer/hypothesis sequences independently? If so, did that yield any improvements?
