## Review for ICLR 2017 – A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

This paper investigates a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, a common subproblem in many NLP tasks like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The authors systematically explore different comparison functions within this framework, focusing on element-wise operations and demonstrating their effectiveness across four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The core contribution lies in the empirical finding that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex comparison methods like neural networks or neural tensor networks.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation, the contributions are incremental and do not represent a significant advancement warranting acceptance to ICLR. The core idea of the compare-aggregate framework is well-established, and the main contribution – the comparison of different comparison functions – lacks sufficient novelty to justify publication at a top-tier conference. The performance gains achieved with the proposed methods are moderate and do not demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over existing state-of-the-art approaches.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper adequately summarizes existing approaches to sequence matching, placing the work within the context of the “compare-aggregate” paradigm. The experimental setup is well-defined, utilizing four publicly available datasets covering a range of tasks. The presentation of results is clear and includes comparisons to baseline models. However, the key findings – that element-wise operations perform competitively – feel somewhat intuitive. While the systematic evaluation is commendable, the lack of a strong theoretical justification for *why* these simple operations work well diminishes the impact of the empirical results. 

The claim that the model achieves "state-of-the-art" performance needs careful scrutiny.  While the authors report competitive numbers, the specific baselines used vary across datasets and aren't always the most recent or strongest available. For instance, the SNLI results don’t directly compare to the very latest models. The improvements on other datasets are also modest.

Furthermore, the ablation studies, while present, are relatively limited. It would be helpful to see a more granular analysis of the contribution of individual components, beyond simply removing the preprocessing or attention layers.  Finally, the paper feels somewhat descriptive rather than providing deep insight into the underlying mechanisms driving the observed performance.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

The paper could be strengthened by addressing the following points:

*   **Theoretical Motivation:** Provide a more compelling explanation for why element-wise subtraction and multiplication are effective comparison functions. Is there a connection to semantic similarity metrics or underlying representations?
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Include a qualitative analysis of the comparison vectors produced by different functions. Visualizing these vectors for specific examples could provide valuable intuition into *how* the different methods capture semantic relationships.  Specifically, how do the subtraction and multiplication functions behave on examples with different types of semantic relationships (synonymy, antonymy, entailment, contradiction)?
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Ensure a fairer comparison to state-of-the-art baselines. Consider including more recent and competitive models, particularly for SNLI, and clearly state which baselines were re-implemented versus taken from published results.
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** Expand the ablation studies to investigate the impact of different embedding sizes, attention mechanisms, and CNN configurations.
*   **Error Analysis**: Include a discussion of error cases. Understanding *where* the model fails could provide insights for future improvements.
*   **Discussion of Limitations**: Explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the work, such as the potential sensitivity to hyperparameter tuning and the limited exploration of more complex comparison functions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed discussion of why you believe element-wise subtraction and multiplication are particularly effective comparison functions?  Are there any connections to existing theories of semantic similarity or representation learning?
2.  Could you provide examples of comparison vectors (e.g., the output of the subtraction or multiplication function) for a few representative examples from the datasets?  This would help illustrate how these functions capture semantic relationships.
3.  What effort was made to ensure the baselines used in your experiments are competitive with current state-of-the-art models, particularly for the SNLI dataset?
4.  Were any attempts made to combine the element-wise operations with more complex comparison functions (e.g., using an element-wise operation as input to a neural network)? If so, what were the results?
5.  Do you observe any performance differences between the datasets depending on the average sentence length? And if so, can you explain why this might be the case?
