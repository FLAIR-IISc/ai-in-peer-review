## Review of “A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES”

**1. Summary**

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching, a crucial task in NLP encompassing areas like machine comprehension and textual entailment. The authors investigate different comparison functions within this framework, ultimately demonstrating that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex methods like neural networks and neural tensor networks. The model is evaluated on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results. The paper is well-written, clearly outlining the methodology and presenting comprehensive experimental results. The contribution lies in the systematic evaluation of comparison functions and the demonstration of the effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework across various sequence matching tasks.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** The paper addresses an important problem in NLP with a well-motivated and thoroughly evaluated approach. The results are compelling and demonstrate a valuable insight – that simpler comparison functions can be surprisingly effective.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper successfully tackles the problem of sequence matching, a foundational task in NLP. The motivation for exploring different comparison functions within the compare-aggregate framework is strong, as previous work often overlooked this crucial component. The approach is well-grounded in existing literature, building upon established frameworks like match-LSTM and decomposable attention, while offering a novel contribution through its systematic comparison of different comparison functions. 

The empirical support for the claims is robust. The authors evaluate their model on four distinct datasets, covering question answering and textual entailment, demonstrating its generalizability. The comparisons to published baselines are comprehensive, and the reported results consistently show competitive or state-of-the-art performance, particularly on the InsuranceQA dataset. The ablation studies (Table 4) further strengthen the findings, highlighting the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. The inclusion of code further supports reproducibility.

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong overall, several minor points could be addressed to improve clarity and impact:

*   **Figure 2:** The visualization of the CNN layer activations is difficult to interpret, especially in print as the authors acknowledge. Increasing the contrast, labeling axes more clearly, or presenting the data in a different format (e.g., heatmap) would enhance readability.
*   **Preprocessing & Attention Clarification:** The reviewer’s request for clarification on the benefit of the preprocessing and attention steps is valid. While the ablation study addresses this, a more detailed discussion of *why* these components are beneficial, especially in relation to the chosen comparison functions, would be helpful. Providing results without these components, as requested, is essential.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings (e.g., embedding dimension, CNN filter sizes) would increase confidence in the robustness of the findings.
*   **Typo:** As noted, "including a some" on page 4, line 5 should be corrected to "including some".

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide results for all four datasets *without* the preprocessing and attention layers? This would directly address the reviewer’s concern and allow for a clearer understanding of their individual contributions.
2.  Regarding the success of the SUB and MULT comparison functions, do you have any theoretical insights into *why* these element-wise operations perform so well, especially in comparison to more complex neural network approaches? Is it related to preserving more dimension-specific information, as suggested in the paper?
3.  The paper states the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for MovieQA, InsuranceQA, while order-sensitive approaches exist. Could you elaborate on whether exploring order-sensitive preprocessing would be a valuable avenue for future research, potentially enhancing performance on datasets where word order is critical?
4.  Could you provide more details on the specific hyperparameter tuning process? What range of values were explored for key parameters, and how were the optimal values selected for each dataset?
