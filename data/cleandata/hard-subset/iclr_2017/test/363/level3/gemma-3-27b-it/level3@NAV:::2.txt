## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching, exploring various word-level comparison functions followed by CNN-based aggregation. The authors evaluate their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and demonstrate competitive, and often superior, performance compared to existing baselines. The work is well-motivated, addressing a key component of sequence matching – the comparison function – which is often overlooked. The extensive experimental evaluation is a significant strength, showcasing the generalizability of the proposed framework. The paper is generally well-written and clearly articulates its contributions. I did notice a minor typo on page 4, line 5: “including a some” should be “including some.”

**Decision: Accept**

My primary reason for acceptance is the solid empirical evidence supporting the claims. The extensive experiments across four datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework and, importantly, highlight the surprisingly strong performance of simple, element-wise comparison functions. This challenges the reliance on more complex neural network-based approaches for this task. The second key reason is the practical relevance of the work; a better understanding of effective comparison functions contributes to improved performance in a range of NLP applications.

The paper convincingly answers the three key questions. Firstly, it addresses the problem of effective sequence matching, specifically focusing on the word-level comparison step. Secondly, the approach is well-motivated within the existing literature on compare-aggregate models, building on prior work (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016) but crucially extending it with a systematic evaluation of comparison functions. Finally, the paper supports its claims with rigorous empirical results, demonstrating statistically significant improvements over existing baselines on multiple datasets.

**Additional Feedback:**

The preprocessing and attention steps, while mentioned, aren't fully justified in terms of their benefit. The authors mention order-insensitivity of the preprocessing layer, but the contribution of these steps beyond potentially normalizing input is unclear. **Could the authors provide a more detailed explanation of the intended benefits of the preprocessing and attention layers? Furthermore, could they present results *without* these layers to quantify their impact on performance?** Figure 2 is difficult to interpret, particularly in printed form. The visual representation of the CNN activation is blurry and lacks sufficient clarity to draw meaningful conclusions. **Improving the resolution and clarity of Figure 2 is crucial for enhancing the paper’s impact.** 

Finally, while the ablation study (Table 4) is helpful, it would be beneficial to see an ablation study specifically focused on the individual components of the SUBMULT+NN comparison function (SUB and MULT) to better understand their relative contributions.
