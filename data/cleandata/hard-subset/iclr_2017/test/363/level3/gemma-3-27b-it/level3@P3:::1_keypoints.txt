- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate model using word-level comparison functions followed by CNN aggregation for sequence matching (tested on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Main contribution is a systematic empirical comparison of different comparison functions, finding element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally perform best.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary reason: incremental work with limited novelty; refines existing paradigms rather than introducing a new one.  
- Empirical evaluation across four datasets is thorough and shows complex functions (e.g., neural tensor networks) are not necessarily superior.  
- The paper fails to deeply explore why subtraction and multiplication work well; connections to semantic similarity are superficial.  
- Ablation studies exist but don’t explain *why* components have differing impacts across scenarios.  
- Heavy dependence on prior work (Wang & Jiang, He & Lin, Parikh et al.); improvements are measurable but not paradigm-shifting.  
- The claim of demonstrating “general effectiveness” of the framework is overstated; results mainly show effectiveness of specific comparison functions.  
- Suggestion: add qualitative analysis (examples, comparison vectors, visualizations) to illustrate how different functions behave.  
- Suggestion: perform detailed error analysis to identify failure cases and function-specific weaknesses.  
- Suggestion: provide theoretical/mathematical justification linking subtraction/multiplication to properties of vector space semantics.  
- Suggestion: include broader hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (learning rate, embedding size, CNN filters, etc.).  
- Suggestion: compare to stronger, more recent baselines (especially for SNLI) to strengthen claims.  
- Questions for authors: analyze order-sensitivity (e.g., LSTM preprocessing) and its impact; provide visualizations of the comparison layer; clarify what semantic properties subtraction/multiplication capture; discuss task types that might be ill-suited for element-wise subtraction/multiplication.
