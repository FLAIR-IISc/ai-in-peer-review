- Introduces a "compare-aggregate" sequence-matching model using attentive LSTMs, element-wise comparison (SUB, MULT), and a CNN aggregator; evaluated on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI with code released.  
- Empirically compares six comparison functions and finds simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural comparison functions.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Contribution is incremental—compare-aggregate is not novel and the paper mainly tests existing variations.  
- Main reason 2: Lacks qualitative evaluation of comparison functions across diverse sentence/linguistic phenomena, reducing interpretability.  
- Supporting argument: The work repeats prior frameworks (e.g., Wang & Jiang 2016; Parikh et al. 2016) and offers limited methodological innovation.  
- Supporting argument: No qualitative examples or case studies to explain why SUB and MULT perform better on certain sentence types.  
- Supporting argument: Limited theoretical justification for why element-wise operations balance flexibility and simplicity across tasks.  
- Suggestion: Add qualitative case studies (negation, paraphrase, word order, synonymy/antonymy) to illustrate function behavior.  
- Suggestion: Better contextualize results within recent transformer-based models and current NLP advances (e.g., BERT).  
- Suggestion: Provide theoretical analysis of inductive biases or reasons element-wise ops succeed over neural comparators.  
- Suggestion: Explore task-specific adaptations, e.g., handling varied sequence lengths between datasets like MovieQA vs SNLI.  
- Questions for authors: How do functions handle negation, synonymy, antonymy—any qualitative examples?  
- Questions for authors: Were transformer baselines (e.g., BERT) tried, and how is relevance justified given current NLP trends?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are results to hyperparameters (embedding dimensionality, CNN window size, etc.)?
