Summary and contributions
- The paper studies the general compare-aggregate paradigm for sequence matching tasks (QA/answer selection, machine comprehension, and textual entailment). It encodes the two sequences, aligns them with attention, compares each token with its attentive counterpart using a family of comparison functions, and aggregates the token-wise matches with a CNN for final prediction. In SNLI, the token encodings incorporate LSTM; in other tasks, a gated, order-insensitive preprocessing is used; attention is one-directional.
- The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions: NN, NTN, Euclidean+Cosine, element-wise subtraction (SUB), element-wise multiplication (MULT), and SUB+MULT fed to NN. Across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), element-wise subtraction and multiplication—either alone or combined (SUBMULT+NN)—generally perform best.
- The model attains competitive or state-of-the-art results on three datasets and strong results on SNLI, demonstrating that simple element-wise operations can outperform more expressive but parameter-heavy matchers.
- Ablations suggest both attention and the lightweight preprocessing gates contribute, with attention being especially important when sequence lengths differ substantially.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The novelty is incremental: the architecture largely follows established compare-aggregate designs; the new technical element is primarily a careful benchmarking of simple element-wise comparison functions.
- The empirical study, while useful, lacks deeper analysis and rigor that would elevate the insight (e.g., significance tests, capacity-controlled comparisons, and qualitative/error analyses explaining why subtraction/multiplication work better).

Supporting arguments
On the question/problem:
- The paper targets how best to compare aligned token representations in compare-aggregate models for sequence matching. This is timely and practically important.

Motivation and placement in literature:
- The motivation is clear: prior compare-aggregate models often default to MLPs or tensor layers for comparison; similarity-based alternatives are plausible but under-explored. Related work is properly cited (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM, pairwise interaction CNN), and the paper positions itself as a unifying, cross-task study. However, it largely repackages known components (attention, token-level matching, CNN/LSTM aggregators).

Do the results support the claims?
- Strengths:
  - Evaluations span four benchmarks and a standardized set of comparison functions; results consistently favor SUB, MULT, and their combination (SUBMULT+NN), aligning with the claim that element-wise operations are strong.
  - Ablations verify the importance of attention and gating layers.
- Limitations:
  - No statistical significance reporting or variance across random seeds; several datasets are small (WikiQA), so differences may not be robust.
  - Capacity and regularization are not carefully matched across comparison functions. NN/NTN baselines have many more parameters yet no L2 or comparable regularization; fixed (non-trainable) word embeddings may disproportionately handicap NN/NTN relative to parameter-free SUB/MULT.
  - Aggregation is fixed to a 1-layer CNN; the study does not test whether the conclusions hold with alternative aggregators (e.g., BiLSTM/Transformer pooling).
  - The attention is one-directional; two-way alignment might change comparisons.
  - Qualitative/error analysis is minimal; beyond a brief CNN activation visualization, the paper does not illuminate when subtraction vs multiplication helps (e.g., negation, antonymy, lexical overlap, paraphrase, syntax).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Add qualitative analyses that probe what each comparison captures. For instance, illustrate cases where subtraction (sensitive to directional differences/negation) vs multiplication (overlap gating) succeeds or fails across:
  - High lexical overlap vs paraphrastic pairs
  - Negation and antonymy
  - Multi-word expressions and compositional phenomena
  - Premise–hypothesis length mismatch and noisy distractors
- Report statistical significance and variability across multiple seeds. Provide per-dataset effect sizes.
- Control for capacity: ensure the NN/NTN comparators have comparable effective capacity or regularize them fairly (L2, dropout on comparator, weight decay). Include a parameter-count table and a wall-clock/compute cost comparison.
- Explore two-sided attention and alternative aggregators (BiLSTM, Transformer-style pooling) to test robustness of the findings beyond CNNs.
- Examine the impact of fine-tuning embeddings versus keeping them fixed; the latter may disadvantage learned comparators.
- Provide error breakdowns by linguistic phenomena and by length/overlap strata. This will make the empirical insights more generalizable.
- Clarify preprocessing: when LSTM-based contextualization is used (SNLI) versus order-insensitive gating, and how that choice affects performance.

Questions for the authors
1. Capacity and regularization: How did you ensure fair capacity across comparison functions? Did you try weight decay or comparator-specific dropout for NN/NTN, and if so, how did results change?
2. Variance and significance: What is the variance across random seeds for each comparator on each dataset? Are the reported improvements statistically significant?
3. Embedding fine-tuning: You keep GloVe fixed. What happens if you fine-tune embeddings? Do NN/NTN close the gap with SUB/MULT?
4. Contextualization: Beyond SNLI, did you try order-sensitive encoders (BiLSTM) for InsuranceQA/WikiQA/MovieQA? Does the ranking of comparators change when token vectors are richer?
5. Attention directionality: Have you tested bi-directional (premise↔hypothesis) attention? Does this alter the relative performance of subtraction vs multiplication?
6. Aggregation choice: Why a single-layer CNN? Do deeper CNNs or sequence models (BiLSTM/Transformer) change the conclusions?
7. Analysis by phenomenon: Do subtraction/multiplication excel in specific linguistic conditions (e.g., negation, coreference, long-distance alignment)? Can you provide targeted analyses or case studies?
8. Hyperparameters: For small datasets (WikiQA), how sensitive are results to learning rate/batch size/dropout? Please include a sensitivity analysis.
