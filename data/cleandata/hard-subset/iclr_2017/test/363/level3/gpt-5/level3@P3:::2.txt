Summary
This paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences that first performs word-level comparison and then aggregates the comparison sequence with a CNN. The key contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), highlighting that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and their combination with a small NN (SUBMULT+NN) can outperform heavier neural comparisons (NN/NTN) and similarity-only measures (EUCCOS). The model is evaluated across four datasets spanning different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or very competitive results and demonstrating that the same architecture transfers well. The paper is well written and the empirical study is extensive, with helpful ablations indicating the importance of both the preprocessing (gated projection) and attention layers.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, general empirical contribution: a simple, well-motivated framework that performs at or near state-of-the-art across four diverse benchmarks.
- Clear methodological insight: element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) consistently work best, providing a practical design guideline for future work.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Matching pairs of text sequences for tasks such as NLI, answer selection, ranking, and machine comprehension. The paper frames the problem uniformly and focuses on the word-level comparison function within a compare-aggregate pipeline.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. It positions itself relative to Siamese encoders, attention-based models (e.g., Rocktäschel et al.), match-LSTM and decomposable attention (Parikh et al.). The focus on the comparison function fills a gap: prior works typically default to feed-forward layers or simple similarities without a systematic analysis.
- Do the results support the claims with rigor? Largely yes. The model is evaluated on four datasets with appropriate metrics and published baselines. The findings that SUBMULT+NN and element-wise operations are strong are consistent across tasks. Ablations suggest the gating and attention layers matter, especially depending on sequence-length mismatch. Areas to strengthen include reporting variance across runs, statistical significance testing, parameter counts, and more granular ablations (see below).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify and quantify the benefit of preprocessing and attention:
  - Please report full results without preprocessing (no gating) and without attention across all four datasets, ideally for at least EUCCOS and SUBMULT+NN, and also the full model. A per-dataset delta table would make the observed trends (length mismatch sensitivity) more convincing.
  - Provide parameter counts and training time changes when these components are removed.
- Aggregation choice: CNN vs alternatives. Since prior work aggregates with summation, concatenation, or RNNs, a small comparison to a 1-layer BiLSTM aggregator and a pooling baseline would isolate the contribution of CNN.
- Directionality of attention: You use one-directional attention (A attends to Q). Please add a two-directional (mutual) variant or briefly justify with ablations why one-directional is preferable here.
- Embeddings: You freeze GloVe. Include a comparison with fine-tuning embeddings (and possibly character or subword features) to gauge ceiling performance and whether the comparison-function conclusions still hold.
- Robustness and rigor:
  - Report mean±std over multiple random seeds; add significance tests against the strongest baselines.
  - Detail hyperparameter tuning ranges per dataset and confirm tuning was done only on dev sets.
- Fairness and capacity control: Include parameter counts for each comparison function (NN, NTN, SUBMULT+NN) and confirm results are not simply due to capacity differences.
- Interpretability: The Figure 2 visualization is promising. Improve figure quality (higher resolution, larger fonts, colorblind-safe palette) and consider adding example attention maps to relate attention and CNN activations.
- Efficiency and scalability: Provide asymptotic and empirical runtime (tokens/sec) and memory usage for long sequences (e.g., MovieQA), and discuss how attention and CNN scale with sequence length.
- Minor issues:
  - Typo on page 4, line 5: “including a some” → “including some”.
  - Clean up a few reference formatting inconsistencies (escaped characters, duplicate author-year variants).

Questions for the authors
1) Preprocessing and attention:
   - Could you provide a comprehensive ablation table (w/o preprocessing; w/o attention; both removed) for each dataset and for at least EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN? How do these components affect performance as sequence-length disparity changes?
   - How sensitive are results to the preprocessing dimensionality l and to the use of contextualized preprocessing (e.g., replacing the gate with a light-weight 1D conv or BiLSTM)?
2) Comparison functions:
   - Why do you think SUB and MULT work so well? Any analysis of learned weights in SUBMULT+NN that indicates which signal dominates?
   - Did you try normalizing vectors before SUB/MULT (e.g., layer norm or L2 norm) and does that change the relative ranking?
3) Aggregation:
   - How does a BiLSTM or self-attentive aggregator compare to CNN with similar parameter counts? Any benefits from stacking multiple CNN layers?
4) Attention directionality:
   - Have you tried two-way attention (both Q→A and A→Q) as in decomposable attention? If so, what trade-offs did you observe?
5) Embeddings and initialization:
   - What is the effect of fine-tuning GloVe and/or using character/subword embeddings? Do the conclusions about comparison functions hold?
6) Robustness and significance:
   - Please report mean±std over multiple seeds and significance versus the strongest baselines on each dataset.
7) Efficiency:
   - Provide training/inference time and memory usage by dataset and sequence length. Are there bottlenecks when P (plot) is very long in MovieQA?
8) Reproducibility:
   - The code link is provided—could you include exact configs and random seeds for the reported numbers, and specify any dataset-specific preprocessing?

Overall, this is a solid, clearly written paper that offers both practical guidance (use element-wise compare functions) and a broadly applicable architecture. With the clarified ablations and strengthened rigor, it will make a strong contribution.
