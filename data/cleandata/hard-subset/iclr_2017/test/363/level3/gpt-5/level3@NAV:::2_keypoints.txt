- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework that aligns words with attention then aggregates comparison vectors via a CNN.  
- Systematically studies six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Finds simple element-wise operators (SUB, MULT, combination) often outperform more expressive neural alternatives.  
- Evaluated on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) with comparisons to strong published baselines.  
- Paper is clearly written and well positioned within compare-aggregate and attention literature.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept) because of a useful, generalizable insight and broad empirical support.  
- Addresses the problem of word-level comparison for entailment and QA tasks.  
- Motivation is timely and grounded in prior work (decomposable attention, match-LSTM, pairwise interactions).  
- Empirical evidence is strong on three datasets and competitive on SNLI, with cross-dataset evaluation and ablations.  
- Request full ablations quantifying benefit of preprocessing (gating) and attention across datasets and comparison functions.  
- Ask to report statistical significance, confidence intervals, and training variance across seeds.  
- Clarify aggregation details: CNN hyperparameters and whether simple pooling yields similar results.  
- Assess sensitivity to updating vs. freezing embeddings, embedding dimensionality, and sequence length handling.  
- Provide computational cost (training/inference time, memory) and discuss one-directional vs. bi-directional attention trade-offs.  
- Improve Figure 2 readability (resolution, fonts, colorblind-friendly palette) and fix the typo “including a some.”  
- Questions: share complete ablation tables without preprocessing and/or attention across all datasets.  
- Question: do element-wise methods still dominate when embeddings are fine-tuned?  
- Question: how robust are conclusions across seeds and hyperparameters; any significance tests?  
- Question: what is the precise CNN configuration and its incremental contribution versus simple pooling?  
- Question: for MovieQA, how much do Q–P vs. A–P matching individually contribute to final accuracy?
