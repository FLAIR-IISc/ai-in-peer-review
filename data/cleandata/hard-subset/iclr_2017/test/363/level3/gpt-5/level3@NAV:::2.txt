1. Summary
This paper proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for text sequence matching that first performs word-level alignment via attention and then aggregates comparison vectors with a CNN. A key contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), showing that simple element-wise operators (SUB, MULT, and their combination) often outperform more expressive neural alternatives. The approach is evaluated across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), with extensive comparisons to strong published baselines. The paper is clearly written and positions itself well within recent compare-aggregate and attention-based literature.

2. Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Reason: The paper offers a useful, generalizable insight—simple element-wise comparison functions are strong choices in compare-aggregate models—and supports it with broad, competitive empirical results across diverse tasks.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem: Matching text sequences for tasks such as entailment and QA. The paper targets how to best compare word-level representations before aggregation.
- Motivation and placement: Well motivated by limitations of sequence-level encoders and informed by prior compare-aggregate work (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM, pairwise word interactions). The focus on the comparison function is timely and underexplored.
- Evidence: Results are strong on three datasets and competitive on SNLI. The cross-dataset evaluation, comparisons to published baselines, and ablation discussion are compelling, though some analyses need clarification (see below). Claims about element-wise functions are generally supported.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help, not central to the decision)
- Please quantify the benefit of the preprocessing (gating) and attention layers with full ablations: report results per dataset and per comparison function for (a) no preprocessing, (b) no attention, and (c) neither, ideally with variances over multiple runs.
- Report statistical significance or confidence intervals, and training variance across seeds.
- Clarify aggregation: CNN hyperparameters (filter widths/counts), and whether simpler pooling yields similar gains.
- Assess sensitivity to updating vs. freezing embeddings, embedding dimensionality, and sequence length handling.
- Provide computational cost (training/inference time, memory) and discuss one-directional vs. bi-directional attention trade-offs.
- Improve Figure 2 readability (higher resolution, larger fonts, colorblind-friendly palette) for print.
- Fix typo: “including a some” → “including some” (page 4, line 5).

Questions for the authors
- Can you share complete ablation tables without preprocessing and/or attention across all datasets?
- Do element-wise methods still dominate when embeddings are fine-tuned?
- How robust are conclusions across seeds and hyperparameters? Any significance tests?
- What is the precise CNN configuration and its incremental contribution vs. simple pooling?
- For MovieQA, how much does Q–P vs. A–P matching individually contribute to final accuracy?
