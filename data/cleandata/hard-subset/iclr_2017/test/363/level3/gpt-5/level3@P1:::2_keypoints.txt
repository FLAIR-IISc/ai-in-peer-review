- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate architecture: attention-based word alignment, word-level comparison, CNN aggregation for decision-making.  
- Main contribution: systematic comparison of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCLIDEAN+COSINE, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Empirical finding: simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) are often competitive with or superior to parameterized NN/NTN across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Model is simple, fast, broadly applicable, and code is released; ablations show attention and preprocessing matter.  
- Decision: Accept — strong empirical study and useful practical guidance favoring SUB/MULT or SUBMULT+NN over heavier comparison modules.  
- Novelty/placement: Focus on the comparison function is a novel and well-motivated contribution relative to prior compare-aggregate work.  
- Motivation: Attention + parameter-light, similarity-preserving operations bridge rigid metrics and over-parameterized NNs, matching semantic similarity inductive bias.  
- Empirical support: Evaluations span diverse datasets with consistent trends; SUBMULT+NN often wins, EUCCOS/MULT sometimes match.  
- Limitations: Relies on reported baselines (not re-implementations), lacks statistical significance tests and multiple-seed variance estimates.  
- Suggested ablation improvements: Provide full ablation tables removing preprocessing, attention, and both (per dataset) with confidence intervals or significance tests.  
- Gate analysis: Analyze whether preprocessing gates down-weight stopwords or behave differently across tasks (e.g., via histograms/visualizations).  
- Aggregator comparison: Compare CNN aggregator to sum/mean pooling, BiLSTM, and deeper CNNs; report accuracy vs parameter count and runtime.  
- Parameter efficiency: Report parameter counts and training/inference time for NN/NTN vs SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN and clarify hyperparameter tuning protocol.  
- Attention directionality: Compare one-directional attention to bi-directional attention (A-to-Q and Q-to-A) to isolate effects on results.  
- Embeddings/context: Evaluate trainable embeddings and a lightweight contextual encoder (e.g., shallow BiLSTM) to confirm robustness of element-wise comparison advantage.  
- Per-dataset analysis: Provide breakdowns and qualitative examples showing when SUB vs MULT vs EUCCOS performs best, relating to overlap vs paraphrase.  
- Presentation fixes: Correct typo (“including a some”), improve Figure 2 resolution/annotations, and ensure consistent notation and symbol definitions.  
- Question: Provide complete ablation numbers per dataset for removing preprocessing/attention with multiple random seeds and significance testing.  
- Question: Describe hyperparameter tuning protocol per comparison function and dataset; was tuning shared to ensure fairness?  
- Question: Why use a CNN for aggregation and how does it compare in accuracy and efficiency to pooling/BiLSTM?  
- Question: Did you try bi-directional attention or iterative alignment refinement, and do SUB/MULT conclusions still hold?  
- Question: What is the effect of trainable embeddings or adding a shallow contextual encoder on comparison-function rankings?  
- Question: For MovieQA, how are long plots handled (truncation/windowing), and how sensitive are results to plot length and attention concentration?  
- Question: Report parameter counts and per-example inference time for NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN to support accuracy–efficiency claims.  
- Question: In SNLI, detail the preprocessing LSTM architecture and whether comparison-function findings persist with and without the LSTM.
