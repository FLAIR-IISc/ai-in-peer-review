- Proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture: gating-based preprocessing, unidirectional soft attention to align words, word-level comparison functions, and a CNN aggregator.
- Central contribution is an empirical cross-task study of six comparison-function families (NN, NTN, Euclid+Cos, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise operations, especially SUBMULT+NN, consistently perform strongly and often beat NN/NTN.
- Evaluated on four datasets (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, MovieQA) with state-of-the-art or competitive results.
- Ablations show both preprocessing gates and attention are important; their relative impact depends on sequence length mismatch.
- Code release supports reproducibility.
- Decision: Accept — reasons: clear empirical finding on comparison functions and a simple, general architecture with strong cross-benchmark performance.
- Problem addressed: generic two-text matching (entailment, answer selection, machine comprehension) with focus on the underexplored token-level comparison step.
- Placement in literature: well-motivated among attention/compare-aggregate work; systematic cross-dataset comparison is a useful, novel contribution despite some incremental design choices.
- Evidence: results and ablations support claims that compare-aggregate works broadly and element-wise comparisons are strong baselines.
- Limitation noted: order-insensitive preprocessing may cap performance on long sequences (e.g., MovieQA); authors appropriately acknowledge this.
- Missing/reporting suggestions: include parameter counts, training/runtime, statistical significance, and number of runs/seeds.
- Clarify attention formulation and cite Luong et al. if using bilinear/general scoring rather than Bahdanau additive attention.
- Recommend exploring light contextualization (small CNN, position-aware self-attention) to address order sensitivity on long inputs.
- Suggest capacity-controlled NTN variants (low-rank/factorized) for fairer comparison with low-capacity element-wise methods.
- Connect SUB and MULT heuristics to prior sentence-level matching features (Mou et al., Tai et al.) and position this work as a word-level extension.
- Consider untying preprocessing parameters for Q and A or using conditional gating to allow role-specific weighting.
- Additional analyses requested: freeze vs. fine-tune embeddings, replace CNN aggregator with pooling/RNN ablation, deeper CNN/kernel-size effects, expanded error analysis of when element-wise methods win/lose.
- Specific questions for authors: exact attention scoring used, parameter sharing details, impact of adding light contextual encoders, NTN capacity-control experiments, rationale for one-layer CNN, embedding fine-tuning/OOV handling, run counts/statistical significance, and experiments with bi-directional attention.
