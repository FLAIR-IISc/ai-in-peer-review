- Problem & approach: Proposes a compare-aggregate framework that aligns sequences with attention, applies element-wise comparison functions, and aggregates token comparisons with a CNN for prediction.  
- Main contribution: Systematic empirical evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclid+Cosine, SUB, MULT, SUB+MULT+NN) across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Key finding: Simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication, and their combination with a small NN, consistently outperform more expressive neural comparison modules.  
- Empirical strength: Framework yields strong, often state-of-the-art results on three QA datasets and remains competitive on SNLI; code is released and paper is clear.  
- Decision: Accept — contribution is incremental but valuable due to breadth, clarity, and reproducibility.  
- Rigor: Experiments control architecture around the comparison unit enabling a fair head-to-head assessment.  
- Motivation & positioning: Well-motivated within compare-aggregate literature and timely focus on comparison function design.  
- Ablation insights: Preprocessing and attention ablations meaningfully support claims about their roles across different sequence lengths.  
- Suggested qualitative analysis: Provide examples showing where SUB vs MULT succeed/fail on phenomena like synonymy, negation, numeric mismatch, overlap, and antonymy.  
- Statistical/computational reporting: Report statistical significance, parameter counts, and training/inference costs for each comparison function.  
- Preprocessing consistency: Either unify preprocessing across tasks (LSTM vs non-contextual gating) or justify differences; consider bidirectional attention as a stronger baseline.  
- Robustness analyses: Analyze sensitivity to freezing embeddings, sequence length, OOV/rare words, and hyperparameters (dropout, learning rate).  
- Aggregation choice question: Justify using a CNN for aggregation and compare to LSTM/Transformer aggregators under identical settings.  
- Attention design question: Explore how two-way attention affects results versus the one-directional attention used.  
- Error analysis request: Provide an error typology showing when SUB vs MULT helps or hurts (e.g., negation, coreference, numeric reasoning).  
- Quantitative reproducibility questions: Are improvements over NN/NTN statistically significant across runs; what is the variance?  
- Efficiency questions: What are parameter counts, FLOPs, and wall-clock training/inference times for each comparison function?
