- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework that compares words then aggregates comparisons with a CNN for text sequence matching.
- Systematically studies six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).
- Finds simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and SUBMULT+NN outperform heavier NN/NTN and similarity-only measures.
- Evaluated on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) with state-of-the-art or competitive results.
- Shows the same architecture transfers well across tasks and is well written with extensive empirical study.
- Ablations indicate gated preprocessing and attention layers are important, especially with sequence-length mismatch.
- Decision: Accept — strong, general empirical contribution and clear methodological insight for future designs.
- Requests full ablation results (no gating, no attention, both removed) per dataset and per comparison function to quantify effects.
- Ask to report parameter counts and training-time differences when removing components to control for capacity.
- Suggest comparing CNN aggregator to alternatives (1-layer BiLSTM, pooling) to isolate CNN contribution.
- Recommend testing two-directional (mutual) attention or justifying the one-directional choice with ablations.
- Request comparison with fine-tuning embeddings and adding character/subword features to assess ceiling performance.
- Ask for robustness: report mean±std over multiple seeds, statistical significance, and hyperparameter tuning details.
- Urge fairness checks: parameter counts per comparison function to ensure results aren’t due to capacity differences.
- Suggest improving interpretability visuals (higher-res, larger fonts, colorblind-safe palette) and adding attention maps.
- Request efficiency and scalability analysis: asymptotics, tokens/sec, memory usage for long sequences (e.g., MovieQA).
- Minor fixes: correct typo (“including a some”), clean reference formatting inconsistencies.
- Reproducibility: include exact configs, random seeds, and dataset-specific preprocessing in the released code.
- Specific author questions: sensitivity to preprocessing dimensionality and contextualized preprocessing; why SUB/MULT work well; effects of normalization; aggregator comparisons; two-way attention trade-offs; embedding fine-tuning effects; and detailed runtime/memory statistics.
