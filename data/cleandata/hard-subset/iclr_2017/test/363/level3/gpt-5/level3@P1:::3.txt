Summary and contributions
This paper studies a general compare-aggregate framework for text sequence matching: (i) apply soft attention to align token representations between two sequences; (ii) compare each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other via a pluggable comparison function; (iii) aggregate the sequence of local comparison vectors with a CNN to make the final prediction. The paper’s main contribution is a thorough, cross-task empirical analysis of the comparison module. Six comparison functions are evaluated, including simple element-wise operators (SUB, MULT), a combined SUBMULT+NN, and higher-capacity layers (NN, NTN) as well as distance/similarity (Euclidean + cosine). Experiments across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) show that simple element-wise operations, especially SUBMULT+NN, are highly competitive or superior to more complex alternatives. The model achieves state-of-the-art or best-reported results on three datasets and competitive results on SNLI, with ablations highlighting the importance of attention and preprocessing gates.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical evidence across diverse tasks that simple, principled comparison functions outperform or match more complex neural alternatives; (2) A clear, general framework with careful ablations and reproducibility (public code), making the findings broadly useful to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Matching two text sequences is central to QA, entailment, and related tasks. Prior work (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM, pairwise word interactions) indicates the value of compare-aggregate, but has not systematically dissected comparison functions across tasks. This paper directly targets that gap, which is timely and practically important.
- Placement in literature: The model is close in spirit to decomposable attention (Parikh et al., 2016) and match-LSTM/word-interaction methods, but differs in the aggregation choice (CNN), uses one-directional attention, and, crucially, centers the empirical study on comparison functions, including element-wise operators that have been under-explored at the word-level in this context. The connection to heuristic matching cues used at sentence level (difference and product) is appropriate and valuable.
- Evidence and rigor: Results are reported on four benchmarks with appropriate metrics (accuracy, MAP/MRR). Comparisons include strong baselines and recent models. Ablations isolating attention and preprocessing layers are informative, and the cross-dataset consistency of SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN is compelling. The proposed explanation (distance/similarity-preserving operators as a good middle ground between rigid metrics and over-parameterized neural layers) is plausible and supported by data.
- Limitations acknowledged: The model’s preprocessing for long sequences is order-insensitive (except SNLI), which can cap upper-bound performance; nevertheless, the approach still surpasses order-sensitive baselines on three datasets. The paper’s novelty is incremental relative to prior compare-aggregate models, but the systematic analysis and multi-dataset validation constitute a meaningful contribution.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect my accept decision)
- Clarify attention citation and form: The attention scoring used matches Luong et al.’s “general”/bilinear form more than Bahdanau-style; please cite Luong et al. (2015) explicitly and position your attention as asymmetric (A attends to Q).
- Discuss order sensitivity: For long contexts (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA), the preprocessing is bag-of-words with gates. Please emphasize this early and discuss when order-insensitive preprocessing is acceptable, and where it may fail. A lightweight positional encoding or local RNN/CNN before attention might improve robustness.
- Parameterization and overfitting: NTN likely overfits due to its cubic parameter growth. Consider reporting parameter counts and adding a low-rank or reduced-output tensor variant (e.g., input dim l, output dim m << l), or factorized bilinear forms, to make capacity more comparable across functions.
- Relation to prior heuristic matching: SUB and MULT closely mirror heuristic matching features (abs/subtraction and element-wise product) used by Mou et al. (2016) at the sentence level. Please highlight this connection and what’s new here (systematic word-level evaluation within a compare-aggregate pipeline).
- Shared preprocessing parameters: You use the same gating parameterization for Q and A. Intuitively, salience patterns differ for questions vs answers (or premise vs hypothesis). Consider separate parameter sets or conditional gating (e.g., question-conditioned gates for answers).
- Experimental clarity: 
  - Report variance over multiple runs and statistical significance where claims hinge on small margins.
  - Detail CNN aggregation (filter widths, number of filters, pooling), and justify CNN vs RNN/mean pooling empirically.
  - Analyze training time and memory cost, especially the attention matrix and the impact on long sequences (MovieQA).
  - Explore embedding fine-tuning and OOV handling beyond zero init; freezing GloVe may depress results on domain-specific data.
- Analysis: Provide error typology by dataset, and per-length/lexical overlap breakdowns to better understand when SUB/MULT shine. A comparison of one-way vs two-way attention would also be instructive.

Questions for the authors
1. Did you try fine-tuning word embeddings or subword embeddings? How did this affect performance across datasets?
2. Can you report parameter counts and training time for each comparison function (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) and include significance tests over multiple random seeds?
3. How sensitive are results to CNN hyperparameters (filter widths, counts, pooling)? Did an RNN/biLSTM aggregator change the ranking of comparison functions?
4. For long contexts (MovieQA), did you consider bidirectional (co-)attention or multi-hop attention? Would that alter the relative merits of comparison functions?
5. Did separate preprocessing/gating parameters for Q and A help? Alternatively, did conditioning A’s gates on Q’s representation (or vice versa) help?
6. For NTN, did a low-rank/factorized or reduced-output variant mitigate overfitting? If tested, how did it compare to SUBMULT+NN?
7. What is the impact of using order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., a shallow CNN or GRU) on WikiQA/InsuranceQA? Does SUB/MULT remain best under those settings?
