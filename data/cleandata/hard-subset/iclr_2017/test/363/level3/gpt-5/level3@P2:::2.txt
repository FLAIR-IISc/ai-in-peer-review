1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies sequence matching in a general compare-aggregate framework: (a) align each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted representation of the other sequence, (b) compare the aligned vectors with a chosen comparison function, and (c) aggregate the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN for prediction. The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions—standard feed-forward (NN), neural tensor network (NTN), Euclidean+cosine (EUCCOS), and three element-wise variants (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). Experiments on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) show competitive or state-of-the-art results, and reveal that simple element-wise functions (especially SUB/MULT and their combination) often outperform more complex neural alternatives. The paper is clearly written, the model is simple and broadly applicable, and the empirical findings about comparison functions are useful to the community.

2. Decision
Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) Thorough, multi-dataset empirical study that isolates the impact of comparison functions and yields a clear, actionable finding (element-wise SUB/MULT work best). (2) Strong results across three QA datasets and competitive SNLI performance, demonstrating the generality and practicality of the approach.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: The paper addresses how to best compare two text sequences for tasks such as QA and NLI. Specifically, it asks which word-level comparison functions are most effective within a compare-aggregate architecture and whether this architecture generalizes across tasks.
- Motivation and positioning: The work is well situated relative to match-LSTM, decomposable attention, and pairwise interaction CNNs. The novelty lies less in proposing a new architecture and more in systematically dissecting the comparison function choices and showing surprising strength of element-wise operations, along with a CNN-based aggregation across several tasks.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - Empirical rigor: Results are reported on four datasets with published baselines. The model attains SOTA or very strong results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and competitive performance on SNLI. The ablation discussion indicates that both preprocessing (gating) and attention contribute, with task-dependent importance.
  - Scientific clarity: The study convincingly supports the claim that SUB/MULT-based comparisons are powerful. The observation that EUCCOS is competitive in some settings further grounds the analysis.
  - Caveats: (i) The dimensionality mismatch across comparison functions (EUCCOS yields 2-D vs. l-D for others) could bias aggregation capacity; controlling for this would strengthen claims. (ii) Given related prior work (e.g., Parikh et al.), the architectural novelty is incremental; the paper’s value is mainly in the comparative analysis and cross-task validation. (iii) More detail on variance across random seeds and statistical significance would increase confidence.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the accept decision)
- Clarify and deepen the ablation:
  - Report full, per-dataset numbers for removing preprocessing, removing attention, and removing both, ideally with confidence intervals over multiple runs.
  - Provide a fairness control for EUCCOS by (a) projecting EUCCOS to l dimensions via a learned layer before CNN, or (b) projecting other functions to 2-D, and report the impact.
- Analysis and efficiency:
  - Include parameter counts and training/inference speed comparisons across comparison functions (notably NTN vs. SUB/MULT).
  - Report sensitivity to l, filter widths/counts in the CNN, and whether gains persist when embeddings are fine-tuned (they are currently fixed).
  - Provide results averaged over multiple random seeds with standard deviations and significance tests against strong baselines.
- Architectural clarifications:
  - Specify CNN details (filter sizes, numbers, pooling strategy) and any task-specific differences.
  - Discuss why one-directional attention suffices and whether bi-directional attention helps or hurts.
  - For MovieQA, describe how long plots are handled (truncation, sliding windows), and whether memory limits affect alignment.
- Presentation:
  - Figure 2 is hard to read, especially in print. Increase resolution, font sizes, and color/contrast; consider adding captions that explain the highlighted regions.
  - Fix the typo: “including a some” → “including some” (page 4, line 5).
  - Consider a table that cleanly consolidates the comparison-function results across tasks for quick reference.

Questions for the authors
- Ablations and fairness:
  - Please provide exact per-dataset results for models without preprocessing, without attention, and without both. How does training time change when these components are removed?
  - EUCCOS vs. others: How much of the gap is due to dimensionality (2-D vs. l-D) feeding into the CNN? Could you add a learned projection so all comparison outputs have the same dimensionality before aggregation and report results?
  - How stable are the rankings of comparison functions across random seeds? Please provide mean±std over at least 3–5 runs.
- Architectural choices:
  - Why choose a CNN aggregator over simple pooling or an RNN/Transformer aggregator? Did you compare to sum/mean pooling or a small BiLSTM aggregator under the same settings?
  - Your preprocessing is order-insensitive for three tasks. Did you try a light-weight contextualizer (e.g., a shallow BiLSTM) and, if so, how did it affect results and cost?
  - Did you try bi-directional attention (aligning A→Q and Q→A) and concatenating comparisons? Any gains?
- Training details:
  - What are the filter widths and counts in the CNN, and the pooling operation? Any task-specific tuning?
  - Embeddings are fixed. Do results improve with fine-tuning or with character/subword embeddings for OOV handling?
  - What regularization beyond dropout was tried (weight decay, layer norm)? Any effect on NTN vs. SUB/MULT?
- Scope and generality:
  - Can you comment on how the element-wise findings might transfer to other pairwise tasks (e.g., paraphrase identification, dialogue response selection)?
  - For datasets with highly imbalanced sequence lengths (e.g., MovieQA plots), can you quantify how attention vs. preprocessing contributes (numbers per dataset)?
