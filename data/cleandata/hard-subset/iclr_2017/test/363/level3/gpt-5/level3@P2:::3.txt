Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences: (i) a light gating-based preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) a unidirectional soft attention to produce aligned pairs (a_j, h_j), (iii) a comparison function applied at the word level, and (iv) a CNN to aggregate local comparison results for final prediction.
- The central contribution is an empirical, cross-task study of comparison functions. Six families are evaluated: NN, NTN, Euclidean+Cosine, and three element-wise options (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The authors find that simple element-wise operations—especially the combination SUBMULT+NN—consistently perform strongly and often outperform NN/NTN.
- The model is evaluated on four datasets spanning different sequence matching tasks (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, MovieQA), showing state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. Ablations indicate both the preprocessing gates and attention are important; their relative contribution depends on sequence length mismatch.
- Code release is a plus and supports reproducibility.

Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) solid, clear empirical finding about the effectiveness of simple element-wise comparison operators across tasks; (2) a single, simple architecture that generalizes well to multiple benchmarks, with strong performance and informative ablations.

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled?
- The paper targets generic two-text matching in NLP (entailment, answer selection, and machine comprehension). It focuses the investigation on the comparison step within compare-aggregate pipelines, a component underexplored relative to attention and aggregation.

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper situates itself among attention-based and compare-aggregate models (e.g., Parikh et al., Wang & Jiang, He & Lin), while asking a concrete, under-studied question: which comparison function is most effective at the token level? The design choices—unidirectional attention, word-level compare, CNN aggregation—are reasonable and clearly explained.
- Some aspects are incremental relative to Parikh et al. and prior work by the authors, but the systematic cross-dataset analysis of comparison functions is new and practically useful.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- The empirical results across four datasets, plus ablations removing preprocessing and attention, support the claims that (a) compare-aggregate works broadly, and (b) element-wise comparison functions are strong baselines and often superior to heavier NN/NTN alternatives.
- The observation that order-insensitive preprocessing still yields strong performance (except on SNLI where LSTM is added) is interesting, though it may cap upper-bound performance for long sequences; acknowledging this limitation is appropriate.
- The paper would benefit from reporting parameter counts, training time, and statistical significance, but the current evidence is nonetheless convincing.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Clarify attention formulation and citations: The attention score appears closest to Luong et al.’s “general/bilinear” form rather than Bahdanau’s additive attention; please cite Luong et al. accordingly and describe the exact scoring function.
- Discuss order sensitivity: For long inputs (MovieQA plots, InsuranceQA answers, WikiQA sentences), the preprocessing is order-insensitive. Consider emphasizing this limitation and, if possible, add a lightweight contextualization (e.g., small-width CNN, position-aware self-attention, or gated convolutions) to quantify the potential gains.
- Comparison function capacity: The NTN has high capacity and may overfit. Consider a low-rank/low-output variant (e.g., input dim l, output dim m ≪ l; factorized tensor or bilinear pooling) to provide a fairer capacity-controlled comparison with SUB/MULT.
- Relation to prior matching heuristics: The SUB and MULT (and their concatenation with a small NN) are closely related to heuristic matching features used at the sentence level by Mou et al. (2016) and Tai et al. (2015). Please make this connection explicit and position your word-level use as an extension/generalization in the compare-aggregate pipeline.
- Parameter sharing in preprocessing: You appear to use the same preprocessing parameters for Q and A. Given their different roles (e.g., question vs. answer), consider discussing/trying non-shared parameters or conditional gating to allow task-specific weighting.
- Reporting and analysis:
  - Provide parameter counts for each comparison variant, and include training/runtime comparisons.
  - Add statistical significance tests across seeds, especially when differences are small.
  - Report the effect of freezing vs. fine-tuning embeddings.
  - Include an ablation replacing the CNN aggregator with simple pooling or RNN, to show the necessity of CNN specifically.
  - Expand error analysis to categorize when element-wise methods win vs. lose (e.g., lexical overlap vs. paraphrase).

Questions for the authors
- Attention scoring: Can you confirm the exact attention scoring function (is it Luong-general: a_j^T W q_i plus bias)? Did you try additive (Bahdanau) attention, and if so, how did it compare?
- Parameter sharing: Are Wi/Wu/bi/bu shared between Q and A? If so, have you tried untying them, or conditioning gates on the counterpart sequence (e.g., question-aware answer gating)?
- Order sensitivity: For the long-sequence tasks, what is the performance change when adding a light contextual encoder (e.g., 1–2-layer CNN or BiLSTM with small hidden size) before attention? Does this reduce the observed dependence on word overlap?
- NTN capacity control: Did you evaluate a low-rank or reduced-output NTN (or factorized bilinear pooling) to mitigate overfitting? If not, could you report preliminary results or comment on feasibility?
- Aggregation choice: Why a one-layer CNN rather than pooling or a small RNN? Did deeper CNNs help? Any observations on kernel sizes most effective across tasks?
- Embeddings: You freeze GloVe. Did fine-tuning help on any dataset? How are OOVs handled beyond zero initialization (e.g., character CNN)?
- Robustness and significance: How many runs per configuration? Are improvements statistically significant on WikiQA (MAP/MRR) and SNLI?
- Two-directional attention: Parikh et al. use bi-directional alignment. Did you try symmetrical attention or inter-sentence attention in both directions, and what trade-offs did you observe?

Overall, this is a clear, timely paper with a simple, general model and a useful take-away: element-wise comparison functions are strong, broadly applicable choices for compare-aggregate architectures. I recommend acceptance.
