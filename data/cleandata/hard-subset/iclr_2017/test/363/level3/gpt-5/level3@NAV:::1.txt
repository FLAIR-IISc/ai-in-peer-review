Summary and contributions: The paper addresses the problem of matching text sequences for tasks such as QA, answer selection, and textual entailment. It proposes a general compare-aggregate framework that first computes attentive, word-level alignments between two sequences, then applies element-wise comparison functions, and finally aggregates the per-token comparisons with a CNN for prediction. The key contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+Cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and their combination) across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The study finds that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication, and their combination with a small NN, consistently outperform more expressive neural comparison modules. Results are strong and often state-of-the-art, and the paper is clearly written with code released.

Decision: Accept. Reason: The paper provides a careful, multi-dataset empirical study that yields a robust, practically valuable insight (element-wise comparison functions are strong baselines and often best). While incremental, the breadth, clarity, and reproducibility make it a useful contribution to the community.

Supporting arguments: 
- The approach is well-motivated and well-situated within the compare-aggregate literature, contrasting with decomposable attention and pairwise-interaction CNNs. The choice to focus on the comparison function is timely and grounded in similarity measurement.
- Empirically, the framework performs competitively or best on three QA datasets and remains strong on SNLI. The ablations on preprocessing and attention meaningfully support claims about their roles across length regimes.
- The study is rigorous in controlling the architecture around the comparison unit, enabling a fair head-to-head assessment of matching functions.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the accept decision):
- Provide qualitative analyses illustrating how subtraction vs multiplication behave across sentence phenomena (e.g., synonymy/paraphrase, negation, numerical mismatch, lexical overlap, antonymy), and where each fails.
- Report statistical significance, parameter counts, and training/inference cost for each comparison function to contextualize gains vs complexity.
- Unify preprocessing across tasks (LSTM vs non-contextual gating) or justify differences; consider bidirectional attention as a stronger aggregation baseline.
- Analyze sensitivity to freezing word embeddings, sequence length, and OOV/rare words; discuss robustness and generalization.

Questions for the authors:
- Are improvements over NN/NTN statistically significant across runs? What is the variance?
- What are parameter counts and FLOPs for each comparison function, and their wall-clock training/inference times?
- Why choose CNN for aggregation; how does it compare to LSTM/Transformers under identical settings?
- How does two-way attention affect results versus the one-directional attention used here?
- Can you provide an error typology showing where SUB vs MULT helps or hurts (e.g., negation, coreference, numeric reasoning)?
- How sensitive are results to freezing embeddings and to the dropout/learning-rate choices?
