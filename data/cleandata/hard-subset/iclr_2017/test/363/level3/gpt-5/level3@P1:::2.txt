1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies sequence matching problems (machine comprehension, answer selection, textual entailment) through a general compare-aggregate architecture that first aligns and compares words (via attention-weighted correspondences) and then aggregates comparison results with a CNN to make a decision. The main contribution is a systematic investigation of six comparison functions at the word level: NN, NTN, EUCLIDEAN+COSINE, SUB (element-wise squared difference), MULT (element-wise product), and SUBMULT+NN (concatenated SUB and MULT followed by a small NN). Across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), the authors show that simple element-wise comparisons (especially SUB, MULT, and their combination) are competitive with or superior to parameterized NN/NTN alternatives, often achieving state-of-the-art or near state-of-the-art performance. The model is simple, fast, and broadly applicable; the authors release code and present ablations indicating the roles of attention and preprocessing gates.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons:
- Broad, well-motivated empirical study demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions consistently outperform heavier neural comparison modules across multiple tasks and datasets.
- Clear, general architecture with practical guidance for practitioners (e.g., prefer SUB/MULT or SUBMULT+NN), yielding strong performance and a useful negative result about NN/NTN comparisons.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and placement in literature: The paper targets a central question—how to compare two text sequences effectively—and positions itself well among recent compare-aggregate approaches (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise interaction CNNs). The focus on the comparison function is novel relative to prior work that largely fixed this component (often NN/MLP) and varied higher-level architectures.
- Approach motivation: The use of attention to align words and then compare them is well justified. Exploring similarity-preserving, parameter-light operations (SUB/MULT) bridges the gap between rigid metrics (cosine/Euclidean) and expressive but potentially over-parameterized NN/NTN, matching the inductive bias of semantic similarity tasks.
- Empirical support: Evaluations span four datasets with different characteristics, baselines are competitive and representative of the time, and the ablations suggest both attention and preprocessing matter, with different importance depending on sequence-length mismatch. The finding that SUBMULT+NN generally wins (and EUCCOS/MULT sometimes match it) is consistent and practically valuable.
- Rigor and limitations: While results are strong, the work leans on reported baselines rather than re-implementations, and there are no statistical significance tests or multiple-seed variance. More detailed ablations (e.g., full removal of preprocessing/attention across all datasets; aggregator alternatives) would further solidify the claims. Nonetheless, given the breadth of datasets and consistent trends, the empirical support is convincing.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and quantify the contribution of preprocessing and attention:
  - Provide full ablation tables (for all datasets) that remove (a) preprocessing only, (b) attention only, and (c) both, ideally for at least the best-performing comparison functions, with confidence intervals or significance tests across multiple runs.
  - Discuss whether the gates mostly down-weight stopwords or behave differently across tasks; a histogram/visualization would help.
- Aggregation choice: Compare the CNN aggregator to alternatives (sum/mean pooling as in decomposable attention, BiLSTM, deeper CNN). Reporting accuracy vs. parameter count/runtimes would substantiate the CNN choice.
- Parameter efficiency and fairness: Report parameter counts per comparison function (NN/NTN vs. SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN) and training/inference time. Clarify the hyperparameter tuning protocol to avoid selection bias across functions.
- Attention directionality: Since your attention is one-directional, add a comparison to bi-directional attention (A-to-Q and Q-to-A) to isolate how much this choice affects results relative to the comparison function.
- Embeddings and context: You freeze GloVe and use order-insensitive preprocessing for three datasets. Include results with trainable embeddings and a lightweight contextualization (e.g., a shallow BiLSTM) to verify that the observed advantage of element-wise comparisons persists.
- Analysis of when each function wins: Provide per-dataset breakdown and qualitative cases where SUB vs. MULT vs. EUCCOS performs best; discuss links to lexical overlap vs. semantic paraphrase.
- Clarity and presentation:
  - Fix the typo: “including a some” → “including some” (page 4, line 5).
  - Figure 2 is hard to read, especially in print. Increase resolution, font size, and contrast; annotate key regions explicitly.
  - Ensure consistent notation (e.g., reuse l, d) and define all symbols upon first use.

Questions for the authors
- Could you provide complete ablation numbers (per dataset) for removing preprocessing and/or attention, with multiple random seeds and significance testing?
- How were hyperparameters tuned per comparison function and dataset? Was a shared tuning protocol enforced to ensure fairness?
- Why a CNN for aggregation over comparisons? How does it compare to sum/mean pooling and BiLSTM in both accuracy and efficiency?
- Did you try bi-directional attention or iterative refinement of alignments? If so, do the conclusions about SUB/MULT still hold?
- What is the effect of making embeddings trainable and/or adding a shallow contextual encoder in the preprocessing layer on the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- For MovieQA, how do you handle very long plots in practice (truncation, windowing)? How sensitive are results to plot length and attention concentration?
- Can you report parameter counts and per-example inference time for NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN to support the claim that element-wise functions are a good accuracy–efficiency trade-off?
- In SNLI, you mention incorporating LSTM into preprocessing. Please detail the exact architecture and whether the comparison-function findings remain consistent with and without the LSTM.
