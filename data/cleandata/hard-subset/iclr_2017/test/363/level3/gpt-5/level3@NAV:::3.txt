Summary: The paper proposes a general and practical compare-aggregate modeling strategy for two-text NLU: soft attention to align tokens, a comparison function operating on aligned pairs, and a CNN aggregation module for prediction. The key contribution is a careful empirical analysis of the comparison component across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), showing that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and their combination) consistently match or outperform NN/NTN and distance-based baselines. The approach is timely, easy to implement, and delivers strong results on important NLP problems.

Decision: Accept. The work is well-executed and empirically compelling; the systematic, cross-task analysis of comparison functions provides actionable insight despite some incremental aspects relative to prior compare-aggregate models.

Supporting arguments: 
- Problem and motivation are clear, and the approach is well placed in the literature on decomposable attention and compare-aggregate frameworks. While close to Parikh et al. and the authors’ own earlier work, the focused ablation on comparison functions is novel and useful.
- Results are strong and broadly consistent; the analysis of the comparison layer is careful and supports the main claims. The model is straightforward, reproducible (code released), and performs competitively or better than baselines across diverse tasks.
- A caveat: for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), the preprocessing is order-insensitive, which likely limits the upper bound; this should be foregrounded in the introduction/discussion.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the decision):
- Clarify/cite attention: the scoring is closer to Luong et al. (2015) bilinear/general attention than Bahdanau; please update citations accordingly.
- NTN likely overfits due to parameter count; consider a low-rank/tensor variant with shape l×l×m (m << l) or CP/Tucker factorization.
- SUBMULTNN resembles sentence-pair heuristic matching in Lili Mou et al. (2016); acknowledge the similarity.
- Question the use of the same preprocessing parameters for Q and A; task asymmetry may benefit from distinct gates or conditioning on role.
- Discuss/order-sensitivity: try positional encodings or lightweight contextualization for long sequences; add a permutation-sensitivity test.
- Report parameter counts, training variance over multiple seeds, and runtime/memory; compare CNN aggregation to a Decomposable-style sum/FF aggregator on the same comparisons.

Questions for the authors:
- Did you try separate preprocessing parameter sets for Q vs A, or role-conditioned gates?
- How sensitive are conclusions to updating embeddings and to random seeds?
- Can you provide parameter counts and learning curves for NN vs NTN vs SUB/MULT?
- Does a low-rank NTN (l×l×m) close the gap without overfitting?
- How does CNN aggregation compare to simple pooling/FF on the same comparison vectors?
- Can you quantify performance loss when shuffling word order in long sequences?
