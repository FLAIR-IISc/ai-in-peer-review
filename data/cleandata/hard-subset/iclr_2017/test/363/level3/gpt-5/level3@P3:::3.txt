Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes a simple, general compare-aggregate architecture for two-text NLU: (i) soft attention aligns tokens between sequences, (ii) a comparison function operates on each aligned pair, and (iii) a CNN aggregates comparison vectors into task outputs. The design is intentionally modular and task-agnostic.
- A central contribution is a careful empirical analysis of the comparison function. The paper benchmarks six variants (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) and finds that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and their combination with a small NN tend to outperform more parameterized alternatives.
- The model is evaluated on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or strong results with a straightforward pipeline. The work is timely and addresses a core NLP problem (matching text sequences) with a clear, reproducible strategy.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- The paper provides a general and practical modeling recipe that is easy to reuse across tasks, and its systematic analysis of comparison functions is novel and useful to the community.
- Despite being close to prior compare-aggregate work (notably Parikh et al. and the authors’ own match-LSTM line), the paper makes sufficient contributions in empirical breadth, ablations, and insights into comparison functions.

Supporting arguments
1) What is the specific problem? Matching two text sequences across tasks (entailment, answer selection, machine comprehension) efficiently and effectively, with a specific focus on what comparison function works best within a compare-aggregate framework.
2) Is the approach well-motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It builds on decomposable/compare-aggregate architectures and attentive matching, positioning itself clearly relative to Parikh et al. (2016), Wang & Jiang (2016), and He & Lin (2016). The emphasis on the comparison layer is well motivated by the nature of semantic similarity.
3) Do the results support the claims? Generally yes. The model is competitive or superior on four benchmarks, and the ablations isolate the impact of attention, preprocessing, and comparison functions. The finding that simple element-wise comparisons are strong is consistent across datasets. Some design choices (frozen embeddings, order-insensitive preprocessing for three tasks) might cap the upper bound, but the conclusions about comparisons remain supported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Word order sensitivity and upper bound: For longer sequences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA), the preprocessing/gating is order-insensitive (and SNLI uses LSTMs). This likely limits performance ceiling; please foreground this limitation in the introduction/discussion and, if possible, add a variant with lightweight order modeling (e.g., shallow BiLSTM/TemporalConv over Q/A, positional encodings, or self-attention with relative positions) to quantify the headroom.
- Attention mechanism citation and formulation: Your attention score in Eq. 2 is closer to Luong et al. (2015)’s “general” bilinear scoring (h^T W s) than to Bahdanau’s additive attention. Please cite Luong (or another bilinear reference) as the primary antecedent, clarifying the one-directional design choice and its trade-offs versus the two-way attention in Parikh et al.
- Comparison component capacity: The NTN likely overfits due to its parameter count. Consider a low-rank/llm tensor variant with an intermediate dimension m << l (e.g., T ≈ ∑r u_r v_r^T with r=m, or factorized bilinear pooling), which should reduce capacity while preserving useful multiplicative interactions. Reporting params and regularization effects (L2, dropout) across comparison functions would strengthen the overfitting diagnosis.
- Relation to Mou et al. (2016): SUBMULT+NN resembles the heuristic sentence-level matching features in Mou et al. (|u−v| and u⊙v). Please acknowledge this connection explicitly, noting that your contribution applies similar heuristics at the word-alignment level within a compare-aggregate pipeline.
- Parameter sharing in preprocessing: Eq. 1 appears to share Wi, Wu, bi, bu across Q and A. For QA/MC, questions and answers often require different gating/weighting. Please justify sharing vs. using distinct parameters, and consider reporting results for untying these parameters.
- Symmetry and directionality: You use one-directional attention (A attends to Q). A brief comparison to bidirectional alignment (as in Parikh et al.) on at least one dataset would help isolate when one-directional suffices.
- Embeddings: You freeze GloVe. A small experiment on fine-tuning embeddings (or retrofitting/task-tuned subwords) may improve performance and help assess whether the comparison-function results are robust to embedding quality.
- Efficiency: The model is practically appealing. Reporting throughput and parameter counts for each comparison function (especially NN vs. SUB/MULT vs. NTN) would make the trade-offs clearer.

Questions for the authors
1) Attention variant: Can you confirm Eq. 2 is equivalent to Luong “general” attention? If so, please clarify why this choice was preferred over additive attention and whether you observed any empirical differences.
2) Directionality: Did you try symmetric/bidirectional attention or co-attention? If so, how did it affect results and computation?
3) Order modeling: For the long-sequence tasks, have you tried adding lightweight order-aware preprocessing (e.g., 1–2 layer BiLSTM or temporal CNN) on Q and/or A/P? What is the delta, and does it change the ranking of comparison functions?
4) NTN capacity: What are the parameter counts per comparison function and the validation curves? Would a low-rank/factorized NTN (with output dimension m) mitigate overfitting?
5) Preprocessing parameter sharing: Did you experiment with separate Wi/Wu (and biases) for Q vs. A? In QA, did untying help by allowing different gating distributions?
6) Similarity to Mou et al.: Please comment on the relationship between your SUB/MULT features and Mou et al.’s heuristic matching. Any insights on why these element-wise ops are so robust at the aligned token level?
7) Embedding tuning: Did fine-tuning embeddings change the relative performance of comparison functions or close gaps on SNLI?
8) Aggregation choice: Why CNN over simpler pooling (as in Parikh) or RNN aggregation? Any sensitivity to window sizes and depths?

Overall, this is a clear, useful paper that distills a practical recipe for two-text matching and provides valuable empirical guidance on comparison functions. Despite being somewhat incremental relative to prior compare-aggregate models, the breadth of evaluation and the focused analysis justify acceptance.
