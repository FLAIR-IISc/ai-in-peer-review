Summary and contributions
- The paper studies sequence matching via a “compare-aggregate” framework: (i) attend to align token-level representations between two sequences, (ii) compare an attended pair of vectors per token with a chosen comparison function, and (iii) aggregate the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN to predict the label. A lightweight gating “preprocessing” layer reweights tokens, and attention is one-directional.
- The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of comparison functions at the word level (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and SUB+MULT followed by NN). Across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and especially SUBMULT+NN) generally outperform NN/NTN, yielding state-of-the-art or competitive results.
- The paper argues that preserving dimension-wise information (rather than compressing to a scalar similarity) is a good middle ground between rigid metrics (e.g., cosine) and highly flexible NN/NTN.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
- Primary reason: Limited novelty. The overall “compare-aggregate” architecture, attention alignment, and element-wise comparison features have been explored in prior work; the main new contribution is an empirical comparison of comparison functions across datasets.
- Secondary reason: The empirical study, while broad, lacks sufficient rigor to firmly establish the claims (no significance testing, incomplete analysis of fairness to baselines, limited qualitative/error analysis).

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: The paper focuses on text-pair modeling where fine-grained alignment is needed (entailment, answer selection, machine comprehension). This is an important problem. The framing is clear and the tasks chosen are reasonable proxies for “sequence matching.”

2) Motivation and placement in the literature: The compare-aggregate pattern is well-established (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction models). The paper’s focus on the comparison function is interesting. However:
- Element-wise features (difference, product) have been used extensively for sentence pair tasks (e.g., Tai et al., Mou et al.), and cosine/Euclidean/dot-product are common (He & Lin).
- The aggregation via CNN and one-directional attention are incremental design choices; the motivation for CNN vs alternatives (sum, BiLSTM, pooling) is limited.
- The preprocessing gate is order-insensitive for three tasks; this choice is under-motivated given known benefits of contextual encoders.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Strengths: Multi-dataset evaluation; consistent trend that SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) are strong; simple comparisons sometimes beat NN/NTN. Ablation suggests preprocessing and attention both help, with intuitive task-dependent importance.
- Weaknesses:
  - No statistical significance tests; effect sizes are not contextualized (e.g., variance across runs).
  - Baseline fairness: numbers are taken from prior papers; training recipes/hyperparameters differ; it is unclear whether current models use identical resources (embeddings frozen; LSTM used only for SNLI). Parameter counts and training time are not reported.
  - The claim of “state-of-the-art” merits caution without strong, matched baselines, especially on SNLI where many contemporary models exist.
  - Limited qualitative analysis: aside from a brief CNN activation visualization, there is no systematic error analysis or probing of when subtraction vs multiplication helps (e.g., negation, synonymy, lexical overlap, length mismatch).

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not strictly part of the decision)
- Deepen the analysis of comparison functions:
  - Provide controlled studies showing how SUB vs MULT behave under linguistic phenomena (negation, antonyms, paraphrases, lexical overlap, compositionality), length ratios, and OOV rates.
  - Plot performance vs. token overlap and sentence length bins; show per-class performance on SNLI.
  - Report sensitivity to freezing vs fine-tuning embeddings; try contextual encoders (biLSTM/GRU) in the preprocessing layer uniformly across tasks.
- Strengthen empirical rigor:
  - Report mean ± std over multiple runs; conduct significance tests.
  - Ensure baseline fairness by re-implementing strong contemporaries (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM) under the same training/evaluation pipeline.
  - Include parameter counts, training time, and memory footprint; compare compute cost across comparison functions.
- Architecture justification:
  - Justify CNN aggregation vs alternatives (sum/mean pooling, BiLSTM/TreeLSTM) with ablations.
  - Explore bi-directional attention and residual connections in the comparison layer; consider trainable scalings of SUB/MULT or gating between them.
- Qualitative insights:
  - Provide more attention maps and saliency/gradient-based attributions showing which dimensions and tokens drive decisions for different comparison functions.
  - Include error typologies with examples.
- Broaden scope:
  - Evaluate on additional pairwise tasks (e.g., paraphrase identification, STS) to test generality.
  - Consider multi-task training to substantiate the claim of a shared architecture across tasks.

Questions for the authors
- Baseline fairness and significance:
  - Did you run multiple seeds and compute variance and significance? If not, please provide.
  - For each dataset, are the splits and candidate pools exactly matching those of the reported baselines (especially InsuranceQA and WikiQA)? Any filtering/post-processing differences?
- Architecture choices:
  - Why choose one-directional attention and CNN aggregation over bi-directional attention and/or BiLSTM aggregation? Do bi-directional attention or recurrent aggregators change the relative ranking of comparison functions?
  - Why keep embeddings frozen? How do results change when fine-tuning, and do the trends for SUB/MULT persist?
  - For SNLI you add LSTM in preprocessing but not elsewhere; does adding LSTM across all tasks improve results, and does it alter the comparison-function conclusions?
- Comparison functions:
  - How sensitive are SUB/MULT to vector scale and normalization? Did you compare with cosine- or L2-normalized versions of MULT/SUB?
  - Can you report performance when using only SUB or only MULT vs their concatenation with/without an NN, across tasks and with parameter counts controlled?
  - How do comparison functions behave on specific linguistic phenomena (negation, hypernymy, reorderings)? Any targeted evaluation?
- Evaluation details:
  - Please provide parameter counts, training/inference time per example, and memory usage for each comparison function.
  - Did you tune the same hyperparameters for all comparison functions, or per-function tuning? If the latter, how was fairness ensured?
- Reproducibility:
  - Is the released code sufficient to reproduce all tables, including ablations and visualizations? Are there any external preprocessing steps not documented?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and the empirical take-away—that simple element-wise comparisons are strong—is useful, but the novelty is limited and the empirical evidence needs tighter controls and deeper analysis to meet the acceptance bar.
