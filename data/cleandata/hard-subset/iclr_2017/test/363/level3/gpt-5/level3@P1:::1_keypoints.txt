- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework: attention-weighted word alignments, per-pair comparison functions, CNN aggregation for sequence matching tasks.
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN.
- Central empirical finding: simple element-wise operators (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often match or outperform heavier parametric comparators across four datasets.
- Model yields best or near-best results on three QA datasets and competitive performance on SNLI.
- Ablations show both attention and a lightweight gating-based preprocessing layer help; attention is more critical for datasets with disparate sequence lengths.
- Decision: Weak accept, justified by strong empirical evidence and a clear, general framework with careful ablations.
- Tackles fine-grained sequence matching and isolates the often-overlooked design choice of the comparison function.
- Well-motivated in relation to prior compare-aggregate work (match-LSTM, decomposable attention); rationale for similarity-preserving operators is sound.
- Results largely support claims but suffer from lack of unified baseline re-implementation and missing variance/statistical significance reporting.
- Comparisons are partly confounded by unequal representational capacity across comparison functions (e.g., EUCCOS output dimensionality).
- Strength: Clear, modular, and easily adaptable architecture with released code and solid ablations.
- Strength: Practical and impactful insight that simple element-wise comparators can outperform complex parametrized forms.
- Weakness: Incremental novelty—main contribution is empirical rather than a novel architecture.
- Weakness: Limited statistical analysis and fixed embeddings may bias outcomes and interact with comparator choice.
- Weakness: Aggregation choice (CNN) and one-directional attention are not deeply justified relative to alternatives.
- Recommendation: Control for capacity—report parameter counts and FLOPs, and equalize output dimensionality across comparators.
- Recommendation: Report mean/std over multiple seeds, significance tests, learning curves, and early stopping criteria.
- Recommendation: Test fine-tuning embeddings and stronger contextual encoders to verify stability of comparator ranking.
- Recommendation: Compare aggregation methods (CNN vs RNN/biLSTM/pooling) and bi-directional attention to test generality of findings.
- Recommendation: Provide deeper qualitative analysis of the comparison layer (per-dimension behavior, failure cases).
- Recommendation: Report training/inference time, memory usage, and truncation strategies for long sequences like MovieQA plots.
- Recommendation: Re-run or align strong baselines under the same preprocessing and embedding setup where feasible.
- Question: How does performance change when matching output dimensionality across comparison functions and what are parameter counts per variant?
- Question: Does the SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN ranking persist when fine-tuning embeddings or using contextual encoders?
- Question: What is the variance across seeds for best settings and are improvements statistically significant?
- Question: Why use one-directional attention and a CNN aggregator—do SUB/MULT still dominate with bi-directional attention or RNN aggregation?
- Question: On SNLI, how much improvement comes from order-sensitive preprocessing versus the comparison function itself?
- Question: For EUCCOS, did you try learned scaling, temperature, or projections to account for capacity differences?
- Question: What truncation or sampling strategies are used for long inputs (MovieQA), and how sensitive are results to plot length?
- Question: Have you tested cross-domain transfer (e.g., train on SNLI, evaluate on WikiQA) to assess task-agnosticism of learned comparators?
