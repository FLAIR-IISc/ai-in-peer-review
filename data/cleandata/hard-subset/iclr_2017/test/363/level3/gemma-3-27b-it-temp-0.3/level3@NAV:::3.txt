## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences, focusing on the impact of different comparison functions. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), highlighting that element-wise comparison functions can perform surprisingly well. The work is well-motivated, building upon existing compare-aggregate approaches, and provides a thorough empirical analysis of the comparison component.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend acceptance. While the contribution is somewhat incremental, the systematic evaluation of comparison functions and the demonstration of strong performance across multiple tasks are valuable. The paper addresses an important problem in NLP and offers practical insights into model design. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully tackles the problem of sequence matching, a core task in many NLP applications. The proposed framework is clearly presented and logically structured. The empirical results are compelling, consistently showing competitive or state-of-the-art performance. The ablation studies provide further evidence for the importance of the chosen components. The authors’ willingness to share their code is also a positive aspect. The detailed analysis of different comparison functions is a key strength, offering practical guidance for future work.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Word Order Insensitivity:** The authors should explicitly acknowledge the observed word order insensitivity for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI) in the introduction and discussion. This limitation should be discussed in terms of potential future work to address it, perhaps by incorporating positional encoding or recurrent layers.
*   **Attention Mechanism Reference:** The attention mechanism described is more aligned with Luong et al. (2015) than Bahdanau et al. (2014). The authors should cite Luong et al. or a more relevant reference.
*   **NTN Parameter Reduction:** Given the risk of overfitting with the Neural Tensor Network (NTN), the authors should consider experimenting with a lower-dimensional tensor variant (input dim *l*, smaller output dim *m*) to reduce the model's capacity.
*   **SubMultNN Similarity:** The SubMultNN approach resembles the sentence-level matching strategy from Lili Mou et al. (2016). This similarity should be noted in the related work section.
*   **Preprocessing Parameters:** The authors use the same preprocessing parameters for both the question and answer/hypothesis. It would be beneficial to investigate whether different weighting or preprocessing strategies for each sequence could improve performance. Why were these parameters chosen and are they optimal for both sequences?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the reasons for choosing element-wise preprocessing and how it impacts the model's ability to capture contextual information?
2.  Given the observed word order insensitivity, have you considered incorporating positional encoding or recurrent layers to address this limitation? If so, what were the results?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with the different comparison functions, and how does this impact the scalability of the model to very long sequences?
