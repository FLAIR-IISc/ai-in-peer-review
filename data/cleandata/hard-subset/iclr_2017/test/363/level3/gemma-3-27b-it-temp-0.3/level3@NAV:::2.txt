## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching, focusing on the impact of different comparison functions at the word level, followed by CNN-based aggregation. The authors evaluate their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and demonstrate competitive performance compared to existing baselines. The work is well-motivated, building upon prior research in sequence matching and identifying a gap in systematic evaluation of comparison functions. The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the proposed model and experimental setup.

**Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper. The work addresses a relevant problem in NLP – sequence matching – and provides a thorough empirical investigation of a key component within existing compare-aggregate frameworks: the word-level comparison function. The extensive experiments across multiple datasets strengthen the validity of the findings. 

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully demonstrates that simple, element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. This is a significant finding that challenges the assumption that more complex models are always superior. The comprehensive experimental evaluation, including ablation studies, provides strong support for the claims made by the authors. The consistent performance gains observed across different datasets suggest the robustness and generalizability of the proposed approach. The inclusion of diverse datasets – ranging from question answering to textual entailment – is a strength, highlighting the broad applicability of the framework.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, clarifying the role of the preprocessing and attention layers would be beneficial. The authors mention their importance in the results section, but a more detailed explanation of *why* these components contribute to performance would be valuable. Specifically, **I would like to see results presented without the preprocessing and attention layers to quantify their individual impact.** This would provide a clearer understanding of the relative contribution of each component to the overall performance. 

Figure 2 is difficult to interpret, especially in print. The visual representation of the CNN activations needs to be improved for clarity. Consider using a heatmap with a more distinct color scale or highlighting the most salient activations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind using element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as comparison functions? What specific properties of these operations make them well-suited for capturing semantic similarity at the word level?
2.  The paper mentions the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive. Does this limitation impact performance on tasks where word order is crucial? If so, could you discuss potential modifications to the preprocessing layer to address this issue?
3.  What is the computational cost associated with the different comparison functions? Are there any significant trade-offs between performance and computational efficiency?
