## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

This paper presents a compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching tasks, focusing on the impact of different comparison functions within that framework. The authors systematically evaluate six comparison functions – including element-wise subtraction and multiplication – across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). They demonstrate that element-wise operations often outperform more complex methods like neural tensor networks, highlighting the effectiveness of a surprisingly simple approach. The work is well-motivated by the prevalence of sequence matching in NLP and builds upon existing compare-aggregate models.

**Reject.** While the paper presents solid empirical results, the contribution feels incremental. The core idea of exploring different comparison functions within a compare-aggregate framework isn’t entirely novel, and the observed benefits of element-wise operations, while interesting, don't represent a significant breakthrough. The paper primarily *validates* existing approaches rather than introducing a fundamentally new one.

The paper adequately addresses the question of how different comparison functions impact performance in a compare-aggregate model. The approach is well-motivated, building on established work in sequence matching and acknowledging limitations of prior studies. The results are supported by experiments on four datasets, with clear comparisons to baselines. The statistical significance of the improvements isn’t explicitly stated, but the consistent performance across datasets lends credibility to the claims. However, the lack of a truly novel contribution weakens the overall impact.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper would be significantly strengthened by a qualitative analysis. Showing *why* subtraction and multiplication work well – perhaps through attention weight visualizations or examples of specific sentence pairs where these functions excel – would provide valuable insight. It would also be helpful to explore the interaction between the preprocessing layer and the comparison function. Does the benefit of subtraction/multiplication vary depending on the preprocessing method? Furthermore, the paper could benefit from a discussion of the limitations of element-wise operations. Are there scenarios where they are likely to underperform?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide examples of sentence pairs where subtraction or multiplication clearly outperform other comparison functions, and explain *why* this might be the case?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Did you experiment with different embeddings, and if so, how did they affect the relative performance of the comparison functions?
3.  The paper mentions the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive for some tasks. Could you elaborate on why this is beneficial in those cases, and whether order-sensitivity would be expected to improve performance on other tasks?
