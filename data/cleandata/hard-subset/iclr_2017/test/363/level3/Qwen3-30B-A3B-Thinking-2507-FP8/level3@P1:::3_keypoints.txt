- Proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching with systematic evaluation of word-level comparison functions.  
- Framework: preprocessing layer, attention-based alignment, comparison function for aligned tokens, CNN aggregation.  
- Main finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex neural comparison functions across tasks.  
- Empirical evaluation on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) shows state-of-the-art or competitive results.  
- SUBMULT+NN is the most generally effective comparison function; SUB and MULT are computationally efficient and parameter-free.  
- Ablation studies show attention and preprocessing layers are important to performance.  
- Results are statistically significant and code is available, supporting reproducibility.  
- Recommendation: accept, based on meaningful contribution and strong empirical evidence across diverse datasets.  
- Limitation: preprocessing layer is order-insensitive for longer sequences, which may cap model performance and should be highlighted.  
- Attention implementation is closer to Luong et al. (2015); that citation should be added.  
- NTN has many parameters and risks overfitting; consider a smaller tensor variant to reduce capacity.  
- SUBMULT+NN resembles Mou et al. (2016)'s sentence-level matching; authors should acknowledge and clarify differences.  
- Preprocessing parameters are shared for question/answer but may need task-specific weighting—investigate separate parameters.  
- Question 1: Clarify comparison to Wang et al. (2016) IARNN-Gate given slightly higher reported accuracy (85.2%) than this paper's 84.5% on InsuranceQA.  
- Question 2: Explain how an order-insensitive preprocessing layer still yields superior MovieQA performance and what aspects of the comparison function enable this.  
- Question 3: Did you tune dropout per dataset (e.g., smaller WikiQA), and if so, how did dataset-specific dropout affect results?
