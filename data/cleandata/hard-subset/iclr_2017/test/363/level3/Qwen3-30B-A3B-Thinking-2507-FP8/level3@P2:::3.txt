

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, focusing on the critical comparison component that operates on word-level representations. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations SUB and MULT) across four diverse NLP tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. Their key contribution is demonstrating that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches, with the SUBMULT+NN combination achieving the best overall results. The work provides a thorough empirical analysis that significantly advances our understanding of sequence matching.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a systematic analysis of comparison functions in the "compare-aggregate" framework, demonstrating that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural approaches. While the overall framework builds on prior work, the thorough empirical investigation across multiple datasets provides meaningful insights for the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses an important NLP problem with a well-motivated approach. The authors clearly identify the gap in prior work regarding the systematic evaluation of comparison functions, which is a critical component of sequence matching models. Their empirical evaluation across four different datasets is comprehensive and convincing, showing that simple comparison functions can achieve state-of-the-art results on multiple tasks. The paper makes a clear contribution to the understanding of sequence matching by demonstrating that the comparison function is a key design choice that can significantly impact performance.

The paper is timely and relevant, addressing a fundamental NLP problem with a straightforward approach that yields good results. The authors provide sufficient evidence to support their claims, with results consistently showing that their element-wise comparison functions outperform more complex neural network approaches across multiple datasets. The code is also made publicly available, enhancing reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper should explicitly acknowledge the limitation that the model is insensitive to word order for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), which empirically limits its upper-bound performance. This limitation should be discussed in the introduction and discussion.
- The attention mechanism should be more clearly positioned relative to Luong et al. (2015) rather than being described as "general attention." The model appears closer to Luong's bilinear scoring approach.
- Consider addressing potential overfitting in NTN by using a smaller tensor variant (input dimension l, smaller output dimension m) to reduce model capacity.
- Note the similarity between SubMultNN and Lili Mou et al.'s sentence-level matching strategy, and clarify the key differences.
- Explain why the same preprocessing parameters are used for both questions and answers, as these may require different weighting for optimal performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The model is described as being order-insensitive due to the preprocessing layer. How does this affect the model's performance compared to more order-sensitive approaches, particularly for longer sequences?
2. The attention mechanism is described as "general attention" but appears to follow Luong et al. (2015) more closely than Bahdanau et al. How do you position your attention mechanism relative to these prior works, and why is it described as "general"?
3. Given the large number of parameters in NTN, could you explore a variant with reduced capacity (e.g., smaller output dimension) to prevent potential overfitting?
4. How does your SubMultNN comparison function relate to Mou et al.'s sentence-level matching strategy, and what are the key differences in implementation or performance?
5. Why were the same preprocessing parameters used for both questions and answers? Would different parameters for each improve performance, and if so, why weren't they explored?
