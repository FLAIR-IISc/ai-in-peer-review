

**Review**

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, focusing on systematic evaluation of different comparison functions between word-level representations. The authors present a model that uses soft attention for token alignment, various comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations SUB and MULT), and CNN aggregation for final classification. They evaluate their approach across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions often outperform more complex neural network-based approaches.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: (1) The paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple tasks, revealing that simple element-wise operations can be highly effective; (2) The empirical analysis is thorough and well-executed, showing consistent improvements over baselines on three of four datasets.

The paper makes a reasonable contribution that is timely and relevant to sequence matching problems in NLP. While the incremental nature of the work is borderline (building on Parikh et al. and the authors' prior work), the systematic evaluation across multiple datasets and the surprising effectiveness of simple comparison functions provide sufficient novelty.

To improve the paper, please address the following: (1) Note in the introduction/discussion that the model is insensitive to word order for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), which empirically limits performance; (2) Cite Luong et al. (2015) for the attention mechanism, which more closely resembles their bilinear scoring approach than Bahdanau et al.; (3) Address the potential overfitting risk of NTN by suggesting a reduced-capacity tensor variant (input dim l, output dim m); (4) Acknowledge the similarity between SubMultNN and Mou et al.'s sentence-level matching strategy; (5) Clarify why the same preprocessing parameters are used for both sequences, as question and answer may require different weighting.
