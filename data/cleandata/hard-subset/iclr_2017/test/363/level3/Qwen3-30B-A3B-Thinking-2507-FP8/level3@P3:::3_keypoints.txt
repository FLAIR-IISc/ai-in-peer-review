- Paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework using soft attention, various comparison functions, and CNN aggregation for sequence matching.  
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) outperform more complex neural comparators across four datasets.  
- Contributions: systematic evaluation of six comparison functions and strong empirical results across MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Decision: accept with minor revisions.  
- Paper is well-motivated, situates itself relative to prior compare-aggregate work, and provides thorough experiments supporting claims.  
- Contribution is valuable but somewhat incremental relative to prior work; breadth of datasets justifies acceptance.  
- Revise citation: attention mechanism aligns more with Luong et al. (2015) than Bahdanau et al. (2014).  
- Acknowledge similarity between SubMultNN and Mou et al. (2016) sentence-level matching.  
- Discuss limitation: preprocessing layer makes model order-insensitive for longer sequences, lowering upper-bound performance for tasks except SNLI.  
- NTN risk: large parameter count may cause overfitting; consider lower-capacity LLM tensor variant to reduce parameters.  
- Justify or reconsider using identical preprocessing parameters for both sequences (questions vs. answers) and discuss potential benefits of task-specific parameters.  
- Ask authors to explain why performance is weaker on SNLI versus other datasets.  
- Request insight on why attention matters more for longer sequences while preprocessing matters more for shorter ones, and whether this generalizes.  
- Ask whether using task-specific preprocessing parameters (e.g., different gates) would improve performance.
