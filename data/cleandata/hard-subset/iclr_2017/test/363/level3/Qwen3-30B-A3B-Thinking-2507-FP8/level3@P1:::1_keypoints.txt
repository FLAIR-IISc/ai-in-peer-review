- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework for matching text sequences across multiple NLP tasks (QA, entailment).
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, focusing on element-wise operations (SUB, MULT).
- Demonstrates strong performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI; SUB and MULT often outperform NN/NTN.
- Main contribution is empirical validation that a general compare-aggregate framework and simple element-wise ops work well across tasks.
- Recommendation: reject — paper judged incremental rather than innovative.
- Framework closely follows prior work (Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b); SUB/MULT previously used (Mou et al., 2016).
- Authors fail to clearly demonstrate how their framework differs from or improves upon existing compare-aggregate approaches.
- Empirical evaluation is thorough but lacks theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work better.
- Paper does not show whether element-wise ops are capturing the same information as complex models in a different form.
- Ablation studies show attention/preprocessing matter but do not explain why element-wise comparisons are especially effective.
- Needs deeper analysis: qualitative examples of where different comparison functions succeed/fail and links between attention visualizations and performance.
- Poor positioning against related work; not clearly differentiated from decomposable attention or pairwise word interaction models.
- Suggested improvements: add theory for element-wise efficacy, qualitative examples, clearer differentiation from prior work, and discuss limitations/failure cases.
- Two specific author questions: Why do SUB/MULT outperform neural networks (alignment with semantics vs reduced overfitting)? Why does EUCCOS perform well on MovieQA despite being standard similarity measures?
