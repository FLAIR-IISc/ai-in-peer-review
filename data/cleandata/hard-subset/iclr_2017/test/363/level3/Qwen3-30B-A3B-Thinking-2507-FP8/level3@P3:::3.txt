

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary
This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, which involves aligning tokens between two sequences using soft attention, applying various comparison functions to the aligned token pairs, and aggregating the comparison results using CNNs. The authors make a significant contribution by systematically analyzing six different comparison functions, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that outperform standard neural network approaches across four different datasets. The paper demonstrates that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can achieve competitive or superior results compared to more complex neural network-based approaches. The work is timely and relevant, addressing an important NLP problem with a straightforward yet effective solution that yields good empirical results across multiple tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically exploring the comparison component in sequence matching models, which has been under-explored in previous work. The authors test their framework across four different NLP tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), demonstrating the general applicability of their approach. Their empirical analysis reveals that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches, which is a significant insight for the community.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing its contribution from prior work. While the overall framework builds on previous "compare-aggregate" models (Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016), the authors make a compelling case for the importance of systematically evaluating comparison functions, which has not been adequately addressed before. The experimental results are thorough and support the authors' claims about the effectiveness of their approach.

The paper's contributions are sufficient for acceptance despite being borderline incremental relative to prior work. The authors provide detailed empirical analysis across multiple datasets, which is more comprehensive than previous studies that typically test on one or two tasks. The results consistently show that the proposed model achieves competitive or superior performance to state-of-the-art approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further improve the paper, the authors should address the following points:

1. The attention mechanism described in the paper is more closely aligned with Luong et al.'s (2015) general scoring approach rather than Bahdanau et al.'s (2014) additive scoring. The authors should cite Luong et al. (2015) as the more relevant reference for their attention mechanism.

2. The authors should acknowledge the similarity between their SubMultNN model and the sentence-level matching strategy proposed by Mou et al. (2016). This would provide better context for their contribution within the literature.

3. The paper should explicitly note that for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI), the model is insensitive to word order due to the order-insensitive preprocessing layer (Equation 1). This limitation should be discussed in the introduction and discussion sections, as it empirically limits the model's upper-bound performance.

4. The NTN component risks overfitting due to its large number of parameters. The authors could reduce capacity by using an LLM tensor variant (input dimension l, smaller output dimension m) as suggested in the key points. This would make the model more efficient while maintaining performance.

5. The authors should address why the same preprocessing parameters are used for questions and answers. Given that these components may require different weighting, the paper would benefit from a discussion of this design choice and potentially exploring task-specific preprocessing parameters.

## Questions for Authors
1. In Table 3, the model achieves the best performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, but is only competitive on SNLI. What specific characteristics of the SNLI dataset might explain this difference in performance compared to the other datasets?

2. The paper notes that for longer sequences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), the attention layer plays a more important role, while for shorter sequences (WikiQA, SNLI), the preprocessing layer is more important. Could the authors provide more insight into why this difference exists, and whether this pattern holds across other sequence matching tasks?

3. The authors use the same preprocessing parameters for both sequences. Given that questions and answers likely have different semantic characteristics, would using task-specific preprocessing parameters (e.g., different gate values) improve performance?
