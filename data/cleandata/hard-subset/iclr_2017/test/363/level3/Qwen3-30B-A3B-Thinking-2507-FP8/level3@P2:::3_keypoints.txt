- Proposes a compare-aggregate framework focusing on word-level comparison and evaluates six comparison functions (including SUB and MULT).  
- Empirically evaluated across four datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Finds simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform complex neural approaches; SUBMULT+NN performs best.  
- Provides a thorough empirical analysis and releases code for reproducibility.  
- Decision: Accept — valuable systematic analysis despite building on prior work.  
- Main contribution: demonstrates the comparison function is a critical design choice that significantly affects sequence-matching performance.  
- Limitation: model is order-insensitive for longer sequences (all tasks except SNLI) and this upper-bounds performance — should be acknowledged and discussed.  
- Attention mechanism should be positioned relative to Luong et al. (2015) (bilinear scoring) rather than labeled as "general attention."  
- Suggest reducing NTN capacity (e.g., smaller tensor/output dimension) to mitigate potential overfitting.  
- Note similarity between SubMultNN and Mou et al.'s sentence-level matching; clarify distinctions.  
- Question: How does order-insensitivity from preprocessing affect performance vs. order-sensitive approaches for longer sequences?  
- Question: Why describe the attention mechanism as "general" when it appears closer to Luong et al. than Bahdanau et al.?  
- Question: Can you explore NTN variants with reduced output dimension to prevent overfitting?  
- Question: How does SubMultNN relate to Mou et al.'s matching strategy in implementation and performance?  
- Question: Why use the same preprocessing parameters for questions and answers; would separate/hyperparameterized preprocessing improve results?
