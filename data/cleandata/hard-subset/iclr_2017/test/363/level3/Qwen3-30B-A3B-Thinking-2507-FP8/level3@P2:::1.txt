

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a systematic investigation of the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in natural language processing. The authors propose a general model that first compares word-level representations between sequences using various comparison functions, followed by aggregation through convolutional neural networks. The key contribution lies in the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions (including neural network-based approaches and novel element-wise operations), demonstrating that simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication often outperform more complex neural network-based methods across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The work effectively establishes the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence matching tasks, providing valuable empirical evidence for practitioners in the field.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating different comparison functions in a "compare-aggregate" framework, showing that simple element-wise operations can be highly effective for sequence matching problems. The work is well-motivated, well-executed, and provides useful insights that could guide future research in sequence matching.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper effectively addresses two important limitations in previous work: (1) most sequence matching models have been tested on only one or two specific tasks, and (2) the comparison function used has not been systematically evaluated. By testing their framework across four different datasets representing four distinct tasks, the authors convincingly demonstrate that the compare-aggregate framework is broadly applicable. More importantly, their systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions provides compelling evidence that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural network-based approaches, which is a significant insight for the field.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines and evaluation metrics for each task. The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results across all four datasets, with particularly strong performance on InsuranceQA where it outperforms all previous models. The ablation studies further strengthen the claims by demonstrating the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers in the framework.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper makes a solid contribution, there are opportunities to enhance its impact. The authors could include qualitative analysis showing how different comparison functions behave on varied sentence types (e.g., contrasting examples where SUB works well versus where it doesn't). This would help readers better understand the strengths and limitations of each comparison function. Additionally, the paper mentions that their order-insensitive preprocessing layer still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive layers, but doesn't explore this finding in depth. A brief discussion of why this might be the case would be valuable, especially for tasks where word order might be expected to matter.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper demonstrates that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform well across multiple datasets, but the authors don't provide much insight into why these simple operations work so well. Could you elaborate on what specific semantic relationships these operations capture that are particularly relevant for sequence matching?

2. The authors note that EUCCOS (Euclidean distance + cosine similarity) performs nearly as well as the best comparison function on MovieQA. Could you provide specific examples from this dataset where EUCCOS works particularly well or where it fails compared to SUB or MULT?

3. The paper mentions that the attention layer plays a more important role in tasks with significant length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) while the preprocessing layer is more important for tasks with smaller length differences (WikiQA, SNLI). Could you elaborate on why this pattern emerges, and whether it might generalize to other sequence matching tasks?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the literature on sequence matching, providing both empirical evidence and practical insights about the comparison function component of "compare-aggregate" models. The systematic approach to evaluating different comparison functions across multiple datasets is particularly compelling, and the findings that simple element-wise operations can be highly effective will likely inform future work in this area.
