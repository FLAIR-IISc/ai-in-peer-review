

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which performs word-level matching followed by aggregation using convolutional neural networks. The key contribution is the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions for matching word vectors, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that outperform standard neural network approaches across four diverse NLP tasks: MovieQA (machine comprehension), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (question answering), and SNLI (textual entailment). The authors demonstrate that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can achieve better results than more complex neural network-based approaches, while maintaining strong performance across multiple datasets. The paper is well-written, presents thorough experiments with comprehensive comparisons to state-of-the-art baselines, and provides valuable insights into the design of sequence matching models.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to the sequence matching literature by systematically evaluating comparison functions in the "compare-aggregate" framework, demonstrating that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches across multiple NLP tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses two important limitations of previous work: (1) previous models were tested on limited tasks, and (2) previous work did not systematically evaluate comparison functions. By testing on four different datasets representing different sequence matching problems, the authors provide strong evidence that the "compare-aggregate" framework is broadly effective. More importantly, their systematic evaluation of six comparison functions provides valuable insights into what types of operations work best for sequence matching.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with comprehensive comparisons to multiple baselines across four datasets using appropriate metrics (accuracy for classification tasks, MAP and MRR for ranking tasks). The paper demonstrates that their model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on all four datasets, with the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperforming more complex approaches. The ablation studies further support the importance of key components in the framework.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, clearly distinguishing its contributions from prior work on "compare-aggregate" frameworks while building on established techniques. The motivation for exploring element-wise operations is well-justified by the nature of sequence matching tasks, where semantic similarity is the primary concern.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is well-written and makes a solid contribution, but a few improvements could enhance its clarity and impact:

- The preprocessing layer is described as "order-insensitive," yet the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. It would be helpful to include a brief explanation of why the word-by-word comparison is sufficient despite losing contextual information, or to include results without the preprocessing layer to clarify its necessity.

- Figure 2 is difficult to read in print format, particularly the small text and color scheme. Please improve the figure's quality and readability for publication.

- There is a minor typo: "including a some" on page 4, line 5 should be "including some."

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To better understand the contribution and ensure confidence in the assessment, I would like clarification on the following points:

1. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive" and that the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Could you provide results without the preprocessing layer to clarify its contribution? This would help distinguish whether the performance gain comes from the word-level matching itself or from the specific preprocessing approach.

2. The attention layer is described as "one-directional." How does this differ from the two-directional attention used in previous work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016), and why is one-directional attention sufficient for the tasks evaluated?

3. The paper claims that element-wise comparison functions work better than neural network-based approaches. Given that neural networks are generally more expressive, could you provide more insight into why these simpler operations work better for sequence matching tasks? Is this specific to the NLP domain, or would similar results be observed in other domains?

The authors have made a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature by systematically evaluating comparison functions and demonstrating their effectiveness across multiple tasks. With the minor clarifications requested, this paper would make a strong contribution to ICLR 2017.
