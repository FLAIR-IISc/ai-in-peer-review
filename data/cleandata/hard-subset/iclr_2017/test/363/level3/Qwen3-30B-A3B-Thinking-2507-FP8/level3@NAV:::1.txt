

**Review**

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching tasks, focusing on systematically evaluating different comparison functions between word-level representations. The authors demonstrate that simple element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication) consistently outperform more complex neural network-based comparison methods across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), while maintaining a general framework applicable to multiple sequence matching tasks. The work provides a valuable empirical analysis that helps clarify which comparison mechanisms are most effective for semantic matching.

**Decision:** Reject

**Key reasons:** The work is incremental with limited novelty, and the empirical evaluation lacks sufficient depth to justify the claims.

**Supporting arguments:** While the systematic comparison across multiple datasets is a positive contribution, the core framework (compare-aggregate) and the basic comparison functions tested are not novel. The paper builds directly on established work by Parikh et al. (2016) and He & Lin (2016), with the main innovation being the evaluation of element-wise operations—already noted in prior work by Mou et al. (2016). The performance improvements reported are marginal compared to state-of-the-art baselines, and the paper fails to establish why these simple operations work better, limiting theoretical contribution. The lack of qualitative analysis to explain *why* subtraction/multiplication outperform other methods further weakens the scientific rigor.

**Additional feedback:** To strengthen future work, include qualitative analysis showing how different comparison functions behave on specific sentence types (e.g., negations, idioms, long vs. short sentences) to provide deeper insights into their relative strengths. Also, clarify how the framework handles cases where the element-wise operations might fail.

**Questions for authors:** 
1. How do you explain the consistent outperformance of element-wise operations despite their simplicity, especially when compared to neural tensor networks that have been shown effective in similar tasks?
2. Could you provide more details on the implementation of the preprocessing layer and how it specifically contributes to the model's effectiveness, given that it's order-insensitive yet still outperforms order-sensitive baselines?
