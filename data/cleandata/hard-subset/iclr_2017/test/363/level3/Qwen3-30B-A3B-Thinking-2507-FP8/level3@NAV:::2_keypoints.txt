- Proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework and systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions for sequence matching in NLP.  
- Finds simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural comparison functions across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Fills a literature gap by empirically validating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence-matching tasks.  
- Recommends acceptance with minor revisions due to a valuable, well-motivated empirical analysis.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with strong baselines and appropriate metrics supporting the claims.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: (1) systematic multi-task evaluation of comparison functions, (2) scientifically rigorous results with well-chosen baselines.  
- Minor revision requests: correct typo on page 4, line 5 ("including a some" → "including some").  
- Minor revision requests: clarify the specific benefit of preprocessing and attention by reporting results without these components (ablation).  
- Minor revision requests: improve Figure 2 for better print readability.  
- Asks for clarification why the order-insensitive preprocessing layer performs well despite not capturing context, compared to order-sensitive approaches.  
- Requests ablation results to quantify the claimed importance of word-level matching by showing performance when key components are removed.
