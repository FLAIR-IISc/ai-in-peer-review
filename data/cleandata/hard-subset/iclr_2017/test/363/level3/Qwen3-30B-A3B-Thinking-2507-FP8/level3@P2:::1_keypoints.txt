- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" model that compares word-level representations with various comparison functions and aggregates via CNNs.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including neural and novel element-wise operations.  
- Shows simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform complex neural comparison functions.  
- Demonstrates applicability across four diverse datasets/tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Empirical results are strong and competitive, achieving state-of-the-art on InsuranceQA.  
- Paper addresses prior gaps: most models tested on few tasks and comparison functions not systematically evaluated.  
- Evaluation is rigorous with appropriate baselines, metrics, and ablation studies confirming importance of preprocessing and attention.  
- Suggests adding qualitative analyses showing when different comparison functions succeed or fail.  
- Recommends deeper discussion of why an order-insensitive preprocessing layer can outperform order-sensitive layers.  
- Asks authors to explain why SUB and MULT capture effective semantic relationships for matching.  
- Requests examples where EUCCOS succeeds or fails compared to SUB/MULT, and clarification on why attention matters more for long-length-difference tasks while preprocessing matters for short ones.
