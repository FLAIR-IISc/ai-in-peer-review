

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences that systematically evaluates different comparison functions at the word level. The authors demonstrate that simple element-wise operations (SUB: element-wise subtraction, and MULT: element-wise multiplication) can outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions across four diverse NLP tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The framework consists of a preprocessing layer, attention mechanism for alignment, comparison function for matching aligned tokens, and CNN for aggregation. The authors make a strong case that this general framework is effective across multiple sequence matching problems, with their model achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on all four datasets.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper makes a meaningful contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions for sequence matching, revealing that simple element-wise operations often outperform more complex neural network approaches; and (2) the empirical evaluation across four diverse datasets provides strong evidence for the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework.

The paper's contributions are well-supported by thorough empirical analysis. The authors test six different comparison functions across four distinct tasks, showing that SUB and MULT achieve the best results on multiple datasets, with SUBMULT+NN being the most generally effective. Their findings that simple element-wise operations can outperform standard neural network approaches (NN and NTN) are particularly noteworthy, as these comparison functions are computationally efficient and require no additional parameters. The ablation studies demonstrating the importance of the attention and preprocessing layers further strengthen their claims. The results are statistically significant, with the model outperforming all baselines on InsuranceQA and achieving competitive results on the other datasets.

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and makes a timely contribution to the growing literature on sequence matching. The empirical analysis is comprehensive, and the code availability enhances reproducibility. The comparison with prior work, particularly Parikh et al. (2016) and Wang & Jiang (2016b), is appropriate and well-placed in the literature.

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. The authors note that their model is insensitive to word order for longer sequences (MovieQA and InsuranceQA), which they attribute to the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive. This limitation should be more prominently discussed in the introduction and discussion sections, as it represents a fundamental constraint on the model's upper-bound performance.

2. The attention mechanism is closer to Luong et al. (2015)'s bilinear scoring than to Bahdanau et al.'s attention. The authors should cite Luong et al. (2015) as the more relevant reference for their attention mechanism.

3. The NTN comparison function has many parameters and risks overfitting. The authors should consider using a smaller tensor variant (e.g., input dimension l, output dimension m < l) to reduce capacity and potentially improve performance.

4. The SUBMULT+NN comparison function resembles the sentence-level matching strategy from Mou et al. (2016). The authors should acknowledge this similarity and clarify how their approach differs.

5. The preprocessing parameters are shared between question and answer sequences, but these may require different weighting. The authors should investigate whether task-specific preprocessing parameters would improve performance.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper claims that the model achieves state-of-the-art results on InsuranceQA, but the reported accuracy (84.5%) is slightly lower than the 85.2% reported in Wang et al. (2016) for IARNN-Gate. Could you clarify how your results compare to this baseline?
2. For the MovieQA dataset, the authors report that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive yet still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. What specific aspects of the comparison function allow this to happen, and how might this inform future work on sequence matching?
3. The authors use the same dropout rate across all four datasets. Given that WikiQA is significantly smaller than the others, did you experiment with different dropout rates for different datasets, and if so, how did this affect performance?
