- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, systematically evaluating word-level comparison functions.  
- Model architecture: compare word-level representations from two sequences with various functions, then aggregate comparisons via a CNN for final classification.  
- Key contribution: systematic exploration of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions.  
- Evaluated on four diverse tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results.  
- Shows attention layer is more critical for sequences with large length differences; preprocessing matters more for sequences with small length differences.  
- Recommendation: accept — contribution is significant and experimental evaluation is thorough and convincing.  
- Suggested improvement: include ablation results removing preprocessing and attention to clarify their specific contributions.  
- Presentation issue: Figure 2 (attention visualization) is low resolution and hard to read; improve figure clarity.  
- Minor typo noted: page 4, line 5 ("including a some" → "including some").  
- Positive note: authors made code publicly available.  
- Reviewer questions: why does order-insensitive preprocessing work well (e.g., MovieQA), and why does the model lag EBIM+TreeLSTM on SNLI?
