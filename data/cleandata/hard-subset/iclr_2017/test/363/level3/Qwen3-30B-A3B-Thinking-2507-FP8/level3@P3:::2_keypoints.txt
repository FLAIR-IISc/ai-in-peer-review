- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework performing word-level comparisons followed by CNN-based aggregation, testing six comparison functions including novel SUB and MULT.  
- Evaluates the model on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Shows simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) can outperform more complex neural approaches.  
- Contribution: systematic analysis of comparison functions and demonstration of framework effectiveness across multiple sequence-matching tasks.  
- Decision: Accept — motivated by a meaningful problem and strong empirical validation across datasets.  
- Gap identified: prior work lacked systematic, multi-task evaluation of compare-aggregate models.  
- Empirical strength: outperforms previous approaches on three of four datasets, with particularly strong results on InsuranceQA.  
- Finding significance: element-wise operations’ strong performance is surprising and suggests a promising direction for future research.  
- Paper quality: well-written, clear structure, strong related-work connection.  
- Ablation: authors provide some ablation on preprocessing and attention, but the benefits of these components are not fully articulated.  
- Typo: fix "including a some" to "including some."  
- Request to add ablations removing preprocessing and attention entirely to isolate their contributions.  
- Visualization: improve Figure 2 (attention-weight visualization) for readability, especially in print.  
- Discussion: elaborate on why order-insensitive preprocessing still outperforms order-sensitive approaches.  
- Clarifying questions requested: report performance without preprocessing and without attention separately.  
- Clarifying questions requested: explain why element-wise comparison functions outperform neural-network-based approaches.  
- Clarifying questions requested: discuss whether fine-tuning GloVe embeddings would change results and why fixed embeddings were used/preferred.
