

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences across multiple NLP tasks including question answering and textual entailment. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, with a particular focus on element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication). The model demonstrates strong performance across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), with element-wise comparison functions outperforming neural network-based approaches in most cases. The paper makes a valuable contribution by empirically validating that a general compare-aggregate framework can be effectively applied across multiple sequence matching tasks, and that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches for this specific purpose.

I recommend rejection. The paper is incremental rather than innovative. While the systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple datasets is commendable, the framework itself follows closely from existing work (Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b), and the element-wise operations tested (SUB and MULT) have been previously used in related work (Mou et al., 2016). The authors do not sufficiently demonstrate how their framework differs from or improves upon existing compare-aggregate approaches beyond the specific comparison functions they evaluate. The main contribution is a systematic evaluation of existing methods rather than a novel approach.

The empirical evaluation is well-executed and thorough, with results clearly showing that SUB and MULT generally outperform NN and NTN across the four datasets. However, the paper fails to provide sufficient theoretical justification for why these element-wise operations work better, nor does it adequately explain the relationship between the comparison functions and the semantic matching task. The paper mentions that these simple operations work better than neural networks, but doesn't provide evidence that they're not simply capturing the same information as more complex models in a different form. The ablation studies show that the attention and preprocessing layers are important, but don't provide insight into why the element-wise comparisons are particularly effective.

The paper would benefit from more in-depth analysis of the comparison functions. For instance, the authors note that "some simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can work better than standard neural network and neural tensor network," but don't explain why. A qualitative analysis showing how these comparison functions behave on specific sentence pairs (e.g., where semantic similarity is high but word overlap is low) would provide valuable insights. The paper's Figure 2 shows some visualization of attention, but doesn't connect these visualizations to the comparison functions' performance.

Additionally, the paper should more clearly position itself against related work. While the authors mention the "compare-aggregate" framework, they don't sufficiently distinguish their model from existing approaches like Parikh et al.'s decomposable attention model or He & Lin's pairwise word interaction model. The paper states that their "model can be applied to different tasks" but doesn't demonstrate how their approach is more general or flexible than existing frameworks.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1. Adding a more detailed theoretical justification for why element-wise operations might work better for semantic matching tasks
2. Including qualitative examples showing how different comparison functions handle various semantic matching scenarios
3. Providing a clearer differentiation between this work and existing compare-aggregate models
4. Discussing limitations of the element-wise approaches (when do they fail compared to neural networks?)

I have two specific questions for the authors:
1. Given that the element-wise comparison functions SUB and MULT are simpler than neural networks, why do they work better for sequence matching tasks? Is it because they're better aligned with the semantic similarity metric, or is it due to other factors like reduced overfitting?
2. How do the authors explain the success of EUCCOS (which uses Euclidean distance and cosine similarity) on MovieQA, when these are typically used as similarity measures rather than as part of a comparison function in neural models?
