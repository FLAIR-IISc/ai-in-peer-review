

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, focusing on systematic evaluation of different word-level comparison functions. The authors present a model that first compares word-level representations from two sequences using various functions, then aggregates these comparisons with a CNN for final classification. The key contribution is the systematic exploration of six different comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with empirical evidence showing that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across four diverse sequence matching tasks: MovieQA (machine comprehension), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (question answering), and SNLI (textual entailment).

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by demonstrating that a general "compare-aggregate" framework can be effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, and by providing systematic evidence that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches. The experimental evaluation across four different datasets with extensive comparisons to published baselines is thorough and convincing.

The supporting arguments for acceptance are strong. First, the paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by systematically evaluating different comparison functions, which previous "compare-aggregate" models have largely overlooked. The authors demonstrate that their framework achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on all four datasets, with the SUB and MULT comparison functions consistently performing well. Second, the paper provides valuable empirical insights about the relative importance of different components in the framework - for instance, showing that the attention layer is more critical for sequences with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), while the preprocessing layer matters more for sequences with smaller length differences (WikiQA, SNLI). This nuanced understanding of the framework's components is particularly valuable for future research.

For further improvement, the authors should clarify the benefit of their preprocessing and attention steps. Specifically, the paper would be strengthened by including results without the preprocessing and attention layers to demonstrate their specific contribution to performance. Additionally, Figure 2, which visualizes the attention mechanism, is difficult to read in print due to poor resolution and small text size - this figure should be improved for better clarity.

The paper is well-written and organized, though there is a minor typo on page 4, line 5 ("including a some" should be "including some"). The authors have also made their code publicly available, which is commendable.

To better understand the model's performance, I have two specific questions: 
1) The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Could the authors elaborate on why the order-insensitive approach works well, especially for tasks like MovieQA where word order might seem important? 
2) For the SNLI dataset, the model achieves competitive but not state-of-the-art results. How does the authors explain the performance gap between their model and the current state-of-the-art (EBIM+TreeLSTM) on this particular dataset, and what might be the key differences in their approaches that lead to this gap?
