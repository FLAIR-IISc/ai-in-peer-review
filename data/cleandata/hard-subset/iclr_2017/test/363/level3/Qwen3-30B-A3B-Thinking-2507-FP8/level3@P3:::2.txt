

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences that performs word-level matching followed by CNN-based aggregation. The authors systematically investigate six different comparison functions for matching word representations, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that have not been widely used in previous work. They evaluate their model across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) representing different sequence matching tasks, demonstrating that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper makes a clear contribution by showing that the compare-aggregate framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks and by providing a systematic analysis of comparison functions that can guide future research.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

The key reasons for this decision are: (1) The paper addresses a meaningful problem in NLP with strong empirical validation across multiple datasets, and (2) The authors provide a thorough, systematic comparison of different comparison functions that yields interesting and potentially impactful findings.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper clearly identifies a gap in the literature: previous compare-aggregate models have been tested on only one or two tasks and haven't systematically evaluated different comparison functions. The authors effectively motivate their work by showing that sequence matching problems inherently require measuring semantic similarity, which should guide the choice of comparison functions.

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering four distinct datasets with different task setups (question answering and textual entailment). The results demonstrate that their general compare-aggregate framework outperforms previous approaches on three of the four datasets, with particularly strong results on InsuranceQA. The finding that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches is both surprising and significant, suggesting a promising direction for future research.

The paper is well-written and clearly structured, with a strong connection to related work. The ablation study that examines the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers provides valuable insights into the model's components.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, I recommend the following improvements:

1. The typo "including a some" (page 4, line 5) should be corrected to "including some."

2. The preprocessing and attention layers are important components of the model, but their specific benefits are not clearly articulated. The authors should provide results without these components to clarify their contribution. Specifically, it would be helpful to see how the model performs when these layers are removed entirely, as this would help determine if the benefits are due to the comparison functions themselves or the preprocessing/attention mechanisms.

3. Figure 2 (which visualizes the attention weights) is difficult to read, especially in print. The authors should improve the figure's quality to ensure the patterns they're illustrating are clear to readers.

4. The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. This is an interesting finding that deserves more discussion. The authors should elaborate on why order-insensitive processing works well for these tasks.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

To help me be more confident in my assessment, I would like the authors to address:

1. Could you provide the model performance without the preprocessing and attention layers (as separate ablation studies) to clarify the specific contribution of these components? This would help determine whether the observed benefits are due to the comparison functions or the preprocessing/attention mechanisms.

2. The paper states that element-wise comparison functions work better than neural network-based approaches, but doesn't fully explain why. Could you provide more insight into why these simpler functions might be more effective for sequence matching tasks?

3. The authors mention that the model uses GloVe embeddings without updating them during training. Would you expect similar results if the embeddings were fine-tuned? If not, why do you think the fixed embeddings work better for this framework?
