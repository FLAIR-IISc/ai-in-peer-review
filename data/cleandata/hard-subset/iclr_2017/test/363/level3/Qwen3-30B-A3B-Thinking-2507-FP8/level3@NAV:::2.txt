

This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching in NLP tasks, focusing on systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions at the word level. The authors demonstrate that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The work addresses a significant gap in the literature by empirically verifying the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence matching tasks, rather than being limited to a single application.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing a thorough empirical analysis of comparison functions, which is well-motivated and positioned within the relevant literature. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive, with extensive comparisons to multiple baselines across four different datasets, demonstrating strong empirical support for their claims.

The main reasons for acceptance are: (1) The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple tasks, and (2) The results are scientifically rigorous with well-chosen baselines and appropriate evaluation metrics.

For improvement, please address these minor issues: (1) Correct the typo on page 4, line 5 ("including a some" → "including some"); (2) Clarify the specific benefit of the preprocessing and attention steps by providing results without these components; (3) Improve Figure 2 for better readability in print. 

I also request clarification on why the preprocessing layer (which is order-insensitive) performs well despite not capturing context, especially compared to previous order-sensitive approaches. Understanding this would strengthen the paper's contribution to the field. The authors' claim that word-level matching plays a "very important role" is compelling but would be more convincing with ablation results showing performance differences when removing these key components.
