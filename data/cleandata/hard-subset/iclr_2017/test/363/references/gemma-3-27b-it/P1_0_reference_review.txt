## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks. The authors argue that this framework is generally applicable and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within it, applied to four datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. They find that an element-wise subtraction and multiplication-based comparison function (SUBMULT) performs consistently well across all tasks, demonstrating the strength of the compare-aggregate approach and identifying a potentially undervalued comparison method. The authors release their code, promoting reproducibility and further research.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical investigation of the compare-aggregate framework and delivers valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function. The consistent performance across diverse datasets strengthens the claim of general applicability.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and establishes its relevance across various NLP tasks. The motivation for systematically studying comparison functions is well-justified, as previous work largely overlooks this crucial component.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results presented in Table 3 demonstrate that the proposed model, utilizing the SUBMULT comparison function, achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on all four datasets. The robustness of this comparison function across different tasks is particularly noteworthy.
* **Systematic Evaluation:** The authors conduct a thorough evaluation of six comparison functions, offering valuable insights for future research. The ablation study (Table 4) further supports the importance of the attention and preprocessing layers.
* **Reproducibility:** Releasing the code is a significant positive aspect, enabling the community to reproduce the results and build upon this work.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Discussion of Limitations:** While the paper demonstrates strong performance, it would be beneficial to acknowledge limitations. For instance, the model relies on pre-trained word embeddings. A discussion about the potential impact of different embedding choices or exploring end-to-end training with learnable embeddings would strengthen the paper.
* **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The paper identifies SUBMULT as the best performer, but a more detailed analysis of *why* it works so well would be valuable. Is it capturing specific semantic relationships? Are there specific types of matching errors where SUBMULT excels? Visualizations beyond those in Figure 2 might be helpful here.
* **Computational Complexity:** A brief discussion on the computational complexity of the different comparison functions would be useful, particularly for long sequences.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the authors mention tuning the dropout rate, a more detailed description of the hyperparameter search process and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters would be appreciated.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the preprocessing layer:** You mention that it’s order-insensitive.  Could you elaborate on why you made this design choice and whether you experimented with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., LSTM/GRU) and what the results were?  Are there specific tasks where you anticipate an order-sensitive approach would be more beneficial?
* **Regarding SUBMULT’s success:**  Do you have any hypotheses as to why SUBMULT consistently outperforms other comparison functions? Is there a connection to the types of semantic relationships captured by element-wise subtraction and multiplication?
* **Regarding SNLI performance:** While the SNLI results are competitive, they are not state-of-the-art. Could you discuss any potential reasons for this and whether further architectural modifications or training techniques might improve performance on this specific dataset?
* **Regarding the CNN Aggregation:** You mentioned using a single CNN layer for aggregation. Did you experiment with more complex CNN architectures (e.g., multiple layers, different filter sizes) or other aggregation methods (e.g., attention mechanisms, recurrent networks)?  If so, what were the results?



In conclusion, this is a well-written and insightful paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sequence matching. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions and the strong empirical results justify its acceptance, with the suggested revisions potentially further enhancing its impact.
