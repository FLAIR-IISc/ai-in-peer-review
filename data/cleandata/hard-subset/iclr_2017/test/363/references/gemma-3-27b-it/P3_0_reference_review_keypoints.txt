- Paper studies the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching, evaluating six comparison functions (including element-wise SUB and MULT) across multiple NLP tasks.
- Authors test on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI and report strong, often state-of-the-art results; code is publicly released.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Key empirical finding: simple element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) perform surprisingly well, sometimes outperforming more complex neural comparison functions.
- Thorough experimentation: comparisons to relevant baselines and an ablation study show attention, preprocessing, and word-level comparison are important.
- Reproducibility is a strength due to public code release.
- Requested deeper analysis to explain why SUB and MULT work well, e.g., theoretical intuition or representational comparisons.
- Ask for more comprehensive hyperparameter sensitivity analysis to see if optimal settings and robustness transfer across datasets.
- Suggest reporting computational cost differences between comparison functions, especially for longer sequences and practical deployment.
- SNLI results are competitive but not leading; request detailed comparison and replication notes relative to stronger baselines like EBIM+TreeLSTM.
- Recommend richer visualizations (e.g., attention weights alongside comparison outputs) to better illustrate model behavior.
- Specific author questions: why SUB/MULT succeed, computational cost differences, embedding choice effects on comparison functions, replication details for SNLI baselines, and sensitivity of preprocessing layer parameters.
