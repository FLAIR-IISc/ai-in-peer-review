This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for matching text sequences, evaluating it on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and systematically comparing six different comparison functions. The core idea – a flexible framework allowing for experimentation with different comparison functions – is valuable and well-motivated. The empirical evaluation across diverse tasks is a significant contribution.

**Reject** – While the work is sound and demonstrates the effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework, the improvements presented over strong baselines are often marginal, and the analysis lacks depth regarding *why* certain comparison functions perform better. The paper reads more as an engineering study (trying various components) than a scientific investigation (explaining observed phenomena).

The key arguments supporting this rejection are:

1. **Limited Novelty & Impact:** The “compare-aggregate” framework is already well-established. The primary contribution is an empirical evaluation of different comparison functions *within* this framework. While valuable, the observed performance gains do not represent a substantial leap forward in the field. The best performing function, SUBMULT+NN, isn’t drastically superior to existing approaches. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate that this framework and its specific best-performing component offers significant advantages.
2. **Insufficient Analysis:** The results show that SUBMULT+NN generally performs best, but the paper doesn’t delve deeply into *why*. Why does element-wise multiplication/subtraction work better than, for instance, a learned neural network comparison? The visualizations in Figure 2 are a start, but more comprehensive analysis is required. The authors mention the functions capture similarity, but don’t thoroughly explore this claim.  Ablation studies beyond removing preprocessing/attention would be helpful (e.g., impact of CNN architecture).

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Baseline Comparison:** The baselines are sometimes reported without clear details on their configurations. Providing precise settings (hyperparameters, embedding dimensions, etc.) for comparison would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The tables present results without statistical significance testing. Reporting p-values or confidence intervals is crucial to determine if the observed differences are truly meaningful.
*   **Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis for a selection of examples from each dataset could reveal insights into the limitations of the model and suggest directions for future research. What types of errors does the model consistently make?
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:**  Describe the hyperparameter tuning process more thoroughly. Were the hyperparameters tuned for each comparison function individually? If so, details should be provided.
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost associated with each comparison function would be useful, particularly given the modest performance improvements observed.
*   **Theoretical Justification:**  Expand on the theoretical motivations for why element-wise operations might be effective for semantic similarity.  Connect the findings to relevant concepts from representation learning or information theory.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed information on the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically whether hyperparameters were tuned independently for each comparison function?
2.  The paper highlights the effectiveness of element-wise operations. Can you elaborate on potential reasons why these operations, compared to more complex learned functions, might be particularly well-suited for capturing semantic similarity in this context?
3.  You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive.  Did you experiment with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., LSTMs) and if so, why were they ultimately not adopted?
4.  The performance improvements over strong baselines are often modest. Can you discuss the practical significance of these improvements in real-world applications?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the model makes, perhaps with specific examples illustrating common failure cases?
6.  How does the performance change as the sequence lengths increase in the datasets? Are there any limitations related to handling long sequences?
