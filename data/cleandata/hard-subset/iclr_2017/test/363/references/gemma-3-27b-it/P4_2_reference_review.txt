## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching, a common approach in tasks like textual entailment and question answering. The authors argue that this framework is broadly applicable and focuses on systematically evaluating different comparison functions within it. They test their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and find that a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUB and MULT) performs best overall. The paper presents a strong empirical evaluation and makes the code publicly available.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** The paper presents a thorough empirical investigation of a commonly used framework, offering valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function. The systematic evaluation across multiple datasets strengthens the findings and justifies acceptance.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Well-defined Problem:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and its relevance to various NLP tasks. The introduction provides good examples to illustrate the problem.
* **Sound Motivation:** The motivation for exploring different comparison functions is well-justified. The authors correctly identify a gap in previous work, which primarily focused on task-specific models rather than a generalized framework with systematic component evaluation. The argument that comparison should reflect semantic similarity is sensible.
* **Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed and executed. Testing the model on four different datasets with varying characteristics (question answering vs. entailment) is a significant strength. The inclusion of established baselines allows for a fair comparison.  The ablation studies add further insight.
* **Clear Results and Analysis:** The results are presented clearly in tables and the analysis is insightful. The observation that element-wise operations outperform more complex comparison functions (like neural networks) is noteworthy and counterintuitive, warranting further investigation.
* **Reproducibility:** The availability of the code promotes reproducibility and encourages further research.

**4. Additional Feedback**

* **Preprocessing Details:** While the paper describes the preprocessing layer, it would be helpful to discuss *why* this layer might be beneficial. The intuition behind using gate values to control word importance could be expanded.
* **Attention Mechanism:** The attention mechanism is briefly described. A more detailed explanation of its implementation and perhaps a visualization of attention weights would enhance understanding.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The authors mention tuning dropout. A brief discussion of other hyperparameter tuning efforts (learning rate, CNN filter sizes, etc.) would be valuable.
* **Statistical Significance:**  Adding statistical significance tests to the results tables would strengthen the claims of superiority over baselines.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  Acknowledging potential limitations, such as the scope of comparison functions explored or the specific word embeddings used, would be beneficial.

**5. Questions to the Authors**

1. The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet performs well. Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is it simply that the word-level comparison is sufficient to capture semantic relationships regardless of order, or are there other factors at play?
2. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the choice of element-wise subtraction and multiplication as the optimal comparison function? What properties of these operations make them particularly well-suited for capturing semantic similarity in this context?
3. The ablation study shows the importance of both the preprocessing and attention layers. Were there any interactions observed between these layers during tuning? Did optimizing one layer necessitate adjustments to the other?
4.  You mention the potential for extending this work to multi-task learning.  What challenges do you anticipate in adapting the model to a multi-task setting, and how would you address them?
5. Could you comment on the computational cost of the different comparison functions, particularly NTN, compared to SUB and MULT? Is the efficiency of SUB and MULT a contributing factor to their strong performance?
