## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks (Question Answering and Textual Entailment). The authors argue that this framework is generally applicable and focus on systematically evaluating different comparison functions within it. They test their model on four datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – and demonstrate strong performance, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results. A key finding is that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as comparison functions outperform more complex alternatives like neural networks and standard similarity measures. The authors also release their code, promoting reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated and empirically sound investigation of the compare-aggregate framework. The systematic evaluation of different comparison functions is a significant contribution, and the results are compelling. The fact that a relatively simple comparison function (SUB/MULT) performs well suggests a potentially important insight into the nature of sequence matching.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and motivates its study with relevant examples and a review of existing work. The limitations of previous studies – limited task scope and insufficient attention to the comparison function – are well articulated.
* **Rigorous Experimentation:** The evaluation across four diverse datasets is a strong point. This demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed approach beyond specific tasks.  The choice of datasets, covering both QA and NLI, is sensible.
* **Compelling Results:** The achieved performance on the four datasets is competitive with, and often surpasses, state-of-the-art baselines. The finding that SUB and MULT consistently perform well is particularly noteworthy and challenges conventional wisdom.
* **Ablation studies:** The ablation studies mentioned in section 3.3 help to rationalize the performance of the different model components.
* **Code Availability:** Releasing the code promotes reproducibility and allows other researchers to build upon this work.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Elaborate on Preprocessing:** While the paper mentions a preprocessing layer, the motivation and impact of using gates (Equation 1) aren't fully explained. A more detailed discussion of *why* this preprocessing step is beneficial, especially in the context of the tasks, would be helpful.  Could the authors comment on the impact of the gate values on different word types (e.g., stopwords)?
* **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The observation that element-wise operations work well is fascinating. Can the authors offer a more intuitive explanation of *why* these functions are effective? Are there specific characteristics of the datasets or tasks that make them particularly suitable?
* **Statistical Significance:** While the results are reported in tables, it’s unclear if the performance differences are statistically significant. Including statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion on hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., embedding dimension, CNN filter sizes) would be valuable.  Were any hyperparameters particularly critical to performance?
* **Clarify CNN Aggregation:** While the CNN aggregation is mentioned, a more detailed explanation of the CNN configuration (filter sizes, number of filters, etc.) would be beneficial.
* **Related work expansion**: The related work section could benefit from including more recent advances in sequence matching, such as transformer-based models.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the preprocessing layer:**  You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, and it still performs well. Can you explain why this is the case, given that word order is often crucial for semantic understanding?  Could you share some examples of the gate values learned for different word types?
* **Regarding the comparison functions:** What specific properties of the SUB and MULT operations do you believe contribute to their superior performance?  Is there a connection between these operations and the specific types of relationships captured in the datasets (e.g., semantic similarity, entailment)?
* **Regarding statistical significance:**  Have you performed any statistical significance tests to determine whether the observed performance differences between the different comparison functions and the baselines are statistically significant?
* **Regarding computational cost:** How does the computational cost of the different comparison functions compare?  Does the superior performance of SUB and MULT come at a significant computational cost?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of sequence matching. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions is particularly insightful, and the reported results are compelling. Addressing the feedback and questions above would further strengthen the paper.
