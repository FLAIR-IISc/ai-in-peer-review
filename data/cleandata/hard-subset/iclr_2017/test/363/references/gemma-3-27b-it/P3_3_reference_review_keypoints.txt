- Paper studies the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching and systematically evaluates six comparison functions across tasks.
- Experiments run on four datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.
- Main finding: element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) generally outperform more complex functions (e.g., neural nets, NTN).
- Proposed compare-aggregate model achieves strong, often state-of-the-art, results across tasks.
- Code is publicly available, supporting reproducibility.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strong motivation: fills a gap by analyzing the comparison function choice in sequence matching.
- Strong empirical evidence: consistent competitive performance and informative ablation studies.
- Comprehensive evaluation: analyses of which comparison functions work best per dataset and inclusion of relevant baselines.
- Reproducibility praised due to released code.
- Suggest deeper analysis on why SUB and MULT succeed, including visualizations of learned representations.
- Recommend reporting parameter counts for each comparison function to assess model complexity versus performance.
- Ask for more details on hyperparameter search (spaces and methods) used in experiments.
- Request discussion on why SNLI results lag relative to other datasets and characteristics that may explain it.
- Ask for clarification of the pre-processing layer’s role and why its order-insensitivity is not detrimental.
- Question about future work: how compare-aggregate and comparison function choices could be adapted for multi-task learning.
