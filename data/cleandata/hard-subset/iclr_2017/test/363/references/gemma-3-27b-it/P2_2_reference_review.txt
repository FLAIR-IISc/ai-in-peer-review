## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, a popular approach in recent NLP literature. The authors argue for the general applicability of this framework across diverse tasks (QA, textual entailment) and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within it. Their key finding is that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform standard neural network approaches and cosine similarity-based measures as comparison functions.  The paper presents empirical results on four datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – demonstrating competitive performance and providing valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function within the compare-aggregate paradigm. The code availability is a significant plus.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical investigation of a widely-used framework, identifies a surprisingly effective comparison function, and demonstrates good performance across multiple tasks. The systematic comparison of comparison functions is a particularly valuable contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Well-defined Problem & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and provides strong motivation for investigating the compare-aggregate framework, highlighting its prevalence in recent literature.  The motivation for focusing on the comparison function is also compelling – the authors rightly point out that this component has been relatively underexplored.
* **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are well-designed, covering four distinct datasets representing different sequence matching tasks.  The inclusion of multiple baselines and the ablation study add credibility to the results.  The reported performance is competitive with state-of-the-art methods.
* **Novel & Interesting Finding:** The finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform best is somewhat counterintuitive, given the popularity of more complex neural network based comparison functions. This suggests a valuable, and potentially overlooked, property of these sequence matching tasks.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. The model architecture is described clearly, and the experimental setup is well-defined.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Deeper Analysis of *Why* Element-wise Operations Work:** While the paper *shows* that SUB and MULT work well, it doesn't provide a particularly deep explanation of *why*.  Expanding the discussion on the semantic implications of element-wise operations – what aspects of similarity are they capturing? – would strengthen the paper.  Perhaps connecting it to feature-level interactions could be fruitful.
* **Ablation Study Specificity:** The ablation study (Table 4) could be more granular. For example, ablating *only* the preprocessing layer vs. *only* the attention layer would provide more specific insights into the contribution of each component.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity, especially for the comparison functions, would be helpful. Were the optimal hyperparameters similar across tasks?
* **Error Analysis:** Including a qualitative error analysis, looking at examples where the model fails, could provide additional insights and highlight potential areas for improvement.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:**  How does the computational cost of the different comparison functions compare? This could be relevant for practical applications.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive and that the word-by-word comparison plays a crucial role.  Could you elaborate on the interplay between these two components? Specifically, does the order-insensitivity of the preprocessing layer *enable* the effectiveness of the word-by-word comparison?
2. The results show SUB and MULT performing well, especially when compared to NTN. NTN is designed to capture complex interactions. What are your thoughts on why these more complex interactions don't seem to be necessary for achieving good performance in these tasks? Is it possible the datasets don't require that level of complexity?
3.  In the introduction, you state "We believe that these findings will be useful for future research on sequence matching problems." Could you elaborate on potential directions for future research based on your findings?  Specifically, how can the insights from this work be applied to other sequence matching tasks or integrated with other modeling techniques?
4. Could you provide more details on how the learning rate was selected for each dataset? Was it chosen via a grid search, and if so, over what range of values?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of sequence matching models. Addressing the feedback and answering the questions above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
