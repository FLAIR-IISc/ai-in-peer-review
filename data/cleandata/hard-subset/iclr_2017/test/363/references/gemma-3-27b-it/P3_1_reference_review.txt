## ICLR 2024 Review: A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within this framework, identifying element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as consistently strong performers.  The key contributions are demonstrating the generality of the compare-aggregate approach across tasks and highlighting the efficacy of often-overlooked element-wise comparison functions for semantic similarity assessment. The code availability is also a positive aspect.

**2. Decision**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical investigation of a well-established framework, provides valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function, and demonstrates strong performance across a diverse set of datasets. While the contributions are incremental, the systematic nature of the study and the clear results justify acceptance.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results on all four datasets, providing strong evidence for the effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework. The consistent outperformance of SUB and MULT across datasets is particularly noteworthy.
* **Systematic Evaluation:** The authors perform a rigorous comparison of six different comparison functions, going beyond simple neural network implementations to explore more intuitive approaches like element-wise operations. This systematicity is a major strength of the paper.
* **Well-Motivated Work:** The paper clearly articulates the limitations of existing sequence matching approaches (reliance on single vector representations, insufficient attention to the comparison function) and positions their work as a logical step toward addressing these issues.
* **Relevance to the Community:** Sequence matching is a fundamental problem in NLP, and understanding the nuances of different comparison functions within the compare-aggregate framework is valuable to researchers working on a wide range of tasks.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The figures and tables are informative and contribute to a clear understanding of the experimental results.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement)**

* **Ablation Study Depth:** While the ablation study (Table 4) is helpful, it could be expanded. Specifically, investigating the interaction between the preprocessing layer and the attention mechanism more thoroughly (e.g., removing the preprocessing layer on a per-dataset basis) would provide deeper insights.
* **Comparison Function Analysis:** The paper identifies SUB and MULT as strong performers.  It would be beneficial to provide a more intuitive explanation for *why* these functions work well, potentially connecting their performance to specific characteristics of the datasets or tasks.  Is there a dataset where a different comparison function outperforms SUB/MULT, and if so, why?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter settings, particularly the dimensionality *l* of the hidden layers and the choice of optimizer.
* **Computational Cost:** Briefly discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions. Are there any significant trade-offs between performance and efficiency?
* **Visualization of Attention Weights:** While the visualization of CNN activations is helpful, visualizing the attention weights generated by the attention mechanism could provide further insights into how the model is making its predictions.
* **Future Work:** Expanding on the idea of multi-task learning mentioned in the conclusion would strengthen the paper.  

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet achieves good results.  Could you elaborate on why this seems to work well, and whether you experimented with order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., using an LSTM)?
*  The ablation study demonstrates the importance of the attention layer. Did you try different attention mechanisms (e.g., bidirectional attention) and, if so, what were the results?
*  Can you provide more insight into the performance differences between the datasets? For example, why does EUCCOS perform so well on MovieQA?
*  The paper primarily focuses on comparing the performance of different comparison functions. Is there any analysis of *how* these functions differ in terms of the types of semantic relationships they capture?



In conclusion, this is a well-executed paper that makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of sequence matching. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points outlined above.
