- Paper studies the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching and systematically evaluates six comparison functions across tasks (QA, entailment).  
- Key finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform neural-network and cosine-based comparison functions.  
- Empirical results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI show competitive performance; code is available.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Strength: Well-defined problem and strong motivation to study the comparison function within the compare-aggregate paradigm.  
- Strength: Strong empirical evaluation with multiple baselines and ablation studies across diverse datasets.  
- Strength: Novel and surprising finding that simple element-wise comparisons are highly effective.  
- Strength: Clear presentation and well-described model and experimental setup.  
- Improvement: Provide deeper analysis explaining why SUB and MULT work well and what similarity aspects they capture.  
- Improvement: Make the ablation study more granular (e.g., ablate preprocessing layer alone vs. attention alone).  
- Improvement: Discuss hyperparameter sensitivity across tasks and whether optimal settings generalize.  
- Improvement: Include qualitative error analysis to illustrate failure cases and guide improvements.  
- Improvement: Report computational cost comparisons for different comparison functions.  
- Question: Clarify the interplay between the order-insensitive preprocessing layer and the importance of word-by-word comparison.  
- Question: Explain why complex models like NTN don’t provide gains here—are datasets insufficiently complex?  
- Question: Suggest future research directions and how these findings can inform other sequence matching tasks or models.  
- Question: Provide details on learning-rate selection methodology (grid search range or other procedure).
