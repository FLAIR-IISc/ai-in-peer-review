## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks. The authors argue that this framework is broadly applicable and systematically evaluates six different comparison functions within it, testing their performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI datasets.  Their key finding is that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as comparison functions outperform standard neural network-based approaches and even cosine similarity, providing a surprisingly effective and efficient solution for this type of problem. The paper further provides visualizations supporting the model's learned attention mechanisms.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical investigation of a relevant and practical framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across multiple datasets and offer a valuable insight into the importance of the comparison function within the compare-aggregate paradigm.  The consistent performance of element-wise operations is a noteworthy and counterintuitive result.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and establishes its importance in NLP. The motivation for focusing on the comparison function is well-reasoned, as existing work has largely treated it as a standard component without systematic evaluation.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The consistent outperformance of SUB and MULT across four datasets is compelling. The results are presented clearly in tables and analyzed effectively.  The comparison to existing state-of-the-art models is thorough.
* **Rigorous Experimentation:** The authors demonstrate a commendable effort in systematically evaluating different comparison functions, controlling for other aspects of the model.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors made their code available, which is crucial for verifying the results and facilitating further research.
* **Insightful Analysis:** The visualizations of attention weights, though limited, provide a reasonable explanation of the model's behaviour, lending support to the claims.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more in-depth discussion of *why* SUB and MULT work so well would be beneficial. Is there a connection to information theory, representational geometry, or other relevant concepts? Exploring this could elevate the paper from a purely empirical contribution to a more theoretically grounded one.
* **Ablation Study Details:** Table 4 provides ablation study results but lacks detail regarding the exact configurations tested. It would be helpful to clarify which components were removed or altered and the specific impact on performance.
* **Dataset Specific Nuances:**  The authors acknowledge task-specific model structures, but a more detailed discussion of the rationale behind these choices would be useful. Are there inherent properties of each dataset that favour certain comparison functions or architectural elements?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Provide a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the dimensionality of the hidden layers (l).
* **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis, even on a small subset of examples, could offer insights into the limitations of the model and potential avenues for future improvement.



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the success of SUB and MULT:** Could you elaborate on why you believe element-wise operations are so effective? Do you hypothesize this is due to their simplicity, computational efficiency, or an inherent property of how semantic similarity is encoded in word embeddings?
* **Concerning preprocessing:** You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive.  Did you experiment with order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., RNNs) on all datasets, and if so, what were the results?  Was the computational cost a significant factor in the decision to use the order-insensitive approach?
* **SNLI Performance:** While the performance on SNLI is competitive, it is not state-of-the-art. Are there specific characteristics of the SNLI dataset that might explain this?  Could further tuning or architectural modifications improve performance on this task?
* **Computational Cost:** Can you provide a comparison of the computational cost (training and inference time) for models using different comparison functions, particularly comparing SUB/MULT to more complex options like NTN?  This would further highlight the efficiency benefits of your approach.



Overall, this is a well-written and insightful paper with a clear contribution to the field of sequence matching.  Addressing the feedback above would further strengthen the work and make it even more impactful.
