- Paper evaluates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks.
- Systematically compares six comparison functions on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- Finds simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural comparison methods.
- Reports competitive or state-of-the-art results on the tested datasets.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.
- Strength: Clear problem definition and strong motivation for studying comparison functions.
- Strength: Strong empirical evaluation across four diverse datasets with solid baselines.
- Strength: Novel, counterintuitive finding that SUB and MULT work particularly well.
- Strength: Rigorous experimentation including ablation studies that support conclusions.
- Strength: Code release enhances reproducibility.
- Suggestion: Provide deeper analysis (e.g., visualizations or probing) to explain why SUB and MULT excel.
- Suggestion: Include statistical significance testing to strengthen claims.
- Suggestion: Give detailed hyperparameter tuning procedures and ensure fairness across comparisons.
- Suggestion: Discuss reasons for not achieving SOTA on SNLI and potential task-specific adaptations.
- Suggestion: Explicitly state study limitations (e.g., choice of GloVe embeddings, limited comparison functions).
- Suggestion: Expand related work to cover recent transformer- and attention-based sequence-matching advances.
- Question: Did you try other embeddings (Word2Vec, fastText, BERT) and how did they affect performance?
- Question: Provide detailed ablation study methodology, metrics, and observed performance changes.
- Question: Hypotheses on why SUB and MULT capture important relationships (directional differences, similarity).
- Question: What model modifications might improve SNLI performance given its differences from QA tasks?
- Question: What is the computational overhead of SUB/MULT versus NN or NTN, and implications for scalability?
