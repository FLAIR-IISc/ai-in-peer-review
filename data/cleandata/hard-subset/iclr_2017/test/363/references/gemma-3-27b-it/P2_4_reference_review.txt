## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching, a common approach in NLP tasks like textual entailment and question answering. The authors argue that this framework is broadly applicable and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within it across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  Their key finding is that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform surprisingly well as comparison functions, often outperforming more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper also demonstrates competitive performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on the chosen datasets and provides publicly available code.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical investigation of a well-established framework and identifies a surprisingly effective, yet simple, comparison function. The comprehensive evaluation across multiple datasets strengthens the findings.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Well-defined Problem and Motivation:** The paper clearly articulates the importance of sequence matching in NLP and the prevalence of the compare-aggregate framework. The motivation for investigating different comparison functions is strong, as previous work hasn’t systematically explored this aspect.
* **Strong Empirical Evaluation:**  Testing the model on four datasets with different characteristics (question answering variations and textual entailment) lends credibility to the claim of broad applicability. The comparisons to existing state-of-the-art models are pertinent and demonstrate competitive performance.
* **Novel and Interesting Findings:** The discovery that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex comparison functions is a significant and counter-intuitive result. This suggests that sophisticated models aren't always necessary, and simpler approaches can be highly effective in this context.
* **Clear Presentation & Reproducibility:** The paper is generally well-written and organized.  The availability of code is a substantial positive point for reproducibility and further research.
* **Ablation studies:** Provide insight into the relative importance of different components of the proposed model.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Deeper Dive into *Why* SUB and MULT Work:** While the paper observes that SUB and MULT perform well, a more detailed discussion of *why* they are effective would be valuable.  Is it because they efficiently capture semantic similarity? Does the element-wise nature lend itself to gradient flow? Speculation on the underlying mechanisms would strengthen the analysis.
* **Error Analysis:** Some error analysis on a subset of the datasets could offer insights into scenarios where the model fails and potentially suggest directions for future improvements.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper mentions tuning the dropout rate, a more comprehensive sensitivity analysis of key hyperparameters (e.g., embedding dimension, CNN filter sizes) would be beneficial.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions. This is relevant since simpler functions like SUB and MULT may have advantages in efficiency, especially for long sequences.
* **Related Work - More Recent Approaches:** The related work section could benefit from including more recent and relevant publications, specifically those exploring contrastive learning or learned similarity metrics.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on your hypothesis regarding *why* SUB and MULT perform well as comparison functions? Do you have any insights into the specific properties of these operations that make them suitable for capturing semantic similarity in this context?
2.  Did you observe any consistent patterns in the types of errors the model made on the different datasets?  Could you provide some example cases where the model failed, and potentially explain why?
3.  What is the computational complexity of each of the comparison functions you evaluated, particularly for sequences of varying lengths? How do these computational costs compare in practice?
4.  You mention the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive for MovieQA and InsuranceQA but important for WikiQA and SNLI. Could you provide some intuition for this difference? Is there a characteristic of these datasets that drives this?
5.  Would it be possible to provide a more detailed breakdown of the hyperparameter search process? What range of values did you explore for key hyperparameters like embedding dimension, learning rate, and batch size?



Overall, this is a well-executed study that provides valuable insights into the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching. The acceptance recommendation is based on the strength of the empirical evaluation and the novelty of the findings. Addressing the feedback and answering the questions above would further strengthen the paper.
