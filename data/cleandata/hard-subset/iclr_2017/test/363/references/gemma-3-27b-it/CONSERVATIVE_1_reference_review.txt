This paper investigates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, empirically validating its effectiveness across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and systematically evaluating different comparison functions. The core contribution lies in demonstrating the broad applicability of this framework and identifying element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as surprisingly effective comparison functions.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonable exploration of the compare-aggregate framework and demonstrates competitive results, the lack of substantial novelty and the limited depth of analysis warrant rejection. The work largely confirms existing understanding – that compare-aggregate *can* work – rather than presenting a breakthrough. The empirical gains achieved with SUB and MULT are incremental and not convincingly justified beyond observation.

My primary concerns stem from the limited methodological rigor and comparative analysis. The paper tests numerous configurations but relies heavily on reporting aggregate results, obscuring the statistical significance of observed improvements. The baselines, while present, are not consistently state-of-the-art for all tasks, weakening the claim of demonstrated superiority. Furthermore, the ablation studies are insufficient to dissect the contribution of each component, particularly the preprocessing and attention layers. The justification for the element-wise operations being “a good compromise” feels intuitive rather than empirically supported. The observation that preprocessing/attention importance varies with sequence length difference, while interesting, lacks a rigorous explanation or further exploration.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

* **Novelty:** The compare-aggregate framework is already well-established in the literature (as the authors acknowledge). The primary contribution – the identification of SUB and MULT – feels more like serendipitous observation than a theoretically driven discovery.  Why do these functions perform well? The paper doesn't delve into the underlying reasons.
* **Experimental Rigor:** The paper presents results in tables, but lacks statistical significance testing. Are the observed improvements genuinely significant, or due to random chance? Reporting p-values and confidence intervals is crucial.  The choice of baselines is also questionable; more recent and stronger baselines should be included for a fair comparison, particularly on SNLI.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies are too superficial. Simply removing layers isn’t enough. A more granular analysis, varying hyperparameters within each component, is needed to understand the interplay between different parts of the model.  For example, how does the performance of SUB and MULT change with different embedding dimensions or CNN filter sizes?
* **Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The comparison functions are presented, but not deeply analyzed. A visualization of the learned values within SUB and MULT (e.g., the distributions of the element-wise differences or products) could provide insights into their behavior.
* **Preprocessing Layer:** The paper acknowledges the order-insensitivity of the preprocessing step.  While it performs well, this is somewhat surprising given the importance of word order in natural language. A more detailed justification for this choice, and perhaps an exploration of order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., LSTMs), is warranted.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

* **Statistical Significance:** Add statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to all reported results.
* **Stronger Baselines:** Include more competitive and recent baselines for each dataset.
* **Granular Ablation Studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies, varying hyperparameters and analyzing the impact of each component.
* **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical explanation for why SUB and MULT perform well. Relate them to established concepts in semantic similarity.
* **Visualization:** Visualize the learned representations and attention weights to gain further insights into the model’s behavior.
* **Error Analysis:** Include a qualitative error analysis. What types of examples does the model struggle with, and why?

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide statistical significance tests for all reported results?
* Why were specific baselines chosen over others?  Were state-of-the-art baselines considered but excluded, and if so, why?
* Can you provide a more detailed justification for the surprisingly good performance of SUB and MULT? What specific properties of these functions make them suitable for sequence matching?
* What is the computational cost of each comparison function? Does the efficiency of SUB and MULT contribute to their performance?
