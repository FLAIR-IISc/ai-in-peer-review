- Paper evaluates the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching across NLP tasks and finds SUBMULT (element-wise subtraction and multiplication) consistently effective.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions for its solid empirical study and insights.
- Clear problem definition and strong motivation to systematically study comparison functions.
- Strong empirical results: SUBMULT achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and competitive results on SNLI.
- Systematic evaluation of six comparison functions and ablation studies showing the importance of attention and preprocessing layers.
- Reproducibility: authors release their code.
- Suggestion to discuss limitations, notably reliance on pre-trained word embeddings and potential for end-to-end learnable embeddings.
- Suggest deeper analysis of why SUBMULT works, including semantic interpretation and targeted error analysis/visualizations.
- Request brief discussion of computational complexity of comparison functions, especially for long sequences.
- Ask for more detail on hyperparameter search and sensitivity (e.g., dropout tuning).
- Question about the order-insensitive preprocessing layer and whether order-sensitive methods (LSTM/GRU) were tried and how they performed.
- Question asking for hypotheses on why SUBMULT consistently outperforms other functions and what semantic relations it captures.
- Question about SNLI results: reasons for not reaching state-of-the-art and possible architectural or training improvements.
- Question about CNN aggregation: whether deeper/multi-filter CNNs or alternative aggregation methods (attention, RNNs) were explored and their outcomes.
- Conclusion: well-written, valuable contribution; suggested revisions would further strengthen the paper.
