- Paper systematically evaluates the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Focuses on comparing six comparison functions and shows element-wise SUB and MULT perform best.  
- Experiments on four diverse datasets support the claim of general applicability.  
- Strong empirical results; SUB and MULT consistently outperform more complex functions.  
- Ablation study shows preprocessing and attention layers are important for performance.  
- Paper is well-written, clearly explains architecture and experimental setup.  
- Code is publicly released, aiding reproducibility.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance due to the valuable empirical study and solid execution.  
- Suggest deeper analysis of why SUB and MULT work (e.g., inspect learned weights/activations).  
- Request more details on hyperparameter tuning and sensitivity (dropout, learning rate variance).  
- Recommend reporting statistical significance tests to confirm performance differences.  
- Ask for discussion of computational cost of comparison functions, especially at higher embedding dims.  
- Suggest connecting element-wise operations to established similarity/distance measures (e.g., Manhattan).  
- Questions for authors: provide intuition for why SUB/MULT capture semantic similarity well.  
- Questions for authors: report any dataset-specific patterns, effects of preprocessing order-sensitivity, and anticipated multi-tasking challenges.
