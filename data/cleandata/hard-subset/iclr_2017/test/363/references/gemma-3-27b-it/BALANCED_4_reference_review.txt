This paper tackles the problem of sequence matching, a fundamental task in NLP with applications in areas like textual entailment and question answering. The authors investigate the “compare-aggregate” framework, proposing a model within this framework and systematically evaluating different comparison functions across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the work explores a relevant and important area, and presents a reasonably thorough empirical evaluation, it lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core contribution—testing the compare-aggregate framework with different comparison functions—feels incremental and doesn’t offer a substantial advancement over existing work that has already explored this space (as acknowledged in the related work section). The empirical results, while positive, do not demonstrate a breakthrough or uncover particularly surprising findings.

My assessment is based on the following arguments:

1. **Limited Novelty:** The compare-aggregate framework is not new (Wang & Jiang 2016b, He & Lin 2016, Parikh et al. 2016). The paper’s primary contribution is applying it to a wider range of datasets *and* testing different comparison functions. While the systematic comparison of functions is valuable, the functions themselves are largely standard, and the element-wise operations, while performing well, aren't radically innovative.  The “SUBMULT+NN” combination feels like a straightforward extension of existing approaches rather than a novel architectural component.

2. **Incremental Improvement, Not a Paradigm Shift:** The paper demonstrates that the proposed model performs well, achieving competitive or state-of-the-art results on the chosen datasets. However, the improvements are often marginal and don't signal a substantial leap forward in the field. The results don't justify the claim that this framework is "very effective" in a groundbreaking way. The observed effectiveness is more a validation of the existing framework than a demonstration of a significantly superior approach.

3. **Insufficient Justification for Design Choices:**  The rationale for focusing on element-wise operations ("a good compromise between highly flexible functions...and highly restrictive functions") isn’t thoroughly explored or substantiated beyond the empirical results. It would be helpful to provide a more theoretical or intuitive explanation for *why* these operations are expected to be effective for sequence matching. The authors mention intuition based on semantic similarity, but this isn't fully developed.



Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

* **Strengthen the Novelty Argument:**  Clearly articulate *what* makes this work stand out from previous explorations of the compare-aggregate framework. Is it the specific combination of functions? The scale of the empirical study?  A deeper analysis of *why* the chosen functions work well?
* **Ablation Studies in Detail:** The ablation study (Table 4) is brief.  Provide a more detailed analysis of the impact of each component (preprocessing layer, attention layer) on performance for *each* dataset.
* **Error Analysis:** Include an error analysis to identify cases where the model fails and to understand the limitations of the approach. What types of sequence matching problems does it struggle with?
* **Theoretical Underpinnings:** Explore the theoretical justification for the chosen comparison functions. Is there a connection to similarity measures or other relevant concepts from information retrieval or representation learning?
* **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions and their impact on scalability.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Report the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters (learning rate, dropout rate, embedding dimension).



I have the following questions for the authors:

* **Why were these specific datasets chosen?** What characteristics of these datasets make them particularly well-suited for evaluating the compare-aggregate framework?
* **Could you elaborate on the “gate values” in the preprocessing layer?** What specific characteristics of the input sequences are they intended to capture?
* **How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings (GloVe)?** Have you experimented with other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText)?
* **The paper states “the word-by-word comparison part plays a very important role.” Can you quantify this?** Are there scenarios where this approach is less effective, and if so, what are they?
* **Regarding the observation that the preprocessing layer is more important for WikiQA and SNLI, could you hypothesize why this is the case, given the differences between these datasets and MovieQA/InsuranceQA?**
