This paper investigates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, a common approach in tasks like textual entailment and question answering. The authors argue this framework is broadly applicable and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within it across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). They find that element-wise subtraction and multiplication consistently perform well, even outperforming more complex comparison methods like neural networks.

**Reject.** While the empirical exploration is thorough, the core contribution feels incremental. The “compare-aggregate” framework is already well-established, and demonstrating that a simple element-wise operation can be competitive doesn’t represent a substantial advancement. The novelty lies primarily in the breadth of the empirical evaluation, but the resulting insights aren't transformative enough to warrant acceptance given the competitive ICLR standards.

The paper’s strength lies in its comprehensive experimental setup. Testing the model across four diverse datasets is commendable and provides stronger evidence for the framework's generalizability than previous work. The ablation studies (Table 4) are also helpful in understanding the importance of different components. The analysis of visualization of CNN layers is nice. However, the choice of baselines feels uneven. While strong baselines are included for SNLI, for the other datasets, it’s less clear that the state-of-the-art is truly being challenged, making the performance gains less impactful. Furthermore, the discussion on *why* element-wise operations work so well is superficial. The authors suggest it's a good compromise between flexibility and similarity measurement, but this intuition isn't rigorously explored or justified. The claims of superior performance need to be stronger than “competitive” across all datasets. The relatively small performance improvements over established baselines don't justify the paper’s claims of significant contribution.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The paper would benefit from a more in-depth investigation of *why* SUB and MULT work well. Is it due to specific statistical properties of word embeddings? Does it relate to the task-specific data characteristics? A theoretical analysis, or at least a more detailed empirical exploration, is needed.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** For WikiQA, InsuranceQA, and MovieQA, please include the most recent and powerful baselines available. It’s crucial to demonstrate a clear and substantial improvement over the state-of-the-art.
*   **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis could provide valuable insights into the limitations of the model and the strengths of the SUB and MULT comparison functions. Where does the model fail, and can this be linked to specific linguistic phenomena or dataset biases?
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** A more comprehensive ablation study, perhaps exploring different combinations of preprocessing, attention, and aggregation layers, could clarify the relative contributions of each component.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions. Are SUB and MULT more efficient than the neural network-based approaches? This could be a valuable practical consideration.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but performs well on tasks where word order is important. Can you elaborate on why this seems to be the case? Is it the comparison function that compensates for the lack of order information, or are there other factors at play?
2.  Could you provide examples of cases where the SUB and MULT comparison functions outperform other methods, and conversely, cases where they fail? This would help to understand their strengths and weaknesses.
3.  Have you explored any regularization techniques to prevent overfitting, particularly given the relatively simple structure of the model?
4.  What is the sensitivity of the performance to the embedding dimensionality (d)? Would increasing d significantly impact the results, and if so, for which comparison functions?
