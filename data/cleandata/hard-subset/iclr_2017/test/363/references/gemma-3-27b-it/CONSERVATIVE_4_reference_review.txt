This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, aiming to demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple datasets and systematically evaluate different comparison functions within this framework. The authors test their model on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, finding that an element-wise subtraction and multiplication-based comparison function performs best overall.

**Reject.** While the paper explores a sensible framework and presents a thorough empirical evaluation, the results, while positive, do not demonstrate a significant enough advance to warrant acceptance. The core idea – a compare-aggregate framework – is not novel, and the improvements offered by the specific comparison function, while consistent, are incremental and lack a strong theoretical justification. The extensive experimental validation is appreciated, but doesn’t overcome the lack of substantial novelty.

The paper builds upon established “compare-aggregate” models (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016), but the contribution feels more like an engineering study – a careful tuning of existing components – than a fundamentally new approach. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature regarding the systematic evaluation of comparison functions. However, the choice of these functions feels somewhat ad-hoc. Why element-wise subtraction and multiplication? The paper offers a weak intuition ("a good compromise between highly flexible functions…and highly restrictive functions"), but a more rigorous rationale grounded in the nature of semantic similarity or information theory would be beneficial.

The empirical evaluation is extensive, which is commendable. However, the improvements over strong baselines are not always substantial, and the paper lacks a detailed error analysis. Presenting examples where the model fails, and discussing *why* it fails, would strengthen the claims. The sensitivity of the results to the preprocessing layer is mentioned but not fully explored. The effect of varying the dimensionality 'l' of the hidden layers is also not discussed, which is crucial for understanding the model’s performance. While the paper mentions the limitations of using GloVe embeddings and not updating them, it does not discuss how sensitive the results are to changes in the embedding layer.

Further, the selection of baselines is not entirely consistent across all datasets, and some baselines are cited without being clearly defined within the paper. For instance, the details of the “IR model” baseline for InsuranceQA remain opaque.  The CNN aggregation layer, while mentioned, is not explored with different kernel sizes or architectural variations.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Theoretical grounding:**  Provide a stronger theoretical justification for the chosen comparison functions. Connect them to principles of semantic similarity or distributional semantics.
*   **Error Analysis:** Include a detailed error analysis to identify the types of errors the model makes and potential areas for improvement.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  More detailed ablation studies are needed. For example, investigate the contribution of the preprocessing layer, different attention mechanisms, and variations in the CNN aggregation layer.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to key hyperparameters, such as embedding dimension, learning rate, and dropout rate.
*   **Baseline Consistency:** Ensure a consistent and thorough comparison against strong, well-defined baselines across all datasets.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the proposed model compared to the baselines.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the specific rationale behind choosing element-wise subtraction and multiplication as comparison functions? Is there any theoretical justification or intuition beyond being a “compromise”?
*   Can you provide examples of instances where the model fails, and explain why you believe it fails in those cases?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Have you experimented with different embedding techniques or fine-tuning the embeddings during training?
*   What is the computational overhead of the proposed model compared to the baselines, particularly regarding the element-wise comparison functions?
