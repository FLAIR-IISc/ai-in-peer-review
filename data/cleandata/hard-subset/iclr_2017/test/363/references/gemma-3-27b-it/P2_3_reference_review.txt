## Review for ICLR 2024 - A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

**1. Summary of Claims:**

This paper investigates the widely used "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks. The authors argue this framework is broadly applicable and focuses on systematically evaluating different comparison functions within it. They test their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and find that a simple element-wise subtraction and multiplication comparison function outperforms others, including more complex neural network approaches. The core contributions are demonstrating the general effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework across tasks and identifying a surprisingly effective, yet often overlooked, comparison function.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical study with clear results. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions is valuable and the findings challenge some common assumptions in the field. The broad applicability demonstrated across multiple datasets strengthens the contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and provides a strong motivation for investigating the compare-aggregate framework. The limitations of existing work – lack of cross-dataset evaluation and insufficient attention to the comparison function – are well articulated.
* **Sound Methodology:**  The experimental setup is well-defined.  Testing the model across four diverse datasets is a strong point, and the baseline comparisons are appropriate.  The ablation studies, while not extensive, provide some insight into the importance of different components.
* **Convincing Results:** The results consistently show the superiority of the SUB and MULT comparison functions across most datasets.  While the performance gains aren't massive, they are consistent and statistically significant (implied, though statistical significance testing isn't explicitly stated - see feedback). The analysis, including the visualization of CNN attention, offers some intuition behind these results.
* **Reproducibility:** The authors provide code, which is a significant positive factor, enhancing reproducibility and allowing others to build upon their work.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Statistical Significance:** The paper should explicitly state whether the performance differences reported are statistically significant.  Including p-values or confidence intervals would greatly strengthen the claims.
* **Ablation Study Expansion:** While an ablation study is included, it’s relatively limited.  More in-depth ablation focusing on the impact of the preprocessing layer (with and without LSTM) and attention mechanism individually would be beneficial.  How does performance degrade without each component?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper mentions tuning the dropout rate for WikiQA, but doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the model to other hyperparameters. A brief analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity could bolster the robustness of the findings.
* **Discussion of Negative Results:** It would be valuable to discuss *why* some comparison functions perform worse than others.  What are the limitations of neural tensor networks or cosine similarity in this specific context?  A deeper theoretical discussion could elevate the paper.
* **Related Work Expansion:** The related work section could be expanded to include more recent advances in sequence matching, particularly those utilizing transformers (e.g., BERT-based models).  How does this compare-aggregate approach potentially complement or contrast with these newer methods?

**5. Questions for Authors:**

* **Statistical Significance:** Can you provide p-values or confidence intervals for the reported performance differences to demonstrate statistical significance?
* **Comparison Function Intuition:**  You mention that SUB and MULT capture semantic similarity.  Could you elaborate on the intuition behind *why* element-wise operations are effective in capturing semantic relationships compared to, say, a neural network that can learn more complex combinations?
* **Scalability:**  How does the computational cost of the SUB and MULT comparison functions scale with sequence length compared to the more complex methods like NTN? Is this a factor in the observed performance differences, especially for very long sequences?
* **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Were there any significant challenges encountered during hyperparameter tuning? Are there any specific hyperparameter settings that are particularly sensitive to performance?



Overall, this is a well-written and well-executed paper with a valuable contribution to the field of sequence matching. Addressing the feedback above would further strengthen the paper and make it even more impactful.
