## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching across four diverse datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within this framework, ultimately finding that element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUB and MULT) perform best overall. The paper demonstrates strong performance compared to state-of-the-art methods on the chosen datasets, suggesting the generality and robustness of their approach.  The authors also provide code for reproducibility.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** The paper presents a thorough empirical investigation of a widely used framework (compare-aggregate) for sequence matching, identifying a surprisingly effective and under-explored comparison function (element-wise operations). The strong results across multiple datasets justify the claims made.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Problem Definition & Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and motivates the study by highlighting the limitations of existing compare-aggregate models, specifically the lack of systematic comparison of comparison functions. The application to diverse datasets strengthens the argument for the framework's generality.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed model is a reasonable extension of existing compare-aggregate architectures. The description of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison, aggregation) is clear. The six comparison functions are well-defined and represent a good range of options.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are compelling. The consistent performance of SUB and MULT across different datasets suggests a genuine advantage. The ablation studies (mentioned in section 3.4) add further insight into the importance of various components.  The comparison to strong baselines adds credibility.
* **Novelty:** While the compare-aggregate framework isn’t novel, the *systematic* evaluation of different comparison functions, and the surprising finding regarding the effectiveness of element-wise operations, constitute a significant contribution.  This challenges the common practice of using more complex comparison functions like neural networks.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The paper is well-written and presented. However, a few minor improvements could enhance its impact:

* **Hyperparameter Details:** More details on hyperparameter tuning, beyond mentioning dropout and learning rate for WikiQA, would be valuable.  Providing a table with key hyperparameters for each dataset would improve reproducibility.
* **Statistical Significance:** While the performance improvements are reported, the inclusion of statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) would strengthen the conclusions.
* **Error Analysis:** A brief error analysis of the model’s failures on each dataset could provide further insight and suggest future research directions.  For example, are there specific types of questions or sentence pairs where the model consistently struggles?
* **Visualization of Attention:** While Figure 2 visualizes the CNN layer, visualizing the attention weights learned by the model could help understand *where* the model is focusing its attention when making predictions.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Comparing the computational cost of different comparison functions (NN vs. SUB/MULT) could be informative, as this might be a practical consideration for large-scale applications.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Did you experiment with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., LSTMs) on MovieQA or InsuranceQA, and if so, did they improve performance?  If not, could you elaborate on why you chose to prioritize computational efficiency in these tasks?
2.  The results show SUBMULT+NN often performs comparably to SUB and MULT. What is the benefit of including the additional NN layer in this case? Is it primarily for regularization, or does it learn to combine the outputs of SUB and MULT in a non-trivial way?
3.  Can you provide more details on the specific hardware used for your experiments and the training time for each dataset? This will further aid in reproducibility and help assess the practical feasibility of your approach.
4.   You state that the element-wise comparison functions are not widely used for word-level matching. Do you have any hypotheses as to *why* this is the case? Could it be due to difficulties in initialization or optimization?
