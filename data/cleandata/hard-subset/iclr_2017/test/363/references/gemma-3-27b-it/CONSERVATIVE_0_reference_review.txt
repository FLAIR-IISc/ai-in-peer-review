This paper investigates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, empirically demonstrating its effectiveness across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and systematically evaluating six different comparison functions. The core claim is that this framework is broadly applicable and that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform surprisingly well as comparison functions.

Reject. While the paper presents a thorough empirical evaluation, the core contribution feels incremental. The “compare-aggregate” framework itself is not novel, and the observed superiority of element-wise operations, while interesting, isn’t sufficiently justified or deeply explored to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The paper lacks a strong theoretical grounding for *why* these simple operations work well, relying heavily on empirical observation. Furthermore, the comparisons to existing state-of-the-art methods aren't consistently compelling, and the performance gains often appear marginal.

The paper adequately motivates the problem of sequence matching and correctly identifies limitations in prior work – namely, a lack of cross-dataset validation and insufficient attention to the comparison function. The experimental setup is comprehensive, testing various comparison functions on diverse datasets. The ablation studies are helpful in understanding the contribution of different components. However, the paper's contribution feels largely exploratory rather than providing significant new insight. The core idea – applying a compare-aggregate framework – has been previously established, and the key finding – the effectiveness of SUB and MULT – lacks a compelling explanation. Why do element-wise operations, seemingly simplistic, outperform more sophisticated approaches like neural networks or tensor networks? The paper hints at semantic similarity but doesn’t rigorously connect this intuition to the empirical results. The improvements over existing baselines, while present, are often small and don't consistently justify the complexity added by the proposed model. The analysis of the CNN layer’s attention, while visually informative, lacks quantitative analysis. For example, how does the CNN’s attention correlate with human-annotated importance or other established metrics?

Additional Feedback:

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper would benefit greatly from a more thorough theoretical discussion of why element-wise operations are effective for sequence matching. Perhaps relating this to properties of word embeddings or semantic similarity measures.
*   **Error Analysis:** A detailed error analysis could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the proposed model and comparison functions. What types of examples does the model consistently fail on?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A sensitivity analysis of the hyperparameters, especially for the different comparison functions, would strengthen the robustness of the findings.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values) for all performance comparisons to ensure that the observed improvements are not due to chance.
* **Ablation Study Details:** More detail on the ablation study results would be helpful. It’s stated that preprocessing and attention layers are important, but the *magnitude* of their impact isn't clear.
* **Baseline Selection:** Justify the selection of the baselines more thoroughly. Are these truly the state-of-the-art methods for each dataset, or are there more recent and relevant baselines?

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide a theoretical explanation for why the SUB and MULT comparison functions outperform more complex alternatives?
*   What types of errors does your model consistently make, and how do these errors vary with the choice of comparison function?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, and have you performed a sensitivity analysis?
*   What are the computational costs associated with the different comparison functions, and how do these costs scale with sequence length?
*   Have you considered incorporating contextual information (e.g., using LSTMs or Transformers) into the preprocessing step, and if so, what was the impact on performance?
