This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, aiming to demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple datasets and systematically evaluate different comparison functions within the framework. The authors test their model on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, identifying an element-wise subtraction and multiplication-based comparison function as performing best.

Reject. While the paper addresses a relevant problem and presents a reasonably well-structured model, the numerous minor flaws and inconsistencies, coupled with a lack of truly compelling results, lead me to recommend rejection. The paper feels underdeveloped and insufficiently rigorous for ICLR.

The primary concerns stem from a lack of thoroughness in the experimental setup and analysis. First, the baselines chosen are inconsistent across datasets, making fair comparison difficult. Some baselines are recent state-of-the-art while others are considerably older, obscuring the true contribution of the proposed method. Second, the ablation studies (Table 4) are insufficient. For example, removing the preprocessing layer demonstrates a negligible impact on some datasets. Is this truly representative, or is the ablation poorly designed? The impact of the attention layer is also insufficiently explored. Third, the claims regarding the superiority of the element-wise comparison functions are not convincingly demonstrated. The improvements over other methods, while sometimes statistically significant, are often marginal. The authors state that SUBMULT+NN is the best *in general*, but the results show it isn’t uniformly superior across all datasets. Fourth, the analysis of the CNN layer's visualization (Figure 2) feels superficial and doesn’t provide meaningful insights. Simply stating the CNN focuses on matched words isn’t insightful; this is an expected behavior. Fifth, the writing quality is uneven. The introduction is repetitive and the descriptions of the comparison functions lack clarity. The presentation of equations could be improved for readability. Sixth, the preprocessing step utilizing gate values is not well justified and its impact seems minor, despite the authors’ claims. The equation uses element-wise multiplication with `eQ` and `eA`, but the dimensions of these are never explicitly defined. Finally, the link to the code is a GitHub repository, but the repository appears very sparsely populated with only a README file at the time of review. 

To improve this paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Strengthen Baselines:** Ensure a fair comparison by including state-of-the-art baselines for *all* datasets, ideally re-implemented or carefully sourced with clear reporting of hyperparameter settings.
*   **Comprehensive Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies, systematically evaluating the contribution of each component of the model (preprocessing, attention, comparison function, aggregation).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Rigorously report statistical significance tests for all reported results, including effect sizes, to demonstrate the robustness of the findings.
*   **In-depth Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the comparison functions, investigating *why* the element-wise operations perform well. Offer theoretical justifications. 
*   **Improve Visualization:** Go beyond superficial visualization. Quantify the attention weights and provide examples of where the model succeeds and fails.
*   **Clarify Writing:** Refine the writing for clarity and conciseness. Ensure all equations are properly defined and explained.
*   **Complete Code:** Provide a fully functional and documented codebase to allow for reproducibility.
* **Preprocessing Details:** Clearly define the dimensions of the `eQ` and `eA` variables used in the preprocessing step. Explain the purpose and impact of this step more thoroughly.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors:

1.  Why were specific baselines chosen for each dataset? What steps were taken to ensure a fair comparison?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the intuition behind the element-wise subtraction and multiplication comparison function? Why does this outperform more sophisticated methods like neural networks?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, especially the learning rate and dropout rate? Was a systematic hyperparameter search performed?
4.  What is the computational cost of the different comparison functions? Is there a trade-off between performance and computational efficiency?
5.  Can you provide more examples of where the model fails and analyze the reasons for these failures?
