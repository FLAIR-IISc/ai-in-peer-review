This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, systematically evaluating different comparison functions within this framework across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The core contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of this general framework and identifying element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as surprisingly effective comparison functions, outperforming more complex methods like neural networks in several scenarios.

**Reject.** While the experimental breadth is commendable, the novelty feels incremental, and the analysis, while thorough, doesn't offer a truly groundbreaking insight. The "compare-aggregate" framework wasn't novel to this paper, and the finding that simple element-wise operations can be competitive doesn’t fundamentally shift our understanding of sequence matching. It feels more like a careful empirical study *within* an established paradigm, rather than a paradigm shift itself.

The paper’s primary strength is the comprehensive empirical evaluation. Testing the model on four different datasets is a significant undertaking and strengthens the claim that the “compare-aggregate” framework is broadly applicable. The ablation studies and analysis of visualization (Figure 2) provide some intuition for why certain comparison functions perform better. The code release is also a positive aspect. However, the paper lacks a deeper theoretical justification for *why* these element-wise operations perform well. While the authors hypothesize it’s a good compromise between flexibility and similarity measurement, this feels somewhat post-hoc. The performance gains over strong baselines aren't consistently large enough to warrant strong acceptance. The choice of baselines is also questionable – some seem slightly outdated (e.g., older InsuranceQA baselines), potentially inflating the relative gains. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a clear advantage over more recent and sophisticated attention mechanisms beyond the specific configurations tested. Furthermore, the CNN aggregation layer seems a bit arbitrarily chosen. Why a CNN specifically? Was this investigated or justified beyond being a common practice?

Additional Feedback (to improve the paper, not impacting the reject decision):

*   **Theoretical grounding:** Explore a more rigorous theoretical explanation for the success of SUB and MULT. Could these be related to specific properties of the embedding space or the nature of semantic similarity?
*   **Baseline comparison:** Include more recent and competitive baselines, especially for InsuranceQA. Consider state-of-the-art transformer-based models.
*   **Aggregation Layer Exploration:** Justify the choice of CNN for aggregation. Consider alternatives like attention-based aggregation or recurrent networks and compare their performance.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide a more detailed error analysis. What types of examples does the model struggle with, and why? This would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more in-depth analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the comparison functions, would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost associated with different comparison functions, particularly in the context of long sequences.

Questions for the Authors:

*   Why was a CNN chosen as the aggregation layer, and were any other aggregation methods considered? If so, what were the results?
*   The paper states element-wise operations are a "good compromise." Can you elaborate on the mathematical properties of SUB and MULT that make them particularly suitable for measuring semantic similarity, beyond intuition?
*   Could you provide a more detailed error analysis, with specific examples of instances where the model fails and an explanation of the potential reasons?
*   How do the computational costs of different comparison functions scale with sequence length, and are there any optimization strategies to mitigate these costs?
