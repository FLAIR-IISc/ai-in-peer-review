- Paper studies the compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching and systematically evaluates different comparison functions.  
- Experiments on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI show competitive performance.  
- Key finding: element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) are particularly effective.  
- Authors provide code for reproducibility.  
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Clear problem definition and motivation for focusing on the comparison function.  
- Strong empirical validation across diverse QA and entailment tasks.  
- Novel contribution: challenges the need for complex neural comparison functions.  
- Reproducibility strengthened by publicly available implementation.  
- Suggest deeper analysis/theoretical explanation of why SUB and MULT work well.  
- Ablation study should report detailed per-dataset numbers for removing preprocessing and attention.  
- Discuss sensitivity to hyperparameters, especially CNN aggregation architecture.  
- Include qualitative error analysis to show model failure modes.  
- Expand related work to cover transformer-based models and connections to attention mechanisms.  
- Questions: why use (non-fine-tuned) GloVe embeddings and were embeddings fine-tuned in experiments?  
- Question: how does the order-insensitive preprocessing layer affect tasks where word order matters (e.g., SNLI)?  
- Question: what CNN architecture (kernel sizes, filters) was used and were alternatives explored?  
- Request examples where SUB/MULT succeed but neural-network comparisons fail.  
- Request clarification on computational cost differences between SUB/MULT and NN/NTN comparison functions.
