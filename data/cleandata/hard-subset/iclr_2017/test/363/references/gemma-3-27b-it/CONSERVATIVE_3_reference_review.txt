This paper investigates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, arguing its general applicability across diverse tasks and focusing on the impact of the comparison function. The authors test their model on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and systematically evaluate six comparison functions, finding element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform best. They present competitive results and release their code.

**Reject.** While the paper explores a reasonable research direction, the lack of substantial novelty and the somewhat underwhelming results, especially given the extensive experimentation, lead me to recommend rejection. The paper largely confirms existing intuitions about the compare-aggregate framework without offering a breakthrough or convincingly demonstrating significant advantages over established methods.

My primary concern is the incremental nature of the contribution. The “compare-aggregate” framework is already well-established (as the authors acknowledge), and systematically testing comparison functions feels like an exhaustive empirical study rather than a novel research contribution. The finding that element-wise operations work well isn’t particularly surprising given their efficiency and ability to capture interaction between vector dimensions. Furthermore, the performance gains over strong baselines are often marginal. The claim of achieving “state-of-the-art” performance is overstated; while competitive, it isn’t consistently superior across all tasks and datasets, and the baselines used are not always the very latest.

Specifically, the performance on SNLI appears lagging behind current SOTA (EBIM+TreeLSTM). The choice of baselines appears somewhat selective – while present, they aren’t always the most recent or competitive models available for each task. The ablation studies, while present, are not extensive enough to fully justify the design choices of the overall model (e.g., the specific CNN aggregation layer). The discussion of why SUB and MULT work well is somewhat superficial; a deeper analysis of *what* these functions capture that others don't would strengthen the argument. The visualization in Figure 2 is descriptive but lacks quantitative support or insightful conclusions. It simply *shows* attention, not *why* it’s happening or its impact on the model's decisions.

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Novelty:** Focus on the aspect that truly differentiates this work. Is it the combination of the framework with specific datasets? A deeper analysis of the element-wise operations? Articulating this clearly would strengthen the paper.
*   **Baselines:** Include more recent and competitive baselines, particularly for SNLI.  A thorough comparison against the current SOTA is crucial.
*   **Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the results. Why do SUB and MULT work better?  What do they capture that other comparison functions miss? Are there specific types of examples where these functions excel or fail?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Expand the ablation studies to investigate the contribution of different components of the model more rigorously. For example, what is the impact of the preprocessing layer?  What is the optimal configuration of the CNN aggregation layer?
*   **Error Analysis:** Include a qualitative error analysis. Examine examples where the model fails to identify patterns or weaknesses.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed comparison of the computational cost of the different comparison functions? Is the improved performance of SUB and MULT worth the computational overhead (if any)?
2.  Why was the CNN chosen for aggregation instead of alternatives like attention mechanisms or recurrent networks?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings? Were other embedding types considered?
4.  The paper mentions the preprocessing layer being order-insensitive. Could you discuss the potential drawbacks of this choice and whether incorporating word order information (e.g., using RNNs) was considered and, if so, why it was rejected?
