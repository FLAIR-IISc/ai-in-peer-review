## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary of Claims & Contributions:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of a “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks (Question Answering and Textual Entailment). The authors argue that this framework is generally applicable and focuses on systematically evaluating different comparison functions within it. They demonstrate strong performance on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and highlight the effectiveness of element-wise subtraction and multiplication as comparison functions. The paper also provides code for reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a solid empirical evaluation of the compare-aggregate framework and provides valuable insights into the choice of comparison functions. The results are convincing, and the work is well-motivated.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper builds upon existing work in sequence matching and identifies a crucial gap – a systematic evaluation of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks and a detailed analysis of comparison function choices. The rationale for exploring element-wise operations as alternatives to standard neural networks or similarity metrics is sound.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The model consistently achieves strong performance on all four datasets, often outperforming existing baselines. The fact that a relatively simple comparison function (SUB and MULT) performs well across different tasks strengthens the claim about the framework's generality.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The paper provides a comprehensive experimental setup, including details on datasets, hyperparameters, and baselines. The ablation studies (although brief) offer some insight into the contribution of different components of the model.
* **Reproducibility:** The release of code is a significant positive point, enabling other researchers to verify the results and build upon this work.

**4. Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** While the paper demonstrates that SUB and MULT work well, it would be beneficial to provide a more in-depth analysis of *why* they are effective.  Is it due to their ability to capture specific types of semantic relationships? A visualization of the learned representations or an analysis of the resulting vectors *t<sub>j</sub>* could be illuminating.
* **More Detailed Ablation Studies:** Expanding the ablation studies beyond just removing preprocessing and attention layers would be valuable. For instance, investigating the impact of different CNN architectures or dimensionality of word embeddings.
* **Discussion of Limitations:** The paper doesn’t explicitly discuss potential limitations of the approach.  For example, how might the model perform on very long sequences, or on tasks requiring more complex reasoning? Addressing these limitations would strengthen the work.
* **Clarify Preprocessing:** The preprocessing layer is mentioned as important, but its purpose isn't fully explained. What type of words does it suppress, and how does this contribute to performance?  Elaborating on the gate values would be helpful.
* **Statistical Significance:** While the results are generally strong, it would be more robust to include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to confirm the improvements over baselines.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the choice of element-wise operations:** What motivated the specific choice of subtraction and multiplication, and what theoretical underpinnings support their effectiveness in capturing semantic similarity?
* **Regarding the preprocessing layer:** Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the gate values and how they are learned? Are there specific patterns in the learned gate values that suggest the model is focusing on particular types of words?
* **Regarding scaling to longer sequences:** Have you experimented with the model on datasets with significantly longer sequences? If so, what challenges did you encounter, and what strategies did you use to address them?
* **Regarding the CNN aggregation:** Why was a CNN chosen for aggregation instead of other methods like recurrent networks or attention mechanisms?  Did you experiment with these alternatives?



Overall, this is a well-executed and valuable contribution to the field of sequence matching. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions within the compare-aggregate framework provides useful insights for future research, and the strong empirical results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach.  Addressing the feedback above would further enhance the paper's impact.
