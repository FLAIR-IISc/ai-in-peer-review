This paper introduces a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, systematically evaluating different comparison functions across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The core contribution lies in demonstrating the effectiveness of this framework and, notably, identifying element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as surprisingly strong comparison functions, outperforming more complex methods like neural networks and tensor networks in many cases.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are encouraging and the systematic evaluation of comparison functions is a valuable contribution, the paper feels somewhat exploratory rather than presenting a truly novel *model* or a significant theoretical advance. The compare-aggregate framework itself is not new, and the improvements, while consistent, aren't paradigm-shifting. The work feels more like a thorough engineering study than a conceptual breakthrough.

The paper adequately demonstrates the effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework by showing strong performance on four different datasets. The systematic evaluation of six comparison functions – including the less common element-wise operations – is a key strength. The ablation studies (though limited in scope, as discussed below) provide some insight into the importance of different components. The consistent outperformance of SUB and MULT across several tasks is a noteworthy empirical finding, suggesting a potentially overlooked aspect of sequence matching. The code availability is also a positive point.

However, several aspects weaken the overall impact. The model architecture itself is relatively standard, relying on established components like attention and CNNs. While these are well-implemented, there's a lack of novel architectural ideas. The ablation studies are insufficient; it would be beneficial to see more granular ablation, for example, examining the impact of the preprocessing layer *with and without* attention, or different CNN configurations. The justification for *why* SUB and MULT perform so well remains somewhat speculative. The authors suggest it’s because they capture semantic similarity, but a more in-depth analysis of the learned representations or the behavior of these functions would be valuable. Furthermore, the paper largely focuses on empirical results. Some theoretical discussion around why element-wise operations might be suitable for capturing semantic relationships could strengthen the work. Finally, the paper doesn’t adequately address limitations. For instance, how might this model perform on very long sequences where the computational cost of comparing all word pairs becomes prohibitive?  Are there any scenarios where more complex comparison functions would demonstrably outperform SUB and MULT?

Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** Explore *why* SUB and MULT work so well. Visualize the learned representations, analyze the values produced by these functions for different word pairs, and perhaps relate them to known semantic similarity metrics.
*   **More Comprehensive Ablation Studies:** Include more granular ablation experiments to isolate the impact of individual components.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more theoretical underpinning for the observed results. Why are element-wise operations suitable for this task?
*   **Discussion of Limitations:** Acknowledge and discuss the limitations of the approach, such as its potential scalability issues with long sequences.
*   **Comparison to More Recent Baselines:** Include more recent and state-of-the-art baselines where available, particularly for SNLI.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide an example of a case where a more complex comparison function (e.g., NTN) *does* outperform SUB or MULT, and if so, what characteristics distinguish that case?
*   Have you explored any techniques to mitigate the computational cost of pairwise comparisons for very long sequences?
*   The preprocessing layer is described as order-insensitive. How sensitive are the results to the choice of gate values (Wi, Wu, bi, bu)? Have you tried alternative preprocessing methods, such as incorporating positional embeddings?
*   Can you provide more insight into the attention weights learned by the model? Are there any consistent patterns that emerge across different datasets?
