This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, empirically demonstrating its effectiveness across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors systematically evaluate six comparison functions, finding that element-wise subtraction and multiplication perform best overall. The contributions are presenting a generally effective framework and identifying a surprisingly effective, yet previously underexplored, comparison function.

**Reject.** While the paper tackles a relevant problem and demonstrates promising results, the meticulous examination reveals numerous inconsistencies, methodological shortcomings, and a lack of rigorous comparison, ultimately undermining the confidence in the claims. The sheer volume of minor flaws suggests a lack of carefulness throughout the research process.

The core issue lies in the lack of detail and justification for numerous experimental choices. For instance, the preprocessing layer uses gate values, but the rationale behind this choice, especially given the claim of order-insensitivity and potential loss of contextual information, is not clearly explained. The ablation study (Table 4) is insufficient. Removing *either* the preprocessing or attention layer is too coarse-grained. What happens when both are removed? What happens when only *parts* of the preprocessing are removed (e.g., just the tanh activation)? The choice of dimensionality (l=150) seems arbitrary – was any sensitivity analysis performed? The dropout rate is mentioned as being tuned, but the specific values used for each dataset are not reported. 

Furthermore, the baselines are not uniformly compared. The paper "simply took the reported performance" which creates the possibility of using results from different implementations or slightly different experimental settings, making direct comparisons unreliable. The experimental setup itself feels incomplete. The authors state they did not update GloVe embeddings, which is fine, but justification for this choice is lacking. Why not try fine-tuning them? The CNN aggregation layer is described superficially. What kernel sizes are used? How many filters? The implementation details for each dataset are glossed over.

The analysis of results is also problematic. The statement that the model outperforms "all previous models" on InsuranceQA is misleading, as some baselines are only compared in terms of MAP or MRR and are not directly comparable to the accuracy metric used for the presented model. The visualization in Figure 2 is unconvincing and lacks quantitative support. Claiming it "shows" something without backing it up with numbers is weak. 

The related work section is adequate but lacks depth. The authors mention many relevant papers but do not provide a critical discussion of their strengths and weaknesses in relation to the proposed approach. The writing quality is also an issue, with several instances of awkward phrasing and unclear sentences.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision, though acceptance remains unlikely):**

*   **Reproducibility:** The paper lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the results. Provide a comprehensive list of hyperparameters, training settings, and implementation details for *each* dataset. Release the code with clear instructions.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study, investigating the impact of different components of the model (preprocessing layers, attention mechanisms, comparison functions, CNN parameters) individually and in combination.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Ensure a fair comparison with baselines by either reimplementing them using the same code base or carefully controlling for implementation differences. Report all relevant metrics for each baseline.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate that the performance improvements are statistically significant.
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform a detailed error analysis to identify the types of errors the model makes and gain insights into its limitations.
*   **Comparison Function Justification:** Explain *why* element-wise subtraction and multiplication work well. Is there a theoretical justification, or is it purely empirical?
*   **Preprocessing Rationale:** Provide a more compelling justification for the preprocessing layer, especially given the potential loss of contextual information.
*   **Clarity and Precision:** Revise the writing to improve clarity, precision, and conciseness.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed list of all hyperparameters used for each dataset, including learning rate, batch size, dropout rate, CNN kernel sizes, and number of filters?
2.  What were the results of a more comprehensive ablation study, including removing different components of the preprocessing layer?
3.  How were the baselines selected, and can you confirm that the reported results are directly comparable to those of your model in terms of evaluation metrics and experimental settings?
4.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the error analysis, including examples of incorrect predictions and the reasons behind them?
5.  What is the theoretical basis for the effectiveness of the element-wise subtraction and multiplication comparison functions?
