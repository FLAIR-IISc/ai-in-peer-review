This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, exploring the impact of different comparison functions within this framework. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their model across four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – and identify element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as particularly effective comparison functions. They claim to show the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework and contribute a systematic evaluation of comparison function choices.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous justification for the observed findings. The contribution feels incremental, and the exploration of comparison functions, while systematic, doesn't reveal particularly surprising or theoretically grounded insights. The reliance on empirical performance without a deeper analysis of *why* certain functions work better than others is a significant weakness.

The primary concern lies in the lack of substantial novelty. The "compare-aggregate" framework is already well-established (as the authors acknowledge), and the presented model is largely a variation within this existing paradigm. The core contribution – testing different comparison functions – feels more like an engineering study than a scientific investigation. While thorough empirical evaluation is valuable, it doesn’t compensate for the absence of strong theoretical motivation or a clear explanation of *why* element-wise operations outperform others. The paper states these functions are a "good middle ground," but doesn’t articulate what properties make them so. 

Furthermore, the experimental setup, while broad in terms of datasets, lacks depth in baseline comparison. While state-of-the-art results are reported on some datasets, the comparison is often with a heterogeneous set of models published over several years, making a fair assessment difficult. A more direct comparison against stronger, more recent baselines, particularly those also employing the compare-aggregate framework with sophisticated comparison mechanisms (e.g., more complex neural networks), is needed. The ablation studies, while present, are not comprehensive enough to fully understand the contribution of each component. The claim that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive yet important is somewhat contradictory and requires further clarification.

Additional feedback:

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper would benefit significantly from a more in-depth discussion of why the element-wise operations perform well. Is it related to capturing specific relationships between word embeddings? Could it be explained in terms of information preservation or noise reduction? A theoretical analysis, even speculative, would strengthen the contribution.
*   **Baseline Comparison:** Conduct a more rigorous comparison with recent, strong baselines, focusing on models that also utilize the compare-aggregate framework. Consider implementing and comparing against a few key baselines directly.
*   **Ablation Study:** Expand the ablation study to include more granular analysis of the preprocessing layer, attention mechanism, and CNN aggregation layer. What is the impact of different CNN configurations?
*   **Error Analysis:** A qualitative error analysis could provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of the model. Examining misclassified examples might reveal patterns and suggest avenues for improvement.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model to different hyperparameters, particularly for the comparison functions.
*   **Generalizability:** Discuss the limitations of the findings. Are the observed benefits of element-wise operations specific to the datasets used, or are they likely to generalize to other sequence matching tasks?

Questions for the authors:

1.  Can you elaborate on the theoretical motivation behind the superior performance of the SUB and MULT comparison functions? What specific properties of these operations make them well-suited for sequence matching?
2.  Why was a more comprehensive comparison against recent, state-of-the-art baselines within the compare-aggregate framework not conducted?
3.  The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet suggests it plays a crucial role. Can you reconcile these seemingly contradictory statements?
4.  Could you provide examples of specific error cases where the model fails and explain why the chosen comparison function might be inadequate in those situations?
5.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of word embeddings (e.g., GloVe vs. Word2Vec vs. fastText)? Did you experiment with different embeddings?
