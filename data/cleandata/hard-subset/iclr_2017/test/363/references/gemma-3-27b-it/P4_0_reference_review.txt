This paper investigates the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching, aiming to demonstrate its effectiveness across multiple datasets and to identify a strong comparison function within the framework. The authors test their model on four datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – and evaluate six different comparison functions. They find that element-wise subtraction and multiplication generally perform best.

**Reject** with a recommendation for revision. While the paper presents a reasonable exploration of the compare-aggregate framework and demonstrates competitive performance, the novelty is limited, and the experimental analysis lacks sufficient depth to fully justify the conclusions drawn.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of substantial novelty. The compare-aggregate framework itself is not new (citing Wang & Jiang, 2016b, He & Lin, 2016, and Parikh et al., 2016), and the experimentation focuses on varying the comparison function within that existing structure. While identifying the optimal comparison function is a valid contribution, it doesn’t represent a breakthrough.  The claim that their work demonstrates the "general effectiveness" of the framework feels weak given that prior works *already* demonstrated effectiveness.  The empirical contribution focuses on confirming this effectiveness on a new set of datasets, rather than introducing a fundamentally new approach.

Second, the experimental analysis, while comprehensive in terms of dataset coverage, lacks depth in several areas. The ablation studies (Table 4) are relatively limited. For instance, removing the preprocessing layer or attention layer individually doesn’t provide a nuanced understanding of their interplay. The analysis of *why* certain comparison functions work better than others is largely superficial – it states that element-wise operations are a “good compromise” but doesn't offer a strong theoretical or intuitive explanation beyond that. Furthermore, the hyperparameter tuning process is only briefly described, raising concerns about the robustness of the results. The claim that the model outperforms prior baselines requires more scrutiny, as some baselines are simply cited without clear implementation details or access to their code for direct comparison. Finally, the visualizations in Figure 2, while potentially helpful, are not thoroughly discussed within the main text.

Additional feedback:

*   **Clarify Novelty:** The introduction should more clearly articulate the specific gap in the literature that this work addresses. Is it the breadth of datasets tested? The systematic comparison of comparison functions? This needs to be explicitly stated.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** Provide a more in-depth theoretical justification for why the SUB and MULT functions work well. Connect these functions to properties of semantic similarity or the specific tasks being addressed.
*   **More Detailed Ablation Studies:** Conduct more extensive ablation studies, potentially examining combinations of layer removals and varying the size of the CNN aggregation layer.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Detail the hyperparameter tuning process used for each dataset and each comparison function. Report the range of values explored and the criteria used for selection.
*   **Baseline Details:** Provide more details about the baseline models, including links to code or clear implementation descriptions.
*   **Visualization Discussion:** Elaborate on the insights gained from the visualizations in Figure 2. What specific patterns are observed, and how do they relate to the model’s performance?
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for the results in Table 3 to support the claims of superiority.

Questions for the authors:

1.  Could you provide a more detailed justification for why you chose to focus on element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as a "good compromise" between neural networks and similarity measures?
2.  What specific steps were taken to ensure a fair comparison between your model and the baselines, especially those for which you did not have access to the code?
3.  Can you elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process? What search strategies were employed, and what criteria were used to select the best hyperparameters?
4.  The preprocessing layer is described as order-insensitive. Is there any consideration given to the potential loss of contextual information, and why is this tradeoff acceptable for the tasks considered?
5. How sensitive are the results to different word embedding initializations? Were multiple initializations tried, and if so, how consistent were the findings?
