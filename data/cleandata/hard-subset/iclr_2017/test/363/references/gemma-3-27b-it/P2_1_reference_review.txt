## Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks. The authors argue that this framework is generally applicable and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within it, applied to four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). They find that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform surprisingly well, often outperforming more complex neural network-based comparison methods.  The paper demonstrates competitive or state-of-the-art results on the tested datasets and provides valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function within the compare-aggregate paradigm.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated and thoroughly-conducted empirical study. The results are compelling and provide useful guidance for future work in sequence matching. The systematic comparison of different comparison functions is a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Clear Problem Definition and Motivation:** The paper clearly defines the sequence matching problem and provides a good overview of existing approaches, highlighting the limitations of previous work concerning the generalizability of compare-aggregate models and the under-exploration of comparison functions.
* **Strong Empirical Evaluation:** The evaluation across four diverse datasets is a key strength. This demonstrates the general applicability of the proposed model, addressing a core limitation identified in the introduction. The inclusion of strong baselines provides a good benchmark for comparison.
* **Novel Findings:** The discovery that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex comparison functions is a noteworthy and counterintuitive finding. This suggests that capturing semantic similarity directly, rather than relying on complex neural networks, is crucial for this task.
* **Rigorous Experimentation:**  The ablation studies, although brief, add credibility to the findings and offer insights into the relative importance of different components of the model (preprocessing and attention).
* **Code Availability:** Providing publicly available code enhances reproducibility and facilitates further research.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** While the authors identify SUB and MULT as performing well, a deeper analysis of *why* they work better than other methods would be beneficial. For example, exploring the learned representations produced by each comparison function through visualization or probing could provide further insight.
* **Statistical Significance:** While the paper presents results, including statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests or ANOVA) would strengthen the claims and demonstrate the robustness of the findings.
* **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process.  Were all hyperparameters tuned equally across datasets and comparison functions?
* **SNLI Performance:** The SNLI results are competitive but not state-of-the-art.  A discussion of the potential reasons for this, and whether the model architecture could be adapted specifically for this task, would be helpful.
* **Discussion of Limitations:**  Explicitly discuss the limitations of the study. For example, the choice of word embeddings (GloVe) might impact results, and the comparison functions were limited to those explored in this work.
* **Related Work:** Expand the related work section to include more recent advances in sequence matching, particularly those leveraging transformers or attention mechanisms beyond the models cited.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Regarding the choice of GloVe embeddings:**  Did you experiment with other pre-trained embeddings (e.g., Word2Vec, fastText, or contextualized embeddings like BERT) and, if so, how did the performance change?
2. **Regarding the ablation studies:** Can you elaborate on the specific details of the ablation studies? What metrics were used to assess the impact of removing each component, and what were the specific observed changes in performance?
3. **Regarding the element-wise operations:**  Do you have any hypothesis about why the SUB and MULT functions are so effective?  Is it related to their ability to capture directional differences or relationships between word embeddings?
4. **Regarding the SNLI dataset:**  What modifications, if any, would you consider to improve the performance of the model on the SNLI dataset, given its distinct characteristics compared to the question answering tasks?
5. **Regarding computational cost:** What is the computational overhead of using SUB and MULT compared to NN or NTN? Does this impact scalability to longer sequences?




Overall, this is a strong paper with valuable contributions to the field of sequence matching.  The systematic evaluation and insightful findings make it a worthy addition to ICLR.  Addressing the suggested feedback would further strengthen the paper and enhance its impact.
