This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for matching text sequences and investigates the impact of different comparison functions within this framework. The authors evaluate their model on four diverse datasets – MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI – and demonstrate strong performance, highlighting the effectiveness of element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as comparison functions.

**Decision: Reject**

While the paper presents a reasonably well-executed empirical study, the contributions feel incremental and lack substantial novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core idea of the compare-aggregate framework is well-established, and the primary contribution – the systematic evaluation of comparison functions – doesn't reveal fundamentally surprising or insightful results. The finding that element-wise operations perform well isn't particularly groundbreaking, given their connection to established similarity measures like Euclidean distance and cosine similarity.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The compare-aggregate framework has been previously explored (citations provided are adequate). The paper doesn't introduce a new architectural component or theoretical insight that significantly advances the field. It’s more of an engineering investigation of existing concepts.
2. **Incremental Findings:** The observation that SUB and MULT perform well is interesting, but the paper doesn’t offer a strong *explanation* for why these functions are superior. The intuition provided (connection to Euclidean distance/cosine similarity) feels intuitive and doesn't constitute a novel theoretical contribution.
3. **Lack of Depth in Analysis:** The ablation study (Table 4) is limited. It would be valuable to see ablations on the attention mechanism and preprocessing layer *individually* for each dataset, not just combined. Furthermore, analyzing *where* the model succeeds and fails would strengthen the conclusions. For example, are specific types of questions or sentence pairs where SUB/MULT perform particularly well?
4. **Presentation of Results:** Table 3 presents a lot of numbers, but lacks a clear takeaway. It’s difficult to quickly discern the statistical significance of the improvements and which comparisons are most important. More focused statistical testing and a clearer presentation of key results would be beneficial.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical justification for why element-wise operations might be well-suited for sequence matching. Could this be linked to properties of semantic similarity spaces or information theory?
* **Error Analysis:** Include a qualitative error analysis. Show examples where the model fails, and discuss potential reasons for these failures. This will provide more actionable insights.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters. A simple sensitivity analysis would improve the robustness of the findings.
* **Computational Cost:** Briefly discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions.  Are there tradeoffs between performance and efficiency?
* **Code Availability:** While code is mentioned as being available, a link to a persistent repository (e.g., GitHub) should be included directly in the paper, and versioned.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive.  Is this a deliberate design choice, and if so, why? Have you experimented with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., using LSTMs/GRUs)?
2. The ablation study combines removal of the attention and preprocessing layers. Can you provide results for removing each layer individually for each dataset?
3.  Could you elaborate on the connection between the performance of SUB and MULT and the underlying geometric properties of word embeddings? Is there a specific relationship between these functions and established similarity metrics beyond the intuitive connection to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity?
4.  The paper presents results on four datasets. Were any other datasets considered and rejected, and if so, why? Were there datasets where the proposed model performed poorly or where the findings differed?
