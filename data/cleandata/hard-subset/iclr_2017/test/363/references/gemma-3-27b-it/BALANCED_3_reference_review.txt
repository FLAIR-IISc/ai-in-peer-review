This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for matching text sequences, evaluating it on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and systematically comparing six different comparison functions. The core idea – exploring different comparison functions within a compare-aggregate framework – is a valuable contribution.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of convincing novelty and substantial improvement over existing baselines, especially given the extensive experimentation. While the systematic evaluation of comparison functions is positive, the results don't demonstrate a compelling advantage that justifies a publication at ICLR. Secondly, the paper lacks sufficient depth in analyzing *why* certain comparison functions perform better, beyond simply stating that they do.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The “compare-aggregate” framework itself is well-established (citations to Wang & Jiang 2016b, He & Lin 2016, Parikh et al. 2016).  The core contribution of exploring different comparison functions feels incremental, particularly as some functions (e.g., cosine similarity) were already briefly touched upon in He & Lin (2016). The paper doesn’t introduce a fundamentally new architectural component or learning paradigm.

2. **Incremental Performance Gains:** While the model performs well (and sometimes achieves SOTA), the improvements over existing baselines aren't dramatic, and are dataset-dependent.  The results in Table 3 show improvements, but many are marginal (e.g., a few percentage points on some datasets). Given the significant computational cost of experimentation across four datasets, these gains seem modest.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Comparison Functions:**  The paper identifies that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform well. However, it doesn't provide a deep, insightful explanation of *why* these functions are effective.  Is it because they capture specific semantic relationships better? Do they mitigate the vanishing gradient problem? A more detailed analysis of the learned parameters within these comparison functions, or visualizations of their activations, would strengthen the findings. The discussion about the connection to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity feels superficial.

4. **Lack of Ablation Study Depth:** Table 4 provides an ablation study, but it's limited. It focuses only on removing the preprocessing or attention layers. It would be beneficial to see ablation studies specifically focused on the comparison function itself (e.g., removing the ReLU activation in NN or NTN).

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

* **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper would be strengthened by a more theoretical motivation for the choice of comparison functions. Could insights from representation learning or information theory guide the selection of these functions?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the performance to different hyperparameters, especially for the comparison functions. Are the optimal hyperparameters consistent across datasets?
* **Error Analysis:** Perform a detailed error analysis to identify the types of examples where the model fails.  This could provide clues as to the limitations of the compare-aggregate framework and suggest avenues for future research.
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the different comparison functions. Are there trade-offs between accuracy and efficiency?
* **Contextual Information:** Although the authors acknowledge the lack of context in the preprocessing step, explore incorporating contextual information, perhaps through a bidirectional RNN or transformer-based embeddings, and analyzing the impact.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Given that He & Lin (2016) already experimented with cosine similarity and Euclidean distance, what specifically motivated the choice to *systematically* evaluate these functions alongside other less common ones?
2.  Why do the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform so well, particularly in comparison to more complex functions like NTN? Can you provide a more detailed explanation beyond stating they're a “good compromise”?
3.  The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive. Did you experiment with order-sensitive preprocessing methods (e.g., LSTMs) and, if so, what were the results?
4. Could you provide a more in-depth discussion about the learned parameters within the best-performing comparison function (SUBMULT+NN)? What do these parameters reveal about the semantic relationships being captured?
5. The error analysis would provide useful insights. Can you share some examples of cases where the model fails, and what might be causing these failures?
