This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for matching text sequences and systematically evaluates different comparison functions within this framework across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The core idea is well-motivated, building upon previous work demonstrating the effectiveness of comparing smaller textual units before aggregation. The paper’s contribution lies in its empirical verification of the framework’s generalizability and its in-depth comparison of different comparison functions, identifying element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as surprisingly effective.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of sufficient novelty and a disconnect between the reported performance and the claims of significant findings. While the systematic evaluation is a positive aspect, the performance improvements achieved are incremental and do not demonstrably outperform strong baselines on most tasks, especially given the amount of experimentation. Secondly, the paper presents results without adequately addressing the hyperparameter tuning process, specifically in how choices were made across the datasets and comparison functions, raising concerns about the fairness of the comparisons.

The paper effectively summarizes existing “compare-aggregate” approaches and positions its work within the literature. The motivation for exploring different comparison functions is reasonable, stemming from the observation that prior work often defaults to standard feedforward networks without thorough investigation. The experimental setup, involving four datasets, is commendable in its attempt to assess generalizability.

However, the performance gains are not compelling enough to justify the extensive experimentation. For example, on SNLI, the results are competitive but not state-of-the-art, and the improvement over baselines is small. On WikiQA, while improvements are reported, the dataset is acknowledged as small, limiting the significance of the conclusions. The claim that the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) “work the best across the different datasets” feels overstated given the mixed results and the relatively small margins of improvement. The ablation study (Table 4) is helpful but doesn't provide a strong justification for the superior performance of SUB and MULT. 

The paper lacks a discussion of statistical significance for the performance differences observed. Simply reporting accuracy/MAP/MRR values without statistical tests makes it difficult to determine whether the observed improvements are genuine or due to random chance. The effect of the preprocessing layer is mentioned but isn’t clearly quantified. Additionally, the paper doesn't provide enough detail regarding the hyperparameter optimization process. Were the hyperparameters tuned for each comparison function independently? Were the same hyperparameters used across all datasets? These details are crucial for evaluating the fairness and reliability of the comparisons. The visualizations in Figure 2 are interesting but lack a clear connection to the quantitative results.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Include statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate the robustness of the performance differences.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Provide a detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process for each comparison function and dataset. This should include the search space, optimization method, and any regularization techniques used.
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:** Expand the ablation study to include more granular analyses. For example, investigate the impact of different attention mechanisms or preprocessing layers.
*   **Error Analysis:** Conduct an error analysis to identify common failure cases and provide insights into the limitations of the model.
*   **Comparison Function Analysis:** Provide a more theoretical justification for why SUB and MULT perform well. Is there a connection to specific properties of the datasets or tasks?
*   **Preprocessing Layer Investigation:** Conduct a more rigorous evaluation of the preprocessing layer, perhaps by comparing performance with and without it, and with different configurations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide statistical significance tests to support your performance claims?
2.  Please elaborate on the hyperparameter tuning process. Specifically, how did you choose the hyperparameters for each comparison function and dataset?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the limitations of the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) and scenarios where they might not perform well?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of your model to the choice of word embeddings (GloVe)? Have you experimented with other embedding methods?
5.  What specific types of errors does the model make, and how do these errors vary across different comparison functions?
