## Review for ICLR 2024: A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

**1. Summary of Claims & Contributions:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness of the “compare-aggregate” framework for sequence matching across diverse NLP tasks. The authors argue that this framework is broadly applicable and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions within it – including less-explored element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) – on four datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI. They demonstrate that their model achieves strong performance, often outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods, and that element-wise comparison functions perform surprisingly well across tasks. The code is publicly available, promoting reproducibility.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept (with minor revisions)**. The paper presents a solid empirical study of a widely used framework, providing valuable insights into the importance of the comparison function. The results are compelling, demonstrating both the general effectiveness of the compare-aggregate approach and the surprisingly strong performance of simple element-wise operations.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Relevance and Motivation:** The paper addresses a fundamental problem in NLP – sequence matching – and builds upon a well-established and increasingly popular framework. The motivation for systematically investigating different comparison functions is sound, as previous work has largely focused on task-specific models without a comprehensive analysis of this crucial component.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The authors demonstrate competitive or state-of-the-art performance on four different datasets, spanning question answering and textual entailment. This supports their claim that the compare-aggregate framework is broadly applicable.
* **Novelty of Findings:** The finding that SUB and MULT perform well, often surpassing more complex neural network-based comparison functions, is a significant and unexpected result. This challenges the assumption that more complex models are always necessary and provides a valuable alternative for practical applications.
* **Thorough Experimentation:** The authors compare their model against relevant baselines and provide an ablation study (Table 4), suggesting that the attention and preprocessing layers are important, and that the word-level comparison is vital.
* **Reproducibility:** The public availability of the code is a significant strength.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Deeper Dive into the Success of SUB/MULT:** While the results show SUB and MULT performing well, the paper lacks a detailed explanation *why* these functions are effective. Is it the preservation of dimensional information, as suggested in the discussion? Providing a more theoretical or intuitive understanding could strengthen the paper significantly.  Perhaps an analysis of the learned representations resulting from these functions compared to the others would provide insights.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While ablation studies are present, a more comprehensive analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity for the comparison functions would be useful.  Are the optimal hyperparameters consistent across datasets?  Are the simple comparison functions less sensitive to hyperparameter tuning?
* **Computational Cost:**  Briefly discuss the computational cost of different comparison functions. While model performance is vital, practical considerations like efficiency are important.
* **SNLI Performance:** The SNLI results are competitive but not leading. A more detailed analysis comparing the model to the stronger SNLI baselines (e.g., EBIM+TreeLSTM) would be valuable.  Was there any difficulty in replicating the setup of the more complex baselines for fair comparison?
* **Figures:** Figure 2 is a good start to visualization, but could be extended. Visualizing the attention weights alongside the comparison results would offer further insight into how the model makes its decisions.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Why do SUB and MULT perform so well?** Do the learned representations from these simple functions exhibit specific properties that contribute to their success?
* **What is the computational cost difference between the different comparison functions, especially for longer sequences?**
* **You mention the importance of word-level comparison. Could you elaborate on the impact of different word embedding choices on the performance of the different comparison functions?**  Are some functions more sensitive to the quality of the embeddings?
* **Regarding the SNLI results, can you provide further detail on the comparison against EBIM+TreeLSTM?** Were there any limitations in replicating the setup of this baseline?
* **The preprocessing layer seems important. How sensitive is the model to the parameters within this preprocessing layer (Wi, Wu, bi, bu)?**



In conclusion, this is a well-written and thoroughly researched paper that makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of sequence matching. The findings regarding the effectiveness of simple comparison functions are particularly noteworthy and have the potential to influence future work in this area.  Addressing the feedback outlined above would further strengthen the paper and make it even more impactful.
