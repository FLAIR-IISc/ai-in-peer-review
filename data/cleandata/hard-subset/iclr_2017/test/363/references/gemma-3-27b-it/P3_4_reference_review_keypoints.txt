- Paper investigates the compare-aggregate framework across multiple NLP tasks and evaluates six comparison functions, claiming element-wise SUB and MULT are most effective.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Clear motivation: well-defined importance of sequence matching and gaps in prior compare-aggregate evaluations.
- Strong empirical results: SUB and MULT consistently outperform more complex methods on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI; ablations and tables are convincing.
- Thorough experimentation: systematic comparison of six comparison functions and task-specific adaptations demonstrate rigor.
- Reproducibility: authors release code, improving impact.
- Request to discuss computational cost: clarify if SUB/MULT are significantly cheaper than NN/NTN.
- Suggest adding qualitative error analysis to reveal strengths/weaknesses and failure modes of SUB/MULT.
- Ask for deeper connection to semantic similarity metrics and explanation why element-wise ops beat cosine similarity.
- Visualization improvement: highlight specific words contributing most to CNN attention in Figure 2.
- Related work: expand discussion on contextualized embeddings (BERT, RoBERTa) and their impact within this framework.
- Question on preprocessing layer: explain why an order-insensitive preprocessing works well when word order matters and the role of gate values.
- Question on comparison function choice: justify why SUB and MULT are good compromises beyond empirical positioning.
- SNLI question: explain challenges causing non–state-of-the-art SNLI results and potential improvements.
- Ablation study request: provide detailed breakdown for removing both attention and preprocessing simultaneously.
