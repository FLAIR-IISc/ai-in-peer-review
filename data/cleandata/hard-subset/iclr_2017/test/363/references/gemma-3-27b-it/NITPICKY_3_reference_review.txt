This paper introduces a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, empirically demonstrating its effectiveness across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions, identifying element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as particularly effective. The contributions lie in validating the generalizability of the “compare-aggregate” framework and providing insights into effective comparison function design.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a reasonable approach and empirical results, the level of rigor and attention to detail is insufficient for acceptance at ICLR. The paper suffers from numerous methodological shortcomings, inconsistencies, and a lack of thorough analysis that, cumulatively, undermine the credibility of the findings.

The primary concerns stem from the experimental setup and reporting. Firstly, the hyperparameter tuning process is inadequately described. The authors state they "tuned the dropout on the embedding layer" for WikiQA and learning rate/batch size, but provide no details on the search space, method (e.g., grid search, random search), or criteria used. The lack of such details is particularly problematic for a study focused on empirical validation. Secondly, the baseline comparisons are uneven. For some datasets (SNLI, WikiQA), strong baselines are included, while others (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) rely on older or less competitive models. This makes it difficult to assess the true improvement offered by the proposed approach. Thirdly, the ablation studies (Table 4) are limited in scope. While showing the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers, they do not investigate the individual contribution of different components *within* those layers. For example, ablating the entire preprocessing layer is not particularly informative; analyzing the impact of the gate values specifically would be more insightful. Fourthly, the visualization in Figure 2 is superficial and adds limited value. Simply highlighting matched words does not provide meaningful insights into *why* the model makes specific predictions. A more rigorous analysis, such as attention weight analysis or error case studies, would be more valuable. Fifthly, the paper claims the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet uses tanh activation. Tanh can introduce some order sensitivity due to the changing gradient flow. This warrants further discussion. Finally, the writing contains several inconsistencies and minor errors, further detracting from the overall quality of the paper. For example, the description of Equation 10 contains variable name inconsistencies, and the referencing is sometimes sloppy (e.g., referring to 'Luong et al.,? \Q2015\E').

Here are additional points that would significantly improve the paper:

*   **Reproducibility:** Providing detailed implementation and training details is crucial for reproducibility. This includes specific hyperparameter settings, optimization parameters, and hardware configuration.
*   **Statistical Significance:** The paper lacks any discussion of statistical significance. Reporting p-values or confidence intervals for the performance improvements would strengthen the conclusions.
*   **Error Analysis:** A thorough error analysis, examining cases where the model fails, would provide valuable insights into its limitations and potential areas for improvement.
*   **Comparison Function Analysis:** The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of why the element-wise comparison functions perform well. Is it due to their simplicity, their ability to capture specific types of semantic relationships, or other factors?
* **Ablation Study Expansion:** Include ablations on the attention mechanism (e.g. different attention types) and the preprocessing layer’s parameters (gate values).

I have several questions for the authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the hyperparameter tuning process, including the search space, method, and criteria used for each dataset?
2.  Why were certain baselines selected over others for MovieQA and InsuranceQA? Would the results change if stronger baselines were included?
3.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using tanh in the preprocessing layer, given that it may introduce some order sensitivity?
4.  What specific types of errors does the model make, and can you provide examples?
5.  Could you provide the code used for the experiment to allow for reproducibility?
