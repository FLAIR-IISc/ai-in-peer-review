This paper proposes a “compare-aggregate” model for sequence matching, systematically evaluating different comparison functions across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors argue for the general effectiveness of this framework and identify element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) as strong comparison function candidates. The paper contributes an empirical demonstration of the framework’s versatility and a comparative analysis of comparison functions, alongside code release.

**Reject.** While the paper explores a relevant and established framework, the meticulous nature of my review reveals a significant number of inconsistencies, unsubstantiated claims, and a lack of rigor in experimental details that ultimately undermine confidence in the results. The presentation is also frequently imprecise.

Here's a detailed breakdown:

1. **Lack of Novelty & Justification for Extensive Experimentation:** The “compare-aggregate” framework is already well-established. The core contribution lies in the empirical comparison of comparison functions. However, the motivation for testing *six* functions feels arbitrary. The rationale provided ("a good compromise between highly flexible functions and highly restrictive functions") is vague. Why are these specific six chosen, and what principled basis underlies this selection beyond prior work (He & Lin, 2016)?  The claim that previous studies "did not pay much attention" is an overstatement; He & Lin (2016) *explicitly* experimented with cosine similarity, Euclidean distance, and dot product. The novelty of the paper hinges on a more comprehensive, yet insufficiently justified, exploration.

2. **Experimental Rigor & Reporting Issues:**
    * **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The description of hyperparameter tuning is inadequate. The authors mention tuning dropout for WikiQA but provide no detail. For other datasets, it is stated hyperparameters were *not* tuned. This makes direct comparison across datasets questionable. How sensitive are results to learning rate and batch size?
    * **Preprocessing Details:** The preprocessing layer’s purpose (“to use some gate values to control the importance of different words”) is stated but lacks a clear explanation. The use of outer product with ‘eQ’ and ‘eA’ is also unexplained and makes the mathematical notation confusing. What are the dimensions of these vectors and why is this operation necessary? The justification for why an RNN wasn't used (computational expense) seems weak and wasn't adequately explored given the limited sequence lengths of some datasets.
    * **CNN Aggregation:** The CNN aggregation layer is described as "one-layer," but the filter sizes, number of filters, and activation function are not specified. These choices significantly impact performance.
    * **Baseline Comparison:**  The baselines reported are often from different years.  A fair comparison requires re-implementing or using a standardized library for all baselines to ensure consistent implementation details. Simply stating “we took the reported performance” is insufficient.
    * **Table 3 Results:** The results in Table 3 lack sufficient statistical significance testing. Reporting accuracy alone without standard deviations or p-values weakens the claim of performance improvement. Are the observed improvements statistically significant?
    * **Ablation Study (Table 4):** The ablation study is very limited. It only tests the removal of the preprocessing and attention layers. What about ablating specific comparison functions or the CNN layer?

3. **Imprecise Language and Lack of Clarity:** The paper contains several instances of imprecise language. For instance, "Some simple comparison functions can achieve better performance than the neural networks or neural tensor network comparison functions," is a vague statement. Which datasets specifically demonstrate this trend?  The phrasing throughout lacks the necessary precision expected of a rigorous scientific paper. The discussion of Figure 2 is descriptive but lacks analytical depth – it simply states what the visualization *shows* rather than *explaining why* it's significant.

4. **Minor Issues:**
    * The link to the code repository is broken (https://github.com/shuohangwang/SeqMatchSeq returns a 404).
    * References are not consistently formatted.



**Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarify the motivation:** Develop a stronger argument for the selection of comparison functions beyond simply referencing prior work.
* **Provide full experimental details:** Report all hyperparameters, training procedures, and implementation details to ensure reproducibility.
* **Perform statistical significance testing:** Include standard deviations or p-values in the results to demonstrate the significance of observed improvements.
* **Conduct a more thorough ablation study:** Evaluate the contribution of individual components of the model.
* **Revisit the literature review:** Accurately represent the contributions of prior work and avoid overstated claims.
* **Address the broken code link.**

**Questions for the Authors:**

* Can you provide a more detailed justification for the choice of the six comparison functions?
* What hyperparameter search strategy was used, and why were parameters not tuned for all datasets?
* What specific filter sizes and number of filters were used in the CNN aggregation layer?
* Can you provide statistical significance testing results for the performance improvements reported in Table 3?
* Were attempts made to reproduce the baseline results? If so, what were the findings? If not, why not?
* How sensitive are the results to different word embedding initializations?
