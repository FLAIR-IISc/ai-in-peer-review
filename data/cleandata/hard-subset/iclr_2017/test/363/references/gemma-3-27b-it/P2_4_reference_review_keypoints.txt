- Paper studies the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching, evaluating six comparison functions across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).
- Key finding: simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) often outperform more complex neural comparison functions.
- Model achieves competitive performance with state-of-the-art methods and the authors provide publicly available code.
- Recommendation: Accept with minor revisions based on solid empirical investigation and surprising, useful findings.
- Strength: Well-motivated problem statement emphasizing the prevalence of compare-aggregate in NLP.
- Strength: Strong empirical evaluation across diverse datasets supports broad applicability claims.
- Strength: Novel, counter-intuitive result showing simple element-wise comparisons are highly effective.
- Strength: Clear presentation, organized paper, and reproducible code.
- Strength: Ablation studies give insight into component importance.
- Improvement: Provide deeper analysis on why SUB and MULT work (mechanisms behind their effectiveness).
- Improvement: Add error analysis to show failure modes and guide future work.
- Improvement: Conduct broader hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (embedding size, CNN filters, etc.).
- Improvement: Discuss computational cost of different comparison functions, especially for long sequences.
- Improvement: Update related work to include more recent approaches (e.g., contrastive learning, learned similarity metrics).
- Question for authors: Can you elaborate on hypotheses or evidence explaining why SUB and MULT perform well?
- Question for authors: Were there consistent error patterns across datasets and can you give example failure cases?
- Question for authors: What is the computational complexity and practical cost of each comparison function for varying sequence lengths?
- Question for authors: Why is the preprocessing layer order-insensitive for MovieQA/InsuranceQA but important for WikiQA/SNLI—what dataset characteristics drive this?
- Question for authors: Provide more details on hyperparameter search ranges (embedding dimension, learning rate, batch size).
