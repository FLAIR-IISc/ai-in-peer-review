### **1. Summary**

This paper proposes a general model for text sequence matching based on the "compare-aggregate" framework. The core principle is to decompose the matching problem into two stages: first, a local, word-level comparison between two sequences, followed by an aggregation of these local comparison results to make a final decision. The paper's primary contributions are twofold. First, it demonstrates the general effectiveness of this framework by applying a single, unified model architecture to four distinct and challenging tasks: three different question-answering datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and a natural language inference task (SNLI). The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all four benchmarks. Second, the paper conducts a systematic and insightful empirical study of six different comparison functions that can be used in the "compare" step. It finds that simple, element-wise operations—specifically subtraction (SUB) and multiplication (MULT)—are surprisingly powerful, often outperforming more complex and parameter-heavy alternatives like neural networks or tensor networks.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Generalization:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the "compare-aggregate" framework is a robust and generally applicable solution for a wide range of sequence matching problems. Successfully applying one core model to four diverse tasks with state-of-the-art results is a significant strength and validates the paper's central hypothesis.
2.  **Valuable Systematic Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The systematic evaluation of different comparison functions is a valuable contribution to the field. The finding that simple, parameter-free, element-wise functions like SUB and MULT are highly effective is an important, non-obvious insight that offers practical guidance for future model design, favoring simpler and potentially more efficient approaches.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

**Strengths:**
*   **Broad Empirical Evaluation:** The choice of four datasets spanning different domains and task formulations (answer selection, answer ranking, NLI, machine comprehension) provides a strong testbed for the model's generality. The consistent high performance across these tasks is impressive and convincingly supports the authors' claims.
*   **Rigorous Comparison:** The study of six distinct comparison functions, ranging from simple distance metrics (EUCCOS) to complex parameterized models (NN, NTN), is methodical and well-executed. This analysis moves beyond simply proposing a new architecture and instead provides a deeper understanding of a critical model component. The results, particularly the success of `SUBMULT+NN`, provide a clear and actionable takeaway.
*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written, with the model architecture and experimental setup described clearly. The authors' decision to make their code publicly available is commendable and enhances the impact and reproducibility of their work.
*   **Strong Results:** The model achieves state-of-the-art performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and performance on SNLI is competitive with top models from that period. This demonstrates that the proposed implementation of the compare-aggregate framework is highly effective.

**Weaknesses:**
*   **Limited Architectural Novelty:** The overall model architecture bears a strong resemblance to prior work, most notably the Decomposable Attention Model by Parikh et al. (2016). While the authors acknowledge this connection, the paper's primary contribution lies in its broad evaluation and systematic analysis rather than in architectural innovation. The distinctions from prior work (e.g., one-directional attention, CNN aggregation) could be more clearly motivated and analyzed.
*   **Preprocessing Layer Design:** The preprocessing layer (Eq. 1), which applies a position-unaware gate to word embeddings, is a key component but its design is not fully justified. The authors note that an RNN would be better but computationally expensive, yet they incorporate an LSTM for the SNLI task only. The rationale for this task-specific design choice is unclear and feels somewhat ad-hoc. A more consistent approach or a clearer explanation of the trade-offs would strengthen the paper.
*   **Analysis Could Be Deeper:** The qualitative analysis in Section 3.4 is a good start, but the interpretation of the CNN visualization is high-level. The paper would benefit from a more in-depth analysis, perhaps including a quantitative study of the attention weights or an error analysis to understand the failure modes of the different comparison functions.

### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the work and are not critical to my decision to accept.
*   **Direct Comparison to Decomposable Attention:** On the SNLI dataset, it would be highly informative to include a direct comparison to the Decomposable Attention model. This would help disentangle the performance contributions of the different comparison functions versus other architectural differences (e.g., CNN vs. FFN aggregation, uni- vs. bi-directional attention).
*   **Ablation of Preprocessing:** The paper ablates the preprocessing and attention layers, but a more detailed analysis of the preprocessing layer itself would be useful. For example, showing the performance impact of using a standard BiLSTM encoder on one of the QA tasks (even if it's slower) would help quantify the trade-off between performance and efficiency that motivated the current design.
*   **Computational Efficiency:** A brief discussion on the computational complexity and training times of the different comparison functions would be a valuable addition. Quantifying the efficiency gains of using parameter-free functions like SUB and MULT would further bolster the argument for their use.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if you could clarify the following points in your author response:
1.  Could you elaborate on the motivation for incorporating an LSTM into the preprocessing layer for the SNLI task but not for the other three tasks? Did you experiment with using LSTMs for the QA tasks, and if so, how did it affect performance and computational cost?
2.  Your model utilizes one-directional attention (from sequence A to sequence Q). This is in contrast to some contemporary models like Parikh et al. (2016) which use two-directional attention. What was the rationale behind this design choice, and did you explore a two-directional variant?
3.  Regarding the MovieQA task, the model matches the Question to the Plot and the Answer to the Plot independently, before concatenating the results. Have you considered an architecture with more direct interaction, for example, by first creating a question-aware plot representation and then matching the answer against that?
