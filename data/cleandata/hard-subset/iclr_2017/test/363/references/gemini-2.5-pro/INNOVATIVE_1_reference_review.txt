### Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**Summary of Contributions**
This paper presents a systematic exploration of the "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language sequence matching. The authors propose a general model architecture and apply it consistently across four different tasks: three distinct question-answering datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one textual entailment dataset (SNLI). The primary contribution is not the framework itself, which the authors acknowledge has appeared in recent literature, but rather a focused and novel investigation into the local comparison function used within this framework. The paper systematically evaluates six different comparison functions, ranging from standard neural networks to simpler, distance-based metrics. The key finding is that simple, element-wise operations—specifically subtraction and multiplication (SUB, MULT)—are surprisingly effective, often outperforming more complex, parameter-heavy alternatives and achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all four tasks.

**Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The core strength lies in its bold and impactful central claim: that the choice of comparison function is critical, and that simple, semantically intuitive functions can be superior to more complex, generic ones. This work moves beyond simply proposing another new architecture and instead provides a valuable, generalizable insight into an emerging class of models. This is precisely the kind of work that can influence the direction of future research by promoting simpler, more interpretable, and highly effective components.

**Supporting Arguments**
The paper's value is rooted in its novelty and conceptual clarity, which I prioritize highly.

1.  **Novelty in Simplicity:** While the overall compare-aggregate structure is not entirely new, the paper's main contribution—the systematic study of the comparison function and the advocacy for element-wise operations—is a significant and novel contribution. In a field often driven by increasing architectural complexity, the finding that simple functions like `(a-h)*(a-h)` and `a*h` are top performers is a bold, counter-intuitive, and refreshing result. It challenges the default assumption that a feed-forward network is always the best choice for combining vectors and provides a strong, principled alternative. This insight has the potential for wide impact, as these comparison functions are computationally efficient and easy to implement.

2.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Support:** The authors' decision to test their single model framework across four diverse datasets is commendable and provides powerful evidence for the generality of their claims. Achieving state-of-the-art results on three datasets and competitive performance on the highly contested SNLI benchmark with the same core idea demonstrates that their findings are not an artifact of a single task. This rigorous validation makes the central claim about the effectiveness of SUB/MULT functions much more compelling.

3.  **Identifies a Clear Direction:** This paper does more than just report good results; it provides a clear takeaway for researchers working on sequence matching. The conclusion is not just "our model works well," but rather "this specific component, the comparison function, is crucial, and here are the principles for designing a good one." This moves the conversation from architecture-hacking to understanding the fundamental operations needed for semantic matching.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**
While I am enthusiastic about this paper, the following points could help strengthen it further. These are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The intuition connecting SUB/MULT to Euclidean/Cosine distance is excellent. This could be strengthened. For instance, SUB preserves dimensional information that is lost when collapsed into a single scalar distance. It would be insightful to discuss why preserving this per-dimension difference is so beneficial. Does the model learn to use specific dimensions for specific types of semantic relations?
*   **Role of the Preprocessing Layer:** The paper notes that the simple, order-insensitive preprocessing layer works well, concluding that word-by-word comparison is paramount. This is a strong claim. However, it would be interesting to see an ablation where a more powerful, order-sensitive encoder (like a BiLSTM, as used for SNLI) is applied to the other tasks. This would help quantify exactly how much is gained or lost, and in which contexts context-free preprocessing is sufficient.
*   **Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a nice illustrative example. To make it more empirically grounded, could you perhaps show an aggregated visualization of feature activations for correctly vs. incorrectly classified examples? This might reveal systematic patterns in what the comparison layer focuses on for successful predictions.

**Questions for the Authors**
To clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following:
1.  In your experiments, the comparison functions `SUB`, `MULT`, and `NN` all produce a comparison vector `t_j` of dimensionality `l`. How sensitive is the performance of the `NN` and `NTN` comparators to this output dimensionality? Is it possible their generality is hindered by being constrained to the same dimensionality as the simpler functions, or did you find this consistency to be unimportant?
2.  The `SUB` function is defined as element-wise `(a-h)*(a-h)`. This is effectively the squared difference, which erases the directionality of the difference. Did you experiment with using the raw difference vector `(a-h)` or the absolute difference `|a-h|`? What was the motivation for choosing the squared version?
3.  The word embeddings were kept static during training. Given that the model relies heavily on fine-grained element-wise interactions, it seems plausible that allowing the embeddings to be fine-tuned could help create a vector space more amenable to these specific comparison operations. Did you experiment with this, and if so, what were the results?
