### Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a general neural network model based on the "compare-aggregate" framework for matching pairs of text sequences. The authors argue that while this framework has appeared in recent literature, its general applicability has not been systematically demonstrated, and the choice of the core "comparison function" has been underexplored. The paper's primary contributions are twofold:

1.  It demonstrates the effectiveness and versatility of a unified compare-aggregate model by applying it to four different and challenging tasks: answer selection (InsuranceQA, WikiQA), machine comprehension (MovieQA), and natural language inference (SNLI). The model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all these benchmarks.
2.  It conducts a systematic empirical evaluation of six different comparison functions that operate at the word level. The key finding is that simple, element-wise operations—specifically subtraction (SUB) and multiplication (MULT)—are highly effective, often outperforming more complex and heavily parameterized functions like standard neural networks (NN) or neural tensor networks (NTN). The combination of these element-wise features fed into an NN (SUBMULT+NN) consistently yields the best or near-best performance.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The paper's central claim of the general effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework is convincingly supported by strong empirical results on four diverse datasets. Achieving state-of-the-art performance on three of them with a single, coherent architecture is a significant result that validates the proposed approach.
2.  **Valuable Architectural Insights:** The systematic study of comparison functions provides a genuinely useful insight for practitioners and researchers in the field. The finding that simple element-wise functions like SUB and MULT are powerful is an important contribution. It highlights that carefully chosen inductive biases for comparing vectors can be more effective than relying on generic, highly flexible transformations, offering a valuable lesson in model design that favors simplicity and interpretability.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The strength of this paper lies in its clear hypothesis, rigorous execution, and impactful results. The authors identify a gap in the literature: while compare-aggregate models exist, they are often task-specific, and the critical comparison step is not well-motivated or explored. This work directly addresses that gap.

The choice of datasets is excellent for demonstrating the model's versatility. SNLI requires nuanced understanding of logical relationships, while the QA tasks vary from selecting from a large pool of candidates (InsuranceQA) to ranking (WikiQA) and multi-evidence reasoning (MovieQA). The fact that the proposed model, with minimal task-specific modifications, excels on all of them is compelling evidence for its robustness.

The investigation into comparison functions (Section 2.3) is the paper's most significant novel contribution. The authors correctly position their proposed SUB and MULT functions as a middle ground between the information-lossy EUCCOS and the overly general NN/NTN. The intuition that SUB relates to Euclidean distance and MULT to cosine similarity, while preserving dimensional information, is well-explained and persuasive. The results in Table 3 strongly support this hypothesis, with SUBMULT+NN emerging as a clear winner across the board. This finding is not trivial and provides a concrete, actionable recommendation for designing future sequence matching models.

The ablation study in Table 4 further strengthens the paper by demonstrating the utility of the attention and preprocessing components, offering additional insights into when each component is most critical (e.g., attention for sequences of disparate lengths).

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could help improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Clarification of the Preprocessing Layer:** The description of the preprocessing layer is somewhat ambiguous. The authors state it is "order-insensitive" but then mention an LSTM was incorporated for the SNLI task. This is a critical architectural detail. It would be beneficial to explicitly state how the LSTM was integrated with Equation 1. For instance, was a BiLSTM run over the initial embeddings, with its hidden states then used as input to the gating mechanism? This distinction is important, as it speaks to when contextualization is necessary before the comparison stage.
*   **Discussion of One-Directional Attention:** The model uses one-directional attention (sequence A attends to Q). This is a departure from other contemporary models like BiDAF or the Decomposable Attention Network (Parikh et al., 2016) which employ symmetric or bi-directional attention. A brief discussion motivating this design choice would be welcome. Was it for simplicity, computational efficiency, or did empirical tests show that bi-directional attention provided no significant benefit for these tasks?
*   **Deeper Analysis:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a good first step towards interpretability. However, the analysis could be deepened. For example, could the authors analyze the learned weights of the SUBMULT+NN function? It would be interesting to see if the model learns to weigh the "difference" features (from SUB) differently from the "agreement" features (from MULT) across different tasks (e.g., emphasizing differences for the "contradiction" class in SNLI).
*   **Model Naming:** For improved readability and future citation, giving the model a distinct name (e.g., "Compare-Aggregate Net" or "CA-CNN") would be helpful instead of referring to it as "our model".

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To aid my final assessment, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  Could you elaborate on the precise architecture used for the SNLI task where an LSTM was incorporated into the "order-insensitive" preprocessing layer? Your results on SNLI are competitive but slightly below the SOTA at the time; do you believe this order-sensitive preprocessing is a key reason for the strong performance, and would it benefit the other tasks as well?
2.  Your best-performing comparison function, SUBMULT+NN, computes element-wise features and then feeds them into a neural network. A common alternative in the literature is to concatenate these features with the original vectors, e.g., `t_j = f([a_j, h_j, a_j \odot h_j, (a_j - h_j) \odot (a_j - h_j)])`. Did you experiment with this variant, and if so, how did it compare?
3.  For the MovieQA task, the comparison vectors for (Question, Plot) and (Answer, Plot) are concatenated at each position `j` of the plot (`tk,j = [tqj; tak,j]`). This implicitly aligns the j-th plot word's interaction with the question and the answer. What was the rationale for this specific concatenation strategy over, for example, aggregating the (Q, P) and (A, P) interactions separately before combining them at a higher level?
