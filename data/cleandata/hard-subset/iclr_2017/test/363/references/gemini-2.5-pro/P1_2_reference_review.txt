### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the effectiveness of a general "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching problems. The authors make two primary contributions. First, they demonstrate that a single, unified model architecture based on this framework can achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across four different and diverse tasks: machine comprehension (MovieQA), answer selection (InsuranceQA, WikiQA), and textual entailment (SNLI). Second, and more importantly, the paper provides a systematic empirical evaluation of six different comparison functions that operate at the word or sub-sequence level. The key finding is that comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUB, MULT) are surprisingly effective, often outperforming more complex, parameterized functions like neural networks (NN) or neural tensor networks (NTN), as well as more restrictive functions like Euclidean distance and cosine similarity (EUCCOS). The authors argue that these element-wise functions provide a good balance between expressiveness and inductive bias for measuring semantic similarity.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Strong Empirical Validation of a General Framework:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the "compare-aggregate" architecture is a powerful and general-purpose tool for a wide variety of sequence matching tasks. By testing a single model on four distinct datasets and achieving excellent results, the work helps to unify and consolidate a line of research that had previously been presented in more task-specific contexts.
2.  **Novel and Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The systematic study of the comparison function is a significant contribution. The finding that relatively simple, parameter-free element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) are highly effective is an important and practical insight for the community. This analysis goes beyond simply proposing a new model and instead provides valuable understanding about *why* certain architectural components work well for this class of problems.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's strengths lie in its clear motivation, rigorous and extensive experiments, and the practical value of its findings.

The authors correctly identify a trend towards compare-aggregate models in recent literature (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016) and astutely point out two gaps: a lack of testing on a wide range of tasks to prove generality, and an insufficient investigation into the core "compare" component. This paper directly and successfully addresses both gaps.

The experimental setup is impressive. Applying the same core architecture to four datasets with different characteristics (e.g., varying sequence lengths, different matching goals) and comparing against strong, task-specific baselines provides robust support for the model's general effectiveness. The results, which show state-of-the-art performance on three tasks and competitive performance on the fourth, are convincing.

The central contribution—the analysis of comparison functions—is well-executed. The paper hypothesizes that functions like SUB and MULT offer a better trade-off than the overly general NN/NTN and the overly restrictive EUCCOS. This hypothesis is well-supported by the results presented. This finding is valuable because it suggests that injecting a strong inductive bias for comparing vectors dimension-wise is beneficial, and it offers a simple, efficient, and effective component for future model designs. The ablation study mentioned in Section 3.3, which confirms the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers, further strengthens the paper's conclusions about the model's design.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

1.  **Justification for One-Directional Attention:** The model uses a one-directional attention mechanism (attending from sequence A to sequence Q) to create context vectors `h_j`. This is noted as a difference from the decomposable attention model of Parikh et al. (2016), which uses symmetric, two-directional attention. A brief discussion on why this choice was made would be beneficial. Was it for simplicity, computational efficiency, or was a symmetric version found not to improve performance? This is particularly relevant for a task like SNLI, where the relationship between premise and hypothesis can be symmetric or asymmetric.

2.  **Discussion on the Order-Insensitive Preprocessing:** The preprocessing layer (Eq. 1) is order-insensitive. The authors note this and state that the model's strong performance suggests the word-by-word comparison is the most critical component. While this is a plausible conclusion, it would be interesting to discuss the alternative possibility: perhaps some of these tasks (especially QA tasks) are less sensitive to word order than previously thought and rely more on lexical overlap and keyword matching, which the model captures well. Acknowledging this nuance would make the argument more complete.

3.  **Clarity on Task-Specific Modifications:** The paper mentions that for the SNLI task, an LSTM was incorporated into the preprocessing layer, but not for the other tasks. It would be helpful to explicitly state the modified preprocessing formula for SNLI and provide a brief rationale for why this task, in particular, required an order-sensitive component to achieve competitive results. This would add valuable insight into the characteristics of different sequence matching problems.

4.  **Minor Clarifications:**
    *   In Eq. 2, the attention formulation `softmax((WgQ + bg)T A)` is slightly non-standard. Typically, attention is `softmax(A^T W Q)`. Please double-check the formula and confirm the dimensions and operations are as intended.
    *   The paper states word embeddings are not updated. Given the strong results, this is an interesting choice. A brief comment on the motivation (e.g., preventing overfitting, faster training) would be helpful.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the performance of a symmetric, two-directional attention mechanism within your framework? Did you experiment with this, and if so, how did it affect performance and training time compared to the one-directional version?
2.  What was the specific architectural modification to the preprocessing layer for the SNLI task where an LSTM was used? Why do you believe this was necessary for SNLI but not for the QA tasks?
3.  The SUBMULT+NN variant often performs best. Does this suggest that while the element-wise operations provide a powerful and well-biased feature representation, a final learned linear projection is still crucial for optimally mapping these features to the specific task?
