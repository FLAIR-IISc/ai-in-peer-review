### Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper presents a model for text sequence matching based on the "compare-aggregate" framework. The authors claim two main contributions. First, they argue for the general applicability of this framework by testing a single model architecture across four different sequence matching tasks: three distinct question-answering settings (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one textual entailment task (SNLI). They demonstrate strong empirical performance, achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results on these benchmarks. Second, the paper conducts a systematic evaluation of six different word-level comparison functions within their model, concluding that functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication, while not widely used, generally outperform more complex neural network-based functions and simpler distance metrics.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Lack of Methodological Rigor and Inconsistent Model Definition:** The central claim of proposing and evaluating a "general model" is significantly undermined by numerous undocumented or poorly justified task-specific modifications. The model applied is not consistent across the four datasets, which calls into question the conclusions about the framework's generality. Key architectural choices are presented without sufficient justification, and crucial implementation details are omitted.
2.  **Poor Presentation and Numerous Formatting Errors:** The paper suffers from a distracting number of typographical errors, grammatical mistakes, and severe formatting inconsistencies, most notably in the reference list. The overall lack of polish suggests a hastily prepared manuscript and raises concerns about the rigor of the underlying work.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision is based on a meticulous examination of the paper's methodology and presentation. While the empirical results are promising, they are built upon a methodologically shaky and poorly described foundation.

*   **Contradiction of the "General Model" Claim:** The paper's primary assertion is the evaluation of a single, general model. However, this is demonstrably false.
    *   In Section 2.2, it is stated: "In our experiments, we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task." This is a fundamental change to the preprocessing layer for one specific task, contradicting the claim of an order-insensitive preprocessing approach and making the cross-task comparison of the framework's effectiveness inconsistent. Why was this necessary for SNLI and not others? This is not justified.
    *   The model structure for MovieQA (Section 3.1) is substantially different, involving three-way matching (Q-P, A-P) and concatenation of comparison vectors before aggregation. This is a bespoke architecture, not a direct application of the model described in Section 2. The paper fails to frame this as a necessary adaptation and instead presents it as part of the general model's application, which is misleading.

*   **Insufficient Methodological Justification and Detail:**
    *   The model uses one-directional attention, a deviation from the bi-directional attention used in a key cited work (Parikh et al., 2016). This choice is presented as a minor difference but is not justified. One-directional attention is arguably less powerful, and its sufficiency or potential benefits (e.g., efficiency) are not discussed.
    *   The aggregation layer (Section 2.4) uses a CNN, but its architecture is not detailed. The comparison functions produce vectors `tj` of varying dimensionality (e.g., 2 for EUCCOS, `l` for SUB/MULT, `l` for SUBMULT+NN). How the CNN architecture adapts to these different input sizes is completely ignored, leaving a critical gap in the model's description.
    *   The motivation for the preprocessing layer (Eq. 1) is "inspired by the use of gates in LSTM and GRU." This is a vague, hand-wavy justification for a novel, order-insensitive gating mechanism. Its advantages over a simple linear layer or other alternatives are not explored.

*   **Pervasive Presentation Flaws:**
    *   **References:** The reference list is improperly formatted to an unacceptable degree. Entries contain raw BibTeX-like artifacts (e.g., `Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,`). This is not a minor stylistic issue; it makes the bibliography difficult to read and suggests a profound lack of care in the paper's preparation.
    *   **Typos and Grammar:** Multiple errors are present throughout the text, such as "We will fist introduce..." (Section 3), "implementation details of the modes" (Section 3.1), and "Siamense network" (Section 4).
    *   **Inconsistent Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors state they tuned learning rate and batch size only for WikiQA "which is a relatively small dataset" (Section 3.1). This ad-hoc approach complicates the interpretation of results and weakens the claim of a systematic evaluation.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While I am recommending rejection, the core idea of systematically analyzing comparison functions is valuable. To improve the paper for a future submission, I suggest the following:

*   **Be Honest About the Model's Generality:** Either develop a truly general model that can be applied to all four tasks with minimal changes (e.g., only to the final output layer) or reframe the paper. The latter would involve explicitly stating that the "compare-aggregate" *paradigm* is general, but requires specific architectural motifs for different task structures (e.g., answer selection vs. 3-way comprehension). Each adaptation must be clearly described and motivated.
*   **Rigorously Detail the Method:** Provide complete architectural details. Specify the CNN parameters (filter sizes, number of filters) and explain how it handles variable-dimension inputs. Justify every significant design choice, especially those that deviate from prior work (e.g., one-directional attention).
*   **Thoroughly Proofread and Reformat:** The paper requires a complete pass for grammar, spelling, and style. The reference section must be entirely rebuilt to follow the conference's formatting guidelines. A clean, professional presentation is a prerequisite for serious consideration.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide a clear justification for using a one-directional attention mechanism? Did you experiment with a bi-directional approach, and if so, how did it compare?
2.  What is the empirical or theoretical rationale for applying an LSTM in the preprocessing stage for the SNLI task alone? Does this not suggest that the proposed order-insensitive preprocessing is insufficient for syntactically complex tasks?
3.  Please provide the specific architectural details for the aggregation CNN, and explain how it was configured to process the outputs of the different comparison functions, whose dimensionalities range from 2 to `l`.
