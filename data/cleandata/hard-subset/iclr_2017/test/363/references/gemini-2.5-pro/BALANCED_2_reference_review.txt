### **Summary**

This paper presents a general neural architecture for sequence matching based on the "compare-aggregate" framework. The authors' primary contributions are twofold. First, they demonstrate the broad applicability and effectiveness of this framework by applying a single, adaptable model architecture to four different and challenging sequence matching tasks: three distinct question-answering datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one natural language inference dataset (SNLI). The proposed model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all four benchmarks. Second, the paper conducts a systematic investigation into the choice of the comparison function, a core component of the framework that has been underexplored. It evaluates six different functions, ranging from simple distance metrics to complex neural networks, and finds that functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication are remarkably effective and consistently outperform more conventional choices.

### **Decision: Accept**

My recommendation is to accept this paper. The key reasons are:

1.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The paper provides a convincing empirical study that validates the "compare-aggregate" framework as a general and powerful approach for a wide variety of sequence matching tasks. This is a valuable contribution that consolidates and extends findings from previous works that were more narrowly focused.
2.  **Novel and Actionable Insights on Comparison Functions:** The systematic analysis of different comparison functions is a clear and novel contribution. The finding that relatively simple, parameter-free element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) are highly effective is an important and practical insight for researchers working on related problems.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The strengths of this paper lie in its clear motivation, rigorous experimentation, and impactful findings.

**Generalizability of the Framework:** While prior works have introduced models within the "compare-aggregate" paradigm (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b), they typically focused on one or two tasks. This paper significantly strengthens the case for this paradigm by showing that a single, coherent architecture can achieve excellent performance on four datasets with different characteristics (e.g., varying sequence lengths, multi-sequence input in MovieQA, ranking vs. classification). The strong results reported in Table 3, achieving state-of-the-art on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, provide compelling evidence for the model's effectiveness and generality.

**Systematic Study of Comparison Functions:** The core novelty of the paper is its focused study of the comparison function `f`. The authors rightly point out that this component is often chosen without systematic justification. By comparing six distinct functions, they provide a valuable service to the community. The results are insightful: the most complex functions (NN, NTN) are not the best performers. Instead, the SUBMULT+NN function consistently performs at the top, and even the simpler, parameter-free SUB and MULT functions are extremely competitive. This suggests that explicitly modeling vector differences and similarities through element-wise operations captures a crucial inductive bias for these tasks, a finding that is not obvious and could guide future model design.

**Methodological Rigor:** The experimental setup is solid. The authors compare against strong and relevant baselines for each task. The inclusion of an ablation study (Table 4) reinforces the importance of the model's components (preprocessing and attention), adding credibility to the design. The architectural details and task-specific adaptations are explained clearly, making the work reproducible.

### **Additional Feedback**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper further and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Analysis of the Preprocessing Layer:** The paper mentions that the order-insensitive preprocessing layer was sufficient for most tasks, but an LSTM was incorporated for SNLI. While the ablation shows the layer is important, a more detailed discussion of the impact of order-insensitivity would be beneficial. For example, did the order-sensitive version provide a significant boost on SNLI? Was it tested on other tasks like WikiQA, and if so, how did it perform? This would provide a clearer picture of the performance vs. computational cost trade-off.
*   **Attention Mechanism:** The paper notes that its attention is one-directional (A attends to Q), in contrast to the two-directional attention in Parikh et al. (2016). A brief justification for this design choice would be helpful. Is it for simplicity, efficiency, or was it found to be empirically superior for these tasks?
*   **Parameter Efficiency of Comparison Functions:** The paper's argument for the simpler comparison functions (SUB, MULT) could be further strengthened by discussing their parameter efficiency. The NN, NTN, and SUBMULT+NN functions add learnable parameters, whereas SUB, MULT, and EUCCOS are parameter-free. Highlighting that these functions are not only effective but also more compact would be a valuable addition.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a nice start. A more in-depth qualitative or error analysis could provide deeper insights. For instance, are there specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., negation, antonyms) where certain comparison functions fail while others succeed? This could help build a more nuanced understanding of *why* some functions perform better than others.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points during the rebuttal period:

1.  Could you elaborate on the performance impact of using an order-sensitive LSTM in the preprocessing layer for the SNLI task, as mentioned in Section 2.2? How did this compare to the order-insensitive version on that specific dataset?
2.  The dimensionality of the comparison vector `t_j` varies significantly between functions (e.g., 2 for EUCCOS vs. 150 for MULT). How was the subsequent CNN aggregation layer adapted to handle these different input dimensionalities, or was its architecture kept fixed? If fixed, how do you think this might have influenced the relative performance of the comparison functions?
3.  In the MovieQA task, the model matches both the Question (Q) and Answer (A) to the Plot (P) separately before concatenating the results. Did you consider a more integrated approach, such as a three-way comparison or having the Q-P matching results influence the A-P matching (e.g., via attention)? What is your intuition on why the separate matching approach worked so well?
