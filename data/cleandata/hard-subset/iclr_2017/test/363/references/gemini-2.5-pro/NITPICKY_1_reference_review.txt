### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language sequence matching problems. The authors propose a general model architecture and systematically evaluate its performance across four distinct tasks: question answering on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA, and textual entailment on SNLI. The main contributions are twofold: (1) an empirical demonstration that a single, general compare-aggregate model can achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across this diverse set of tasks, and (2) a systematic analysis of six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations. The study finds that simple, element-wise comparison functions (based on subtraction and multiplication), which have not been widely adopted, often outperform more complex, parameterized functions like standard neural networks or neural tensor networks.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Limited Technical Novelty:** The proposed model is a composition of well-established components (gated word embeddings, standard attention, CNN aggregation). The overall framework is acknowledged by the authors to be common in recent literature (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016). The primary contribution is thus an extensive empirical study rather than a novel method, and while the findings are interesting, the work does not introduce a sufficiently new concept or technique to warrant publication at ICLR.
2.  **Significant Methodological Simplifications:** The paper relies on several methodological choices that are either questionable or insufficiently justified. The order-insensitive preprocessing layer is a particularly significant weakness for a model intended to process text sequences. Furthermore, the use of one-directional attention and zero-initialization for unknown word embeddings are suboptimal choices that are not defended against more standard alternatives.
3.  **Poor Presentation Quality:** The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of formatting errors, most egregiously in the bibliography, where nearly every citation is corrupted (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,"). This lack of care in preparation severely undermines the credibility of the work and suggests a rushed submission process.

### Supporting Arguments

**On Novelty:** The paper's strength lies in its breadth of evaluation. However, the architectural components themselves are not new. The "compare-aggregate" paradigm is the explicit starting point. The preprocessing step in Eq. (1) is a simple gating mechanism on a bag-of-words representation. The attention in Eq. (2) is standard Luong-style attention. The aggregation in Eq. (9) is a standard text classification CNN (Kim, 2014). Even the core subject of investigation—the comparison functions—are not entirely novel. The authors cite Mou et al. (2016) for the use of SUB and MULT functions. The paper's contribution is therefore confined to being the first to combine these specific pieces and test them extensively. While a useful contribution to the field, it reads more as a strong workshop paper or a technical report on best practices than a novel research paper suitable for ICLR.

**On Methodology:** Several design choices are concerning:
*   **Order-Insensitive Preprocessing:** For most of the tasks, the model begins by processing the input sequences `Q` and `A` in an order-insensitive manner (Section 2.2). The authors state "the word order does not matter" for this step. For tasks like textual entailment (SNLI), where syntactic structure and negation scoping are critical, this is a major deficiency. The authors mention in passing that "we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task," but this is an ad-hoc fix, and its details and impact are not clearly presented or analyzed. The claim in Section 3.3 that the success of this order-insensitive layer proves the primacy of word-by-word comparison is a speculative leap; an equally valid interpretation is that the model succeeded *despite* this limitation, and a proper sequential encoder would improve performance further.
*   **One-Directional Attention:** The model uses attention to align each word in sequence `A` with the entirety of sequence `Q`. This is a one-directional mechanism. Competing models like that of Parikh et al. (2016) use bi-directional attention (i.e., also aligning `Q` with `A`). The paper provides no justification for this simpler, and potentially less powerful, choice.
*   **Zero-Vector Initialization:** Initializing word embeddings not found in GloVe with zero vectors is a poor practice. Zero vectors can lead to vanishing gradients or null outputs in multiplicative or normalization layers. Random initialization from a suitable distribution is the standard and more robust approach.
*   **EUCCOS Comparison Function:** The `EUCCOS` function compresses the `l`-dimensional vectors `aj` and `hj` into a 2-dimensional vector `tj`. This constitutes a massive information bottleneck. While its strong performance on MovieQA is noted, this may say more about the simplicity of the dataset than the merit of the function. This extreme design choice is not adequately discussed.

**On Presentation:** The quality of the manuscript is simply not up to the standards of a top-tier conference. The reference section is unusable, with garbled BibTeX output for almost every entry. This level of carelessness is a significant concern. The notation `⊗ eX` is defined but is non-standard and could be expressed more clearly using broadcasting terminology.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The core strength of this paper is the systematic comparison of the interaction functions. The paper would be stronger if it were framed more explicitly around this analysis, perhaps with a more robust and standardized backbone architecture (e.g., using a BiLSTM encoder for all tasks) to ensure that the observed performance differences are truly due to the comparison functions and not artifacts of a weak preprocessing layer.
*   The ablation study (Table 4, discussed but not shown) is crucial. It would be beneficial to expand this analysis to justify other design choices, such as one-directional vs. bi-directional attention.
*   The handling of the three-sequence MovieQA task is interesting but seems heuristic. A more principled approach could involve hierarchical attention or more complex fusion mechanisms to model the P-Q-A interactions, rather than simple concatenation of comparison vectors.
*   The visualization in Figure 2 is a good step towards interpretability, but the explanation is vague. It should be clarified precisely what is being visualized (e.g., max-over-time pooling values for which specific filters?) and how this supports the claims being made.
*   Thorough proofreading and correction of the bibliography are mandatory before any future submission.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you please clarify the exact architecture used for the SNLI task? Was the LSTM pre-processing layer part of the main model being compared in Table 3, or was it an additional component whose results were reported separately? How was this LSTM integrated with Equation (1)?
2.  What was the rationale for choosing one-directional attention over the bi-directional attention used in similar contemporary models? Was this decision supported by preliminary experiments?
3.  The ablation study conclusion that the preprocessing layer is more important for SNLI/WikiQA while attention is more important for MovieQA/InsuranceQA is interesting. Is there a more rigorous way to demonstrate this beyond the final performance numbers, for instance, by analyzing gradient magnitudes or attention weight distributions for these tasks?
