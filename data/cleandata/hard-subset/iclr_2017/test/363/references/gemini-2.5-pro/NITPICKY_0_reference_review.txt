### Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper investigates the effectiveness and generality of the "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The authors propose a general model within this framework and evaluate it on four different datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, which span question answering, answer ranking, and textual entailment. The primary contributions are twofold: (1) an empirical demonstration that a single, unified compare-aggregate architecture can achieve state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across these diverse tasks, and (2) a systematic evaluation of six different word-level comparison functions. The authors find that comparison functions based on element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication), which have not been widely adopted, outperform more common choices like feedforward networks or simple similarity metrics.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Severe Lack of Professionalism in Presentation:** The manuscript is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, notational inconsistencies, and imprecise descriptions. These issues, ranging from a potential major policy violation to sloppy bibliographic formatting, collectively undermine the credibility and scientific rigor of the work.
2.  **Insufficient Methodological Clarity and Justification:** Critical details about the model architecture, particularly its task-specific adaptations, are either missing or inadequately explained. This opacity makes it difficult to fully understand the experiments, reproduce the results, and assess the central claim that a "general model" is being evaluated.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision:**

1.  **Presentation and Formatting Issues:**
    *   **Potential Anonymity Violation:** Footnote 1 provides a public link to a GitHub repository. For a double-blind review process, this is a serious breach of anonymity and is often grounds for immediate rejection.
    *   **Sloppy Notation:** The paper uses the same symbols (`Q`, `A`) to denote both matrices of word embeddings and the lengths of the corresponding sequences. This is confusing and leads to ambiguity in reading the equations. For instance, in Section 2.1, `Q ∈ Rd×Q` is defined, which is highly unorthodox.
    *   **Imprecise Terminology:** In Section 2.2, the operation `bi ⊗ eQ` is described as an "outer product," which is incorrect; it is a broadcasting operation used to tile a vector into a matrix. In Section 2.4, the output dimension of the CNN is given as `r ∈ Rnl`, where `n` is vaguely defined as "the number of windows in CNN." This lacks the necessary precision regarding filter sizes and counts.
    *   **Bibliography Errors:** The references section is replete with formatting errors. For example, citations include raw BibTeX-like artifacts such as `Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,`. The citation `Wan et al., )` contains a stray parenthesis. This suggests a lack of careful proofreading.

2.  **Methodological Opacity:**
    *   **Under-specified Model Variations:** In Section 2.2, the authors state, "In our experiments, we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task." This is a crucial architectural modification that is mentioned only in passing. The paper does not explain *how* the LSTM was integrated into the order-insensitive preprocessing formula (Eq. 1). Since SNLI is the only task where the model is not state-of-the-art, and the only task where this modification is made, this detail is critical for interpreting the results. It significantly weakens the claim of evaluating a single "general model."
    *   **Unjustified Architectural Choices:** For the MovieQA task, the model matches the question `Q` and answer `Ak` to the plot `P` separately, then concatenates the resulting comparison vectors `tqj` and `tak,j` before feeding them to the aggregation CNN. This is a complex design choice that is presented without any justification. Why is this specific fusion strategy chosen over other alternatives, such as late fusion after separate aggregations?
    *   **Inconsistent Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors state that the learning rate and batch size were tuned for the small WikiQA dataset but fixed for the others. This inconsistent approach raises concerns about whether the model's full potential was realized on all tasks, potentially biasing the results and conclusions about the relative performance of different comparison functions.

3.  **Weak Analysis:**
    *   The ablation study analysis in Section 3.3 makes speculative claims, such as attention being more important for sequences with large length differences. While plausible, this is presented as a finding without rigorous supporting analysis beyond a simple inspection of the ablation table.
    *   The visualization in Section 3.4 is poorly explained. It is unclear what "the max values in each dimension of the convolutional layer" represents. The subsequent interpretation is anecdotal and relies on hypotheses ("We hypothesize that...") rather than providing concrete evidence or insight into the model's mechanisms.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

1.  **Thoroughly Revise and Proofread:** The manuscript requires a comprehensive revision to fix all notational inconsistencies, imprecise language, and formatting errors. The bibliography must be properly formatted. The potential anonymity breach must be addressed in any future submission.
2.  **Clarify the Methodology:** Provide a complete and unambiguous description of the model.
    *   Create a separate, clear diagram or equation set explaining precisely how LSTM is integrated for the SNLI task. Justify why this task required a different preprocessing approach.
    *   Elaborate on the MovieQA architecture. Justify the choice to concatenate comparison vectors.
    *   Use distinct and clear notation throughout (e.g., `L_Q` for sequence length, `Q` for the matrix).
3.  **Strengthen the Analysis:** The empirical results are strong, but the analysis is superficial.
    *   Instead of qualitative visualizations, consider more quantitative analyses. For example, analyzing the attention weights (`G` matrix) could provide more direct evidence of what the model focuses on.
    *   If making claims about the relative importance of components for different task types, provide a more principled analysis or at least frame the discussion more cautiously as a hypothesis for future work.
4.  **Refine Contribution Framing:** The systematic comparison of `f` is a solid contribution. However, the novelty of the element-wise functions themselves is slightly overstated, as the paper correctly cites prior work (Mou et al., 2016). The contribution should be framed more explicitly as providing strong, multi-task evidence for the superiority of these specific functions within a robust compare-aggregate framework.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide the exact formulation of the LSTM-based preprocessing layer used for the SNLI task and justify its necessity compared to the order-insensitive version used for other tasks?
*   What was the rationale for the specific fusion strategy in the MovieQA model (concatenating comparison vectors before aggregation)? Were alternative strategies, such as separate aggregation followed by fusion, explored?
*   Given the inconsistent hyperparameter tuning, how can we be confident that the reported performance differences between comparison functions are robust and not an artifact of a tuning process that may have favored certain configurations on certain datasets?
