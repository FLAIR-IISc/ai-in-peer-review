### Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents a neural network model for text sequence matching that follows a "compare-aggregate" framework. The authors claim two main contributions: first, they demonstrate the general effectiveness of this framework by applying a single model architecture to four distinct tasks—three variants of Question Answering (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one Textual Entailment task (SNLI)—achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results. Second, and more centrally, the paper provides a systematic empirical evaluation of six different comparison functions used at the word-level matching stage. The key finding is that relatively simple, parameter-free, element-wise functions (based on subtraction and multiplication) often outperform more complex, parameterized functions like standard neural networks or neural tensor networks. This suggests that for similarity-based tasks, explicitly modeling vector differences and interactions is highly effective.

**2. Recommendation and Justification**

**Recommendation:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Significant Lack of Methodological Clarity and Rigor:** The description of the model is plagued by ambiguous, non-standard notation and critical omissions. Key components, such as the application of the softmax in the attention mechanism and the structure of the CNN aggregation, are imprecisely defined, making the work difficult to understand and reproduce.
2.  **Inconsistent Model Definition and Overstated Generality:** The paper claims to propose a "general model" but then describes substantial, task-specific architectural modifications for different datasets. Furthermore, it makes contradictory statements regarding the model's properties (e.g., order sensitivity), which fundamentally confuses the reader about what was actually implemented.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

While the empirical results appear strong and the investigation of comparison functions is a worthwhile endeavor, the paper's execution is undermined by a pervasive lack of attention to detail.

**Methodological Ambiguities and Notational Flaws:**
*   **Notation:** The use of `Q` and `A` to denote both matrices and their respective lengths (`d×Q` and `d×A`) is confusing and poor practice. Standard notation like `L_Q` and `L_A` for lengths would improve clarity.
*   **Preprocessing Layer (Eq. 1):** The text states that `⊙` is element-wise multiplication, but this symbol is omitted from the equation itself, appearing as `tanh(...) tanh(...)` instead of `tanh(...) ⊙ tanh(...)`. The broadcasting notation `(· ⊗ eX)` is non-standard and its explanation is clumsy; standard linear algebra broadcasting conventions would be clearer.
*   **Attention Layer (Eq. 2):** The equation `G = softmax(...)` is critically underspecified. It is not stated along which axis the softmax normalization is applied. For the attention mechanism to function correctly (i.e., for weights over sequence `Q` to sum to 1 for each element of `A`), this must be a column-wise softmax. As written, it is ambiguous.
*   **Comparison Functions:** In Equation (5), it is unclear if `‖aj − hj‖2` refers to the L2 norm (Euclidean distance) or the squared L2 norm. In Equations (6) and (7), the element-wise multiplication operator `⊙` is again missing.
*   **Aggregation Layer (Eq. 9):** The output dimension `r ∈ R^{nl}` is defined using `n` as the "number of windows in CNN". This is non-standard terminology. In the context of Kim (2014), the output dimension is determined by the number of filters used for each window size, not the "number of windows". This suggests a potential misunderstanding of the baseline CNN architecture.

**Inconsistencies and Misleading Claims of Generality:**
*   The central premise of a single, "general model" is weakened by the details in Section 3.1. The architecture for MovieQA involves a three-way matching of (Plot, Question, Answer) by first creating two sets of comparison vectors and concatenating them—a significant departure from the two-sequence model in Figure 1. Similarly, the InsuranceQA and WikiQA tasks require an additional trainable selection layer (Eq. 10) on top of the CNN aggregator, which is not part of the core model description. These are not minor tweaks but substantial architectural changes.
*   There is a direct contradiction regarding the model's handling of word order. Section 3.3 states, "our preprocessing layer is order-insensitive," but Section 2.2 quietly mentions, "we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task." This is a crucial detail that is buried and subsequently contradicted. The model is order-sensitive for one of the four tasks, which invalidates the unqualified claim of order-insensitivity.

**Presentation and Formatting:**
*   The manuscript is rife with formatting errors. The bibliography is entirely malformed, with artifacts like `\.,? \Q2014\E,` appearing in almost every entry. This demonstrates a severe lack of care in manuscript preparation and is unacceptable for a submission to a top-tier venue. Typographical errors, such as the incorrectly rendered umlaut in "Rocktäschel", further contribute to a negative impression of the work's overall quality.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The core contribution—the systematic analysis of comparison functions—is valuable. I encourage the authors to revise the paper to focus on this strength.
*   Be precise and transparent about the model architecture. It would be better to present a "base framework" and then explicitly define the specific "instantiations" used for each task, along with justifications for the modifications. This is more honest and scientifically sound than claiming a single "general model."
*   The entire method section should be rewritten with careful attention to standard mathematical notation and clarity. Every equation and variable must be unambiguously defined.
*   The superficial analysis in Section 3.4 should be either removed or substantially improved with more rigorous, quantitative evidence to support the hypotheses about what the model is learning.
*   The entire manuscript requires thorough proofreading to correct the numerous formatting and typographical errors, especially in the bibliography.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you clarify if the softmax in Equation (2) is applied column-wise?
2.  Could you explicitly state which model components (e.g., the comparison function `f`, the preprocessing layers) share parameters when handling the three-way matching in the MovieQA task?
3.  Why was a one-directional attention mechanism chosen over the bi-directional attention used in comparable work like Parikh et al. (2016)? Was an ablation performed to justify this simplification?
4.  Regarding the use of LSTM for the SNLI task: was it applied to both the premise and the hypothesis? If so, does this not make the comparison layer's inputs contextualized word representations, fundamentally changing the nature of the "word-by-word" comparison being studied?
