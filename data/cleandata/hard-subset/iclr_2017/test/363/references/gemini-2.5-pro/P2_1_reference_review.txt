### Paper Review

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper presents a general model based on the "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The authors' primary contributions are twofold. First, they demonstrate the general effectiveness of this framework by applying a single, unified architecture to four distinct and challenging datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA (for question answering), and SNLI (for textual entailment). They show that their model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across all these tasks. Second, and more importantly, the paper conducts a systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions that operate at the word- or token-level within the framework. They compare standard neural network-based functions (NN, NTN) and similarity-based functions (EUCCOS) with less common, element-wise operational functions (SUB, MULT). The central finding is that comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUB, MULT, and their combination) consistently perform best across the diverse tasks, despite their simplicity and lack of trainable parameters.

**Decision and Key Reasons:**
**Decision: Accept**

1.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The paper's core strength lies in its extensive empirical evaluation. By testing a single architectural paradigm on four diverse tasks, the authors convincingly support their claim about the general applicability and robustness of the compare-aggregate framework. This is a valuable contribution, as many architectural papers focus on a single task, leaving questions about generalizability. The model achieves impressive performance, often surpassing more complex, task-specific models.

2.  **Novel and Insightful Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The systematic investigation into the comparison function `f` is a significant contribution. This component is often treated as a standard, off-the-shelf neural layer in related work. This paper challenges that assumption by demonstrating that simpler, non-parametric, element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) are not only viable but often superior. This finding is both practical—offering a simple and effective component for future models—and insightful, suggesting that preserving dimensional information through element-wise operations is more effective for this task than either collapsing it into a scalar similarity (like EUCCOS) or transforming it through a generic, fully-connected layer (like NN).

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper is well-motivated and clearly situated within the existing literature. It correctly identifies a trend towards compare-aggregate models (citing Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b) and pinpoints a crucial, underexplored component: the comparison function.

The experimental setup is rigorous. The choice of datasets covers different facets of sequence matching: answer selection (InsuranceQA), answer ranking (WikiQA), machine comprehension involving a third context sequence (MovieQA), and logical inference (SNLI). The baselines chosen for each task are strong and represent the state-of-the-art at the time of writing, making the reported performance gains meaningful. For instance, the significant improvement on InsuranceQA across all tested comparison functions is particularly compelling evidence of the model's effectiveness.

The analysis of the results is clear and supports the main claims. The paper correctly highlights that the SUBMULT+NN variant is the strongest overall performer, providing a good balance of simplicity and expressive power. The ablation study, which shows the importance of both the preprocessing and attention layers, further strengthens the paper by demonstrating that the model's components are all contributing to its success. The finding that the order-insensitive preprocessing layer works well on several tasks is an interesting result in itself, suggesting that for certain QA tasks, the fine-grained word-level matching is more critical than capturing sequential context at the initial encoding stage.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could further improve its clarity and impact:

1.  **Architectural Novelty:** The authors are transparent that their model follows the general framework of Parikh et al. (2016). It would be beneficial to include a small table or diagram explicitly comparing their architecture (e.g., one-directional attention, CNN aggregation) with closely related works like the Decomposable Attention Model to more clearly delineate the precise architectural differences versus the novel focus on the comparison function analysis.

2.  **Details on the Aggregation Layer:** The paper states that a one-layer CNN is used for aggregation but provides few details. Information on the number of filters, window sizes, and the type of pooling used would be essential for reproducibility and for understanding its role in the model.

3.  **Preprocessing Layer Rationale:** The gated, order-insensitive preprocessing layer is an interesting design choice. The authors mention adding an LSTM for the SNLI task but not for others. A more detailed explanation of this decision would be helpful. Was it based on preliminary experiments, or is there a task-specific intuition (e.g., syntax and word order being more critical for logical entailment)? The finding that order-insensitivity works well for several QA tasks is powerful and deserves slightly more discussion.

4.  **Qualitative Analysis:** The visualization in Figure 2 is a good start for interpreting the model's behavior. However, the analysis is brief. Expanding this with a few more examples, perhaps including a failure case, would provide deeper insight into what the model learns and where it struggles. For example, does the model fail when paraphrasing is required, since the element-wise comparisons might favor lexical overlap?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The superior performance of the SUB and MULT functions over the parameterized NN and NTN is a key result. Do you have a hypothesis as to why this is the case? Could it be related to the parameter count, where NN/NTN are more prone to overfitting on these datasets, or is there a more fundamental inductive bias from the element-wise operations that is well-suited for semantic comparison?

2.  For the MovieQA task, the model matches the Question to the Plot and the Answer to the Plot independently, and then concatenates the resulting comparison vectors. Was an alternative, more hierarchical approach considered? For example, using the Question-Plot attention weights to first select relevant parts of the plot, and then matching the Answer against only that attended context.

3.  The attention mechanism is one-directional (A attends to Q). Why was this chosen over a symmetric, two-directional attention mechanism where Q also attends to A, as used in some other contemporary models? Was this for simplification, or was it found to perform better empirically?
