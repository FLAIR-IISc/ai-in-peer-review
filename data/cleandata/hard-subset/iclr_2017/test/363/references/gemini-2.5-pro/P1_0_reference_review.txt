### Review Summary

This paper presents a general "compare-aggregate" neural network model for text sequence matching and evaluates it across four diverse tasks: three for question answering (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one for textual entailment (SNLI). The primary contributions are twofold. First, it provides strong empirical evidence that the compare-aggregate framework is a robust and general approach for a wide variety of sequence matching problems. Second, it conducts a systematic investigation into the "comparison" function, evaluating six different methods and finding that functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUB, MULT) consistently outperform more complex, parameterized alternatives like standard neural networks (NN) or neural tensor networks (NTN).

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Strong Empirical Validation of a General Framework:** The paper's core strength lies in its extensive and convincing experimental evaluation. By testing a single, coherent model architecture on four distinct datasets with different characteristics, the authors convincingly demonstrate the generality and effectiveness of the compare-aggregate approach, achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across the board. This is a significant contribution that validates a promising research direction.
2.  **Novel and Practical Insight on Comparison Functions:** The systematic study of different comparison functions provides a valuable and actionable insight for the community. The finding that simpler, element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex, learned functions is non-obvious and highly practical. It suggests that for word-level matching, preserving dimensional information through element-wise comparison is more effective than collapsing it into a learned feature vector, which is a key takeaway for future model design.

### Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's claims are well-supported by its rigorous methodology and results.

**On Generality (Reason 1):** The choice of datasets is excellent for demonstrating the model's versatility. MovieQA involves matching a question and an answer against a long plot document. InsuranceQA and WikiQA are answer selection/ranking tasks with different sequence length characteristics. SNLI is a classic classification task on sentence pairs. The fact that the proposed model, with only minor task-specific adaptations in the final layer, achieves top-tier performance on all four (e.g., SOTA on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and competitive on SNLI) is compelling evidence for its robustness. This successfully addresses the first limitation of prior work identified by the authors—that previous compare-aggregate models were typically tested on only one or two tasks.

**On Comparison Functions (Reason 2):** The analysis in Section 2.3 is clear and well-motivated. The authors correctly position NN/NTN as highly flexible but potentially too general, and EUCCOS as well-justified but potentially lossy. Their hypothesis that element-wise SUB and MULT functions offer a "good compromise" is a central, testable claim. The results in Table 3 strongly support this hypothesis. Across all four datasets, SUBMULT+NN is the best or very close to the best performing model. Crucially, even the parameter-free SUB and MULT functions alone are often superior to the fully-parameterized NN and NTN functions. This systematic comparison is a novel contribution that moves beyond the ad-hoc choices of comparison functions in previous work and provides clear guidance for practitioners.

The paper is also well-written, clearly situated within the existing literature, and acknowledges its relationship to prior work like Parikh et al. (2016) while highlighting its own distinct contributions.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve its quality. These points are suggestions for improvement and do not detract from my decision to accept.

1.  **Justification for the Preprocessing Layer:** The gated preprocessing layer in Eq. (1) is an interesting design choice, particularly because it is order-insensitive. The paper notes that an LSTM was incorporated *only* for the SNLI task. This raises a question: why not use a standard recurrent encoder (e.g., a BiLSTM) for all tasks to capture contextual word representations before the attention and comparison steps? While the paper mentions computational cost, the current order-insensitive approach is a significant deviation from standard practice in sequence modeling. A more detailed justification for this design choice, perhaps with an ablation study comparing it to a BiLSTM encoder on one of the QA tasks, would strengthen the paper.
2.  **One-Directional Attention:** The model uses one-directional attention, where each word in sequence A attends to all words in sequence Q. Many state-of-the-art models for sequence-pair tasks (e.g., Parikh et al. (2016), cited by the authors) use a symmetric, bidirectional attention mechanism. Was a bidirectional approach considered? A brief comment on this design choice would be helpful for readers to understand the trade-offs.
3.  **Deeper Analysis of Results:** The analysis in Section 3.3 is good but could be deeper. For instance, why might the simpler EUCCOS perform so well on MovieQA specifically? Why are the preprocessing and attention layers more important for different tasks? The current explanation ("big difference in length") is intuitive but could be substantiated with more data analysis. A brief error analysis could also provide valuable insights into the model's failure modes.

### Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to use the order-insensitive gated preprocessing layer instead of a more standard BiLSTM encoder across all tasks? Was this primarily for computational efficiency, or did you find in preliminary experiments that word order was less important for the QA tasks you selected?
2.  The paper's central finding is the effectiveness of element-wise comparison functions (SUB/MULT). Do you have an intuition as to why these parameter-free or lightly-parameterized functions outperform more flexible, fully-learned functions like NN and NTN? Does this suggest that the task is more about preserving and directly comparing alignment signals across embedding dimensions, rather than learning complex, non-linear interactions at the word-pair level?
3.  In the MovieQA task setup, you compare both Q and A_k to the plot P separately and then concatenate the results. Have you experimented with more complex three-way interactions, for example, by first creating a question-aware representation of the plot, and then matching the answer against that?
