### **Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper presents an empirical study of the "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching. The authors propose a general model architecture within this framework and evaluate it across four distinct tasks: question answering on three different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and natural language inference (SNLI). The primary claimed contributions are twofold: first, demonstrating the general effectiveness of this framework across a diverse set of problems by achieving strong, often state-of-the-art, performance; and second, conducting a systematic evaluation of six different word-level comparison functions. The paper concludes that simple, element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT), which they claim are under-explored, often outperform more complex, parameterized functions like neural networks or simpler metrics like Euclidean distance.

**2. Recommendation: Reject**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents a potentially valuable empirical investigation, I recommend rejection due to a significant number of methodological inconsistencies, a lack of scientific rigor in the experimental setup, and a severe lack of attention to detail in the presentation. The accumulation of these flaws undermines the credibility of the paper's central claims regarding the model's generality and the systematic nature of its evaluation.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

My decision is based on the following key issues:

*   **Inconsistent Model Architecture:** The paper claims to test a single "general model" to demonstrate the broad applicability of the compare-aggregate framework. However, the model architecture is not consistent across the evaluated tasks.
    *   In Section 2.2, it is stated: "In our experiments, we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task." This means the preprocessing for SNLI is order-sensitive, while for the other three tasks, it is order-insensitive. This is a critical architectural difference that invalidates a direct comparison and weakens the claim of a single, general framework being tested.
    *   In Section 3.1, a bespoke, multi-step matching process is described for the MovieQA task, involving separate comparisons of (Question, Plot) and (Answer, Plot) before concatenating the results. This is a significant deviation from the core model presented in Section 2 and further contradicts the claim of applying one general model.

*   **Lack of Experimental Rigor:** The experimental methodology contains several questionable choices and inconsistencies that cast doubt on the fairness of the comparisons.
    *   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** The authors state that for most datasets, the batch size was 30 and learning rate 0.002, but "For WikiQA, which is a relatively small dataset, we also tune the learning rate and the batch size." This inconsistent tuning strategy is not justified. It is unclear if the results on other datasets could be improved with similar tuning, or if WikiQA's strong performance is an artifact of more dedicated optimization.
    *   **OOV Word Initialization:** Initializing out-of-vocabulary word embeddings with zero vectors is a suboptimal choice. This can hinder learning for these words, and standard practice is to use a small random initialization. While perhaps a minor point, it is indicative of a lack of familiarity with established best practices.

*   **Overstated Novelty and Minor Contradictions:** The paper's framing of its contributions is slightly overstated.
    *   The paper claims that element-wise comparison functions like SUB and MULT are "not widely used." However, it correctly cites Mou et al. (2016), who previously used these exact functions for a similar task. The contribution is more an *evaluation* of these functions against others, not their introduction.
    *   The claim of "notable differences" from Parikh et al. (2016) is debatable. The changes (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation) are incremental modifications rather than a fundamental departure in framework.

*   **Severe Presentation and Formatting Issues:** The paper is marred by an unacceptable level of sloppiness, which makes it difficult to trust the technical content.
    *   The **References** section is catastrophically malformed (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Bahdanau et al\.,"). This is not a minor typographical error; it renders the entire bibliography unreadable and suggests a profound lack of care in manuscript preparation.
    *   There are smaller formatting errors throughout, such as the citation `(Wan et al., )` missing a year in Section 4, inconsistent use of spacing around operators, and non-standard notation (` ` for element-wise multiplication instead of the more common `◦`).

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The core idea of empirically comparing comparison functions is valuable. To improve the paper for a future submission, I suggest the following:

*   **Methodological Consistency:** The strongest version of this paper would apply the *exact same* model architecture to all four tasks. If task-specific modifications are necessary, they must be rigorously justified, and an ablation study should show what happens when the "general" model is used instead. The use of LSTM should be consistent: either use it for all tasks or for none.
*   **Rigor in Experiments:** Standardize the hyperparameter tuning process across all datasets. Justify all implementation choices, such as OOV initialization.
*   **Clarity and Precision:** Refine the claims of novelty to accurately reflect the contribution as a systematic comparison rather than an invention of new methods. Use standard mathematical notation (e.g., `◦` for element-wise products, proper vector concatenation notation). The description of the `⊗ eX` operation is clumsy and could be replaced with more standard terminology like "tiling" or "broadcasting."
*   **Deeper Analysis:** The analysis in Section 3.4 is superficial. The visualization suggests the model focuses on lexical overlap. A more insightful analysis would probe what semantic, non-lexical phenomena are captured by the comparison vectors, perhaps through targeted examples or error analysis.
*   **Proofreading:** The paper requires extensive and meticulous proofreading to fix all formatting errors, especially the reference list. This is non-negotiable for a top-tier publication.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please justify the decision to use an order-sensitive LSTM preprocessor for SNLI exclusively, while using an order-insensitive one for other tasks? How does this choice impact your conclusion that the word-by-word comparison, rather than contextual preprocessing, is the key component?
2.  What is the performance of your base model (from Section 2) on the MovieQA task without the task-specific modifications described in Section 3.1? This is crucial for understanding whether the framework itself is general, or if it requires significant expert-designed modifications for each new task.
3.  The reference list is unreadable due to severe formatting errors. Was this a technical issue during the submission process, or an oversight during manuscript preparation?
