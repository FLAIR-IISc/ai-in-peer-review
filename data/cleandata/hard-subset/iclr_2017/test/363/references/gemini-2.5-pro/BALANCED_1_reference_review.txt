### **Summary**

This paper proposes a general model under the "compare-aggregate" framework for a variety of text sequence matching tasks. The authors' main contributions are twofold. First, they demonstrate the broad applicability and effectiveness of this framework by applying a single, unified architecture to four distinct and challenging benchmarks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA (all question answering variants), and SNLI (natural language inference), achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results across the board. Second, and more importantly, the paper conducts a systematic and focused investigation into the "compare" component of the framework. It evaluates six different word-level comparison functions, ranging from standard neural networks to simple distance metrics, and finds that functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication offer the best and most consistent performance. This empirical result provides a valuable and practical insight for designing future sequence matching models.

### **Decision: Accept**

My decision is to accept this paper. The two key reasons are:

1.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of a general "compare-aggregate" architecture. By achieving excellent results on four diverse tasks with minimal task-specific modifications, the authors convincingly argue for the framework's robustness and generality, establishing it as a very strong baseline.
2.  **Clear and Actionable Scientific Contribution:** The systematic analysis of comparison functions is a significant contribution. The finding that relatively simple, element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) often outperform more complex, heavily-parameterized functions (NN, NTN) is an important and non-obvious result that can directly inform future model design in this area.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths lie in its clear focus, rigorous experimentation, and the practical utility of its findings.

*   **Problem Formulation and Motivation:** The authors correctly identify a trend towards "compare-aggregate" models in the literature and astutely note two gaps: a lack of testing on a wide range of tasks and a lack of systematic study into the comparison function itself. The paper is well-motivated to fill these gaps.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** The evaluation is thorough. The choice of four datasets with different characteristics (e.g., matching a question to long plots in MovieQA vs. short hypotheses in SNLI) strongly supports the claim of generality. The reported results consistently match or surpass strong, published baselines, including task-specific state-of-the-art models. The inclusion of an ablation study that demonstrates the utility of the preprocessing and attention layers further strengthens the paper's claims.
*   **Clarity and Impact of Findings:** The core finding regarding the superiority of element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, and their combination) is clearly presented and backed by the results in Table 3. This is an impactful contribution because it suggests that for word-level comparison, preserving dimensional information via element-wise operations is more effective than either collapsing it into a scalar (like in EUCCOS) or transforming it with a fully-connected layer that may over-parameterize the comparison. This provides a clear design guideline for other researchers.
*   **Clarity of Writing:** The paper is well-written, the model is described clearly, and the experimental setup and results are easy to follow.

While the architectural components themselves (attention, CNNs, gated units) are not novel, the paper's contribution is not in proposing new building blocks but in the synthesis, systematic evaluation, and the resulting insights, which are of high value to the ICLR community.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Justification for Architectural Choices:** The paper notes its differences from the contemporary Decomposable Attention Model of Parikh et al. (2016), such as using one-directional attention and a CNN for aggregation. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion motivating these choices. For instance, why might a CNN aggregator be preferable to the feed-forward network used by Parikh et al.? Does one-directional attention offer computational benefits or was it sufficient for these tasks?
*   **Preprocessing Layer Heterogeneity:** The preprocessing layer uses an LSTM for the SNLI task but a simpler, order-insensitive gated mechanism for the other three tasks. The motivation for this is unclear. Was this due to performance differences, computational cost on longer sequences (e.g., MovieQA plots), or was word order deemed less critical for the QA tasks? Clarifying this would help readers understand the model's design principles more deeply.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Comparison Functions:** The analysis in Section 3.4 is a good start. However, it could be extended to provide more insight into the *differences* between the comparison functions. For example, a qualitative error analysis showing a case where SUBMULT+NN succeeds but the simpler EUCCOS fails (or vice versa) would offer a more nuanced understanding of *why* certain functions are better. Does the effectiveness of SUB/MULT suggest that matching is often about identifying specific feature dimensions that align or diverge between word vectors?

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following points in their rebuttal to help clarify my understanding.

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to incorporate an LSTM in the preprocessing layer for the SNLI dataset but not for the QA datasets? Was this an empirical choice based on validation performance, or was it a hypothesis based on the nature of the tasks?
2.  The paper's core contribution is the systematic analysis of comparison functions. Beyond the strong empirical results, do you have any intuition or qualitative analysis that explains why the element-wise SUB and MULT functions consistently outperform the more heavily parameterized NN and NTN functions? Does this suggest that a simple, structured comparison is more robust than a learned, unstructured transformation at the word level?
3.  Your model uses one-directional attention (attending from sequence A to sequence Q). Did you experiment with bi-directional attention (i.e., also attending from Q to A), as is common in similar models? If so, how did it affect performance?
