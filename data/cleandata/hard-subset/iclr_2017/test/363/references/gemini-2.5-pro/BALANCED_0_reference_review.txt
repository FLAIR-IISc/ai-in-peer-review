### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates a general "compare-aggregate" framework for natural language sequence matching problems. The authors argue that while this framework has appeared in various forms in recent literature, its general effectiveness across different tasks has not been systematically demonstrated, and the choice of the core "comparison function" has been underexplored.

The paper's main contributions are twofold:
1.  It proposes a general model instantiating the compare-aggregate framework and demonstrates its effectiveness by achieving state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on four diverse sequence matching tasks: textual entailment (SNLI) and three different question-answering benchmarks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA).
2.  It conducts a systematic empirical comparison of six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, ranging from standard neural networks to simple distance metrics. The key finding is that comparison functions based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication are particularly effective, often outperforming more complex, parameterized alternatives.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is a well-executed empirical paper that provides valuable insights for the NLP community. My decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Strong and Broad Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling evidence for the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework. Testing a single model architecture across four different and challenging datasets is a significant strength and convincingly supports the authors' primary claim. The strong performance across the board underscores the robustness of the approach.
2.  **Valuable and Actionable Findings on Comparison Functions:** The systematic study of word-level comparison functions is a focused and useful contribution. It addresses a specific, often overlooked, model design choice and offers a clear, data-driven recommendation: element-wise operations (subtraction and multiplication) are a powerful and efficient choice. This finding is non-obvious and provides practical guidance for researchers developing similar models.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's main strength lies in its clear-headed and thorough empirical methodology. Instead of proposing a completely novel and complex architecture, the authors focus on understanding, generalizing, and improving a promising existing framework.

**Strengths:**
*   **Motivation and Clarity:** The paper is well-motivated, identifying a clear gap in existing research. It is well-written, and the model architecture is explained clearly and concisely.
*   **Comprehensive Experiments:** The evaluation on four datasets with different characteristics (e.g., task type, sequence lengths) is a major strength. The fact that the model, with minimal task-specific modifications, excels on all of them is strong proof of its general effectiveness. The results on InsuranceQA, in particular, where the model outperforms all baselines with every comparison function, are very impressive.
*   **Systematic Comparison:** The core of the paper is the methodical comparison of the six functions in Section 2.3. This is a good piece of scientific investigation, moving from highly expressive but unconstrained functions (NN, NTN) to highly constrained ones (EUCCOS), and finally motivating the element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) as a "good compromise." The experimental results in Table 3 directly support this hypothesis.
*   **Ablation Study:** The ablation study in Table 4, which shows the relative importance of the preprocessing and attention layers for different tasks, adds another layer of valuable analysis to the paper.

**Weaknesses:**
While the paper is strong, there are areas for improvement. The primary weakness is the limited architectural novelty. The authors correctly position their work relative to Parikh et al. (2016) and others, and the contribution rightly lies in the systematic evaluation rather than a novel architecture. However, some design choices feel slightly ad-hoc and could be better justified. For instance, the use of an LSTM in the preprocessing layer for SNLI but not for the other three tasks is explained by computational cost, but this is not substantiated with data on sequence lengths or timings, making the choice feel arbitrary.

Additionally, the analysis in Section 3.4 is somewhat superficial. While the visualization is a nice addition, the explanation amounts to stating that the model focuses on matching words. A deeper analysis into *why* the SUB and MULT functions are so effective would have elevated the paper's impact further. For example, does subtraction excel at capturing antonymy or semantic divergence, while multiplication is better for synonymy?

Despite these points, the weaknesses do not undermine the paper's core empirical contributions, which are solid and valuable.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the manuscript and future work, and they do not detract from my decision to accept.

*   **Deeper Analysis:** The analysis of *why* certain comparison functions perform better could be expanded. Beyond the visualization, you could analyze the distribution of values in the `t_j` vectors produced by SUB vs. MULT for specific linguistic phenomena (e.g., synonyms, antonyms, related terms). This could provide more concrete evidence for your hypothesis that they represent a "good compromise."
*   **Error Analysis:** An error analysis comparing the best model (SUBMULT+NN) with a weaker one (e.g., NN or EUCCOS) would be insightful. Are there specific types of examples (e.g., involving negation, complex syntax) where the choice of comparison function makes the most difference?
*   **Justification of Architectural Choices:** It would be beneficial to provide more rigorous justification for certain design choices. For example, a brief table with average sequence lengths and processing times per batch for each dataset would strengthen the argument for omitting the LSTM from the preprocessing layer on the QA tasks. Similarly, justifying the choice of a CNN for aggregation over a simpler pooling mechanism (like in Parikh et al.) or an RNN would make the model design feel more principled.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

I would appreciate it if the authors could clarify the following points in their rebuttal:

1.  Could you elaborate on the decision to use an LSTM in the preprocessing layer for SNLI but not for the other tasks? Was this decision based on hyperparameter tuning on the dev sets, or was there a prior hypothesis about the nature of the SNLI task that motivated this? Providing average sequence lengths for all four datasets would be helpful context.
2.  Your model uses one-directional attention (attending from sequence Q to A), whereas some related work like the Decomposable Attention model uses a symmetric, two-directional attention mechanism. Could you comment on the motivation for this choice and its potential impact? Did you experiment with a symmetric version?
3.  The paper's key finding is the effectiveness of element-wise comparison functions. Do you have a more developed intuition for *why* these operations are so well-suited for comparing word-level representations in this framework, compared to a learned, unconstrained transformation like the NN function?
