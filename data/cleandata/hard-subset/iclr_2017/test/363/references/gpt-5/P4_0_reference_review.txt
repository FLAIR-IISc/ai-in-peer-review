1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for sequence matching and evaluates it across four tasks/datasets: MovieQA (machine comprehension with answer candidates), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (answer ranking), and SNLI (textual entailment). The model has four components: (i) an order-insensitive preprocessing “gating” layer applied to word embeddings, (ii) a one-directional attention from one sequence to the other, (iii) a token-level comparison function applied to each attended pair, and (iv) a CNN aggregator over the sequence of comparison vectors. The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+Cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and a combination of subtraction/multiplication followed by NN). The authors report that element-wise subtraction and multiplication, especially their combination with a shallow NN, perform strongly and often best. They also provide ablations indicating the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers, and qualitative visualizations. Code is made available.

2. Decision: Reject (primary reasons: limited novelty relative to prior compare-aggregate work; insufficiently rigorous empirical control)
- Novelty: The architecture closely resembles established compare-aggregate models (e.g., decomposable attention; pairwise interaction CNNs; match-LSTM). The specific comparison heuristics (element-wise subtraction and multiplication, or their concatenation with other features) are known and have been used in prior sentence-pair literature; thus the methodological contribution is incremental.
- Empirical rigor and clarity: Baselines are not re-implemented under controlled settings; key implementation details and some dimensional consistency issues (e.g., EUCCOS output and CNN aggregation dimensionality) are unclear; no statistical significance analysis is reported.

3. Supporting arguments
- Literature placement and novelty: The paper situates itself among compare-aggregate approaches (Parikh et al., He & Lin, Wang & Jiang), but the proposed pipeline—soft attention to produce aligned vectors, token-level comparison, and aggregation—maps closely to decomposable attention and related models. The paper’s central novelty claim is the systematic study of comparison functions and the finding that element-wise subtraction/multiplication are effective. However, subtraction/multiplication (often combined with concatenation) have been used previously in sentence-pair models (e.g., Tai et al., Mou et al., and many follow-ups, including variants in decomposable attention implementations). The contribution is thus largely empirical consolidation across tasks, rather than a new algorithmic idea.
- Technical soundness: The components (gated preprocessing, attention, element-wise comparison, CNN aggregation) are standard and technically reasonable. The one-directional attention and order-insensitive preprocessing are conscious design choices; ablations suggest both layers contribute. However, certain specifications are incomplete:
  - The EUCCOS comparator returns a 2D vector per token while other comparators return l-dimensional vectors; the paper defines r in terms of l and sets l=150 globally, but does not explain how CNN filters are adapted for heterogenous comparator dimensionalities.
  - For SNLI, the preprocessing “incorporates LSTM,” but the modification is not fully described (e.g., where sequence modeling is inserted, whether both Q and A use LSTM, parameter sharing).
  - The softmax axis in the attention matrix and exact normalization are not explicitly specified.
- Empirical setup: Results are compared to reported numbers rather than re-running baselines with matched resources (embeddings, preprocessing, candidate pools), which can make leaderboard comparisons less conclusive. No statistical tests are provided. The datasets differ substantially in setup (e.g., InsuranceQA candidate pools); the paper does not clarify whether the same splits and candidate settings as in the baselines are used in all cases. Nevertheless, the breadth across four datasets and the ablations are positives.
- Clarity: The paper is generally readable, with equations for each layer. Some notational inconsistencies/typos and missing details (as above) reduce clarity. The motivation for CNN aggregation vs alternatives (sum/mean pooling, BiLSTM) is not deeply discussed, though the choice is serviceable.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decisive for the recommendation)
- Clarify dimensionality and implementation details:
  - Explain how the CNN aggregator is configured for each comparator, especially when EUCCOS yields 2D token vectors. If a projection to a fixed l is used, specify it.
  - Provide the exact attention normalization (softmax over Q for each token in A, or other).
  - Detail the SNLI variant with LSTM in preprocessing: architecture, parameter sharing, and how it affects comparability with non-SNLI settings.
- Strengthen empirical rigor:
  - Where feasible, re-implement key baselines or run ablations that isolate the impact of each component under a controlled setup (same embeddings, tokenization, candidate pools, and training regimes).
  - Report variance over multiple runs and statistical significance.
  - Include parameter counts and training time to contextualize efficiency claims.
- Expand comparisons and analysis:
  - Include a comparator that concatenates [a, h, a−h, a⊙h] followed by an MLP (a commonly used heuristic in prior work) to directly connect with decomposable attention variants.
  - Explore bi-directional attention and alternative aggregators (e.g., mean/sum pooling, self-attentive pooling, BiLSTM) to validate the specific benefit of CNN aggregation.
  - Provide error analyses per task (e.g., length sensitivity, lexical overlap vs paraphrase) to substantiate the claim that word-by-word comparison is crucial even with order-insensitive preprocessing.
- Revisit novelty positioning: Recalibrate claims about the novelty of element-wise comparison functions by acknowledging prior widespread use and emphasizing the cross-task, systematic evaluation aspect.
- Reproducibility: Ensure code reproduces reported numbers with fixed seeds and includes preprocessing scripts, exact hyperparameters, and links to specific dataset versions/splits.

Questions for the authors
- EUCCOS comparator: How do you handle the 2D token vectors in the CNN aggregator configured for l=150? Is there a learned projection to l, or do you adjust filter shapes per comparator?
- SNLI preprocessing: What is the exact architecture when “incorporating LSTM”? Are both Q and A encoded with LSTMs before attention? Are parameters shared? How does this affect fairness when comparing to other tasks where preprocessing is order-insensitive?
- Baselines and data settings: For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, which specific splits and candidate pool sizes were used? Were all preprocessing steps (tokenization, stopword filtering) matched to those in cited baselines?
- Comparator variants: Did you evaluate concatenating [a, h, a−h, a⊙h] followed by an MLP (a common heuristic)? How do results compare to SUBMULT+NN?
- Attention: Did you try bi-directional or multi-head attention? If so, what was the impact?
- Robustness: What is the variance across random seeds? Are gains consistent across runs?
- Computational aspects: What are parameter counts and training times across the six comparators? Are element-wise comparators consistently more efficient?
- Error analysis: On which phenomena (e.g., paraphrase, negation, long-range dependencies) does the order-insensitive preprocessing hurt or help?
