- Paper studies a compare-aggregate sequence-matching model with gated preprocessing, one-directional attention, six token-level comparison functions, and CNN aggregation; claims element-wise comparisons are strong, achieves SOTA/competitive results, and releases code.  
- Decision: Weak Accept — motivated by strong empirical evidence and a simple, general architecture with ablations.  
- Contribution: Provides a unified, cross-task empirical study of compare-aggregate models and systematic evaluation of comparison functions.  
- Model: Clear pipeline — gated preprocessing, attention (one-directional), six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), and single-layer CNN aggregator.  
- Main empirical finding: Element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) often outperform parametric/similarity-based functions across datasets.  
- Performance: Reports SOTA or near-SOTA on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA and competitive results on SNLI despite model simplicity.  
- Ablations: Removing attention or preprocessing degrades performance; their importance varies with sequence length imbalance.  
- Novelty/positioning feedback: Need to explicitly state novelty relative to Parikh et al. (decomposable attention) and Mou et al. (element-wise ops) and highlight the cross-task empirical insight up front.  
- Reporting rigor: Request means, standard deviations, and statistical significance tests across runs.  
- Dataset clarity: Clarify dataset versions and settings (InsuranceQA version and candidate pool size, WikiQA filtered vs unfiltered, MovieQA text-only split) and confirm official splits.  
- Baseline fairness: Discuss potential confounds from not re-implementing baselines; re-implement strong baselines (e.g., decomposable attention) for apples-to-apples comparisons.  
- Embeddings: Analyze impact of freezing vs fine-tuning embeddings and consider character/subword or contextual embeddings.  
- Aggregator sensitivity: Report sensitivity to CNN hyperparameters (filter counts/windows) and compare CNN to sum/mean, RNN, or self-attention aggregators.  
- Attention design: Compare one-directional vs bi-directional (and multi-head) attention effects.  
- Efficiency/accounting: Provide parameter counts, runtime/memory measurements, and scaling behavior (especially on long MovieQA plots).  
- Interpretability/analysis: Expand beyond one visualization — attention weight distributions, which feature dimensions drive SUB vs MULT, and case studies where element-wise ops help/fail.  
- Error analysis: Break down errors by phenomena (lexical overlap, reasoning needs, length effects) per dataset.  
- Writing/clarity: Tighten notation for preprocessing, ensure consistent equations, and list hyperparameter search ranges and early stopping criteria.  
- Questions for authors: Which InsuranceQA version and candidate pool size? Was WikiQA filtered? Were official splits strictly followed?  
- Questions for authors: Can you re-implement Parikh et al.’s decomposable attention under your setup?  
- Questions for authors: Why keep embeddings fixed and does fine-tuning change comparison-function rankings?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are results to the CNN aggregator versus sum/GRU/LSTM, and would that change the comparison-function ranking?  
- Questions for authors: Did you try bi-directional or multi-head attention, and what were the results?  
- Questions for authors: How stable are results across random seeds (report mean±std), and what are parameter counts and training/inference times?  
- Overall verdict: Valuable empirical study with modest novelty; recommend acceptance if experimental rigor and clarity issues are addressed.
