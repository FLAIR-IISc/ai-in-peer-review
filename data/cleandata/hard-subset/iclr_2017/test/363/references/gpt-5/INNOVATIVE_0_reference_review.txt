Summary
The paper studies sequence matching through a general “compare–aggregate” framework: (i) a light gating-based preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) unidirectional attention to align one sequence to another at the word level, (iii) a word-level comparison function, and (iv) a CNN aggregator for final prediction. The main contribution is an empirical, cross-task evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, element-wise SUB, element-wise MULT, and SUB+MULT followed by a small NN). Across four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), the model attains SOTA or competitive performance, and a key finding is that simple element-wise operations (especially SUB+MULT+NN) consistently perform best, suggesting that lightweight, similarity-oriented comparators can outperform more flexible neural combiners. The model is simple, fast, and broadly applicable; code is released.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: Limited novelty beyond prior compare–aggregate models; the core architectural ideas (word-level alignment and aggregation) and the element-wise comparison trick have appeared before. The paper is primarily an empirical bake-off of comparison functions with modest architectural tweaks, without deeper conceptual advances or analysis explaining why these choices work.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: The compare–aggregate paradigm and attention-based word alignment are well established (e.g., Decomposable Attention, match-LSTM, pairwise word interaction models). Element-wise operations for matching were also used in prior work (e.g., Mou et al., 2016). The proposed SUB/MULT and their combination are framed as new insights, but they largely revalidate known practices across more tasks. The CNN-based aggregator and unidirectional attention are minor variants rather than new concepts.
- Empirical scope vs. insight: The cross-dataset evaluation is useful, and results are strong, especially on InsuranceQA and MovieQA. However, the paper stops at reporting empirical gains and a high-level observation that element-wise ops work well. There is limited analysis into why SUB/MULT succeed (e.g., probing feature distributions, ablations on dimensionality, or ties to metric learning theory). No statistical significance testing is reported, and effect sizes per task are not fully contextualized against strong contemporary baselines reimplemented under a common protocol.
- Experimental rigor and fairness: Embeddings are kept fixed; this choice may disadvantage baselines that tune embeddings in their original reports. Hyperparameter tuning appears limited (except WikiQA), and baseline comparisons rely on reported numbers rather than controlled reimplementations. The preprocessing is order-insensitive (except for SNLI), yet claims generality across tasks with different structure; the evidence is promising but would benefit from more controlled comparisons (e.g., same encoder with/without order, bidirectional attention, and different aggregators).
- Clarity and completeness: The paper is generally clear, but several key details are thin: compute/parameter budgets, sensitivity analyses (l, CNN windows/filters), behavior on long sequences (efficiency), and ablations that quantify the specific contributions of attention vs. gating vs. aggregation across all tasks.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen novelty: Either (a) propose a principled, unified comparator that subsumes SUB/MULT/EUCCOS with learnable weights/temperatures and show it consistently outperforms, or (b) provide deeper analysis/theory explaining why element-wise operations are a sweet spot between rigidity and overflexibility.
- Fairness and rigor: Reimplement key baselines under a shared setup; report parameter counts, FLOPs, and training time. Provide statistical significance. Tune embeddings (frozen vs. fine-tuned) and report the effects.
- Analysis: Include controlled ablations per dataset for attention (uni- vs. bi-directional), order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., lightweight contextual encoders), and aggregator choices (CNN vs. sum/mean/self-attention). Conduct sensitivity analyses over hidden size l, number of CNN filters, and comparison vector dimensionality.
- Interpretability: Go beyond qualitative heatmaps; quantify which dimensions of SUB vs. MULT dominate, how they correlate with lexical overlap vs. paraphrase, and how gating suppresses stopwords.
- Related work: More explicitly distinguish your comparisons from Mou et al. (2016) and Parikh et al. (2016), clarifying what is genuinely new (e.g., cross-task validation, specific SUB+MULT+NN design), and discuss limitations of prior element-wise comparators.

Questions for the authors
1) How large are the gains of SUB+MULT+NN over the best alternative per dataset, and are they statistically significant? Please report averaged results with confidence intervals.
2) Why choose unidirectional attention only? Did you try bidirectional or multi-hop attention, and how did they affect the relative ranking of comparison functions?
3) What is the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings? Do SUB/MULT still dominate when embeddings are trained end-to-end?
4) How sensitive are results to the CNN aggregator design (kernel sizes, number of filters)? Would a simple pooling suffice?
5) Can you provide parameter counts and training/inference costs across comparison functions? Are the simpler comparators also more efficient?
6) The preprocessing gate is order-insensitive for most tasks. Do small contextual encoders (e.g., depth-1 CNN or lightweight self-attention) change the conclusions about the best comparator?
7) SUB/MULT were previously used at sentence level or as heuristic features. Can you clarify what is new in your use at word-level and how your findings differ from those earlier observations?

Overall, the paper offers a clean empirical study with solid results, but lacks a sufficiently novel conceptual contribution for ICLR. Strengthening the methodological novelty or providing deeper theoretical/analytical insight could change my assessment.
