Summary of contributions
- The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences. It instantiates a simple pipeline: (i) order-insensitive gated preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) unidirectional attention to align each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other, (iii) token-wise comparison, and (iv) a CNN aggregator for final prediction.
- The core contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, neural tensor network, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and a combined SUB+MULT+NN), with the finding that element-wise subtraction/multiplication (especially SUBMULT+NN) consistently perform best or near-best.
- The model is evaluated across four tasks/datasets with different formats and length regimes (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving state of the art or competitive results, and ablations indicate the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. Code is released.

Decision
Weak accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear empirical contribution with a simple, strong, and general architecture validated across diverse tasks; (2) actionable and well-supported insight that element-wise comparison functions are robust and effective. Novelty is incremental relative to contemporaneous compare-aggregate work, but the breadth and clarity of empirical analysis make the paper useful to the community.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
1) Specific problem: The paper tackles the general problem of matching two sequences for semantic relatedness/entailment/QA selection, asking which comparison function is most appropriate within a compare-aggregate framework and whether a single architecture transfers across tasks.
2) Motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned within the literature on attention-based and compare-aggregate models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction). It identifies a gap: prior works largely fixed the comparison function (often a generic feedforward layer), without systematically studying alternatives aligned with the objective of measuring similarity. Referencing Mou et al. and Tai et al. shows awareness that element-wise operations exist, but their application and evaluation as token-level comparators in a compare-aggregate pipeline across multiple tasks is a worthwhile focus.
3) Support for claims and rigor: Results on four datasets support the claims that (i) the architecture transfers, and (ii) element-wise comparators are competitive or superior. The ablations (removing preprocessing or attention) provide causal evidence about component utility and offer a nuanced observation about their relative importance depending on length asymmetry between sequences. The study covers multiple baselines from the literature per dataset.

Strengths:
- Breadth: multi-dataset, multi-task evaluation increases external validity and practical relevance.
- Clarity and simplicity: the architecture and comparison functions are clearly specified; the empirical takeaways are easy to adopt.
- Useful negative/positive results: neural tensor and generic NN comparators are not necessarily superior; simple element-wise ops work well. This is actionable for practitioners.
- Some analysis beyond headline figures (component ablations, qualitative visualizations).

Limitations:
- Novelty is modest: the overall framework and even the element-wise SUB/MULT operations have appeared in related contexts; the unique contribution is more systematic evaluation than architectural innovation.
- Empirical rigor could be stronger: no statistical significance tests, limited reporting on variance across runs, and relatively light hyperparameter discussion. Fixing word embeddings (no finetuning) may underutilize capacity and affects fairness of comparisons to strong baselines that finetune.
- Comparisons are taken from reported numbers rather than re-implemented common codebase; this risks dataset-specific preprocessing discrepancies. Some contemporaneous strong baselines (e.g., richer bidirectional attention or tree/ESIM-like variants) are included, but a more unified head-to-head would strengthen claims.
- Design choices (unidirectional attention, order-insensitive preprocessing except SNLI, CNN aggregation) are plausible but not deeply justified against alternatives (e.g., bidirectional attention, RNN/BiLSTM aggregation).

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report mean and standard deviation over multiple seeds; include significance tests for key comparisons (e.g., SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN).
- Provide parameter counts and training/inference time to contextualize the “simplicity” and efficiency claims; long-sequence settings (MovieQA plots) are especially relevant for complexity analysis.
- Clarify hyperparameters and architecture specifics (number and widths of CNN filters, window sizes, dropout placements, attention temperature/scaling) and whether they are fixed across tasks.
- Explore bidirectional attention and order-sensitive preprocessing (e.g., BiLSTM encoders) in at least one task to isolate whether the comparison finding holds when context is stronger.
- Consider updating word embeddings (or show a comparison) to ensure conclusions are not an artifact of frozen embeddings.
- Add an error analysis to illuminate where element-wise comparators help or fail (e.g., lexical overlap vs paraphrase vs negation).
- Discuss generalization to multi-sentence or paragraph-level settings with greater length asymmetry and memory constraints; pruning/attention sparsity could be relevant.
- Calibrate claims on novelty: better acknowledge prior uses of element-wise ops and sharpen the unique contribution as a systematic, cross-task study.

Questions for the authors
- Did you observe variance across random seeds? Are the improvements of SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN over NN/NTN statistically significant?
- How many CNN filters and what kernel sizes are used in aggregation? Are these shared across tasks, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
- Why unidirectional attention rather than bidirectional (e.g., as in decomposable attention)? Does bidirectional attention change the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- For MovieQA, how are plots tokenized/segmented (sentence vs word level), and how do you handle very long contexts computationally? What is the time/memory footprint?
- What happens if you finetune embeddings? Does the relative performance of comparison functions persist?
- Can you report parameter counts per comparison function (especially NTN vs SUB/MULT) to disentangle accuracy from capacity/regularization effects?
- In SNLI you add LSTM in preprocessing; do element-wise comparators remain best when all tasks use contextual encoders?
- Have you tried replacing the CNN aggregator with simple pooling or an RNN? Does the choice of aggregator interact with the comparison function?
