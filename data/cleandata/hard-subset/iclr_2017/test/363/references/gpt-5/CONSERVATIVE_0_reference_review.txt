Summary
The paper proposes a general “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences. It first aligns words in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other, compares aligned word vectors using one of six comparison functions (including element-wise subtraction and multiplication), and aggregates the comparison vectors with a CNN for classification/ranking. The authors evaluate on four tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and claim competitive or state-of-the-art results. A key empirical finding is that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) are consistently strong and often outperform neural network or tensor-based comparison functions. The paper positions this as evidence that compare-aggregate is broadly applicable and that element-wise operations are effective word-level comparators.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) Limited novelty over established compare-aggregate architectures; the main contribution is a modest empirical comparison of comparison functions without strong methodological control. (2) Empirical evaluation lacks rigor needed to substantiate broad claims: baselines are not re-implemented under a common setup, capacity is not controlled across comparison functions, and important experimental details (e.g., candidate pool handling, variance, significance) are missing.

Supporting arguments
- On the specific problem: The paper addresses generic pairwise sequence matching for NLI and QA. This is a well-studied setting; the proposed architecture is very close to existing decomposable/compare-aggregate models (e.g., Parikh et al., Wang & Jiang), with minor architectural variations (unidirectional attention, CNN aggregation).
- On motivation/positioning: The high-level motivation (compare locally, then aggregate) is well-placed in the literature. However, the claimed novelty—systematically comparing comparison functions—has been partially explored (e.g., cosine/dot/Euclidean and element-wise ops in prior work). The paper does not sufficiently differentiate its approach from related models beyond swapping the aggregation module and selecting a set of comparison operators.
- On empirical support and rigor:
  - Baseline comparability: Results are taken from the literature rather than re-implemented and tuned under a shared pipeline. This makes it difficult to attribute gains to the proposed method rather than differences in preprocessing, embeddings, or training protocols. For instance, some prior systems fine-tune embeddings or use context-sensitive encoders by default.
  - Capacity control: The comparison functions have very different parameter counts (EUCCOS/SUB/MULT have none; NN/NTN have many). The study does not control for model capacity or regularize to parity, confounding conclusions about which function is intrinsically better.
  - Reporting/statistics: No multi-seed runs, standard deviations, or statistical significance are reported. Given the modest margins typical on these benchmarks, this weakens the strength of the claims.
  - Dataset specifics: The InsuranceQA setup is under-specified (candidate pool size/sampling strategy strongly affects accuracy). For MovieQA, only the text plot subset is used; comparisons may not reflect the strongest text-only baselines at the time. For SNLI, the preprocessing differs (LSTM used only there), undercutting the “single general model” claim.
  - Ablations: While removal of preprocessing/attention is reported, ablations are limited. There is no study isolating the contribution of the CNN aggregator versus simpler pooling (as in prior decomposable attention). The gating “preprocessing” is order-insensitive, yet the model’s strength is attributed to word-by-word comparison without demonstrating that adding contextualization does not further change the conclusions.
  - Implementation choices: Static GloVe is used without fine-tuning, which may disadvantage the method relative to stronger baselines; conversely, it may artificially favor simpler comparison functions. No analysis of efficiency, memory, or latency is provided.

Additional feedback (to help improve the work)
- Re-implement and tune strong baselines within a unified codebase, including decomposable attention, match-LSTM variants, and recent pairwise interaction CNNs, using the same embeddings, tokenization, and training budgets.
- Control for parameter counts across comparison functions (e.g., add a small bottleneck MLP after EUCCOS/SUB/MULT) and report results with/without capacity matching.
- Report mean/std over multiple random seeds and statistical tests; provide learning curves and calibration metrics.
- Clarify InsuranceQA protocol (full-pool vs. candidate subsets), and include both standard test sets (Test1/Test2). For WikiQA, ensure consistent filtering and question types.
- Expand ablations: aggregator choices (sum/mean/atten-pooling vs CNN), bidirectional attention, with/without contextualization (BiLSTM/GRU) for all datasets, and the effect of fine-tuning embeddings.
- Provide efficiency metrics and parameter counts per variant; discuss when simple element-wise functions suffice versus when richer comparators help.
- Strengthen the analysis: more comprehensive attention maps, error typology, performance vs. sequence length/lexical overlap.

Questions for the authors
1) How large is the candidate pool used for InsuranceQA during training and testing? Do you evaluate on the official full-pool setting, and which test sets (Test1/Test2)?  
2) Did you attempt to control for parameter counts across comparison functions? If not, can you provide results with capacity-matched variants and report parameter/MACs per model?  
3) Why is the preprocessing step order-insensitive on three tasks but LSTM-based on SNLI? Does using the same preprocessing across all tasks change the conclusions?  
4) Are embeddings fine-tuned in any experiment? If not, how do results change with fine-tuning or with contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM before attention) across all datasets?  
5) How many random seeds were run? Please report mean/std and significance tests for the main claims.  
6) For MovieQA, which exact text-only split is used, and are the compared baselines matched to that split and preprocessing?  
7) How does CNN aggregation compare to simpler sum/mean or attentive pooling in your setup?  
8) Can you provide training/inference time and memory usage, and discuss trade-offs among the comparison functions?
