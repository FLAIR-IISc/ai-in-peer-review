- Proposes a task-agnostic “compare-aggregate” model: gating preprocessing, one-directional attention, six word-level comparison functions, CNN aggregation; code released.  
- Evaluated on SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and MovieQA; reports strong / SOTA or competitive results.  
- Main empirical finding: simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) outperform more complex NN/NTN comparators.  
- Decision: Weak Accept — value lies in broad empirical study and practical takeaway that difference/product are robust defaults.  
- Architecture is simple, competitive, and yields strong results on three datasets, competitive on SNLI.  
- Well-positioned relative to prior compare-aggregate work; fills gap by systematically evaluating comparison functions.  
- Methodological clarity: attention and six comparison options are clearly formalized; MovieQA adaptation reasonable.  
- Empirical claims: SUBMULT+NN best overall; EUCCOS/element-wise ops can beat NN/NTN; attention and preprocessing matter.  
- Practical impact: recommends low-parameter element-wise features as a durable default for pairwise matching models.  
- Novelty concern: largely a recombination of known components; incremental contribution centered on systematic evaluation.  
- Experimental rigor concerns: relies on reported baselines rather than reimplementations, lacks variance/significance testing, and unclear embedding fine-tuning policy.  
- Efficiency claim unsupported: no parameter counts or training/inference time comparisons provided.  
- Analysis depth limited: little exploration of aggregation choice (CNN vs pooling/RNN), attention directionality, error types, or gating relation to highway/GLU.  
- Task-specific engineering weakens generality claim (e.g., added LSTMs for SNLI, special MovieQA handling); no single-stack variant shown.  
- Recommendation: report averages/stddevs and significance tests across runs.  
- Recommendation: control for embedding fine-tuning and show results with/without it.  
- Recommendation: report parameter counts and training speed per comparison function to substantiate “simple/efficient” claims.  
- Recommendation: include alternatives for aggregation (sum/avg pooling, BiLSTM) and bidirectional attention comparisons.  
- Recommendation: clarify preprocessing layer, relate to highway/GLU, and compare to contextual encoders (CNN/BiLSTM) across tasks.  
- Recommendation: present failure cases and per-phenomenon breakdowns (lexical overlap, negation, paraphrase, long-distance deps).  
- Recommendation: analyze EUCCOS scaling/normalization effects and whether feature normalization helps.  
- Recommendation: broaden evaluation with additional pairwise tasks (e.g., paraphrase identification / Quora) and detail MovieQA plot handling to avoid leakage.  
- Question: Were GloVe embeddings frozen across all datasets, and how do results change if embeddings are fine-tuned?  
- Question: What is run-to-run variance and are SUB/MULT gains over NN/NTN statistically significant?  
- Question: Why use CNN aggregation rather than pooling or RNNs, and do SUB/MULT advantages hold with other aggregators?  
- Question: Have you tested bidirectional attention (e.g., Parikh et al.); does it help or reduce simplicity benefits?  
- Question: Did you compare the gating preprocessing to highway/GLU or shallow contextual encoders for preprocessing?  
- Question: Can you provide training/inference time and parameter counts per comparison function to assess efficiency/regularization effects?  
- Question: How are long MovieQA plots handled (max length, windowing, selection), and is there any risk of leakage?  
- Question: For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, were strong baselines re-evaluated under the same preprocessing and embedding choices for fair comparison?
