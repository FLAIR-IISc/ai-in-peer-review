Summary and contributions
- The paper studies a “compare-aggregate” architecture for matching text sequences and makes two main claims: (i) the framework is generally effective across varied tasks (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, MovieQA), and (ii) at the word-level, simple element-wise comparison functions (particularly squared difference and product, and a combination thereof with a light NN) outperform standard MLP/NTN and basic distance measures.
- The model has four components: an order-insensitive gating-based preprocessing layer; a one-directional attention to obtain, for each token in one sequence, an attention-weighted representation of the other; a word-level comparison function f among six candidates (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN); and a CNN-based aggregation for final prediction. Code is made available.

Decision: Reject. Key reasons
- Limited novelty: The compare-aggregate paradigm and the key comparison heuristics (element-wise difference/product) have appeared in prior work. The contribution is largely an empirical comparison of known scoring functions within a familiar architecture, with modest architectural tweaks (gating preprocessor, CNN aggregator) and task-specific wiring.
- Insufficient experimental rigor and clarity: Important experimental details and evaluation choices are missing/underspecified; baselines are not re-implemented; fairness across tasks is questionable; and several notational, formatting, and citation issues detract from clarity and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
1) Novelty and positioning
- Compare-aggregate architectures (e.g., match-LSTM; decomposable attention; word-pair interaction models) are well known. The claimed novelty is systematic evaluation of comparison functions and an order-insensitive gate plus CNN aggregator. However, element-wise difference/product features have been used previously (e.g., Mou et al., Tai et al.), and concatenations such as [u, v, |u−v|, u⊙v] are standard. The paper does not convincingly separate itself from these prior formulations or provide new theoretical insights.
- The attention mechanism is one-directional and standard; using CNN for aggregation instead of sum/concat is an incremental modification without analysis of why/how it helps.

2) Experimental rigor and comparability
- Baselines: Results are taken from the literature rather than re-implemented under consistent settings. This is problematic across four tasks whose preprocessing, embeddings, and candidate pool sizes differ substantially. Conclusions about “state-of-the-art” are fragile without controlled comparisons.
- Hyperparameters/training: Embeddings are fixed (not fine-tuned) without justification or ablation; no L2 regularization; batch size/learning rate vary across datasets; only dropout is tuned for most. No variance or statistical significance is reported. It is unclear whether results are averaged over multiple runs.
- Task-specific evaluation choices are unclear:
  - InsuranceQA: Which evaluation protocol (size of candidate pool, test1/test2) is used? Accuracy with 50 vs 500 vs full pool is not interchangeable.
  - WikiQA: Are questions with no correct answer removed (the standard MAP/MRR setting)?
  - MovieQA: Which split and evaluation server? How is the long plot handled (truncation/windowing)? Runtime/memory constraints for attention over long P are not discussed.
  - SNLI: The preprocessor uniquely uses LSTM here (but not elsewhere), weakening the “shared architecture” claim.
- Comparison functions: EUCCOS mixes distance and cosine with very different scales; there is no normalization/temperature discussion. The very common baseline [a, h, |a−h|, a⊙h] followed by an MLP is not compared explicitly (SUBMULT+NN is related but omits raw a and h), making the conclusion about the “best” comparison incomplete.

3) Clarity, notation, and formatting
- Notation overloading and missing operators: The symbol for element-wise multiplication is declared but not always displayed (Eq. 1). Q and A denote both sequences and their lengths; Q is reused to denote the preprocessed matrix; l vs d shift midstream.
- Attention definition lacks axis specification for softmax (over Q or A?). Scaling/normalization is unspecified.
- Numerous typos and formatting issues: “Siamense,” “will fist introduce,” “modes,” broken citations with placeholders (e.g., “? \Q2014\E”), dangling parentheses in Related Work, incomplete author-year formatting, footnote placement (“1”) and URL in body text, etc. Figures/tables referenced (Figs. 1–2, Tables 1–4) are not present; reference to analysis plots without quantitative support.
- Architectural details missing: CNN filter widths, number of filters (nl ambiguously defined), pooling strategy, training epochs, early stopping, gradient clipping, initialization, parameter counts.

4) Evidence vs claims
- Claims of broad effectiveness and superiority are undermined by the above issues. Without controlled re-implementations and statistical tests, it is hard to ascribe gains to the comparison function vs other components (preprocessing gate, CNN aggregator, task-specific choices).
- The ablation findings (importance of preprocessing vs attention depending on length mismatch) are interesting but not quantified in the text and not tied to strong analysis.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper
- Strengthen novelty: Either introduce a principled new comparison operator with analysis, or provide a rigorous, controlled empirical study comparing a comprehensive set of comparison features (including [a, h, |a−h|, a⊙h]) across tasks with standardized settings.
- Re-implement key baselines under the same embeddings, preprocessing, and candidate pools. Report means/std over multiple seeds and significance tests.
- Clarify all evaluation protocols per dataset (splits, metrics, pools, filtering), and ensure test-set evaluation is comparable to prior work.
- Provide full architectural/training details (filter sizes, counts, pooling, epochs, early stopping criteria, gradient clipping, initialization). Include model size and runtime.
- Normalize EUCCOS features or apply a learned affine/temperature; compare against cosine-only and distance-only; analyze scale sensitivity.
- Add bi-directional attention and compare; analyze why CNN aggregation helps; ablate the gating preprocessor and explore contextualized encoders consistently across tasks.
- Improve notation (distinct symbols for lengths vs matrices; avoid overloading Q/A; show all operators) and fix formatting/citation errors.

Questions for the authors
1) What exact evaluation settings were used for InsuranceQA (candidate pool size, test1/test2) and WikiQA (filtered vs unfiltered)? For MovieQA, which split and test server, and how were long plots handled (truncation/window size)?
2) Over how many random seeds are results reported? Are differences statistically significant?
3) What are the CNN hyperparameters (kernel widths, number of filters, pooling)? What is nl precisely?
4) Along which axis is the attention softmax applied in Eq. (2)? Did you try two-way attention?
5) Did you try fine-tuning embeddings? How sensitive are results to this choice and to dropout/L2?
6) How does SUBMULT+NN compare to the common [a, h, |a−h|, a⊙h] + MLP baseline?
7) For EUCCOS, how do you handle scale differences between Euclidean distance and cosine? Any normalization or learned re-scaling?
8) Can you provide per-dataset ablations quantifying the contributions of preprocessing, attention, aggregation, and comparison functions with standard deviations?
