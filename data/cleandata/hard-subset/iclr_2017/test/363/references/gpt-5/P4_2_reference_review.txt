1. Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” architecture for matching text sequences. It first aligns tokens in one sequence to the other with attention, compares each token-pair, and then aggregates the local comparison results to make a decision.
- The main methodological focus is a systematic evaluation of six word-level comparison functions: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN. The authors hypothesize that element-wise operators (SUB, MULT) provide a good balance between overly restrictive similarity scores and overly flexible neural layers.
- The model is applied, with minimal task-specific adjustments, to four datasets spanning different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The authors report strong results (SOTA or competitive) and ablations indicating the importance of attention and their preprocessing gate.
- A secondary contribution is the finding that element-wise comparison (especially SUB/MULT and their combination) tends to outperform learned neural comparators across datasets. Code is released.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) Strong, consistent empirical evidence across diverse benchmarks showing the practicality of the compare-aggregate architecture and the effectiveness of simple element-wise comparison functions; (ii) Clear, well-motivated study that isolates and analyzes the comparison layer, yielding generalizable insights.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and placement: Matching text sequences underpins many NLP tasks. The paper is well-placed within recent work on attention and compare-aggregate models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interactions). The focus on the comparison function fills a gap: prior works typically adopted a default feedforward comparator or a small set of similarities without systematic analysis.
- Technical soundness: The architecture is standard and defensible: attention aligns A to Q; comparison produces per-token match vectors; a CNN aggregates the sequence of matches. The attention, comparison functions (including element-wise operations), and aggregator are all stated concretely. The relationships between SUB/MULT and Euclidean/cosine are correctly characterized.
- Empirical support: The model’s application to four datasets with differing formats (selection, ranking, entailment, machine comprehension with long contexts) supports the claim of generality. The ablation indicating the roles of preprocessing vs. attention under different length regimes is informative. The consistent advantage of element-wise SUB/MULT (or their combination) across tasks is a useful, reusable finding.
- Clarity: The paper is mostly clear and well organized. The problem setup, layer descriptions, and comparison variants are easy to follow.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Report complete experimental details per dataset: kernel sizes/filters in the CNN aggregator, softmax normalization axis in attention, training schedules, and any task-specific sampling strategies (especially for InsuranceQA with large candidate pools).
- Statistical rigor: Provide mean/std over multiple random seeds and statistical significance tests for headline improvements. Indicate early-stopping criteria and dev-set usage to avoid overfitting or implicit test tuning.
- Baseline parity: Where possible, re-implement strong baselines under the same embeddings/training regime, or at least control for critical variables (e.g., whether embeddings are frozen vs. fine-tuned) to isolate the contribution of the comparison layer.
- Architecture choices: Justify the one-directional attention vs. bi-directional attention and the choice of CNN vs. simple pooling/FFN aggregation with ablations. A direct head-to-head with the Decomposable Attention model using your comparison functions would strengthen the analysis.
- Order information: For three tasks, the preprocessing gate is order-insensitive. Consider adding lightweight contextualization (e.g., shallow CNN/GRU) and quantify the trade-off between order sensitivity and efficiency, especially for longer inputs.
- Efficiency and scalability: Provide training/inference time, parameter counts, and memory use; discuss the computational appeal of element-wise comparators relative to learned neural comparators.
- Error analysis: Include qualitative or quantitative error breakdowns to illustrate when SUB/MULT succeed or fail, and whether gains come from particular phenomena (lexical overlap vs. paraphrase vs. negation).

Questions for the authors
- Attention normalization: In Eq. (2), over which dimension is the softmax applied (column-wise to yield attention over Q for each token in A)? Please clarify the exact normalization and whether you tried different scoring functions (e.g., dot-product vs. bilinear vs. scaled dot-product).
- Candidate handling in InsuranceQA and WikiQA: How are negatives sampled during training given large candidate pools? Is the softmax in Eq. (10) computed over all candidates per question or a sampled subset? How does this choice impact MAP/MRR?
- Embeddings: You freeze GloVe. Did you try fine-tuning embeddings, and if so, does the ranking of comparison functions change? Do character/subword embeddings affect results on out-of-vocabulary cases?
- Aggregator: What filter widths and number of filters are used in the CNN? Did you compare against max/mean pooling or a BiLSTM aggregator on [t1..tA]?
- Comparison-layer variants: Beyond SUB, MULT, and their concatenation, did you try absolute difference (|a − h|) or normalized versions (e.g., scaling by norms)? Is SUBMULT without the subsequent NN competitive?
- Robustness and significance: How many runs were averaged for the reported results? Are performance differences between comparison functions statistically significant?
- Fairness vs. prior work: For SNLI, your preprocessing includes an LSTM while for other tasks it does not. Could differences in contextualization confound cross-dataset conclusions about the comparison layer? Can you provide a version with a uniform preprocessing choice across tasks?
- Generalization: Have you tested on additional datasets (e.g., Quora paraphrase, SQuAD sentence-level selection) or multi-task training as suggested in the conclusion? Do the same comparison-layer conclusions hold?

Overall, the paper offers a clear, well-motivated study with strong empirical results and a practically useful conclusion about word-level comparison functions in compare-aggregate architectures. The suggested clarifications and additional experiments would further strengthen the claims and improve reproducibility.
