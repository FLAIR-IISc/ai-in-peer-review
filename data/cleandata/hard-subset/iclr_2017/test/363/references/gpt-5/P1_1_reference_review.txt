Summary of claims and contributions
- Problem: Matching two text sequences is central to tasks like textual entailment and QA. Prior “encode-then-compare” approaches often compress each sequence into a single vector and/or use generic comparison layers without analyzing alternatives.
- Approach: A general compare-aggregate architecture: (i) order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” of token embeddings; (ii) unidirectional attention from one sequence to the other to produce token-aligned soft matches; (iii) word-level comparison functions f(aj, hj); (iv) CNN aggregation over the sequence of comparison vectors for final prediction. For MovieQA, they match both Q–P and A–P and aggregate jointly.
- Core contribution: A systematic evaluation of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), highlighting that simple element-wise differences and products (and their combination) outperform more flexible neural layers and classical similarity/distance functions across tasks.
- Empirical results: On four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), the model delivers state-of-the-art or competitive performance; in particular, strong gains on InsuranceQA and WikiQA. Ablations suggest both the preprocessing gates and attention contribute, with attention more critical for long/length-mismatched pairs.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, consistent empirical evidence across four diverse benchmarks supporting a simple, general architecture and a clear, actionable finding about comparison functions. (2) Released code and well-scoped ablations increase practical value. Novelty is modest, but the empirical contribution is sound and likely useful to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well-placed in the growing literature on compare-aggregate models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise interaction CNNs). It identifies a salient gap: the lack of systematic analysis of the comparison function itself, which is central to these architectures. The proposed element-wise SUB/MULT are motivated as retaining more dimension-wise information than scalar similarities while encoding similarity/relatedness more directly than generic NNs. This is reasonable and borne out empirically.
- Method soundness: The architecture is straightforward and appropriate. The gating layer is a lightweight way to reweight tokens; unidirectional attention aligns each token with an attention-weighted summary of the other sequence; the CNN aggregator provides local composition over comparisons. The multi-candidate scoring for QA tasks is standard.
- Empirical support: Evaluations on four datasets with different formats (classification, ranking, candidate selection; short vs. long texts) strengthen the generality claim. The comparison function study is thorough, and results are consistent: SUBMULT+NN tends to be best; simple SUB/MULT often outperform NN/NTN; EUCCOS is surprisingly strong in some settings. The ablation reporting that attention is more important for length-mismatched cases is insightful.
- Limitations affecting strength of claims: (i) Novelty is primarily empirical; the overall framework is close to contemporaneous decomposable/compare-aggregate models, with differences (CNN aggregator, unidirectional attention, gating). (ii) Some implementation details are underspecified (e.g., CNN filter widths/counts, exact LSTM variant used on SNLI), making full reproducibility harder without the code. (iii) Fairness in comparing comparison functions could be discussed more (parameter counts differ: EUCCOS/SUB/MULT are parameter-free vs. NN/NTN). (iv) No statistical significance testing is reported, and some baselines are taken from literature without reimplementation or consistent tuning. (v) The decision to fix word embeddings may limit performance and should be examined for its impact.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper
- Clarify architectural details: specify CNN aggregation hyperparameters (filter widths, number of filters/windows n, pooling scheme) and the exact “preprocessing with LSTM” setup for SNLI (uni/bi-LSTM, hidden size, where gating applies).
- Control for capacity in comparison-function study: report parameter counts and, if possible, match capacities (e.g., scale the NN layer to approximate the DOF of SUBMULT+NN) or add a small MLP on top of EUCCOS/SUB/MULT to assess whether differences stem from function family vs. parameterization.
- Aggregation ablations: compare CNN to simple sum/avg pooling or a small BiLSTM to isolate whether the gains are due to f(·) or the aggregator.
- Embedding training: report results with fine-tuned embeddings and with character/subword features, especially for WikiQA and InsuranceQA where OOV and morphology can matter.
- Attention direction: consider/evaluate bidirectional attention (each sequence attending to the other) to test whether the unidirectional choice sacrifices performance.
- Efficiency: briefly discuss computational/memory costs for long plots in MovieQA (attention over P×Q and P×A) and any truncation strategies.
- Reporting and significance: add standard deviations over multiple runs and statistical tests for key comparisons.
- Writing and references: fix typos (“Siamense”, citation formatting) and ensure related work coverage is up to date and precise.

Questions for the authors
- SNLI variant: What is the exact preprocessing with LSTM (architecture, hidden size, bidirectionality)? How much does adding order-sensitive preprocessing change results relative to the order-insensitive gate?
- CNN aggregator: What filter widths and numbers were used? Did you try alternative aggregators (sum/avg pooling, RNN)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Parameter fairness: How do you control for the fact that EUCCOS/SUB/MULT are parameter-free while SUBMULT+NN adds parameters? Could a higher-capacity NN/NTN close the gap?
- Embeddings: Why were embeddings kept fixed? What is the delta when fine-tuning them? Any difference in the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- Bidirectional attention: Did you try letting both sequences attend to each other (à la decomposable attention)? If so, what changed?
- MovieQA efficiency: How are long plots handled (max length, truncation)? What is the typical Q/P/A length and the resulting attention matrix sizes?
- Significance/reproducibility: Are the reported improvements statistically significant over multiple seeds? Can you provide training-time variance for key datasets?
- Error analysis: What types of examples benefit most from SUB/MULT? Are there specific linguistic phenomena (negation, paraphrase) where NN/NTN does better/worse?

Overall, despite modest novelty, the paper delivers clear, broadly applicable empirical insights about word-level comparison functions within a widely used paradigm, with strong results across multiple benchmarks.
