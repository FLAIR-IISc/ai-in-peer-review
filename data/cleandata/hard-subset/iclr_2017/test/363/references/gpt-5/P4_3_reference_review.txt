1. Summary of contributions
- The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences, instantiated with (i) an order-insensitive gating-based preprocessing layer, (ii) a unidirectional attention mechanism to align one sequence to another, (iii) a word-level comparison function f between each token representation and its attention-weighted counterpart, and (iv) a CNN-based aggregation for final prediction.
- The main claimed contributions are: (a) demonstrating the effectiveness and generality of this architecture across four diverse tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), reaching state-of-the-art or competitive performance; and (b) a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with the finding that element-wise subtraction/multiplication (and their combination with a small NN) consistently perform best or very competitively, offering a practical guideline for model design.
- The paper includes ablations indicating the importance of attention and preprocessing depending on sequence length disparities and provides qualitative visualizations to illustrate where the CNN aggregator attends.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence across multiple, distinct sequence matching tasks that supports the generality of the compare-aggregate framework and the practical effectiveness of simple element-wise comparison functions.
- Clear, well-motivated analysis isolating the role of the comparison function, filling a gap in prior work where this design choice was underexplored.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity:
- The model is described clearly with precise equations for preprocessing, attention, comparison, and aggregation. The focus on disentangling comparison functions and aligning them to the semantic similarity objective is well motivated and easy to follow.
- Task-specific adaptations (e.g., combining P-Q and P-A comparisons in MovieQA) are explained at a level that allows reproduction, and the design choices (e.g., one-directional attention, CNN aggregation) are contrasted with related models (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016).

Technical soundness:
- The architecture components are standard and sensible; the comparison functions are well defined and cover a spectrum from highly parameterized (NTN) to parameter-free (EUCCOS, SUB, MULT).
- The attention formulation and gating step are consistent with common practice. The aggregation via a shallow CNN is a reasonable alternative to summation/concatenation.
- Ablations support claims about the contribution of attention vs. preprocessing for different sequence-length regimes; qualitative analyses match the intended behavior of the model.

Novelty:
- The compare-aggregate paradigm and decomposable attention are known; element-wise operations have appeared in prior work. The novelty lies primarily in the systematic cross-task evaluation of comparison functions within a unified framework and the empirical conclusion that element-wise SUB/MULT (and their combination) are strong choices. This constitutes incremental but valuable insight for the community.

Empirical results and rigor:
- Results indicate SOTA or very competitive performance on three QA datasets and competitive results on SNLI. The conclusions about comparison functions (SUBMULT+NN best overall; simple EUCCOS surprisingly strong) are consistent across tasks.
- The ablation study supports the stated claims about the importance of attention and preprocessing. Code availability further supports reproducibility.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Experimental protocol details:
  - Report the exact candidate pool sizes and selection protocols for InsuranceQA (e.g., fixed 500-candidate evaluation vs. full-pool), and whether you follow the standard splits and filtering protocols for WikiQA (removing questions with no positive answers). This ensures apples-to-apples comparison with baselines.
  - Provide the CNN aggregation hyperparameters (filter widths, number of filters, activation, pooling strategy) and dropout rates used per dataset, and the axis of the attention softmax (over Q for each A token).
  - Report variance across multiple random seeds and, if feasible, statistical significance for key comparisons, as some performance differences between comparison functions appear modest.
- Fairness and training details:
  - Clarify whether baselines fine-tune embeddings and discuss the impact of not fine-tuning GloVe in your model. A small study on fine-tuning vs. frozen embeddings would strengthen the empirical rigor.
  - For SNLI, precisely describe how LSTM is incorporated into preprocessing and whether other tasks might also benefit from order-sensitive preprocessing.
- Analysis and efficiency:
  - Include parameter counts and runtime/memory comparisons for NN, NTN, SUB/MULT, and SUBMULT+NN, to quantify the efficiency/accuracy trade-offs that motivate element-wise operations.
  - Consider a bidirectional attention variant to assess whether the one-directional choice leaves performance on the table, especially on symmetric tasks like SNLI.
- Broader applicability:
  - Discuss limitations of order-insensitive preprocessing when strong contextual phenomena (negation, coreference) are present and whether contextual encoders (RNN/CNN) in preprocessing materially change the comparison-function ranking.

Questions for the authors
- InsuranceQA:
  - What candidate pool size and selection protocol were used at evaluation time? Did you follow the standard 500-candidate setup? How were negatives sampled during training?
  - Are your reported numbers directly comparable to prior work’s evaluation protocol and splits?
- WikiQA:
  - Did you filter out questions with no correct answers as in Yang et al. (2015)? Which official split/evaluation script was used?
  - Is training pointwise, pairwise, or listwise for ranking, and how does Eq. 10 integrate with that choice?
- Attention and aggregation:
  - Over which dimension is the softmax in Eq. 2 applied (confirm: over Q for each A column)? Did you try temperature scaling or alternative scoring functions?
  - What are the CNN filter widths, number of filters, pooling type (max/avg), and activation? Did you try deeper CNNs or simple summation as in Parikh et al.?
- Preprocessing:
  - For SNLI, how exactly is LSTM integrated into Eq. 1? Are gates computed from contextualized states? Did you test contextual preprocessing on other datasets?
  - Why keep embeddings frozen? What is the impact of fine-tuning on performance and on the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- Robustness:
  - How many runs per setting were averaged? Please report mean±std for key results. Were improvements of SUB/MULT vs. NN/NTN statistically significant?
  - How does performance vary with sequence length and vocabulary coverage (e.g., OOV rate), given embeddings are fixed?

Overall, this paper provides a clear, well-executed empirical study isolating the role of comparison functions in compare-aggregate models, with strong cross-task results and actionable guidance for practitioners.
