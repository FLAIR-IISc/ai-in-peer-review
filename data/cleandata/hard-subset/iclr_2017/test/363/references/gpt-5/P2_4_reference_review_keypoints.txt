- Paper studies a compare-aggregate framework with gating-based preprocessing, unidirectional attention, word-level comparison functions, and a CNN aggregator.

- Main contribution: systematic evaluation of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) showing element-wise SUB and MULT (and their combo) are strong practical choices.

- Evaluation on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI yields SOTA/competitive results on three QA datasets and near-SOTA on SNLI; ablations show preprocessing gates and attention matter; code released.

- Decision: Weak Accept.

- Key reasons for decision: strong empirical performance across diverse tasks and a clear, useful empirical finding that simple element-wise comparisons are robust and practical.

- Relation to prior work: incremental novelty relative to decomposable attention and pairwise interaction CNNs, but valuable cross-task synthesis and emphasis on element-wise comparisons.

- Empirical strengths: breadth of datasets, systematic comparison of six variants, and informative ablation studies aligning with intuition about sequence length asymmetry.

- Empirical concerns: capacity fairness not fully established (EUCCOS produces 2D vectors vs others), downstream CNN capacity may bias results.

- Statistical concerns: no reporting of variance or statistical significance across runs.

- Baseline concerns: missing matched-capacity/reimplemented baselines (e.g., Parikh et al. with CNN aggregator, bidirectional/ESIM-style attention) to strengthen claims.

- Implementation clarity issues: some details underspecified (parameter counts, CNN filter sizes/numbers, MovieQA truncation, SNLI preprocessing LSTM specifics).

- Reproducibility: high-level clarity and released code help, but notational gaps and missing details remain.

- Suggested fixes: report parameter counts and CNN configs, normalize comparison output dimensions, provide mean/std and significance tests, include stronger/matched baselines, and quantify computational cost/latency.

- Suggested analyses to add: error typology, sensitivity to embedding fine-tuning, impact of bidirectional attention, effect of adding contextual encoders uniformly across tasks.

- Clarify MovieQA setup: specify track, plot preprocessing/truncation, and baseline comparability.

- Minor fixes: clean up typos and notation inconsistencies.

- Question 1: Provide exact dimensions and parameter counts for each comparison variant and CNN filter/window settings; did you adjust CNN capacity for EUCCOS’s 2D tj?

- Question 2: How were hyperparameters tuned per dataset and per comparison function, and were settings kept consistent across functions?

- Question 3: What is the variance across random seeds for each dataset/function and are reported gains statistically significant?

- Question 4: Did you try bidirectional/co-attention or iterative alignment; if so, how did results compare, and if not, why restrict to unidirectional?

- Question 5: You keep GloVe fixed—does fine-tuning embeddings or adding subword features change the relative performance of comparison functions?

- Question 6: For SNLI, specify the preprocessing LSTM architecture, whether it’s shared across sequences, and its effect size.

- Question 7: For MovieQA, how are very long plots handled (truncation/chunking) and are baselines evaluated on the same split/text-only track?

- Question 8: Have you evaluated on additional sequence-pair tasks (e.g., Quora paraphrase, SQuAD-style settings) to further validate generality?
