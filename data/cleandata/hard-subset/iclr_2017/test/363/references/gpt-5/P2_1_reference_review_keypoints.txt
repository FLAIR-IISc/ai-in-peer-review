- Proposes a general compare-aggregate sequence-matching architecture with gating preprocessing, one-directional attention, six word-level comparison functions, and CNN-based aggregation.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) and finds element-wise SUB/MULT (and their combo) surprisingly strong.  
- Evaluates on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and reports SOTA on three QA benchmarks and competitive SNLI performance.  
- Ablation studies suggest attention and preprocessing roles vary with relative sequence lengths; qualitative visualizations add intuition.  
- Decision: Accept — motivated by strong cross-task empirical performance and a clear, actionable insight about simple element-wise comparisons.  
- Paper is well situated in prior compare-aggregate literature and fills gaps by providing a unified cross-task validation and a systematic comparison-function study.  
- Empirical claims are largely supported by consistent cross-dataset results and careful comparison-function experiments.  
- Main novelty is moderate since architecture resembles prior work (decomposable attention etc.); primary contribution is the comparison-function study and empirical generality.  
- Rigor concerns: no report of variance or statistical significance to assess robustness across random seeds.  
- Rigor concerns: unclear whether parameter budgets were matched across comparison functions, potentially confounding results favoring parameter-free methods.  
- Missing task-specific protocol details (InsuranceQA candidate pool size, WikiQA filtering, SNLI preprocessing/encoder choices).  
- Using frozen embeddings and zeroed OOVs may bias results and underuse model capacity; sensitivity to fine-tuning is unknown.  
- Baseline comparisons rely on reported numbers with heterogeneous preprocessing/tuning; a head-to-head reimplementation (e.g., decomposable attention) under identical settings is recommended.  
- Recommended: report mean/std over runs and perform significance testing for key comparisons (SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN).  
- Recommended: control for model capacity by matching parameter counts or providing size-normalized analyses to disentangle capacity from functional form.  
- Recommended: clarify dataset protocols for InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI and provide training details (early stopping, epochs, sequence limits).  
- Recommended: add ablation comparing CNN aggregation to simpler pooling (sum/mean/max) to justify architectural choice.  
- Recommended: experiment with training/fine-tuning embeddings and non-zero OOV initialization and report efficiency (training/inference time, memory) since element-wise ops may be faster.  
- Minor: fix writing/typos, reference formatting, and ensure consistent notation for the six comparison functions.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of comparison-function rankings to frozen vs fine-tuned embeddings; whether parameter counts were matched; InsuranceQA candidate pool size and comparability; WikiQA setting (filtered/unfiltered); effects of bidirectional attention; CNN vs pooling benefits across datasets; per-length/category performance breakdowns.
