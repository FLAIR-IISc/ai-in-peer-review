1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies sequence matching via a compare-aggregate framework, arguing it is broadly effective across tasks. It proposes a general architecture with (i) a lightweight gating “preprocessing” layer, (ii) one-directional attention to align tokens, (iii) a word-level comparison function f, and (iv) a CNN-based aggregation. The main methodological contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), including two simple element-wise choices (SUB and MULT) and their combination. The paper evaluates on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), reports strong results (SOTA on three QA datasets and competitive on SNLI), and provides ablations indicating the roles of attention vs preprocessing. The key empirical finding is that element-wise comparison functions perform surprisingly well across tasks, often outperforming more parameterized NN/NTN or scalar similarity measures.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical performance across four datasets with a single general architecture, including SOTA on three diverse QA benchmarks.
- Clear, well-motivated, and useful comparative study of comparison functions that yields a simple, generalizable design insight (element-wise subtraction/multiplication work best).

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Matching two sequences (e.g., QA, textual entailment) by computing fine-grained token-level interactions and aggregating them for a final decision.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper correctly situates itself within compare-aggregate models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise interaction CNNs). It articulates two gaps: lack of a unified, cross-task validation of the general framework, and lack of systematic study of comparison functions at the word level. The link between task objectives (semantic similarity/relatedness) and the choice of comparison function is well argued.

Do results and analysis support the claims?
- Largely yes. The cross-task results substantiate the claim of general effectiveness. The comparison-function study is careful and consistent: element-wise SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) are strong across datasets, often outperforming NN/NTN and EUCCOS. Ablations on attention and preprocessing give plausible explanations tied to relative sequence lengths. The qualitative visualizations provide intuition. The code release aids reproducibility.

Caveats and weaknesses:
- Novelty is moderate: the architecture is close to prior compare-aggregate models (e.g., decomposable attention), with variations (CNN aggregation, one-way attention, gating). The main novelty is the systematic comparison-function study and demonstration of generality across tasks.
- Rigor details are light in places:
  - No statistical significance or variance reported; unclear robustness across seeds.
  - Unclear if parameter budgets are matched across comparison choices (NN/NTN vs parameter-free SUB/MULT), which could confound conclusions on small datasets.
  - Some task-specific details are missing (e.g., InsuranceQA candidate pool size/protocol; WikiQA filtering; SNLI preprocessing choices).
  - Embeddings are frozen and OOVs set to zero; while simplifying, this may bias results and underutilize the model capacity.
- Baseline coverage is reasonable but limited by taking reported numbers; fairness of comparison may vary across pre-processing and tuning regimes. A head-to-head reimplementation of a strong compare-aggregate baseline (e.g., Parikh et al.) under identical settings would strengthen claims about architectural choices (CNN aggregation, one-way attention).

Overall, despite incremental architecture, the empirical breadth and the crisp, actionable insight on word-level comparison functions make this work valuable.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Report mean and standard deviation over multiple runs; perform significance testing on key comparisons (especially between SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN and NN/NTN).
- Control for model capacity: match parameter counts across comparison variants or add regularization to the larger ones; alternatively, add a size-normalized analysis to disentangle capacity from functional form.
- Clarify task protocols:
  - InsuranceQA: candidate pool size at test time; negative sampling; official split adherence.
  - WikiQA: whether the standard “filtered” setting is used; handling of questions with no correct answers.
  - SNLI: exact encoder choice for preprocessing (LSTM vs gating), sequence length limits, and training details (early stopping/epochs).
- Compare against a reimplemented decomposable attention baseline under identical data and embedding settings, to isolate the effect of CNN aggregation and one-way attention.
- Analyze aggregation choices: why CNN vs simple sum/mean/max pooling? A small ablation would strengthen the architectural claim.
- Consider training embeddings (with or without fine-tuning schedules) and non-zero OOV initialization; discuss the trade-offs and any impact on comparison-function efficacy.
- Efficiency analysis: report training/inference costs and memory footprint; element-wise operations likely confer speed advantages—quantify them.
- Writing/typos: clean up references (some formatting issues) and define all symbols upon first use. Ensure consistent notation for the six comparison functions across text and equations.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the conclusions about comparison functions to training embeddings vs frozen embeddings? Does fine-tuning change the ranking of SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN?
- Did you match the number of trainable parameters across comparison variants? If not, can you provide a capacity-controlled comparison?
- For InsuranceQA, what is the candidate pool size at test time, and is your setting directly comparable to prior reported numbers?
- On WikiQA, which evaluation setting (filtered/unfiltered) and preprocessing did you use? Are the improvements robust under both?
- Have you tried bidirectional attention (i.e., attending A to Q and Q to A) in your framework, and if so, how does it affect performance?
- Does the CNN aggregation improve over simpler pooling aggregators across all datasets, or only on longer sequences like MovieQA?
- Could you provide per-category or per-length breakdowns to better understand when SUB vs MULT vs SUBMULT+NN excel?
