1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences and evaluates it across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The key idea is to align one sequence to the other with attention, compare aligned word-level representations, and then aggregate local comparison vectors via a CNN for final prediction. A central contribution is a systematic study of six comparison functions, including two simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN), contrasted against neural (NN/NTN) and distance/similarity (Euclidean+cosine) functions. Empirically, the model achieves state-of-the-art or strong results on three QA datasets and competitive results on SNLI. The analysis suggests element-wise comparison functions are an effective, parameter-light middle ground between rigid distances and highly flexible neural layers. The paper also includes ablations indicating the importance of preprocessing gates and attention, and releases code.

2. Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical performance across multiple, diverse sequence-matching tasks, with a simple, efficient architecture and clear practical takeaway on effective comparison functions (especially SUBMULT+NN and element-wise ops).
- A systematic, cross-task analysis of comparison functions that provides actionable guidance beyond any single dataset.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper squarely targets a core NLP problem—matching pairs of sequences for semantic similarity/relatedness—and argues that word-level compare-aggregate architectures are broadly effective. This is well-grounded in recent literature (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise interaction models).
- Placement in literature and novelty: The architecture is close in spirit to decomposable attention and match-LSTM; the novelty is incremental structurally (one-directional attention and CNN aggregation). However, the careful, controlled comparison of multiple comparison functions across four datasets is new and useful. The element-wise SUB/MULT analysis is especially insightful and under-explored for word-level matching.
- Empirical support and rigor: Results indicate SOTA or competitive performance on three QA datasets and strong performance on SNLI. The use of four tasks strengthens claims of generality, and ablations explore the roles of preprocessing vs. attention under differing sequence length regimes. That said, the paper relies on reported baselines rather than controlled reimplementations; there are no statistical significance tests or variance reporting; and embeddings are frozen, which may limit the conclusions about absolute performance and could interact with the effectiveness of different comparison functions. Nonetheless, the overall empirical support is convincing that compare-aggregate with simple element-wise comparisons works well in practice.

4. Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons: Where feasible, reimplement or at least standardize baselines (same pretrained embeddings, fine-tuning decisions, and training budgets). Report variance across runs and statistical significance for key gains.
- Embeddings and encoders: Freezing GloVe is a conservative choice. Please report the effect of fine-tuning, and consider adding light contextualization (e.g., a shallow BiLSTM/GRU or convolutional encoders) to the preprocessing layer for all tasks to quantify the trade-off between order-sensitivity and efficiency. Clarify why LSTM preprocessing is only used for SNLI.
- Attention design: Compare one-directional vs. bi-directional alignment (as in decomposable attention). Multi-hop attention or cross-attention between Q and A (especially in MovieQA) may further improve results; an ablation would be informative.
- Aggregation choice: Justify the CNN aggregator vs. alternatives (sum/mean, RNN, self-attention). Provide an ablation on window sizes, number of filters, and the impact on performance/efficiency.
- Comparison functions: The SUB/MULT insight is valuable. Consider adding variants like scaled cosine, learned Mahalanobis distance, bilinear forms, or temperature-scaled element-wise ops to probe the space between rigid distances and MLPs. Provide parameter counts and computational costs for each variant.
- Analysis: Include error analyses and qualitative visualizations beyond maxima, covering failure modes (e.g., lexical overlap traps, negation, long-range dependencies). Explore how performance varies with sequence length mismatch, vocabulary overlap, and attention entropy.
- Reporting: Add learning curves and sensitivity analyses to dropout, hidden size l, and optimizer settings. Include wall-clock/runtime and memory comparisons to support the “efficient” claim.
- Clarity and presentation: Clean up reference formatting, and clarify notation (e.g., reuse of Q/Q̄, dimensions for tensors in NTN). For MovieQA, make explicit whether there is any direct Q–A comparison in addition to Q–P and A–P.

Questions for the authors
- How do results change when fine-tuning embeddings and/or adding lightweight contextual encoders in preprocessing across all tasks? Does the relative ranking of comparison functions (SUB/MULT vs. NN/NTN/EUCCOS) persist?
- Why restrict attention to one-directional alignment? Have you tried bi-directional attention or cross-attention between Q and A (especially for MovieQA), and with what effect?
- What guided the choice of CNN for aggregation? How sensitive are results to kernel sizes and number of filters, and how does CNN compare to an RNN or self-attention aggregator?
- Can you report parameter counts, FLOPs, and training/inference times per variant (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) to quantify the efficiency-accuracy trade-offs?
- For MovieQA, is there any direct Q–A interaction? If not, does adding it help?
- How robust are the findings across random seeds? Please provide mean/std over multiple runs and significance tests for key improvements.
- Do SUB and MULT complement each other consistently across datasets, or does one dominate in certain regimes (e.g., high lexical overlap vs. paraphrastic variation)?
- How does performance vary with sequence length differences and attention entropy (e.g., long plots in MovieQA vs. short sentences in SNLI)?
- Could you include an error breakdown (e.g., negation, numerical reasoning, coreference) to better understand the limits of word-level comparisons without richer context?

Overall, this is a solid, well-executed empirical study with practical insights. With strengthened experimental rigor and broader ablations, it would make a strong contribution.
