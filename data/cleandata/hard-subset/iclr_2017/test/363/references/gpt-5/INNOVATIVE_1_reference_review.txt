Summary of contributions
- The paper adopts the compare-aggregate paradigm for text sequence matching and argues that it is a general, effective template across tasks. The model consists of a per-token gating “preprocessing” layer, one-directional attention to produce aligned contextualized vectors, a word-level comparison function, and a CNN aggregation module.
- The central contribution is a systematic comparison of six word-level comparison functions: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN. The authors find that simple element-wise operations (SUB/MULT) and their combination with a small NN generally outperform heavier parametric comparators and sometimes even outperform cosine/Euclidean.
- The model is evaluated on four datasets spanning QA and NLI (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving strong performance—SOTA or competitive—without heavy contextual encoders in preprocessing for three tasks.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty: The architecture is a straightforward recombination of established components within the already well-known compare-aggregate paradigm (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016; He & Lin 2016; match-LSTM). The main novelty—emphasizing element-wise SUB/MULT at the word level—is incremental.
2) Insufficiently controlled empirical evidence for the core claim: While cross-task results are strong, the comparisons among functions are not fully controlled (e.g., dimensionality/parameter-count parity, fairness vis-à-vis baselines), reducing the strength of the central conclusion.

Supporting arguments
On the three criteria:
1) Problem tackled: Provide a general compare-aggregate model for sequence matching and systematically investigate word-level comparison functions, arguing that element-wise operations offer a sweet spot between rigid similarities and over-parameterized comparators.
2) Motivation and placement: The paper is well positioned within the literature, clearly referencing match-LSTM, decomposable attention, and pairwise interaction models. However, the conceptual advance beyond these works is modest. Using element-wise operations as comparison features has been explored in Siamese settings; the novelty here is mainly applying them at word-level with attention.
3) Do results support claims: Results across four datasets are encouraging, and the ablations on attention and preprocessing are helpful. However:
   - Comparison functions are not matched for capacity. EUCCOS yields a 2-d vector while SUB/MULT yield l-d vectors that flow into a CNN; this could bias capacity and expressivity. A projection to l dims for EUCCOS would be a fairer control.
   - Baselines are taken from prior reports, not re-run under a uniform setting; preprocessing, embeddings, and data filtering often differ across papers, which complicates SOTA claims.
   - The choice of one-directional attention and order-insensitive preprocessing is interesting, but the justification and alternatives (e.g., bi-directional attention, lightweight contextual encoders) are not explored.
   - No statistical significance testing, and limited hyperparameter details for the CNN aggregation layer.

Strengths
- Clear, task-agnostic pipeline; simple, efficient components.
- Cross-task experiments and ablation analyses provide some evidence of generality.
- The empirical finding that simple element-wise comparisons are competitive or superior is a useful takeaway for practitioners.

Weaknesses limiting acceptance
- Novelty is incremental: the framework and most components are established, and SUB/MULT features are well known in sentence-pair modeling.
- The central empirical claim (element-wise operations are best) is undercut by capacity mismatches and incomplete controls.
- Missing analyses on when different comparators win/fail; limited qualitative error analysis.
- Limited detail on the CNN aggregator (filter widths, number of filters, pooling), and no comparison to equally simple alternatives (sum/mean pooling + MLP) under identical settings.

Suggestions to improve (constructive, not decisive)
- Control for capacity across comparators: project EUCCOS to l dims, or reduce SUB/MULT via bottlenecks; report parameter counts and training times.
- Include bi-directional attention and a simple contextual preprocessing variant (e.g., 1-D conv or light GRU) to test whether the “order-insensitive” claim holds more generally.
- Report statistical significance and confidence intervals; provide per-task hyperparameters, CNN architecture details, and training stability metrics.
- Provide an error analysis: where SUB vs MULT helps; task-specific patterns; failure modes where contextual order becomes essential.
- Ensure fair SOTA comparisons: re-run strong baselines under the same embeddings, preprocessing, and splits, or carefully discuss differences.
- Explore multi-task training (as hinted) to strengthen the “general framework” claim.

Questions for the authors
- How do you control for the representational capacity difference between EUCCOS (2 dims) and SUB/MULT (l dims)? What happens if you pass EUCCOS through the same NN to produce an l-d vector before aggregation?
- Did you evaluate bi-directional attention or symmetrical compare-aggregate (both directions) and how does it affect the conclusions about comparison functions?
- How sensitive are the findings to fine-tuning word embeddings, to the hidden size l, and to the CNN filter widths/number?
- For WikiQA and InsuranceQA, can you confirm that your data preprocessing and candidate pools exactly match prior work you compare against?
- Can you provide training/inference time and memory usage across comparison functions to support the “simple and effective” claim?
- In cases requiring strong compositional reasoning, does the order-insensitive preprocessing degrade performance? Could a lightweight contextual layer change the relative ranking of comparison functions?

Overall, this is a solid empirical study with useful takeaways, but lacks a sufficiently novel modeling idea for ICLR under a novelty-focused bar.
