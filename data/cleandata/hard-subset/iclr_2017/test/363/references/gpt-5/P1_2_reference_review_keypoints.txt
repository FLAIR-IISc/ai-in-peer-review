- Paper studies a simple "compare-aggregate" architecture (gating preprocessing, one-directional attention, word-level comparison, CNN aggregation) for sequence matching.
- Main contribution is a systematic evaluation of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCLIDEAN+COSINE, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), highlighting simple element-wise SUB and MULT.
- Experiments on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI show competitive or state-of-the-art results on three datasets and strong SNLI performance; code is released.
- Decision: Accept — due to strong, consistent empirical results across diverse tasks with a single simple architecture.
- Key insight: element-wise comparison (SUB/MULT and SUBMULT+NN) is a robust, high-performing middle ground between rigid similarity measures and over-parameterized neural comparators.
- Literature placement: well-situated among prior compare-aggregate models and convincingly motivates focusing on the comparison function.
- Hypothesis support: element-wise ops preserve dimension-wise information and capture distance/similarity, which empirical results corroborate.
- Breadth/generalization: one backbone applied across multiple task types demonstrates the approach's versatility.
- Experimental design: controlled comparisons within a single backbone and ablations (preprocessing, attention) provide useful diagnostic insights.
- Simplicity/efficiency: design avoids heavy encoders for long sequences, making it practically appealing.
- Reproducibility: code release is a positive contribution.
- Fairness suggestion: EUCLIDEAN+COSINE outputs 2D vectors — propose projecting it to l dimensions to control for capacity in comparisons.
- Statistical rigor: request reporting mean/std over runs, statistical significance, validation curves, and sensitivity analyses for key hyperparameters.
- Training details: ask for more hyperparameters (CNN kernel sizes, filter counts, pooling, parameter counts) and runtime/memory comparisons across functions.
- Embeddings: ask to report effects of fine-tuning GloVe and whether comparison-function rankings change, plus OOV handling sensitivity.
- Attention variants: recommend evaluating bi-directional/co-attention or multi-head attention to verify if conclusions hold and if performance improves.
- Preprocessing: suggest testing lightweight contextualization (convs or shallow BiLSTM) and reporting its effect, especially on long-sequence tasks.
- Baselines/splits: ensure inclusion of strong compare-aggregate baselines (e.g., Decomposable Attention) with comparable preprocessing/embeddings and clarify dataset splits.
- Error analysis: request qualitative failure cases and per-phenomenon breakdowns (lexical overlap, paraphrase, negation, multi-sentence reasoning).
- Efficiency benchmarks: ask for time/throughput and memory plots on long sequences to substantiate efficiency claims.
- Question — EUCCOS capacity: did you try projecting EUCLIDEAN+COSINE to l dims to separate capacity from measure choice?
- Question — robustness: how stable are comparison-function rankings across seeds and hyperparameters (please report mean/std)?
- Question — embeddings: what changes if embeddings are fine-tuned; do SUB/MULT remain superior and how sensitive are results to OOVs?
- Question — attention experiments: did you try bi-directional or multi-head attention and did that change rankings or improve performance?
- Question — aggregation: if CNN is replaced by LSTM or pooling, do SUB/MULT still dominate and how much does CNN contribute?
- Question — MovieQA specifics: how are very long plots handled (truncation/sliding), what are computational/memory costs, and does attention become diffuse?
- Question — preprocessing on SNLI: how critical is the LSTM preprocessing to SNLI results and can a unified preprocessing work across tasks?
- Question — interpretability: do certain embedding dimensions consistently dominate for SUB/MULT and what do visualizations reveal about their behaviors?
