Summary
This paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching two text sequences and, more specifically, systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions within that framework (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The model uses a lightweight, order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” on word embeddings, a one-directional attention from one sequence to the other, a comparison module applied token-wise between a token and its attention-weighted counterpart, and a CNN-based aggregation for final prediction. The authors apply the architecture to four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), claiming state-of-the-art or highly competitive results, and find that element-wise subtraction and multiplication (alone or combined with a small NN) consistently perform best. The paper’s main contributions are: (1) evidence that a simple compare-aggregate design transfers well across tasks, and (2) a careful, cross-task comparison of word-level comparison functions indicating that element-wise operations are strong, parameter-efficient choices.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: The paper provides a clean, general architecture and delivers strong empirical performance across diverse tasks with a clear, replicable takeaway: simple element-wise comparison features are highly effective. While the conceptual novelty is incremental relative to prior compare-aggregate work, the systematic cross-task evaluation and practical findings are valuable to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and motivation: The work is well placed in the literature on attention-based and compare-aggregate models (e.g., Parikh et al., Rocktäschel et al., Wang & Jiang, He & Lin). The gap identified—insufficient attention to the choice of the local comparison function and a lack of cross-task evaluation—is real and relevant. The hypothesis that element-wise operations provide a useful middle ground between highly parameterized MLP/NTN and overly compact scalar similarities is reasonable and borne out empirically.

- Methodological clarity: The architecture is clear: (i) position-wise gated preprocessing (akin to highway/GLU-style gating but order-insensitive), (ii) one-directional attention (bilinear form), (iii) token-wise comparison, and (iv) CNN aggregation. The MovieQA extension that matches both Q–P and A–P and aggregates over P is sensible.

- Empirical support: The paper reports strong results on three QA datasets and competitive results on SNLI, with consistent advantages for SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN. The ablations suggest both preprocessing and attention matter, with attention helping more in length-mismatched settings. These results support the claims that (a) the framework is broadly useful and (b) element-wise comparison is robust.

- Practical significance: The finding that simple, parameter-light comparison functions often outperform or match heavier NN/NTN designs is an actionable insight likely to influence future model design and baselines. The cross-task perspective increases confidence in generality.

- Concerns/limitations:
  - Novelty is incremental: The overall framework is close to prior compare-aggregate models (e.g., decomposable attention). The main novelty is the systematic comparison of local f(·,·) and the cross-task evaluation.
  - Statistical rigor: The paper does not report confidence intervals, multiple runs, or significance tests; given modest performance gaps in some settings, variance estimates would increase confidence.
  - Capacity control: Comparisons among NN/NTN vs SUB/MULT may confound parameterization and regularization effects. Capacity-matched MLPs or weight-decayed variants would help isolate the effect of the comparison operation itself.
  - Aggregation choice: The CNN aggregator is not ablated against simpler sum/mean pooling (as in Parikh et al.) or RNN aggregation, leaving unclear how much gain comes from aggregation vs comparison.
  - One-directional attention: Bi-directional alignment has been important in related work. It would strengthen the case to show whether the conclusion about comparison functions holds under bi-directional attention.
  - Candidate listwise scoring: For InsuranceQA/MovieQA, the final scoring p(k|R) is listwise and depends on all candidates jointly. This may be advantageous relative to pointwise baselines; fairness and impact should be discussed or ablated.
  - Preprocessing choices: The order-insensitive gating is unusual for sentence matching. While the authors note adding LSTMs for SNLI, the asymmetry across tasks complicates claims of a unified framework.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report variance across multiple seeds and significance tests for key comparisons, especially where improvements are small.
- Provide parameter counts and capacity-controlled baselines (e.g., skinny MLPs) to ensure improvements are not solely due to regularization effects of element-wise features.
- Ablate the CNN aggregator versus sum/mean pooling or light-weight RNNs to quantify its contribution and to align more directly with Parikh et al.
- Explore bi-directional attention and show whether the relative ordering of comparison functions persists.
- Clarify and ablate listwise scoring (Eq. 10) vs independent per-candidate scoring to assess its contribution, particularly on InsuranceQA/MovieQA where it could yield additional gains over baselines.
- Expand implementation details: kernel sizes/number of filters (n), stride, padding, dropout locations, and early stopping criteria to ease replication.
- Consider fine-tuning embeddings or replacing zero-initialized OOVs with learned vectors; report the impact on results.
- Provide runtime and memory considerations for attention over long plots (MovieQA), and any truncation/stride heuristics used.
- Add qualitative error analysis and per-length or per-overlap breakdowns to diagnose where SUB/MULT help most.
- Relate the gating layer more explicitly to highway networks/GLU to connect with broader sequence modeling literature.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are your conclusions about comparison functions to the choice of aggregator? Do SUB/MULT still win with simple pooling or an RNN aggregator?
2) What is the variance across runs? Please report mean±std over at least 3 seeds for the main results, and indicate which differences are statistically significant.
3) Can you provide capacity-matched comparisons (e.g., small MLPs with similar parameter counts to SUB/MULT) to disentangle parameterization from functional form?
4) How much does the listwise candidate scoring (Eq. 10) contribute versus scoring each candidate independently? Please provide an ablation.
5) Why choose one-directional attention only? Does bi-directional attention improve results? If so, do the same comparison-function rankings hold?
6) For MovieQA, how do you handle very long plots (truncation/splitting)? What are the computational costs?
7) For SNLI, you use an LSTM in preprocessing, whereas for other tasks you don’t. How does this affect comparability, and do your main findings hold without the LSTM on SNLI?
8) Did you try fine-tuning word embeddings or learned OOV vectors? What is the impact?

Overall, despite incremental novelty, the paper provides clear, generalizable empirical insights and a simple, effective design. With strengthened analysis and ablations as suggested, it would make a solid contribution.
