Summary
The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences and, in particular, systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions within this framework: NN, NTN, EUCCOS (Euclidean distance + cosine similarity), SUB (element-wise squared difference), MULT (element-wise product), and SUBMULT+NN (concatenation of SUB and MULT followed by a small NN). The model consists of a lightweight, order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” layer, a one-directional attention from one sequence to the other, a per-token comparison using one of the above functions, and a CNN aggregation layer for classification/ranking. The approach is evaluated across four tasks/datasets: MovieQA (MC), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (ranking), and SNLI (entailment). Results show strong performance on three QA datasets and competitive results on SNLI; element-wise comparison functions (especially SUBMULT+NN, and in some cases even the parameter-free SUB or MULT) perform best overall. The paper provides ablations indicating that both the preprocessing gates and attention contribute, with attention more helpful when sequence lengths are very different.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons:
- Clear empirical contribution: a simple, general compare-aggregate model that performs strongly across multiple, diverse sequence matching tasks, with a careful, cross-task study of comparison functions showing that simple element-wise operations are robust and effective.
- Practical insight: the finding that SUB/MULT outperform more flexible NNs/NTNs is valuable for future work; the approach is easy to implement and efficient.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Generality and breadth: The same architecture is instantiated on four distinct problems with minimal task-specific modifications, supporting the claim that compare-aggregate is broadly useful.
- Systematic study of comparison functions: Evaluates six alternatives under a common framework and training protocol, revealing that element-wise operations are strong middle ground between rigid similarity metrics and over-parameterized NNs/NTNs.
- Competitive/state-of-the-art results on several datasets, with consistent gains on InsuranceQA and strong performance on WikiQA and MovieQA; SNLI results are competitive given the simplicity.
- Ablation analysis: Demonstrates the contribution of the preprocessing gates and attention, with reasonable interpretations tied to sequence length differences.
- Reproducibility: Code is released; model details are described, including optimizer and key hyperparameters.

Weaknesses
- Novelty is moderate: The architecture follows prior compare-aggregate designs (e.g., Parikh et al., match-LSTM, pairwise interactions). The main novelty is the cross-task, controlled comparison of comparison functions and a particular aggregation choice (CNN).
- Limited analysis depth: While ablations are provided, there is little statistical analysis (variance across runs, significance tests), no parameter-count or runtime comparisons, and limited error analysis. Claims about order-insensitive preprocessing vs. order-sensitive models could be supported more rigorously.
- One-directional attention only: Many strong models use bi-directional/co-attention; it is unclear whether the element-wise findings hold under more expressive attention.
- Potential lexical matching bias: Visualizations and results may indicate the model is driven by lexical overlap; robustness to paraphrase/semantic variation is not analyzed.
- Some implementation choices are under-motivated: embeddings are kept fixed; SNLI alone uses an LSTM in preprocessing, which breaks strict architectural uniformity across tasks.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide statistical robustness: report mean/std over multiple seeds and significance tests, especially when differences among comparison functions are small.
- Parameter and efficiency analysis: include number of parameters, training time, and inference speed; compare the cost/benefit of NN/NTN vs. SUB/MULT.
- Clarify aggregation: justify the choice of a 1-layer CNN vs. alternatives (sum/avg pooling, RNN/Transformer, self-attention). A small study could show whether the comparison-function findings are invariant to the aggregator.
- Expand attention analysis: compare one-directional vs. bi-directional/co-attention to understand interactions with the comparison functions.
- Control for embeddings: show results with trainable embeddings and with contextual encoders (e.g., LSTM/GRU) uniformly across tasks to assess whether the element-wise findings persist.
- Error analysis: provide qualitative errors and breakdowns (e.g., by lexical overlap, paraphrase, length, negation) to support claims about what the model learns.
- Fairness of baselines: where feasible, re-implement or at least harmonize training settings for key baselines; otherwise, detail differences in data preprocessing and candidate pools (e.g., WikiQA) that could affect comparability.

Questions for the authors
- Statistical robustness: How many runs per setting were conducted? Please report variance and significance for the best-performing comparison functions.
- Embeddings: Why fix GloVe embeddings? Do results improve or ranking of comparison functions change when embeddings are fine-tuned?
- SNLI preprocessing: You mention incorporating LSTM only for SNLI. Please detail how it is inserted relative to gates and attention, and whether using LSTM on other tasks changes the conclusions.
- EUCCOS scaling: Since EUCCOS yields a 2D vector per token, how is it normalized across tokens/positions before CNN? Did you experiment with temperature/scaling or normalization to stabilize training?
- Attention formulation: Did you try bi-directional or co-attention? If so, did the relative ordering of comparison functions change?
- MovieQA plots: How do you handle very long plots (truncation, sliding windows)? What fraction of the plot is attended on average?
- Lexical overlap: Did you evaluate robustness to paraphrase (e.g., on SNLI subsets with low overlap)? Are gains mainly driven by overlap-sensitive cases?
- Hyperparameters and architecture details: Please provide window sizes, number of filters in CNN, and l sensitivity analyses. Are improvements consistent across these choices?
- Reproducibility: The code release is appreciated. Does it include scripts to replicate all four datasets with the reported preprocessing and candidate generation?
