Summary and contributions
The paper investigates a broad, task-agnostic “compare-aggregate” architecture for matching text sequences and systematically studies the impact of the word-level comparison function. The model: (i) preprocesses word embeddings with a gating layer, (ii) uses one-directional attention to align each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other, (iii) compares aligned vectors via one of six functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), and (iv) aggregates with a CNN to predict a label. The paper evaluates across four tasks/datasets—SNLI (entailment), InsuranceQA and WikiQA (answer selection/ranking), and MovieQA (machine comprehension with plot synopses)—and reports strong to state-of-the-art results, finding that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, and their combination) consistently outperform more general neural comparison modules. The paper also includes ablations indicating the importance of preprocessing and attention, and releases code.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Broad empirical study across diverse sequence matching tasks and a focused, useful takeaway: simple element-wise comparison features (difference and product) are strong, robust choices. This is a practically impactful result for the community.
- The architecture is simple and competitive; results are strong on three datasets and competitive on SNLI.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well-placed with respect to prior “compare-aggregate” work (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word-interaction models). The specific focus on the comparison function is timely and underexplored; prior works often defaulted to MLPs or global distances without a systematic evaluation. The argument that we want functions that reflect similarity yet preserve dimension-wise structure is reasonable, and the proposed SUB/MULT variants are grounded in prior heuristics (e.g., Mou et al. 2016) but evaluated more broadly.
- Methodological clarity: The high-level model is clearly described. The attention mechanism and comparison layer are formalized, and the six comparison options are explicit. The MovieQA adaptation (dual matching of question–plot and answer–plot) is reasonable.
- Empirical support: Although detailed tables are not included in the excerpt, the narrative claims that: (1) the method achieves SOTA or very competitive results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, and WikiQA; (2) SUBMULT+NN is best overall; (3) very simple comparisons (EUCCOS or element-wise ops) can outperform NN/NTN; (4) attention and preprocessing both matter, with their relative importance depending on sequence length asymmetry. These findings are plausible and align with what we know from decomposable attention and later NLI systems that use difference/product features.
- Practical impact: The conclusion that element-wise difference and product are strong, low-parameter choices is both simple and durable, and can standardize a design choice in future pairwise matching models.

Concerns and limitations
- Novelty: The architecture is a recombination of known building blocks (attention-based alignment, difference/product features, CNN aggregation). The novelty lies mainly in the systematic evaluation of comparison functions across tasks. This is incremental rather than conceptual.
- Experimental rigor: Comparisons appear based on reported numbers rather than reimplementations; no statistical significance or variance is reported. The generality claim is somewhat weakened by task-specific deviations (e.g., adding LSTMs for SNLI, special handling for MovieQA). No efficiency analysis is provided despite claims about RNN cost. It is unclear whether word embeddings are frozen across all tasks (the paper says not updated), which can disadvantage the model relative to baselines that fine-tune embeddings.
- Analysis depth: While ablations on attention vs preprocessing are informative, there is limited analysis of aggregation (why CNN vs pooling/LSTM), bidirectional attention vs the chosen unidirectional variant, or error typologies. The gating “preprocessing” resembles highway/GLU-like components; the connection and design choices are not discussed.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Strengthen the empirical section:
  - Report full numbers with standard deviations over multiple runs, and conduct significance tests.
  - Control for embedding fine-tuning; show with/without fine-tuning for fairness and to check robustness.
  - Provide parameter counts per comparison function and training speed to substantiate the “simple and efficient” claim.
  - Include results with alternative aggregation (sum/avg pooling, BiLSTM) and bidirectional attention to justify the CNN and one-way attention choices.
- Clarify the preprocessing layer:
  - Discuss relation to highway networks/GLUs and why an order-insensitive gating is preferable in three tasks; show a variant with contextualization (BiLSTM or CNN) beyond SNLI to quantify trade-offs.
- Generality vs task-specific engineering:
  - Be explicit where the model diverges per task (e.g., SNLI adds LSTM; MovieQA uses two alignments). Consider a “single-stack” variant applied unchanged across tasks to support the generality claim.
- Expand analysis:
  - Provide failure cases and per-phenomenon breakdowns (e.g., lexical overlap vs paraphrase, negation, long-distance dependencies).
  - For EUCCOS, explain the impact of scale (cosine vs unnormalized product), and whether feature normalization helps.
- Broaden evaluation:
  - If feasible, include an additional pairwise matching task (e.g., paraphrase identification, Quora duplicates) to corroborate findings.
  - For MovieQA, detail handling of long plots (truncation, stride), and ensure no leakage from plot synopses formatting.

Questions for the authors
1. Embeddings: Were GloVe embeddings frozen across all datasets? Many baselines fine-tune embeddings; how do your results change if you fine-tune?
2. Variance/significance: What is the run-to-run variance across random seeds? Are the gains of SUB/MULT over NN/NTN statistically significant?
3. Aggregation choice: Why CNN over simple pooling or RNN aggregation? Do SUB/MULT’s advantages persist across different aggregators?
4. Attention directionality: Have you tested bidirectional attention (as in Parikh et al.)? Does it improve performance or diminish the simplicity advantages?
5. Preprocessing layer: This gating resembles highway/GLU mechanisms. Did you compare against a highway nonlinearity or a shallow contextual encoder (e.g., 1D CNN/BiLSTM) for the preprocessing step?
6. Efficiency: Can you report training/inference time and parameter counts per comparison function? Is SUB/MULT’s superiority partly due to regularization via fewer parameters?
7. MovieQA specifics: How are long plots handled (max length, windowing)? Are question/answer-aware plot segments selected before matching, and if so, how?
8. Baselines: For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, did you re-evaluate any strong recent baselines under your data preprocessing and embedding choices to ensure apples-to-apples comparison?

Overall, despite limited conceptual novelty, the empirical study is useful, the model is simple and strong across tasks, and the main takeaway—element-wise comparison is an excellent default—is valuable to the community. Hence a weak accept.
