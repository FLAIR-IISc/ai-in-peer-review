Summary of contributions
- The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” paradigm for text sequence matching, arguing it applies broadly across tasks. It instantiates a lightweight model with (i) a per-token gating “preprocessing” layer, (ii) one-directional attention to align A to Q, (iii) a token-wise comparison function, and (iv) a CNN aggregator for classification.
- A central contribution is a systematic comparison of six comparison functions: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN. The authors hypothesize that element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) strike a good balance between expressivity and inductive bias toward similarity, and empirically find SUBMULT+NN (and sometimes EUCCOS) to perform best.
- Experiments on four datasets spanning two problem types—QA (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and textual entailment (SNLI)—show competitive to SOTA results in several settings, with ablations indicating that attention and the gating layer are important and that word-by-word comparison contributes substantially. Code is provided.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty/insufficiently distinct contribution beyond prior compare-aggregate and decomposable attention architectures; the primary novelty is the comparison-function study, which lacks rigorous capacity controls and fairness checks.
- Empirical evaluation is promising but not sufficiently rigorous: baselines are uneven/outdated in places, comparison functions differ in dimensionality/capacity, and several experimental details and protocol choices are unclear (e.g., dataset variants, significance, hyperparameter tuning).

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement in literature:
  - The architecture is close to prior compare-aggregate work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016), with relatively minor differences (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation, per-token gating). The claim that element-wise comparison is under-explored is weaker given prior use of |u−v| and u⊙v features in NLI and matching.
  - The paper’s most distinctive component is the comparison-function study, yet the methodology does not convincingly isolate the effect of the comparison due to capacity and normalization differences.

- Fairness and rigor of comparisons:
  - Capacity mismatch: EUCCOS yields a 2D vector per token, whereas NN/NTN/SUB/MULT/SUBMULT produce l-dimensional vectors. Feeding 2D inputs to the same CNN likely handicaps EUCCOS. A fair test would match downstream capacity (e.g., project all tj to a fixed l with a linear layer).
  - Parameter count: SUB and MULT are parameter-free while NN/NTN add parameters; SUBMULT+NN adds even more. Without parameter-matched variants, it is difficult to attribute gains to the comparison operation vs model size.
  - Normalization: MULT is presented as “related to cosine,” but cosine requires normalization. Evaluating normalized variants (e.g., u/||u|| ⊙ v/||v||, or adding |u−v| commonly used in NLI) would strengthen the analysis.

- Experimental scope and baselines:
  - SNLI baselines include strong 2016 models, but more recent decomposable/ESIM-like systems with |u−v| and u⊙v would be relevant; a head-to-head with Parikh et al. using matched data and resources is warranted.
  - WikiQA: it is unclear whether the standard filtered setting (removing questions with no positive answers) is followed. MAP/MRR can change substantially with protocol.
  - MovieQA: baselines seem limited to early systems; the field has more recent attention-based readers. It is unclear how long plots are handled (truncation, stride, or memory limits).
  - InsuranceQA: specify which test set(s) (Test1/Test2) and candidate pool sizes; many works report both.

- Architecture and implementation details:
  - The “order-insensitive” preprocessing layer contrasts with using LSTM for SNLI only. This weakens the claim of a single general architecture and may influence comparative outcomes.
  - Critical details of the CNN aggregator (filter widths, number of filters, pooling) are missing; these can strongly affect results.
  - Embeddings are frozen; this is atypical for SNLI and QA and could disadvantage baselines that fine-tune, or alternatively understate what your model can achieve.

- Analysis:
  - Ablations showing the value of attention and preprocessing are useful, but no statistical significance is reported.
  - Visualization is qualitative; more quantitative error analyses (e.g., by syntactic phenomena, lexical overlap) would bolster claims about where element-wise comparisons help.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper; not all impact my decision)
- Ensure capacity-matched comparisons: add a small learned projection on top of all tj to a common dimension l before the CNN, so EUCCOS/NN/NTN/SUB/MULT/SUBMULT feed equally expressive signals downstream.
- Include normalized variants and common heuristics: |u−v|, u⊙v, concatenation with [u;v], and cosine with learned temperature; evaluate whether normalization helps stability.
- Try bi-directional attention (A↔Q) and compare to your one-directional variant; this is known to help in decomposition models.
- Standardize the architecture across datasets or explicitly justify and quantify the gains from SNLI’s LSTM preprocessing. Alternatively, add a version without LSTM on SNLI to isolate the effect of comparison choice.
- Strengthen baselines and protocols:
  - WikiQA: specify filtered/unfiltered setting; report both if possible.
  - InsuranceQA: report Test1/Test2 with candidate pool sizes; include recent attentive baselines if available.
  - MovieQA: detail context handling (max length, truncation, sliding window) and compare with contemporaneous neural readers on the same split.
- Provide statistical significance checks and multiple runs with mean±std to assess robustness, especially when differences between comparison functions are small.
- Report model size, training time, and memory footprint; element-wise functions may offer efficiency benefits worth quantifying.
- Clarify hyperparameters: CNN filter widths and counts, pooling strategy, dropout rates per layer, early stopping criterion.

Questions for the authors
1. How do you ensure fair capacity across comparison functions, particularly for EUCCOS vs higher-dimensional tj variants? Did you try projecting EUCCOS to l with a learned linear layer?
2. For WikiQA, did you use the standard filtered setting? If so, please report both MAP/MRR for filtered and unfiltered to facilitate comparison.
3. For InsuranceQA, which test sets and candidate pool sizes were used? Please report results on both Test1 and Test2 if applicable.
4. Why freeze GloVe embeddings across all tasks? Did fine-tuning change the relative ranking of comparison functions?
5. For SNLI, what is the exact LSTM preprocessing used (hidden size, directionality)? How much does it contribute relative to the comparison choice?
6. Did you evaluate normalized variants of MULT (cosine-like) and the widely used |u−v| feature? If so, how did they compare?
7. What are the CNN aggregator specifics (filter widths, number of filters, pooling)? Did aggregation choice affect which comparison function is best?
8. For MovieQA, how do you handle long plots (max length, truncation policy)? What is the computational complexity and average memory usage per example?
9. Are the improvements statistically significant across multiple random seeds?

Overall, the paper is clear and the empirical results are encouraging, but stronger controls, clearer protocols, and more rigorous comparisons are needed to convincingly support the central claims.
