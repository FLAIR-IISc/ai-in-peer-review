1) Summary and contributions
The paper studies sequence matching with a simple compare-aggregate architecture. Given two sequences, it computes attention from one to the other, compares aligned token representations using a choice among six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCLIDEAN+COSINE, element-wise SUB, element-wise MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), and aggregates the comparison sequence with a CNN for prediction. A light gating “preprocessing” layer reweights token embeddings; attention is one-directional; and the aggregation uses a single-layer CNN. The main claims are: (a) the compare-aggregate framework is broadly effective across diverse tasks; (b) the choice of comparison function is crucial and element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) are strong; (c) the model achieves state-of-the-art or very competitive results on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI; (d) ablations show the importance of preprocessing and attention. The authors release code.

2) Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical evidence across multiple datasets that simple, element-wise comparison functions are robust and competitive, providing a useful, broadly applicable insight. (2) A clear, simple architecture with ablations and cross-task validation demonstrating the generality of the compare-aggregate pattern.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work targets a widely relevant problem (text sequence matching) and addresses two specific gaps: the lack of a unified, cross-task empirical study of compare-aggregate models, and a systematic evaluation of comparison functions. The paper is well placed in the literature, contrasting with prior task-specific models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention) and noting that element-wise operations have been underexplored as explicit word-level comparison functions within attention-based matching.

- Approach: The model is straightforward and clearly described: gated preprocessing, attention, token-level comparison, CNN aggregation. The focus on the comparison function is well-motivated, and the suite of six functions spans flexible parametric (NN, NTN), similarity/distance (EUCCOS), and element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The MovieQA adaptation (matching Q–P and A–P) is a sensible extension.

- Empirical support: The reported results indicate SOTA or near-SOTA on three QA datasets and competitive performance on SNLI, despite the model’s simplicity and mostly order-insensitive preprocessing (except adding LSTM for SNLI). The ablations (removing attention or preprocessing) provide evidence that both components matter and that their importance varies with sequence length imbalance. The finding that SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN often outperform NN/NTN/EUCCOS is consistent across datasets and is a practically valuable takeaway.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (these points aim to help; they are not all part of my decision)
- Novelty and positioning: Be crisper about what is new relative to Parikh et al. (decomposable attention) and Mou et al. (element-wise ops). Your main contribution appears to be the systematic, cross-task evaluation and the empirical insight about element-wise comparisons; state this explicitly up front and in the conclusion.

- Experimental rigor and fairness:
  - Report exact numbers (means, standard deviations across runs) and statistical significance tests where appropriate.
  - Clarify dataset settings (e.g., InsuranceQA version, candidate pool sizes; WikiQA filtered/unfiltered splits; MovieQA text-only split) and confirm official splits are used.
  - Since you do not re-implement baselines, discuss potential confounds due to different preprocessing or resources. Where possible, include strong contemporary baselines re-implemented in your codebase for apples-to-apples comparisons (e.g., decomposable attention).

- Model details and ablations:
  - Analyze the impact of freezing embeddings vs fine-tuning (and possibly try character/subword or contextual embeddings). Freezing may handicap performance and might interact with the comparison functions.
  - Provide sensitivity to l, number of CNN filters/windows, and CNN vs alternative aggregation (sum/mean, RNN, self-attention). A direct comparison to the decomposable attention aggregator would be insightful.
  - Compare one-directional vs bi-directional attention, which is common in modern pairwise matching.
  - Quantify parameter counts and runtime/memory to support claims of simplicity/efficiency.

- Analysis:
  - Expand interpretability beyond a single visualization: e.g., distribution of attention weights, which dimensions/features are most informative for SUB vs MULT, and case studies where element-wise ops help/fail relative to NN/NTN.
  - Error analysis per dataset (e.g., lexical overlap vs reasoning, length effects) to contextualize where the approach excels.

- Writing/clarity:
  - Tighten notation for preprocessing and ensure equations are consistent and self-contained.
  - Explicitly list the hyperparameter search ranges and early stopping criteria.

Questions for the authors
- Dataset protocols: Which InsuranceQA version and candidate pool size are used? For WikiQA, did you use the standard filtered setting? Are official training/dev/test splits strictly followed for all datasets?
- Baselines: Can you include a re-implementation of Parikh et al.’s decomposable attention under your exact setup to isolate the effect of your comparison functions and aggregation?
- Embeddings: Why were embeddings kept fixed? Does fine-tuning change the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- Aggregation: How sensitive are results to the CNN aggregator? Would a simple sum or a GRU/LSTM aggregator materially change performance or the comparison-function ranking?
- Attention: Did you try bi-directional or multi-head attention? If so, how do results change?
- Robustness: How stable are results across random seeds? Please report mean±std over multiple runs.
- Efficiency: What are parameter counts and training/inference time per dataset? How does O(QA) attention scale on the longest MovieQA plots, and do you need truncation?

Overall, this is a well-executed empirical study with a clear, practical insight about word-level comparison functions in compare-aggregate models. While the methodological novelty is modest, the cross-task evidence and actionable findings make it a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance after addressing the experimental rigor and clarity points.
