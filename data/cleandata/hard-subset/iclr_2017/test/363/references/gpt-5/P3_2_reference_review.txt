Summary and contributions
- The paper studies a general compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching and evaluates it across four tasks/datasets: MovieQA (MC), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (ranking), and SNLI (NLI). The model aligns one sequence to another via attention, compares aligned word representations with a choice of comparison function f, and aggregates the token-level comparisons with a CNN for classification/ranking.
- The main methodological contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions: neural net (NN), neural tensor network (NTN), Euclidean+cosine (EUCCOS), element-wise subtraction (SUB), element-wise multiplication (MULT), and a combined SUBMULT+NN. The paper argues and shows empirically that element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) are strong, often outperforming more expressive neural comparators.
- An additional architectural component is a light-weight, order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” layer before attention, and a one-way attention from the second sequence to the first; aggregation is done with a CNN rather than a simple pooling/sum.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Solid empirical study that isolates and compares comparison functions within a widely-used framework, with consistent gains and competitive-to-SOTA results across diverse tasks. (2) The findings about element-wise operations are simple, general, and practically useful to the community.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The paper is well-motivated. A compare-aggregate pattern underlies many recent pairwise models; however, most works either fix a comparator (often an MLP) or use intuitively motivated but not systematically compared similarity measures. Evaluating a set of plausible f’s across tasks is valuable. The framing relative to Parikh et al. (2016), match-LSTM (Wang & Jiang, 2016), and He & Lin (2016) is appropriate; the focus on the comparison function is a distinct emphasis.
- Method: The architecture is clear: (i) gate-based preprocessing per token (no context), (ii) one-way soft attention to form aligned representations, (iii) token-level comparison, (iv) CNN aggregation. The chosen comparison functions span a reasonable spectrum: flexible (NN/NTN), rigid (EUCCOS), and mid-ground (SUB/MULT).
- Empirical support: Across four datasets, the model is competitive or better than reported baselines; the observation that SUBMULT+NN generally performs best and that even extremely simple SUB/MULT often outperforms NN/NTN is consistent with the intuition that lexicalized alignment plus simple similarity is effective. The ablation indicating the importance of the attention and preprocessing layers adds credibility. Code release is a plus for reproducibility.
- Practical implications: The result that element-wise subtraction and multiplication, without heavy parametrization, are robust across tasks is actionable. The architecture is simple and efficient (no recurrent encoders in three tasks), useful for long sequences (e.g., MovieQA plots).

Concerns and limitations
- Novelty: The overall architecture is close to decomposable attention and related models; the main novelty lies in the systematic comparator study and minor architectural choices (CNN aggregation, gating). This is incremental rather than a fundamentally new modeling contribution.
- Fairness and rigor of comparisons: (i) Embeddings are frozen; several baselines may fine-tune embeddings—this can unfairly penalize/benefit either side. (ii) EUCCOS yields 2-D token comparisons while others produce l-D; capacity is not controlled across f choices (no projection to equalize dimensionality), making it hard to disentangle the effect of the comparator from downstream capacity. (iii) No statistical significance tests are reported.
- Context modeling: The preprocessing is order-insensitive (except for SNLI), and attention is one-way. Given strong evidence that bidirectional/iterative alignment and contextual encoders (e.g., ESIM/BI-attention) help, it would be useful to test whether the comparator conclusions hold when context is better modeled.
- Experimental detail: Some task-specific choices are under-specified (e.g., the exact SNLI classification head; details of negative sampling or filtering in WikiQA; whether standard evaluation protocols—e.g., filtering questions with no correct answers—are followed). Training details (early stopping, dropout values, dev splits) are sparse.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Control capacity: Add a linear layer to map all token-level comparison outputs to a common dimension before CNN aggregation so comparisons between EUCCOS and others are capacity-matched.
- Stronger baselines: Include a bi-directional attention/decomposable attention variant with CNN or pooling aggregation; compare to ESIM-like settings to show that the comparator findings persist when context is modeled.
- Significance and robustness: Report confidence intervals or significance tests; add robustness checks (varying embedding fine-tuning on/off; different l; different CNN kernel sizes).
- Analysis: Provide error analyses per phenomenon (e.g., lexical overlap vs paraphrase on SNLI; length sensitivity on MovieQA). Include runtime/memory comparisons to highlight the efficiency benefits of simple comparators.
- Clarify implementation: Specify the normalization axis of the attention softmax; detail the SNLI classification head; list dev/test splits and any data filtering; report tuned hyperparameters per dataset and early stopping.

Questions for the authors
- Capacity control: How do you ensure fair comparison between EUCCOS (2-D per token) and higher-dimensional comparators? Did you try projecting all token-level comparisons to a shared dimensionality before CNN?
- Attention directionality: Did you try bi-directional or iterative attention? If so, do the relative rankings of comparators hold?
- Context encoders: Beyond SNLI, what happens if you replace the order-insensitive preprocessing with a light BiLSTM/GRU? Do SUB/MULT still dominate?
- Embedding fine-tuning: Did you try updating GloVe? How do results change and do conclusions about comparators persist?
- WikiQA protocol: Did you follow the standard filtered evaluation (removing questions with no correct answers)? Please provide MAP/MRR per the standard and confirm splits.
- Statistical tests: Are differences (e.g., SUBMULT+NN vs NN/NTN) statistically significant across multiple runs?
- Efficiency: Can you report training/inference time and memory usage per setting, particularly for long MovieQA plots?

Overall, this is a careful, useful empirical study with broadly applicable takeaways. While the architectural novelty is limited, the clarity and consistency of the findings on simple element-wise comparison functions justify publication.
