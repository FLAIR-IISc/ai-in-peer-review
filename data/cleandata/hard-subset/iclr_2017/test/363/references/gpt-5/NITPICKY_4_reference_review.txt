Summary
The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” architecture for text sequence matching. The pipeline: (i) order-insensitive gated preprocessing of word embeddings for both sequences; (ii) one-directional attention to align each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted summary of the other; (iii) word-level comparison via one of six functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN); and (iv) aggregation with a one-layer CNN for final prediction. The authors argue the framework is broadly applicable and, through experiments on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI, claim competitive or state-of-the-art performance. A central empirical finding is that simple element-wise comparisons (SUB, MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN) often outperform more expressive neural comparison functions, suggesting a strong, simple bias toward similarity-focused matching is beneficial.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty relative to existing compare-aggregate work; the primary claimed contribution—evaluating element-wise comparison functions—has prior art and is not sufficiently distinguished or rigorously isolated.
2) Insufficient methodological clarity and experimental rigor (task-specific deviations, unclear losses, potential unfairness in comparisons, and numerous missing/ambiguous details), undermining confidence in the claims.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: The compare-aggregate paradigm is well-established (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016; Wang & Jiang 2016; He & Lin 2016). Element-wise features (difference and product) have been used before for sentence matching/NLI (e.g., Tai et al. 2015; Mou et al. 2016). The paper’s main claim—to systematically compare six comparison functions—would be valuable if carefully controlled, but current deviations across tasks and model variants blur attribution of gains to the comparison functions.
- Inconsistent model across tasks: For SNLI the preprocessing becomes LSTM-based (order-sensitive), while other tasks use an order-insensitive gating. For MovieQA, a tri-partite matching is used. These task-specific changes confound the interpretation of which component (comparison vs. preprocessing/aggregation/design) drives improvements.
- Aggregation ambiguity: The one-layer CNN is underspecified (filter widths, number of filters n, pooling strategy). The paper states r ∈ R^{n l} regardless of the comparison function, but EUCCOS produces 2-dim t_j vectors; the dimensional inconsistency is not addressed (is there a projection to l?).
- Training objectives and candidate sets: For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, it is unclear how learning handles multiple correct answers and variable K. Eqn. 10 uses a softmax over K answers, but WikiQA has multiple positives; softmax is ill-suited unless the data are pre-filtered to single positives. No clarity on whether pairwise/listwise losses were tried, whether candidate pools were the full set or sampled, and whether settings match those of baselines. This threatens fairness of comparisons.
- Attention details: The softmax axis and normalization in G = softmax((WgQ + bg ⊗ eQ)^T A) are not explicitly specified. It is said to attend over Q for each A token, but the exact normalization and implementation matter for reproducibility.
- Preprocessing layer: The gating resembles a Highway layer but is not cited as such; sharing vs. not sharing parameters between Q and A is unclear. The operator for element-wise multiplication is missing in Eqn. (1) (“where is element-wise multiplication”).
- Hyperparameters and regularization: Embeddings are frozen; no L2 regularization is used; dropout is tuned only on embeddings. Given heavy models (e.g., NTN), this is unusual. No report of parameter counts, training stability, or runtime. OOV initialized to zero is a poor default and may bias comparisons vs. baselines that fine-tune or use subword features.
- Experimental reporting: Tables/Figures with numbers are referenced but not included here, preventing verification. The authors did not reimplement baselines; reported numbers may reflect differing data splits or candidate pool sizes (notably for InsuranceQA). For WikiQA, MAP/MRR typically exclude questions with no correct answers; the paper does not specify.
- Claims vs. analysis: The ablation referencing the importance of attention vs. preprocessing is asserted but quantitative details are missing. The “general effectiveness” claim is weakened by task-specific changes and insufficiently controlled comparisons.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clearly define all tensor shapes and operations. Specify the softmax axis in attention, CNN filter widths, number of filters, activation, and pooling. Unify notation (avoid reusing Q for both pre/post-processed matrices).
- Normalize capacity across comparison functions. If EUCCOS yields 2D features while others yield l, add a projection so downstream capacity is comparable; otherwise results may be confounded by feature dimensionality.
- Use consistent architectures across tasks for the comparison-function study; if task-specific modifications are necessary, report parallel results with the same backbone to isolate the effect of f.
- Clarify training losses for QA ranking with multiple positives. Consider pairwise or listwise objectives and report both classification and ranking setups for fair comparison to prior work.
- Improve baselines and fairness: re-implement strong baselines under the same data splits and candidate pools; report hyperparameter tuning ranges; include statistical significance.
- Consider fine-tuning embeddings or adding subword features; report sensitivity analyses (e.g., effect of freezing embeddings).
- Cite and relate to Highway Networks for the gating layer; discuss why order-insensitive preprocessing is desirable and when it fails.
- Fix numerous typographical and formatting issues in references and text (e.g., “Siamense”, missing years, escaped characters, incomplete citations).

Questions for the authors
1) How exactly is the softmax applied in the attention matrix G (row-wise over Q for each A column)? Please provide the precise normalization and any masking used.
2) What are the CNN details: filter widths, number of filters n, activation, pooling (max/avg), and padding? How do you reconcile the dimensionality when tj is 2D (EUCCOS) vs. l (others)?
3) For SNLI, how is LSTM integrated into Eqn. (1)? Is the rest of the pipeline identical? Are parameters shared between Q and A in preprocessing/attention?
4) For InsuranceQA and WikiQA: What are K at training and test time? Do you use the full candidate pool or sampled candidates? What loss is used when multiple answers are correct (softmax seems incompatible)?
5) Did you ensure identical preprocessing, data splits, and candidate pools as the reported baselines, especially for InsuranceQA (test1/test2, pool sizes) and WikiQA (filtering questions with no positives)?
6) Are embeddings shared across sequences and frozen throughout? Did you try fine-tuning or character/subword features, and how does that affect the ranking of comparison functions?
7) How sensitive are the results to l, dropout, and learning rate? Do SUB/MULT remain superior across a broad hyperparameter sweep?
8) Can you provide parameter counts for each comparison function (especially NTN) and training time/memory, to support the “good compromise” efficiency claim?
