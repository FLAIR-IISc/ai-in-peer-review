1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences and evaluates it across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The model consists of: (i) a lightweight gating-based preprocessing layer, (ii) one-directional attention to align each token in one sequence to an attention-weighted representation of the other, (iii) a word-level comparison function f(aj, hj), and (iv) a CNN-based aggregation. The central contribution is a systematic comparison of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The empirical finding is that element-wise subtraction and multiplication, especially their combination (SUBMULT+NN), are consistently strong and often superior to standard MLP or tensor comparisons, yielding competitive or state-of-the-art performance on the evaluated datasets. The paper further provides ablations suggesting the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers. Code is released.

2) Decision: Reject (main reasons)
- Limited novelty beyond prior compare-aggregate and decomposable attention frameworks; the main technical difference is a systematic evaluation of comparison functions that have been explored in related work (e.g., element-wise difference/product) and incremental architectural choices (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation).
- Empirical support, while suggestive, lacks sufficient rigor and detail for an ICLR-level contribution: incomplete reporting of hyperparameter/search protocols, parameter counts, significance testing, and careful comparisons against closely related baselines under matched settings.

3) Supporting arguments
What is the specific problem tackled? 
- The paper addresses generic sequence matching (e.g., NLI, answer selection, machine comprehension) and asks which word-level comparison functions are most effective within a compare-aggregate framework.

Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- Motivation is clear: sequence-level single-vector matching underperforms; compare-aggregate models that align and compare local units work better. The paper positions itself with respect to Rocktäschel et al., Parikh et al., He & Lin, and Wang & Jiang. However, the architectural core closely resembles decomposable attention and other compare-aggregate models; the novelty mainly lies in evaluating element-wise SUB/MULT versus NN/NTN/EUCCOS and using a CNN for aggregation. Since element-wise diff/product have been used before for sentence pair tasks (e.g., Tai et al., Mou et al.), the incremental novelty is moderate.

Does the paper support the claims rigorously?
- Positives: multi-dataset evaluation, consistent gains for SUBMULT+NN, ablations indicating contributions from preprocessing and attention, and code availability.
- Concerns:
  - Fairness and completeness of baselines: A direct, controlled comparison with the decomposable attention model and match-LSTM under identical preprocessing, embeddings (frozen vs trainable), and hyperparameter search is missing. Some baselines are reported from the literature rather than re-run, which risks mismatches in data splits, candidate pool sizes (notably InsuranceQA), or preprocessing.
  - Reporting: Key details are under-specified (CNN kernel widths/strides, number of filters, dropout per layer, sequence truncation, tokenization, max lengths, batch sizes per task except one, early stopping criteria). Parameter counts and training time are not provided.
  - Statistical rigor: No confidence intervals or significance tests are reported. Given small absolute differences among comparison functions, it is hard to judge robustness.
  - Context encoding: For three datasets the preprocessing is order-insensitive, yet the paper claims strong performance and draws conclusions about the compare step’s primacy; this is interesting but would be stronger with matched comparisons to order-sensitive encoders across all tasks (BiLSTM/GRU vs gating).
  - Analysis depth: The ablation statements are qualitative; quantitative ablation numbers (and per-dataset deltas) are not included in the text provided. The claim that SUB/MULT approximate cosine/Euclidean but preserve dimension-wise information is sensible; further probing (e.g., normalization effects, scaling sensitivity, contribution of each term in SUBMULT+NN) would bolster the conclusion.

4) Additional feedback and questions to help improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Novelty/positioning:
  - Clarify the conceptual differences from decomposable attention: both align, compare, and aggregate. What does one-directional attention and CNN aggregation buy in accuracy, compute, or parameter efficiency? Add head-to-head comparisons with matched settings and parameter budgets.
  - Discuss connections to heuristic matching features (u, v, |u−v|, u⊙v) widely used in NLI; situate SUB/MULT within that lineage and explain when they should outperform NN/NTN theoretically.

- Experimental rigor:
  - Provide parameter counts and FLOPs, plus training/inference time. Are SUB/MULT advantageous in efficiency?
  - Report variance across runs and statistical significance.
  - Detail hyperparameter search spaces and selection criteria for each dataset; include CNN kernel sizes, number of filters, stride, pooling strategy; dropout rates per layer; max sequence lengths; and preprocessing specifics (lowercasing, OOV handling).
  - Clarify InsuranceQA setup (candidate pool size at test time, sample construction) and MovieQA setting (which split, exact use of plot synopses, and whether competing methods use the same textual input only).
  - Indicate whether word embeddings are frozen for all tasks; provide a comparison where embeddings are fine-tuned to test robustness of conclusions about comparison functions.

- Ablations and alternatives:
  - Quantify the contribution of each layer (preprocessing gate, attention, aggregation choice) per dataset.
  - Compare aggregation via CNN vs BiLSTM vs simple pooling under identical comparison functions.
  - Evaluate two-directional attention and/or bilinear attention to see if it interacts with SUB/MULT benefits.
  - Test normalization variants: scale-invariant SUB/MULT (e.g., layernorm or cosine-normalized inputs) and learned weighting between the SUB and MULT branches.

- Analysis:
  - Provide error analyses per dataset (e.g., lexical overlap, paraphrase, negation, long-distance dependencies) to understand where element-wise comparison helps or fails.
  - Examine length sensitivity: how does performance vary with sequence length mismatch; support claims in Section 3.3 with stratified results.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact CNN configurations (filter widths, number of filters, pooling) and do results change materially with different kernel sizes?
- For InsuranceQA, what is the test-time candidate pool size and construction? Are your results directly comparable to the cited baselines under the same pool size and split?
- Did you run multiple seeds and observe consistent ranking of comparison functions? Please report mean and standard deviation.
- How does the model perform when embeddings are fine-tuned? Does the relative advantage of SUB/MULT persist?
- Why use one-directional attention only? How do results change with symmetric (bi-directional) attention?
- For SNLI, you incorporated LSTM in preprocessing; why not apply order-sensitive preprocessing to the other tasks to test the claim about the primacy of compare over encode?
- Can you provide a controlled comparison to Parikh et al. (2016) with matched preprocessing, embedding settings, and capacity?
- Have you tried a simple feature concatenation [aj, hj, |aj−hj|, aj⊙hj] followed by a small MLP (a common NLI baseline)? How does it compare to SUBMULT+NN?

Overall, this is a clear and useful empirical study. With stronger positioning, tighter experimental controls, and deeper analysis, it could make a solid contribution.
