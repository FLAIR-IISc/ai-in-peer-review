- Paper studies a compare-aggregate architecture for sequence matching, systematically evaluating six token-level comparison functions.  
- Architecture: attention-align tokens, compare each token with attention-weighted counterpart via f, aggregate comparisons with a CNN, predict labels.  
- Main contributions: systematic comparison-function study and cross-task demonstration of a simple, general architecture with released code.  
- Empirical finding: element-wise comparisons, especially SUBMULT+NN, generally outperform NN/NTN and EUCCOS across datasets.  
- Evaluated on four diverse tasks (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, MovieQA) showing strong or state-of-the-art results.  
- Paper provides ablations showing attention and preprocessing layers matter, especially for long or unequal-length contexts.  
- Practical advantage: efficient design avoiding expensive encoders for long contexts and relying on simple matrix ops and a shallow CNN.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: solid empirical evidence clarifying the impact of comparison functions and offering a generalizable design insight.  
- Key reason 2: simple, efficient architecture competitive with more complex models, supported by ablations.  
- Novelty concern: architecture largely incremental and similar to prior decomposable attention/interaction models; main novelty is systematic evaluation of f.  
- Missing experimental details: CNN hyperparameters, training specifics, early stopping, seeds, and variance are underspecified.  
- Statistical rigor concern: no report of variance across runs or significance testing for comparisons.  
- Evaluation protocol gaps: unclear candidate pool sizes and negative sampling for InsuranceQA/WikiQA, hindering baseline comparability.  
- Contextualization concern: preprocessing is order-insensitive and lacks contextual encoders, raising questions about semantic alignment vs shallow matching.  
- Aggregation choice concern: no ablation comparing CNN to sum/mean, attentive pooling, or BiLSTM to isolate the effect of f.  
- Baselines: insufficient comparison/discussion against contemporaneous models like ESIM/Decomp-Attention under matched settings and MovieQA setup differences.  
- Suggested implementation fixes: report full hyperparameters, dropout, optimization, runtimes, parameter counts, and memory footprints.  
- Suggested robustness analyses: multiple seeds with mean±std, significance tests, learning curves, and embedding sensitivity.  
- Suggested additional ablations: aggregator types, bi-directional attention, and lightweight contextual encoders before attention.  
- Fairness/protocol suggestions: clarify candidate set sizes, preprocessing, and ensure baselines use identical splits and filtering.  
- Analysis suggestions: provide richer qualitative/error analysis, phenomena benefiting from SUB vs MULT, and failure modes.  
- Questions for authors requested: exact CNN hyperparameters and comparisons to other pooling; number of runs and variance; candidate pool sizes and protocol parity; bi-directional attention results; computational cost and parameter counts per f; effect of adding LSTM preprocessing; impact of fine-tuning embeddings.
