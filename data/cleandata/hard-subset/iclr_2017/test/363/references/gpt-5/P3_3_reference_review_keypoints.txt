- Paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture and systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions, finding element-wise SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) perform best across tasks.

- Decision: Weak Accept — strong empirical, replicable findings despite incremental novelty.

- Positioning: Well-situated in attention/compare-aggregate literature; identifies a real gap in evaluating local comparison functions across tasks.

- Hypothesis: Element-wise operations are a useful middle ground between heavy NN/NTN and overly compact scalar similarities; supported by experiments.

- Architecture: Clear pipeline — gated preprocessing, one-directional bilinear attention, token-wise comparison, CNN aggregation; sensible MovieQA extension.

- Empirical support: Strong results on three QA datasets and competitive SNLI performance with consistent advantages for SUB/MULT variants.

- Ablations: Preprocessing and attention improve performance; attention helps more when sequences have mismatched lengths.

- Practical significance: Simple, parameter-light comparison functions are effective and make strong, actionable baselines.

- Novelty concern: Framework is close to prior work (e.g., decomposable attention); primary contribution is systematic comparison and cross-task evaluation.

- Statistical rigor concern: No confidence intervals, multiple runs, or significance tests reported; variance estimates are missing.

- Capacity-control concern: Comparisons between NN/NTN and SUB/MULT may be confounded by parameterization/regularization differences.

- Aggregation concern: CNN aggregator not ablated against sum/mean pooling or RNNs, so its contribution is unclear.

- Attention-direction concern: Only one-directional attention evaluated; bi-directional alignment effects not tested.

- Listwise scoring concern: Final listwise scoring for InsuranceQA/MovieQA may advantage the model over pointwise baselines and needs analysis/ablation.

- Preprocessing asymmetry concern: Order-insensitive gating and use of LSTM on SNLI create inconsistencies across tasks, complicating unified claims.

- Improvement: Report mean±std over multiple seeds and perform statistical significance tests for key comparisons.

- Improvement: Provide parameter counts and capacity-matched baselines (e.g., skinny MLPs) to isolate functional effects.

- Improvement: Ablate CNN aggregator vs sum/mean pooling and lightweight RNNs to quantify aggregation impact.

- Improvement: Evaluate bi-directional attention and verify whether comparison-function rankings persist.

- Improvement: Ablate listwise vs independent per-candidate scoring to measure its contribution on QA tasks.

- Improvement: Expand implementation details (CNN kernel sizes/filters, stride, padding, dropout locations, early stopping) for reproducibility.

- Improvement: Try fine-tuning embeddings and learned OOV vectors and report their effects.

- Improvement: Provide runtime and memory costs for long inputs (MovieQA) and any truncation/splitting heuristics used.

- Improvement: Add qualitative error analysis and per-length/overlap breakdowns to show where SUB/MULT help most.

- Improvement: Relate the gating layer to highway networks/GLU to connect with broader literature.

- Questions for authors — 1: Do SUB/MULT still outperform with simple pooling or an RNN aggregator?  
- Question 2: What is the variance across runs; please report mean±std over ≥3 seeds and significance?  
- Question 3: Can you provide capacity-matched comparisons (small MLPs) to disentangle parameterization from functional form?  
- Question 4: How much does listwise candidate scoring contribute vs independent scoring — please ablate?  
- Question 5: Why use one-directional attention only; does bi-directional attention change results or rankings?  
- Question 6: How are very long MovieQA plots handled (truncation/splitting) and what are the computational costs?  
- Question 7: For SNLI you use an LSTM in preprocessing — do main findings hold without the LSTM?  
- Question 8: Did you try fine-tuning embeddings or learned OOV vectors, and what was the impact?
