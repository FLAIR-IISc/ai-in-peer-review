- Paper studies a general "compare-aggregate" framework for text matching with gating, unidirectional attention, per-pair comparison functions, and CNN aggregation.  
- Main contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) across QA and NLI datasets.  
- Reports strong/competitive results and finds element-wise operations (especially SUBMULT+NN) perform best on average; code released.  
- Decision: Reject (borderline).  
- Primary critique: Limited novelty—architecture combines well-known components and the main novelty (comparison-function study) is incremental for ICLR.  
- Primary critique: Incomplete experimental rigor—capacity differences, lack of variance reporting, and unclear baseline fairness undermine empirical claims.  
- Positioning: Prior work already uses element-wise ops and compare-aggregate ideas; architectural changes (unidirectional attention, CNN, gating) are incremental versus strong baselines.  
- Claims vs evidence: Different comparison functions produce outputs of different dimensionality/capacity, so gains may reflect capacity/regularization differences rather than function form.  
- Statistical rigor: Results are single-run numbers with no standard deviations or significance testing; sensitivity to random seeds is unreported.  
- Baseline fairness: Baselines taken from literature without re-implementation or matched training/embedding/tuning, raising questions about SOTA claims.  
- Architecture analysis: No strong justification or empirical comparison for unidirectional attention and CNN aggregation versus alternatives (bidirectional attention, RNNs, pooling).  
- Order sensitivity: Paper notes order-insensitive preprocessing still outperforms order-sensitive baselines but lacks diagnostics or experiments to explain this.  
- Efficiency/scalability: Attention cost and memory/time for long inputs (e.g., MovieQA plots) are not discussed.  
- Suggested fixes: Control capacity by projecting each f output to equal size and report parameter counts/FLOPs.  
- Suggested fixes: Report results averaged over multiple seeds with standard deviations and statistical tests.  
- Suggested fixes: Re-implement at least one strong baseline with the same preprocessing and embeddings to ensure fair comparison.  
- Suggested fixes: Ablate aggregation choices (CNN vs. sum/mean vs. BiLSTM/Transformer) and test bidirectional attention.  
- Suggested fixes: Evaluate order-aware preprocessing across tasks and provide richer error analysis by phenomenon.  
- Suggested fixes: Report computational complexity and wall-clock training/inference times for long inputs.  
- Key questions for authors: Do SUB/MULT still outperform when outputs are projected to equal size? What are standard deviations across seeds and are improvements significant?  
- Key questions for authors: Were baselines re-run with the same candidate pools and preprocessing? Did you test two-way attention, BiLSTM/self-attention preprocessing, or compare aggregation modules?  
- Key questions for authors: What are memory/time costs for long MovieQA inputs, and what failure modes (negation, numbers, paraphrase) does the model exhibit?
