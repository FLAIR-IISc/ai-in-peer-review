Summary of contributions
- Problem: Matching two text sequences is central to QA and NLI. The paper argues that “compare-aggregate” architectures are broadly effective and that the choice of word-level comparison function is crucial.
- Approach: A simple pipeline: (i) order-insensitive gated preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) unidirectional attention from one sequence to the other, (iii) word-level comparison, (iv) CNN aggregation. The key focus is a systematic study of six comparison functions: NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction (SUB), element-wise multiplication (MULT), and a combination (SUBMULT+NN).
- Claims: (a) The framework is broadly applicable across diverse tasks; (b) element-wise comparison functions (especially SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) are strong or best; (c) the model achieves competitive or state-of-the-art performance on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and competitive results on SNLI; (d) attention and preprocessing gates matter, with differing importance depending on length asymmetry.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Limited novelty over established compare-aggregate models (particularly Parikh et al. 2016) with only modest architectural variations; (2) insufficient experimental rigor to support broad claims of superiority and generality (no re-implemented, controlled baselines; no significance/variance; task-specific deviations undermine the “single general model” claim).

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning
  - The overall architecture closely mirrors prior decomposable attention/compare-aggregate work: token-level alignment via attention, per-token comparison, aggregation. The differences (a CNN aggregator vs summation/FFN, unidirectional attention, a simple gating preprocessor) are incremental.
  - The central claim—element-wise SUB/MULT work well—is valuable but not novel in spirit; such heuristics have been used in pair modeling and cited (e.g., Mou et al., 2016). The paper does not introduce new comparison operators beyond a straightforward concatenation and MLP.
  - The attention directionality choice and order-insensitive preprocessing are not sufficiently motivated theoretically, nor contrasted empirically with readily available variants (e.g., bi-directional attention, contextual encoders).

- Empirical support and rigor
  - Baselines are taken from reported numbers rather than re-implemented under a shared setup, making fairness uncertain (different preprocessing, embeddings, hyperparameter budgets, training data splits, and stopping criteria can materially change outcomes).
  - The method varies across tasks (e.g., adding LSTM contextualization only for SNLI; special triplet matching scheme for MovieQA). This weakens the claim of a single general architecture.
  - No statistical significance tests or variance over seeds are reported. Given that several gains appear modest in prior literature, the lack of variance undermines confidence in claimed improvements.
  - The choice to freeze GloVe embeddings and the absence of contextual encoders for three tasks is interesting, but the paper does not explore whether the core conclusions (especially the advantage of SUB/MULT) hold once competitive contextual encoders (BiLSTM/CNN) or updated embeddings are used.
  - The paper asserts state-of-the-art on several datasets but does not ensure contemporary, strong baselines (e.g., more recent attentive and interaction-based models) nor provide apples-to-apples comparisons.
  - Ablations are limited: we see removal of preprocessing/attention, but not a systematic study of aggregator choices (CNN vs pooling vs RNN), attention directionality, or parameter counts’ impact (important for comparing NTN vs light operators).

- Clarity and completeness
  - Some mathematical definitions are terse (e.g., tensor shapes for NTN, exact CNN configuration, window sizes, number of filters).
  - The rationale for the gating preprocessor (vs simple linear projection or layer norm) is not clearly motivated, and its behavior is not analyzed beyond a high-level statement.
  - No error analysis or qualitative analysis beyond a brief visualization, and no discussion of failure modes or task-dependent behaviors.

Strengths
- Clear articulation of the compare-aggregate pipeline and a clean, simple implementation.
- Cross-task evaluation is useful, and the emphasis on the comparison function is well motivated for this family of models.
- The empirical finding that element-wise operators are strong baselines is practical and likely to inform future work. Code release is appreciated.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen the empirical rigor: re-implement strong baselines under a unified setup; report standard deviations across multiple runs; conduct significance testing; include parameter counts and training time to contextualize comparisons (especially against NTN/NN).
- Expand ablations: aggregator variants (sum/mean pooling, BiLSTM), attention variants (bi-directional, multi-head), contextual encoders (BiLSTM/Transformer) while holding comparison functions fixed to test the generality of the SUB/MULT insight.
- Provide controlled experiments where only the comparison function changes, across all tasks, with the rest fixed. Consider a shared backbone across tasks to bolster the “general model” claim.
- Analyze sensitivity to embedding training (frozen vs fine-tuned), dimension, and vocabulary coverage, since SUB/MULT can be sensitive to embedding quality.
- Offer error analysis: where do SUB/MULT help or hurt (e.g., lexical overlap vs compositional reasoning, negation, paraphrase)?
- Clarify details: exact CNN configs, attention scoring function, normalization, and implementation choices that impact reproducibility. Clean up references/typos.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the conclusions to random seeds? Please report mean±std over at least 5 runs and significance tests for the main comparisons.
- Do your findings about SUB/MULT persist when using contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM, CNN, or Transformer) and/or when fine-tuning embeddings?
- How does bi-directional attention (and/or multi-head attention) affect results and the relative ranking of comparison functions?
- Can you provide parameter counts and training time for each comparison function, and discuss whether NTN underperforms due to overfitting or optimization challenges?
- For MovieQA, how much of the gain comes from the triplet-matching formulation vs the comparison function itself? Can you isolate this with a controlled ablation?
- Did you evaluate alternate aggregators (e.g., sum/mean pooling, max pooling, BiLSTM) to test whether CNN is critical?
- Given task-specific architectural deviations (e.g., adding LSTM only for SNLI), what is the true “core” model you recommend practitioners use across tasks?
- Can you provide an error analysis highlighting categories where element-wise comparisons outperform NN/NTN and where they fail?
