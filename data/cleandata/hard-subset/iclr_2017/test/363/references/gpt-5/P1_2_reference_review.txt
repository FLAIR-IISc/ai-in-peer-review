1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates a simple and general “compare-aggregate” architecture for matching two text sequences, with a particular focus on the choice of the word-level comparison function. The model consists of: (i) an order-insensitive gating-based preprocessing layer, (ii) a one-directional attention layer that aligns one sequence to the other, (iii) a word-level comparison function applied to each aligned pair, and (iv) a CNN-based aggregation over the sequence of comparison vectors for final prediction. The key contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCLIDEAN+COSINE, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), including two simple element-wise functions (SUB and MULT) that are not widely used for word-level matching. Experiments on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) show competitive or state-of-the-art performance on three (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and strong results on SNLI. Ablations indicate both the preprocessing and attention components contribute, with their relative importance depending on sequence length differences. The authors also provide qualitative visualizations and release code.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical results across diverse tasks with a single, simple architecture, and a careful, isolated study of comparison functions.
- Clear, actionable insight that element-wise comparison (SUB/MULT and SUBMULT+NN) is a robust, high-performing choice, bridging the gap between rigid similarity measures and over-parameterized neural comparators.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The work is well-situated amid prior “compare-aggregate” models (e.g., match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction). The paper identifies an under-explored but central component—the comparison function—and justifies why similarity-aware operations could be preferable to generic neural layers. The hypothesis that element-wise operations preserve dimension-wise information while reflecting distance/similarity is compelling and borne out empirically.
- Breadth and generality: Applying one architecture to four different tasks (answer selection, passage-based MC, and NLI) strengthens the claim of generality. The results reportedly surpass prior work on three datasets and are competitive on SNLI, indicating the framework’s versatility.
- Experimental design: The comparison functions are tested within the same backbone, allowing controlled comparisons. The ablation study (removing preprocessing or attention) provides useful diagnostic insight about when each component matters (e.g., attention helps more for length-mismatched sequences).
- Simplicity and efficiency: The model avoids expensive encoders for long sequences (order-insensitive preprocessing and one-directional attention) yet remains highly competitive. This simplicity is a practical contribution.
- Reproducibility: Code release enhances impact.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect the accept decision)
- Fair capacity comparison: EUCLIDEAN+COSINE yields only a 2D vector while others produce l-dimensional representations. Consider passing EUCCOS through a learned projection to l dimensions to better control for capacity, or report an additional variant to ensure fairness.
- Statistical rigor: Report variance across multiple random seeds and statistical significance tests on key benchmarks. Include validation curves and sensitivity analyses (e.g., l, number of CNN filters/windows, dropout, learning rate).
- Training details: Provide more hyperparameters (CNN kernel sizes, number of filters, pooling strategy, parameter counts) and training/runtime/memory comparisons across comparison functions.
- Embeddings: You freeze GloVe embeddings; discuss how fine-tuning affects results and whether the relative ranking of comparison functions changes.
- Attention directionality: The model uses one-directional attention. Evaluate a bi-directional/co-attention variant to ascertain whether the main conclusions about comparison functions hold and whether performance improves further.
- Preprocessing layer: The gate resembles a position-wise highway/GLU-like transform but is order-insensitive. Consider reporting the effect of adding lightweight contextualization (e.g., convolutional or shallow BiLSTM) on long-sequence tasks, and whether that changes the relative importance of the comparison layer.
- Baselines and contemporaries: Ensure the comparison set includes strong “compare-aggregate” models (e.g., Decomposable Attention) on all applicable datasets with comparable preprocessing and embeddings for fairness. Clarify dataset splits and whether you use the standard public splits.
- Error analysis: Add qualitative failure cases and per-phenomenon breakdowns (e.g., lexical overlap vs paraphrase, negation, multi-sentence reasoning) to better understand where element-wise comparison helps or struggles.
- Efficiency: Provide time/throughput benchmarks on long sequences (MovieQA plots) to substantiate the efficiency appeal versus heavy encoders.

5. Questions for the authors
- Capacity control: Since EUCCOS yields 2D outputs, did you try augmenting it (e.g., project to l dims) to disentangle the effect of representation size from the choice of measure?
- Robustness: How stable are the rankings of comparison functions across random seeds and hyperparameter choices? Please report mean and std over multiple runs.
- Embeddings: What happens if you fine-tune word embeddings? Do SUB/MULT remain superior? How sensitive are results to OOV handling?
- Attention: Did you try bi-directional attention (aligning Q↔A) or multi-head variants? Any performance gains or changes in the comparison-function ranking?
- Aggregation: If you replace the CNN aggregator with an LSTM or simple pooling, do SUB/MULT still dominate? How much does the CNN contribute relative to the comparison choice?
- MovieQA specifics: How do you handle very long plots—any truncation or sliding windows? What is the computational cost and memory footprint? Does attention become diffuse on long inputs, and if so, how does the model cope?
- Preprocessing layer: For SNLI, you incorporate LSTM in preprocessing. How critical is that change to your SNLI performance? Could a unified preprocessing (with or without order sensitivity) be used across all tasks?
- Interpretability: For SUB/MULT, do certain embedding dimensions consistently dominate? Any insights from visualizations that differentiate SUB vs MULT behaviors?

Overall, this paper offers a well-motivated and practically useful study with strong empirical evidence across tasks. The central insight about simple element-wise comparison is valuable to the community, and the work is likely to influence future sequence matching designs.
