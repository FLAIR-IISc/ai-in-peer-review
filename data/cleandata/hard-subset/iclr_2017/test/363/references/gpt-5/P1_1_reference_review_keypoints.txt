- Problem: Matching two text sequences is central to tasks like entailment and QA; prior encode-then-compare methods overly compress sequences or use generic comparisons.  
- Approach: Proposes a compare-aggregate pipeline with gating preprocessing, unidirectional attention, word-level comparisons f(aj,hj), and CNN aggregation; MovieQA matches Q–P and A–P jointly.  
- Core contribution: Systematic evaluation of six word-level comparison functions, finding element-wise SUB/MULT (and their combo) outperform flexible neural layers and classical similarities.  
- Empirical results: State-of-the-art or competitive on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI, with notable gains on InsuranceQA and WikiQA.  
- Ablations: Preprocessing gates and attention both help, with attention more critical for long or length-mismatched pairs.  
- Decision: Weak Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Strong, consistent empirical evidence across four diverse benchmarks supporting a simple, general architecture and actionable findings about comparison functions.  
- Key reason 2: Released code and well-scoped ablations increase practical value despite modest novelty.  
- Motivation/positioning: Fills a gap by systematically analyzing the comparison function within compare-aggregate models.  
- Method soundness: Architecture is straightforward and appropriate (gating, unidirectional attention, CNN aggregator).  
- Empirical support detail: SUBMULT+NN often best; SUB/MULT beat NN/NTN; EUCCOS is strong in some settings; attention importance varies with length mismatch.  
- Limitation 1: Novelty is mainly empirical; overall framework similar to existing compare-aggregate models.  
- Limitation 2: Some implementation details (CNN hyperparams, LSTM variant) are underspecified, harming reproducibility.  
- Limitation 3: Fairness concern in comparison-function study since parameter counts differ between methods.  
- Limitation 4: No statistical significance testing and some baselines taken from literature without reimplementation/tuning.  
- Limitation 5: Fixing word embeddings may limit performance; impact not fully examined.  
- Feedback 1: Clarify CNN aggregation hyperparameters and exact LSTM preprocessing setup for SNLI.  
- Feedback 2: Control for capacity in comparison-function study and report parameter counts or match capacities.  
- Feedback 3: Ablate aggregation choices (CNN vs sum/avg pooling or BiLSTM) to isolate sources of gain.  
- Feedback 4: Report results with fine-tuned embeddings and character/subword features, especially for WikiQA and InsuranceQA.  
- Feedback 5: Evaluate bidirectional attention to test the unidirectional choice.  
- Feedback 6: Discuss computational/memory costs and truncation strategies for long MovieQA plots.  
- Feedback 7: Add standard deviations/multiple-run stats and significance tests for key comparisons.  
- Feedback 8: Fix typos and improve reference formatting/coverage.  
- Questions for authors: Clarify SNLI preprocessing (LSTM details), CNN filter widths/numbers, alternative aggregators tried, parameter-fairness controls, embedding tuning effects, bidirectional attention experiments, MovieQA truncation/lengths, and result variance across seeds.
