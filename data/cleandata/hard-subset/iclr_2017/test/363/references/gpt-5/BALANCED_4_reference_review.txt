1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies text sequence matching under a compare-aggregate paradigm. It proposes a general architecture that (i) applies a lightweight gating “preprocessing” over word embeddings, (ii) aligns one sequence to another via attention, (iii) compares aligned word pairs using a family of comparison functions, and (iv) aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN for final prediction. The main contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with the finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, or their combination) generally outperform more flexible neural comparison modules. The approach is evaluated across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving strong results and, in several cases, improving over prior methods. The paper includes ablations on attention and preprocessing and provides qualitative visualizations.

2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons: (1) Limited novelty beyond contemporaneous compare-aggregate models; the main technical addition (element-wise SUB/MULT comparisons) is incremental and has prior usage. (2) The empirical study, while broad, does not adequately control for capacity/architecture differences across comparison functions and tasks, weakening the strength of the central claim.

3. Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement in the literature: The paper builds on a well-established line of work (e.g., match-LSTM; decomposable attention; pairwise word interaction). The core framework (attend-compare-aggregate) and even the use of element-wise comparisons have been explored (e.g., Mou et al., 2016). The primary novelty is a cross-task empirical comparison of several f functions and a specific aggregation choice (CNN). While potentially useful, this is incremental.

- Rigor of the comparison-function study: Several comparison functions differ drastically in representational capacity and dimensionality. EUCCOS yields a 2-D vector per position, while SUB/MULT preserve l dimensions, and SUBMULT+NN adds an extra projection. Without controlling parameter counts or injecting a capacity-matching projection for EUCCOS/NTN/NN variants, performance differences can conflate capacity with the choice of similarity measure. This undermines the central conclusion that element-wise operations are superior.

- Architecture consistency across tasks: The claim of a “general” model is weakened by task-specific deviations (e.g., adding LSTM context only for SNLI; order-insensitive preprocessing for other tasks). This makes it hard to attribute cross-task gains to the comparison function versus other modeling choices.

- Baselines and fairness: The paper relies on reported baselines rather than reimplementing under a common training regime. For ranking tasks, the proposed listwise softmax over candidates (Eq. 10) may provide a training advantage relative to pointwise/pairwise baselines; this should be discussed or normalized. More recent or stronger baselines (for the time) with bi-directional attention or richer context encoders are not compared uniformly.

- Missing analyses: No statistical significance testing, limited variance reporting, and limited error analysis per phenomenon. Hyperparameters for the CNN aggregator (filter widths, counts) and attention normalization axis are not fully specified. Embeddings are not fine-tuned and OOVs are set to zero, which can materially affect the relative performance of comparison functions that are sensitive to scale/magnitude.

- Modeling choices: The attention is unidirectional; interactions between Q and A (or Q, A, P in MovieQA) are only partially captured. For MovieQA, the model aligns Q–P and A–P independently, then concatenates, neglecting direct Q–A interaction, which could be important.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Control capacity across f: Add a learnable projection so all comparison functions output the same dimensionality before aggregation, or match total parameter counts. Report results with/without this capacity control.

- Strengthen contextual encoding: Use lightweight contextualization (e.g., bidirectional RNNs) or convolutional encoders consistently across tasks, or show that contextualization does not change the relative ranking of comparison functions.

- Bidirectional/iterative attention: Compare one- vs two-directional attention and its effect on the comparison functions. This will situate your contribution against decomposable attention more directly.

- Aggregation choices: Compare CNN vs LSTM vs simple pooling for aggregation under the same capacity budget; report when and why CNN helps.

- Training and evaluation rigor: Report mean/std across multiple random seeds; perform significance tests. Provide details of CNN filter widths/counts, dropout rates, early stopping criteria, and attention softmax axis. Consider fine-tuning embeddings or normalizing embeddings to decouple scale effects from the choice of f.

- Baselines: Where feasible, re-run strong baselines under your training setup (including listwise training where appropriate) or add stronger contemporaneous baselines to solidify the empirical claim.

- Analysis: Provide per-phenomenon error analysis (e.g., lexical overlap vs paraphrase, negation, length mismatch) to understand when element-wise functions help. In MovieQA, analyze cases requiring Q–A interaction beyond P alignment.

Questions for the authors
- Capacity control: How many parameters are attributable to the comparison layer under each f? Did you try projecting EUCCOS to l dimensions before CNN to control capacity? Do conclusions hold then?

- Attention details: Along which axis is the softmax in G applied? Did you try bi-directional attention or multi-hop/iterative alignment? Any change in the relative ranking of f?

- CNN aggregator: What kernel sizes and number of filters were used? Did you try LSTM/Transformer aggregators, and if so, how did they compare at matched capacity?

- Training protocol: How many runs/seeds per experiment, and what is the variance? Did you use early stopping on a dev set? Are improvements statistically significant?

- Embeddings: Why choose not to fine-tune embeddings and to set OOVs to zero? How sensitive are SUB/MULT to embedding normalization or scaling?

- InsuranceQA/WikiQA losses: Your listwise softmax couples candidates per question. Do any baselines use listwise training? If not, can you provide pairwise/pointwise variants of your model for a fairer comparison?

- MovieQA design: Why exclude direct Q–A interaction? Did you try incorporating it (e.g., tri-attention)? Does it change the effect of the comparison function?

- Generality claim: Since SNLI uses an LSTM in preprocessing but other tasks do not, can you report results where all tasks share the exact same components to support the “general framework” claim?
