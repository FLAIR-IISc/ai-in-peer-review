Summary and contributions
- The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” framework for matching two text sequences. The model consists of: (i) a lightweight gating-based preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) a unidirectional attention to align words in one sequence to the other, (iii) a word-level comparison function applied to aligned pairs, and (iv) a CNN aggregator over the sequence of comparison vectors for final prediction.
- The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions at the word level: two neural (NN, NTN), a 2D similarity pair (Euclidean + cosine), two element-wise operations (SUB: squared difference; MULT: Hadamard product), and a combined SUBMULT+NN. The authors hypothesize and show that simple element-wise comparisons (especially SUB and MULT, and their combination with a small NN) are strong middle grounds between under- and over-parameterized choices.
- The model is evaluated across four tasks/datasets with distinct setups: MovieQA (multiple-choice machine comprehension over plot synopses), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (answer ranking; MAP/MRR), and SNLI (textual entailment). Reported results are SOTA or competitive on three QA datasets and close to SOTA on SNLI. Ablations indicate both preprocessing gates and attention matter, with different importance depending on sequence length asymmetry. Code is released.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical performance across diverse sequence matching tasks with a simple, well-motivated model that isolates the role of the comparison function; (2) Clear and useful empirical finding that element-wise subtraction/multiplication features are robustly effective, offering a practical recipe.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Matching text sequences is fundamental; prior work (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction) uses compare-aggregate but pays less attention to the specific comparison function. The paper targets this gap explicitly and evaluates alternatives across varied tasks, which is timely and practically valuable.
- Relation to literature: The model is closely related to Parikh et al.’s decomposable attention and He & Lin’s pairwise interaction CNN. Differences include a CNN aggregator (vs FFN/concat), a unidirectional attention, and the emphasis on element-wise comparisons and their systematic study. The novelty is incremental, but the cross-task synthesis and thorough comparison study are worthwhile.
- Empirical support and rigor:
  - Breadth: Four datasets with different formats strengthens the generality claim. The ablation on attention vs preprocessing is informative and aligns with intuition about length asymmetry.
  - Comparison functions: Evaluating six variants, including parameter-free element-wise ones, is a meaningful exploration; the finding that SUBMULT+NN often wins and that EUCCOS can be surprisingly competitive is useful.
  - Concerns: (i) Capacity fairness is not fully established—EUCCOS yields 2D vectors while others produce l-dimensional ones; the downstream CNN/classifier capacity may advantage some variants. Parameter counts, filter configs, and hyperparameter tuning parity should be reported to ensure fair comparisons. (ii) Statistical significance/variance across runs is not discussed. (iii) Some baselines are strong, but closely related variants (e.g., an in-house implementation of Parikh et al. with matched capacity, bidirectional attention, or ESIM-style bi-attention) would make the case stronger. (iv) Certain implementation details (CNN filter sizes/number, truncation policies for MovieQA plots, preprocessing LSTM specifics for SNLI) are underspecified.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The high-level description is clear; equations for gating, attention, and comparison are explicit, and code availability helps. Some notational and detail gaps remain (see below).

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Report parameter counts and CNN details (window sizes, number of filters) and ensure/argue capacity parity across comparison functions. Consider normalizing the dimension of tj (e.g., projecting all variants to a fixed l with a learned linear layer) to decouple function choice from downstream capacity.
- Provide mean/std over multiple runs and statistical tests, especially when improvements are modest.
- Include more recent or closely related baselines with matched infrastructure (e.g., a reimplementation of decomposable attention with your CNN aggregator, bi-attention baselines).
- Analyze computational cost and latency; element-wise comparisons may be faster—quantify this benefit.
- Extend analysis: (i) error typology across tasks; (ii) sensitivity to embedding training (you fix GloVe; does fine-tuning change the ranking of comparison functions?); (iii) impact of bidirectional attention vs unidirectional; (iv) effect of incorporating contextual encoders (BiLSTM) uniformly across tasks to separate the contribution of comparison functions from preprocessing capacity.
- Clarify the MovieQA setting (which track, pre-processing of long plots, candidate retrieval/truncation) to ensure comparability with reported baselines.
- Clean up minor typos/notation (e.g., consistent vector/matrix symbols, missing indices).

Questions for the authors
1) Capacity fairness: What are the exact dimensions and parameter counts for each comparison variant? How many CNN filters and what window sizes are used? Did you adjust CNN capacity to compensate for EUCCOS’s 2D tj?
2) Hyperparameters: How were hyperparameters tuned per dataset and per comparison function? Were the same settings used across functions to avoid cherry-picking?
3) Variance: What is the variance across random seeds for each dataset and function? Are the reported gains statistically significant?
4) Attention design: Did you try bidirectional (co-)attention or iterative alignment? If so, how did results change? If not, why constrain to unidirectional?
5) Embeddings: You keep GloVe fixed. Does fine-tuning embeddings (or using character/subword features) change the relative performance of comparison functions or overall results?
6) SNLI preprocessing: You mention incorporating LSTM into the preprocessing for SNLI. Please specify the exact architecture, whether it’s shared across sequences, and its effect size.
7) MovieQA specifics: How do you handle very long plots (truncation/chunking)? Are your baselines evaluated on the same split and textual-only track?
8) Generalization: Have you tested on additional sequence-pair tasks (e.g., Quora paraphrase, SQuAD-style span QA with compare-aggregate blocks) to further validate generality?

Overall, despite limited conceptual novelty relative to contemporary compare-aggregate models, the paper offers a clear, well-motivated, and practically useful empirical study across tasks, with strong performance and actionable insights about comparison functions.
