- Paper evaluates a compare-aggregate architecture and six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN).  
- Model uses a gating preprocessing layer, one-directional attention, per-token comparison, and a CNN aggregation layer.  
- Evaluated on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI; strong on three QA datasets and competitive on SNLI.  
- Element-wise comparison functions (especially SUBMULT+NN, and often SUB or MULT) perform best overall.  
- Ablations show both preprocessing gates and attention help; attention is more helpful when sequence lengths differ.  
- Decision: Accept (weak accept).  
- Key reason: clear empirical contribution — a simple, general model that performs strongly across diverse tasks.  
- Key reason: practical insight that SUB/MULT often outperform flexible NNs/NTNs, making them efficient and easy to implement.  
- Strength: same architecture applied to four distinct tasks with minimal task-specific changes, demonstrating generality.  
- Strength: systematic, controlled comparison of six comparison functions under a unified training protocol.  
- Strength: competitive/state-of-the-art results on several datasets (consistent gains on InsuranceQA, strong on WikiQA and MovieQA).  
- Strength: ablation analysis demonstrating contributions of components and interpretable effects tied to sequence length.  
- Strength: code released and key model/hyperparameter details provided, aiding reproducibility.  
- Weakness: moderate novelty — architecture follows prior compare-aggregate designs; main novelty is cross-task comparison and CNN aggregation choice.  
- Weakness: limited analysis depth — lacks variance/significance reporting, parameter/runtime comparisons, and detailed error analysis.  
- Weakness: uses only one-directional attention; unclear if element-wise findings hold with bi-directional/co-attention.  
- Weakness: potential lexical matching bias; robustness to paraphrase/semantic variation not analyzed.  
- Weakness: some implementation choices under-motivated (fixed embeddings, using LSTM only for SNLI breaks uniformity).  
- Feedback: report mean/std over multiple seeds and significance tests for comparisons.  
- Feedback: include parameter counts, training time, and inference speed; compare cost/benefit of NN/NTN vs SUB/MULT.  
- Feedback: justify and/or compare the 1-layer CNN aggregator against alternatives (sum/avg, RNNs, self-attention).  
- Feedback: compare one-directional vs. bi-directional/co-attention to study interactions with comparison functions.  
- Feedback: test with trainable embeddings and uniform contextual encoders across tasks to confirm findings persist.  
- Feedback: add qualitative error analysis and breakdowns (lexical overlap, paraphrase, length, negation).  
- Feedback: ensure fairness of baselines or harmonize preprocessing/candidate generation differences across datasets.  
- Questions: How many runs per setting were conducted; please report variance and significance for best functions.  
- Questions: Why fix GloVe embeddings and do results change if embeddings are fine-tuned?  
- Questions: How is the LSTM used in SNLI inserted relative to gates and attention, and does using LSTM on other tasks change conclusions?  
- Questions: How is EUCCOS’s 2D vector normalized/scaled before CNN; did you try temperature/normalization?  
- Questions: Did you try bi-directional or co-attention, and did the ordering of comparison functions change?  
- Questions: How are very long MovieQA plots handled (truncation/sliding windows) and what fraction is attended on average?  
- Questions: Did you evaluate robustness to paraphrase or SNLI subsets with low lexical overlap to rule out overlap-driven gains?  
- Questions: Please provide CNN window sizes, number of filters, and sensitivity analyses — are improvements consistent across these choices?  
- Questions: Does the released code include scripts to replicate all four datasets with the reported preprocessing and candidate generation?
