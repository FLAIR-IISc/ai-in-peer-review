Summary
The paper proposes a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching two text sequences. It emphasizes the comparison function at the word level, systematically evaluating six variants: NN, NTN, EUCCOS (Euclidean + cosine), SUB (element-wise squared difference), MULT (element-wise product), and SUBMULT+NN (concatenated SUB and MULT followed by a feedforward layer). The model pipeline includes a per-word gating “preprocessing” step, a one-directional attention to obtain an aligned representation for each token, comparison, and a one-layer CNN for aggregation. The authors evaluate on four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), claim strong to SOTA results on three, and conclude that element-wise SUB/MULT (especially SUBMULT+NN) is a robust and effective choice across tasks. Code is said to be released.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) Insufficient novelty beyond existing compare-aggregate/heuristic matching lines of work; (2) Significant clarity, methodological, and reporting issues that impede reproducibility and weaken empirical support; (3) Numerous formatting and notational inconsistencies.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: The compare-aggregate framework and element-wise comparison features (SUB/MULT) are already established (e.g., Mou et al., 2016; heuristic matching in NLI, decomposable attention). The contribution is mainly a cross-dataset comparison of simple functions. While potentially useful, this is incremental and does not substantially advance modeling or theory. Differences from Parikh et al. (2016) (one-way attention, CNN aggregator) are modest and not compellingly justified or ablated.

- Methodological clarity and rigor:
  - Preprocessing/gating (Eq. 1): The operator for element-wise multiplication is missing in the equation (the text says “where is element-wise multiplication”). Notation is overloaded (Q denotes both a matrix and a length; Q and Q after preprocessing use the same symbol). The gating resembles a simple per-token highway-like transform but is not clearly described, motivated, or ablated beyond high-level statements.
  - Attention (Eq. 2): The axis of the softmax and normalization details are not specified. Dimensions are plausible but not carefully explained, especially given notation overloading.
  - Comparison functions: EUCCOS yields a 2-D vector per token, whereas other choices produce l-dimensional vectors. Yet Eq. 9 claims r ∈ R^{n l} and the text fixes l=150. How is the aggregation architecture adapted for EUCCOS (and other dimension-mismatched variants)? This is a critical missing detail.
  - Aggregation: The one-layer CNN is insufficiently specified (filter widths, number of filters, pooling, padding/stride). The role of CNN vs. simple pooling/averaging/biLSTM aggregation is not examined.
  - Training: Word embeddings are fixed to GloVe; OOVs are zero-initialized (no UNK or finetuning), which is a strong and unusual choice—no justification or sensitivity analysis. No L2 regularization is used; dropout is only tuned on the embedding layer; no early stopping/seeding or variance reporting. For WikiQA hyperparameters are tuned differently than others, raising fairness concerns.
  - Task-specific setups: For InsuranceQA (large candidate pool), the negative sampling or candidate selection strategy (K) during training is unspecified; for MovieQA, the design only matches Q-P and A-P (no explicit Q-A), which is a design choice requiring motivation and ablation; for SNLI, an LSTM is “incorporated” into the preprocessing, but this is not described.
  - Statistical rigor: No multiple runs, standard deviations, or significance testing. Claims of “best” or “SOTA” are not qualified with variability.

- Comparison fairness:
  - Capacity matching across comparison functions is not ensured (e.g., EUCCOS being 2-D vs. 150-D for others) yet results are compared directly; this can confound conclusions about which comparison “works best”.
  - Baselines are taken from literature without ensuring comparable settings (embedding finetuning, optimization, preprocessing). Stronger contemporary baselines (e.g., ESIM/bi-attention variants) are not included; the one-directional attention vs. more standard bi-directional alignment is not compared.

- Empirical claims:
  - The conclusions that element-wise SUB/MULT outperform NN/NTN would be much more convincing with: (a) capacity-controlled setups, (b) multiple random seeds, (c) ablations on attention directionality, aggregation choice, and embedding finetuning, and (d) parameter-count/runtime analyses.
  - The paper’s explanation of when attention vs. preprocessing is more important is speculative; detailed quantitative ablations are not shown.

- Presentation and formatting:
  - Numerous reference formatting errors and placeholders (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”), typos (“Siamense”), incomplete citations (“Wan et al., ”), inconsistent author formatting.
  - Notation overload (Q as matrix and length), inconsistent symbols, missing operators, and vague dimension statements hamper readability.
  - Minor but pervasive inconsistencies (e.g., “one-layer CNN” with “n windows” but unspecified widths).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and standardize notation. Use distinct symbols for sequence length vs. matrices. Fix Eq. 1 to explicitly show element-wise multiplication and define all operators.
- Precisely specify attention normalization axes and aggregation CNN hyperparameters. Provide a complete configuration appendix.
- Ensure capacity-controlled comparisons among comparison functions (match output dimensionality and parameter counts where possible), and report parameter counts and FLOPs.
- Report results over multiple runs with mean±std, and conduct statistical significance tests.
- Include stronger baselines and ablations: two-way/bi-attention vs. one-way, alternative aggregators (max/mean pooling, BiLSTM), with/without embedding finetuning, and Q-A matching for MovieQA.
- Detail InsuranceQA candidate sampling strategy and K during training; analyze sensitivity to K.
- Justify fixed GloVe and zero OOV initialization, or provide experiments showing impact.
- Improve the related work positioning: clearly distinguish your contributions from Mou et al. (2016), Parikh et al. (2016), ESIM-style models, and pairwise interaction models.
- Clean up references and formatting to meet conference standards.

Questions for the authors
- How do you reconcile the dimension mismatch for EUCCOS (2-D) with the CNN aggregator expecting l-dimensional inputs and r ∈ R^{n l}? Did you change l for EUCCOS, pad, or alter the CNN? Please provide exact shapes for all variants.
- What are the CNN aggregation hyperparameters (filter widths, number of filters per width, pooling strategy), and are they shared across datasets and comparison functions?
- For InsuranceQA, what is K during training and evaluation? How are negative candidates sampled, and is the sampling consistent across runs?
- Why fix embeddings and zero-initialize OOVs? What happens if you finetune embeddings or use an UNK vector? Any sensitivity analysis?
- What is the exact modification to the preprocessing for SNLI when “incorporating LSTM”? Please provide the equations and associated hyperparameters.
- Did you try bi-directional attention (Q→A and A→Q)? If so, how does it compare? If not, why favor one-directional attention?
- Are your improvements statistically significant over the strongest baselines (with multiple seeds)?
- Can you share parameter counts and training times per variant to support the “computationally expensive” claims about order-sensitive preprocessing?
