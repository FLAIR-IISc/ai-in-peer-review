Summary and contributions
The paper studies sequence matching through a general compare-aggregate architecture and offers two main contributions: (i) a unified model instantiation that applies the same pipeline (gated pre-processing, one-directional attention, token-wise comparison, and CNN aggregation) across four tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results; and (ii) a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), with the key empirical finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN) are robust and often superior to more flexible but less inductively biased choices like NN/NTN. The paper further includes ablations (attention vs. pre-processing) and qualitative visualization to shed light on how the model focuses during aggregation. Code is released.

Decision: Accept (weak)
Key reasons: (1) Strong and consistent empirical results across multiple, diverse sequence-pair tasks with a single architecture, demonstrating practical generality. (2) Clear, well-executed analysis isolating the impact of the comparison function, with a useful and broadly applicable takeaway that element-wise SUB/MULT are strong baselines for token-level matching.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a central question in modern NLP—how best to compare two text sequences—framing it in the now-common compare-aggregate paradigm. The motivation is sound: many prior works use token-level alignment but under-explore the specific form of the comparison function. The hypothesis that matching requires similarity-sensitive operations is well-placed in the literature and borne out empirically.

- Approach: The model is a reasonable synthesis of prior elements (gated pre-processing, attention, token-wise comparison, CNN aggregation). While not groundbreaking architecturally, it is cleanly specified, task-agnostic, and contrasts meaningfully with Parikh et al. (2016) via the aggregation choice (CNN vs. bag/sum) and the focus on the comparison function. The attention is one-directional (simpler and cheaper), which is pragmatic for long contexts, and the gating pre-processing is a lightweight alternative to full context encoders.

- Empirical support and rigor: Results reportedly match or surpass SOTA on three datasets and are competitive on SNLI, indicating external validity. The ablations showing the relative importance of attention vs. pre-processing as a function of length mismatch are insightful. The breadth of tasks (answer selection, passage QA with long contexts, and NLI) strengthens the claim of generality. The key claim—that element-wise SUB/MULT (and SUBMULT+NN) outperform NN/NTN—appears consistently supported.

- Practical insights: The finding that fixed GloVe embeddings with simple comparison functions perform strongly is useful for practitioners; it suggests that injecting similarity-aware, low-parameter interactions at the alignment stage can substitute for heavier capacity upstream.

Caveats and limitations (did not preclude acceptance)
- Novelty is incremental. The architecture is close to decomposable attention/match-LSTM variants. The primary new insight is the systematic comparison of f, plus minor design choices (CNN aggregation, gating pre-processing). The contribution is more empirical than theoretical.

- Methodological details that could affect fairness are under-specified: (i) capacity matching across comparison functions is unclear (e.g., EUCCOS produces 2-d features vs. l-d for others; the downstream CNN thus sees vastly different input sizes), (ii) the CNN aggregation hyperparameters (filter widths, number of filters, pooling) and parameter counts are not fully documented, and (iii) variance across random seeds and statistical significance are not reported.

- Some choices may artificially cap performance (e.g., freezing embeddings across all tasks; order-insensitive pre-processing for most datasets). While the paper notes this and still achieves strong results, it leaves open whether even stronger baselines (e.g., bidirectional attention, contextual encoders) would change the relative ranking among comparison functions.

- Baseline comparability: pulling numbers from prior work is standard, but contemporaneous strong baselines (e.g., bi-attentive decomposable/ESIM variants on SNLI) might outperform the reported results; for QA datasets, confirm that splits and evaluation protocols match prior SOTA.

Additional feedback and suggestions (to help improve the paper)
- Control for capacity in f: To better isolate the comparison function, match the dimensionality seen by the aggregator. For EUCCOS, consider projecting aj,hj into a shared space before computing distances, or augment EUCCOS to l dims (e.g., per-dimension distances) so CNN capacity is comparable. Report parameter counts per variant.

- Variance and significance: Report mean/std over multiple runs and statistical tests for improvements, particularly when margins are small.

- Stronger baselines and ablations: (i) Add a two-way attention variant; (ii) compare CNN aggregation to BiLSTM/transformer-style self-attentive aggregators; (iii) include a bilinear/biaffine comparison function (aT W h and [a;h;a◦h;|a−h|]) to position SUB/MULT against widely used NLI heuristics; (iv) test learned vs. frozen embeddings.

- Clarify implementation details: Specify CNN filter widths, counts, pooling strategy, and n; detail pre-processing gate initialization and relation to highway/GLU layers; for SNLI, describe the LSTM pre-processing more fully.

- Error analysis: Provide qualitative failure cases to understand when SUB/MULT underperform (e.g., paraphrastic vs. antonymic relations, negation, long-range reordering).

- Efficiency: Include training/inference speed and memory comparisons across f choices; the low-parameter functions are likely faster—quantify this benefit.

- Generality: If feasible, add an additional paraphrase/similarity dataset or MultiNLI to further support cross-domain generality.

Questions for the authors
1) How did you control for the differing output dimensionality of EUCCOS versus SUB/MULT/NN in the aggregator? Could the lower EUCCOS capacity explain some performance gaps?  
2) What are the CNN aggregation hyperparameters (window sizes, number of filters per size, pooling)? How sensitive are results to these?  
3) Did you run multiple seeds? What is the variance across runs, especially for WikiQA where datasets are small?  
4) For SNLI, how close are your results to contemporaneous ESIM/bi-attentive baselines trained with fine-tuned embeddings? If behind, does enabling trainable embeddings close the gap while preserving the comparison-function ranking?  
5) Have you tried a simple biaffine/bilinear comparison or the common [a, h, |a−h|, a◦h] heuristic? How does it compare to SUBMULT+NN?  
6) In MovieQA, how do you segment and index the long plot, and what is the computational cost? Would two-way attention help there without prohibitive overhead?  
7) Can you share parameter counts and FLOPs for each comparison function to better quantify the accuracy–efficiency trade-off?
