- Paper studies compare-aggregate architectures for text matching and the importance of word-level comparison functions.
- Proposed pipeline: gated preprocessing, unidirectional attention, word-level comparison, and CNN aggregation; tests six comparison functions.
- Main claims: broad applicability, element-wise functions (SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN) are strongest, competitive or SOTA on several datasets, and attention/gating matter.
- Decision: Reject due to limited novelty and insufficient experimental rigor.
- Novelty concern: overall architecture closely mirrors prior decomposable attention/compare-aggregate work; differences are incremental.
- Claim about element-wise operators lacks novelty since similar heuristics have prior use in pair modeling.
- Attention directionality and order-insensitive preprocessing are not theoretically motivated or empirically contrasted with alternatives.
- Empirical fairness issue: baselines taken from reported numbers instead of re-implemented under a shared setup.
- Task-specific model variations (e.g., LSTM for SNLI, triplet matching for MovieQA) undermine the “single general model” claim.
- No statistical significance testing or variance across random seeds reported, weakening confidence in modest gains.
- The paper doesn’t test whether findings hold with contextual encoders (BiLSTM/Transformer) or when fine-tuning embeddings.
- SOTA claims are not supported by apples-to-apples comparisons with contemporary strong baselines.
- Ablations are limited; missing systematic studies of aggregator choices, attention variants, and parameter-count effects (especially NTN vs light operators).
- Presentation issues: terse mathematical definitions and missing implementation details (NTN tensor shapes, CNN config, window sizes, filters).
- Gating preprocessor rationale and empirical behavior are not clearly motivated or analyzed.
- No error analysis, qualitative breakdowns, or discussion of failure modes provided.
- Strengths: clear articulation of the compare-aggregate pipeline, clean/simple implementation, useful cross-task evaluation, and practical finding that element-wise operators are strong; code release is positive.
- Recommendation: re-implement strong baselines under a unified setup, report standard deviations and significance, and include parameter counts and training time.
- Suggested ablations: aggregator variants (sum/mean/max pooling, BiLSTM), attention variants (bi-directional, multi-head), and contextual encoders while holding comparison functions fixed.
- Controlled experiment request: vary only the comparison function across tasks with a shared backbone to support generality.
- Analyze sensitivity to embedding choices (frozen vs fine-tuned), embedding dimension, and vocabulary coverage.
- Provide detailed error analysis: where SUB/MULT help or hurt (lexical overlap, compositional reasoning, negation, paraphrase).
- Clarify reproducibility details: exact CNN configs, attention scoring, normalization, and other implementation choices.
- Questions for authors: report mean±std over multiple runs and statistical significance for main comparisons.
- Questions for authors: do SUB/MULT findings persist with contextual encoders, bi-directional or multi-head attention, and when fine-tuning embeddings?
