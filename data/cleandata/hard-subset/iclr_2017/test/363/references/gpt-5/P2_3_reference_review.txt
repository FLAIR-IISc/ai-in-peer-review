1. Summary and contributions
This paper tackles the general problem of matching two (or more) text sequences for downstream decisions (e.g., QA answer selection, textual entailment). It adopts the “compare–aggregate” paradigm: attend one sequence to the other; compare aligned word-level representations; aggregate comparison outputs for classification. The two main claimed contributions are: (i) demonstrating the generality and effectiveness of this framework across four diverse tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI); and (ii) systematically studying six comparison functions, including simple element-wise operators (SUB, MULT) and their combination, showing that element-wise functions are strong and, in many cases, superior to neural MLP/NTN or cosine/Euclidean similarities. The model includes a lightweight gating “preprocessing” layer, a one-directional attention, several choices of comparison function, and a CNN aggregator. The authors also present ablations (removing preprocessing/attention) and some qualitative analysis, and release code.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Broad empirical contribution with a careful, targeted study of comparison functions inside a widely used framework, supported across four tasks. The simple element-wise comparisons emerging as robust winners is a useful and generalizable insight for the community.
- The approach is well-motivated, easy to implement, and competitive to state-of-the-art on several benchmarks, with ablations that substantiate the design choices.

3. Supporting arguments
Problem and motivation: The paper focuses on sequence matching—a core building block across NLI and QA—and homes in on a specific, under-explored component: the compare function at the word/aligned-token level. Prior works (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interaction, etc.) exemplify the compare–aggregate idea, but few dissect the comparison function systematically. This gap is clearly articulated and well-placed in literature.

Method: The model is straightforward: (1) order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” over embeddings; (2) one-way attention to align tokens; (3) comparison f(aj, hj) with six choices (NN, NTN, EUC+COS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN); (4) CNN aggregation. The design is sensible and easy to repurpose; the choice to decouple the comparison function for a focused study is justified.

Empirical evidence and rigor: Results (as reported) show:
- Strong performance across four datasets; SOTA or near-SOTA on MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA; competitive on SNLI.
- SUBMULT+NN tends to perform best overall; notably, pure element-wise SUB/MULT—parameter-free—are highly competitive and sometimes best, outperforming NN/NTN and EUC+COS.
- Ablations indicate both attention and the gating preprocessing help, with attention being more impactful when sequence lengths differ substantially (MovieQA/InsuranceQA), and preprocessing helping more when lengths are closer (WikiQA/SNLI).
- Code release aids reproducibility.

These findings support the central claims: the compare–aggregate framework generalizes, and simple element-wise compare functions are a strong, practical choice. While the analysis is largely empirical, the connection between SUB/MULT and Euclidean/cosine similarities provides helpful intuition.

Limitations:
- Novelty is incremental in architecture; the primary contribution is empirical. The framework closely resembles decomposable attention and related models, differing mainly by the systematic comparison study, CNN aggregation, and a gating preprocessor.
- Baseline comparison relies on reported numbers from prior papers rather than controlled reimplementations; potential discrepancies in data preprocessing, external resources, and tuning may affect fairness. Statistical significance is not discussed.
- The SNLI variant adds LSTM in preprocessing, which breaks strict architectural uniformity and complicates cross-task claims of a single unified model.
- MovieQA baselines are relatively weak compared to later reading comprehension models; stronger contemporary baselines would better substantiate SOTA claims.
- Limited error analysis and no discussion of parameter counts or efficiency trade-offs, despite efficiency being an implicit advantage of element-wise compare functions.
- References contain formatting issues; some related models (e.g., bidirectional attention) are not compared.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not decisive for acceptance)
- Provide full numeric results with standard deviations and significance tests across multiple runs to strengthen empirical claims.
- Report parameter counts, FLOPs, and training/runtime comparisons across comparison functions to highlight efficiency benefits of element-wise operators.
- Include stronger baselines where feasible, or at least discuss contemporaneous models more deeply (e.g., bidirectional attention in aggregation, pointer mechanisms for MC/QA).
- Unify the architecture across tasks as much as possible (or justify deviations) to reinforce the “general framework” claim.
- Expand analysis: (i) error breakdowns by phenomena (lexical overlap, paraphrase, negation); (ii) sensitivity to embedding initialization and finetuning; (iii) impact of bidirectional vs one-directional attention.
- Clean up references and improve clarity around preprocessing/tokenization and dataset splits.
- Consider adding paraphrase datasets (e.g., MSRP/Quora) to broaden coverage of “sequence matching.”

5. Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the findings (ranking of comparison functions) to embedding finetuning vs freezing? Did you try ELMo/other contextual embeddings or subword models?
- Did you run multiple seeds and, if so, are the improvements statistically significant? Please report mean±std across runs.
- Can you quantify parameter counts and training/inference times for each comparison function to substantiate the “simple and effective” claim?
- For SNLI, why introduce LSTM only there? If you remove it and use the same order-insensitive preprocessor, how much does accuracy drop, and do the relative rankings of comparison functions persist?
- How does bidirectional attention (attend A→Q and Q→A) affect performance and the relative behavior of SUB/MULT vs NN/NTN?
- For MovieQA, can you compare against stronger neural MC baselines under the same data regime? Alternatively, justify differences in supervision/resources if direct comparisons are not feasible.
- Do SUB and MULT remain strong when there is heavy lexical divergence (low word overlap) or substantial syntactic variation? Any targeted stress tests?

Overall, this paper offers a clear, broadly useful empirical contribution: simple element-wise comparison functions are strong choices within compare–aggregate models across tasks. Despite limited architectural novelty, the systematic study and multi-dataset validation merit acceptance.
