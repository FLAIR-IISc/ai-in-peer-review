Summary and contributions
- This paper adopts the compare-aggregate paradigm for sequence matching and evaluates it across four tasks/datasets (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, MovieQA). The model uses: a simple gated preprocessing per token, a one-directional attention to align tokens, a per-token comparison f(aj, hj), and a CNN aggregator for final prediction.
- The central contribution is an empirical study of six comparison functions at the word level (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), with the main finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and especially their combination with a shallow NN) perform strongly and often better than more complex neural comparators.
- The paper reports competitive or state-of-the-art results on multiple datasets and provides some ablations (removing preprocessing or attention) and qualitative visualizations. Code is made available.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Limited novelty beyond existing compare-aggregate models; the main new technical element (element-wise SUB/MULT) is incremental and has prior usage, while other architectural choices (attention, CNN aggregation) are standard.
2) Insufficient experimental rigor to substantiate broad claims of generality: baselines are not re-implemented under a unified setting; no variance/significance analyses; several important implementation and evaluation details are missing or ambiguous, raising concerns about fairness and reproducibility.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and novelty: The work is well placed within compare-aggregate literature (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM, pairwise interaction models). However, the architecture differences (one-directional attention, CNN aggregator, order-insensitive gating) are relatively minor variations. The emphasis on comparison functions is useful, but SUB/MULT have been used before in sentence-pair models, and the paper does not provide a deeper analysis (e.g., why/when these element-wise ops help, or their relationship to normalized bilinear forms).
- Experimental rigor and fairness:
  - Baseline handling: The paper reports published numbers for baselines rather than re-implementing and tuning them with the same embeddings, preprocessing, and hyperparameter budgets. This can confound comparisons, especially across datasets with different evaluation setups (e.g., InsuranceQA negative sampling/pool size).
  - Statistical validity: There are no multiple-run averages, standard deviations, or significance testing. Given modest margins over strong baselines, this is a concern.
  - Hyperparameter and capacity control: The comparator functions have very different parameter counts and inductive biases. The study does not normalize model capacity or report parameter counts, making it hard to attribute gains to the comparison function rather than model size or regularization effects.
  - Training/evaluation specifics are under-specified: CNN filter widths, number of filters n, attention softmax axis, learning rate schedules/early stopping, and implementation details for SNLI “incorporated LSTM” are not transparent. For WikiQA, the training objective for ranking (pointwise vs pairwise vs listwise) is not clearly stated, while MAP/MRR are evaluation metrics.
- Claims of generality: While four datasets is appreciable, they are all within sentence/short-paragraph matching and QA selection; the claim that the framework is generally effective would be more convincing with more diverse tasks (e.g., paraphrase, semantic textual similarity, natural language reasoning beyond entailment) and controlled comparisons (same encoder, different comparators).
- Analysis depth: The ablations remove attention or preprocessing, but the paper does not examine sensitivity to tokenization, embedding normalization, scale issues (SUB/MULT are scale-sensitive), sequence length, or OOV handling. The qualitative visualizations are suggestive but limited.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all part of the decision)
- Re-implement and tune strong baselines in a unified framework with shared embeddings, identical preprocessing, and comparable parameter budgets; report results with multiple seeds and significance tests.
- Report parameter counts per comparator, and include variants that equalize capacity (e.g., add a projection layer so EUCCOS outputs comparable dimensionality to others).
- Clarify and expand implementation details: CNN kernel sizes/strides/padding, number of filters, attention normalization axis, objective for ranking tasks, negative sampling strategy and candidate pool size for InsuranceQA.
- Analyze robustness: results with fine-tuning vs frozen embeddings; embedding normalization; effect of sequence length mismatch; performance by question type; calibration and confidence.
- Strengthen interpretability: show attention/comparison maps; examine which dimensions of SUB/MULT matter; provide error analyses across tasks.
- Situate the gating layer relative to highway/GLU layers; consider comparing to a standard highway network as preprocessing.
- Explore stronger/modern baselines and variants (e.g., bi-directional attention, pooling alternatives, self-attention within sequences) to better understand where the gains come from.

Questions for the authors
1) Attention details: In Eq. (2), along which axis is softmax applied to G? How is attention normalized for each aj? Did you try bi-directional attention, and if so, with what effect?
2) CNN aggregator: What are the kernel widths, number of filters (n), and padding/stride choices? How sensitive are results to these choices?
3) Ranking objectives: For WikiQA and InsuranceQA, what is the training loss (pointwise cross-entropy over candidates vs pairwise/listwise)? For InsuranceQA, what is the candidate pool size during training and evaluation, and how are negatives sampled?
4) SNLI preprocessing: You state “we only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for SNLI.” Please specify precisely where the LSTM is used, how it interfaces with the gating and attention, and whether parameters are shared with other components.
5) Embeddings: Why fix GloVe rather than fine-tuning? Do results change materially with fine-tuning or with embedding normalization? How are OOVs handled beyond zero initialization?
6) Capacity control: What are the parameter counts for each comparator? Did you attempt to match capacities across comparators? For EUCCOS, did you try adding a learned projection before computing distance/similarity?
7) Variability and significance: What is the variance across random seeds? Are improvements statistically significant on each dataset?
8) Dataset-specific preprocessing: What tokenization/casing rules were used? Any pruning of sentences/passages? For MovieQA, how is the plot segmented and selected for each QA pair?

Overall, the paper is a useful empirical note on comparator choices within compare-aggregate models, but the novelty is limited and the experimental support is not yet rigorous enough for ICLR.
