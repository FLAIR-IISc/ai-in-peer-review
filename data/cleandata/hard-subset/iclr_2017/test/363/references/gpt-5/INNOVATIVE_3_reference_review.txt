1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” architecture for matching two text sequences. It instantiates a simple pipeline with (i) order-insensitive preprocessing gates, (ii) one-way attention to align A to Q, (iii) a word-level comparison function f applied to each aligned pair, and (iv) a CNN aggregator for final prediction. The main claimed contributions are:
- A cross-task empirical validation of the compare-aggregate paradigm on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving strong or state-of-the-art results on three.
- A systematic comparison of six word-level comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction SUB, element-wise multiplication MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), with the finding that element-wise operations (especially the SUB+MULT combination) are consistently strong and often best.

The paper argues that element-wise comparison hits a sweet spot between overly general parametric f (NN/NTN) and overly restrictive scalar similarities (cosine, Euclidean), and that the framework is broadly applicable.

2) Decision
Reject. Primary reason: The novelty is limited and largely incremental over existing compare-aggregate/attention-based matching architectures; the principal contribution is an empirical comparison of known comparison functions and a cross-task evaluation, which, while useful, is not sufficiently conceptually new for ICLR.

3) Supporting arguments
- Limited architectural novelty: The model is very close to prior compare-aggregate work (e.g., Decomposable Attention, match-LSTM, pairwise word-interaction). Differences such as using a CNN instead of summation/concatenation for aggregation, a simple gating preprocessor, and one-directional attention feel minor. The paper acknowledges Mou et al. (2016) already using element-wise operations, and He & Lin (2016) exploring cosine/Euclidean/dot-product—so the core comparison ideas are not new.
- Main contribution is empirical: The paper’s strongest contribution is a systematic comparison of f across tasks. This is valuable, but falls short of introducing a substantially new concept or mechanism. The insight that SUB and MULT are strong (and their concatenation + small NN is best) is useful, but unsurprising given widespread use of such heuristics in sentence pair modeling.
- Inconsistent “general” model: The preprocessing is order-insensitive, but for SNLI the authors add an LSTM. This undermines the claim of a single general framework and blurs attribution of gains (e.g., do improvements on SNLI stem from contextualization rather than f?).
- Experimental rigor and fairness: 
  - Baselines are taken from reported numbers; not re-implemented under uniform conditions. Some baselines are comparatively dated. No statistical significance or variance across random seeds is reported.
  - Parameter counts and representational capacity are not controlled across comparison functions (e.g., EUCCOS yields 2-D features; others yield l-D; SUBMULT+NN adds parameters). This makes it hard to isolate the effect of the comparison operation from capacity.
  - The attention is one-directional; many strong contemporaries use bi-attention/co-attention. It is unclear whether the conclusions about f hold under stronger alignment modules.
- Analysis depth: There is an ablation of preprocessing and attention, and a visualization, but limited error analysis or probing of when/why element-wise operators help. Efficiency/complexity analysis (especially for long MovieQA plots) is missing.

Overall, the work is competently executed and the results are strong, but the core idea is not sufficiently new or bold.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all affect my decision)
- Strengthen positioning vs. Parikh et al. (2016) and co-attention models: clearly articulate the conceptual novelty beyond a systematic study of f. If the key thesis is “comparison functions matter more than aggregation,” consider formalizing this and providing stronger evidence.
- Control capacity across f: 
  - Match dimensionality/parameters (e.g., project SUB/MULT to 2-D or up-project EUCCOS to l via a learned linear layer) and compare. Report parameter counts and FLOPs.
  - Report performance variance over multiple seeds; include significance tests.
- Unify the architecture: Either use the same preprocessing across all datasets or clearly separate results to avoid confounds. Evaluate a light contextualization (e.g., small BiLSTM or convolution) consistently to show the f findings are robust.
- Compare with stronger/modern baselines under a common setup, including a bi-attention variant of your model, to verify if your conclusions about f persist when alignment is stronger.
- Provide more detailed analyses: 
  - Error typology, length sensitivity, lexical overlap vs. paraphrase cases.
  - Efficiency and memory footprints; especially for MovieQA’s long plots.
- Clarify experimental details: exact CNN filter sizes, numbers of filters, pooling, candidate sizes K, negative sampling strategies if any, truncation of long contexts, and how attention is computed over very long sequences.
- Consider fine-tuning embeddings and report its effect; this can also help support the generality of your conclusions.

Questions for the authors
- Novelty and framing: Beyond a cross-task empirical study and minor architectural choices (CNN aggregator, gating), what is the new conceptual contribution relative to Decomposable Attention and prior word-interaction models?
- Capacity control: How do you ensure that differences in performance among NN/NTN/EUCCOS/SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN are not primarily due to dimensionality and parameter-count differences? Can you report controlled comparisons?
- Directionality of attention: Why restrict to one-directional attention? Did you try co-attention/bi-attention, and do your conclusions about the best f still hold?
- Contextualization: For SNLI you used an LSTM in preprocessing. How does this affect the comparative ranking of the f functions? Are the findings robust without LSTM?
- Robustness: What is the variance across runs? Are improvements statistically significant?
- MovieQA specifics: How do you handle very long plots (truncation, sliding windows, retrieval)? What is the computational complexity? Do your conclusions about f hold under any retrieval/preselection step?
- Training setup: Did you try fine-tuning word embeddings? How sensitive are the results to hyperparameters (e.g., dropout, learning rate) across tasks?

I appreciate the clear writing, code release, and the attempt to distill effective comparison operators. With stronger novelty or a more principled theoretical account and tighter empirical controls, this could become a more compelling contribution.
