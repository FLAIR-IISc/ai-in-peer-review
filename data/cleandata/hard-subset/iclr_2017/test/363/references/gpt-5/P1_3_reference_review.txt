Summary and contributions
The paper investigates a general “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences, arguing that comparing at the word (or small-unit) level followed by aggregation is broadly effective across tasks. The model includes: (i) a simple gating-based preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) a unidirectional attention from one sequence to another, (iii) a comparison function f applied to each pair of attended vectors, and (iv) a CNN to aggregate the comparison sequence. The central contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN), including element-wise subtraction and multiplication, across four datasets spanning QA (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and NLI (SNLI). The authors report strong results—state-of-the-art or competitive on several datasets—and find that element-wise operations (especially SUBMULT+NN) perform best on average. They also provide ablations showing the importance of preprocessing and attention and release code.

Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- Limited novelty: The compare-aggregate paradigm, attention, and element-wise comparisons are well established, and the proposed architecture is a relatively incremental combination of known components. The main novelty—systematic study of comparison functions—is valuable but not sufficient for ICLR.
- Incomplete experimental rigor and controls: Several comparisons are not capacity-controlled; statistical significance, variance across runs, and fairness of baselines are unclear, limiting confidence in the empirical claims.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning in literature: The paper is well motivated by prior compare-aggregate works (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM, pairwise word interaction). However, element-wise SUB/MULT were already used (e.g., Mou et al., 2016) and cosine/Euclidean-based comparisons are standard. The architectural differences (unidirectional attention; CNN aggregation; a gating “preprocessing”) appear incremental relative to strong baselines like Parikh et al. (2016).
- Claims vs. evidence: The main claim—that element-wise comparison functions strike a good balance between expressive NN/NTN and restrictive Euclidean/cosine—is plausible and supported by reported numbers. Yet the experiments do not control for parameter count across f choices: EUCCOS (2-d output) vs. NN/NTN (many parameters) vs. SUB/MULT (parameter-free) feed different-sized signals into the CNN. Without capacity-matched variants (e.g., project SUB/MULT through the same-sized MLP), it’s hard to isolate whether the gain comes from the form of f or from regularization/optimization side-effects.
- Statistical rigor: The paper reports single-number results with no standard deviations, no significance testing, and no discussion of sensitivity to random seeds, especially important across four tasks of different sizes (e.g., WikiQA is small and prone to variance).
- Baseline fairness: The baselines are taken from the literature “as reported,” while the proposed model is tuned (dropout, learning rate, batch size), and uses GloVe initialization. Some baselines use different candidate pool sizes, data preprocessing, or training protocols. Without re-implementations or careful normalization, the SOTA claims are less robust.
- Architecture analysis: The choice of unidirectional attention and CNN aggregator is not deeply justified against alternatives (e.g., two-way/decomposable attention, RNN or Transformer-style aggregation). An ablation comparing CNN vs. simple sum/mean vs. RNN would clarify the importance of the aggregation module beyond adopting a known CNN.
- Order sensitivity: For three tasks, the preprocessing is order-insensitive; nonetheless the model outperforms order-sensitive baselines. This is an interesting finding, but the paper does not provide deeper diagnostics (e.g., error analysis on phenomena requiring word order, or comparisons where order-aware preprocessing is toggled). It is unclear whether the datasets favor lexical overlap matching.
- Efficiency and scalability: The attention computation scales with sequence lengths (e.g., plot synopses in MovieQA), but memory/time costs and training efficiency are not discussed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all are decision-critical)
- Control for capacity: For each f, pass its output through an identical-sized projection MLP so the downstream CNN receives equal-dimensional signals. Report parameter counts and FLOPs.
- Report variability: Provide results averaged over multiple seeds with standard deviations; include statistical tests on key comparisons.
- Fairer baselines: Re-implement at least one strong compare-aggregate baseline (e.g., decomposable attention) with the same embeddings and training setup; ensure the same candidate pool sizes and evaluation scripts on InsuranceQA and WikiQA.
- Aggregation ablation: Compare CNN vs. sum/mean pooling vs. BiLSTM/Transformer encoders; include bidirectional attention.
- Order sensitivity: Evaluate order-aware preprocessing (e.g., BiLSTM or self-attention) across all tasks, not only SNLI, to clarify when order helps.
- Error analysis: Provide qualitative analyses beyond the CNN activation heatmaps, focusing on categories (lexical overlap vs. paraphrase vs. negation vs. long-range reasoning).
- Efficiency: Discuss computational complexity and wall-clock times for long plots in MovieQA.

Questions for the authors
- Capacity control: How do results change if you apply the same-sized projection MLP after each comparison function so the CNN always sees the same dimensionality? Do SUB/MULT still win?
- Variance: What is the standard deviation across different random seeds for each dataset? Are the improvements statistically significant?
- Baseline parity: For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, what are the exact candidate pool sizes and evaluation scripts used? Did you re-run baselines under your preprocessing and GloVe settings?
- Attention direction: Did you try two-way (decomposable) attention? How do results compare to your one-directional attention?
- Aggregation choice: Why a one-layer CNN instead of a BiLSTM or simple pooling? Any empirical comparison?
- Order modeling: For MovieQA and InsuranceQA, what happens if you add a lightweight order-aware encoder (e.g., small-width CNN or BiGRU) in preprocessing?
- Resource usage: What are memory/time costs for MovieQA (P is long)? Any truncation or stride/windowing strategies?
- Analysis of failures: On SNLI, where are errors concentrated (e.g., contradiction, negation, numbers)? Does the element-wise comparison struggle with particular linguistic phenomena?

Overall, the paper is clear, the cross-task perspective is useful, and the empirical observation on element-wise comparisons is interesting. However, the novelty is modest and the empirical methodology needs stronger controls and analyses to meet ICLR’s bar.
