1) Summary of contributions
The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” architecture for matching two text sequences. The model consists of: (i) an order-insensitive gating-based preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) a one-directional attention mechanism to align one sequence to the other, (iii) a word-level comparison function f that combines an attended vector with the corresponding token vector, and (iv) a CNN-based aggregation over the sequence of comparison vectors for final prediction. The main focus is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN. The paper reports results on four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and claims strong or state-of-the-art performance, with simple element-wise SUB/MULT-based comparisons performing best overall. Ablation suggests preprocessing and attention both help, with relative importance depending on sequence-length asymmetry. Code is released.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficient methodological clarity and rigor to ensure reproducibility and validate claims (notation ambiguities, missing critical hyperparameters/protocol choices, absent figures/tables, no variance/significance).
- Limited novelty relative to prior compare-aggregate models; the main technical advance is a comparison of word-level similarity functions, which is incremental and not evaluated with sufficient thoroughness or fairness.

3) Supporting arguments
Novelty and positioning
- The compare-aggregate paradigm and attention-based alignment are well-established (e.g., decomposable attention, match-LSTM). The architectural differences (CNN aggregation, specific gating preproc) are modest; the main novelty is the set of comparison functions, some already used in prior pair modeling work. The paper does not convincingly distinguish its model from Parikh et al. (2016) beyond minor design choices.

Rigor and clarity
- Critical details are missing or ambiguous:
  • Notation: Q and A are re-used for both original and preprocessed matrices; it is unclear whether Wi/Wu/bi/bu in Eq. (1) are shared between sequences. eQ and eA are not clearly defined (assumed all-ones of appropriate length).  
  • CNN aggregation: kernel widths, number of filters, strides, pooling scheme, n (“number of windows”) are unspecified.  
  • SNLI “incorporated LSTM” preprocessing is underspecified (uni/bi-LSTM? layers? hidden size? dropout? how integrated with Eq. (1)?).  
  • Attention masking and padding treatment are not described.  
  • Training regimen lacks epochs, early stopping criteria, gradient clipping, scheduling, and seed/variance reporting.  
  • Embeddings are fixed (not updated), which is an unusual choice and may unfairly limit comparisons or affect generalization; rationale and sensitivity are not provided.

Experimental protocol and fairness
- Tables and figures referenced (Tables 1–4, Figure 1–2) are not present in the text provided, preventing verification of numeric claims and ablations.
- Results are compared to reported baselines only; no standardized re-implementation or controlled re-runs are provided. There is no discussion of variance or statistical significance.
- For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, exact evaluation protocols (candidate pool sizes, negative sampling, question-level pooling, filtering of answers, and adherence to standard splits) are not described. InsuranceQA often involves large candidate pools; the softmax over K (Eq. 10) suggests per-question candidate sets but details are absent.
- MovieQA plots can be very long; the O(Q·P) attention cost is potentially large. Truncation or memory strategies are not described, raising concerns about practicality and reproducibility.

Scope of comparisons
- The comparison-function study omits strong and commonly used alternatives, e.g., bilinear/biaffine similarity, parameterized cosine, scaled dot-product, multi-head/bilinear pooling, and absolute difference + product + linear layer baselines common in sentence-pair models.  
- Aggregation is restricted to a single CNN choice; no comparison to summation/mean-pooling, RNN aggregation, or attention-based pooling as in prior work.  
- The claim that element-wise functions are a “good middle ground” is plausible but not thoroughly probed with diagnostics (e.g., dimensional importance, normalization, scale-sensitivity; EUCCOS combines heterogeneous scales without normalization).

Generalizability
- The “general” architecture changes across tasks (e.g., LSTM used only on SNLI; different matching schemes for MovieQA), weakening the claim of a single general model.
- No error analysis or per-category analysis (e.g., length, lexical overlap, paraphrase phenomena) to explain when and why element-wise comparisons help.

Presentation and formatting
- Numerous typographical and formatting issues (e.g., “Siamense,” inconsistent hyphenation and capitalization, malformed references with escaped tokens and question marks, missing apostrophes and diacritics) and incomplete Related Work entries detract from clarity and polish.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and fix notation in Section 2.2; explicitly denote preprocessed representations with distinct symbols; state whether parameters are shared across the two sequences. Define eQ/eA and justify choices.
- Provide full implementation details: CNN kernel sizes and counts, pooling, dropout placements, early stopping, gradient clipping, epochs, random seeds. Report mean±std over multiple runs and significance tests.
- Detail the exact evaluation protocols per dataset (splits, candidate sets, truncation, preprocessing/tokenization, max lengths, padding/masking, OOV handling).
- Expand the comparison function suite (bilinear/biaffine, parameterized cosine/dot, scaled dot-product, multi-head variants) and compare aggregation mechanisms (sum/mean, self-attention, RNN).
- Report computational complexity, memory usage, and wall-clock comparisons; discuss the impact of fixing vs. fine-tuning embeddings; include sensitivity analyses to l, embedding dimension, and kernel widths.
- Provide more insight: attention maps, per-phenomenon breakdowns, and ablations isolating the contribution of SUB vs. MULT vs. their combination.
- Clean up references and formatting; ensure all tables/figures are present and legible.

Questions for the authors
- In Eq. (1), are Wi, Wu, bi, bu shared across Q and A, or separate per sequence? Please clarify eQ/eA definitions.
- For SNLI, what is the exact LSTM configuration and how is it integrated with the gating preproc?
- What are the CNN kernel widths, number of filters, pooling strategy, and “n” in r ∈ R^{nl}? Is CNN temporal (over positions) with feature maps of size l?
- For InsuranceQA, what is the candidate pool during training and evaluation? Do you evaluate with the full answer set, and how is Eq. (10) instantiated in that setting?
- How are long MovieQA plots handled (truncation limits, stride, memory optimization)? What are typical Q and P lengths used?
- How is padding/masking handled in attention and aggregation?
- Which GloVe variant (dimension, vocabulary) is used? Why fix embeddings, and how does performance change if they are fine-tuned?
- How are EUCLIDEAN and COSINE combined in EUCCOS given scale differences? Any normalization or learned rescaling?
- Did you try bilinear similarity, |a−h| and a⊙h concatenation with a linear layer (without ReLU) as in common sentence-pair models, or biaffine scoring?
- Please provide variance across runs and significance tests for the main claims (especially the superiority of SUB/MULT/SUBMULT+NN).
