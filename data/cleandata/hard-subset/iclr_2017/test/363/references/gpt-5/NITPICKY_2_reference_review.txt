Summary
The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for sequence matching and, more specifically, systematically evaluates a set of local comparison functions used to compare an attended representation of one sequence against tokens of the other. The model consists of (i) an order-insensitive gated preprocessing layer that reweights word embeddings, (ii) a one-directional attention mechanism, (iii) a word-level comparison function (six variants: NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN), and (iv) a CNN-based aggregator for final prediction. Experiments on four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) suggest the framework is broadly effective and that element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, and especially SUBMULT+NN) generally outperform standard neural layers or simple distance/cosine features. The work claims state-of-the-art or competitive performance across datasets and releases code.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient clarity and rigor in experimental methodology and model specification, hampering reproducibility and interpretability. (2) Limited novelty beyond prior compare-aggregate models; the main contribution—cataloging comparison functions—is incremental and undermined by missing details and inconsistencies.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: The compare-aggregate paradigm and one-directional attention have been explored in prior work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016). The primary novelty here is a systematic evaluation of local comparison functions, but related element-wise heuristics have appeared (e.g., Mou et al., 2016). The architectural differences (CNN aggregator, order-insensitive gating) are minor variations without strong theoretical motivation or ablation against equally simple alternatives (e.g., sum/mean pooling, BiLSTM aggregation). Claims of generality are not strongly substantiated beyond the choice of four tasks.
- Methodological clarity: Several definitions are ambiguous or underspecified.
  - Preprocessing uses Q ∈ R^{d×Q} and produces “Q ∈ R^{l×Q}” with the same symbol, causing confusion. The gating block replaces embeddings with σ(WiX+bi⊗e)⊙tanh(WuX+bu⊗e) but there is no residual pathway to the original embeddings; motivation and effect are not analyzed.
  - Attention: G = softmax((WgQ+bg⊗eQ)^T A). The axis of the softmax is not stated; implementation choice (column-wise vs row-wise normalization) matters. Dimensions are consistent but the text should explicitly define the normalization and masking.
  - Comparison EUCCOS yields a 2D feature [||a−h||, cos(a,h)], whose scale is very different from l-dimensional SUB/MULT; the downstream CNN configuration (filter widths, number of channels) is not specified, making it hard to see how these heterogeneous features are handled fairly.
  - Aggregation: The CNN details (kernel sizes, number of filters, pooling strategy, padding/stride) are missing. The number of windows n is mentioned but not defined or tuned. No justification for CNN vs other aggregators, and no ablation.
- Experimental rigor and fairness:
  - Critical dataset details are missing. For InsuranceQA, the candidate pool size/protocol (the standard 500-candidate setting vs smaller sampled pools) is not specified; comparability to prior reported numbers is therefore unclear. For WikiQA, the common filtering to questions with at least one correct answer is not stated. For MovieQA, plot length handling (truncation strategy, memory constraints) is omitted.
  - Training/eval details: No reporting of variance across runs, no statistical significance testing, and minimal hyperparameter details (only dropout tuned; learning rate and batch size tuned only for WikiQA). Embeddings are frozen for all tasks without justification; for SNLI the preprocessing includes an LSTM, breaking cross-task consistency.
  - Baselines are taken from the literature without reimplementation; given possible differences in data preprocessing and candidate pools, this can overstate gains. Stronger contemporaneous SNLI baselines (e.g., ESIM-like models) are not discussed; for QA, more recent attention architectures are absent.
  - Ablations are referenced (removing preprocessing or attention) but quantitative results (Table 4) are not included in the text we can evaluate; the narrative claims (e.g., attention more important for longer/length-mismatch tasks) are plausible but unsubstantiated here.
- Claims vs evidence: The paper claims state-of-the-art on several datasets and that SUBMULT+NN performs best overall; without full tables and significance, these broad claims are hard to verify. Additionally, using frozen GloVe and order-insensitive preprocessing yet surpassing order-sensitive models across tasks is surprising and warrants deeper analysis (e.g., error analysis, length stratification, sensitivity to embedding updates).
- Presentation quality: Numerous typographical, grammatical, and bibliographic issues (e.g., “Siamense,” missing years, dangling citations like “Wan et al., )”, escaped codes \Q2016\E, inconsistent author formatting) reduce professionalism and trust. Some equations misuse notation or omit operator definitions.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Unify notation (use distinct symbols for raw vs preprocessed sequences). Explicitly specify the softmax dimension in attention and provide shapes at each step.
- Provide complete CNN aggregator details and include ablations against simpler aggregators (sum/mean, BiLSTM, self-attention).
- Report all dataset protocols (splits, filtering, candidate pool sizes), truncation/padding policies, and preprocessing. Where possible, re-run strong baselines under your preprocessing, or include a sanity baseline with your codebase.
- Add training details: epoch counts, early stopping, seed control, variance over multiple runs, parameter counts per comparison function, and wall-clock/runtime complexity (especially for long plots in MovieQA).
- Analyze why element-wise operations help: e.g., visualize distributions of SUB/MULT features, study robustness to embedding quality, and report per-length/per-overlap performance.
- Improve writing and fix references; ensure all related compare-aggregate works are fairly covered.

Questions for the authors
1) InsuranceQA: What exact evaluation protocol and candidate pool size did you use? Did you use the standard 500-candidate setting? How were candidates sampled during training and evaluation?
2) WikiQA: Did you apply the standard filter to only keep questions with at least one correct answer? What tokenization/lowercasing and stopword handling were used?
3) MovieQA: What was the average/maximum plot length used? Did you truncate plots or questions/answers? How do you handle computational/memory limits for attention over long plots?
4) Attention: Along which axis is the softmax applied in G? Are attention scores masked for padding?
5) Aggregator: What are the CNN kernel sizes, number of filters, pooling strategy, and padding? How did you handle the 2D EUCCOS features vs l-dimensional features to ensure a fair comparison?
6) SNLI: Why is LSTM-based preprocessing applied only for SNLI? How does this affect cross-task comparability? What is the performance with/without LSTM on SNLI?
7) Embeddings: Why were embeddings frozen? What is the impact of fine-tuning on each dataset?
8) Robustness: How many runs did you average? Please report mean±std and significance testing for your main claims.
9) Parameter efficiency: What are the parameter counts across comparison functions (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN)? Are gains due to capacity or inductive bias?
10) Code: The paper states code is available. Does it include exact configs to reproduce all reported tables, and preprocessed data/splits?
