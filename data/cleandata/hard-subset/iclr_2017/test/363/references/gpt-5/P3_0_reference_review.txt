Summary and contributions
This paper investigates a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences. The model aligns one sequence to another via attention, compares each token representation aj to an attention-weighted representation hj of the other sequence using a chosen comparison function f, and aggregates the sequence of comparison vectors with a CNN to predict a label. The key contributions are: (1) a systematic study of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and their concatenation followed by NN), with the hypothesis that simple element-wise operators strike a good balance between inductive bias and capacity; (2) a demonstration that the same architecture, with minimal task-specific adaptation, performs strongly across four different sequence matching tasks (SNLI, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, MovieQA). Empirically, element-wise comparison (especially SUBMULT+NN) generally outperforms more flexible NN/NTN and simple EUCCOS, and the model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on all datasets. The authors release code.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Solid empirical evidence across diverse tasks that clarifies the impact of the comparison function, yielding a useful, generalizable design insight; (2) A simple, efficient architecture that performs on par or better than more complex models, with ablations supporting the role of attention and preprocessing.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a central subproblem in pairwise sequence modeling: how to compare aligned token representations before aggregation. While compare-aggregate frameworks are not new (e.g., Parikh et al. 2016; Wang & Jiang 2016; He & Lin 2016), prior work did not isolate and evaluate comparison functions systematically across tasks. The motivation to search for comparison functions with the right inductive bias for similarity is well placed in the literature and timely.

- Methodology: The architecture is clean: (i) a light, order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” per word; (ii) one-directional attention from A to Q to form hj; (iii) token-level comparison via f; (iv) CNN aggregation. The comparison functions span a spectrum from highly parameterized (NN/NTN) to geometric (EUCCOS) to element-wise (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The element-wise operators have an appealing interpretation: they retain dimension-wise information underlying L2 distance and cosine similarity, respectively.

- Empirical support: The model is evaluated on four datasets with distinct characteristics (entailment vs QA; short vs long contexts), and shows: (a) strong or SOTA results overall; (b) consistent advantage of element-wise comparisons, with SUBMULT+NN usually best; (c) useful ablations indicating the preprocessing and attention layers matter, with attention more critical when sequence lengths differ substantially (MovieQA, InsuranceQA). This cross-task consistency strengthens the central claim.

- Practicality: The model avoids expensive sequence encoders for long contexts (except SNLI), uses simple matrix ops for attention and comparison, and aggregates via a shallow CNN, suggesting favorable efficiency. Code release enhances reproducibility.

Concerns and limitations
- Novelty: The architectural template is not novel, and one-directional attention plus token-wise comparison followed by pooling/aggregation is close to prior decomposable attention/interaction models. The main novelty is the systematic evaluation of f and the cross-task demonstration. This is incremental but worthwhile.

- Missing experimental detail and rigor: Key implementation specifics are underspecified (e.g., CNN kernel widths, number of filters, stride/pooling, training epochs, early stopping, seed variance). There is no discussion of statistical significance or variance across runs. For InsuranceQA/WikiQA, evaluation protocols (candidate pool size, negative sampling) should be explicit to ensure comparability with reported baselines.

- Contextualization: For three tasks, the preprocessing is order-insensitive, and there is no contextual encoder before comparison. While results are strong, this choice may conflate exact match and shallow similarity with true semantic alignment; additional experiments with contextual encoders (e.g., BiLSTM before attention) would clarify the trade-off between efficiency and accuracy.

- Aggregation choice: The paper adopts a one-layer CNN but does not ablate against simpler alternatives (sum/mean pooling, attentive pooling) or more expressive ones (BiLSTM aggregation), leaving open whether gains are due to f or the aggregator.

- Baselines and contemporaneous models: On SNLI, it would help to include or discuss closely related ESIM/Decomp-Attention variants under matched settings. For MovieQA, clarify whether competing methods use the same plot synopsis setup.

Constructive feedback for improvement
- Report full implementation details: CNN filter sizes and counts, pooling, dropout rates for all layers, optimization schedule, early stopping criteria, runtime/parameter counts per comparison function, and memory footprint.

- Add robustness analysis: Multiple random seeds with mean±std; significance tests for key comparisons; learning curves; sensitivity to fixed vs fine-tuned embeddings.

- Strengthen ablations: (i) aggregator ablation (sum/mean, attentive pooling, BiLSTM) to isolate the role of f; (ii) bi-directional attention vs one-directional; (iii) adding lightweight contextualization (e.g., 1D CNN or BiLSTM) before attention for long contexts.

- Fairness and protocols: For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, detail candidate set sizes, filtering, and whether answer-only or question-only heuristics could explain gains; ensure baselines follow identical splits and pre-processing.

- Analysis: Provide qualitative/error analysis beyond two visualizations—e.g., which phenomena benefit from SUB vs MULT; failure modes; sensitivity to lexical overlap vs paraphrase.

Questions for the authors
1) What are the exact CNN hyperparameters (kernel widths, number of filters per width, pooling) and their impact? Did you try sum/mean or attentive pooling; how do results compare?

2) How many runs per configuration were used? Please report variance and statistical tests for the principal claims (e.g., SUBMULT+NN > NN/NTN on each dataset).

3) For InsuranceQA and WikiQA, what are the candidate pool sizes at test time, and are your settings identical to the reported baselines? Any reimplementation differences (tokenization, OOV handling)?

4) Did you evaluate bi-directional attention (A-to-Q and Q-to-A)? If so, does it change the relative ranking of comparison functions?

5) What is the computational cost and parameter count per comparison function (NN, NTN, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN)? Does the improved accuracy of SUBMULT+NN come with notable cost?

6) On SNLI, when adding LSTM to preprocessing, do element-wise comparisons still dominate? How sensitive are results to using contextual encoders on the other datasets?

7) You freeze GloVe embeddings. Does fine-tuning change the relative performance of f? If not, this strengthens the claim that the comparison function is the key driver.

Overall, while the novelty is incremental, the paper provides clear, actionable insights about comparison functions in compare-aggregate models, validated across multiple tasks, and would be of interest to the community.
