Summary
The paper studies a general “compare-aggregate” framework for matching text sequences. It instantiates a pipeline with (i) a lightweight gating-based preprocessing layer, (ii) one-directional attention to align words across sequences, (iii) a word-level comparison function f, and (iv) a CNN aggregator. The main thrust is a systematic comparison of six comparison functions, including two simple element-wise operators (SUB and MULT) and their combination (SUBMULT+NN). The model is evaluated across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), achieving strong or SOTA results in several cases. The key empirical finding is that element-wise subtraction and multiplication—either alone or combined—consistently perform very well, rivaling or outperforming more parameterized neural comparison modules.

Decision: Reject
Reason: The technical novelty is limited. The overall architecture follows prior “compare-aggregate” designs, and the central new ingredient—element-wise SUB/MULT comparisons—has appeared before. The contribution is largely an empirical consolidation across tasks and a modest architectural variant (CNN aggregation), rather than a new modeling concept or learning principle.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Matching two sequences for tasks like entailment, answer selection, and machine comprehension. The paper’s concrete question is which word-level comparison function best serves a compare-aggregate architecture.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly situates itself alongside match-LSTM, decomposable attention, and pairwise word-interaction models, and motivates focusing on the comparison function. However, it underplays prior use of element-wise operations for sentence pair tasks (e.g., Mou et al., 2016) and related similarity functions (cosine, Euclidean, dot product) that are already common.
- Do the results support the claims? Empirically, yes: the model is competitive/strong on four datasets, and the ablations indicate the value of attention and preprocessing layers. The cross-task study of f is useful. That said, several choices complicate the claim of generality:
  - The preprocessing is order-insensitive for three datasets but LSTM-enhanced for SNLI, weakening the “single general structure” claim.
  - The EUCCOS comparator yields a 2D vector while others return l-dimensional outputs; the fairness of aggregation (via the same CNN) could be discussed more thoroughly (e.g., capacity differences).
  - Comparisons to the strongest contemporaneous baselines are uneven. For SNLI, only some well-known models are cited; more recent or stronger variants (e.g., bi-directional attention/ESIM-style components) are not included. Statistical significance and variance are not reported.
  - The analysis is mostly qualitative; there is no theoretical insight explaining why element-wise operators work so well, beyond intuitive connections to cosine/Euclidean.

Novelty and significance
- The compare-aggregate paradigm, attention, and CNN/LSTM aggregators are established. The central empirical observation—that simple element-wise SUB/MULT can be highly effective—is valuable but incremental and has precursors in the literature.
- The architecture choice (one-directional attention, CNN aggregator) does not introduce a new modeling principle.
- The multi-task evaluation is a strength, but primarily an engineering consolidation rather than a new direction.

Overall, this is a solid empirical paper with clear writing and careful experiments, but it does not introduce a sufficiently novel or bold idea to warrant acceptance under a novelty-leaning standard.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and strengthen the literature positioning of SUB/MULT (e.g., beyond Mou et al., discuss bilinear/tri-linear, parameterized similarity, and heuristic feature combos widely used in sentence pair models).
- Report variance and statistical significance across multiple runs; include model size and training time comparisons to highlight the efficiency of simple comparators.
- Unify the preprocessing across tasks or justify the SNLI-specific LSTM variant; quantify how much of the SNLI gain comes from adding order-sensitive preprocessing.
- Compare CNN aggregation against simpler sum/mean pooling and against RNN/Transformer-based aggregators with matched capacity.
- Include bi-directional attention and/or cross-attention baselines to test whether the observed advantage of SUB/MULT persists when alignment is stronger.
- Expand the ablation on attention directionality and on the gating layer itself (e.g., removing σ or tanh, sharing parameters across Q/A).
- Provide error analyses across tasks to illuminate when different f functions win/lose; probe length sensitivity and robustness to paraphrase/lexical variation.
- Clean up references and formatting glitches.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are the findings about SUB/MULT to embedding fine-tuning? You freeze GloVe; do the rankings of f change if embeddings are updated?
- Can you report mean±std over multiple seeds for the main comparisons? Are the differences between SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN and NN/NTN statistically significant?
- For EUCCOS (2D output), did you control for representational capacity in the aggregator (e.g., expanding channels) to ensure a fair comparison?
- Why one-directional attention? Does bi-directional (premise↔hypothesis) change the relative effectiveness of the comparison functions?
- On SNLI, how much does the LSTM-based preprocessing alone improve performance compared to the order-insensitive version? Please provide a clear ablation.
- Did you evaluate bilinear or parameterized similarity (a^T W h) as an intermediate point between EUCCOS and NN? If so, how does it compare?
- How does the model scale with sequence length in MovieQA plot synopses? Any memory/time trade-offs compared to more compressive approaches?

If the authors can strengthen the baselines, add rigorous variance/significance reporting, unify the architecture across tasks, and provide deeper analyses (or theory) explaining why element-wise comparisons excel, the work would be much more compelling.
