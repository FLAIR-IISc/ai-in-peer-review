Summary
The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching text sequences and evaluates six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN). The model uses (i) a simple gating-based preprocessing of word embeddings, (ii) a one-directional attention to align one sequence to the other, (iii) word-level comparison, and (iv) CNN-based aggregation. The authors test on four datasets spanning entailment (SNLI) and QA/answer selection (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA), report strong or state-of-the-art results on three of them, and conclude that simple element-wise comparisons (especially SUB and MULT, with or without a small NN) are robust and competitive across tasks. Code is released.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Limited novelty relative to established compare-aggregate and heuristic-matching designs; the strongest comparison function (SUBMULT+NN) closely mirrors widely used element-wise features in prior work. (2) The empirical support is not rigorous enough: baselines are not re-implemented, comparisons are not controlled for parameter count, and ablations/hyperparameter sensitivity and statistical significance are insufficient to substantiate the cross-task generality claim.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and placement in literature: The paper’s core framework and the central idea of comparing aligned word vectors with element-wise operations are well established (e.g., decomposable attention; heuristic matching with [a, b, a−b, a⊙b] in NLI models, and earlier sentence-pair CNN/RNN works). SUB and MULT are explicitly credited to prior work. The proposed SUBMULT+NN (essentially ReLU(W·[ (a−h)⊙(a−h); a⊙h ]+b) or similar) is very close to the standard heuristic feature set used in ESIM-style models. The architectural differences (one-directional attention, CNN aggregation, order-insensitive preprocessing) seem incremental choices rather than conceptually new contributions.

- Methodological rigor and fairness:
  - The comparisons between comparison functions are not parameter-controlled. NN/NTN introduce many more parameters than SUB/MULT; without matching capacity (or regularization strength) the conclusion that element-wise methods “work best” could be confounded by overfitting or underfitting. A fair test would match the output dimensionality and total parameter budget across variants or apply weight decay/early stopping tuned per variant.
  - Baselines are taken from reported numbers only. This risks mismatch in data preprocessing, word embeddings, and training budgets. No strong, re-implemented baselines with identical embeddings and optimization are provided.
  - On SNLI, stronger established baselines (e.g., ESIM/Decomposable Attention with BiLSTM encoders and bidirectional cross-attention) are not matched in architecture or capacity. The paper cites EBIM+TreeLSTM but does not present a like-for-like comparison.
  - Training and tuning are uneven: authors tune dropout (and LR/batch for WikiQA only) and fix GloVe without fine-tuning. It is unclear whether each comparison function and dataset received comparable tuning. No variance across runs or significance tests are reported.
  - For ranking (WikiQA), the loss is not clearly tailored to ranking (e.g., pairwise/listwise objectives); yet evaluation is MAP/MRR. Using a softmax over candidates may be mismatched to the metric.

- Claims vs evidence:
  - The central claim of cross-task generality is plausible but not conclusively established. The four datasets vary, but the experimental protocol is not sufficiently standardized across tasks, and key design choices (e.g., order-insensitive preprocessing for long sequences) are not compared with established order-sensitive alternatives under equal conditions.
  - The reported ablation insights (attention vs preprocessing importance) are interesting but lack detailed numbers and significance, and conclusions about context insensitivity could be sensitive to the chosen datasets and aggregation.

- Technical clarity:
  - Some components need clearer specification: softmax normalization axis in attention, CNN filter widths/number of filters, and how output dimensionality is matched across comparison functions.
  - For InsuranceQA and MovieQA, evaluation details (candidate pool sizes, official splits, negative sampling, whether listwise scoring uses all candidates) are underspecified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Control capacity across comparison functions. For example, set the comparison output dimension to a fixed size for all variants and tune regularization uniformly. Report parameter counts and training curves.
- Re-implement one or two strong baselines per task under the same embedding initialization, preprocessing, and optimization. For SNLI, include a competitive ESIM/Decomposable Attention baseline with bidirectional attention and BiLSTM aggregation.
- Report mean/std over multiple runs and statistical significance, and provide hyperparameter sensitivity (dropout, learning rate, embedding fine-tuning).
- Explore bidirectional attention and stronger aggregators (BiLSTM vs CNN) in a controlled ablation; this will help disentangle where gains come from.
- For ranking tasks, consider pairwise/listwise losses aligned with MAP/MRR, and compare to strong learning-to-rank baselines.
- Clarify evaluation protocols for InsuranceQA (Test1/Test2, size of candidate pool) and MovieQA (use of official splits, how plots are truncated/processed).
- Provide qualitative analyses beyond max-CNN activations, e.g., attention maps and error analyses across phenomena (lexical overlap, paraphrase, negation).

Questions for the authors
1. How do you control for differing parameter counts across comparison functions? Can you report parameter counts and rerun with matched output dimensionality and tuned regularization to ensure a fair comparison?
2. What are the exact attention softmax normalization details and CNN hyperparameters (filter widths, counts)? Are these shared across datasets and variants?
3. For SNLI, how does your best model compare to a matched ESIM-style baseline that uses [a, h, a−h, a⊙h] and BiLSTM aggregation under identical training settings?
4. For WikiQA, what loss do you optimize? Have you tried pairwise/listwise ranking losses, and how do they affect MAP/MRR?
5. For InsuranceQA, which official test sets and candidate pool sizes are used? Do you use the full pool during training/inference or sampled negatives?
6. Can you provide results with fine-tuned embeddings and with bidirectional attention to test whether the “element-wise is best” conclusion holds under stronger encoders?
7. Please include multiple runs with mean/std and significance tests for key comparisons and ablations.
