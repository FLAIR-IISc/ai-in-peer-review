Summary
The paper studies a “compare-aggregate” framework for matching two text sequences and evaluates it across four tasks/datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The model computes attention from one sequence to another, compares word-level representations using one of six comparison functions (NN, NTN, Euclidean+cosine, element-wise subtraction, element-wise multiplication, and a combination of subtraction/multiplication followed by NN), and aggregates with a CNN for final prediction. The core claims are: (1) the compare-aggregate framework is effective across diverse sequence matching problems; (2) simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) are competitive or superior to more flexible neural comparison units; and (3) the authors provide a systematic comparison of comparison functions and ablations indicating the roles of preprocessing and attention. Code is released.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: Limited novelty over established compare-aggregate architectures and insufficient experimental rigor to support broad claims of generality and superiority.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: The proposed architecture is very close to prior compare-aggregate models (e.g., decomposable attention, Parikh et al., 2016; match-LSTM, Wang & Jiang, 2016). The main differences—choice of comparison functions (element-wise ops) and a CNN aggregator—are incremental, and similar element-wise heuristics have been used in earlier pairwise sentence models. The paper asserts generality across tasks, but the changes relative to established baselines are modest and not convincingly isolated.

- Methodological rigor and fairness:
  - Baselines are taken from reported numbers rather than re-implemented under a unified training regime. This makes it difficult to assess fairness in data preprocessing, tokenization, embeddings, and hyperparameter budgets.
  - No statistical significance testing is reported. Given modest margins typical in these tasks, effect sizes and confidence intervals are essential.
  - Capacity mismatch across comparison functions: EUCCOS outputs 2-D features, while others produce l-dimensional vectors (l=150). Feeding these into the same CNN confounds conclusions because the downstream capacity differs. A fair comparison should either (i) project all comparison outputs to a fixed dimension before aggregation or (ii) adjust the aggregator to normalize capacity.
  - Word embeddings are frozen. While this may simplify training, it may also disadvantage certain comparison functions and reduce comparability to baselines that fine-tune embeddings. At minimum, show results with/without fine-tuning.
  - The purportedly order-insensitive preprocessing is replaced by LSTM only for SNLI, undermining the claim of a single general architecture. The impact of this deviation is not quantified.

- Experimental coverage and analysis:
  - The choice of one-directional attention is not justified against two-directional (as in decomposable attention). An ablation comparing one- vs two-way attention would directly test whether the architectural simplification harms/helps.
  - The aggregation choice (CNN) is not compared to simpler sum/mean pooling used in earlier work. This leaves open whether gains come from the aggregator rather than the comparison function.
  - Hyperparameter tuning is minimally described and appears uneven across datasets (e.g., only WikiQA tunes LR and batch size). It is unclear whether dev sets were used consistently and whether early stopping/regularization were harmonized.
  - No efficiency analysis (speed/memory) relative to prior models is provided, despite claims that element-wise functions are simpler; empirical training/inference cost would strengthen the practical value argument.

- Claims vs evidence:
  - The conclusion that SUB/MULT are best “overall” is not strongly supported without capacity-controlled comparisons and significance tests. The text acknowledges EUCCOS is nearly best on MovieQA; yet the narrative emphasizes SUBMULT+NN as generally best without comprehensive per-task statistical backing.
  - The generality claim is limited by only four datasets, two of which are closely related QA selection tasks, and by architectural changes across tasks (SNLI uses LSTM preprocessing).

Constructive feedback
- Re-implement strong baselines under a common codebase and training protocol. Include decomposable attention, match-LSTM, ABCNN, and a bidirectional attention baseline. Report results with identical embeddings, tokenization, and hyperparameter search spaces.
- Control for capacity. Ensure all comparison functions emit the same dimensionality before aggregation (e.g., via a shared projection), or scale the aggregator accordingly. Then perform significance tests across multiple random seeds.
- Provide ablations for: one- vs two-directional attention; CNN aggregation vs sum/mean pooling; frozen vs fine-tuned embeddings; and order-sensitive vs order-insensitive preprocessing across all tasks (not only SNLI).
- Report training/inference time and parameter counts to substantiate the “simple yet effective” narrative.
- Clarify the role of the preprocessing gate: quantify its contribution vs a simple identity or LayerNorm; analyze gate patterns (e.g., on stopwords) to support the intuition.
- Improve clarity on task-specific adaptations for MovieQA (how Q/P/A are jointly handled, sequence length constraints, and attention masking) and justify design choices.

Questions for the authors
1. How large are the improvements over baselines under a controlled, single-codebase setting with identical preprocessing and tuning? Can you report mean ± std over multiple runs and significance tests?
2. How do results change if you equalize the dimensionality of comparison outputs (e.g., project EUCCOS to l dims via a learned layer before CNN)?
3. Why restrict to one-directional attention? What is the delta if you adopt two-way attention as in Parikh et al. (2016)?
4. What is the effect of fine-tuning word embeddings? Do SUB/MULT still outperform NN/NTN when embeddings are updated?
5. For SNLI, using LSTM in preprocessing deviates from the “order-insensitive” story. Can you report results with the same preprocessing used in other tasks, and quantify the trade-off?
6. How sensitive are results to the choice of CNN aggregator (kernel sizes, number of filters)? Would a simpler sum/avg pooling achieve similar performance?
7. What are the training/inference costs (time, FLOPs, parameters) per dataset, and how do they compare to decomposable attention and match-LSTM?
8. Can you provide error analyses illustrating where SUB/MULT helps over NN/NTN, beyond the qualitative visualizations?

Overall, the paper addresses a relevant question and the cross-task perspective is valuable. However, the novelty is limited and the empirical evidence, as currently presented, is not sufficiently rigorous to support acceptance.
