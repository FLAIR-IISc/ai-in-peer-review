1) Summary of what the paper claims/contributes (being positive)
- The paper studies a general compare-aggregate architecture for matching two text sequences. It instantiates a pipeline with (i) a lightweight, order-insensitive gating “preprocessing” of word embeddings, (ii) one-way attention from one sequence to the other to produce aligned context vectors, (iii) a word-level comparison function f(aj, hj), and (iv) a CNN to aggregate token-level comparison vectors for final classification.
- The core contribution is a systematic evaluation of six comparison functions, including two simple element-wise options (SUB: squared difference; MULT: element-wise product) and a composite SUBMULT+NN, alongside standard NN, neural tensor network, and Euclidean+cosine similarity.
- The model is evaluated across four tasks/datasets of different flavors (SNLI entailment; InsuranceQA answer selection; WikiQA ranking; MovieQA plot-based multiple choice), showing strong or state-of-the-art results on three and competitive results on SNLI. The paper concludes that element-wise comparisons (especially SUB and MULT, and their combination with a small NN) are robust, simple, and effective for sequence matching.

2) Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- Strength: Clear, well-motivated study isolating the comparison function within the compare-aggregate framework, with broad empirical validation across four datasets and strong results. The finding that element-wise comparisons are strong baselines is valuable and practically useful.
- Limitation: Conceptual novelty is moderate given prior compare-aggregate work; some design choices (unidirectional attention, CNN aggregation, order-insensitive preprocessing) are insufficiently justified or ablated; empirical rigor (e.g., variance, significance, fairness vs. baselines) could be stronger.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The paper is well situated in the family of models that avoid compressing entire sequences into single vectors and instead align and compare local units (match-LSTM, decomposable attention, pairwise word interactions). Focusing on the comparison function is a meaningful angle: prior work often defaulted to generic MLPs or simple similarities without systematically assessing trade-offs between expressiveness and inductive bias. The choice to emphasize element-wise operators is well argued (they retain per-dimension information related to Euclidean/cosine while being parameter-light).
- Methodological clarity: The architecture is described clearly. The preprocessing gate is essentially a per-token multiplicative gate (sigmoid times tanh), analogous to highway-lite mechanisms, and is order-insensitive (except for SNLI where LSTM is used). Attention is one-directional (A attends to Q). The six comparison functions are defined precisely. Aggregation via 1-layer CNN is simple and plausible. Code is promised for reproducibility.
- Empirical support:
  - Breadth: Results on four distinct tasks adds weight to the “general framework” claim.
  - Findings: SUBMULT+NN often performs best, while EUCCOS and the parameter-free element-wise SUB/MULT are surprisingly strong. The ablation claims (importance of attention for large length mismatch; usefulness of preprocessing for shorter sequences) are plausible and align with intuition.
  - Strength: Achieving or surpassing existing reported results on InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and MovieQA indicates the approach is competitive; being “very competitive” on SNLI is also encouraging, given that stronger bespoke architectures exist.
- Limitations/concerns:
  - Novelty: The overall architecture is close to decomposable attention and other compare-aggregate approaches; the main novelty is the systematic investigation of f and the cross-task validation. While practically valuable, the conceptual advance is incremental.
  - Missing details: The CNN aggregation hyperparameters (filter widths, number of filters) are not specified; the precise attention normalization axis is implied rather than spelled out; SNLI’s LSTM “incorporation” into preprocessing is under-specified.
  - Rigor: No statistical significance or variance across seeds; limited hyperparameter tuning discussion; no runtime/parameter count comparisons (important if promoting parameter-light comparison functions). Baselines are taken from the literature without re-implementation; it is unclear if embedding updates, preprocessing, or training regimes match, raising fairness concerns.
  - Design choices: Only unidirectional attention is used; a direct comparison to bi-directional attention or RNN-based aggregation (vs. CNN) would help isolate where gains come from. The gating is order-insensitive in three tasks; stronger context encoders might change the relative ranking of comparison functions.
  - Analysis: Figure-based visualizations are helpful but qualitative; more systematic error analysis (e.g., lexical overlap vs. paraphrase, negation, length sensitivity) would strengthen claims about what the comparison functions capture.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Report means and standard deviations over multiple random seeds; provide statistical tests on key improvements, especially when differences are small across comparison functions.
- Detail CNN aggregation hyperparameters and provide an ablation comparing CNN vs. sum/avg pooling vs. RNN/Transformer aggregation. Similarly, compare unidirectional vs. bidirectional attention.
- Add a parameter count and wall-clock/runtime comparison to substantiate the “good compromise” claim for SUB/MULT vs. NN/NTN.
- Clarify the SNLI variant: where and how LSTMs are applied; whether embeddings are fine-tuned; whether preprocessing still includes gating before/after LSTM.
- Expand fairness discussion vs. baselines: indicate whether baselines fine-tune embeddings, use additional features, or different training data; optionally include a strong in-house baseline with identical embeddings and training regime (e.g., Siamese BiLSTMs with element-wise features).
- Provide error analyses stratified by lexical overlap, length ratios, negation/syntax, and OOV rate to better understand when element-wise comparisons shine or fail.
- Consider including a bi-attention variant and/or pointer-style aggregation to test whether the comparison-function conclusions hold under stronger alignment modules.

Questions for the authors
- Attention details: Over which dimension is the softmax in G applied (per aj over Q columns)? Did you try bi-directional attention, and if so, how did it affect the relative performance of the comparison functions?
- Aggregation: What filter widths and number of filters were used in the CNN? Did you try simple pooling or RNN aggregation? Does the ranking of comparison functions change with a different aggregator?
- Preprocessing: For SNLI, please specify precisely how LSTMs are incorporated into the preprocessing. Did you compare against highway layers or contextual encoders without gates?
- Training/setup: Are word embeddings always frozen across all datasets? How sensitive are results to fine-tuning embeddings? Please report variance across multiple runs.
- Fairness: For key baselines (especially on InsuranceQA/WikiQA), do they fine-tune embeddings or use additional features? Can you include a matched in-house baseline to control for these factors?
- Efficiency: How do the comparison functions differ in parameter count and speed? Is SUB/MULT materially faster or less memory intensive?
- Generality: Would the observed conclusions (SUB/MULT being robust) hold when order-sensitive contextual encoders (BiLSTM/CNN/Transformer) are used before attention on all tasks?
