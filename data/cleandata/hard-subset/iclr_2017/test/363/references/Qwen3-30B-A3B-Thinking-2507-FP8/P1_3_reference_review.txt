

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes two significant contributions to the field of sequence matching in NLP. First, it demonstrates that the "compare-aggregate" framework is broadly effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, not just limited to textual entailment as previous work suggested. The authors validate this by applying their model to four distinct datasets: MovieQA (machine comprehension), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (question answering), and SNLI (textual entailment). Second, the paper systematically evaluates six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, revealing that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across multiple tasks. The authors provide a compelling argument for why these simple yet effective comparison functions work well, based on their alignment with the semantic similarity nature of sequence matching problems.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons: The paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to a well-established area of NLP. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple tasks is novel and addresses a significant gap in the literature. The empirical results are strong and well-supported, with the model achieving state-of-the-art or competitive results across four different sequence matching tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly identify limitations in previous "compare-aggregate" models: their narrow scope (tested on only one or two tasks) and lack of systematic evaluation of comparison functions. They position their work as filling these gaps by testing their model across four different datasets and comparing six different comparison functions, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that they find surprisingly effective.

The empirical evaluation is rigorous. The authors use appropriate datasets representing diverse sequence matching tasks, employ standard evaluation metrics for each task, and conduct thorough ablation studies to understand the importance of different model components (preprocessing vs. attention layers). The results consistently show that their general "compare-aggregate" structure outperforms baselines across three of the four datasets, with competitive performance on the fourth.

The claim that element-wise comparison functions work particularly well is well-supported. The paper provides clear evidence that SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN consistently outperform more complex comparison functions (NN, NTN) across multiple datasets. The authors provide a thoughtful justification for why these simple functions might work better than more complex neural approaches—because they preserve information about the original vectors while capturing the semantic similarity intuition central to sequence matching.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions for improvement:

1. **Statistical significance**: The paper doesn't include statistical significance testing for the improvements over baselines. Adding t-tests or similar would strengthen the empirical claims.

2. **Computational efficiency**: The paper mentions that their element-wise operations are computationally efficient, but doesn't provide actual runtime comparisons with more complex comparison functions. This would be valuable for practitioners.

3. **Ablation study details**: The ablation study (Table 4) shows that removing preprocessing or attention layers hurts performance, but it would be helpful to see how much performance is lost at different points in the model, not just the complete removal.

4. **Qualitative analysis**: The paper mentions visualizing the max values in convolutional layers (Figure 2), but a more detailed qualitative analysis of what the model is learning (e.g., showing examples where SUB/MULT outperforms other functions) would strengthen the argument for why these simple functions work.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role." Could you elaborate on why this might be the case? Is it because attention helps bridge the length gap between sequences?

2. The authors claim that their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Could you provide more insight into why word-by-word comparison might be more important than contextual information in these tasks?

3. The paper mentions that SUB is related to Euclidean distance but preserves more information. Could you provide a more formal justification for why this preservation of information leads to better performance?

4. The authors use the same model structure for all four datasets with minimal task-specific modifications. Could you discuss the challenges you faced in making the model generalizable across these different tasks?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature by demonstrating the broad applicability of the compare-aggregate framework and providing evidence that simple element-wise comparison functions can outperform more complex approaches. The empirical results are compelling and the methodological approach is sound, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
