

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks across multiple NLP domains, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions for word-level matching. The authors test their model on four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) and find that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. They demonstrate that the compare-aggregate framework is effective across multiple sequence matching problems, and provide valuable empirical insights about comparison functions.

## Decision: Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The core "compare-aggregate" framework is not novel - it has been previously proposed by Wang & Jiang (2016b), He & Lin (2016), and Parikh et al. (2016). The paper does not introduce a fundamentally new approach or conceptual breakthrough.

2. The main contribution is a systematic empirical evaluation of comparison functions within an existing framework, which constitutes an incremental contribution rather than a significant new concept or direction. While valuable, this does not meet ICLR's threshold for novelty.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's contribution is methodologically sound but lacks the boldness and novelty ICLR prioritizes. The authors correctly identify a gap in the literature regarding the systematic evaluation of comparison functions, but their work doesn't represent a substantial shift in how sequence matching is approached. 

The paper makes a valid observation that element-wise operations work well, but this is a practical insight rather than a conceptual advancement. As a reviewer who values novel ideas over thorough methodological validation, I find the paper's contribution to be incrementally valuable rather than transformative. The paper doesn't introduce new theoretical insights, a fundamentally different architecture, or a paradigm shift in how sequence matching is conceptualized.

The authors do test their framework on four different datasets, which is commendable, but this merely demonstrates the framework's generalizability rather than introducing a new framework. The paper is essentially "applying an existing framework to multiple tasks and evaluating its components," which is a common practice but doesn't rise to the level of novelty ICLR seeks.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a stronger theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work well, rather than just empirical results. A deeper analysis of how these operations capture semantic relationships would strengthen the contribution.

2. The paper claims that "our model following the 'compare-aggregate' framework is very effective," but it's unclear whether the effectiveness stems from the framework itself or from the specific comparison functions they tested. A more careful analysis of what makes the framework effective would be valuable.

3. The paper would be more impactful if it discussed how these findings could lead to new research directions, rather than just presenting results. For instance, how might these insights inform the design of future sequence matching models?

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you differentiate the novelty of this work from the previous "compare-aggregate" models by Wang & Jiang (2016b), He & Lin (2016), and Parikh et al. (2016)? What specific new conceptual contribution does this paper make?

2. The paper states that "a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication works the best." Could you elaborate on why these simple operations outperform more complex neural network-based approaches, beyond empirical observation? Is there a theoretical basis for this finding?

3. The paper tests on four datasets but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of why the framework works better on some tasks than others. What are the specific characteristics of the sequence matching problems that make the compare-aggregate framework particularly effective? This would help establish whether the framework has a broader theoretical justification beyond empirical success.
