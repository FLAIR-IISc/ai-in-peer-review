

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, focusing on systematic evaluation of different comparison functions that operate at the word level. The authors claim two main contributions: (1) demonstrating the general effectiveness of their framework across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), and (2) showing that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform more standard neural network-based comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS) across these tasks. The authors present a model that follows the compare-aggregate framework with six different comparison functions, and report that SUBMULT+NN achieves the best results overall.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its core claim that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) are generally superior for sequence matching tasks. The experimental evaluation lacks rigor, statistical significance testing, and adequate theoretical justification for the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary claim—that element-wise comparison functions work better than standard neural network-based approaches—lacks sufficient empirical support. While the authors evaluate six comparison functions across four datasets, they fail to provide:

1. **Statistical significance testing**: The paper reports accuracy scores without indicating whether the differences between comparison functions are statistically significant. For example, the paper claims SUBMULT+NN "is the best in general" but doesn't provide p-values or confidence intervals to support this claim. Without statistical validation, the reported performance differences could be due to random variation rather than meaningful differences in model effectiveness.

2. **Adequate ablation studies**: The authors claim that "some simple comparison functions can achieve better performance than the neural networks," but provide minimal analysis of *why* this might be the case. The paper doesn't investigate how the dimensionality of the embedding space affects the performance of different comparison functions, nor does it explore the relationship between dataset characteristics (e.g., sequence length, vocabulary size) and the relative performance of different comparison functions.

3. **Comparison to state-of-the-art baselines**: The baselines used for comparison (especially for SNLI) are outdated. The paper compares against models from 2015-2016, while the state-of-the-art on SNLI has advanced significantly with transformer-based models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa). This undermines the paper's claim of "state-of-the-art performance."

4. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The authors claim that SUB and MULT are "a good compromise between highly flexible functions using standard neural network models and highly restrictive functions based on cosine similarity and/or Euclidean distance," but provide no theoretical justification for why this specific form of compromise would work better. The paper cites Mou et al. (2016) for using SUB and MULT, but doesn't explain why these would be particularly effective for sequence matching tasks.

5. **Inconsistent implementation**: The paper states that their model "can be applied to different tasks" but admits to tuning parameters for different datasets (e.g., "For WikiQA, which is a relatively small dataset, we also tune the learning rate and the batch size"). This contradicts the paper's claim of a "general" framework, as it suggests the model is not truly task-agnostic.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this paper for potential future submission, the authors should:

1. **Conduct rigorous statistical analysis**: Perform paired t-tests or Wilcoxon signed-rank tests to determine whether the performance differences between comparison functions are statistically significant.

2. **Expand the ablation studies**: Investigate why certain comparison functions work better than others. For example, analyze how the dimensionality of the embedding space affects the performance of different comparison functions, or how the relative lengths of sequences in the dataset impact the choice of comparison function.

3. **Update the baselines**: Compare against more recent state-of-the-art models, particularly transformer-based approaches that have become dominant in sequence matching tasks.

4. **Provide theoretical justification**: Offer a more compelling argument for why element-wise operations might be particularly effective for sequence matching tasks, potentially drawing on insights from information theory or semantic similarity metrics.

5. **Clarify the "general" framework claim**: Demonstrate that the same model structure with the same hyperparameters works across all tasks without task-specific tuning, or explicitly acknowledge the need for task-specific adaptation.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide statistical significance testing for the performance differences reported in Table 3? Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the claimed superiority of SUBMULT+NN is meaningful.

2. Why does the paper report that the simplest comparison function (EUCCOS) achieves "nearly the best performance" on MovieQA, while element-wise functions work best on WikiQA? What specific characteristics of these datasets explain these differences?

3. Given that the authors acknowledge their model requires different hyperparameters for different datasets (e.g., "For WikiQA, which is a relatively small dataset, we also tune the learning rate and the batch size"), how can they claim to have demonstrated a "general" framework for sequence matching?

4. How do the authors reconcile their claim that "the preprocessing layer and the attention layer for word selection to be important" with their observation that "our model can still outperform the previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing layer"? This seems contradictory.
