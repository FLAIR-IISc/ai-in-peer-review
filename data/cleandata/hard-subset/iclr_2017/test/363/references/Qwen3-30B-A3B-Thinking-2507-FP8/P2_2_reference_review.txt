

# Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching problems in NLP, with two key contributions: (1) demonstrating the framework's effectiveness across four different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), and (2) systematically evaluating six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations. The authors argue that previous work on this framework was limited to one or two tasks and didn't properly evaluate comparison functions. They introduce two novel element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) that outperform neural network-based approaches across multiple datasets, with SUBMULT+NN (combining SUB and MULT with a neural network) achieving the best overall performance.

## 2. Decision

I recommend *weak acceptance* (borderline accept). The paper makes a reasonable contribution by demonstrating the generalizability of the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple tasks and providing systematic evaluation of comparison functions. However, the novelty is somewhat limited, and the experimental evaluation could be more rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Strengths:**
- The empirical validation across four distinct tasks is a significant contribution. Previous work on the "compare-aggregate" framework had been limited to one or two tasks, so this generalization study is valuable.
- The systematic comparison of six different comparison functions is well-motivated and provides useful insights for future work. The finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform well is particularly interesting and counterintuitive to some extent.
- The results are generally strong, with the model outperforming baselines on three of the four datasets, and achieving competitive performance on the fourth.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Limited novelty**: The authors acknowledge that SUB and MULT were previously used by Mou et al. (2016), but they don't clearly articulate how their work extends beyond this. The paper doesn't sufficiently distinguish itself from previous work that already used these comparison functions.
- **Unfair comparison of functions**: The comparison functions have different parameter counts (NN and NTN have many parameters while SUB and MULT are parameter-free), yet the paper doesn't account for this in the evaluation. A fairer comparison would normalize for the number of parameters.
- **Lack of implementation details**: The paper lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility, such as specific hyperparameters for each dataset, exact CNN architectures, and the exact implementation of the preprocessing layer for different tasks.
- **Questionable generalization claim**: While the authors claim their model is "general," the implementation details vary significantly across tasks (e.g., MovieQA requires a different structure with plot matching), suggesting it's not as general as claimed.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

**Clarify novelty**: The paper should more explicitly state how it extends previous work. Specifically, the authors should clarify what new insights they've gained from applying SUB and MULT within the "compare-aggregate" framework that weren't previously available in Mou et al. (2016).

**Improve comparison fairness**: The paper should compare the different functions while accounting for the number of parameters. For example, the authors could include a table showing performance versus parameter count for each comparison function.

**Enhance ablation studies**: The ablation study (Table 4) shows that removing preprocessing or attention layers hurts performance, but it doesn't explain *why*. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of when these components are most important (e.g., for sequences with large length differences vs. small differences).

**Provide implementation details**: To improve reproducibility, the authors should include specific hyperparameters used for each dataset (e.g., CNN window sizes, number of filters, dropout rates) and clarify how the preprocessing layer was implemented for different tasks.

**Address word order**: The authors claim their model outperforms previous work even though their preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive." This is an interesting finding but requires more explanation. How does the model capture contextual information if the preprocessing layer ignores word order?

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper cites Mou et al. (2016) as having used SUB and MULT, but doesn't explain how this work extends theirs. Can the authors clarify the specific novelty of applying these functions within the "compare-aggregate" framework?

2. The comparison functions have different parameter counts. How do the authors justify comparing performance without accounting for this difference in model complexity?

3. The authors claim their model is "general" but the implementation details vary significantly across tasks. How could they make the model truly general without task-specific adaptations?

4. In Table 3, EUCCOS performs nearly as well as SUBMULT+NN on MovieQA. However, EUCCOS produces a 2-dimensional vector while SUBMULT+NN produces a higher-dimensional vector. Is the performance difference simply due to dimensionality, or is there something more fundamental at play?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating the generalizability of the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple sequence matching tasks and providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions. While the novelty is somewhat limited, the empirical findings are useful for the community. With the suggested clarifications and improvements, this paper could make a stronger contribution to the field. I recommend weak acceptance with the understanding that the authors should address the concerns about novelty and experimental rigor in the final version.
