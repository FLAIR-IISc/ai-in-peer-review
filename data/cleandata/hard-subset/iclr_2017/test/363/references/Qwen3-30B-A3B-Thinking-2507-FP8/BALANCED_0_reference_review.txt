

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions at the word level. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), showing that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. The key contributions are: (1) providing empirical evidence that the compare-aggregate framework generalizes across multiple sequence matching tasks, and (2) conducting a systematic evaluation of comparison functions, revealing that simple element-wise operations can be highly effective for sequence matching.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The main reasons for rejection are: (1) the paper makes an overly broad claim about the generalizability of the "compare-aggregate" framework without sufficient evidence, and (2) the experimental evaluation lacks statistical rigor and theoretical justification for the key findings.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's central claim—that the compare-aggregate framework is effective for "a wide range of sequence matching problems"—is not sufficiently supported by the experimental evidence. The authors test their model on only four datasets, all related to question answering or textual entailment, which are closely related tasks. This limited scope does not justify the broad claim of generalizability. For instance, the framework's effectiveness for paraphrase identification, dialogue response selection, or other sequence matching tasks remains unverified.

Furthermore, the paper lacks statistical significance testing for the reported performance improvements. Without these tests, it's difficult to determine whether the observed differences between comparison functions (e.g., SUB vs. NN) are statistically meaningful or simply due to random variation in the results. The paper also fails to provide theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work better than neural network-based comparisons. The claim that these functions represent "a good compromise" between highly flexible and highly restrictive approaches is made without supporting analysis.

The paper's claim that their order-insensitive preprocessing layer outperforms previous work with order-sensitive layers is particularly problematic. The authors state that "our model can still outperform the previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing layer," yet they don't provide a clear explanation for how their model captures contextual information without considering word order. This undermines a key assumption in sequence matching tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse sequence matching tasks beyond QA and entailment (e.g., paraphrase identification, dialogue response selection). This would provide stronger evidence for the generalizability claim.

2. Include statistical significance tests for all performance comparisons (between comparison functions and with baselines). This is essential for scientific rigor in machine learning research.

3. Provide theoretical analysis of why element-wise comparison functions work well. The authors could explore the mathematical properties of these functions in relation to semantic similarity measurement.

4. Clarify the role of the preprocessing layer. If the model is order-insensitive yet performs well, the authors should explain how contextual information is captured and why this approach is effective for sequence matching.

5. Conduct more detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison, aggregation) to the overall performance.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims the "compare-aggregate" framework is effective for "a wide range of sequence matching problems," but the evaluation is limited to four datasets. Which specific sequence matching tasks outside of QA and entailment do you believe would benefit most from your framework, and why?

2. The preprocessing layer is explicitly described as "order-insensitive." How does the model capture contextual information without considering word order, and why does this approach work better than order-sensitive methods for sequence matching tasks?

3. For the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT), can you provide a theoretical analysis that explains why these operations are better suited for measuring semantic similarity than neural network-based comparisons?

4. The paper states that "some simple comparison functions can achieve better performance than the neural networks or neural tensor network comparison functions." Given that neural networks are highly flexible, why do you believe they underperform in this context? Is this specific to the datasets tested or a more general phenomenon?

The paper addresses an important problem in sequence matching with a well-structured framework, but the claims about generalizability and the effectiveness of simple comparison functions require stronger evidence to support their broad assertions. With additional experiments, theoretical justification, and statistical validation, this work could make a valuable contribution to the field.
