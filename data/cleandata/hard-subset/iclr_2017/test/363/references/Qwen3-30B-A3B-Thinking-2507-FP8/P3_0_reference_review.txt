

# Peer Review: A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) across four distinct sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors claim two key contributions: (1) demonstrating the general effectiveness of the compare-aggregate framework across diverse sequence matching problems, and (2) showing that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis across multiple datasets and tasks, making a valuable contribution to understanding the design space of sequence matching models.

## 2. Decision
**Weak Accept**. The paper makes a meaningful contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions in the compare-aggregate framework, which is a well-motivated gap in the literature. However, the paper requires significant clarification on theoretical justification for the element-wise comparison functions, and the results should be contextualized against more recent state-of-the-art models.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Strengths:**
The paper's primary strength is its systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple sequence matching tasks. Previous work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016) used the compare-aggregate framework but didn't systematically compare different comparison functions, making this empirical investigation valuable. The evaluation across four diverse datasets (three QA tasks with different architectures and one textual entailment task) provides strong evidence that the compare-aggregate framework is generalizable.

The paper also makes a compelling empirical finding: element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. This is particularly interesting because SUB and MULT are simple, parameter-free operations that have been previously underutilized in this context. The finding that SUBMULT+NN (combining SUB and MULT with a simple NN) achieves the best results provides a practical and efficient solution.

**Weaknesses:**
The paper's main weakness is the lack of theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work better. The authors claim these functions represent "a good middle ground" between highly flexible and restrictive approaches but provide no formal analysis or intuition about why this is the case. For instance, why would element-wise subtraction/multiplication better capture semantic similarity than neural networks that are specifically designed to learn complex relationships?

The preprocessing layer (Equation 1) is also undermotivated. The authors claim it's "order-insensitive" and that their model outperforms previous work despite this limitation, but they don't explain how this can be beneficial for sequence matching where word order is crucial. This contradicts established NLP wisdom that word order matters significantly for semantic understanding.

The baselines are outdated for SNLI (the most recent baseline is from 2016, while BERT-based models have since become the state-of-the-art), making it difficult to assess the true significance of the results. For example, the paper claims "very competitive performance" on SNLI, but without comparison to modern models, this claim is difficult to evaluate.

## 4. Additional Feedback

**Theoretical Justification:** The authors should provide a more rigorous theoretical argument for why element-wise operations might outperform neural network-based comparison functions. They could explore the relationship between these operations and semantic similarity metrics (e.g., how SUB relates to Euclidean distance, how MULT relates to cosine similarity), and why preserving the full vector information (rather than just a scalar similarity measure) might be beneficial.

**Preprocessing Layer:** The paper's claim that an order-insensitive preprocessing layer can outperform order-sensitive approaches needs stronger justification. The authors should provide more detailed ablation studies showing the performance differences between their approach and order-sensitive alternatives, and explain how their model compensates for the loss of word order information.

**Modern Baselines:** The paper should include comparisons with recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., BERT, RoBERTa, or other transformer-based models) for the SNLI dataset. Without this, the claim of "competitive performance" is difficult to validate, as the baseline models are now several years old.

**Visualization Clarity:** The visualization in Figure 2 is interesting but lacks sufficient explanation. The authors should clarify what specific features the CNN is capturing in the max values across dimensions, and how these features relate to the model's predictions.

**Practical Implications:** The paper could better articulate the practical implications of their findings. For example, the simplicity of SUB and MULT (parameter-free) could be a significant advantage for resource-constrained applications, which the authors don't explicitly discuss.

## Key Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide a more rigorous theoretical explanation for why element-wise comparison functions outperform neural network-based approaches? How do these operations relate to the underlying semantic similarity that sequence matching tasks aim to capture?

2. Given that word order is generally crucial in NLP, how does your order-insensitive preprocessing layer compensate for this limitation, and why does it still outperform order-sensitive approaches?

3. Why did you not include more recent baselines (e.g., BERT-based models) for the SNLI dataset, and how would your results compare to these modern approaches?

4. The paper claims that "the simplest comparison function EUCCOS achieves nearly the best performance in MovieQA," but doesn't provide specific numbers. Could you clarify the exact performance differences between EUCCOS and the best performing method (SUBMULT+NN) on this dataset?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the understanding of sequence matching models, but would be significantly strengthened by addressing these theoretical and contextual gaps. With these revisions, it would be a strong candidate for ICLR.
