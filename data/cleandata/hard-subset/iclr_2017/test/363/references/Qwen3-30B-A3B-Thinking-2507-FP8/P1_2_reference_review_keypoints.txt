- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching and demonstrates effectiveness across four tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).

- Systematically evaluates six word-level comparison functions, introducing novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT).

- Shows SUB and MULT consistently outperform more complex neural network–based comparison functions.

- Decision: Accept.

- Key reason 1: Demonstrates broad applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence-matching tasks.

- Key reason 2: Provides systematic evaluation of comparison functions and valuable insights from the novel element-wise operations.

- Strength: Thorough, systematic experiments across four datasets addressing an underexplored choice (word-level comparison function).

- Strength: Ablation study supports importance of components (e.g., attention layer for tasks with large sequence length differences).

- Insight: Preprocessing vs. attention components matter depending on task characteristics; order-insensitive preprocessing can outperform order-sensitive approaches in some cases.

- Improvement 1: Provide more detail on preprocessing layer implementation and interpretation of learned gate values.

- Improvement 2: Explain why order-insensitive preprocessing suffices for MovieQA and analyze Figure 2 visualizations in more depth.

- Improvement 3: Include statistical significance testing (e.g., paired t-tests across runs) to support reported improvements.

- Improvement 4: Discuss computational efficiency differences between element-wise operations and neural network–based comparison functions.

- Improvement 5: Clarify handling of differing task setups (SNLI with K=1 vs other tasks with K>1) and implications for comparability.

- Question 1: Why does an order-insensitive preprocessing layer still outperform order-sensitive methods—does word-by-word comparison capture needed context?

- Question 2: Why add a neural network after SUB and MULT in SUBMULT+NN, and is that NN necessary for best performance?

- Question 3: How does the SUB–Euclidean distance relationship affect performance, and why is preserving per-dimension information beneficial?

- Question 4: Why were word embeddings kept fixed across tasks, and would fine-tuning improve performance?
