

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations from two sequences. The authors demonstrate that their framework achieves strong performance across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), with element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) performing particularly well. The work makes a valuable contribution by showing the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework beyond single-task applications and by providing a systematic comparison of comparison functions that have not been thoroughly explored in prior work.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper presents an interesting empirical investigation, but lacks sufficient novelty and theoretical grounding to meet ICLR's high standards. The main reasons are the limited novelty of the framework (which builds directly on prior work) and insufficient justification for why the observed results hold.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is empirical: systematically testing different comparison functions within an existing framework. While this is valuable, it doesn't represent a significant theoretical or methodological advance. The framework itself is largely a recombination of existing components from prior work (Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b), with only minor modifications (e.g., using a CNN for aggregation instead of summation). The novelty claim that "this general framework is effective on many sequence matching problems" is not sufficiently supported, as the authors test on only four datasets (with InsuranceQA and WikiQA being closely related question-answering tasks), and don't demonstrate how their framework would generalize to new tasks beyond these.

The paper also lacks theoretical justification for why the element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) work well. The authors note that these functions "are closely related to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity" but don't provide a compelling explanation for why they outperform other approaches. Without this theoretical grounding, the empirical results feel somewhat coincidental rather than principled.

The experimental evaluation has some limitations: the baselines for some datasets (particularly SNLI) are not state-of-the-art, and the paper doesn't compare with more recent approaches that might have surpassed the baselines used. The ablation study is also limited, only removing the preprocessing and attention layers, without exploring other key components like the specific CNN architecture.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this work for future publication, the authors should:

1. Provide stronger theoretical justification for why element-wise comparison functions work well. The paper should explore the mathematical properties of these functions and how they align with semantic similarity requirements for sequence matching.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include more diverse datasets and more recent state-of-the-art baselines. The current comparison with baselines (particularly for SNLI) is not comprehensive enough to demonstrate the true significance of their approach.

3. Conduct more thorough ablation studies, exploring the impact of different architectural choices (e.g., CNN window sizes, attention mechanisms) on the performance of each comparison function.

4. Explicitly discuss the computational efficiency of the proposed approach compared to other methods, as this is important for practical applications.

5. Provide a more detailed analysis of when each comparison function works best, rather than just reporting overall performance. For example, why does EUCCOS work well on MovieQA but not on WikiQA?

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims that "the general 'compare-aggregate' framework is effective on many sequence matching problems," but only evaluates on four datasets. How would you extend this evaluation to demonstrate the framework's generalizability across more diverse sequence matching tasks?

2. The authors state that "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset." However, SUBMULT+NN (which is parameterized) performed best overall. How do you reconcile this apparent contradiction?

3. The paper notes that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role." Can you provide more concrete evidence for this claim, perhaps through visualization or analysis of attention weights?

4. The authors state that "the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive" but still achieve good results. How does this align with prior work showing the importance of word order in sequence matching? Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of when order matters and when it doesn't?

5. The paper mentions that "the resulting vector tj has the same dimensionality as aj and hj" for SUB and MULT. How does this affect the model's capacity to represent complex comparisons compared to neural network-based comparison functions that can learn higher-dimensional representations?
