

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" model for sequence matching tasks in natural language processing, with a particular focus on systematically evaluating different comparison functions that can be used within this framework. The authors argue that while the "compare-aggregate" framework has been used in previous work, it has been limited to one or two tasks and the comparison functions have not been systematically evaluated. The paper's key contribution is a thorough empirical analysis of six different comparison functions across four different datasets representing three question answering tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA) and one textual entailment task (SNLI). The authors find that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) work particularly well, with SUBMULT+NN (a combination of SUB and MULT followed by a neural network) achieving the best overall performance. The paper demonstrates that their framework is broadly applicable across multiple sequence matching tasks, providing a valuable resource for future research.

## 2. Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper because it makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to the sequence matching literature. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple datasets provides valuable insights that will inform future work in this area. The empirical evaluation is thorough, the results are scientifically rigorous, and the findings have potential to impact the design of future sequence matching models.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper addresses a significant gap in the literature. Previous work on "compare-aggregate" frameworks (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016; Parikh et al., 2016) has been limited to one or two tasks, without verifying the framework's general applicability across different sequence matching problems. The authors' claim that the framework is broadly applicable is well-supported by their experiments across four distinct datasets with different task setups.

The empirical evaluation is strong and scientifically rigorous. The authors test their model on four different datasets representing different sequence matching tasks, which is a significant strength. For each dataset, they compare against multiple strong baselines, providing a comprehensive evaluation. The ablation studies further strengthen their claims by demonstrating the importance of different components of their framework (preprocessing layer, attention layer). The visualization of the CNN layer in Figure 2 provides additional insights into how the model works.

The findings are both novel and practically useful. The discovery that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches is counterintuitive but well-supported by the results. This finding challenges the common assumption that more complex neural network components are always better and provides a valuable insight for future research. The paper's code availability also enhances its reproducibility and utility for the community.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to further improve it:

1. The paper would benefit from statistical significance tests for the performance improvements over baselines. While the results show consistent improvements, statistical tests would provide stronger evidence that these improvements are not due to random fluctuations.

2. The authors claim that SUB and MULT "preserve some information about the different dimensions of the original two vectors" while still being related to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity. A more detailed mathematical analysis would strengthen this claim and provide deeper insight into why these functions work well.

3. The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. I would appreciate more insight into why word-by-word comparison might be more important than context for these tasks. The authors mention this finding but don't deeply explore it.

4. For the MovieQA task, the model needs to match three sequences (plot, question, and answer), which is more complex than the two-sequence matching in other tasks. The authors should clarify how their comparison function handles this more complex matching scenario.

5. The paper states that the element-wise comparison functions "do not need parameters," but SUBMULT+NN does require parameters. The authors should provide more explanation for the performance difference between the parameter-free functions and the parameterized combination.

## Questions for Authors

1. The authors claim that their model outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing despite being order-insensitive themselves. Could you elaborate on why word-by-word comparison might be more important than context in these specific tasks?

2. The paper suggests that SUB is closely related to Euclidean distance but preserves more information. Can you provide a more detailed mathematical explanation of how SUB preserves information that Euclidean distance loses?

3. For the InsuranceQA and MovieQA tasks, which involve sequences of very different lengths, the attention layer appears to play a particularly important role. Could you explain why this is the case, and whether this insight generalizes to other sequence matching tasks with length disparities?

4. The paper mentions that they used LSTM in the preprocessing step for the SNLI task. Why was this necessary for SNLI but not for the other datasets, and how did this affect the comparison functions' performance?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature with its systematic evaluation of comparison functions. The findings have the potential to guide future research in this area, and the thorough evaluation across multiple datasets provides strong evidence for the authors' claims. I recommend acceptance with the understanding that addressing the suggested improvements would further strengthen the paper.
