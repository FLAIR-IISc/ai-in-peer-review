

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## Summary
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching, with a focus on systematically evaluating six different comparison functions between word-level representations. The authors claim their model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance across four different sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), and that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) work particularly well. The contribution lies in demonstrating the framework's generalizability across multiple tasks and providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains numerous minor but significant issues with technical clarity, formatting consistency, and methodological rigor that undermine confidence in the work.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper has several critical minor flaws that collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail:

1. **Missing Table Reference**: The paper references "Table 1" in the introduction but fails to include it in the provided text, which is a critical omission for readers trying to understand the examples.

2. **Inconsistent Reference Formatting**: References contain inconsistent formatting issues (e.g., "Wang and Jiang.,? \Q2016\E" instead of proper citation style), and the code availability note ends with "online.1" with an unexplained superscript 1.

3. **Unclear Technical Formulation**: The preprocessing formula (Equation 1) uses "eQ" and "eA" without defining "e" or explaining how the outer product works. The phrase "repeating the vector or scalar on the left for X times" is ambiguous.

4. **Misleading Claims**: The paper states "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset" - this is misleading. The element-wise operations themselves don't require parameters, but the overall model still has many parameters to learn.

5. **Lack of Specific Evidence**: The paper claims "EUCCOS achieves nearly the best performance in the MovieQA dataset" but provides no specific numerical results to support this claim, making it impossible to verify.

6. **Missing Ablation Results**: The paper mentions "the experiments of removing these two layers separately" but fails to present the actual results of these ablation studies in the results section.

7. **Inconsistent Claims**: The paper claims "a comparison function based on element-wise operations... works the best across the different datasets" (Section 1), but Table 3 shows different comparison functions (SUBMULT+NN, MULT, EUCCOS) performing best on different datasets, contradicting this broad claim.

8. **Figure Reference Omission**: The paper refers to "Figure 1" in Section 2.1 but the figure is not included in the provided text.

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Include all referenced tables and figures in the main text.
2. Correct all reference formatting inconsistencies.
3. Clarify the meaning of "e" in Equation (1) and explain the outer product operation.
4. Provide specific numerical results for all performance claims.
5. Present the ablation study results for removing preprocessing and attention layers.
6. Revise claims about comparison functions to accurately reflect which functions work best on which datasets.
7. Address the misleading statement about element-wise functions "not needing parameters."

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide the exact accuracy numbers for EUCCOS on MovieQA to support the claim that it "achieves nearly the best performance"?
2. Please clarify the meaning of "e" in the preprocessing formula (Equation 1) and explain how the outer product works.
3. Can you share the specific results of the ablation study for removing the preprocessing and attention layers separately?
4. How do you reconcile the claim that "element-wise comparison functions... work the best across the different datasets" with Table 3 showing different functions performing best on different datasets?
5. Why was LSTM incorporated only into the preprocessing for the SNLI task and not for the other tasks?

While the paper presents an interesting systematic evaluation of comparison functions, the numerous minor but critical issues in presentation and technical detail make it difficult to fully trust the results and claims. As a perfectionist reviewer, I find these issues too numerous to overlook despite the paper's potentially valuable contribution.
