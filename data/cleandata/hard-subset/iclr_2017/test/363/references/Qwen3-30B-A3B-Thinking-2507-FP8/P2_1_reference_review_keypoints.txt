- Contribution: Proposes a compare-aggregate model evaluated on four sequence-matching tasks and tests six comparison functions, finding element-wise SUB and MULT perform best.  
- Decision: Reviewer recommends rejection.  
- Limited novelty: Framework is not new and SUB/MULT were used in Mou et al. (2016); authors do not adequately distinguish their work.  
- Lack of theoretical justification: No principled explanation for why element-wise comparisons outperform neural comparison functions.  
- Insufficient baselines: Comparisons omit more recent/stronger state-of-the-art models (e.g., for SNLI).  
- Shallow task analysis: Paper notes performance differences across tasks but fails to explain why those differences occur.  
- Suggestion: Clarify and emphasize novelty (e.g., cross-task validation) and distinguish from prior work.  
- Suggestion: Add theoretical analysis of element-wise comparison functions and their relation to similarity measures.  
- Suggestion: Update experiments to include the latest strong baselines for each dataset.  
- Suggestion: Provide deeper analysis of dataset characteristics to explain varied performance across tasks.  
- Suggestion: Include computational-efficiency comparison of the different comparison functions.  
- Questions for authors: How does this extend beyond Mou et al.? Can you provide theoretical reasons for SUB/MULT’s effectiveness? Have you compared to newer SNLI baselines? Why does performance vary across InsuranceQA/MovieQA vs. WikiQA/SNLI?
