

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching with systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions (including two novel element-wise operations: SUB and MULT). The authors claim their framework is effective across four different sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), with element-wise comparison functions outperforming more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper makes a valuable contribution by demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks and providing a systematic comparison of comparison functions.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that undermine the credibility of the results and make reproducibility difficult.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's technical presentation contains several critical inconsistencies that prevent confident assessment of the claims:

First, the mathematical notation is fundamentally flawed. In Equation (6), the SUB comparison function is defined as "tj = (aj − hj) (aj − hj)" with the explanation that "the operator is element-wise multiplication." However, this would produce a vector of length l (same as aj and hj), while the paper claims this is related to Euclidean distance, which is the square root of the sum of squares. The paper incorrectly states "Euclidean distance is the sum of all the entries of the vector tj," but the sum of the entries of (aj-hj)∘(aj-hj) is not the Euclidean distance—it's the sum of squared differences, which is the square of the Euclidean distance. This fundamental misunderstanding of basic vector operations significantly undermines the technical credibility of the paper.

Second, the paper contains inconsistent dimensionality claims. In Section 2.3, it states that "for both comparison functions, the resulting vector tj has the same dimensionality as aj and hj." However, in Equation (8), SUBMULT+NN concatenates (aj-hj)∘(aj-hj) and aj∘hj (each of dimension l) to create a 2l-dimensional vector before applying a linear transformation, which would result in a vector of dimension l, not 2l as might be implied. The paper fails to clarify this dimensionality shift.

Third, the experimental details are insufficient for reproducibility. The paper uses GloVe embeddings but doesn't specify which version (50d, 100d, 200d, or 300d) was used. The preprocessing layer uses "zero initialization" for out-of-vocabulary words, which is problematic as it creates a zero vector for unknown words rather than using a proper <UNK> token. The paper also fails to specify CNN hyperparameters (kernel size, number of filters), which are critical for the aggregation layer.

Fourth, the results presentation is misleading. The paper claims "the element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset" but doesn't specify which of SUB or MULT achieved the best performance, nor does it provide the exact numbers. The ablation study (Table 4) is insufficiently detailed to understand the impact of removing the preprocessing and attention layers.

Finally, the reference list contains formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E" instead of standard citation format), and the paper claims code is available at "https://github.com/shuohangwang/SeqMatchSeq" but provides no verification or actual repository link.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the mathematical errors regarding Euclidean distance and vector operations. The paper must clarify the relationship between SUB and Euclidean distance, and provide proper mathematical notation.

2. Provide complete experimental details: specify GloVe version, explain the OOV handling, and include all CNN hyperparameters.

3. Clarify the dimensionality of all vectors at each step of the model, especially after concatenation in Equation (8).

4. Provide detailed results breakdown by dataset and comparison function, not just the overall best model (SUBMULT+NN).

5. Verify the code repository link and provide a direct, working URL.

6. Revise the reference list to standard citation format.

7. Clarify the relationship between the preprocessing layer (which is order-insensitive) and the attention layer, and how they interact in different task settings.

The paper addresses an interesting problem with a potentially valuable contribution, but the technical inconsistencies and insufficient details make it impossible to evaluate the claims with confidence. These flaws are not merely "minor" as they directly affect the interpretation of the results and the reproducibility of the work. For a top-tier venue like ICLR, such technical inconsistencies cannot be overlooked.
