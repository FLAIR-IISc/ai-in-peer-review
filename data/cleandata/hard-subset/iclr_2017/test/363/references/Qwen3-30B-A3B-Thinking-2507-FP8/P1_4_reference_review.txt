

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in natural language processing. The authors identify two key limitations in previous work: (1) most studies only test their models on one or two specific tasks, without verifying the framework's general effectiveness across multiple sequence matching problems, and (2) previous work hasn't systematically evaluated different comparison functions for matching word-level units. The authors address these limitations by presenting a unified model that can be applied across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), and by systematically evaluating six different comparison functions (including two novel element-wise operations: SUB and MULT). Their key findings are that the "compare-aggregate" framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, and that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) work particularly well across these tasks.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing a clear gap in the literature through systematic evaluation across multiple tasks and comparison functions. The empirical validation across four different datasets provides strong evidence for the general effectiveness of the "compare-aggregate" framework, and the discovery that element-wise comparison functions work well is both novel and potentially impactful for future work.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and places its work within the relevant literature. The authors clearly identify the limitations in existing work on the "compare-aggregate" framework and provide a compelling rationale for their approach. The literature review is thorough and well-organized, particularly in distinguishing their work from related "siamense" and "attentive" network approaches.

The empirical evaluation is rigorous and comprehensive. The authors test their model across four distinct datasets representing different sequence matching tasks (question answering with different setups and textual entailment), which effectively demonstrates the framework's generalizability. They compare against appropriate baselines for each dataset, and the results consistently show that their model outperforms or matches state-of-the-art approaches. The ablation study (Table 4) provides valuable insights into the importance of different components of their framework.

The most significant contribution is the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions. The authors' finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) work better than neural network-based approaches (NN, NTN) or standard similarity measures (EUCCOS) is both surprising and valuable. This finding challenges conventional wisdom and provides a practical guideline for future work on sequence matching. The fact that simple, parameter-free functions can outperform more complex alternatives is particularly noteworthy.

The paper also makes its code publicly available, which enhances reproducibility and accessibility for the research community.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper makes a strong contribution, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **More detailed analysis of why element-wise functions work well**: The paper notes that SUB and MULT "work the best" but doesn't provide a deep analysis of why these functions are effective. A more thorough discussion of how these functions capture the semantic relationships between words could strengthen the paper. For instance, how do these functions relate to the specific characteristics of the tasks they're applied to?

2. **More comprehensive ablation study**: The ablation study (Table 4) is good but could be expanded to show the individual contribution of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison function) across all four datasets. The paper mentions that attention is more important for tasks with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) while preprocessing is more important for tasks with smaller length differences (WikiQA, SNLI), but this claim would be stronger with more detailed evidence.

3. **Clarification on model implementation**: The paper mentions that for the SNLI task, they incorporated LSTM into the preprocessing layer, but doesn't explain why this was necessary or how it affected the results. A brief explanation would help readers understand the model's flexibility.

4. **Broader implications**: While the paper mentions future work on multi-task learning, it could provide more specific suggestions about how their findings might influence the design of sequence matching models in general.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To better understand the paper's contributions and results, I'd like to ask the following questions:

1. The paper states that "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset." Why do you think these parameter-free functions work particularly well for WikiQA compared to the other datasets? Is there something about the WikiQA task that makes it more amenable to simple comparison functions?

2. In Table 3, your model achieves the best performance on all datasets except SNLI. For SNLI, your model is competitive but not the top performer. Could you elaborate on why the "compare-aggregate" framework might be less effective for textual entailment compared to question answering tasks, and what specific aspects of the SNLI task might make it more challenging for this framework?

3. The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. How do you explain this counterintuitive result, and what does it imply about the importance of word-level comparison versus contextual information in sequence matching tasks?

4. The paper uses a one-layer CNN for aggregation. What would happen if you used a different aggregation method (e.g., attention or simple summation)? How does your choice of aggregation method relate to the comparison functions you're using?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature, and I believe it would be a strong addition to ICLR. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions and the demonstration of the framework's generalizability across multiple tasks provide important insights that will be useful for future research in this area.
