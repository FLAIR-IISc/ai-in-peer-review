- Paper proposes a compare-aggregate framework evaluating six comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) across four sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Authors claim two contributions: demonstrating general effectiveness of compare-aggregate and showing element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) outperform complex NN-based comparisons.  
- Empirical evaluation across four diverse datasets is thorough and shows SUBMULT+NN achieves the best results.  
- Decision: Weak Accept — the work is meaningful but needs clarification and stronger contextualization.  
- Strength: Systematic comparison fills a gap left by prior work and shows generalizability across tasks.  
- Strength: Finding that simple, parameter-free element-wise operations can outperform more complex methods is notable and practically useful.  
- Weakness: No theoretical justification for why element-wise operations outperform neural comparison functions.  
- Weakness: Preprocessing layer is undermotivated and its order-insensitivity contradicts established importance of word order in NLP.  
- Weakness: Baselines (especially for SNLI) are outdated; missing comparisons to modern transformer-based models (e.g., BERT/RoBERTa).  
- Suggestion: Provide theoretical analysis linking SUB/MULT to semantic similarity metrics (e.g., Euclidean, cosine) and why preserving full vectors helps.  
- Suggestion: Include ablation studies contrasting order-insensitive preprocessing with order-sensitive alternatives and explain compensation mechanisms.  
- Suggestion: Add modern baselines (BERT, RoBERTa) for SNLI to contextualize claims of competitive performance.  
- Suggestion: Improve Figure 2 explanations to clarify what CNN features the max-values capture and how they affect predictions.  
- Suggestion: Discuss practical implications of parameter-free methods (e.g., benefits for resource-constrained applications).  
- Key question 1: Provide a rigorous theoretical explanation for why element-wise comparisons outperform neural-network comparisons.  
- Key question 2: Explain how the order-insensitive preprocessing layer compensates for loss of word order and still outperforms order-sensitive methods.  
- Key question 3: Why were modern baselines omitted for SNLI, and how would the proposed methods compare to transformer-based models?  
- Key question 4: Clarify exact performance numbers comparing EUCCOS and SUBMULT+NN on MovieQA.
