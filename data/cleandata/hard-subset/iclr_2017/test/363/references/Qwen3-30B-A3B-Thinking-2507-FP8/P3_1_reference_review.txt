

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching across four distinct NLP tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors identify an important gap in the literature: prior work has either tested on only one or two tasks or failed to systematically evaluate the critical comparison function used at the word level. The key contributions are demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across diverse sequence matching problems and showing that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper presents six comparison functions, including three novel element-wise operations, and provides compelling empirical evidence that these simpler functions often achieve superior results.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a significant contribution to the sequence matching literature by demonstrating the generalizability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks and by providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions. The experimental design is rigorous, the results are compelling, and the findings have clear implications for future work in sequence matching.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several key areas:

1. **Strong motivation and literature placement**: The authors correctly identify that previous work has either tested on limited tasks or neglected to systematically evaluate comparison functions. Their position in the literature is well-justified, with appropriate citations to Parikh et al. (2016), Wang & Jiang (2016b), and He & Lin (2016).

2. **Rigorous empirical evaluation**: Testing on four distinct datasets representing different sequence matching tasks provides strong evidence for the generalizability claim. The ablation studies (Table 4) convincingly demonstrate the importance of different components.

3. **Novel and valuable insight**: The finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions is counterintuitive but well-supported by the results. This is a significant contribution that will guide future research.

4. **Clear technical presentation**: The model architecture and comparison functions are well-described, with sufficient mathematical detail to reproduce the experiments.

Minor weaknesses include some overstatement of novelty regarding element-wise operations (Mou et al., 2016 already used similar approaches) and insufficient evidence for the claim about attention layer importance varying with sequence length differences.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Clarify novelty**: The paper claims SUB and MULT "have not been widely used," but Mou et al. (2016) used similar element-wise operations. The authors should explicitly differentiate their specific implementation and explain why their approach is more effective in the context of the compare-aggregate framework.

2. **Strengthen the length difference claim**: The paper states that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, the attention layer plays a more important role," but the evidence in Table 4 is somewhat weak. The authors should provide more specific analysis (e.g., attention weight distributions for different sequence length pairs) to support this claim.

3. **Address computational efficiency**: The simpler comparison functions likely have lower computational cost than neural network-based approaches. This practical advantage should be mentioned as it's an important consideration for real-world applications.

4. **Clarify context handling**: The paper notes their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive yet the model still outperforms order-sensitive approaches. This is a fascinating finding that deserves more discussion about how context is captured without explicit order processing.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims element-wise comparison functions "have not been widely used," but Mou et al. (2016) already used similar operations. Can you clarify how your specific implementation of SUB and MULT differs from their approach, and why it's more effective in the compare-aggregate framework?

2. You state that for tasks with large sequence length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), the attention layer is more important. Could you provide more detailed evidence for this claim beyond the ablation study in Table 4? For instance, could you analyze how attention weights vary with sequence length differences?

3. Your preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. How does the model capture contextual information without considering word order? Is this a limitation or a strength of the approach?

4. The paper uses a one-layer CNN for aggregation. What was the rationale for this choice, and did you explore deeper CNN architectures? If not, why did you believe a single layer would be sufficient?

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field with its systematic evaluation of comparison functions and demonstration of the generalizability of the compare-aggregate framework. With minor clarifications on novelty and additional evidence for key claims, this work will be valuable for future sequence matching research.
