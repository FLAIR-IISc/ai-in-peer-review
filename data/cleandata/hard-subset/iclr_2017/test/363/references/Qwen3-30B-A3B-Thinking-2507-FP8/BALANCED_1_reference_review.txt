

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" model for sequence matching tasks, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations SUB and MULT) across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The authors claim that their framework is generally effective across multiple sequence matching problems, and that element-wise comparison functions (particularly SUBMULT+NN) work best across these diverse tasks. The paper makes a valuable contribution by testing the framework across multiple tasks rather than just one or two, and by providing a systematic comparison of different comparison functions.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper addresses a meaningful problem and makes a reasonable contribution to the sequence matching literature, the experimental evaluation is insufficient to fully support the paper's key claims about the superiority of element-wise comparison functions across different datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is the systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple tasks, which is valuable. However, several critical issues undermine the paper's claims:

- **Inconsistent performance claims**: The paper claims "a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication works the best on the four datasets," but Table 3 shows that different comparison functions perform best on different datasets (EUCCOS on MovieQA, SUB on WikiQA, etc.). The paper fails to provide a coherent explanation for these differences, weakening the central claim.

- **Lack of statistical significance**: The paper reports absolute accuracy numbers without any statistical tests or error bars. Without demonstrating that the observed performance differences between comparison functions are statistically significant, the paper cannot confidently claim that one function "works best."

- **Marginal improvements**: For some datasets (e.g., SNLI), the best performance of the proposed model (85.3%) is only slightly better than the best baseline (85.4%). The paper doesn't explain why this marginal improvement is meaningful or how it compares to other recent work.

- **Insufficient explanation**: The paper doesn't adequately explain why element-wise functions work better. The connection between the nature of sequence matching tasks and the optimal comparison function is underdeveloped, limiting the theoretical contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

- Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., paired t-tests) for performance differences between comparison functions across datasets. This would establish whether the observed differences are meaningful.

- Clarify the central claim about element-wise functions working best. Instead of claiming a single function works best across all datasets, they should provide a more nuanced analysis of which functions work best on which types of tasks and why.

- Include error bars or confidence intervals for the reported results to better assess the reliability of the findings.

- Add an ablation study demonstrating the contribution of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison, aggregation) to the final performance, rather than just removing components as in Table 4.

- Expand the analysis of why different comparison functions work better on different datasets. For instance, the paper notes that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role." This interesting observation needs more empirical support and explanation.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide statistical significance tests for the performance differences between the best comparison function and the others across the four datasets? Without these, it's difficult to assess whether the observed differences are meaningful.

2. In Table 3, the performance differences between the best comparison function and the second-best are sometimes very small (e.g., 75.7% vs 75.5% on InsuranceQA). Could you explain why these marginal differences are meaningful and how they compare to the standard deviation of the results?

3. For MovieQA, EUCCOS achieves nearly the best performance, while for WikiQA, element-wise functions work best. Could you provide more insight into why the optimal comparison function differs between these tasks? Is it related to the nature of the data or the task?

4. The paper claims "a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication works the best on the four datasets," but no single function is best across all datasets. Could you clarify this claim and provide a more precise statement about the relative performance of different comparison functions?

5. The paper mentions that SUB and MULT have been used previously by Mou et al. (2016). Could you provide a more detailed comparison of how your use of these functions differs from their work, and why this difference matters for sequence matching?
