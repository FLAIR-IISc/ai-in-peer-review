

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks across multiple NLP domains. The authors make two key contributions: (1) demonstrating the framework's effectiveness across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) representing different sequence matching problems, and (2) systematically evaluating six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB: element-wise subtraction followed by element-wise multiplication, and MULT: element-wise multiplication). The authors find that their element-wise comparison functions consistently outperform more complex neural network-based alternatives across all tasks.

## 2. Decision

**Decision: Accept**  
*Key reasons:* The paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions in sequence matching, with the element-wise operations (SUB/MULT) providing a simple yet effective alternative to more complex neural approaches. The empirical validation across four distinct datasets demonstrates the generalizability of their framework.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and addresses a meaningful gap in the literature. Previous work (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016) has adopted the compare-aggregate framework but limited their evaluation to a single task and neglected systematic comparison of comparison functions. The authors' choice to evaluate six comparison functions—including three novel ones (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN)—is particularly valuable.

The experimental design is strong: testing across four different tasks with different characteristics (question answering with varying context lengths, textual entailment) provides compelling evidence for the framework's generalizability. The results are consistent—SUBMULT+NN achieves best performance across all tasks, with element-wise functions (SUB/MULT) performing particularly well on WikiQA and InsuranceQA. The ablation study showing that preprocessing and attention layers are important, with their relative importance depending on sequence length differences, provides useful architectural insights.

The paper makes a strong case for the effectiveness of simple element-wise operations over more complex neural network-based comparisons, which challenges the prevailing trend of using increasingly complex neural architectures for sequence matching. This finding has practical implications for model efficiency and interpretability.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper makes a strong contribution, the following improvements would strengthen it:

1. **Theoretical justification:** The paper lacks a clear theoretical explanation for why element-wise operations work better. The authors note that SUB is related to Euclidean distance and MULT to cosine similarity, but they don't explain why preserving the full vector (rather than just the scalar distance/similarity) is beneficial for sequence matching. A more in-depth analysis of the information preserved in these operations would strengthen the paper.

2. **Comparison with recent work:** The authors don't compare with recent state-of-the-art models that might have used similar comparison functions. For instance, the paper by Wang et al. (2016) on IARNN-Gate uses attention-based comparison that might be related to their approach. A comparison with more recent work would better position their contribution.

3. **Statistical significance:** The results don't include statistical significance tests for the performance differences between comparison functions. Given that the differences between SUB/MULT and other methods are small but consistent, this would add scientific rigor.

4. **Architecture details:** The preprocessing layer is described as "order-insensitive," but the paper doesn't fully explore the implications of this design choice. A discussion of how this might affect different types of sequence matching tasks would be valuable.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

1. The paper claims element-wise operations "work the best across the different datasets," but the results in Table 3 show that EUCCOS (cosine + Euclidean) achieves the best performance on MovieQA. How do you reconcile this with your conclusion that element-wise operations are superior? Is there a specific aspect of MovieQA that makes EUCCOS particularly effective?

2. The authors note that for sequence matching with big length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), attention plays a more important role, while for smaller differences (WikiQA, SNLI), preprocessing is more important. Could you elaborate on why this pattern emerges, and how it relates to the nature of the tasks?

3. The paper doesn't discuss the computational efficiency of the element-wise operations compared to neural network-based approaches. Given that these operations are computationally simpler, would this translate to faster inference times that could be particularly valuable for real-world applications?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature by demonstrating the effectiveness of simple element-wise comparison functions across multiple tasks. With the suggested clarifications, it would be an even stronger contribution to the field.
