

# Peer Review: A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions across four diverse NLP tasks: MovieQA (machine comprehension), InsuranceQA (answer selection), WikiQA (question answering), and SNLI (textual entailment). The authors make two key contributions: (1) demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across multiple sequence matching tasks, and (2) providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions, revealing that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper's empirical rigor is commendable, with thorough ablation studies and visualizations explaining the model's behavior.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The key reasons are: (1) the paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by demonstrating the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework beyond single-task studies, and (2) the systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides valuable insights that will guide future research in sequence matching.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly identify two limitations in prior work: (1) previous studies tested their models on only one or two tasks, and (2) they did not systematically evaluate comparison functions. The authors' approach of testing on four diverse datasets and evaluating six comparison functions directly addresses these limitations.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The paper provides comprehensive results across multiple datasets, with proper baselines, ablation studies, and visualizations. The authors demonstrate that their framework achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on all four datasets, with the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) performing particularly well. The ablation study (Table 4) effectively demonstrates the importance of both the preprocessing and attention layers, with their relative importance varying based on the sequence length differences in different tasks.

The paper's most valuable contribution is the systematic evaluation of comparison functions. Previous work (e.g., Parikh et al., 2016; He & Lin, 2016) used neural network-based comparison functions but did not systematically compare them with simpler alternatives like element-wise operations. The authors' finding that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network-based approaches is both surprising and significant—it suggests that for sequence matching tasks, we should be more careful about the choice of comparison functions and not automatically assume that more complex functions are better.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, it could be improved in a few areas:

- The paper would benefit from a deeper theoretical discussion of why element-wise operations work better than neural network-based approaches. The authors note that SUB is related to Euclidean distance and MULT to cosine similarity, but they don't explain why these simpler functions capture the necessary semantic relationships better than more flexible neural network approaches.

- The paper mentions that the preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive" but doesn't sufficiently address how this limitation is compensated for in the model. For sequence matching tasks where word order matters (e.g., in textual entailment), this could be a significant concern that deserves more discussion.

- The paper claims that SUBMULT+NN is the best comparison function overall, but Table 3 shows that for WikiQA, the simplest comparison function (EUCCOS) achieves nearly the best performance. This apparent contradiction needs clarification in the paper.

- The computational efficiency of the different comparison functions isn't discussed, which would be valuable for practical applications.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that element-wise comparison functions work best, but the results for WikiQA (Table 3) show that EUCCOS achieves nearly the best performance. Can you clarify why this appears to contradict your main claim about element-wise operations being superior?

2. The preprocessing layer is described as "order-insensitive," yet word order is often crucial in sequence matching tasks. How does the model compensate for this limitation, and why is it still effective despite this?

3. In the ablation study (Table 4), removing the attention layer has a larger impact on tasks with big sequence length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA), while removing the preprocessing layer has a larger impact on tasks with smaller length differences (WikiQA, SNLI). What is your interpretation of this observation, and how does it inform the design of sequence matching models?

4. The paper uses a one-directional attention mechanism, but Parikh et al. (2016) used a two-directional approach. What were the specific reasons for choosing a one-directional attention mechanism, and what would be the expected impact of using two-directional attention?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of sequence matching models. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides concrete guidance for future research, and the demonstration of the framework's general applicability across multiple tasks is significant. With the suggested clarifications, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
