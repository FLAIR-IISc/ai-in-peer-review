

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching problems in NLP, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions for word-level matching. The authors claim that their model achieves state-of-the-art performance across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), and that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The work is well-motivated by the observation that previous studies have not systematically evaluated comparison functions for sequence matching, and the authors make a compelling case for the general applicability of their framework across multiple tasks.

## Decision
Reject. The paper contains significant methodological inconsistencies and unclear technical details that undermine the validity of the claimed contributions.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Inconsistent and Unclear Methodology**: The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies that prevent proper understanding and replication of the proposed approach. The comparison functions SUB and MULT are defined with ambiguous notation: "tj = (aj - hj) (aj - hj)" and "tj = aj hj" are not clearly defined as element-wise operations (the paper states they are element-wise, but the notation is misleading). This is a critical flaw since the core contribution hinges on these specific comparison functions. The paper also fails to define key components like "eQ" and "eA" in equation (1), making the preprocessing step incomprehensible.

2. **Unverified Baseline Comparisons**: The paper claims "our model can outperform all the previous models" on InsuranceQA, but the baselines are taken from reported performance in other papers rather than re-implemented and evaluated under identical conditions. This is a serious methodological flaw in empirical evaluation that makes the results difficult to trust. For example, the authors cite "IARNN-Gate" as a baseline but don't specify the exact implementation details that would be necessary to fairly compare.

3. **Inadequate Ablation Study**: The paper states "we find the preprocessing layer and the attention layer for word selection to be important," but fails to provide the ablation study results that would substantiate this claim. The text mentions "experiments of removing these two layers separately" but doesn't present the actual numbers or comparative results. Without this data, the claim lacks empirical support.

4. **Formatting and Reference Inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple formatting errors in references (e.g., "Wang and Jiang.,? \Q2016\E" instead of proper citation format) and a broken GitHub link ("https://github.com/shuohangwang/SeqMatchSeq" is not a valid GitHub repository URL). These are minor but indicate a lack of attention to detail that is unacceptable for a publication venue like ICLR.

5. **Misleading Claims**: The paper claims "the element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset," but this is contradicted by the paper's own description of EUCCOS (which also doesn't require parameters) achieving "nearly the best performance" on MovieQA. The paper fails to clarify why element-wise functions would work better without providing sufficient theoretical justification or analysis of the underlying mechanisms.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify Notation**: The paper must clearly define all mathematical notation, especially the ambiguous element-wise operations and the undefined "eX" in equation (1). The authors should use standard notation for element-wise multiplication (e.g., ⊙ or .*).

2. **Provide Reproducible Results**: The authors should re-implement all baselines on their own hardware and under identical conditions to ensure fair comparison. The paper should include detailed hyperparameters and training conditions for all models.

3. **Include Ablation Study Results**: The paper must present the actual ablation study results for removing the preprocessing and attention layers. Without these numbers, the claim about their importance is unsubstantiated.

4. **Correct Reference Formatting**: The references should be formatted according to standard NLP conference guidelines, with consistent author names, years, and titles.

5. **Clarify the Element-Wise Claim**: The paper should provide a more detailed analysis of why element-wise operations work better than other comparison functions, including theoretical justification and possibly some visualizations of the learned representations.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact mathematical operation for SUB and MULT? The current notation suggests matrix operations, but the paper claims they are element-wise. Please provide a clear mathematical definition.

2. Why did the authors choose to use a one-directional attention mechanism instead of the bidirectional attention used in Parikh et al. (2016), and how did this choice impact the results?

3. Could you provide the ablation study results for removing the preprocessing and attention layers, as claimed in section 3.3?

4. The GitHub link provided appears to be invalid. Could you provide the correct link to the code repository?

5. For the WikiQA dataset, the paper claims element-wise comparison functions "do not need parameters" but achieved the best performance. However, the paper also states that EUCCOS (which also doesn't need parameters) achieved nearly the best performance on MovieQA. How do you explain this apparent contradiction?

The paper addresses an interesting problem in NLP with a potentially valuable contribution. However, the current version contains too many technical inconsistencies and methodological flaws that prevent confident assessment of the claimed results. With careful revision addressing these issues, the paper could be a strong contribution to the field.
