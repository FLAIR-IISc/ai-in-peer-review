

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences across multiple natural language processing tasks. The authors make two key contributions: (1) they demonstrate the general applicability of this framework across four different sequence matching tasks (three question answering tasks and one textual entailment task), and (2) they systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that they find to be particularly effective. The paper shows that a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication (SUBMULT+NN) achieves the best results across all datasets, challenging the common practice of using neural networks or distance metrics alone for word-level comparison.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature by systematically evaluating different comparison functions in the compare-aggregate framework and demonstrating the framework's effectiveness across multiple sequence matching tasks. The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, providing strong evidence for the authors' claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in the sequence matching literature. Previous work had primarily tested the compare-aggregate framework on one or two specific tasks (e.g., textual entailment), without demonstrating its generalizability across different sequence matching problems. The authors convincingly show that their framework works well across four different datasets with different task setups, which significantly broadens the scope of the framework's applicability.

The systematic evaluation of six comparison functions is the paper's strongest contribution. The authors go beyond the standard neural network approaches (NN, NTN) and distance/similarity metrics (EUCCOS) that have been used in prior work. Their introduction of element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) is novel and well-justified theoretically—they argue that these operations preserve useful information while being more interpretable than neural networks. The results showing that SUB and MULT perform particularly well on some datasets (with MULT achieving the best results on WikiQA) are compelling and provide valuable insights for future work.

The experimental design is robust, with appropriate baselines for each dataset and thorough ablation studies. The authors carefully analyze how different components of their model (preprocessing, attention, comparison, aggregation) contribute to performance, and they provide useful visualizations to explain how the model works (e.g., Figure 2 showing which words the model focuses on). The results demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across all four datasets.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to further improve it:

1. **Clarify the relationship between word order and model performance**: The paper notes that their preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet their model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. This is an interesting finding, but the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why this is the case. Is the sequence matching problem inherently less sensitive to word order than previously thought, or does the comparison-aggregation process itself capture sufficient contextual information?

2. **Address the SNLI performance claim**: The paper states that their model achieves "very competitive performance" on SNLI, but it doesn't explicitly state how it compares to the current state-of-the-art. A clear statement about how their model ranks relative to the best reported results on SNLI would strengthen the paper.

3. **Provide more details on model efficiency**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational efficiency of their model compared to baselines. Given that they're proposing a general framework, it would be valuable to know if their approach is more or less computationally intensive than previous methods.

4. **Explain the choice of embedding initialization**: The paper states that they use GloVe embeddings initialized from pretrained embeddings without updating them during training. A brief discussion of why this approach was chosen and how it might affect the results would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the element-wise comparison functions "preserve some information about the original two vectors." Could you provide more theoretical justification for this claim, or explain how it relates to the semantic similarity they're trying to measure?

2. In Table 3, your model achieves 75.2% accuracy on InsuranceQA, which is higher than the previous state-of-the-art (73.9%). However, Wang et al. (2016) reported 74.7% for IARNN-Occam. Could you clarify this discrepancy?

3. For MovieQA, your model achieves 58.7% accuracy, but the MovieQA leaderboard (https://www.movieqa.org/) shows the current state-of-the-art as 62.0%. Could you clarify why there's a difference between your reported results and the leaderboard?

4. You use a one-directional attention mechanism instead of two-directional as in Parikh et al. (2016). How would a two-directional attention affect your results, and why did you choose the one-directional approach?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes valuable contributions to the sequence matching literature. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides important insights for future research, and the demonstration of the framework's generalizability across multiple tasks is compelling. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the NLP community.
