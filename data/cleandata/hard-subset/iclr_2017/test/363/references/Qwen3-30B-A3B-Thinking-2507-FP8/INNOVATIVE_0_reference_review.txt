

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, with two key contributions: (1) systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions (including two novel element-wise operations: SUB and MULT), and (2) empirical validation of the framework's effectiveness across four distinct sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors argue that previous work on this framework has been limited to one or two tasks and has not systematically evaluated comparison functions, which they demonstrate are crucial to performance. Their key insight is that element-wise comparison operations (SUB and MULT) significantly outperform neural network-based comparison functions across multiple datasets, challenging the assumption that more complex models necessarily yield better results.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper's systematic evaluation of comparison functions across multiple sequence matching tasks represents a significant conceptual contribution that advances the field beyond incremental improvements. The insight that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches is bold, well-motivated, and empirically validated across diverse datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's novelty lies in its systematic exploration of comparison functions within the "compare-aggregate" framework, which previous work had not done. While the framework itself isn't new, the authors' focus on the comparison function as a critical design element and their empirical demonstration that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network approaches is genuinely novel and valuable.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly situating itself within the growing body of work on sequence matching using attention-based models. The authors correctly identify the gap in previous work: limited task coverage and lack of systematic comparison function evaluation. Their evaluation across four distinct datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) demonstrates the framework's general applicability, which is a significant contribution beyond the single-task focus of prior work.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous enough for ICLR's standards, with thorough experiments across multiple datasets, ablation studies, and qualitative analysis through visualization. The paper's key insight—that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches for sequence matching—is compellingly supported by the data. The fact that their element-wise approaches outperform neural network-based comparison functions across multiple tasks is a significant finding that challenges conventional wisdom in the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:
- Include statistical significance tests for the performance differences between comparison functions. The paper claims "SUB and MULT work best" but doesn't provide evidence that these differences are statistically significant.
- Provide more theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work well for sequence matching. The paper notes the relationship between SUB and Euclidean distance and between MULT and cosine similarity, but a deeper theoretical explanation would strengthen the contribution.
- Discuss the computational efficiency of the element-wise operations compared to neural network approaches. The paper notes that SUB and MULT don't require parameters, but doesn't discuss inference speed or model size implications.
- Clarify the relationship between their SUB/MULT functions and Mou et al. (2016), who used similar operations in a different context but didn't evaluate them for sequence matching. The paper claims these are "not widely used for word-level matching" but doesn't address this prior work sufficiently.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims SUB and MULT "have not been widely used for word-level matching" but Mou et al. (2016) used similar element-wise operations in their semantic composition model. How does your evaluation differ from theirs, and why weren't these functions evaluated in the context of sequence matching before?

2. The paper states that "the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive" and yet the model still outperforms previous work that uses order-sensitive preprocessing. Could you provide more insight into why the word-by-word comparison part plays such a dominant role in these tasks, and whether the model would benefit from incorporating contextual information in the preprocessing layer?

3. You mention that "the simplest comparison function EUCCOS achieves nearly the best performance in MovieQA." Given that EUCCOS is a combination of Euclidean distance and cosine similarity, could you elaborate on why this specific combination works particularly well for MovieQA but not for other datasets?
