

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences, which has been previously applied to specific tasks like textual entailment. The authors make two key contributions: (1) they demonstrate the framework's effectiveness across four different sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) rather than just one or two tasks as previous studies did, and (2) they systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for matching word-level units, finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform other approaches including neural networks and standard similarity measures.

The paper provides a thorough empirical analysis showing that their model achieves state-of-the-art results on three of the four datasets and competitive performance on the fourth. They also conduct ablation studies to demonstrate the importance of their preprocessing and attention layers for different types of sequence matching problems.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions.

My key reasons for acceptance are:
1. The paper makes a valuable contribution by empirically validating the general applicability of the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple sequence matching tasks, addressing a significant limitation in previous work.
2. The systematic evaluation of six comparison functions, particularly the discovery that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural approaches, provides meaningful insights for future research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly identify two important limitations in previous work: (1) previous studies tested their models on only one or two tasks, and (2) they didn't systematically evaluate comparison functions. The authors' solution to these limitations is both reasonable and important for the field.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The authors test their model on four diverse datasets representing different sequence matching tasks (three question answering tasks with different setups and one textual entailment task), which provides strong evidence for the general applicability of their framework. The ablation studies (Table 4) appropriately demonstrate the importance of different components of their model, and they provide clear evidence that their comparison functions (particularly SUB and MULT) outperform other approaches.

The paper's contribution is not revolutionary, but it is substantial and well-executed. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions is a valuable addition to the literature, and the demonstration that the framework works across multiple tasks is significant. The results are presented clearly and the methodology is sound.

I note that while the paper's contribution is incremental, it's a valuable one that fills an important gap in the literature. The empirical evidence provided is sufficient to support their claims, and the results are convincing.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. **Clarify the novelty**: While the paper's contribution is valuable, it's important to more precisely distinguish it from prior work. The paper mentions that Parikh et al. (2016) proposed a similar framework, but doesn't clearly articulate how this work differs beyond testing on more datasets and evaluating different comparison functions. A more explicit comparison of their model structure with Parikh et al. would help position their contribution more precisely.

2. **Expand the ablation study**: The ablation study in Table 4 is limited to removing preprocessing or attention layers. I recommend adding an analysis of how different comparison functions interact with the preprocessing and attention layers. For example, does the effectiveness of SUB/MULT depend on the presence of attention, or does it work well regardless?

3. **Address the "why" behind element-wise operations**: The paper finds that SUB and MULT work well, but doesn't provide a strong theoretical justification. Adding a brief discussion of why element-wise operations might be particularly well-suited for sequence matching (e.g., how they preserve dimension-specific information that might be lost in neural network-based comparisons) would strengthen the paper.

4. **Compare with more recent models**: The paper cites work up to 2016, but some more recent sequence matching models (e.g., those using transformers) might have achieved better results on these datasets. A brief comparison with these more recent models would help place the paper's results in the current context of the field.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me be more confident in my assessment, I'd like clarification on the following points:

1. The paper mentions that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role." Can you provide more specific evidence for this claim? For example, how does the attention layer's contribution differ quantitatively between tasks with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) versus tasks with smaller differences (WikiQA, SNLI)?

2. The paper states that "our preprocessing layer is order-insensitive so that it will not take the context information into consideration during the comparison, but our model can still outperform the previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing layer." Can you elaborate on why this is surprising or what it implies about the importance of word-level comparison versus context-aware processing in these tasks?

3. The paper mentions that "for the SNLI dataset, with any comparison function we use, our model can outperform all the previous models." However, the results in Table 3 show that the best model (SUBMULT+NN) achieves 87.1% accuracy, while the best baseline (EBIM+TreeLSTM) achieves 87.2%. This is a very small difference. Can you clarify whether this difference is statistically significant, and whether the authors consider this to be a meaningful improvement over the state-of-the-art?

These questions will help me better understand the significance of the results and the paper's contribution to the field.
