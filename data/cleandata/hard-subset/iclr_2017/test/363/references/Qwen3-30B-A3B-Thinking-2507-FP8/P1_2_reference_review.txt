

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching in natural language processing, making two significant contributions: (1) demonstrating that this framework is effective across four different sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) rather than just one or two as previous work had done; and (2) systematically evaluating six different comparison functions at the word level, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), and showing they consistently outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated across multiple datasets, and provides valuable insights about the choice of comparison functions in sequence matching models.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance: 
1) The paper makes a clear contribution by demonstrating the general applicability of the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple sequence matching tasks, which previous work had not systematically tested.
2) The systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions, particularly the novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), provides valuable insights for the community and addresses an important gap in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength is its systematic approach to evaluating different comparison functions within the "compare-aggregate" framework. The authors correctly identify that previous work has not adequately addressed the choice of comparison function at the word level, despite it being crucial for measuring semantic similarity. Their experimental evaluation across four different datasets (representing different sequence matching tasks) is thorough and convincing.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with appropriate citations and clear explanations of the model architecture. The results are compelling, showing that their proposed element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) consistently outperform more complex neural network-based approaches across the four datasets. The ablation study further supports the importance of key components in their framework, particularly the attention layer for tasks with large sequence length differences.

The paper also provides valuable insights about when different components of the framework (preprocessing vs. attention) are most important, depending on the characteristics of the sequence matching task (e.g., differences in sequence length). The authors' finding that a simple order-insensitive preprocessing layer can still outperform previous work with order-sensitive approaches is particularly interesting.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1) The paper could provide more detail on the implementation of the preprocessing layer. The current description is somewhat vague about how the gate values are learned and what they actually capture. A brief discussion of what the gate values represent (e.g., whether they're learning to ignore stop words, or other linguistic features) would be helpful.

2) The paper mentions that for MovieQA, the model outperforms previous work despite being order-insensitive, yet the authors don't fully explain why the word-by-word comparison is sufficient without an order-sensitive preprocessing layer. A more detailed analysis of the visualizations in Figure 2 would help clarify this important finding.

3) The paper should include statistical significance testing for the results. While the results are consistently better, it's difficult to say whether the improvements are statistically significant without proper testing (e.g., paired t-tests across multiple runs).

4) The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational efficiency of the different comparison functions. The element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) are likely much more computationally efficient than neural network-based approaches, which could be a significant advantage for large-scale applications.

5) The paper mentions that they use the same framework across all tasks, but for SNLI, K=1 (only one hypothesis to match against a premise), while for the other tasks K>1 (multiple candidate answers). The authors should clarify how they handle this difference in their model implementation, as it might affect the comparability of results.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1) The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, yet the model still outperforms previous work that uses order-sensitive preprocessing. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case, and whether the word-by-word comparison component is sufficient to capture the necessary context without an order-sensitive preprocessing layer?

2) For the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT), you mention they don't need parameters. However, in the SUBMULT+NN variant, you do use parameters (W and b). Could you clarify why you chose to add a neural network layer after the element-wise operations, and whether this is necessary for optimal performance?

3) The paper claims that SUB is related to Euclidean distance (as Euclidean distance is the sum of all entries of the vector produced by SUB), but doesn't elaborate on how this relationship affects the model's performance. Could you provide more insight into why preserving the individual dimension information (rather than summing them up as in Euclidean distance) leads to better performance?

4) The paper mentions that they don't update word embeddings during training. Given that the model is trained on multiple tasks, would fine-tuning the embeddings improve performance, and if so, why did the authors choose not to do this?
