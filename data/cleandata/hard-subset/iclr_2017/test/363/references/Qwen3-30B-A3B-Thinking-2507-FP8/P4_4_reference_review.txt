

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions
The paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching tasks, which first compares word-level representations from two sequences and then aggregates these comparisons to make a decision. The authors make two main contributions: (1) they demonstrate the general effectiveness of their framework across four different sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), and (2) they systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for word-level matching, finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform best across these tasks. The paper presents empirical results showing that their approach achieves strong performance on all four datasets, with the SUBMULT+NN combination being the most effective.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's main contribution lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core "compare-aggregate" framework has been previously proposed and used in multiple works (Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b; He & Lin, 2016), and the comparison functions evaluated (including SUB and MULT) have been used before in Mou et al. (2016). The empirical validation on multiple tasks, while thorough, does not constitute a significant advancement over existing work.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's primary claim—that the "compare-aggregate" framework is broadly effective across sequence matching tasks—is not novel. Previous work by Parikh et al. (2016) and others has already demonstrated the effectiveness of this framework on multiple tasks. The paper's contribution is limited to applying this existing framework to four different datasets and systematically testing six comparison functions.

The systematic evaluation of comparison functions is interesting but lacks novelty. The authors claim that SUB and MULT "have not been widely used," but Mou et al. (2016) already used these exact comparison functions. The paper fails to properly contextualize this work, citing Mou et al. only in the references but not in the comparison functions section where they should have been discussed. The authors also do not provide a clear theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work better, relying solely on empirical results.

The paper also has a technical limitation: the comparison function EUCCOS produces a 2-dimensional output vector, while all other comparison functions produce vectors of dimensionality l (the hidden layer size). This dimensionality mismatch could unfairly advantage EUCCOS in the aggregation layer, as the input dimensions to the CNN would differ. The authors do not address this issue, which undermines the validity of their comparison.

## 4. Additional feedback for improvement
- Clearly distinguish the paper's contribution from previous work. The framework is not novel, so the paper should focus on what makes their specific implementation and evaluation unique.
- Provide a theoretical justification for why element-wise comparison functions work better, rather than relying solely on empirical results.
- Address the dimensionality mismatch between EUCCOS and other comparison functions, and explain how this was handled in the experiments.
- Include more detailed analysis of why certain comparison functions work better than others, perhaps by examining the learned representations or by conducting additional ablation studies.
- Consider extending the evaluation to more diverse sequence matching tasks to better support the claim of general applicability.

## 5. Questions for the authors
1. How does the paper's "compare-aggregate" framework differ from the one proposed by Parikh et al. (2016) in terms of architecture and performance? The paper states there are "notable differences" but doesn't clearly articulate what they are.
2. Why do the authors believe that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) work better than more complex neural network-based functions, given that the latter have more parameters and expressiveness? Is there a theoretical justification for this?
3. The paper states that SUB and MULT have been used before by Mou et al. (2016), but the authors don't cite this work in the comparison functions section. Why wasn't Mou et al. (2016) properly cited as prior work using these comparison functions?
4. How does the dimensionality mismatch between EUCCOS (2-dimensional output) and the other comparison functions (l-dimensional output) affect the comparison of these methods? Did the authors adjust the model to account for this difference?
