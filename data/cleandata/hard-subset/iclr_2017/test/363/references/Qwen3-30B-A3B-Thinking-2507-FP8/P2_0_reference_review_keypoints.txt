- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" model and systematically evaluates six comparison functions for sequence matching.  
- Experiments cover four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) spanning three QA tasks and one entailment task.  
- Finds element-wise functions (SUB, MULT) perform especially well; SUBMULT+NN achieves best overall results.  
- Claims the framework is broadly applicable across multiple sequence-matching tasks.  
- Recommendation: Accept — clear, well-motivated contribution with thorough empirical evaluation.  
- Strength: Addresses a literature gap where prior work used the framework on only one or two tasks.  
- Strength: Strong empirical setup with multiple baselines, ablation studies, and CNN visualization.  
- Strength: Practical and surprising finding that simple element-wise comparisons can beat more complex neural comparators; code released for reproducibility.  
- Suggestion: Include statistical significance tests for reported performance gains.  
- Suggestion: Provide a deeper mathematical analysis explaining why SUB and MULT preserve information and relate to distances.  
- Suggestion: Investigate why order-insensitive preprocessing outperforms order-sensitive methods and the role of word-by-word comparison vs. context.  
- Suggestion: Clarify how the model handles MovieQA’s three-sequence matching (plot, question, answer).  
- Suggestion: Explain performance differences between parameter-free functions and parameterized SUBMULT+NN.  
- Question: Why might word-by-word comparison matter more than context in these tasks?  
- Question: Can you mathematically show how SUB preserves information lost by Euclidean distance?  
- Question: Why is the attention layer especially important for tasks with large length disparities (InsuranceQA, MovieQA), and does this generalize?  
- Question: Why was LSTM preprocessing used for SNLI but not other datasets, and how did that choice affect comparison function performance?
