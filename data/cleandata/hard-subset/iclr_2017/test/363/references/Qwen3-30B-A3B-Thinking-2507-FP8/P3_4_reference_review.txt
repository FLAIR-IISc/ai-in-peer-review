

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences that can be applied across multiple NLP tasks. The authors make two key contributions: (1) they demonstrate the framework's effectiveness across four diverse datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), showing it outperforms or matches state-of-the-art results; (2) they systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for word-level matching, finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform best across tasks. The paper is well-positioned in the literature, building upon recent work in sequence matching while addressing a clear gap in the systematic evaluation of comparison functions.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a meaningful contribution by demonstrating the generality of the compare-aggregate framework and providing a systematic evaluation of comparison functions. The empirical evidence across multiple tasks is strong, and the findings about element-wise operations are novel and valuable for future research.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strengths are significant. First, the experimental design is robust, testing the framework across four distinct sequence matching tasks with different characteristics (question answering vs. textual entailment, varying sequence length differences, etc.). This addresses a clear gap in the literature where most prior work has been task-specific. Second, the systematic comparison of six different comparison functions is well-executed, with the authors providing clear rationale for each and demonstrating their effectiveness through rigorous experimentation.

The paper's most valuable contribution is the finding that simple element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. This is counterintuitive given the trend toward more complex neural architectures and provides important guidance for future sequence matching work. The authors also provide valuable insights about when preprocessing and attention layers are most important, which adds practical value.

Some minor concerns: The paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the improvements over baselines, which would strengthen the claims. Additionally, the paper doesn't discuss computational efficiency comparisons between the different comparison functions, which might be relevant for practical applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Clarify novelty**: The paper mentions that SUB and MULT have been used by Mou et al. (2016) for word-level matching, but doesn't clearly explain why this is novel in the context of the compare-aggregate framework. Please clarify how the authors' use of these functions within this specific framework differs from previous work.

2. **Statistical significance**: Add statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) for the reported performance improvements over baselines. This is particularly important given that the improvements are often marginal.

3. **Computational efficiency**: Include a discussion of computational efficiency for each comparison function, as this would help practitioners choose between methods for real-world applications.

4. **Ablation study depth**: The ablation study in Table 4 is useful but could be expanded to include more detailed analysis of why certain comparison functions work better for certain tasks (e.g., why SUBMULT+NN works best on InsuranceQA but not on WikiQA).

5. **Comparison with more recent work**: The paper cites work up to 2016, but several strong sequence matching models have been proposed since then (e.g., BERT-based approaches). While the paper is not claiming to be a BERT-based model, it would be valuable to discuss how the proposed framework might integrate with or compare to transformer-based architectures.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "our model can still outperform the previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing layer" despite using an order-insensitive preprocessing layer. Could you elaborate on how the word-by-word comparison in the "compare-aggregate" framework compensates for the lack of context information in the preprocessing step?

2. For the WikiQA dataset, the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) achieved the best results without parameters. Given that these functions are simpler and don't require additional parameters to learn, could you discuss the trade-offs between model complexity and performance in this context?

3. The paper mentions that SUB is related to Euclidean distance but preserves dimensionality information. Could you provide more mathematical insight into why preserving this dimensionality information might be beneficial for sequence matching tasks?

4. The authors state that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role." Could you provide more specific examples from the visualizations (Figure 2) that support this claim?

This is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the sequence matching literature. The systematic evaluation of comparison functions provides important empirical evidence that will guide future research. With the minor revisions suggested above, this paper would be an excellent fit for ICLR.
