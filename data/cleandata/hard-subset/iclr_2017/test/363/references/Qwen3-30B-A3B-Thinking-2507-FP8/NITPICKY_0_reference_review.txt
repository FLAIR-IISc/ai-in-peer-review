

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching problems, testing it across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The key contribution is the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions at the word level, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that the authors claim outperform more complex neural network-based comparison functions. The authors demonstrate that their model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive results on three of the four datasets.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The paper contains multiple critical technical errors and inconsistencies in mathematical notation that undermine the credibility of the proposed model.
2. The paper lacks essential implementation details and statistical validation required to support its claims.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper contains numerous technical errors that make it difficult to assess the validity of the proposed model:

1. **Mathematical notation errors**: 
   - Equation (1) lacks a multiplication symbol between the two terms: "σ(WiQ+ bi ⊗ eQ) tanh(WuQ+ bu ⊗ eQ)" should be "σ(WiQ+ bi ⊗ eQ) * tanh(WuQ+ bu ⊗ eQ)"
   - Equation (3) has inconsistent spacing: "ReLU(W [ aj hj ] + b)" should be "ReLU(W [aj hj] + b)"
   - Equation (5) has incorrect punctuation: "cos(aj ,hj)" should be "cos(aj, hj)"
   - The paper states that SUB and MULT use "element-wise multiplication" but the notation "(aj − hj) (aj − hj)" and "aj hj" is ambiguous and inconsistent with the description.

2. **Critical missing details**:
   - The paper states "For the others, we set the batch size to be 30 and the learning rate 0.002" but doesn't specify which datasets "the others" refers to.
   - The dimensionality of the word embeddings (d) is not specified, though it's used in multiple equations.
   - The paper claims word embeddings "are not updated" but provides no justification for this significant design choice that could impact performance.
   - The CNN layer parameters (number of filters, window size) are completely omitted despite being crucial for reproducibility.

3. **Inconsistent and ambiguous terminology**:
   - The paper uses "l" to represent both the dimensionality of the hidden layers and the length of sequences (e.g., "Q ∈ Rd×Q" where Q is the length, but "l" is used for dimensionality in Equation 1).
   - The abstract claims "a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication works the best" but the paper evaluates SUB and MULT separately, with SUBMULT+NN being the best. This creates confusion about what exactly was found to be optimal.
   - The paper states "the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive" but fails to explain why this is beneficial or how it compares to order-sensitive approaches.

4. **Lack of statistical validation**:
   - The paper claims improvements over baselines but provides no statistical significance tests, making it impossible to determine if the reported improvements are meaningful.
   - The ablation study (Table 4) is mentioned but not provided, preventing verification of the claims about the importance of different layers.

5. **Formatting and reference issues**:
   - The reference section contains numerous formatting inconsistencies (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E" instead of proper citations).
   - The reference for Wan et al. is incomplete ("Wan et al., ).").
   - The paper mentions "Table 1" and "Figure 1" but neither is included in the provided text.

## Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. **Correct all mathematical notation errors** and ensure consistent use of symbols throughout the paper. For example, clarify whether "aj hj" represents element-wise multiplication or concatenation.

2. **Provide all essential implementation details** including:
   - The dimensionality of word embeddings (d)
   - The specific CNN parameters used (number of filters, window size)
   - Justification for not updating word embeddings during training
   - Clarification on what "the others" refers to in the implementation details

3. **Clarify the relationship between SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN** in both the abstract and the main text. The current wording creates confusion about what was found to be optimal.

4. **Include statistical significance testing** for all reported improvements over baselines.

5. **Fix all reference formatting issues** and ensure all references are complete.

6. **Provide the ablation study (Table 4)** to support the claims about the importance of different layers.

7. **Clarify the relationship between the preprocessing layer and sequence order**. If the layer is order-insensitive, explain why this is beneficial for the sequence matching tasks tested.

## Questions for Authors
1. Why did you choose to keep word embeddings fixed during training? Is this a limitation that should be addressed in future work?

2. What is the dimensionality of the word embeddings (d) used in your experiments? This is critical for reproducibility.

3. For the "others" datasets where you set batch size to 30 and learning rate to 0.002, which specific datasets were these?

4. Can you provide the complete ablation study (Table 4) to support your claims about the importance of the preprocessing and attention layers?

5. The paper claims "element-wise subtraction and multiplication" works best, but the best performing function is SUBMULT+NN. Please clarify whether you found the combination of SUB and MULT (without the NN layer) to be optimal or whether the NN layer was necessary for the best performance.

These issues collectively undermine the paper's credibility and scientific rigor. While the general framework is interesting, the numerous technical errors and missing details prevent me from confidently assessing the validity of the claims.
