- Proposes a general compare-aggregate framework for sequence matching and evaluates six comparison functions (including novel SUB and MULT) across four datasets, finding element-wise operations often outperform complex neural methods.
- Decision: Accept — paper provides systematic evaluation and shows simple element-wise comparisons are effective and generalizable.
- Well-motivated gap: prior work used compare-aggregate on single tasks without systematic comparison of comparison functions.
- Evaluates six comparison functions (three novel: SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) across diverse tasks, showing SUBMULT+NN often best and SUB/MULT strong on WikiQA and InsuranceQA.
- Strong experimental design: four tasks with varying characteristics demonstrate framework generalizability.
- Ablation study: preprocessing and attention layers are important, with their relative importance depending on sequence length differences.
- Key claim: simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural comparison functions, implying better efficiency and interpretability.
- Improvement: lacks theoretical justification for why element-wise operations preserve useful information better than scalar distances/similarities.
- Improvement: needs comparison to recent related work (e.g., attention-based models like IARNN-Gate) to better situate contributions.
- Improvement: results lack statistical significance testing to support performance differences.
- Improvement: unclear implications of the “order-insensitive” preprocessing layer; needs deeper discussion of architectural choices.
- Clarification sought: why EUCCOS outperforms element-wise methods on MovieQA despite overall claim.
- Clarification sought: explanation for why attention matters more for large length-difference tasks and preprocessing for small-difference tasks.
- Clarification sought: discussion of computational efficiency and inference-time benefits of element-wise operations versus neural approaches.
