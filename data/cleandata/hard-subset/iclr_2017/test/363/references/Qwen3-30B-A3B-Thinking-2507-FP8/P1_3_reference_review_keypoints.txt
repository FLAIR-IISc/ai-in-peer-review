- Two main contributions: demonstrating the broad effectiveness of the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple sequence-matching tasks and systematically evaluating six comparison functions.  
- Evaluated model on four datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) showing broad applicability.  
- Found that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) outperform more complex NN/NTN approaches.  
- Provides a semantic argument that element-wise operations preserve original vector information and align with similarity-based matching.  
- Empirical results are strong: state-of-the-art or competitive performance across the four tasks.  
- Well-positioned in literature, addressing prior work’s narrow scope and lack of systematic comparison.  
- Rigorous experimental setup with appropriate datasets, metrics, and ablation studies (preprocessing vs. attention).  
- Claims about element-wise function superiority are consistently supported across datasets.  
- Decision: Accept, justified by novelty, strong empirical support, and practical insights.  
- Suggestion: add statistical significance testing to strengthen empirical claims.  
- Suggestion: provide runtime/computational-efficiency comparisons for element-wise vs. complex functions.  
- Suggestion: expand ablation study to show performance loss at intermediate points, not just full removal.  
- Suggestion: include deeper qualitative analysis (examples where SUB/MULT outperform) to clarify learned behaviors.  
- Questions for authors: clarify why attention is more important for sequences with large length differences.  
- Question: explain why order-insensitive preprocessing can outperform order-sensitive methods and the relative importance of word-by-word comparison vs. context.  
- Question: provide a formal explanation for why SUB (related to Euclidean distance) preserves information and yields better performance.  
- Question: discuss challenges and adaptations required to use the same model structure across diverse tasks.
