

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper presents a systematic evaluation of the "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in natural language processing. The authors propose a general model that follows this framework and test it across four different datasets representing diverse sequence matching problems (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The key contribution lies in their systematic comparison of six different word-level comparison functions, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that they find to be particularly effective. The paper demonstrates that their model achieves competitive or superior performance across all four datasets compared to previous approaches, with the SUBMULT+NN combination performing best overall.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents a solid empirical study that makes a modest contribution to the literature, but the novelty is limited and the theoretical insights are insufficient to meet ICLR's high bar for acceptance.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main limitation is that it offers an incremental contribution rather than a significant advance. While the systematic evaluation of comparison functions is valuable, the paper fails to provide strong theoretical justification for why these particular functions (especially the element-wise operations) should work better than standard neural network approaches. The claim that SUB and MULT represent a "good middle ground" between highly flexible neural functions and restrictive similarity measures lacks sufficient motivation.

Furthermore, the paper does not sufficiently address the computational efficiency or scalability of the different comparison functions, which is particularly important for practical applications. The empirical results, while consistent, are not groundbreaking - the paper demonstrates that simple functions can outperform more complex ones, but this finding doesn't represent a significant theoretical or methodological advance.

The experimental section is well-organized and covers multiple datasets, but the ablation study is superficial. The authors mention removing the preprocessing and attention layers but provide minimal details about the impact of these removals on performance. The visualization in Figure 2 is also limited and doesn't provide deep insights into the model's behavior.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper for future publication, I recommend the following improvements:

1. Provide stronger theoretical motivation for the comparison functions, particularly the element-wise operations. Explain why these operations are theoretically better suited for sequence matching than standard neural network approaches.

2. Conduct a more thorough ablation study that includes not just removing layers but also analyzing the contribution of each comparison function component to the final result.

3. Compare the computational complexity and training time of each comparison function to provide practical insights for real-world applications.

4. Include more detailed analysis of why simple comparison functions outperform complex neural approaches. Are there specific characteristics of the datasets that make simple functions more effective?

5. Consider incorporating recent state-of-the-art models as baselines for the SNLI dataset, as the Chen et al. (2016) model might not represent the current SOTA.

6. Expand the visualization section to provide more meaningful insights into the model's decision-making process, rather than just showing attention weights.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper claims that SUB and MULT preserve useful information that is lost in EUCCOS. Can you provide more specific theoretical or empirical evidence to support this claim, rather than just stating it?

2. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive," yet the model still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. What specific aspects of the "compare-aggregate" structure (rather than the preprocessing) enable this strong performance despite the lack of order information?

3. The paper mentions that for sequence matching with big differences in length (MovieQA and InsuranceQA), the attention layer is more important, while for smaller length differences (WikiQA and SNLI), the preprocessing layer is more important. Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim, such as quantitative metrics on how much each layer contributes to performance on different datasets?

4. The paper uses element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) without parameters, yet they outperform neural network-based comparison functions. Could you explain why parameter-free operations might be better suited for sequence matching than parameterized neural approaches, which typically have more expressive power?
