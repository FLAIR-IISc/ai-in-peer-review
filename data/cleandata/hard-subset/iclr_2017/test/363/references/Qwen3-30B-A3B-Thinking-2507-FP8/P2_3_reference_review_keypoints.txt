- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for matching text sequences evaluated across four tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Key contributions: demonstrates framework effectiveness across multiple tasks and systematically evaluates six comparison functions.  
- Finds element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform neural-network and traditional similarity-based comparison functions.  
- Reports state-of-the-art results on three datasets and competitive performance on the fourth, supported by ablation studies.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Main reasons for acceptance: empirical validation of general applicability and the insightful finding that simple element-wise comparisons work best.  
- Strengths: well-motivated, rigorous experiments on diverse datasets, clear results, and sound methodology.  
- Contribution is incremental but fills an important empirical gap in the literature.  
- Suggestion: Clarify novelty and more precisely differentiate from Parikh et al. (2016).  
- Suggestion: Expand ablation studies to examine interactions between comparison functions and preprocessing/attention layers.  
- Suggestion: Provide theoretical or intuitive justification for why SUB/MULT perform well.  
- Suggestion: Compare results with more recent models (e.g., transformer-based approaches) beyond 2016.  
- Question: Provide quantitative evidence of the attention layer’s greater importance for tasks with large length differences (MovieQA, InsuranceQA) versus smaller ones (WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Question: Explain implications of order-insensitive preprocessing outperforming order-sensitive preprocessing—what this says about word-level vs. context-aware processing.  
- Question: Clarify statistical significance and practical meaning of SNLI results (SUBMULT+NN 87.1% vs baseline 87.2%).
