

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, testing it across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The authors make two key contributions: (1) demonstrating that this framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, and (2) systematically evaluating six different comparison functions at the word level, finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches. The paper is well-motivated, addressing limitations in previous work by testing on multiple tasks and providing a systematic comparison of comparison functions.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's key claim—that element-wise comparison functions work better for sequence matching—is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The experimental design lacks proper ablation studies and statistical rigor to establish the validity of their main claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper fails to properly isolate the contribution of the "compare-aggregate" framework itself. The authors claim their model is effective across multiple tasks, but their baselines (e.g., match-LSTM, Decomp Attention) use different architectures and aggregation methods, making it impossible to attribute performance gains specifically to the "compare-aggregate" framework. For instance, the Decomp Attention model also uses a compare-aggregate approach but with different aggregation (summing comparison vectors), so the comparison between their model and Decomp Attention doesn't isolate the framework's contribution.

Furthermore, the systematic comparison of six comparison functions lacks statistical rigor. The paper presents accuracy/MAP/MRR results but doesn't provide statistical significance tests (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) to determine if the performance differences between comparison functions are meaningful. For example, they claim SUBMULT+NN is "the best in general," but without statistical validation, it's unclear whether the small performance differences (e.g., 0.5% on WikiQA) are meaningful or due to random variation.

The paper also lacks a clear theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work better for sequence matching. While the authors note that SUB is "closely related to Euclidean distance" and MULT to "cosine similarity," they don't provide a compelling explanation for why these element-wise operations outperform neural network-based approaches for sequence matching tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (preprocessing, attention, comparison function, aggregation). For example, test how much performance drops when using standard aggregation (summing) instead of CNN, or when removing the attention layer.

2. Add statistical significance tests for all reported results. This is crucial for claiming that one comparison function "works best" across multiple datasets.

3. Provide more theoretical justification for why element-wise operations work better. The paper mentions that these functions "preserve some information" but doesn't explain why this information is particularly useful for sequence matching.

4. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art models (e.g., BERT-based approaches) that have since been published for these datasets. The paper cites works from 2015-2016, but many newer models have been proposed that could provide better baselines.

5. Clarify the relationship between their preprocessing layer and the attention layer. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive" but then claims their model outperforms models with "order-sensitive preprocessing," which seems contradictory.

## 5. Clarification Questions

1. How do you know that the performance gains are due to the "compare-aggregate" framework rather than the specific comparison functions you chose? Could the same comparison functions with a different aggregation method also achieve similar results?

2. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the results comparing the different comparison functions? For example, what is the p-value for the claim that SUBMULT+NN is "the best in general"?

3. The paper states that "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset." Why would parameter-free functions outperform parameterized ones in this case, and how does this align with the general principle that more flexible models can capture more complex patterns?

4. How do your results compare against more recent state-of-the-art models for these datasets (e.g., BERT-based models)? The paper cites works from 2015-2016, but many newer models have been proposed since then.
