- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework and evaluates six comparison functions across four NLP tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI).  
- Claims two contributions: (1) demonstrating framework's general applicability across tasks, and (2) systematic evaluation showing element-wise operations (SUB, MULT) outperform complex NN-based comparisons.  
- Recommends acceptance because the paper fills a literature gap and provides useful guidance on comparison functions.  
- Empirically rigorous: comprehensive results, proper baselines, ablation studies, visualizations, and state-of-the-art or competitive performance on all datasets.  
- Ablation study shows both preprocessing and attention layers are important; their relative importance depends on sequence length differences.  
- Key insight: simple element-wise comparisons can outperform more flexible neural comparison functions, challenging common assumptions.  
- Suggestion: add deeper theoretical discussion explaining why element-wise operations work better than NN approaches.  
- Suggestion: clarify/justify the preprocessing layer being "order-insensitive" and how the model handles word-order-sensitive tasks.  
- Suggestion: resolve apparent contradiction where SUBMULT+NN is claimed best overall but EUCCOS nearly matches on WikiQA.  
- Suggestion: include discussion of computational efficiency for different comparison functions.  
- Question: why do WikiQA results appear to contradict the claim that element-wise operations are superior?  
- Question: how does the model compensate for order-insensitive preprocessing when word order matters?  
- Question: interpretation of ablation result that attention removal hurts tasks with large length differences while preprocessing removal hurts tasks with small length differences?  
- Question: why use one-directional attention instead of two-directional (as in Parikh et al.), and what impact would two-directional attention have?
