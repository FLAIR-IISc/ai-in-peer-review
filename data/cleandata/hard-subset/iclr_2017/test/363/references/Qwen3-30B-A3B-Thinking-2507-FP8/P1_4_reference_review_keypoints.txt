- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching and evaluates it across tasks.  
- Identifies two gaps: limited cross-task evaluation in prior work and lack of systematic comparison of word-level comparison functions.  
- Applies the unified model to four datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.  
- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including novel element-wise SUB and MULT.  
- Finds the compare-aggregate framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks.  
- Finds element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) perform particularly well across tasks.  
- Recommends acceptance, citing strong empirical validation and novel insights.  
- Well-motivated paper with a thorough and well-organized literature review distinguishing related approaches.  
- Rigorous empirical evaluation: tested on four diverse datasets and compared to appropriate baselines.  
- Ablation study provides insight into component importance (preprocessing, attention, comparison).  
- Surprising and valuable result that simple parameter-free element-wise functions can outperform NN/NTN/EUCCOS.  
- Code is publicly released, enhancing reproducibility.  
- Suggests deeper analysis to explain why SUB and MULT work so well, linking to task characteristics.  
- Recommends expanding the ablation study to show per-component effects across all four datasets.  
- Requests clarification on why LSTM was added to preprocessing for SNLI and its impact.  
- Asks for broader implications and more concrete future directions (e.g., multi-task learning).  
- Questions: why parameter-free functions excel on WikiQA specifically?  
- Questions: why the framework is less dominant on SNLI compared to QA tasks?  
- Questions: how to interpret order-insensitive preprocessing outperforming order-sensitive methods?  
- Questions: how would different aggregation methods (attention, summation) affect results and interact with comparison functions?
