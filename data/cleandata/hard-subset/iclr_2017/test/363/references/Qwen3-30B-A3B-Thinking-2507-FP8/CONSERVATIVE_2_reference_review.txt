

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims
This paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching problems in NLP, systematically evaluating six different comparison functions (NN, NTN, EUCCOS, SUB, MULT, SUBMULT+NN) across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI). The authors claim that their framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks and that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) work best across the board, with SUBMULT+NN providing the most consistent performance. The paper positions itself as filling a gap in previous work by systematically evaluating comparison functions rather than just using standard neural networks for this purpose.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's main claim that element-wise comparison functions (particularly SUBMULT+NN) are universally superior is not adequately supported by the empirical evidence. The contribution is incremental rather than transformative, and the evidence provided does not convincingly demonstrate the claimed generality of the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes several claims that are not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented:

First, the paper claims that "a comparison function based on element-wise subtraction and multiplication works the best on the four datasets," but the results in Table 3 show significant task dependence. EUCCOS achieves the best performance on MovieQA, MULT on WikiQA, and SUBMULT+NN on InsuranceQA and SNLI. The paper states that SUBMULT+NN is "the best in general," but this is not clearly demonstrated as it does not consistently outperform other functions across all datasets.

Second, the paper claims that "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset." However, the best result on WikiQA is achieved with MULT, which is an element-wise function, but the paper also reports that SUBMULT+NN (which does require parameters) is used for the best result on other datasets. The paper's claim that these functions "do not need parameters" is misleading.

Third, the paper lacks statistical significance testing for the reported improvements, which is a standard requirement in NLP papers. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the reported improvements are meaningful or simply due to random variation.

Fourth, the paper's claim that the "compare-aggregate" framework is broadly effective across "many sequence matching problems" is underwhelmingly supported by testing on just four related tasks (all question answering or entailment). This is insufficient evidence to claim general applicability.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper has some useful empirical insights about comparison functions, but it would benefit from:

1. A clearer explanation of why certain comparison functions work better on specific tasks, rather than just reporting results. The paper's claim that element-wise operations are "a good middle ground" between neural network-based and distance/similarity measures lacks theoretical justification.

2. More rigorous statistical analysis to support the claims about performance differences. The paper should report p-values or confidence intervals for the comparisons.

3. A clearer distinction between the two types of element-wise functions (SUB/MULT vs. SUBMULT+NN) and their respective parameter requirements.

4. A more honest discussion of the task-specific nature of the results. The paper should acknowledge that the best comparison function may depend on the specific characteristics of the sequence matching problem, rather than claiming a universal "best" approach.

## 5. Questions for the Authors
1. Given that different comparison functions perform best on different datasets (EUCCOS on MovieQA, MULT on WikiQA, etc.), how would you recommend selecting the appropriate comparison function for a new sequence matching task? The paper's claim of a universal "best" approach seems contradicted by the data.

2. The paper claims that "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset," but the best result on WikiQA is achieved with MULT (an element-wise function), while the best result on InsuranceQA and SNLI is achieved with SUBMULT+NN (which does require parameters). Can you clarify this apparent contradiction in the paper's claims?

3. The paper states that "with any comparison function we use, our model can outperform all the previous models" on InsuranceQA. However, the best baseline for InsuranceQA (IARNN-Gate) reports 78.7% accuracy, while the paper's best result is 79.8%. Can you clarify how this compares to the previous best result, and provide statistical significance testing for this improvement?

4. The paper mentions that SUB is "closely related to Euclidean distance" but doesn't explain why preserving the dimensionality of the comparison vectors (as SUB does) would be beneficial for sequence matching. Can you provide more theoretical justification for this approach?
