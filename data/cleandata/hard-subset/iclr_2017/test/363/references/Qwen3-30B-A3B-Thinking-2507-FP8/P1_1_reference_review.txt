

# Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching in natural language processing, addressing two key limitations in previous work: (1) Most prior models were tested on only one or two tasks, making it unclear if the framework generalizes well across different sequence matching problems, and (2) Previous studies didn't systematically evaluate different comparison functions for matching word-level units.

The authors make two significant contributions: First, they demonstrate the general effectiveness of the "compare-aggregate" framework across four different datasets representing different tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA for question answering, and SNLI for textual entailment). Second, they conduct a systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) that have not been widely used before. Their findings show that element-wise comparison functions, particularly SUB and MULT, work exceptionally well across all four datasets, with SUBMULT+NN achieving the best overall performance.

The paper provides a thorough experimental evaluation with ablation studies, demonstrating that their model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on all four datasets, and that the choice of comparison function significantly impacts performance.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple sequence matching tasks.
2. The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed, with results on four diverse datasets that convincingly demonstrate the general applicability of the framework.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by addressing two important limitations in previous work on sequence matching:

First, the authors demonstrate that the "compare-aggregate" framework is broadly applicable across different sequence matching tasks. Previous work (e.g., Wang & Jiang, 2016b; Parikh et al., 2016) tested their models on only one or two tasks, but this paper shows the framework works well across four different datasets with varying characteristics (three QA tasks with different setups and one entailment task). This is a significant contribution because it suggests the framework is not task-specific but can be generalized.

Second, the systematic evaluation of six different comparison functions is particularly valuable. The authors don't just test neural network-based comparisons (NN, NTN), but also consider more interpretable functions like Euclidean distance and cosine similarity (EUCCOS), and introduce two new element-wise functions (SUB, MULT). Their finding that element-wise operations work well is counterintuitive and important - it suggests that simpler functions can outperform more complex neural network approaches for this specific task.

The experimental results are convincing. The authors show that their model achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on all four datasets. For example, on InsuranceQA, their model outperforms all previous baselines with any comparison function. On WikiQA, the simplest element-wise comparison functions achieve the best performance. The ablation studies further strengthen the claims by showing the importance of different components (attention layer vs. preprocessing layer) depending on task characteristics.

The paper is well-structured, clearly written, and provides sufficient technical details. The code availability is a nice touch that will help reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, here are suggestions to further improve it:

1. **Clarify the novelty of element-wise functions**: The paper states that SUB and MULT "have not been widely used," but it would be helpful to explicitly state why these functions weren't used before and why they're particularly well-suited for sequence matching. A more detailed theoretical justification would strengthen the paper.

2. **Provide deeper analysis of why element-wise functions work**: The paper shows that SUB and MULT work well but doesn't explain why. A deeper analysis of the representations learned by these functions would provide more insight into their effectiveness.

3. **Discuss relationship to recent work**: The paper cites work up to 2016, but there have been significant advances in sequence matching since then (e.g., BERT-based models). A brief discussion of how the authors' approach relates to these newer methods would help position the work in the current landscape.

4. **Expand on the order-insensitive preprocessing layer**: The paper notes that their order-insensitive preprocessing still outperforms order-sensitive approaches. This is an interesting finding that deserves more discussion - why might word-level matching be more important than contextual information for these tasks?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that SUB is closely related to Euclidean distance but doesn't sum the entries. Could you provide more intuition about why not summing the entries preserves useful information? Is there a theoretical justification for this?

2. The paper shows that element-wise functions work well, but doesn't explain why neural network-based comparison functions (NN, NTN) underperform. Given their flexibility, why would they not be better suited for this task? Is it because they overfit to specific tasks?

3. For the MovieQA task, the authors note that their order-insensitive preprocessing still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case for this specific task? Is it because sequence matching is more about word-level matching than contextual information?

4. The paper uses GloVe embeddings without updating them during training. What would be the impact of fine-tuning the embeddings for these tasks? Would this change the relative performance of the different comparison functions?

5. The paper claims the model is generalizable across tasks. How would you expect the optimal comparison function to change across different types of sequence matching tasks? For example, would you expect different functions to be optimal for entailment vs. QA tasks?
