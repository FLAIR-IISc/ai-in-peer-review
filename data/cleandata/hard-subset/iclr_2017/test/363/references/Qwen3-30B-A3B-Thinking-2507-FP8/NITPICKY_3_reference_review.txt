

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## Summary
The paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching problems across multiple NLP tasks. The authors systematically evaluate six different comparison functions for word-level matching, including novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT), and demonstrate the framework's effectiveness across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). The authors claim that their approach outperforms previous work on these datasets and that element-wise comparison functions work best across the board.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The paper contains significant methodological inconsistencies that undermine the clarity and scientific rigor of the work.
2. The paper lacks statistical significance testing for the reported results, which is essential for claims about performance improvements.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's methodological flaws are substantial enough to warrant rejection, despite the potentially interesting core idea. Several critical issues need to be addressed:

1. **Inconsistent and confusing notation**: The paper uses ⊗ to denote both element-wise multiplication (as stated in the text) and what appears to be Kronecker product in equations (1) and (2). This is a serious notational error that makes the model definition ambiguous. For example, in equation (1), "bi ⊗ eQ" is described as "repeating the vector or scalar on the left for Q times," which suggests element-wise multiplication, but the symbol ⊗ is standardly used for Kronecker product in mathematics and machine learning literature.

2. **Unsupported and counterintuitive claim**: The paper states "In this preprocessing step, the word order does not matter" (section 2.2), but provides no justification for this claim. This is particularly problematic for NLP tasks where word order is fundamental. The authors later note they "only incorporated LSTM into the formulas above for the SNLI task," which suggests word order does matter for some tasks but not others, contradicting their initial claim.

3. **Misleading claims about parameter requirements**: The paper states "the element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset" (section 3.3). This is factually incorrect as SUB and MULT functions are defined in equations (6) and (7) without parameters, but the resulting vectors are then fed into a CNN with parameters (equation 9). The authors should clarify that while the comparison functions themselves don't require parameters, the overall model still has parameters.

4. **Lack of statistical significance testing**: The paper reports accuracy scores without providing standard deviations or statistical tests (e.g., t-tests) to determine if the improvements over baselines are meaningful. This is a critical omission for machine learning papers, as small differences could be due to random variation rather than model superiority.

5. **Inconsistent presentation of comparison functions**: The paper claims to evaluate "six different comparison functions" but fails to clearly define the SUBMULT+NN function (equation 8) in the text, instead only providing the formula. The relationship between SUB and Euclidean distance is also misrepresented (Euclidean distance is the sum of the elements of the vector produced by SUB, not the same as SUB).

6. **Formatting inconsistencies**: The references contain multiple formatting errors (e.g., "Bahdanau et al\.,? \Q2014\E"), and the paper mentions code availability in a footnote rather than in the main text, which is non-standard for ICLR papers.

## Additional Feedback
- Clarify the notation throughout the paper, especially the use of ⊗. If element-wise multiplication is intended, use * or ⊙ instead of ⊗.
- Justify why the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive, as this contradicts standard NLP practices and needs strong evidence.
- Add statistical significance testing to the results section to validate the claimed performance improvements.
- Correct the misleading claim about element-wise comparison functions not needing parameters.
- Provide a clear definition of the SUBMULT+NN function in the text, not just in equation (8).
- Move the code availability link from the footnote to the main text.

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide evidence or justification for why word order does not matter in the preprocessing step, given that this is a fundamental aspect of most sequence matching tasks in NLP?
2. How do you reconcile the claim that "word order does not matter" with the fact that you incorporate LSTM (which explicitly models order) for the SNLI task?
3. Given that SUB and MULT are defined without parameters (equations 6-7), why does the paper claim these functions "do not need parameters" when the overall model still has parameters in the aggregation layer?
4. Why does the paper claim that SUB is "closely related to Euclidean distance" when Euclidean distance is actually the L2 norm of the vector produced by SUB (i.e., the sum of squares), not the same as SUB?
5. How do you explain the fact that the simplest comparison function (EUCCOS) achieves nearly the best performance on MovieQA, while the element-wise functions work best on WikiQA, given your claim that element-wise functions are generally superior?
