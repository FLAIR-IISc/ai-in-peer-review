

# Peer Review: "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions within the "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching. The authors argue that previous work has not adequately explored the comparison function used to match small textual units (e.g., words), despite the framework's widespread adoption. They introduce a general model that can be applied across multiple sequence matching tasks and systematically evaluate six different comparison functions (including two novel element-wise operations: SUB and MULT) on four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI). Their key finding is that element-wise comparison functions outperform more complex neural network-based approaches, with the combination of SUB and MULT followed by a neural network (SUBMULT+NN) achieving the best overall results.

## 2. Decision

**Weak Accept**

The paper makes a valuable contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions across multiple sequence matching tasks, addressing a clear gap in the literature. However, the novelty of the approach is somewhat limited, and the paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for why element-wise functions work better.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strengths include:

- **Comprehensive empirical evaluation**: Testing on four distinct datasets representing different sequence matching tasks (three question answering tasks and one textual entailment task) provides strong evidence for the generalizability of their framework. This is a significant improvement over previous work that typically evaluates on only one or two tasks.

- **Useful practical insight**: The finding that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural network approaches is valuable for practitioners. For instance, on the InsuranceQA dataset, their model outperforms all previous approaches using any comparison function.

- **Clear framework**: The paper presents a well-structured "compare-aggregate" model that is easy to understand and implement.

However, there are important limitations:

- **Limited novelty**: The "compare-aggregate" framework itself is not new (Parikh et al., 2016; Wang & Jiang, 2016b), and the comparison functions they test include some that have been used before (Euclidean distance, cosine similarity, neural networks). The element-wise functions SUB and MULT have been used by Mou et al. (2016), which the paper cites but doesn't sufficiently build upon.

- **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper claims element-wise functions work better but provides no explanation for why this is the case. Without understanding *why* these simple functions outperform more complex ones, the contribution is primarily empirical rather than conceptual.

- **Inadequate ablation analysis**: While the paper removes preprocessing and attention layers for ablation, it doesn't provide deeper analysis of how different comparison functions affect the attention weights or learned representations, which would help explain their performance differences.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Clarify novelty**: The paper should more clearly distinguish between the novelty of the systematic evaluation framework versus the novelty of the comparison functions themselves. The contribution is primarily in the empirical evaluation across multiple tasks, not in the comparison functions.

- **Provide theoretical justification**: The authors should explore why element-wise operations work better. Could it be that semantic similarity is inherently a local, element-wise property? This would strengthen the paper's contribution.

- **Expand related work**: The paper should more thoroughly compare with recent work on sequence matching, particularly those using similar frameworks (e.g., the decomposable attention model by Parikh et al. (2016) which the authors cite but don't directly compare with in their experiments).

- **Include computational analysis**: The paper should discuss the computational efficiency of the different comparison functions, as this would be important for practical applications.

- **Clarify the role of the preprocessing layer**: The paper notes that their order-insensitive preprocessing outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing, but doesn't explain this counterintuitive finding.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper cites Mou et al. (2016) as having used SUB and MULT, but doesn't explain how their work differs from or builds upon that. Can the authors clarify the relationship between their work and Mou et al. (2016)?

2. The paper claims element-wise comparison functions work better but provides no theoretical explanation. Can the authors provide more insight into why these simple functions might capture semantic similarity more effectively than neural networks?

3. For the MovieQA dataset, the authors report that EUCCOS (Euclidean + Cosine) achieves nearly the best performance. However, for WikiQA, the element-wise functions (which don't require parameters) achieve the best results. Can the authors explain this apparent contradiction?

4. The paper uses accuracy for most datasets but MAP and MRR for WikiQA. Given that WikiQA has multiple correct answers, why not use the same metric (accuracy) for all datasets to enable more direct comparison?

This paper makes a valuable empirical contribution to the sequence matching literature, particularly through its multi-task evaluation. With the suggested clarifications and deeper analysis, it would be a strong addition to ICLR. The core finding about the effectiveness of element-wise comparison functions is promising and warrants further investigation.
