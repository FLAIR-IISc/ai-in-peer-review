

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a "compare-aggregate" model for sequence matching tasks across multiple NLP domains. The authors make two key contributions: (1) they demonstrate the general applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across four distinct sequence matching tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI), and (2) they systematically evaluate six different comparison functions, including two novel element-wise approaches (SUB and MULT) that outperform neural network-based comparison functions across the datasets. The paper provides empirical evidence that simple comparison functions based on element-wise operations can be highly effective for sequence matching problems.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. While the paper makes a valuable empirical contribution by testing the compare-aggregate framework across multiple tasks and evaluating different comparison functions, the novelty is limited, and the paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for its key findings.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

**Limited Novelty**: The core "compare-aggregate" framework is not novel, as the paper correctly acknowledges (citing Wang & Jiang, 2016b; Parikh et al., 2016; etc.). More critically, the comparison functions SUB and MULT were previously used by Mou et al. (2016), which the authors cite but fail to adequately distinguish their work from. The paper claims these functions "have not been widely used," but the Mou et al. (2016) paper specifically uses them. This significantly weakens the novelty claim. The paper doesn't explain how their systematic evaluation across four different tasks provides substantially new insights beyond what Mou et al. might have shown on a single task.

**Lack of Theoretical Justification**: The paper identifies an interesting empirical finding—that element-wise comparison functions work well—but provides no theoretical explanation for why this is the case. The authors note that SUB is "closely related to Euclidean distance" and MULT to "cosine similarity," but they don't provide a principled argument for why these simpler operations outperform neural network-based comparison functions. Without this theoretical grounding, the finding appears to be empirical without deeper understanding, which is less valuable for the NLP community.

**Insufficient Baseline Comparison**: For some datasets, the paper cites baselines that may not represent the current state of the art. For example, on SNLI, the paper cites "EBIM+TreeLSTM" as the state-of-the-art, but the SNLI challenge results from 2015-2016 include several stronger models. The authors should have compared against the most recent and strongest baselines for each dataset to properly establish their model's contribution.

**Limited Analysis of Task Differences**: The paper notes that the model's performance varies across tasks (e.g., attention layer is more important for tasks with large sequence length differences like MovieQA, while preprocessing is more important for tasks with smaller length differences like WikiQA). However, it doesn't provide a deeper analysis of *why* these differences exist, which would be valuable for understanding the framework's limitations and applicability.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

- **Clarify Novelty**: Explicitly distinguish this work from Mou et al. (2016) and explain why their systematic evaluation across four different tasks provides new insights beyond what was previously known from a single task. If the novelty is primarily in the cross-task validation, make that the central claim.

- **Provide Theoretical Justification**: Add a section explaining why element-wise operations might be well-suited for sequence matching problems. Consider analyzing the mathematical properties of these functions in relation to semantic similarity measures.

- **Update Baselines**: Compare against the most recent and strongest baselines for each dataset. For SNLI, for example, include results from models like the ones in the SNLI challenge papers from 2016.

- **Expand Task Analysis**: Provide deeper analysis of why the model performs differently across tasks. This could include examining the specific characteristics of the datasets and how they relate to the model's components.

- **Include Computational Efficiency Analysis**: Consider adding a discussion of computational efficiency for different comparison functions, as this could be an important practical consideration for real-world applications.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does your systematic evaluation of comparison functions across four different tasks provide novel insights beyond what Mou et al. (2016) demonstrated on a single task?

2. Can you provide a theoretical explanation for why element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform neural network-based comparison functions, beyond their relationship to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity?

3. Have you compared your model with more recent baselines for the SNLI dataset (e.g., models from the SNLI challenge results published after 2015)?

4. Can you provide more insight into why the model works particularly well on InsuranceQA and MovieQA but less so on WikiQA and SNLI, beyond noting the sequence length differences?

The paper makes a useful empirical contribution to the sequence matching literature, but the limited novelty and lack of theoretical justification prevent it from meeting ICLR's standards for acceptance. With the suggested revisions, it could become a stronger contribution to the field.
