

# Review for "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks across multiple NLP domains. The authors make two main contributions: (1) They demonstrate the broad applicability of the compare-aggregate framework across four different datasets (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI), and (2) They systematically evaluate six different comparison functions at the word level, finding that element-wise operations (SUB and MULT) perform best across these datasets. The paper is well-structured, with a clear problem definition, thorough experimental setup, and reasonable baselines for comparison. The work builds on established sequence matching approaches and provides empirical validation for a common framework.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's main claim that element-wise comparison functions work "best" across multiple sequence matching tasks is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The empirical results are inconsistent across datasets, and the paper fails to provide rigorous justification for why these simple element-wise operations outperform more expressive neural network-based comparison functions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim—that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) are superior to neural network-based comparison functions across multiple sequence matching tasks—lacks sufficient empirical and theoretical support for acceptance at ICLR.

First, the paper's evidence does not demonstrate consistent superiority of element-wise operations. The authors claim "SUBMULT+NN is the best in general," but their results show that different comparison functions perform best on different datasets: EUCCOS nearly best on MovieQA, element-wise functions best on WikiQA, and SUBMULT+NN best overall. This inconsistency undermines the claim of a general "best" approach. A strong claim of superiority across multiple tasks requires consistent evidence, not a single "best" model on an average metric.

Second, the paper lacks crucial ablation studies to explain why element-wise operations work better. The authors claim these operations are a "good middle ground" between neural networks and simple similarity measures, but they don't provide evidence that this middle ground is actually optimal. They fail to compare against simpler alternatives (e.g., a linear layer with fixed weights), which would better establish whether the element-wise operations are genuinely special or merely a convenient approximation.

Third, the paper doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the results. Given the multiple comparisons across four datasets, the authors should report statistical significance of their improvements over baselines to ensure the results aren't due to chance.

The paper's methodological approach is somewhat ad hoc. While the authors test six comparison functions, they don't provide a principled theoretical justification for why element-wise operations should outperform neural network-based approaches. The paper's motivation for exploring these specific element-wise operations is weak—they simply state they're "closely related" to Euclidean distance and cosine similarity without demonstrating why this relationship should lead to better performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen this work for potential future publication, the authors should:

1. Conduct more rigorous ablation studies to understand why element-wise operations work better on specific tasks. For example, they could analyze the feature representations learned by different comparison functions to see if there's a clear pattern.

2. Provide statistical significance tests for their results, especially when comparing across multiple datasets. The paper should report p-values or confidence intervals for their performance gains.

3. Compare their element-wise approach against a more meaningful baseline—specifically, a neural network-based comparison function with the same number of parameters as their element-wise approach. This would better establish whether the element-wise operations are truly superior or merely a parameter-efficient approximation.

4. Clarify the relationship between the element-wise operations and the specific characteristics of the datasets. The paper notes that "for sequence matching with big difference in length, such as the MovieQA and InsuranceQA tasks, the attention layer plays a more important role," but doesn't explain why element-wise operations work better on certain tasks.

5. Provide a more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between model complexity and performance. The paper claims element-wise operations are a "good middle ground," but doesn't quantify this trade-off.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide statistical significance tests for your main results, particularly the comparisons between SUBMULT+NN and the next best baseline across the four datasets?

2. Why did the element-wise comparison functions perform best on WikiQA but not on the other datasets, given that WikiQA is a ranking task while the others are classification tasks? What specific characteristics of WikiQA might explain this difference?

3. Could you compare your best element-wise approach (SUBMULT+NN) against a neural network-based comparison function with an equivalent number of parameters? This would help establish whether the element-wise approach is truly superior or merely a parameter-efficient approximation.

4. The paper states that "element-wise comparison functions, which do not need parameters can achieve the best performance on the WikiQA dataset." This is surprising given the complexity of the task. Could you elaborate on why parameter-free approaches work better for this specific dataset?
