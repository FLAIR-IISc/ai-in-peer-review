- Proposes systematic evaluation of six comparison functions within a "compare-aggregate" framework across four datasets, introducing SUB and MULT element-wise operations and finding SUBMULT+NN performs best.
- Decision: Weak Accept — valuable empirical contribution but limited novelty and weak theoretical justification.
- Strength: Comprehensive empirical evaluation across diverse tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, SNLI) showing generalizability.
- Strength: Practical insight that simple element-wise operations can outperform more complex neural comparison functions.
- Strength: Presents a clear, easy-to-implement compare-aggregate framework.
- Limitation: Limited novelty because the framework and several comparison functions have been used previously.
- Limitation: Lacks theoretical explanation for why element-wise functions outperform neural approaches.
- Limitation: Ablation analysis is inadequate; does not analyze effects on attention weights or learned representations.
- Feedback: Clarify novelty — emphasize empirical/systematic evaluation vs. novelty of functions.
- Feedback: Provide theoretical or empirical analysis explaining why element-wise operations work better.
- Feedback: Expand related work and directly compare to similar models (e.g., decomposable attention by Parikh et al.).
- Feedback: Include computational efficiency comparisons of different comparison functions.
- Feedback: Clarify why order-insensitive preprocessing outperforms order-sensitive alternatives.
- Question: Clarify how this work differs from or builds on Mou et al. (2016) regarding SUB and MULT.
- Question: Explain why element-wise functions might capture semantic similarity better than neural networks.
- Question: Explain dataset-specific performance differences (e.g., EUCCOS near-best on MovieQA vs. element-wise best on WikiQA).
- Question: Justify metric choices (accuracy vs. MAP/MRR) and consider using consistent metrics for comparability.
