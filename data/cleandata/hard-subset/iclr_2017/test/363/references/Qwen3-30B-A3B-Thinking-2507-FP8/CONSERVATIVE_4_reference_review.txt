

# Review

## Summary of Claims
The paper proposes a systematic evaluation of different comparison functions within the "compare-aggregate" framework for text sequence matching. The authors claim to demonstrate that this framework is effective across four different NLP tasks (MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI) and that element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) outperform standard neural network-based approaches. They present six comparison functions, including two new ones (SUB and MULT) that operate on vectors in an element-wise manner, and report that the combination of these with a neural network (SUBMULT+NN) achieves the best performance.

## Decision
Reject. The paper presents an incremental contribution with insufficient evidence to support its claims.

## Supporting Arguments
As a reviewer who prioritizes methodological rigor and established approaches, I find this paper's contribution to be insufficiently novel and its validation inadequate for ICLR standards. The key issues are:

1. **Lack of sufficient novelty**: The "compare-aggregate" framework is not new (Wang & Jiang, 2016b; Parikh et al., 2016), and the paper does not demonstrate that this framework itself is a significant advancement. The main novelty is the systematic evaluation of comparison functions, but these functions (particularly SUB and MULT) have been previously used by Mou et al. (2016), as cited in the paper. The authors don't provide a compelling justification for why these element-wise functions work better than neural network-based approaches, beyond superficial claims about being a "good middle ground."

2. **Inadequate empirical validation**: The paper compares against baselines that are not state-of-the-art for many of the tasks. For example, on SNLI, they don't compare against more recent transformer-based models that have achieved significant improvements. The reported results are presented as "best" or "competitive" without statistical significance tests. The ablation study in Table 4 is insufficient to isolate the contribution of the comparison function from other model components.

3. **Overstated claims about generalization**: The paper claims to demonstrate the framework's effectiveness across "a wide range of sequence matching problems," but all four datasets represent variants of question answering or textual entailment—tasks that are closely related in nature. This does not constitute a sufficiently diverse set of problems to support the claim of general applicability.

4. **Lack of theoretical justification**: The paper provides no theoretical analysis of why element-wise operations might be better suited for sequence matching problems. The justification is purely empirical and lacks the depth needed to convince a skeptical reviewer.

5. **Inconsistent comparison**: The paper states that "the simplest comparison function EUCCOS achieves nearly the best performance in the MovieQA dataset," yet they also claim that SUBMULT+NN is the best overall. This inconsistency suggests the results may be highly task-dependent without sufficient explanation.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen this work for potential future publication, the authors should:

1. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art models on all datasets, particularly on SNLI where transformer-based approaches have significantly advanced the field.

2. Provide statistical significance tests for all reported performance differences.

3. Conduct a more thorough ablation study that isolates the contribution of the comparison function from other model components.

4. Provide a more compelling theoretical justification for why element-wise operations might work better for sequence matching tasks.

5. Test the framework on more diverse sequence matching problems beyond question answering and textual entailment to truly demonstrate its general applicability.

## Clarifying Questions
1. Why did the authors choose to use the same preprocessing and attention layers across all four datasets without any task-specific adaptation, especially given the different nature of the tasks?

2. What statistical tests were performed to determine if the performance differences between the comparison functions are significant?

3. How do the authors explain the fact that the simplest comparison function (EUCCOS) achieves nearly the best performance on MovieQA, while the more complex SUBMULT+NN is the best overall? This seems contradictory to the paper's main claim.

4. Why were the element-wise comparison functions (SUB and MULT) not used in previous work for sequence matching, and what specific aspects of the current tasks make them particularly effective?

5. How would the authors respond to the possibility that the performance gains are simply due to better hyperparameter tuning rather than a superior comparison function?
