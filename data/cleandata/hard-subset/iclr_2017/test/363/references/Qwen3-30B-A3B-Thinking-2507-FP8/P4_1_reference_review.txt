

# Review of "A COMPARE-AGGREGATE MODEL FOR MATCHING TEXT SEQUENCES"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching tasks in NLP, demonstrating its effectiveness across four different datasets representing different tasks (three question answering datasets: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and one textual entailment dataset: SNLI). The authors' key contributions are: (1) showing that this general framework is effective across multiple sequence matching tasks, not limited to one or two as previous work had done, and (2) systematically evaluating six different comparison functions for matching word-level representations, finding that element-wise operations (SUB, MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) work particularly well across datasets.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a valuable empirical contribution by systematically evaluating comparison functions in the "compare-aggregate" framework across multiple NLP tasks, filling a clear gap in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a well-motivated contribution that addresses two clear limitations in prior work: (1) previous models were tested on limited tasks, and (2) comparison functions were not systematically evaluated. The authors' systematic evaluation of six comparison functions (including novel element-wise operations) is scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, evaluation metrics, and ablation studies.

The empirical results are compelling: their general framework achieves state-of-the-art or highly competitive performance across all four datasets. The finding that simple element-wise comparison functions (SUB, MULT) outperform more complex neural network-based approaches (NN, NTN) is significant and well-supported by the data. The ablation studies further strengthen the claims by demonstrating the importance of different components (preprocessing, attention) in different task settings.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with thorough comparison to relevant prior work in the related work section. The methodology is clearly described, and the experimental setup is appropriate for the tasks being evaluated.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of why element-wise operations work well, beyond the brief explanation provided in Section 2.3. A theoretical justification or more thorough analysis of the properties of these operations would strengthen the contribution.
- The claim that the preprocessing layer is "order-insensitive" but still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing needs more elaboration. The paper states this finding but doesn't fully explain how the model compensates for the lack of contextual information.
- The visualization in Figure 2 is referenced but not included in the provided text. Including this visualization would help readers better understand the model's behavior.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the preprocessing layer is order-insensitive but still outperforms previous work with order-sensitive preprocessing. Can the authors elaborate on why this is the case, and what specific aspects of the word-by-word comparison are most important for these tasks?
2. The authors use a one-directional attention mechanism, but don't discuss why they chose this over bidirectional attention (as used in some prior work). Could the authors clarify this design choice and its impact on performance?
3. For the WikiQA dataset, where element-wise comparison functions performed best, could the authors provide more insight into why these simpler functions work better for ranking tasks compared to classification tasks?
