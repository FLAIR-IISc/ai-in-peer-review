- Proposes a general "compare-aggregate" framework for sequence matching, addressing limited cross-task evaluation and lack of systematic comparison-function studies.

- Demonstrates the framework's effectiveness across four diverse datasets/tasks: MovieQA, InsuranceQA, WikiQA, and SNLI.

- Systematically evaluates six comparison functions, including two novel element-wise operations (SUB and MULT).

- Finds element-wise comparison functions (especially SUB and MULT, and SUBMULT+NN) perform exceptionally well across datasets.

- Provides thorough experimental evaluation and ablation studies showing state-of-the-art or highly competitive results on all four datasets.

- Shows that choice of comparison function significantly impacts performance.

- Argues the framework generalizes beyond task-specific models tested in prior work.

- Observes that simpler element-wise functions can outperform more complex neural comparison functions (NN, NTN).

- Notes order-insensitive preprocessing can outperform order-sensitive approaches in some tasks.

- Paper is well-structured, clearly written, and includes code for reproducibility.

- Suggests clarifying the novelty and theoretical justification for SUB and MULT functions.

- Recommends deeper analysis of why element-wise functions work and why NN/NTN underperform.

- Advises discussing relation to newer sequence-matching advances (e.g., BERT-based models).

- Requests more discussion on why order-insensitive preprocessing can suffice for certain tasks.

- Asks for intuition/theory on why not summing SUB entries preserves useful information.

- Questions whether neural comparison functions underperform due to overfitting or other reasons.

- Asks why order-insensitive preprocessing outperforms for MovieQA and whether word-level matching outweighs context.

- Probes the effect of fine-tuning GloVe embeddings on performance and relative comparison-function ranking.

- Asks how optimal comparison functions might differ across task types (entailment vs. QA).
