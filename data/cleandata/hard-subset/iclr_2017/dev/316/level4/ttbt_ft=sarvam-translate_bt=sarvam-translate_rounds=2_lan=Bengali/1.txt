This paper discusses the problem of achieving differential privacy in a general context, where a group of teachers are trained on isolated datasets of sensitive data, and students make predictions based on public data identified by the teachers through noisy voting. I find this approach to be entirely plausible and very clearly explained by the authors. It would have been better if more discussion had been added about the bound derived from Theorem 1 (and its tightness). The authors have nicely reused the general concept of adding perturbation errors to calculations derived from differentially-private literature, and it is much more extensive (non-convex setting) and beautifully applied in practical contexts compared to several other papers on differentially-private and related topics. The generality of the method, the clear improvement over predecessors, and the clarity of writing make this approach worthy of publication.
