Overall, it's a very good paper, excellent reading, and engaging. The work advances the state-of-the-art knowledge of differentially-private deep learning, is well-written, and relatively thorough.

One thing to keep in mind is that although this method is intended for general application, no theoretical guarantee is given regarding learning performance. Papers on privacy-preserving machine learning often analyze both privacy (in the worst-case scenario, the DP setting) and learning performance (under various assumptions). Since learning performance may depend on the architecture's choice, it is encouraged to experiment in the future using the same dataset with different architectures. If this is not included, please support the choice of architecture used and/or clarify what generalizations can be made about the observed learning performance.

Another point to note is that the epsilons mentioned here cannot be revealed individually; the authors mention that their technique will change the epsilons. However, this needs to be resolved to make meaningful comparisons with the epsilon-delta values mentioned in related work.

Finally, as acknowledged in the paper, the current method may not work with other natural data types. It is encouraged to experiment on other datasets. Also, mention the datasets used.

Other comments:
Some parts of the discussion in the related work are thorough. However, add some discussion/survey of related work on differentially-private semi-supervised learning. For example, in the context of Random Forest, the following paper also proposes differentially-private semi-supervised learning through a teacher-student approach (although not referred to as "teacher-student"). Private labeled data is only used when learning "primary ensemble." A "secondary ensemble" is learned only from unlabeled (non-private) data, where pseudo-labels are created by the primary ensemble.

G. Jagannathan, C. Montioli, and K. Pillaippakkanam: A Semi-Supervised Learning Approach to Differential Privacy. Proc. 2013 IEEE International Conference on Data Mining Workshops, IEEE Workshop on Privacy Aspects of Data Mining (PADM), 2013.

Section C provides a nice comparison of the methods. Ensure that the quantitative results are exactly the same as the GAN results.

For the most part, the paper is very well written. There are some areas where clarification is needed, such as:
- The last paragraph of 3.1. The sentence "all teachers... receive the same training data..." should be rewritten to make it clear that this is not the same for all teachers, but rather applies to the same teachers in the neighboring database.
- 4.1: The authors state: "The number of teachers is limited by a tradeoff between the complexity of the classification task and the available data." However, since this tradeoff is not formal, this statement is ambiguous. In particular, if the analysis is done in an i.i.d. setting, the tradeoff will likely also depend on the relationship between the data distribution and the target hypothesis.
- The discussion in Figure 3 was quite vague in the text and captions and should be corrected for clarity. In the text section, the explanation initially suggests that a large gap is good (as also suggested in the caption). However, it is later stated that the gap is below 20%. These sentences seem contradictory, which was probably not the intention.
