- Paper addresses differential privacy when teachers are trained on disjoint sensitive datasets and a student learns from public data labeled by teachers.  
- Proposes PATE: black-box aggregation of multiple teacher models trained on disjoint data to label public data for a student.  
- Student is trained on labels chosen by noisy voting among teachers, preventing any single teacher from dictating labels and protecting training data privacy.  
- Approach is well-motivated, builds on knowledge aggregation/transfer literature, and is clearly explained.  
- Key technical idea: add noise to teacher vote counts (perturbation) to provide privacy while retaining utility.  
- Empirical evaluation on MNIST and SVHN shows strong privacy/utility trade-offs, claimed state-of-the-art.  
- Theoretical privacy analysis uses the moments accountant to bound privacy cost and supports empirical claims.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance based on relevance, clarity, novelty, and strong theoretical+empirical support.  
- Suggestion to improve: provide more discussion on Theorem 1’s bound and its tightness.  
- Suggestion to improve: explore methods to reduce student dependence on teachers (e.g., active learning, transfer learning).  
- Questions for authors: how number of teachers affects privacy cost and optimal teacher count for a dataset.  
- Questions for authors: how PATE performs on other datasets and what limitations/challenges exist for real-world deployment.
