- Paper proposes a Domain Transfer Network (DTN) with a generative function G = g ∘ f enforcing f-constancy for domain transfer.
- Evaluated on SVHN→MNIST digit transfer and photo-to-emoji generation, showing visually appealing and semantically consistent outputs.
- Main contribution is integrating f-constancy into generation to preserve semantic features across domains.
- Uses a compound loss combining adversarial, f-preserving, and regularization terms (e.g., L_CONST, L_TID).
- Decision: Reject.
- Strength: Well-structured paper with extensive experiments demonstrating the potential of f-constancy.
- Strength: Novel and practical application to emoji generation.
- Strength: Multi-component loss is well-motivated and empirically validated.
- Weakness: Design G = g ∘ f is counter-intuitive and may lose critical information; insufficient justification provided.
- Weakness: Method requires retraining per task, limiting generalizability and robustness.
- Weakness: Poor baseline performance (Equations 1 and 2) is unexplained, weakening claims of improvement.
- Weakness: Figure 5 is unclear in method comparisons; style-transfer baselines are underexplored.
- Suggested revision: Provide detailed analysis of why G = g ∘ f is advantageous despite potential information loss.
- Suggested revision: Clarify causes of baseline failure—architecture, loss design, or training issues.
- Suggested revision: Improve Figure 5 with explicit method markings and quantitative metrics.
- Suggested revision: Compare against style-transfer methods (e.g., Gatys et al., 2016) as baselines for emoji tasks.
- Questions for authors: How does restricted f affect diversity and quality of generated samples; would a more flexible G help?
- Questions for authors: Why does the baseline fail, and how exactly does f-constancy remedy it?
- Questions for authors: Can the method be extended to avoid per-task retraining to improve generalizability?
