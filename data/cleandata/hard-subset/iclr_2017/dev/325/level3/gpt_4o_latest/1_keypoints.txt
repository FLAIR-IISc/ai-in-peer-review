- Proposes a novel generative model that progressively denoises noise into high-quality samples.  
- Introduces "infusion training" which biases the training chain toward the data distribution.  
- Learns a Markov transition operator enabling sample generation in few denoising steps.  
- Computationally efficient and avoids GAN instability.  
- Demonstrates competitive results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CelebA.  
- Produces sharp, varied samples and enables one-shot inpainting.  
- Outperforms prior diffusion-based work in required denoising steps (e.g., Sohl-Dickstein et al.).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strength: novel and computationally attractive alternative to diffusion methods.  
- Strength: strong empirical validation including competitive log-likelihoods and compelling visual samples.  
- Strength: thorough comparison and positioning relative to GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models.  
- Limitation: lacks a tractable variational bound on log-likelihood.  
- Limitation: limited exploration of sensitivity to infusion rates and denoising step counts.  
- Suggestion: use more robust log-likelihood evaluations beyond Parzen estimates.  
- Suggestion: perform sensitivity analysis over infusion rates and number of steps.  
- Suggestion: include direct quantitative comparisons to other diffusion-based models.  
- Suggestion: make minor clarity and grammar improvements, especially around the infusion vs. generative chain.  
- Question: how does performance scale with the number of denoising steps and what are the quality/efficiency trade-offs?  
- Question: can infusion training be adapted to conditional generative tasks?  
- Question: would more advanced architectures (e.g., transformers/attention) improve sample quality?
