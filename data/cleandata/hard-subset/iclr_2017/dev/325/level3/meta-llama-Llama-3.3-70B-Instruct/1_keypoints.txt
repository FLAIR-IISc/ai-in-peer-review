- Proposes PATE: training multiple models on disjoint datasets and using noisy voting to aggregate predictions for strong training-data privacy.  
- Claims PATE achieves excellent utility while providing meaningful worst-case privacy guarantees.  
- Introduces use of the moments accountant technique to improve differential-privacy analysis of knowledge transfer.  
- Demonstrates state-of-the-art privacy/utility trade-offs on benchmarks like MNIST and SVHN.  
- Decision: Accept, based on novelty and empirical effectiveness.  
- Key reason 1: Novel and effective approach to a critical privacy problem, well-motivated and experimentally validated.  
- Key reason 2: Achieves SOTA results and provides thorough privacy analysis using moments accountant.  
- Supporting: Paper is clear, well-structured, shows effectiveness, and discusses limitations and future directions.  
- Question: How does the number of teachers affect privacy cost, and what is the optimal number for a given task?  
- Question: Can PATE be applied to other tasks (e.g., NLP, reinforcement learning)?  
- Question: How does PATE compare quantitatively to other DP methods (gradient-based or Bayesian approaches)?  
- Clarification requested: How is the privacy parameter γ chosen and how does it impact guarantees?  
- Clarification requested: Can other noise types (e.g., Gaussian) be used instead of Laplacian?  
- Clarification requested: How does PATE handle non-partitioned data scenarios like online learning or streaming data?
