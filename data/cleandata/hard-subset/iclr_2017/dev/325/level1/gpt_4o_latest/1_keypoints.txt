- Introduces "infusion training," a novel procedure learning a generative model as a Markov chain transition operator that progressively denoises noise into target-like samples.  
- Uses a single network to avoid GAN instability and aims to be faster than slow diffusion-based sampling.  
- Demonstrates competitive qualitative and quantitative results on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CelebA.  
- Situates the method relative to VAEs, GANs, Generative Stochastic Networks, and diffusion models, highlighting differences and connections.  
- Decision: Accept — the approach is well-motivated, empirically validated, and a meaningful contribution.  
- Novelty and motivation: Infusion training creatively infuses target information into the chain to avoid equilibrium or adversarial pitfalls.  
- Empirical rigor: Comprehensive experiments and metrics (Parzen, likelihood bounds, Inception scores) plus sample and inpainting results showing diverse realistic outputs.  
- Positioning in literature: Clear distinction from related methods with acknowledgment of limitations and future work.  
- Suggestion: Provide a more detailed discussion of theoretical guarantees and implications for robustness/generalizability.  
- Suggestion: Include a deeper comparison with GANs on mode collapse, stability, and computational efficiency.  
- Suggestion: Add ablation studies (infusion rate schedule, number of denoising steps) to clarify design trade-offs.  
- Suggestion: Use more robust evaluation metrics (e.g., FID) instead of relying on Parzen-window estimates.  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive is performance to the infusion rate schedule and could adaptivity help?  
- Questions for authors: Can the method be extended to conditional tasks (e.g., text-to-image, segmentation) and what changes are needed?  
- Questions for authors: How does computational cost (training time, memory) compare to GANs and diffusion models?
