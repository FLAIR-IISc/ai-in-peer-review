- Decision: Accept.  
- Main contribution: Introduces "infusion training," a novel procedure that learns a Markov transition operator to progressively denoise noise into samples matching the target distribution.  
- Key innovation: Infusion training biases the training chain toward targets by injecting information from training examples, enabling efficient denoising.  
- Performance claim: Competitive with GANs and requires fewer denoising steps than reverse diffusion methods.  
- Experimental validation: Thorough experiments on MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CelebA demonstrating strong sample quality, diversity, and inpainting.  
- Metrics reported: Uses Parzen estimates, likelihood bounds, and Inception scores, outperforming traditional GANs on CIFAR-10 Inception score.  
- Theoretical contribution: Provides explanations linking infusion training to other generative methods and offers theoretical insights into its design.  
- Strengths highlighted: Novel, well-motivated method with a single-network, simpler training objective and clear distinction from VAEs/GANs.  
- Presentation improvement: Mathematical sections (e.g., Sec. 2.3) need clearer explanations or visual aids for accessibility.  
- Comparison improvement: Should include deeper discussion of GAN instability and comparisons with state-of-the-art GANs trained with similar resources.  
- Limitations to address: Lacks theoretical guarantees for denoising infusion, and needs more discussion on scalability to larger or higher-dimensional data.  
- Questions for authors: Sensitivity to infusion-rate schedule and its effect on generalizability; extensions to conditional tasks like image-to-image translation; and comparative computational cost (training time/memory) versus GANs/VAEs.
