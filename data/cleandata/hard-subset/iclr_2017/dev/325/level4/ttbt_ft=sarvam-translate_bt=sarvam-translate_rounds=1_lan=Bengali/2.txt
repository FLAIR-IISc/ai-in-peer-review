This paper presents a method for training a generative model through a recursive denoise process. The denoise process begins with a random sample from a rough approximation of the data distribution and creates a high-quality sample through multiple denoise steps. Training is done by setting up a Markov chain that gradually mixes proposals from the current denoise model with a real example from the data distribution; using this chain, the current denoise model is updated to create a modified, "better" sample of the mixing process.

This is a clearly written paper that considers an interesting approach for training a generative model. The simplicity of the presented method impressed me, and I truly enjoyed reading the paper.

The proposed method is novel, although it has clear links to other recent work that aims to draw samples from distributions using denoise models, such as Soh-Dickstein's work and recent work using dynamic energy extraction (DAE) as a generative model.

I think this general aspect of the research is important. The proposed method draws inspiration from the perspective of creating a sample by reducing a power function through changes via a Markov chain, and if successful, it could avoid many of the problems of the current method for directed generative model training, such as:
- Convergence and mode coverage problems like in generative adversarial networks
- The problem of multi-modal distribution modeling that may arise when a strong generative model is combined with a very limited estimation model

However, another method that seems promising for solving these problems, which also has some superficial similarities to the presented work, is the idea of combining the Hamiltonian Monte Carlo approximation with variational inference [1]. So I am not entirely sure that the method presented here will perform better than the mentioned paper; although it may be easier to train. Similarly, although I agree that using an MCMC chain to create a sample in a method like MC-EM is probably very expensive, I am not sure that such a method would work reasonably well, at least for a general MNIST example. In general, it would be better to compare more directly between different estimation methods using a method like an MCMC chain, but I understand that this is probably outside the scope of this paper. However, what I was hoping for was a direct comparison with Soh-Dickstein's method in terms of sampling steps and generation quality, as it is directly related.

Other major points (good and bad):
- Although the method is generally well explained, some training details are missing. The most important point is that it is not mentioned how the alpha or omega are set (I assume omega is 0.01 because that increase is mentioned in the experimental setup). It is also unclear how the alpha affects the generator's power. Using a small alpha in many steps to ensure slow mixing of two distributions seems intuitively reasonable, but it is not clear how much it is necessary or at what point the method will break down (I assume alpha = 1 will not work because the generator will then have to magically denoise a sample from a relatively less informative draw from p0?). The authors mention in a figure caption that the denoise model does not only produce good samples in 1-2 steps, but it may also be a flaw in training the model with a small alpha (at least I don't see any predetermined reason for this). More testing should be done here.
- No infusion chain or generating chain is shown for any complex data distribution, which is unfortunate because I think these would be interesting to see.
- The paper does a good job of evaluating the model against various metrics. Having a limit on log-probability is also good!
- Unfortunately, the current method does not come with any theoretical guarantees. It is unclear for what choices of alpha the method will work and whether it has any deep connection with MCMC sampling or power-based models. In my eyes, this does not diminish the value of the paper, but perhaps a brief sentence in the conclusion will suffice.
