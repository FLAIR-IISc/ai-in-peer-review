Summary:

This paper presents a heuristic approach for training a deep directed generative model, where each layer samples from the same conditional distribution, similar to the transition operator of a Markov chain. Similar to optimizing a variational lower bound, this approach estimates the gradient by replacing the posterior part of the latent with an alternative distribution. However, the estimated distribution is not updated to improve the lower bound; rather, it is built up empirically at each step. Another difference from variational optimization is that the conditional distribution is optimized by greed, rather than following the gradient of the combined log-probability.

Review:

The proposed approach is interesting and seems worthy of further investigation. Given that there are approaches to train the same class of model that are 1) theoretically more robust, 2) of similar computational complexity, and 3) work well in practice (e.g., Rezende and Mohamed, 2015), I am still not convinced of its ability to generate effects. However, my main concern is that empirical evaluation is still quite limited.

I appreciate the authors' inclusion of reasonable estimates of the log-probability. This will enable and encourage future comparisons with this method on the continuous MNIST. However, the authors should note that the numbers taken from Wu et al. (2016) do not represent the performance of VAE. (From the paper: "Therefore, the log-probability values we report should not be directly compared with networks with more flexible observation models. Such observation models can easily achieve much higher log-probability scores, [...])".

It would have been good to compare the inpainting results using other methods. How practical is the proposed approach compared to other approaches? Similar to the diffusion approach by Sohail-Dykstein et al. (2015), the proposed approach appears to be both efficient and effective for inpainting. Not making this a big issue and not properly evaluating it seems like a missed opportunity.
