This paper presents a method for training a generative model through a recursive denoisement process. The denoisement process begins with a raw estimate of a data distribution, a random sample, and through several denoisement steps, a high-quality sample is generated. Training is done by establishing a Markov chain that gradually combines proposals from the current denoisement model with a real example from the data distribution; using this chain, the current denoisement model is updated to introduce modified, "improved" samples from a mixed process.

This is a clearly written paper that considers an interesting approach to training a generative model. I was very impressed by the simplicity of the presented approach, and I really enjoyed reading the paper.

The proposed method is novel, although it has clear connections to other recent works, which aim to sample from a distribution using a denoisement model, such as Sohail-Dickstein's work and recent work on using DAE as a generative model.

I think this general direction of research is important. The proposed process draws inspiration from the approach of generating samples by reducing an energy function through transitions along a Markov chain, and if successful, it could potentially overcome many of the problems of current processes for training directed generative models, such as:
- Convergence and mode coverage problems in generative adversarial networks
- Problems in modeling multi-modal distributions that may arise when a very restrictive estimated estimate model is combined with a powerful generative model

That being said, another method that seems promising for addressing these issues, with some superficial similarities to the presented work, is combining the Hamiltonian Monte Carlo estimate with the variance estimate, as in [1]. Thus, I am not entirely convinced that the method presented here will outperform the mentioned paper; although it may be simpler to train. Similarly, while I agree that using an MCMC chain to generate samples through a process like MC-EM is likely to be very expensive, I am not convinced that such a process would work reasonably well even for a simple amnesty example. Generally, it would be good to make a more direct comparison between different estimation methods using a process like an MCMC chain, but I understand that this is probably outside the scope of this paper. One thing I was hoping for, however, is a direct comparison of the process in terms of sampling steps and generation quality from Sohail-Dickstein, as this is directly related.

Other key points (good and bad):
- Although the method is generally well explained, some training details are missing. Most importantly, it is never mentioned how the alpha or omega are set (I am assuming that omega is 0.01 because this is the increase mentioned in the experimental setup). It is also not clear how the alpha affects the capabilities of the generator. While using a small alpha in several steps to ensure slow mixing of two distributions seems intuitively reasonable, it is not clear how much this is necessary or at what point the process will break down (I assume that alpha = 1 will not work because the generator will then have to magically denoisement a sample from a relatively uninformative draw p0?). The authors mention in a figure caption that the denoisement model does not create good samples in just 1-2 steps, but it could also be an artifact of training the model with a small alpha (at least I see no prior reason for this). More experiments should be done here.
- No infusion chain or generating chain is shown for any more complex data distribution, which is unfortunate because I think it would be interesting to see these.
- The paper does a good job of evaluating the model in relation to several different metrics. The limit on log-probability is also good!
- Unfortunately the current approach does not come with any theoretical guarantees. It is not clear for what alpha choices the process will work and whether there is some deep connection to the MCMC sampling or energy-based model. In my opinion this does not diminish the value of the paper, but perhaps a short sentence in the conclusion would be worthwhile.

Minor points:

- The explanation of the convolutional network you use is beyond my understanding. You write that you use a structure similar to the "improved GAN" paper, which unlike your model, generates samples from a fixed length of random input. Thus I believe that you are not really using a generator with 1 fully connected network after up-convolution, but several steps of convolution after fully connected layer and then up-convolution?
- The option to parameterize variance through sigmoid output units is somewhat unusual, was there any specific reason for this option?
