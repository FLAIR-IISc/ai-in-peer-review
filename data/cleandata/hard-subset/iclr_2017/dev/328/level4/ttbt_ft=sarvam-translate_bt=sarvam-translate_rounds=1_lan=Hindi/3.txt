This paper models spike trains of retinal ganglion cells, which are driven by natural images. I think the word "activity" should be included at the end of the title, otherwise it's formally incorrect.

Anyway, this paper specifically proposes a recurrent network for this time series prediction and compares it to a previous approach of a generalized linear model. Overall, the paradigm described is that when someone can predict spikes well, they can observe and learn how nature does it in the model.

In general, this paper seems commendable, although I'm not sure I learned much. Figure 2 shows the results, indicating that the RNN model can predict spikes a little better. So, that's good. But now what? You've shown that a more complex model can produce a better fit for the data, although there are still some differences in the actual data. Your initial outline was that a better predictive model helps you better understand neural processing in the retina. So, tell us what you learned. I'm not an expert on the retina, but I know that the retina has many layers and recurrence, so I'm not so surprised that the new model is better than the GLM.

It seems that more complex recurrent models like LSTM do not improve performance, according to a statement in the paper. However, it's also difficult to compare at this level because more complex models require more data. Therefore, I would really hope that more layers in the retina and even a more detailed model could ultimately make the prediction even better.

I was also a little surprised that all the neurons in the network share all the same parameters (weights). While the results show that these simplified models can capture spike train features to a great extent, couldn't a model with free parameters ultimately outperform this model (correspondingly with more training data)?
