- Paper applies multitask RNNs to predict primate retinal ganglion cell spiking to natural images, outperforming GLMs.
- Decision: Reject.
- Strength: Well-motivated—addresses GLM limitations and uses multitask learning to mitigate limited data.
- Strength: Scientifically rigorous results—RNNs consistently outperform GLMs across metrics, though gains are modest.
- Weakness: Incremental novelty—use of RNNs for time-series/spiking prediction is unsurprising given prior work.
- Weakness: Main claim of advancing biological understanding is unsupported—paper lacks interpretable insights into retinal computations.
- Weakness: Black-box nature of RNNs acknowledged but not addressed; spatial and temporal nonlinearity analyses are limited.
- Weakness: More complex models (e.g., LSTMs) do not improve performance, implying data limitations or architectural issues.
- Suggestion: Extract interpretable features from the RNN (e.g., analyze learned weights/dynamics) to provide biological insights.
- Suggestion: Explore alternative architectures, LSTM tuning, or regularization to test whether model complexity or data are limiting factors.
- Suggestion: Rigorously justify and test parameter sharing versus free-parameter models across varying dataset sizes.
- Suggestion: Revise title to explicitly reference "activity" (e.g., "Modeling Retinal Ganglion Cell Activity") for clarity.
- Questions for authors: What do first-layer filters reveal about spatial/temporal retinal processing?
- Questions for authors: Why do LSTMs fail to outperform simpler RNNs—insufficient data or hyperparameter issues?
- Questions for authors: How does parameter sharing affect performance as dataset size increases; would free-parameter models eventually win?
