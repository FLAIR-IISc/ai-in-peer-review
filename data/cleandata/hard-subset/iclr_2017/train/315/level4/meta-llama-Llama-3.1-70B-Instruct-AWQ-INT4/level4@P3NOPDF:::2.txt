This paper presents an empirical study that investigates two key claims: (1) stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with smaller batch sizes converges to flatter minima, and (2) flatter minima have better generalization abilities. The authors' work provides valuable insights into the generalization of deep networks, shedding light on some interesting and important questions in this area.

While the paper may not introduce significant novelty, its contribution lies in its thorough exploration of these questions, making it a valuable addition to the existing literature. The study's findings have the potential to impact both theoretical and practical aspects of deep learning research. On the theoretical side, the results can inform the development of new theories by suggesting legitimate assumptions for real-world scenarios. On the practical side, the study's conclusions can be used heuristically to design new algorithms that leverage the manipulation of mini-batch sizes to improve generalization.

The authors have addressed my initial concerns regarding the correctness of their proposed sharpness criterion, which they originally claimed to be scale-invariant. This claim has been removed in the revised version, resolving the issue. Overall, the paper is well-written and clearly presents its contributions, making it easy to follow and understand.
