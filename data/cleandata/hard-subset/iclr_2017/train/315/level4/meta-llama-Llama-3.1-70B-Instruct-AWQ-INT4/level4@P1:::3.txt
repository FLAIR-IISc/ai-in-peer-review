This paper explores the cause of the generalization gap observed in the large-batch regime of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods and its variants. The authors provide numerical evidence that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training and testing functions, which is known to lead to poorer generalization. In contrast, small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers, and the experiments support the view that this is due to the inherent noise in the gradient estimation. The authors also discuss several strategies to attempt to help large-batch methods eliminate this generalization gap.

I decide to reject this paper, primarily due to two reasons. Firstly, while the paper provides interesting insights into the generalization gap of large-batch methods, it does not provide a comprehensive analysis of the underlying causes of this phenomenon. The authors rely heavily on numerical experiments, which, although well-designed, do not provide a complete understanding of the problem. Secondly, the paper lacks a clear theoretical framework for understanding the convergence behavior of large-batch methods. The authors mention recent theoretical work on the loss surface of deep neural networks, but they do not provide a detailed analysis of how their results relate to these theoretical models.

One potential direction for future research is to investigate the effect of adding noise to the gradients during the large-batch regime. The authors mention that small-batch methods use noisy gradients in the step computation, which pushes the iterates out of the basin of attraction of sharp minimizers. It is possible that adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the large-batch regime could provide similar benefits. Another potential direction is to explore the use of robust optimization techniques, such as those mentioned in the paper, to improve the generalization performance of large-batch methods.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer are: (1) Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the convergence behavior of large-batch methods, including a theoretical framework for understanding the underlying causes of the generalization gap? (2) How do your results relate to recent theoretical work on the loss surface of deep neural networks? (3) Can you provide more evidence for the effectiveness of the strategies you propose for eliminating the generalization gap of large-batch methods?

Additional feedback: The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and concise overview of their results. However, there are some minor errors in the text, such as missing citations and incorrect formatting. The authors should also consider providing more details about their experimental setup, including the specific architectures and hyperparameters used in their experiments.
