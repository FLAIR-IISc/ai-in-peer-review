This paper investigates the degradation in generalization performance of deep learning models when trained with large-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors provide numerical evidence that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training and testing functions, which leads to poorer generalization. In contrast, small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers, which are believed to generalize better. The paper explores several strategies to remedy the generalization gap of large-batch methods, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

I recommend accepting this paper, but with some reservations. While the paper is well-written and provides valuable insights into the behavior of large-batch SGD methods, I believe that it would benefit from additional investigations to strengthen its claims. For example, the authors could explore the effect of adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the large-batch regime, which could potentially combine the advantages of both small-batch and large-batch methods.

The paper's main contribution is its identification of the cause of the generalization gap in large-batch methods, which is attributed to the convergence to sharp minimizers. The authors provide convincing numerical evidence to support this claim, including parametric plots and sharpness metrics. However, I would like to see more analysis on the relationship between the sharpness of minimizers and the generalization performance of the model.

The paper also discusses several strategies to remedy the generalization gap of large-batch methods. While these strategies show some promise, I believe that they require more thorough evaluation and comparison to existing methods. For example, the authors could provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for each strategy and compare their performance to other optimization methods.

To further improve the paper, I would suggest that the authors:

* Provide more analysis on the relationship between the sharpness of minimizers and the generalization performance of the model.
* Evaluate the proposed strategies more thoroughly, including hyperparameter tuning and comparison to existing methods.
* Explore the effect of adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the large-batch regime.
* Discuss the implications of their findings for the design of deep learning models and optimization algorithms.

Overall, I believe that this paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of deep learning, and with some additional work, it could be even more impactful.
