- Empirical study of generalization in deep neural networks trained with SGD and variants.  
- Examines why larger batch sizes degrade model quality, providing numerical evidence.  
- Finds large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of training/testing functions, harming generalization.  
- Finds small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers characterized by many small Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Provides a thorough analysis of limitations of large-batch methods and potential benefits of small-batch methods.  
- Proposes strategies to reduce the large-batch generalization gap: data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.  
- Paper is well-written and offers valuable insights with significant implications for theory and practice.  
- Reviewer recommends acceptance.  
- Primary reason: thorough, well-executed empirical study with sound, well-supported analysis.  
- Secondary reason: results meaningfully impact practitioners and may guide new theories/algorithms.  
- Potential criticism: results may not generalize across all architectures and tasks, though authors acknowledge this.  
- Suggestion: include more experimental details (specific architectures, hyperparameters, selection criteria).  
- Suggestion: add more discussion on practical implications and guidance for choosing batch size and hyperparameters.  
- Suggestion: relate findings to other recent work on deep-net generalization and batch size.  
- Questions for authors: (1) provide architectures and hyperparameters used; (2) explain how batch sizes and hyperparameters were selected; (3) offer practitioner guidance on choosing batch size/hyperparameters; (4) clarify relation to other recent work; (5) acknowledge/address limitations or potential criticisms.
