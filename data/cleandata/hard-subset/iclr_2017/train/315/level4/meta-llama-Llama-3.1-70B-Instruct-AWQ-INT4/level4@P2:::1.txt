The paper investigates the cause of the degradation in the quality of deep learning models when using large batch sizes in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods. The authors provide numerical evidence that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training and testing functions, which leads to poorer generalization. In contrast, small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers, which is attributed to the inherent noise in the gradient estimation.

I accept this paper because it addresses a significant issue in deep learning and provides insightful explanations and evidence for the observed phenomenon. The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature.

The authors' main observation is that the lack of generalization ability in large-batch methods is due to the convergence to sharp minimizers. They provide numerical results, including one-dimensional parametric plots and sharpness measures, to support this claim. The paper also discusses several strategies to attempt to remedy the generalization problem of large-batch methods, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

The paper is well-written, and the experiments are thorough and well-designed. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their results and discuss the implications of their findings.

One potential area for improvement is to provide more theoretical analysis to support the empirical results. While the paper provides some theoretical context, a more in-depth analysis of the convergence properties of large-batch methods would strengthen the paper.

Additionally, it would be interesting to see more experiments on different architectures and datasets to further validate the results. The authors mention that they have conducted experiments on other networks and datasets, but it would be helpful to include more details on these experiments.

Some specific questions I would like the authors to address are:

* Can the authors provide more insight into why large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers?
* How do the results change when using different optimizers or architectures?
* Can the authors provide more details on the computational cost of the sharpness measure and how it scales with the size of the network?

Overall, the paper provides valuable insights into the behavior of large-batch methods in deep learning and has the potential to make a significant impact on the field.
