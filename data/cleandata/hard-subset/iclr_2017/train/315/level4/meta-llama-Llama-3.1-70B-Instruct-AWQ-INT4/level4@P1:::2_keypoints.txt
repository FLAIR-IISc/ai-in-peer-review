- Empirical claim: large-batch SGD tends to converge to sharp minimizers causing poorer generalization, while small-batch SGD converges to flat minimizers with better generalization.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason: Thorough and well-motivated empirical study clarifying batch size vs. generalization.  
- Reason: Findings have important theoretical and practical implications, suggesting new research and algorithm design directions.  
- Main contribution: Identifies a correlation between minimizer sharpness and generalization, supported by parametric plots, sharpness metrics, and experiments across architectures.  
- Observation: Flat minimizers found by small-batch methods are more robust to perturbations and generalize better.  
- Remedies tested: Data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization show partial improvement but do not fully resolve the large-batch generalization gap.  
- Suggestion: Provide deeper theoretical insights into why batch size affects generalization.  
- Suggestion: Evaluate robustness of the sharpness metric to different noise types and perturbations.  
- Suggestion: Explore hybrid approaches combining small- and large-batch methods (hierarchical or multi-scale).  
- Question: What is the computational cost and scaling behavior of computing the sharpness metric with network and batch size?  
- Question: Have alternative explanations (e.g., optimization dynamics or regularization differences) for the generalization gap been considered?  
- Question: What are the implications of these findings for other optimization methods like quasi-Newton or second-order methods?
