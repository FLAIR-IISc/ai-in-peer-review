This paper presents an empirical study that investigates the cause of the generalization gap in deep learning models when using large-batch methods. The authors provide numerical evidence that supports the view that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training and testing functions, which leads to poorer generalization.

The authors' approach is well-motivated, and their work is grounded in the literature. They provide a clear and concise explanation of the problem and their methodology, making it easy to follow their arguments. The paper is well-organized, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of their results.

The paper supports its claims through numerical experiments on various deep learning architectures. The authors present one-dimensional parametric plots and perturbation (sharpness) measures to demonstrate the difference in the minimizers obtained by small-batch and large-batch methods. They also provide a detailed analysis of the results, discussing the implications of their findings and potential avenues for future research.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to shed light on some interesting questions around generalization of deep networks. The authors' results have implications for both theory and practice, suggesting what assumptions are legitimate for real scenarios for building new theories, or being used heuristically to develop new algorithms with generalization by smart manipulation of mini-batch sizes.

I recommend accepting this paper. The authors have addressed my earlier concerns, and the paper is well-written and easy to follow. The results are interesting and have implications for the field.

Additional feedback:

* The authors may want to consider discussing the implications of their results for other optimization methods, such as quasi-Newton methods or second-order methods.
* It would be interesting to see if the authors can provide more insight into why large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers. Is this due to the noise in the gradient estimation, or is there another explanation?
* The authors may want to consider providing more details on the computational resources required for their experiments. This would help readers to better understand the scalability of their approach.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the related work. While the authors provide a good overview of the literature, there are some relevant papers that are not mentioned.
* The authors may want to consider providing more insight into the limitations of their approach. Are there any scenarios where their method may not work well?

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the initialization strategies used in your experiments? Did you try different initialization methods, and if so, how did they affect the results?
* How did you choose the hyperparameters for your experiments? Were they tuned using a validation set, or were they chosen based on some other criteria?
* Can you provide more insight into the computational resources required for your experiments? How long did each experiment take to run, and what type of hardware was used?
* Have you tried applying your method to other types of neural networks, such as recurrent neural networks or generative models? If so, how did the results compare to those presented in the paper?
