Summary
The paper presents an empirical study of the generalization gap observed when training deep networks with large mini-batches. The authors provide consistent numerical evidence that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to sharp minima, while small-batch (SB) methods converge to flatter minima. They visualize loss landscapes along parametric curves, propose a practical sharpness metric, and demonstrate that sharp minima correlate with poorer generalization across several architectures and datasets. They also explore mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) and a warm-start protocol that suggests dynamic batching as a potential remedy. Overall, the paper offers a clear and careful empirical investigation of a question that is important for both scalable training and understanding the implicit regularization of SGD.

Decision
Accept. The empirical findings are carefully executed, broadly relevant, and likely to influence both practice (batch-size choices and training protocols) and theory (assumptions about the role of noise and landscape geometry), despite limited novelty.

Key reasons
- Clear, replicable empirical evidence connecting batch size, sharpness, and generalization across multiple architectures.
- Practical importance for parallelization and training at scale, with actionable insights (e.g., warm-start/dynamic batch size).

Supporting arguments
- Breadth: Experiments span fully connected and convolutional models (AlexNet-/VGG-like), with consistent patterns across tasks and datasets.
- Diagnostics: One-dimensional and curvilinear trajectory visualizations convincingly show sharper basins near LB solutions compared to SB ones.
- Sharpness metric: While heuristic, the proposed measure is computationally feasible at scale and differentiates SB and LB minima by 1–2 orders of magnitude in reported experiments.
- Mechanistic insight: Warm-start experiments suggest that SGD’s gradient noise helps escape sharp basins early, after which LB methods can successfully converge to flatter regions.
- Negative results are valuable: Attempts at mitigating the gap (augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) reduce but do not eliminate sharpness, clarifying what does and does not help.
- Clarity and responsiveness: The authors addressed a previous issue by removing an incorrect claim that the sharpness metric is scale-invariant; this resolves my earlier concern.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Fair comparison: Please clarify whether SB and LB runs were matched on number of parameter updates, total epochs, wall-clock compute, or training loss achieved. Differences here can confound conclusions.
- Hyperparameters: Report learning-rate schedules, weight decay, momentum/optimizer hyperparameters, and how they were tuned per batch size. Consider exploring learning-rate scaling and warm-up for large batches to ensure LB is not disadvantaged by suboptimal schedules.
- Sharpness metric sensitivity: Provide an ablation on epsilon (box size), dimension p, and optimizer settings for the inner maximization (e.g., number of L-BFGS-B steps). Also, relate the metric to estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalue (e.g., via power iteration or Lanczos) on smaller models to calibrate the measure.
- Batch-size sweep: You show thresholds for some networks; extending this systematically across all models would strengthen the claim about a critical batch-size regime.
- Regularization controls: Analyze the role of explicit regularizers (weight decay, dropout, label smoothing, batch norm) on sharpness and the gap. Some regularizers may directly alter curvature.
- Optimizer diversity: While ADAM is used extensively, include results with SGD+momentum and other common optimizers for both SB and LB to test optimizer-batch interactions.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing code, configs, seeds, and checkpoints used for sharpness computations to facilitate replication.
- Theory connection: Briefly discuss how your findings align or conflict with existing landscape results and implicit bias theories, and what assumptions future theory might reasonably adopt given your observations.

Questions for the authors
- Matching criteria: By what criterion did you stop training (e.g., convergence of training loss, fixed epochs, early stopping)? Were SB and LB matched on updates, epochs, or attained training loss at evaluation time?
- Learning-rate scaling: How were learning rates and schedules chosen for LB runs? Did you try scaling the learning rate with batch size and/or warm-up? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Definition of “large”: You set LB to 10% of the dataset. How do results vary for intermediate batch sizes (e.g., 1k–10k on CIFAR, or a broader sweep on each model)? Is the sharpness jump gradual or abrupt?
- Sharpness computation: How robust are sharpness rankings to the choice of epsilon, p, and the finite number of inner optimization steps? Do you observe consistent rankings across multiple random A matrices?
- Hessian evidence: For smaller models, can you report top Hessian eigenvalues near SB vs. LB minima to directly corroborate the sharpness metric?
- Optimizer effects: Do the main conclusions hold for SGD with momentum and well-tuned regularization across SB and LB? Any cases where LB+SGD behaves differently from LB+ADAM?
- Regularization: What is the impact of weight decay, dropout rates, and label smoothing on the sharpness gap under LB training?
- Warm-start specifics: In the warm-start experiments, at what stage (loss level or update count) does switching to LB consistently succeed? Can dynamic batch-size schedules reproduce this without an explicit switch?

Overall, this is a thoughtful and useful empirical contribution that clarifies an important phenomenon and points to promising practical remedies.
