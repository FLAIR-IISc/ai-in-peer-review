1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training in deep learning often leads to poorer generalization than small-batch (SB) training. The authors provide numerical evidence across several architectures and datasets that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima—characterized by higher sensitivity of the loss in a neighborhood of the solution—whereas SB methods converge to flatter minima. They support this with 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions, and propose a practical “sharpness” metric based on local perturbations. They also explore mitigations (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), which narrow but do not eliminate the generalization gap. Warm-start experiments indicate that noise early in training can help escape sharp basins, after which LB can refine solutions near flat minima. The work highlights a plausible mechanism linking batch size, optimization noise, landscape geometry, and generalization.

2. Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
- The paper offers a clear, empirically grounded explanation for a widely observed phenomenon: the generalization gap in LB training.
- The findings are practically useful for the community and suggest actionable directions for improving LB training without sacrificing generalization.

3. Supporting arguments
- Empirical scope: The study spans multiple architectures (including AlexNet- and VGG-like CNNs and fully connected networks) and datasets, with consistent observations that LB converges to sharper minima and generalizes worse, despite similar training losses.
- Diagnostic tools: Parametric loss/accuracy plots along paths connecting SB and LB solutions visually and quantitatively highlight the difference in landscape geometry around the respective minima. The proposed sharpness metric, while heuristic, is computationally feasible and correlates with generalization differences across regimes.
- Warm-start evidence: Training first with SB (noisy gradients) and then switching to LB improves generalization and reduces sharpness, suggesting that stochastic noise helps escape sharp basins before LB refines in flatter regions.
- Negative results are informative: Attempts such as data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial/stability training reduce but do not remove the sharpness/generalization gap, helping delimit what does and does not work.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not critical to the decision)
- Consider explicitly testing the effect of injecting rescaled Gaussian noise into gradients during LB training. This may emulate the beneficial exploratory behavior of SB methods while retaining LB scalability. A systematic study of noise variance as a function of batch size could clarify how much noise is needed to recover SB-like generalization.
- Clarify the relationship between the sharpness metric and the Hessian spectrum. While exact spectra are infeasible, reporting approximate top eigenvalues (e.g., via stochastic Lanczos or power iterations) at SB vs. LB solutions would strengthen the link between the metric and curvature.
- Sensitivity analyses:
  - Learning-rate schedules and scaling rules (e.g., linear scaling with batch size) can materially affect LB behavior. A brief ablation across schedules would help isolate batch size vs. optimization hyperparameters.
  - Optimizer dependence: You mention similar results with ADAGRAD, SGD, and adaQN; adding a concise summary (even in an appendix) would increase confidence that the effect is optimizer-agnostic.
  - Initialization: Since LB solutions tend to be closer to the initialization, a controlled study varying initialization scale/distribution might further support the “basin capture” explanation.
- Metric robustness: The sharpness measure depends on ε and the random subspace A. Reporting variance across multiple A realizations and a short sensitivity sweep over ε would help establish robustness.
- Threshold behavior: The noted batch-size thresholds at which generalization degrades sharply are compelling. It would be useful to relate these thresholds to dataset size, model capacity, and gradient noise statistics, possibly normalizing batch size by dataset size or gradient variance.
- Connections to theory: The discussion touches on MDL/Bayesian explanations. If space permits, tightening these links (even qualitatively) would contextualize the empirical findings within existing theory.

Questions for the authors
- Noise injection: Have you tried explicitly adding Gaussian noise to LB gradients, with variance calibrated to match SB gradient noise? If so, how does this compare to SB in terms of sharpness and test accuracy? If not, could you outline how you would choose the noise schedule?
- Sharpness–Hessian link: Can you provide approximate top eigenvalues of the Hessian (or generalized Gauss–Newton) at SB vs. LB solutions to corroborate the sharpness metric?
- Batch-size thresholds: How stable are the observed thresholds across random seeds, learning-rate schedules, and datasets? Do they correlate with measured gradient variance or with the warm-start epoch at which LB starts to generalize well?
- Warm-start dependence: In Figure 5, after how many SB epochs does LB consistently converge to flat minima? Does this transition align with a measurable drop in sharpness or with a phase change in training dynamics (e.g., after a scheduled LR decay)?
- Mitigations: Among data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial/stability training, which most effectively narrows the gap per unit of added computation? Could combining them with gradual batch-size increases (dynamic sampling) close the gap further?

Overall, the paper is interesting and useful, and the proposed explanation—LB methods converging to sharper minima that generalize worse—fits the reported evidence and common practitioner experience. Adding a targeted experiment with rescaled Gaussian gradient noise in the LB regime would further strengthen the work and may provide a practical path to recover SB-like generalization while benefiting from LB parallelism.
