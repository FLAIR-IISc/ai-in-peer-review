1. Summary of what the paper claims to do and contribute
- The paper investigates why training deep networks with large mini-batches often yields worse generalization than small-batch training, despite similar training losses.
- It provides consistent empirical evidence that large-batch (LB) methods converge to sharp minima—characterized by high sensitivity and many large positive Hessian eigenvalues—while small-batch (SB) methods tend to find flatter minima that generalize better.
- Evidence includes 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots interpolating between SB and LB solutions, and a practical sharpness metric based on local maximization within a constrained neighborhood (solved approximately with L-BFGS-B).
- The study spans multiple architectures (AlexNet- and VGG-style CNNs and fully connected networks) and datasets, with results robust to optimizer choice for LB (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD, adaQN).
- It rules out simple overfitting as the cause of the gap and shows that warm-starting LB from SB iterates can recover good generalization by steering LB into flat basins.
- The paper explores remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/stability training) and finds they reduce but do not eliminate the gap; it discusses dynamic sampling as a promising direction.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper offers a clear, well-motivated empirical investigation into an important and timely question in deep learning practice: the generalization gap of large-batch training.
- The evidence is thorough and multifaceted (visualizations, a sharpness metric, warm-start experiments), and the conclusions are consistent across architectures and optimizers.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem relevance: Understanding the batch size–generalization trade-off directly informs scalable training and distributed optimization for deep learning. The paper addresses this gap head-on and connects to widely observed practitioner behavior.
- Positioning: The work relates its findings to prior perspectives (MDL, Bayesian, loss landscape theory) and is aligned with results showing many local minima with similar training loss but differing geometry.
- Methodology: The parametric plots reveal stark differences in landscape geometry near SB vs. LB solutions. The sharpness metric—while heuristic—provides a reproducible, computationally feasible sensitivity measure, with results consistent whether maximizing in full space or random low-dimensional subspaces.
- Robustness: Experiments are repeated from multiple random initializations; multiple optimizers are tested for LB; and both FC and CNN architectures are assessed. The warm-start study is especially compelling in dissecting the role of gradient noise in escaping sharp basins.
- Negative results matter: The attempts to “fix” LB training (augmentation, conservative updates, adversarial/stability training) and their partial effectiveness are valuable to the community and sharpen the paper’s main claim.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (to help; not all are part of my acceptance decision)
- Sharpness metric:
  - Provide more detail on the choice/sensitivity of ε, the dimension p of the subspace A, and the number of L-BFGS-B iterations. Include an ablation to show the metric’s stability under these choices.
  - Validate the metric against top Hessian eigenvalues via Lanczos on a smaller model to strengthen the link to curvature.
  - Clarify the invariance properties of the metric (e.g., reparameterizations, scale of the loss).
- Experimental protocol:
  - Report complete hyperparameters per batch size (learning rate schedules, weight decay, momentum, ADAM betas, BN settings). Ensure hyperparameters are tuned fairly per regime, since LB often requires different schedules.
  - Justify choosing LB as 10% of the training set and show results at multiple large batch sizes to map the “threshold” behavior more systematically across all models.
  - Explicitly control for equalized training loss across SB/LB solutions when comparing sharpness/generalization, to isolate geometry as the driver.
- Additional analyses:
  - Quantify the dimensionality of the sharp subspace (you note ~5%); report how this varies across models and runs.
  - Extend warm-start results with a schedule that gradually increases batch size (dynamic sampling) and compare with fixed LB, to substantiate the proposed remedy.
  - Where possible, add more modern architectures (e.g., residual connections) to test whether architecture changes alter the prevalence of sharp vs. flat minima.
- Presentation:
  - Improve figure and table captions to be fully self-contained (dataset, architecture, optimizer, batch sizes, hyperparameters).
  - Move key definitions (e.g., the sharpness metric) into the main text early, and consolidate references to related notions of sharpness to prevent confusion with convex definitions.

5. Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters and fairness:
  - How were learning rates and schedules selected per batch size? Did you explore LR scaling or warmup tailored to large batches? How sensitive are your conclusions to these choices?
  - What weight decay, momentum, and ADAM betas were used per regime? Were they tuned independently for SB and LB?
- Sharpness metric:
  - How sensitive are sharpness values to ε and to the number of L-BFGS-B steps? Do results qualitatively persist with different ε and p?
  - Can you validate the metric by estimating a few top Hessian eigenvalues on smaller models?
  - In cases where “sharpness” concentrates in a small subspace, can you characterize how many directions dominate and whether those directions align across runs?
- Threshold behavior:
  - How consistently does the batch-size “threshold” appear across architectures/datasets? Is the threshold predictable from gradient variance estimates?
  - Does the threshold shift with different learning rate schedules, label smoothing, or stronger regularization?
- Warm-starting and dynamic sampling:
  - In the warm-start experiments, how many SB epochs are typically needed before LB can lock onto a flat basin? Does this correlate with changes in the sharpness metric of the SB iterates?
  - Have you tried a principled batch-size growth schedule (e.g., based on gradient variance) and compared it against a fixed LB?
- Remedies:
  - For data augmentation and conservative training, could you quantify how much sharpness is reduced relative to baseline LB and whether combining remedies yields further gains?
  - Adversarial/stability training showed no benefit here; can you comment on whether stronger or differently crafted perturbations change the outcome?

Overall, this paper addresses a central practical question with solid empirical methodology and offers actionable insights for the community. I recommend acceptance.
