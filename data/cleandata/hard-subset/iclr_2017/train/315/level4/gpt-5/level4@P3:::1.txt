Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles a timely and practically important question: why do large-batch (LB) training methods for deep networks often exhibit worse generalization than small-batch (SB) methods despite similar training loss?
- It advances the hypothesis that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima—characterized by high curvature and sensitivity—whereas SB methods, due to gradient noise, are more likely to find flat minima that generalize better.
- The authors provide several lines of empirical evidence: 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB/LB solutions, a computationally tractable sharpness metric based on local sensitivity, and warm-start experiments showing that LB can generalize well when initialized near a flat region discovered by SB.
- They test across multiple architectures and datasets (fully connected and convolutional variants akin to AlexNet and VGG), show a batch-size threshold where generalization degrades, and evaluate partial remedies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial training), which narrow but do not close the gap.
- The work situates the phenomenon in the literature (links to MDL/Bayesian interpretations and recent papers on loss landscapes) and discusses implications for parallel training and dynamic batching.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper offers a clear, reproducible empirical characterization of the LB generalization gap and connects it to solution sharpness across diverse architectures, which is valuable to the community.
2) The warm-start and batch-threshold experiments provide actionable insights that have already influenced practice (e.g., dynamic batch sizing), even if the theory is incomplete.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The paper directly addresses the generalization deficit in LB training and asks whether the deficit is due to convergence to a qualitatively different class of minima. This is well posed and highly relevant for scalable training.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well motivated by the practical need to parallelize training and by prior observations of a performance gap. It connects to established ideas (MDL, Bayesian views of flatness, recent landscape analyses) and to optimizer behavior.
- Evidence and rigor: The empirical methodology is thoughtful: (i) SB/LB comparisons across multiple networks, (ii) visualization of loss along paths between solutions, (iii) a tractable sharpness metric (both full-space and random subspaces), and (iv) warm-start studies that causally link exploration noise to landing in flatter regions. Results are consistent across trials and optimizers (with some checks beyond Adam).
- Scope and limits: The work is primarily empirical. The sharpness metric is heuristic and only indirectly related to Hessian spectra, but it is pragmatic and reveals robust differences (often 1–2 orders of magnitude) between SB and LB solutions. The paper is appropriately cautious in interpreting causality and calls for theory.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all affect the accept decision)
- Hyperparameter parity and tuning:
  - Clarify how learning rates, momentum/Adam betas, and schedules were chosen and whether they were tuned separately per batch size. LB training can require different schedules (e.g., linear scaling with warm-up). Showing results with tuned LB schedules would strengthen fairness.
  - Report whether total number of epochs, parameter updates, and examples seen were matched across SB/LB comparisons.

- Sharpness metric and invariances:
  - Discuss the dependence of the sharpness measure on parameterization (e.g., scaling symmetries with batch normalization or ReLU). Although the metric includes a normalization via (|(A+ x)i|+1), it may still be sensitive to reparameterizations. Consider additional scale-invariant variants (e.g., per-weight normalization or filter-wise normalization).
  - Supplement with an approximate top Hessian eigenvalue via a few power iterations (or Fisher information proxy) at SB/LB solutions, at least on smaller models, to corroborate the metric.

- Train vs test curvature:
  - You visualize train and test loss along paths; consider computing the sharpness metric also on the test loss (or an independent validation set) to directly link curvature differences to generalization.

- Role of gradient noise:
  - The warm-start experiment is compelling. To isolate the role of noise, consider experiments that inject calibrated gradient noise into LB updates to match the estimated noise scale of SB gradients. This could further test the “noise helps escape sharp basins” hypothesis.

- Distance in parameter space:
  - The observation that SB solutions are farther from initialization than LB ones is interesting but may be affected by symmetries. Consider complementary measures (e.g., representation similarity or linear mode connectivity) to compare solutions more robustly.

- Optimizer breadth:
  - While Adam is convenient, generalization often differs with SGD + momentum. Including tuned SGD+m experiments for SB/LB would strengthen claims of optimizer-agnosticity.

- Dynamic batch sizing:
  - Since warm-starting LB from SB works, a dynamic batching schedule (increasing batch size over training) seems promising. A small empirical study (even on one or two models) would substantively reinforce this recommendation.

- Reproducibility:
  - Provide code and detailed configs (learning rates, schedules, weight decay, batch norm settings), and random seeds for the reported runs. This will help the community validate and build upon the findings.

Questions for the authors
1) How were learning rates and schedules chosen for SB vs LB? Did you try batch-size-dependent scaling and warm-up for LB? If so, how did sharpness and test accuracy change?
2) Can you report the sharpness metric (or top Hessian eigenvalue estimates) on the test loss at SB/LB solutions? Does the sharpness gap persist on held-out data?
3) How sensitive are your sharpness conclusions to common reparameterizations (e.g., rescaling layers in BN networks)? Have you considered a scale-invariant sharpness proxy?
4) In the warm-start experiments, what fraction of training (epochs or loss level) is typically required before LB consistently converges to the flatter region? Does a gradual batch-size schedule approximate this behavior without a two-stage process?
5) Did you try matching the gradient noise scale across SB and LB (e.g., by adding Gaussian noise to LB gradients)? If so, does LB still converge to sharper minima?
6) Beyond Adam, how do tuned SGD+momentum LB runs compare in terms of sharpness and accuracy across your architectures?

Overall, this paper offers a clear empirical story, raises important questions, and suggests practical directions. Despite limited theory, the evidence is strong enough and the topic significant enough to merit acceptance.
