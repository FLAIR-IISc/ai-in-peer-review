1. Claim: Large-batch (LB) training converges to sharper minima than small-batch (SB) training, explaining worse generalization for LB.  
2. Experiment scope: Evaluated multiple architectures (FC and conv AlexNet/VGG variants) and datasets using ADAM with SB (batch=256) and LB (~10% dataset) across multiple seeds.  
3. Visualization: 1-D linear and curvilinear interpolations between SB and LB solutions show markedly sharper basins around LB solutions.  
4. Sharpness metric: Introduced a tractable local worst-case perturbation metric (full space and random low-dimensional subspaces) showing 10–100× higher sharpness for LB.  
5. Warm-start phenomenon: Running SB for some epochs before switching to LB lets LB reach flatter basins and recover test accuracy, suggesting gradient noise aids exploration.  
6. Remedies: Aggressive augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, and adversarial/stability training narrow but do not eliminate the SB–LB generalization gap.  
7. Practical takeaway: Proposes dynamic batch-size schedules and explicit noise injection as promising strategies for scalable training.  
8. Decision: Reviewer recommends accept based on the paper’s empirical contributions.  
9. Rationale: The paper offers careful, multi-faceted empirical evidence linking batch size to landscape geometry and generalization, with broadly useful methodology.  
10. Problem impact: Addressing LB generalization drop is a central scalability bottleneck with high practical and theoretical importance.  
11. Literature placement: Connects to MDL/Bayesian views and extends prior loss-landscape visualization and robustness analyses.  
12. Evidence rigor: Results use 5 random seeds per config; SB and LB reach similar training loss but diverge on test performance; parametric plots and sharpness metric consistently separate SB vs. LB.  
13. Causality support: Warm-start experiments provide a plausible causal mechanism where early SB noise enables escape to flatter basins.  
14. Robustness: Results reproduced across architectures, datasets, optimizers (briefly), and init choices; remedies tested strengthen the thesis.  
15. Practical relevance: Findings inform scalable training design (dynamic sampling, noise injection) and warn against naive batch scaling.  
16. Suggestion — confounder control: Match training budgets (epochs, gradient evaluations, updates) and report matched-budget protocols.  
17. Suggestion — hyperparameter exploration: Examine LR scaling rules with batch size, momentum, weight decay, and label smoothing effects on noise and sharpness.  
18. Suggestion — sharpen metric: Sensitivity analyses for epsilon, L-BFGS-B iterations, and A; correlate metric with top Hessian eigenvalues.  
19. Suggestion — curvature characterization: Report dimensionality/variance explained by the steep subspace (spectrum decay/effective rank).  
20. Suggestion — optimizer coverage: Include thorough SGD with momentum and modern sharpness-aware optimizers (e.g., SAM) comparisons.  
21. Suggestion — explicit noise diagnosis: Measure gradient noise scale across batch sizes and test whether calibrated noise injection in LB recovers SB-like minima.  
22. Suggestion — reproducibility: Release full hyperparameter grids, schedules, stopping criteria, seeds, and scripts for replication.  
23. Question — training schedules: How were LR schedules, momentum, and weight decay tuned/matched across SB vs. LB, and were standard LR scaling rules evaluated?  
24. Question — budget matching: Are results robust when matching total parameter updates or gradient computations instead of epochs?  
25. Question — sharpness sensitivity: How sensitive are sharpness conclusions to epsilon, inner iterations, and A; and what are correlations with top-k Hessian eigenvalues?  
26. Question — steep subspace quantification: Can you systematically quantify the ~5% low-dimensional concentration (spectrum decay/effective rank) across tasks?  
27. Question — optimizer/regularizer effects: Does a well-tuned SGD with momentum and modern regularizers preserve the SB–LB sharpness gap?  
28. Question — batch-norm interaction: Does batch normalization (ghost/synchronized BN) affect the batch-size–sharpness relationship?  
29. Question — noise injection experiments: Does explicit, calibrated gradient noise in LB allow escape from sharp basins without warm-starting, and do dynamic batch schedules recover SB-like minima?  
30. Question — robustness and warm-start detail: Do sharper LB solutions show worse robustness to distribution shifts, and how does the warm-start epoch threshold vary across models/datasets?
