Summary of contributions
- The paper presents an empirical study of how batch size affects generalization in deep networks. The central claims are: (1) small-batch (SB) methods tend to converge to flatter minima; (2) flatter minima generalize better than sharp minima; and (3) large-batch (LB) methods more often converge to sharp minima, explaining the observed generalization gap when using large batches.
- The authors support these claims with multiple lines of evidence: one-dimensional and curvilinear loss landscape visualizations between SB and LB solutions; a computationally feasible sharpness metric that approximates local sensitivity; experiments across several architectures (including AlexNet- and VGG-like CNNs and fully connected networks) and datasets; and warm-start experiments suggesting that the noise in SB optimization helps escape basins of sharp minima.
- They also explore mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/robust training) and observe partial but incomplete improvements, keeping the core phenomenon intact.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper addresses a timely and practically important question: why do large batches often hurt generalization? It provides clear, replicable empirical evidence linking LB training to sharp minimizers and worse test performance.
- While the novelty is primarily empirical rather than theoretical, the results are likely to influence both theory (by motivating assumptions) and practice (by guiding batch-size choices and training protocols).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The generalization gap with large batches is a widely observed bottleneck for scaling training. The paper is well positioned in the literature, citing theoretical and empirical work on SGD behavior, saddle avoidance, and loss landscape properties, and articulates why resolving this gap matters for parallel scalability and time-to-solution.
- Evidence for claims: The parametric plots convincingly show that SB and LB converge to different regions: the LB minima appear sharper, and this holds on training and test losses. The proposed sharpness metric, while heuristic, consistently shows 1–2 orders of magnitude larger sensitivity at LB solutions across architectures and datasets, and results are robust to searching in full space or random subspaces. Warm-start experiments further suggest that SB noise helps discover flatter regions before LB can refine them.
- Ruling out alternatives: The paper argues the generalization gap is not classical overfitting (no test accuracy decay after a peak), and early stopping would not help. The authors also test alternative optimizers for LB and find similar behavior, strengthening the main claim.
- Scope and limitations: The sharpness measure depends on ε and the subspace dimension p, and the maximization is approximate (limited L-BFGS-B steps), but the trends are strong and consistent. The work is primarily empirical, with no formal guarantees about convergence to sharp minima, which the authors acknowledge and frame as open questions.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to my decision)
- Clarify and systematize the sharpness metric: report sensitivity analyses over ε and p, and detail how results vary with the number of inner maximization iterations. Explicitly relate observed sharpness to estimates of top Hessian eigenvalues where feasible (e.g., via Hessian-vector products) to triangulate the metric.
- Hyperparameters and fairness: Ensure learning rate schedules, momentum/Adam parameters, weight decay, and normalization layers are matched or tuned comparably across SB and LB regimes. Consider reporting both per-epoch and per-update comparisons so readers can disentangle the effects of fewer/noisier gradient steps versus total compute.
- Batch-size thresholds: The batch-size–accuracy curves suggest thresholds where sharpness rises and generalization drops. It would help to quantify these thresholds more systematically across models and datasets, and relate them to gradient noise scale.
- Mitigations: The mitigation experiments are useful; consider adding dynamic batch-size schedules (small-to-large) as a primary result (not just as a discussion tied to warm-starts), since they appear promising in practice.
- Reporting and reproducibility: Where possible, include more statistical details (e.g., confidence intervals, significance tests across the five random restarts) and release code/scripts for sharpness measurement and plotting.

Questions for the authors
1. Sharpness metric:
   - How sensitive are your conclusions to ε, the choice of A (identity vs random subspace), and the number of inner L-BFGS-B iterations?
   - Did you compare the metric to estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalue(s) via power/Lanczos iterations on smaller models?
2. Optimizer and hyperparameters:
   - You report similar behavior with ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD, and adaQN for LB. Were learning-rate schedules and other hyperparameters separately tuned per batch size to ensure fair comparisons?
   - How does explicit weight decay, label smoothing, or gradient clipping affect the sharpness gap?
3. Training budget:
   - You trained “until the loss ceased to improve.” Did SB and LB see comparable numbers of parameter updates or epochs? Could equalizing updates versus epochs change the observed gap?
4. Architecture and parameterization:
   - Do results hold consistently with and without BatchNorm and Dropout? Have you examined whether reparameterizations (e.g., scaling layers) affect the sharpness conclusions?
5. Warm-start and dynamic batching:
   - In the warm-start study, can you more precisely characterize when SB reaches a “flat” region (e.g., via sharpness thresholds) and when LB successfully takes over? Have you tried a scheduled batch-size increase as a standalone training strategy?
6. Data augmentation and robustness:
   - With aggressive augmentation, LB test accuracy improves but minima remain sharp. Do adversarially augmented schemes that target the sharp directions you identify (e.g., subspace-restricted adversaries) help?
7. Generality:
   - Beyond the reported setups, did you observe similar phenomena on larger-scale datasets (e.g., ImageNet) or modern architectures?

Comment on a previous concern
- I previously questioned the claim that the sharpness criterion was scale-invariant. The authors have removed that claim in the revision, which resolves my concern.
