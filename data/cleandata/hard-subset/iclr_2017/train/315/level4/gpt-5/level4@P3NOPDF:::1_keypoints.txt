- Paper analyzes why increasing batch size can degrade optimization and generalization and offers practical insights for batch-size selection.
- Timely and relevant topic for scaling deep learning and practitioners.
- Clear problem statement addressing why larger batches often worsen generalization or cause unstable optimization.
- Conceptual clarity linking gradient noise, loss landscape geometry, and generalization in an understandable way.
- Practical implications that can guide hyperparameter choices and compute/throughput trade-offs.
- Needs clearer scope: specify which architectures, datasets, and tasks the conclusions apply to (CNNs, RNNs, MLPs, etc.).
- Tighten experimental controls by matching total optimization steps and epochs and reporting both for fair comparisons.
- Expand hyperparameter sweeps (LR scaling rules, momentum, weight decay, label smoothing, augmentations) and ablate warmup/decay schedules.
- Evaluate modern stabilizers (batch/layer norm, residuals, stochastic depth, strong augmentation) and compare SGD-momentum to adaptive optimizers under large-batch regimes.
- Quantify sharpness using multiple, scale-invariant proxies and report sensitivity to reparameterizations (Hessian spectrum, gradient noise scale, perturbation measures).
- Ensure compute-fair comparisons across equal wall-clock budgets and equal update counts to reflect practical and scientific perspectives.
- Test stochasticity-injection techniques (gradient noise, dropout schedules, mixup, SWA, ghost batch norm) for recovering generalization at large batch sizes.
- Report variability across multiple random seeds and include confidence intervals to distinguish systematic effects from noise.
- Provide concrete, actionable recommendations (max effective batch size, LR scaling and warmup rules, when accumulation suffices vs. true parallelism).
- Define terms precisely and quantify statements (e.g., specify test-accuracy drops or convergence to sharper minima).
- Add schematic illustrations and summary tables aggregating results across batch sizes, hyperparameters, and models.
- Ensure reproducibility by reporting architectures, preprocessing, exact schedules, batch sizes, hardware, and runtimes.
- Topic has high impact and is likely to influence best practices for large-scale training.
- Key open questions: sensitivity to LR scaling and warmup; persistence with stronger regularization/augmentation; which sharpness metrics predict gaps and are reparameterization-invariant; principled batch-size choice under fixed compute.
- Overall: interesting and useful work that merits acceptance if experimental rigor, broader coverage of modern techniques, and precise quantification are strengthened.
