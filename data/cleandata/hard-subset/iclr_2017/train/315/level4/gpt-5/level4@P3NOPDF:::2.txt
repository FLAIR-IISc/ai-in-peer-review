Summary
This paper presents an empirical study investigating two related hypotheses: (1) stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with smaller mini-batches tends to converge to flatter minima, and (2) flatter minima exhibit better generalization. The authors propose a sharpness-based criterion to quantify flatness and provide experimental evidence supporting both claims. In the revision, the authors removed an earlier claim that their sharpness measure is scale-invariant, addressing a prior concern.

Strengths
- The paper tackles a central and timely question in deep learning: why and when optimization choices, particularly batch size, affect generalization.
- The empirical findings are intuitively compelling and practically relevant, offering a clear narrative that links optimization noise, landscape geometry, and generalization.
- The results can guide both theoretical work (by suggesting realistic assumptions) and practical algorithm design (e.g., informed choices of batch size schedules to improve generalization).
- The authors were responsive to feedback and corrected an overstatement regarding the scale invariance of their sharpness criterion.

Weaknesses
- The novelty is limited. The work is primarily empirical and synthesizes observations that, while valuable, have been discussed in various forms in prior literature.
- The central conclusions rely heavily on a particular operationalization of “flatness” (via a sharpness measure). Although the revision removes an incorrect invariance claim, the dependence on this measure may limit generality and invites careful interpretation.
- The paper would be strengthened by a more thorough discussion of potential confounders (e.g., learning rate, momentum, data augmentation, and regularization) and by explicitly disentangling their effects from batch size.

Significance and Impact
Despite modest novelty, the paper provides useful evidence that can inform both theory and practice:
- For theory: The empirical patterns observed here can help identify which assumptions about loss landscapes and optimization noise are plausible in realistic deep learning settings, potentially guiding new generalization theories.
- For practice: The results motivate heuristic strategies—such as preferring smaller batches or using batch-size schedules—to steer optimization toward flatter regions that generalize better. Even absent formal guarantees, these insights can be directly actionable for practitioners.

Clarity and Presentation
The paper is generally clear and easy to follow. The revision appropriately removes the claim of scale invariance for the sharpness measure. It would still be helpful to:
- Clearly state the limitations of the sharpness criterion and discuss how results might change under alternative flatness metrics.
- Provide more detail on experimental controls (e.g., keeping effective learning rate comparable across batch sizes) to strengthen causal interpretation.

Reproducibility
Assuming standard datasets and architectures were used, the study appears reproducible. Releasing code and detailed hyperparameter settings would further enhance transparency and facilitate follow-up work.

Comments to the Authors
- Thank you for addressing the earlier concern regarding scale invariance. It improves the paper’s accuracy and credibility.
- Consider broadening the analysis to include alternative flatness metrics (e.g., Hessian spectrum approximations) to assess robustness of the conclusions.
- When comparing batch sizes, ensure fair matching of optimization hyperparameters (e.g., scaling learning rate with batch size) and report sensitivity analyses, as these choices can materially affect both sharpness and generalization.
- Discuss how the observed phenomena interact with common regularizers and data augmentation, which also influence flatness and generalization.

Overall Assessment
This is a solid empirical contribution that clarifies an important relationship between batch size, landscape geometry, and generalization. While the conceptual novelty is limited, the study is likely to stimulate further theoretical and algorithmic advances and offers practical guidance. I lean toward acceptance based on the clarity of the empirical message and its potential to influence both research and practice, contingent on improved discussion of limitations and experimental controls.
