This paper presents an interesting and practically useful investigation into the effects of batch size on optimization and generalization in deep networks. The central theme—contrasting large-batch (LB) and small-batch (SB) regimes—addresses a question of high relevance to both research and large-scale training practice. The empirical observations and discussion are likely to inform practitioners’ choices around batch size, learning rate, and training stability.

Strengths:
- The topic is timely and important, with clear implications for scaling training and achieving good generalization.
- The paper appears to be empirically grounded and provides a coherent narrative about how LB and SB regimes differ in their optimization dynamics and the properties of the minima they tend to find.
- The writing is generally clear and the takeaways are actionable.

Areas for improvement and suggestions:
- The paper would benefit from additional experiments that explicitly test whether the advantages commonly associated with SB training can be recovered in an LB setting by injecting appropriately scaled noise into the gradient updates. Concretely:
  - Inject zero-mean Gaussian noise into the LB gradient, with variance calibrated to approximate the gradient noise one would have observed with a smaller batch. A simple approach is to estimate the per-parameter gradient variance using mini-samples from a large batch, then scale the injected noise so that the effective noise scale matches that of SB.
  - Evaluate generalization, sharpness/flatness proxies (e.g., perturbation-based sharpness, Hessian trace approximations, top eigenvalues), and training speed under noise-injected LB versus standard SB baselines.
  - Study schedules: constant noise, annealed noise, and noise coupled to the learning-rate schedule to disentangle the contributions of learning rate and noise.
  - Compare injected-gradient noise to other regularization and stochasticity sources (e.g., dropout, data augmentation, label smoothing) to determine whether gradient noise provides distinctive benefits.
- Control for and report key confounders. Because the effective “noise scale” in stochastic optimization depends on batch size, learning rate, and momentum, it is important to:
  - Systematically sweep learning rate and momentum for each batch size.
  - Report total number of updates and total number of epochs to separate compute budget effects from batch-size effects.
  - Consider training with and without common techniques such as warmup and weight decay to ensure the conclusions are robust.
- Extend coverage across architectures and datasets if feasible. Even a small set of additional models (e.g., a different CNN or a modest transformer) and datasets of varying difficulty can strengthen the generality of the claims.
- Provide more direct evidence for the “geometry” story if that is a central claim. In addition to loss-perturbation sharpness, approximate the Hessian spectrum (e.g., top eigenvalues via power iteration, or trace via Hutchinson) and correlate with generalization gaps across runs and batch sizes.
- Clarify definitions and experimental details for reproducibility:
  - Precisely define “LB” and “SB” in terms of batch sizes relative to dataset size and gradient noise scale.
  - List all hyperparameters, schedules (learning rate, noise, weight decay), and early-stopping criteria.
  - If possible, release code or detailed pseudocode for the noise-injected LB experiment.

Significance and impact:
- If confirmed by the proposed noise-injection experiments, the paper’s conclusions could offer a practical recipe for closing the LB–SB generalization gap while preserving the throughput advantages of large batches. This would be valuable for large-scale training settings where wall-clock efficiency is critical.
- The work also helps clarify the interplay between stochasticity and optimization landscape, which continues to be a central topic in deep learning theory and practice.

Overall assessment:
- I find the paper interesting, useful, and well-motivated. The current results are promising, and the suggested follow-up experiments—especially injecting rescaled Gaussian noise into LB gradients—could significantly strengthen the causal story and practical guidance. I encourage the authors to incorporate these investigations, broaden the experimental coverage where possible, and add more rigorous measures of curvature and noise scale. With these enhancements, the paper would make a strong and impactful contribution.
