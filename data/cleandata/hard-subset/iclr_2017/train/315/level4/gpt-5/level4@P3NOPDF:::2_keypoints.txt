- Studies two hypotheses: small-batch SGD leads to flatter minima, and flatter minima generalize better; uses a sharpness-based flatness measure and removed an earlier (incorrect) scale-invariance claim.  
- Tackles a central, timely question in deep learning linking batch size, optimization noise, landscape geometry, and generalization.  
- Empirical findings are intuitively compelling and practically relevant, guiding theory and algorithm design (e.g., batch-size schedules).  
- Authors were responsive to feedback and corrected the overstatement about scale invariance.  
- Novelty is limited—primarily an empirical synthesis of observations already discussed in prior work.  
- Results depend heavily on a specific sharpness measure, which may limit generality and requires careful interpretation.  
- The paper needs a more thorough treatment of potential confounders (learning rate, momentum, data augmentation, regularization) and disentangling their effects from batch size.  
- For theory: empirical patterns can inform realistic assumptions about loss landscapes and optimization noise.  
- For practice: motivates heuristics like smaller batches or scheduling to favor flatter, better-generalizing minima.  
- Generally clear and well presented, but should explicitly state limitations of the sharpness metric and how alternative metrics might change results.  
- Experimental controls need more detail (e.g., matching effective learning rates across batch sizes) to strengthen causal claims.  
- Likely reproducible with standard datasets/architectures; releasing code and hyperparameters would improve transparency.  
- Recommendations: test alternative flatness metrics (e.g., Hessian approximations), ensure fair hyperparameter matching and sensitivity analyses, and discuss interactions with regularizers and data augmentation.  
- Overall: a solid empirical contribution with modest novelty; leans toward acceptance contingent on better discussion of limitations and experimental controls.
