- Paper claims: small-batch (SB) training finds flatter minima, flatter minima generalize better, and large-batch (LB) training tends to converge to sharp minima explaining LB generalization gap.
- Evidence provided via 1D and curvilinear loss visualizations showing different regions for SB vs LB solutions.
- Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric approximating local sensitivity and uses it across models/datasets.
- Experiments span multiple architectures (AlexNet-/VGG-like CNNs, fully connected nets) and datasets, showing consistent trends.
- Warm-start experiments suggest SB noise helps escape basins of sharp minima, enabling finding flatter regions.
- Mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial training) give partial improvements but do not eliminate the phenomenon.
- Decision: Accept — timely, practically important, and provides clear, replicable empirical evidence linking LB training to sharp minima and worse test performance.
- Novelty is primarily empirical but likely to influence both theory and practice by motivating assumptions and training protocols.
- Problem motivation: Addresses widely observed large-batch generalization bottleneck, well situated in literature on SGD, saddles, and loss landscapes.
- Strength of evidence: Parametric plots and sharpness metric show 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sensitivity at LB solutions, robust across subspace/full-space searches.
- Warm-start results strengthen causal argument that SB noise aids discovery of flatter minima before LB refinement.
- Alternatives ruled out: Not classical overfitting; early stopping unlikely to resolve gap; similar behavior across different LB optimizers.
- Limitations: Sharpness measure depends on ε and subspace dimension p, maximization is approximate, and the work is empirical without formal convergence guarantees.
- Feedback — sharpness metric: Clarify and systematize sensitivity analyses over ε and p, report dependence on inner maximization iterations, and relate to top Hessian eigenvalues where possible.
- Feedback — hyperparameters/fairness: Ensure learning-rate schedules, momentum/Adam params, weight decay, and normalization layers are matched/tuned across SB and LB; report per-epoch and per-update comparisons.
- Feedback — batch-size thresholds: Systematically quantify batch-size thresholds where sharpness and generalization drop and relate to gradient noise scale.
- Feedback — mitigations: Consider dynamic/scheduled small-to-large batch-size training as a primary experimental result (not just warm-start discussion).
- Feedback — reproducibility: Add statistical details (confidence intervals, significance across restarts) and release code/scripts for sharpness measurement and plotting.
- Question — sharpness metric specifics: How sensitive are conclusions to ε, choice of A (identity vs random subspace), and number of inner L-BFGS-B iterations; any comparisons to power/Lanczos Hessian estimates?
- Question — optimizer/hyperparams: Were LR schedules and other hyperparameters separately tuned per batch size; effects of weight decay, label smoothing, or gradient clipping?
- Question — training budget: Did SB and LB see comparable numbers of parameter updates or epochs; would equalizing updates vs epochs change the gap?
- Question — architecture/parameterization: Are results consistent with/without BatchNorm and Dropout; do reparameterizations (e.g., layer scaling) affect sharpness conclusions?
- Question — warm-start/dynamic batching: Can you pinpoint when SB reaches a “flat” region and when LB can take over; have scheduled batch-size increases been tried as a standalone strategy?
- Question — augmentation/robustness: Do adversarial or subspace-targeted augmentations that target identified sharp directions help LB performance?
- Question — generality: Do similar phenomena appear at larger scale (e.g., ImageNet) or with modern architectures?
- Comment on prior concern: Removal of the scale-invariance claim resolves the reviewer’s previous issue.
