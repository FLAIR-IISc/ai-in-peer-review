- Timely problem: investigates why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) despite similar training loss.  
- Hypothesis: LB converges to sharp, high-curvature minima while SB’s gradient noise favors flatter minima that generalize better.  
- Empirical evidence: 1-D/curvilinear interpolations, a tractable local sharpness metric, and warm-start experiments linking SB exploration to flatter minima.  
- Breadth of experiments: tested on multiple architectures/datasets, found a batch-size threshold where generalization degrades, and evaluated partial remedies that reduce but don’t eliminate the gap.  
- Literature placement: connects to MDL/Bayesian interpretations and recent loss-landscape analyses; discusses implications for parallel training and dynamic batching.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key acceptance reasons: clear, reproducible empirical characterization of the LB generalization gap tied to sharpness across diverse models; warm-start and batch-threshold results provide actionable insights.  
- Problem clarity: well-posed, practically relevant question about whether LB and SB find qualitatively different minima.  
- Motivation and placement: well-motivated by scalability needs and prior observations; links to optimizer behavior and theory.  
- Methodological rigor: thoughtful empirical design—SB/LB comparisons, loss-path visualizations, sharpness metrics (full and subspace), and causal warm-start studies with consistent results.  
- Scope and limits: primarily empirical work; sharpness metric is heuristic and only indirectly tied to Hessian spectra but shows robust, often large, differences.  
- Hyperparameter suggestion: clarify and possibly tune learning rates, momenta, and schedules per batch size; report matching of epochs/updates/examples.  
- Sharpness metric suggestion: discuss parameterization dependence and consider scale-invariant normalizations or filter-wise scaling; corroborate with top Hessian eigenvalue estimates.  
- Train vs test curvature: compute sharpness on test/validation loss to directly link curvature to generalization.  
- Role of gradient noise: perform experiments injecting calibrated noise into LB updates to test whether noise alone explains SB’s flatter basins.  
- Distance measures: complement parameter-space distance with representation similarity or mode-connectivity metrics to avoid symmetry issues.  
- Optimizer breadth: include tuned SGD+momentum SB/LB experiments to test optimizer-agnosticity of findings.  
- Dynamic batching: evaluate increasing-batch-size schedules (or two-stage training) as a practical remedy.  
- Reproducibility: release code, detailed configs, and seeds.  
- Question: How were learning rates and schedules chosen for SB vs LB, and were batch-size-dependent scalings/warm-up tried?  
- Question: Can you report sharpness metrics or top Hessian eigenvalue estimates on held-out/test loss—does the gap persist?  
- Question: How sensitive are sharpness conclusions to reparameterizations (e.g., BN rescaling); any scale-invariant proxies tested?  
- Question: In warm-starts, how much training is needed before LB reliably finds the flatter region, and can gradual batch schedules replicate this?  
- Question: Did you try matching gradient-noise scales across SB and LB (e.g., adding Gaussian noise to LB); what were the effects?  
- Question: How do tuned SGD+momentum LB runs compare in sharpness and accuracy across architectures?
