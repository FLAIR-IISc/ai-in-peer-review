- Paper finds large-batch (LB) training converges to sharper minima and worse generalization than small-batch (SB), supported by interpolations, a tractable sharpness metric, and warm-start experiments.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: Clear, reproducible empirical link between batch size, sharpness, and generalization across multiple networks/datasets.  
- Key reason: Presents useful diagnostic tools (interpolation plots, pragmatic sharpness metric) and practical guidance (warm-start/dynamic batching) relevant to scalable training.  
- Empirical rigor: Experiments across six architectures and several datasets, multiple seeds, robust to optimizer choice, showing consistent 1–2 orders-of-magnitude sharpness differences.  
- Practical value: Identifies a batch-size threshold degrading generalization and shows SB pre-training can enable successful LB training; negative mitigation results are informative.  
- Caveat: Sharpness metric depends on parameterization and scaling; ReLU rescalings may alter Hessian magnitudes without changing function behavior.  
- Caveat: Unclear whether hyperparameters (LR schedules, momentum/Adam betas, weight decay, total updates) were fully tuned per batch size—potential confound.  
- Caveat: Using batch sizes of ~10% of data may be extreme; thresholds may depend on dataset size and updates per epoch.  
- Caveat: Theoretical discussion is limited; no formal guarantees.  
- Suggestion: Report per-batch-size LR/momentum/beta schedules, ablations on scaling/warmup, and sensitivity to these choices.  
- Suggestion: Match number of parameter updates or total examples processed across SB and LB to disentangle update count from batch-size effects.  
- Suggestion: Calibrate weight decay across batch sizes and report its effect on sharpness and generalization.  
- Suggestion: Address parameterization invariance—use scale-normalized sharpness, per-layer scaling, or function-space measures (e.g., input–output Jacobian).  
- Suggestion: Complement the heuristic metric with top Hessian eigenvalue estimates (power/Lanczos) to triangulate sharpness evidence.  
- Suggestion: Measure gradient-noise scale and its relation to the batch-size threshold; inject explicit gradient noise into LB to test the escape hypothesis.  
- Suggestion: Test label smoothing, adjusted dropout, and other regularizers under LB to see if flatness/generalization improve without SB pre-training.  
- Suggestion: Clarify sharpness metric choices (ε, p, construction of A), justify L-BFGS-B steps, and release full training scripts, seeds, and configs.  
- Question: How were LR, momentum/Adam betas, and weight decay chosen per batch size; was linear LR scaling or warmup tried?  
- Question: Is the sharpness metric robust under ReLU rescalings; have layer-wise normalizations or normalized parameterizations been tried?  
- Question: Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues (or Ritz values) at SB vs LB solutions?  
- Question: How do results change when equalizing total updates or total examples between SB and LB?  
- Question: In warm-starts, what SB/LB fraction was used and can explicit gradient-noise injection reproduce the improvement?  
- Question: How does the threshold batch size scale with dataset size, model size, and estimated gradient noise—any predictive rule-of-thumb?  
- Question: Were label smoothing, altered dropout rates, or other regularizers tested under LB to reduce sharpness without SB pre-training?
