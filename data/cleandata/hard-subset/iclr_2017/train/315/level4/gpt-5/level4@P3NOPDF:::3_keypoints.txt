- Timely, important topic contrasting large-batch (LB) and small-batch (SB) regimes with practical relevance for scaling and generalization.
- Empirically grounded paper that coherently describes differing optimization dynamics and minima properties for LB vs SB.
- Writing is clear and takeaways are actionable for practitioners.
- Main suggested experiment: inject zero-mean Gaussian noise into LB gradients with variance calibrated to SB gradient noise to test if SB benefits can be recovered.
- Practical implementation: estimate per-parameter gradient variance from mini-samples of a large batch and scale injected noise accordingly.
- Evaluate metrics under noise-injected LB vs SB: generalization, sharpness/flatness proxies, Hessian approximations, top eigenvalues, and training speed.
- Study noise schedules: constant, annealed, and noise coupled to learning-rate schedule to disentangle noise vs learning-rate effects.
- Compare injected-gradient noise to other regularizers/stochasticity (dropout, data augmentation, label smoothing) to assess distinctiveness of gradient noise benefits.
- Control confounders: systematically sweep learning rate and momentum for each batch size, and report total updates and epochs to separate compute effects from batch-size effects.
- Test robustness with/without common techniques (warmup, weight decay) to ensure conclusions generalize across typical training pipelines.
- Broaden experimental coverage across architectures and datasets (e.g., another CNN, modest transformer) to strengthen generality.
- Provide more direct geometry evidence: loss-perturbation sharpness plus Hessian-spectrum approximations (top eigenvalues via power iteration, trace via Hutchinson) correlated with generalization gaps.
- Clarify definitions and details for reproducibility: precise LB/SB definitions, full hyperparameter lists and schedules, early-stopping criteria, and release code or detailed pseudocode.
- Significance: if noise-injection recovers SB generalization, it could close the LB–SB gap while preserving throughput—high practical impact.
- Overall assessment: promising and useful work; recommended to run proposed noise-injection and broader experiments and add curvature/noise-scale analyses to strengthen causal claims.
