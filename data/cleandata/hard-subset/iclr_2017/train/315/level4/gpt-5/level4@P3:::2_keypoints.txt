- Central claim: Large-batch (LB) training converges to sharper minima with higher curvature and sensitivity, while small-batch (SB) training finds flatter minima that generalize better.

- Empirical scope: Experiments across multiple architectures and datasets using ADAM show consistent sharper loss basins for LB vs. SB.

- Visualization evidence: One-dimensional and curvilinear parametric loss plots consistently reveal sharper basins for LB solutions.

- Sharpness metric: A practical heuristic metric approximates local sensitivity via constrained perturbations in full space and random subspaces.

- Batch-size threshold: Experiments indicate a “batch-size threshold” beyond which sharpness and generalization degrade.

- Warm-start finding: LB training can generalize well if initialized near flat regions found by SB (warm-start experiments).

- Remedies tested: Data augmentation, conservative/proximal training, and adversarial training reduce but do not eliminate the LB–SB generalization gap.

- Recommendation: Accept — the paper provides clear, reproducible empirical evidence linking batch size, solution geometry, and generalization, with practical and theoretical relevance.

- Contribution framing: Targets why large batches harm generalization, connecting to flat vs. sharp minima literature and MDL/Bayesian views.

- Evidence and rigor: Multiple trials, architectures, parametric visualizations, warm-start analyses, and exploration of alternatives strengthen the empirical case despite the heuristic metric.

- Metric clarification requested: Provide sensitivity analyses for epsilon, number of L-BFGS-B steps, and subspace dimension p, plus calibration/stability plots.

- Reparameterization concern: Discuss metric behavior under parameter rescaling and batch-normalization-induced symmetries and note limitations.

- Optimizer breadth: Include comparisons with SGD+momentum and experiments using linear learning-rate scaling/warmup for large batches to separate batch-size from optimizer effects.

- Compute/steps control: Report results under matched parameter-update counts, wall-clock, or epochs to rule out confounds between SB and LB comparisons.

- Hessian diagnostics: Add approximate spectral analyses (top eigenvalues via Lanczos/power iteration) to corroborate the sharpness metric and identify high-curvature subspaces.

- Architecture generality: Test on modern architectures (e.g., ResNets) to strengthen external validity.

- Dynamic-batch remedy: Try gradual batch-size increases with learning-rate scaling and compare to Entropy-SGD or explicit noise injection to match gradient noise across batch sizes.

- Question — metric robustness: How sensitive are sharpness measurements to epsilon, L-BFGS-B steps, and subspace dimension p?

- Question — reparameterization artifacts: Do scaling symmetries from batch norm or weight reparameterizations affect the metric and conclusions?

- Question — optimizer effects: Do results hold for SGD+momentum with tuned schedules, linear scaling, warmup, or matched gradient noise?

- Question — matching budgets: Does the generalization gap persist under equal parameter updates or equal wall-clock time?

- Question — threshold characterization: Can the batch-size threshold be predicted from gradient noise scale or curvature estimates?

- Question — landscape subspace: Can you quantify the dimension and alignment of the low-dimensional subspace with large curvature across runs or layers?

- Question — warm-start specifics: How many SB epochs are needed for LB to find flat minima and how does this vary by architecture/dataset?

- Question — regularization interaction: How do weight decay, dropout, and data augmentation intensity interact with batch size in shaping sharpness and generalization?

- Question — generality to larger-scale tasks: Do trends persist on ResNets and larger datasets?
