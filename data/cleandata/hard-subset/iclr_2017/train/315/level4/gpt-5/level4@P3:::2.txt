1. Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training of deep neural networks often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training, despite similar training loss. The authors’ central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima—characterized by high curvature and sensitivity—while SB methods converge to flatter minima, which generalize better. They support this with:

- Empirical evidence across multiple architectures and datasets, using ADAM for both SB and LB regimes.
- One-dimensional and curvilinear parametric plots showing loss landscapes between SB and LB solutions, consistently revealing sharper basins for LB.
- A computationally practical sharpness metric that approximates local sensitivity via constrained perturbations in full space and random subspaces.
- Experiments suggesting a “batch-size threshold” beyond which generalization degrades and sharpness increases.
- Warm-start experiments showing that LB can generalize well if initialized near a flat region found by SB.
- Preliminary tests of remedies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal training, adversarial training), which reduce but do not close the generalization gap.

Overall, the work provides a coherent empirical narrative linking batch size, optimization dynamics, and generalization.

2. Recommendation: Accept
Primary reasons:
- The paper provides clear, reproducible empirical evidence that connects batch size to the geometry of the converged solution and, consequently, to generalization. This addresses a question of high practical relevance.
- The findings are well positioned in the literature and likely to influence both theory (assumptions about noise and landscape geometry) and practice (guidelines for batch sizing, dynamic batching, and algorithm design).

3. Supporting arguments
- Specific question/problem: Why do large batches harm generalization, and what characterizes the solutions obtained by LB vs. SB methods? The paper targets this precisely, going beyond anecdote to structured analyses.
- Motivation and placement: The study builds on known observations of a generalization gap with larger batches and connects to prior views on flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber) and MDL/Bayesian interpretations. It also relates to recent analyses of neural loss landscapes and scalability constraints in distributed training.
- Evidence and rigor: While the sharpness metric is heuristic, it is thoughtfully designed to be feasible at scale and is examined in both full space and random subspaces. Multiple trials and architectures, consistent parametric visualizations, and warm-start analyses collectively strengthen the claims. The authors explicitly show that the gap is not due to classical overfitting (training/testing curves), and they explore several plausible alternative explanations before converging on their main hypothesis.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Clarify the sharpness metric: Provide sensitivity analyses for the epsilon parameter, number of L-BFGS-B steps, and subspace dimension p. Include calibration plots to show stability of the metric across these choices.
- Reparameterization and scale: Explicitly discuss how the metric behaves under parameter rescaling (especially with batch normalization and weight rescalings). You already addressed an earlier scale-invariance claim in revision; adding a short note on remaining invariances/limitations would help.
- Optimizer/hyperparameter breadth: Most results use ADAM. Please include comparisons with SGD+momentum (with tuned learning rate schedules) and with linear learning-rate scaling and warmup commonly used for large-batch training. This will help disentangle batch-size effects from optimizer choices.
- Control for compute/steps: Report results under matched conditions (e.g., fixed number of parameter updates, matched wall-clock, or matched epochs) to rule out confounds. This is especially important when comparing SB vs. LB dynamics.
- Hessian spectrum: Where feasible, add approximate spectral diagnostics (e.g., top eigenvalues via Lanczos/power iteration) to corroborate the sharpness metric and to quantify the low-dimensional subspace where curvature is large.
- Modern architectures: Include experiments on residual networks or other widely used architectures, to strengthen external validity.
- Remedies: Dynamic batch-size schedules appear promising (per warm-start results). A short experiment with gradual batch-size increase and corresponding learning-rate scaling would add practical value. A comparison to Entropy-SGD or explicit noise injection to match gradient noise levels across batch sizes would also be informative.

5. Questions for the authors
- Metric robustness: How sensitive are the sharpness measurements to the choice of epsilon, the number of L-BFGS-B steps, and the subspace dimension p? Do the SB–LB differences persist across a reasonable range?
- Reparameterization: How does the metric behave under scaling symmetries induced by batch normalization or weight reparameterizations? Can you demonstrate that your conclusions are not artifacts of such symmetries?
- Optimizer effects: Do the conclusions hold for SGD with momentum and tuned learning-rate schedules, including linear scaling and warmup for LB? How about matching the noise scale by adding gradient noise to LB updates?
- Matching budgets: When SB and LB are compared under equal numbers of parameter updates (or equal wall-clock), do you still observe the same generalization gap and sharpness differences?
- Threshold phenomenon: Can you characterize the “batch-size threshold” in terms of gradient noise scale or curvature estimates? Is it predictable from measurable quantities during training?
- Landscape structure: You note that steep increases occur in a small-dimensional subspace near LB solutions. Can you quantify that subspace’s dimension and alignment across runs or across layers?
- Warm-starting: In the warm-start experiments, how many SB epochs are typically needed for LB to subsequently find a flat minimum? Does this depend on architecture or dataset?
- Regularization: How do weight decay, dropout rates, and data augmentation intensity interact with batch size in shaping sharpness and generalization?
- Generality: Have you tested the phenomenon on more recent architectures (e.g., ResNets) and larger-scale datasets? If so, do the trends persist?

Overall, despite limited methodological novelty, the paper provides a compelling and timely empirical study that clarifies an important and practically consequential phenomenon, and it suggests concrete directions for both theory and algorithm design.
