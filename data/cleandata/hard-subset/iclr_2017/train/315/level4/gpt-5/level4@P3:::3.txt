Summary and contributions
The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training. Across several architectures and datasets, the authors present consistent empirical evidence that LB methods converge to sharp minima—regions where the loss increases rapidly around the solution—while SB methods converge to flatter minima. The paper supports this claim with (i) 1-D and curvilinear interpolations between SB and LB solutions showing markedly higher curvature near LB solutions, (ii) a computationally tractable “sharpness” metric based on local worst-case perturbations, and (iii) warm-start experiments indicating that early SB noise helps escape sharp basins so that subsequent LB optimization can converge to flatter regions. The work further explores mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative updates, adversarial/stability training) and reports partial but insufficient improvements, motivating dynamic sampling or warm starts. The narrative aligns with intuitions from MDL/Bayesian views and with recent theory on many comparable-value local minima.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Clear, reproducible empirical link between batch size, sharpness, and generalization across multiple networks and datasets.
- Insightful diagnostic tools (interpolation plots and a pragmatic sharpness metric) and practical guidance (warm-start/dynamic batch schedules), with direct implications for scalable training.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The work addresses a practically important question—why simply increasing batch size harms test accuracy—relevant to parallel scaling and training time reduction. The motivation is well grounded in the literature (SGD theory, loss landscape studies, regularization through noise).
- Placement in literature: The paper contrasts SB and LB behavior, situates its view alongside MDL/Bayesian interpretations, and relates to emerging theory on multiplicity of local minima. It also connects to distributed training and dynamic sampling work.
- Empirical rigor: The authors evaluate six architectures (FCN and CNN variants) across several datasets, reporting mean and variance over multiple seeds. They control for optimizer (primarily ADAM) and show that results are robust to alternative optimizers. The parametric plots are persuasive, and the warm-start experiments nicely demonstrate how early noise affects the basin reached. The sharpness metric, while heuristic, is computationally feasible and applied in both full space and random subspaces, giving consistent 1–2 orders-of-magnitude differences between SB and LB solutions.
- Practical value: The threshold behavior in batch size, and the demonstration that SB pre-training enables LB to succeed, offer actionable guidance for scalable training. The negative results (adversarial training, conservative updates) are also informative.

Caveats and limitations (do not preclude acceptance)
- The sharpness metric depends on parameterization and scaling; ReLU networks admit rescalings that can change Hessian magnitudes without affecting function behavior. Although the authors attempt invariance via the constraint set, more discussion and controls would strengthen the claim that measured sharpness tracks generalization rather than parameterization artifacts.
- Hyperparameter tuning across batch regimes is crucial. It is unclear whether learning-rate schedules, momentum/Adam betas, weight decay, and total update counts were fully optimized per batch size or scaled (e.g., linear scaling with warmup). This is a potential confound for generalization and sharpness.
- Batch sizes set as 10% of data may be extremely large for some datasets; results and thresholds could depend on dataset size and number of updates per epoch. Reporting results at multiple LB sizes with matched compute/updates would clarify sensitivity.
- The theoretical discussion is intentionally limited; formal guarantees are left open.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not required for acceptance)
- Hyperparameter controls:
  - Report the learning-rate/momentum/beta schedules used for each batch size, and whether standard scaling rules or warmup were applied. Include ablations showing sensitivity of the LB regime to these choices.
  - Match either the number of parameter updates or total examples processed across SB and LB runs to disentangle “number of updates” from “batch size” effects.
  - Calibrate weight decay across batch sizes (e.g., scale with effective learning rate) and report its effect on sharpness and generalization.

- Sharpness metric and invariance:
  - Discuss parameterization invariance more explicitly. Consider scale-normalized sharpness (e.g., measuring in a normalized parameter space, per-layer weight scaling, or using function-space perturbations such as input–output Jacobian norms).
  - Complement the heuristic metric with estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos at SB and LB solutions to triangulate the evidence.

- Additional diagnostics:
  - Report the relationship between batch size, an estimate of gradient noise scale, and the observed “threshold” where generalization degrades.
  - Include experiments with explicit noise injection (e.g., adding rescaled Gaussian noise to the LB gradients to match SB noise) to test the central hypothesis that noise enables escape from sharp basins.
  - Explore label smoothing and adjustment of dropout/regularization in LB training to see if flatness and generalization improve without SB pre-training.

- Clarity and reproducibility:
  - Specify ε and p in the sharpness metric, detail the construction of A, and justify the choice of 10 L-BFGS-B steps (with a sensitivity check).
  - Release full training scripts, seeds, and configurations for all models, not only the plotting code.

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates, momentum/Adam betas, and weight decay chosen for each batch size? Did you try linear LR scaling and/or warmup for LB? How sensitive are your conclusions to these settings?
- Is your sharpness metric robust under standard ReLU rescalings (e.g., scaling incoming/outgoing weights)? Have you tried layer-wise normalization or measuring sharpness in a normalized parameterization?
- Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues (or a few Ritz values) at SB vs LB solutions to corroborate the metric?
- How do results change when you equalize total parameter updates, or when you equalize total number of examples processed, across SB and LB?
- In the warm-start experiments, what fraction of training is SB vs LB, and is the observed improvement reproducible with explicit gradient-noise injection in LB from the start?
- How does the “threshold” batch size scale with dataset size, model size, and estimated gradient noise? Can you provide a predictive rule-of-thumb?
- Did you test label smoothing, modified dropout rates, or alternative regularizers under LB to reduce sharpness without SB pre-training?

Overall, this is a timely and practically impactful paper with careful experiments and clear messaging. With the suggested clarifications and additional controls, it will be even stronger.
