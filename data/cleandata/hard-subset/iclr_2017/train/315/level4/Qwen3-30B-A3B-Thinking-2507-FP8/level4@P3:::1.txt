

# Review for ICLR 2017

## Summary
This paper addresses a significant practical concern in deep learning: why large-batch training methods often yield worse generalization performance than small-batch methods despite similar training accuracy. The authors propose that this "generalization gap" stems from large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity in the loss landscape), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with lower sensitivity). They support this claim with numerical evidence using parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and explore potential remedies including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting observation but fails to provide sufficient evidence to establish a causal relationship between minimizer sharpness and generalization performance, and the proposed sharpness metric lacks theoretical grounding.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's central claim—that sharp minimizers cause poor generalization—is supported primarily by correlation, not causation. While the authors demonstrate that large-batch methods converge to points with higher values of their sharpness metric (Equation 4) and exhibit worse generalization, they do not establish that sharpness is the causal factor. Alternative explanations remain plausible: the optimization paths taken by large-batch methods might inherently lead to different solutions, with sharpness merely being a correlated property rather than the causal mechanism.

The sharpness metric itself is problematic. The authors define it as a sensitivity measure (Equation 4) but provide insufficient theoretical justification for its use. The metric appears ad-hoc, and the paper fails to convincingly connect it to the established concept of sharp minima in the literature (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Ferris, 1988). While the authors note that the metric relates to the Hessian's largest eigenvalues, they do not demonstrate that the metric reliably captures this relationship, especially in high-dimensional deep learning settings where computing eigenvalues is intractable.

The experimental design also raises concerns. The authors use ADAM for both small and large batch methods, which may not be the most appropriate comparison since ADAM's behavior depends on batch size. The paper would benefit from comparing with other optimizers for large batches to rule out optimizer-specific effects. Additionally, the paper's claim that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting is correct for their specific experiments, but they do not adequately address whether the observed phenomenon might be specific to their network architectures or training regimes.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a clearer theoretical foundation for their sharpness metric, demonstrating how it relates to the established concept of sharp minima in the literature. They should explain why their metric is preferable to other possible measures.

2. Conduct experiments with multiple optimizers for large-batch training to demonstrate that their findings are not specific to ADAM.

3. Include more thorough analysis of the relationship between their sharpness metric and the actual eigenvalues of the Hessian, or at least provide evidence that the metric correlates with theoretical sharpness measures in simpler settings.

4. Address potential alternative explanations for their observations, such as whether the optimization path or initial conditions might be the true causal factors.

## Questions for Authors
1. How do you justify the specific form of your sharpness metric (Equation 4) compared to other possible measures of minimizer sharpness? What theoretical or empirical evidence supports this particular formulation?

2. Given that your metric is based on a local sensitivity measure, how do you account for the possibility that the observed correlation between sharpness and generalization might be spurious, and that the true causal factor could be something else (e.g., the optimization path taken)?

3. Have you tested your sharpness metric on simpler optimization problems where the Hessian can be computed exactly, to verify that it reliably captures the theoretical concept of sharp minima?

The paper identifies an important empirical phenomenon in deep learning, but the evidence presented is insufficient to establish the causal mechanism it proposes. The lack of theoretical grounding for the sharpness metric and the correlational nature of the evidence prevent this work from making a strong contribution to the field. With additional theoretical justification and more rigorous experimental design, this work could be significantly strengthened.
