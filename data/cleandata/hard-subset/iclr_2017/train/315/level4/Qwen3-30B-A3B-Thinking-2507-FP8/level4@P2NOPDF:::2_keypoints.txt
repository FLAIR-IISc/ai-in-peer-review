- Empirical study linking SGD batch size, loss landscape geometry, and generalization.
- Shows two main claims: smaller batch sizes converge to flatter minima, and flatter minima generalize better.
- Strong empirical analysis across architectures and datasets that convincingly demonstrates the batch size–flatter minima–generalization link.
- Practical value: provides an actionable heuristic (use smaller batch sizes) to improve generalization without complex algorithm changes.
- Clarifies how a hyperparameter (batch size) influences model robustness, useful for resource-constrained practitioners.
- Limited novelty: primarily reinforces prior observations (e.g., Keskar et al., Hoffer et al.) rather than offering new theory.
- Methodology is standard and lacks innovative metrics or experimental design to significantly advance the field.
- Significance: offers empirical constraints for theory development and a simple practical strategy for better generalization.
- Minor revision: authors withdrew the scale-invariance claim for their sharpness metric, improving technical correctness.
- Overall: well-executed, empirically rigorous work that meaningfully validates the batch size–generalization relationship despite not being groundbreaking theoretically.
