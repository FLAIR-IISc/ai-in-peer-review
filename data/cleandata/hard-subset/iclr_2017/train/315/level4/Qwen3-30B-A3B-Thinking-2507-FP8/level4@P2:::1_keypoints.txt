- Paper studies why large-batch SGD generalizes worse, attributing it to convergence to sharp minima vs small-batch convergence to flat minima.  
- Proposes and uses a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Eq.4) as an alternative to Hessian eigenvalue calculations.  
- Provides extensive experiments across architectures/datasets using parametric plots and the sharpness metric to support the hypothesis.  
- Finds 1–2 orders-of-magnitude higher sharpness for large-batch solutions and up to ~5% worse generalization.  
- Warm-start experiments show large-batch methods can generalize well when initialized along small-batch trajectories, implying small-batch exploration finds flat minima.  
- Authors rule out alternative explanations (overfitting, saddle points) and relate results to recent loss-surface theory.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions — significant contribution with robust empirical evidence.  
- Request to clarify the theoretical/empirical relationship between the sharpness metric (Eq.4) and the Hessian spectrum, especially the largest eigenvalue.  
- Suggest expanding the discussion of "flatness" and explicitly connecting results to MDL and Bayesian arguments for generalization.  
- Ask for clearer, actionable guidance for practitioners on choosing batch sizes or modifying optimizers; Appendix C is a start but needs more concrete advice.  
- Recommend more detailed, quantitative comparison of the mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial training) to prioritize remedies.  
- Question: provide quantitative evidence for the claim that sharp minimizers rise steeply only along a small subspace (dimensionality of that subspace?).  
- Question: analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to perturbation parameter ε (e.g., test ε from 1e-4 to 1e-2).  
- Question: clarify how large-batch convergence nearer the initialization relates to basins of attraction and implications for initialization strategies.
