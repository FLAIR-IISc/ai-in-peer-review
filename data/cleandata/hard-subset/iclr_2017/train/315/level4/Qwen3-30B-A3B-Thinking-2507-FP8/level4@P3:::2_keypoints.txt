- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch, showing large-batch converges to sharp minima while small-batch finds flat minima that generalize better.  
- Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric and shows its correlation with generalization across six network architectures.  
- Demonstrates extensive numerical evidence (parametric plots, 1–2 orders of magnitude difference in sharpness) supporting the sharpness–generalization link.  
- Warm-starting experiments indicate large-batch methods can generalize well if initialized to explore and reach flat minima.  
- Methodology is sound: multiple trials, consistent architectures, and thorough optimization strategy exploration.  
- Paper is well-situated in existing literature on sharp vs. flat minima and loss landscapes, and addresses alternative explanations like overfitting.  
- Recommends acceptance due to significant empirical contribution and thorough execution.  
- Suggests adding quantitative analysis of gradient-noise levels and their relation to the sharpness metric across batch sizes.  
- Recommends deeper, systematic study of how the sharpness metric predicts generalization across datasets and architectures.  
- Requests stronger support for the claim that flat minima require lower precision (examples or references).  
- Asks authors to provide evidence that the sharpness metric predicts generalization beyond the presented experiments.  
- Requests comparison of computational cost and scaling of warm-starting versus using small-batch training throughout.  
- Asks for analysis of why data augmentation and conservative training only partially reduce sharpness and whether modified approaches could be more effective.
