- Timely, well-motivated analysis of large-batch gradient descent limitations in deep learning.  
- Shows large batches often cause poorer generalization and suboptimal convergence.  
- Provides theoretical and empirical explanation rooted in optimization dynamics.  
- Key insight: large batches reduce gradient noise needed to escape sharp minima.  
- Precise, controlled experiments and refined loss-landscape analysis support claims.  
- Clear connection between SGD behavior and batch size; validated on synthetic and real benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ResNet-32).  
- Methodology is sound with careful hyperparameter sensitivity checks (learning rate schedules, weight decay).  
- Ablations on batch size vs learning rate show adaptive learning rates can partially mitigate large-batch drawbacks.  
- Robustness tested by including momentum and data augmentation in experiments.  
- Minor weakness: limited exploration of batch-size effects across different model architectures (e.g., transformers vs CNNs).  
- Minor weakness: insufficient quantitative analysis of computational cost trade-offs (wall-clock time vs generalization).  
- Missing brief comparison to recent adaptive batch-size methods for further context.  
- Writing and figures are clear; paper situates contributions well within existing literature.  
- Practical takeaway: recommend pairing larger batches with higher learning rates (linear scaling rule).  
- Overall assessment: technically rigorous, empirically grounded, relevant to scalable training; recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
