- Empirical claim: Large-batch training converges to sharp minimizers while small-batch training finds flatter minimizers, explaining the generalization gap.  
- Decision: Accept — strong empirical contribution with sound, well-structured evidence.  
- Evidence: Uses parametric plots, a novel sharpness metric, and systematic comparisons across multiple architectures showing ~1–2 orders-of-magnitude sharpness difference.  
- Practicality: The sharpness metric is computationally feasible for large networks and consistently correlates with generalization performance.  
- Literature and controls: Well-motivated by prior work and experiments rule out alternative hypotheses (overfitting, saddle points); scale-invariance issue addressed.  
- Clarification request: Explain the mechanism by which small-batch noise enables escape from sharp minima, relating noise magnitude to curvature.  
- Clarification request: State whether the reported "threshold batch size" (e.g., 500 for C1) is architecture-dependent or generalizable.  
- Suggestion: Add a brief discussion on implications for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., batch-size schedules) for practitioners.  
- Question for authors: How many small-batch warm-start epochs are minimally required for LB success, and does this threshold scale with network depth/width?  
- Question for authors: How sensitive are results to the box-constraint parameter in the sharpness metric (e.g., 1e-3 vs 5e-4), and is there a theoretical basis for its selection?
