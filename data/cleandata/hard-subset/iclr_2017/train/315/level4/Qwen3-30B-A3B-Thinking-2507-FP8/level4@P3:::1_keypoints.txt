- Paper claims large-batch training converges to sharp minimizers causing worse generalization, supported by parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric.  
- Decision: Reject — interesting observation but insufficient evidence and weak metric grounding.  
- Main critique: evidence is correlational; causality between sharpness and generalization is not established.  
- Sharpness metric (Eq.4) is ad-hoc with insufficient theoretical justification and unclear connection to classical sharp minima.  
- Authors do not show the metric reliably reflects Hessian largest eigenvalues, especially in high-dimensional DL settings.  
- Experimental design concern: using ADAM for both small and large batches may introduce optimizer-specific effects.  
- Results may be specific to the chosen architectures/training regimes; overfitting and other factors are not fully ruled out.  
- Suggestion: provide clearer theoretical foundation and justification for the chosen sharpness metric.  
- Suggestion: run experiments with multiple optimizers for large-batch training to rule out optimizer artifacts.  
- Suggestion: analyze the relationship between the metric and Hessian eigenvalues or validate on simpler problems where Hessians are computable.  
- Suggestion: address alternative explanations such as optimization path, initialization, or architecture dependence.  
- Questions for authors: justify Eq.4’s form, explain how local sensitivity avoids spurious correlation with generalization, and test the metric on simpler problems to verify it captures sharp minima.
