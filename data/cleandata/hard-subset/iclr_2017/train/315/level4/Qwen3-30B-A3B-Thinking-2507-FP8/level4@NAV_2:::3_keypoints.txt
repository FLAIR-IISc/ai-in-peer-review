Paper shows large-batch training converges to sharp minimizers (many large positive Hessian eigenvalues) that generalize poorly while small-batch training finds flat minimizers (many small eigenvalues) that generalize better, supported by compelling numerical evidence and linked to gradient-noise effects; explores mitigation strategies.

2. Reviewer recommends acceptance, citing the paper's importance and thorough, robust numerical evidence across multiple architectures and datasets.

3. Paper is well-situated in prior work on sharp vs. flat minima, carefully rules out alternatives (e.g., overfitting), presents comprehensive experiments, and highlights practical implications for scaling and speeding up training.

4. Paper is well-written and technically sound; reviewer suggests exploring controlled gradient noise in large-batch regimes to bridge the gap and adding a deeper theoretical link between the sharpness metric and Hessian eigenvalues.

5. Reviewer requests clarification on how rescaled Gaussian noise would affect large-batch training dynamics and recommended parameters, and asks whether the benefits of flat minimizers are architecture-dependent and how that affects generalizability.
