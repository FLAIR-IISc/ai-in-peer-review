- Empirical finding: Large-batch SGD converges to sharp minimizers while small-batch SGD finds flatter minima, correlating with generalization differences across architectures.  
- Evidence: Parametric plots and a novel sharpness metric show consistent 1–2 order-of-magnitude sharpness differences between batch regimes.  
- Warm-start result: Small-batch initialization lets large-batch training reach flatter regions, suggesting exploration via noise matters.  
- Decision: Accept with major revisions — needs stronger theoretical grounding and experimental refinement on causality.  
- Strength: Empirically rigorous experiments across multiple architectures, datasets, and the use of random manifolds and warm-starting bolster the findings.  
- Limitation: No causal proof that sharpness causes poor generalization; the sharpness metric is heuristic and confounded by methods that improve generalization without reducing sharpness.  
- Overstated noise claim: Paper attributes differences mainly to gradient noise but warm-start results imply noise is sufficient but not necessary, requiring nuance.  
- Metric theory gap: The sharpness metric lacks explicit connection to the Hessian spectrum and needs validation (e.g., correlation with largest eigenvalue).  
- Suggested experiment: Perform controlled Gaussian-noise injection into large-batch gradients to test whether noise causally drives convergence to flat minima.  
- Fixes clarification: Quantify how much generalization improves relative to sharpness change and explain why data augmentation/conservative training leave minima sharp.  
- Theory alignment: More explicit discussion needed on how results relate to or challenge prior theories (e.g., Choromanska et al., Lee et al.).  
- Key question 1: How does Equation 4 quantitatively relate to the largest eigenvalue of ∇²f(x), and can this be shown on small convex problems?  
- Key question 2: Why do data augmentation and conservative training improve generalization without eliminating sharpness—does this undermine sharpness as the primary cause?  
- Key question 3: In warm-starting, at which epoch does large-batch sharpness first decrease, and does this match the end of small-batch exploration?  
- Overall: With the requested theoretical links and causal experiments, the paper could become a definitive, influential contribution.
