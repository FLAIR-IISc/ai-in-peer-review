

This paper presents an empirical investigation into the relationship between stochastic gradient descent (SGD) batch size, the flatness of minima, and generalization performance in deep networks. Specifically, it demonstrates that smaller batch sizes lead to convergence at flatter minima, and that these flatter minima exhibit superior generalization ability.  

While the paper offers limited theoretical novelty, its empirical contributions are highly valuable for advancing understanding of generalization in deep learning. The findings provide crucial insights into the mechanisms underlying generalization, potentially guiding both theoretical developments and practical algorithm design.  

The significance lies in the dual implications: theoretically, the results inform which assumptions are reasonable for real-world scenarios when constructing new theories; practically, they suggest heuristic approaches for improving generalization through strategic manipulation of mini-batch sizes.  

A prior concern regarding the authors' claim of scale invariance in their sharpness criterion has been appropriately addressed in the revision, as the claim was removed.
