

1. Summary: The paper provides a compelling empirical investigation into the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training in deep learning. It clearly establishes that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity in the loss landscape), while small-batch methods consistently find flatter minimizers. The authors support this claim through rigorous numerical evidence—including parametric plots, a novel sharpness metric, and systematic comparisons across multiple network architectures—demonstrating that sharpness correlates strongly with generalization performance. The work significantly advances our understanding of batch size effects in deep learning optimization.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution by rigorously linking sharp minimizers to the generalization gap, and the evidence presented is scientifically sound and well-structured.

3. Supporting arguments:  
   - The paper directly addresses the core question of *why* large-batch methods generalize poorly (sharp minimizers), providing concrete numerical evidence via the sharpness metric (Equation 4) across diverse architectures (Tables 3–4, Figure 5). The metric’s computational feasibility for large networks is well-justified, and results consistently show a 1–2 order-of-magnitude sharpness difference between small- and large-batch regimes.  
   - The work is well-motivated within the literature (e.g., building on Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) and thoroughly addresses key alternative hypotheses (e.g., overfitting, saddle points) through controlled experiments (Figure 2, Section 2.2). The resolution of the scale-invariance claim (removed in revision) further strengthens methodological rigor.

4. Additional feedback:  
   - Clarify the *mechanism* by which small-batch noise enables escaping sharp minima (beyond "exploratory properties"). A brief discussion of the noise magnitude relative to sharpness curvature would deepen the interpretation.  
   - In Section 3, explicitly state whether the "threshold batch size" (e.g., 500 for C1) is architecture-dependent or a generalizable phenomenon.  
   - Consider adding a short paragraph in the conclusion about the implications for hyperparameter tuning (e.g., batch size schedules) to guide practitioners.

5. Questions for authors:  
   - Your warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) suggest that SB exploration is necessary for LB success. Could you elaborate on the *minimum* number of SB epochs required to achieve this, and whether this threshold scales with network depth/width?  
   - The sharpness metric (Equation 4) uses a box constraint (Equation 3) that scales with the solution. How sensitive are the results to the choice of parameter (e.g., 10⁻³ vs. 5×10⁻⁴), and is there a theoretical basis for this selection?
