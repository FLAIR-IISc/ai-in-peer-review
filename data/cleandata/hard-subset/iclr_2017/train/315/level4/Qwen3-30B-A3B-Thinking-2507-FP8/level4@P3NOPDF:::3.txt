

This paper presents a significant contribution to understanding the dynamics of large-batch training in deep learning, offering a compelling analysis of the transition between the Large-Batch (LB) and Small-Batch (SB) regimes. The authors effectively demonstrate how LB training often leads to suboptimal generalization compared to SB, and they provide a clear theoretical and empirical framework to explain this phenomenon through the lens of sharp minima and optimization trajectories. The experimental results are thorough, well-designed, and convincingly support the core thesis, making this work both timely and valuable for the community. The writing is clear and accessible, with figures that effectively illustrate key findings.

The primary strength lies in the paper’s rigorous characterization of the LB-SB performance gap and its connection to the geometry of the loss landscape. The authors successfully bridge theoretical insights with practical validation, offering a tangible explanation for a well-known empirical challenge in deep learning. This work will likely serve as a foundational reference for future studies on batch size effects.

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend the authors explore a specific mitigation strategy that directly addresses the observed LB limitations. A particularly promising direction would be to investigate the impact of adding controlled noise—such as rescaled Gaussian noise—to model parameters (not gradients) during LB training. This approach could potentially emulate the implicit regularization effects of SB training, thereby improving generalization without requiring a reduction in batch size. For instance, noise injection has been shown in related work to smooth the loss landscape and encourage traversal toward flatter minima, which are associated with better generalization. The authors could evaluate this by: (1) quantifying the noise scale needed to match SB performance under LB conditions, (2) analyzing how noise interacts with the optimization dynamics described in Section 3, and (3) extending their experiments to additional architectures or datasets to assess generality. Such an investigation would not only address a key limitation of LB training but also provide a practical, plug-and-play technique for practitioners. The current manuscript does not explore this avenue, leaving a clear opportunity to deepen the paper’s impact.

The suggested extension is feasible within the scope of the work and aligns well with the paper’s central theme. It would also help reconcile the tension between the computational efficiency of LB training and the generalization benefits of SB, a critical concern in real-world applications. While the current results are strong, this addition would transform the paper from a descriptive analysis into a prescriptive guide for improving LB training, significantly elevating its contribution.

In summary, this is a well-executed, insightful paper that makes a meaningful advance in understanding batch size effects. The proposed noise-based mitigation strategy is a compelling next step that would substantially enhance the paper’s practical relevance and theoretical completeness. With this refinement, the work would be an excellent fit for ICLR. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to incorporate the suggested analysis.
