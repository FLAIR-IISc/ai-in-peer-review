

1. The paper makes a significant contribution to the understanding of generalization behavior in deep learning training, specifically addressing the well-known phenomenon of generalization degradation in large-batch training regimes. The authors present compelling numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by numerous large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), which generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with numerous small eigenvalues), which generalize better. The paper provides a clear explanation for this behavior, linking it to the inherent noise in gradient estimation for small-batch methods, and explores several practical strategies to potentially mitigate this generalization gap.

2. I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning with well-motivated research questions, and (2) the numerical evidence presented is thorough and robust across multiple network architectures and datasets, strongly supporting the authors' claims.

3. The paper is well-placed within the existing literature, building upon previous work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and generalization in deep learning. The authors provide a clear explanation of the phenomenon, carefully rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap (such as overfitting), and present a comprehensive set of experiments across multiple network configurations. The paper's findings have important practical implications for scaling deep learning training to larger distributed systems, as the authors note that eliminating the generalization gap for large-batch methods could potentially reduce training time by orders of magnitude.

4. The paper is well-written and technically sound, but I have a few suggestions to further strengthen it: The authors might consider exploring the addition of controlled noise to the gradients in the large-batch regime, which could potentially bridge the gap between large-batch and small-batch performance by introducing the beneficial exploratory properties of small-batch methods. This approach could provide an elegant solution to the generalization problem while maintaining the parallelization benefits of large-batch training. Additionally, a more detailed theoretical discussion of the relationship between the proposed sharpness metric and the Hessian eigenvalues would further ground the methodology.

5. To help clarify my understanding and provide additional evidence for my assessment, I would appreciate it if the authors could address the following: How do you expect the addition of rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during large-batch training to interact with the current training dynamics, and what specific parameters would you recommend for this approach? Could you provide more insight into whether the beneficial properties of flat minimizers are architecture-dependent, and if so, how this might affect the generalizability of your findings across different network types?
