

The paper investigates the relationship between batch size in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) and model generalization in deep learning, presenting empirical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers with poorer generalization, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers with better generalization. The authors introduce a practical sharpness metric to quantify this phenomenon and explore several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap in large-batch training. This work provides valuable empirical insights into a well-known but poorly understood practical observation in deep learning.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The primary reason is that it provides compelling empirical evidence addressing a significant practical question in deep learning. The second reason is that the paper makes a clear, actionable contribution to understanding the generalization behavior of different batch training regimes.

The paper's main contribution is its systematic empirical investigation of the relationship between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization performance. The authors carefully establish that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting or early-stopping issues, but rather to the convergence to sharp minimizers in large-batch training. Their proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) provides a computationally feasible way to quantify this phenomenon, which is particularly valuable given the impracticality of computing Hessian eigenvalues for large deep networks. The parametric plots and sharpness measurements across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, and fully-connected networks) consistently demonstrate that large-batch methods converge to significantly sharper minimizers than small-batch methods, with a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness metrics. The authors' exploration of warm-starting techniques (Section 3) provides particularly insightful evidence that small-batch methods first explore and find flat minimizers before large-batch methods can converge to them, supporting their central thesis.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, building on the established understanding that flat minima generalize better (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) while addressing a practical gap in our understanding of why large-batch training fails to generalize well despite matching training accuracy. The authors' exploration of potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) is particularly valuable for practitioners, even if these approaches do not fully resolve the generalization gap.

The experimental design is thorough and well-justified. The authors use multiple network architectures, multiple random initializations, and both full-space and random manifold sharpness measurements to ensure robustness. The baseline performance (Table 2) clearly demonstrates the generalization gap across all networks, and the parametric plots (Figures 3, 7) provide compelling visual evidence of the sharpness difference. The paper also thoughtfully addresses the distinction between generalization gap and overfitting (Section 2.2), which is an important clarification for the field.

I have two minor suggestions to strengthen the paper further. First, the authors could more explicitly connect their sharpness metric to the theoretical literature on flat vs. sharp minima, particularly in the context of Bayesian learning and minimum description length theory. Second, while the warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are insightful, the paper could more clearly articulate the practical implications of these findings for training protocols (e.g., "start with small batches for exploration, then transition to large batches for efficiency").

I would appreciate clarification on two points to better assess the generality of the findings. First, the paper mentions that "results on other networks and using other initialization strategies, activation functions, and data sets showed similar behavior" but doesn't provide specific examples. Could the authors share one additional example (e.g., a different activation function or dataset) where this pattern holds? Second, the paper states that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone," but doesn't elaborate on what this means for the geometry of the loss landscape. Could the authors clarify whether the sharp directions are correlated or uncorrelated?

The paper's main limitation is that it primarily presents empirical evidence without providing a theoretical proof of the relationship between batch size and minimizer sharpness. However, the authors appropriately acknowledge this as a direction for future work in Section 4, and the empirical evidence is sufficiently compelling to stand on its own. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding a practical phenomenon in deep learning that has significant implications for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of training algorithms. The insights provided could guide future work in designing training protocols that effectively leverage large-batch training without sacrificing generalization performance.
