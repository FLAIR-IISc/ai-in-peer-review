

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**1. Summary of Contributions**  
This paper investigates the generalization performance gap between large-batch and small-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) methods in deep learning. The authors present compelling numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function—characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian—which exhibit poorer generalization, while small-batch methods converge to flatter minimizers with better generalization properties. The work provides detailed empirical analysis across multiple network architectures and demonstrates that this behavior is linked to the inherent noise in small-batch gradient estimation. The paper also explores several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap in large-batch training.

**2. Decision**  
I recommend rejection. The primary reason is the lack of sufficient novelty: the core claim that flat minima generalize better than sharp minima has been previously established in the literature (e.g., Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016), and this paper primarily provides additional empirical evidence rather than new theoretical insights or fundamentally novel observations.

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
While the empirical results are well-executed and consistent with prior work, the paper does not advance the field significantly beyond existing knowledge. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is presented as novel, but it is essentially a practical approximation of the Hessian's largest eigenvalue, which has been discussed in the literature. The authors acknowledge this in their discussion but fail to clearly articulate how their metric improves upon or differs from existing approaches.

The paper's claim that "large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers" is not new. For instance, Chaudhari et al. (2016) introduced "Entropy-SGD" specifically to avoid sharp minima, and their work predates this paper. The authors' experiments, while thorough, largely confirm rather than extend this established understanding. The exploration of strategies to mitigate the generalization gap (Section E) also lacks novelty—data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training have been widely studied and shown to have limited effectiveness for this specific problem.

Furthermore, the paper does not adequately address the question of whether the observed generalization gap is due to the batch size itself or other confounding factors (e.g., learning rate schedules, optimizer choices). The authors use ADAM for both regimes, which complicates the interpretation since ADAM's behavior may differ between small and large batches.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**  
To strengthen this work for future submission, the authors should:  
- Clearly articulate the novel contribution relative to prior work (e.g., Chaudhari et al., 2016; Keskar et al., 2016). If the novelty is limited to specific experimental configurations or architectures, this should be explicitly stated.  
- Conduct ablation studies to isolate the effect of batch size from other hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, optimizer).  
- Provide a more rigorous theoretical discussion of why sharp minima generalize poorly, rather than relying solely on empirical evidence.  
- Clarify the relationship between their sharpness metric and the Hessian spectrum, including a discussion of its limitations for large-scale deep learning.

**5. Questions for Authors**  
To better assess the paper's claims, I request clarification on the following points:  
1. The paper states that "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers," but does this hold when using the same learning rate schedule for both batch sizes? If not, how do the authors account for the interaction between batch size and learning rate?  
2. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is defined with respect to a neighborhood around the minimizer. How do the authors ensure that this neighborhood is sufficiently large to capture the true curvature of the loss surface, particularly in high-dimensional spaces where sharpness may vary significantly across subspaces?  
3. The paper claims that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting," but the experiments do not include validation curves showing early stopping behavior. Could the gap be partially explained by differences in the training dynamics that would be mitigated by proper early stopping?  
4. In Section 3, the authors observe that warm-starting large-batch training with small-batch iterates improves generalization. How does this strategy compare to simply using a small batch size throughout training in terms of computational cost and convergence speed?  

This work makes a valuable contribution to empirical understanding of generalization in deep learning, but it falls short of providing the novel theoretical or methodological insights required for acceptance at ICLR. With significant revisions addressing the above points, this paper could become a stronger contribution to the field.
