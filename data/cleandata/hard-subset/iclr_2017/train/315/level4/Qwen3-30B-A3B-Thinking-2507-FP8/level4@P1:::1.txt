

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**1. Summary of Contributions**  
This paper makes a valuable contribution by addressing an important empirical observation in deep learning: large-batch training methods often yield models with worse generalization performance than small-batch methods, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that this generalization gap stems from large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with smaller eigenvalues), which are known to generalize better. The paper provides a clear theoretical motivation for this phenomenon, connects it to established concepts in optimization and learning theory, and offers practical experiments across multiple network architectures to support their claims. The work is well-organized and provides a useful framework for understanding and addressing this persistent challenge in deep learning.

**2. Decision**  
Reject. The paper lacks sufficient theoretical grounding to support its core claim about sharp minimizers causing generalization degradation, and the numerical evidence, while suggestive, does not conclusively establish causality.

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
The primary concern is that the paper's central claim—that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers *because* of the batch size, and that this sharpness *causes* the generalization gap—remains unproven. The authors present strong correlations between batch size, sharpness, and generalization, but fail to establish a causal mechanism. For instance, they do not rule out the possibility that sharp minimizers might be a *consequence* rather than a *cause* of poor generalization, or that batch size might influence generalization through mechanisms unrelated to sharpness (e.g., optimization path, learning rate schedules, or regularization effects). The sharpness metric they propose (Equation 4) is computationally feasible but lacks rigorous theoretical justification—it is not clear how it relates to the actual Hessian or generalization error in a way that would allow causal inference.

Additionally, the paper's experimental design has limitations. The sharpness metric (Table 3, 4) is computed using a limited neighborhood around the minimizer (Equation 3), which may not adequately capture the true curvature of the loss surface. The authors acknowledge this by noting their metric "approximates the Ritz value of ∇²f(x) projected onto the column-space of A," but they do not demonstrate that this approximation reliably correlates with generalization performance across diverse architectures or datasets. Furthermore, the experiments with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization (Appendix E) show marginal improvements but do not address the core causal question—these methods may improve generalization without resolving the sharpness issue, suggesting sharpness might not be the root cause.

**4. Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

- Provide a more rigorous theoretical argument for how sharpness *causes* generalization degradation. They could explore connections to existing generalization bounds (e.g., PAC-Bayes or uniform convergence) that explicitly incorporate curvature.
- Conduct ablation studies to isolate the effect of batch size from other confounding factors (e.g., learning rate, momentum, or weight decay), which may explain the observed generalization gap.
- Validate their sharpness metric against established measures of curvature (e.g., by comparing it to exact Hessian eigenvalues on smaller models or using randomized algorithms for Hessian approximation).
- Include experiments where sharpness is artificially manipulated (e.g., via regularization) to observe its direct impact on generalization, rather than merely correlating it with batch size.

**Key Questions for Authors**  
1. How do you rule out the possibility that sharp minimizers are a *symptom* rather than a *cause* of poor generalization? For example, could models with poor generalization inherently exhibit sharp minima due to other factors (e.g., insufficient capacity or overfitting)?
2. In the warm-starting experiments (Figure 5), the large-batch method achieves good generalization only after being initialized with small-batch iterates. Does this suggest that the batch size itself isn't the primary issue, but rather the *initialization* of the optimizer? If so, how does this align with the paper's claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers by default?
3. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) relies on a local neighborhood around the minimizer. How do you ensure that this metric captures the global generalization behavior of the model, rather than just local curvature?
