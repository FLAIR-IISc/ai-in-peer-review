- Paper addresses why large-batch training often generalizes worse than small-batch, attributing it to convergence to "sharp" minimizers versus "flat" ones.
- Provides numerical evidence across architectures and a theoretical motivation linking sharpness (large Hessian eigenvalues) to poorer generalization.
- Well-organized manuscript that offers a useful empirical framework for the large-batch vs small-batch generalization gap.
- Decision: Reject — main claim lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and causality is not established.
- Primary concern: correlations shown (batch size ↔ sharpness ↔ generalization) do not prove that sharpness causes generalization degradation.
- Alternative explanations (sharpness as consequence, optimizer path, learning rate schedules, regularization) are not ruled out.
- The proposed sharpness metric (Eq. 4) is computationally feasible but lacks rigorous justification and clear relation to the true Hessian or generalization error.
- Sharpness estimates use a limited local neighborhood (Eq. 3), which may not capture true curvature or global generalization behavior.
- Appendix experiments (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) show only marginal gains and do not resolve the causal question.
- Suggested improvements: stronger theoretical links to generalization bounds incorporating curvature (e.g., PAC-Bayes or uniform convergence).
- Suggested experiments: ablations to isolate batch-size effects from learning rate/momentum/weight decay confounders.
- Validate the sharpness metric by comparing to exact Hessian eigenvalues on small models or using randomized Hessian approximations.
- Perform experiments that directly manipulate sharpness (via regularization) to observe causal impact on generalization.
- Key question 1: How do you rule out sharpness being a symptom rather than a cause of poor generalization?
- Key question 2: Do warm-start results imply initialization, not batch size, is the primary factor in achieving good generalization?
- Key question 3: How do you ensure the local sharpness metric reflects global generalization behavior rather than only local curvature?
