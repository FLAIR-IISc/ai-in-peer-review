- Paper investigates the large-batch generalization gap and shows large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers, explaining poorer generalization.  
- Introduces a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric for large networks.  
- Validated results across multiple architectures (including AlexNet and VGG), datasets, and training approaches.  
- Recommends acceptance due to significance of the problem and strong empirical support.  
- Reason 1 for acceptance: clearly identifies and rigorously investigates a major practical limitation of large-batch training.  
- Reason 2 for acceptance: employs a well-designed experimental framework with complementary metrics (parametric plots, sharpness metric, warm-starting) that consistently support the central claim.  
- Supporting evidence: observes 1–2 orders of magnitude difference in sharpness metric between small- and large-batch methods across six network configurations.  
- Visual evidence: parametric plots (Fig. 3) and warm-starting experiments (Fig. 5) are particularly convincing.  
- Authors acknowledge limitations of the sharpness metric and explore potential remedies in Appendix E, though remedies don’t fully eliminate sharp minimizers.  
- Suggested improvement: provide more detailed analysis of how the dimensionality of sharp subspaces relates to network architecture and size.  
- Suggested improvement: include a more thorough discussion of how the sharpness metric correlates with the actual Hessian spectrum for large networks.  
- Suggested improvement: explore the impact of different initialization strategies on the observed generalization gap.  
- Clarifying questions: elaborate on the dimensionality of sharp subspaces and relation to architecture/parameter count; specify what modifications might fully address sharp minimizers beyond current remedies; explain how findings interact with recent theoretical work on loss landscapes (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015).
