

This paper makes a valuable contribution by clearly articulating why large-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) often fails to achieve optimal convergence and generalization performance, a critical issue in deep learning optimization. The authors effectively demonstrate that increasing batch size reduces the inherent noise in gradient estimates, which is empirically linked to poorer generalization in practice. Their experimental analysis across multiple architectures and datasets—particularly the compelling comparison of validation accuracy decay with batch size—provides strong evidence for this phenomenon. The discussion of how large batches shift the optimization trajectory toward sharp minima (compared to the flatter minima favored by smaller batches) is especially well-supported and aligns with recent theoretical work on the role of noise in generalization.

However, the paper would benefit from a more nuanced discussion of the *mechanism* through which batch size affects noise. While the authors correctly identify reduced noise as a key factor, they do not sufficiently address *how* this noise reduction interacts with the loss landscape's geometry. For instance, the analysis would be strengthened by explicitly connecting the batch size effect to the curvature of the loss surface near minima (e.g., via Hessian analysis or sharpness metrics), rather than solely relying on empirical observations. The current treatment risks implying that noise reduction is the *sole* cause, whereas evidence suggests batch size also influences the dynamics of escape from local minima or saddle points. A brief theoretical discussion or reference to related work (e.g., by Zhang et al. on noise and generalization) would significantly deepen the argument.

Additionally, the paper’s recommendation to use smaller batches for better generalization is well-motivated but could be more precise. The authors note that "smaller batches are preferable" without acknowledging practical constraints like training speed or hardware limitations. It would be useful to quantify the trade-off: for example, how much accuracy gain is achieved by reducing batch size from 1024 to 128, and at what computational cost. A single sentence on this would make the practical implications clearer for readers.

The writing is generally clear, but a few phrasing choices slightly hinder fluency. For instance, "does not work too well" (in the original draft) is non-idiomatic; the revised text uses "fails to achieve optimal convergence and generalization," which is both precise and natural. The figures are well-designed and effectively support the main claims, particularly Figure 3 showing the validation accuracy vs. batch size curve.

Overall, this is a strong, timely paper that addresses a significant gap in understanding large-batch training. The core argument is sound, the evidence is robust, and the contribution is directly relevant to the deep learning community. With minor refinements to the theoretical discussion and practical considerations, it would be a highly impactful addition to the literature. I recommend acceptance.
