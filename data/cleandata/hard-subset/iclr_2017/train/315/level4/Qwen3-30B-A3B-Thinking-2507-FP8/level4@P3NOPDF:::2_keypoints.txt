- Paper tests two hypotheses: small-batch SGD converges to flatter minima, and flatter minima generalize better.  
- Theoretical novelty is modest, but the empirical study is valuable and timely.  
- Rigorous empirical validation across multiple architectures and datasets shows reduced batch sizes yield flatter minima by the authors' sharpness metric.  
- Flatter minima found with smaller batches correlate strongly with improved test accuracy.  
- Experimental design is robust, statistically careful, and consistent across diverse settings.  
- Authors addressed prior concern by removing the claim of scale-invariance for their sharpness metric.  
- Practical takeaway: reducing batch size is a simple heuristic to improve generalization without architectural changes.  
- The results provide empirical grounding for theory, urging models to account for realistic (small-batch) optimization dynamics.  
- Paper deliberately focuses on empirical evidence and avoids speculative mechanism claims.  
- Presentation is clear and reproducible, enhancing community adoption.  
- Minor limitation: paper lacks deeper analysis of why smaller batches produce flatter minima.  
- Suggestion: explicitly relate the sharpness metric to established measures (e.g., Hessian-based curvature).  
- Overall judgment: well-executed, relevant, and worthy of publication; recommendation: Accept.
