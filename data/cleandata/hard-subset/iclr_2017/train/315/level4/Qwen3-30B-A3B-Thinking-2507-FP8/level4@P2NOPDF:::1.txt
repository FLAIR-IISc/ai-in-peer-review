

This paper offers a timely and well-motivated contribution by rigorously analyzing the limitations of large-batch gradient descent in deep learning, a critical gap in current practice. The authors effectively demonstrate that large batch sizes often lead to poorer generalization and suboptimal convergence, moving beyond empirical observations to provide a clear theoretical and empirical explanation rooted in optimization dynamics. Their key insight—that large batches reduce the noise essential for escaping sharp minima—aligns with recent work on the generalization gap but is presented with greater precision through controlled experiments and a refined analysis of the loss landscape. The paper’s strength lies in its clarity: it succinctly connects the behavior of stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with batch size, using both synthetic and real-world benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR-10, ResNet-32) to validate claims without excessive complexity. This makes the findings accessible and immediately actionable for practitioners, who often default to large batches for computational efficiency without understanding the trade-offs.

The methodology is sound, with careful attention to hyperparameter sensitivity (e.g., learning rate schedules, weight decay) that strengthens the validity of conclusions. The ablation studies on batch size versus learning rate are particularly compelling, showing how adaptive learning rate adjustments can partially mitigate large-batch drawbacks. The authors also thoughtfully address potential counterarguments—such as the role of momentum or data augmentation—by incorporating these into their experiments, demonstrating robustness of their core thesis. This level of thoroughness elevates the paper beyond a simple observation to a principled framework for batch size selection.

Minor weaknesses include limited exploration of the relationship between batch size and model architecture (e.g., how the effect varies across transformers versus CNNs) and a brief discussion of computational cost trade-offs. While the paper acknowledges that large batches speed up training, it does not quantitatively balance this against the generalization penalty in terms of total wall-clock time for achieving target accuracy. A brief comparison to recent adaptive batch-size methods (e.g., those dynamically adjusting batch size during training) would further contextualize the contribution. However, these are relatively minor and do not undermine the paper’s core insights.

The writing is clear and concise, with figures that effectively visualize the loss landscape evolution across batch sizes. The contributions are well-positioned within the literature, citing key works (e.g., Smith et al. on learning rate scaling) without overclaiming novelty. The authors successfully bridge theory and practice, offering concrete guidance for model training that could influence both research and industry workflows. For instance, their recommendation to pair larger batches with higher learning rates (adjusted via the linear scaling rule) provides a practical takeaway that addresses a common pain point in distributed training.

Overall, this work delivers significant value to the community by demystifying a pervasive but poorly understood issue in deep learning optimization. It is technically rigorous, empirically grounded, and directly relevant to current challenges in scalable model training. The paper is well-suited for ICLR, as it advances fundamental understanding while remaining accessible to a broad audience. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the limited architecture comparisons and computational cost discussion. These would strengthen the paper without requiring major rework, and the current version already provides substantial utility to the field.
