

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary of Contribution:**  
This paper addresses a fundamental question in deep learning practice: why large-batch training methods suffer from worse generalization performance compared to small-batch methods. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with small eigenvalues), and that flat minimizers generalize better. They introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify this phenomenon and demonstrate its correlation with generalization performance across six diverse network architectures. The paper also explores several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap for large-batch methods, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

**Decision:**  
I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work makes a significant contribution to understanding a well-documented empirical phenomenon in deep learning, and the empirical evidence is thorough, well-executed, and well-motivated.

**Key Reasons for Acceptance:**  
1. The paper effectively addresses an important practical question that has been observed by practitioners but not systematically explained. The authors provide extensive numerical evidence across multiple network architectures and datasets, demonstrating a clear correlation between sharp minima and poor generalization in the large-batch regime.

2. The paper is well-placed in the literature, building upon existing theoretical work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Rissanen, 1983; MacKay, 1992) and recent theoretical results on loss landscapes (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016). The authors thoughtfully address alternative explanations for the generalization gap, such as overfitting, and provide evidence against them.

**Supporting Arguments:**  
The paper's main contribution is its empirical demonstration of the relationship between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization performance. The parametric plots (Figure 3) clearly show that large-batch solutions are significantly sharper than small-batch solutions along the line connecting them. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) provides a quantitative measure of this difference, with results showing a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness values between small and large batch methods. The authors also provide evidence that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting by showing that training and testing accuracy curves do not exhibit the typical peak-and-decay pattern associated with overfitting.

The paper's methodology is sound, with careful attention to experimental design: multiple trials from different starting points, consistent network architectures, and thorough exploration of various optimization strategies. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) provides particularly insightful evidence that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization if properly initialized, supporting the authors' hypothesis about the role of exploration in finding flat minima.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**  
1. While the paper discusses the noise in gradient estimation as the mechanism by which small-batch methods escape sharp minima, a more quantitative analysis of this noise and its relationship to the sharpness metric would strengthen the argument. For example, it would be valuable to see how the noise level correlates with the sharpness metric across different batch sizes.

2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed exploration of the relationship between the sharpness metric and generalization performance. While the correlation is demonstrated, a more systematic analysis of how the metric predicts generalization across different datasets and network architectures would provide deeper insights.

3. The authors claim that flat minimizers can be specified with lower precision than sharp minimizers, which is why they generalize better. This claim would be more convincing with concrete examples or additional references to support the precision argument.

**Questions for Authors:**  
1. The paper states that the generalization gap is due to convergence to sharp minimizers. Can the authors provide evidence that their sharpness metric is predictive of generalization performance across different datasets and network architectures beyond those presented in the paper?

2. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) shows that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when properly initialized. What is the computational cost of this warm-starting approach compared to simply using small-batch methods throughout training, and how does it scale with network size?

3. The paper mentions that conservative training and data augmentation only partially help reduce the generalization gap. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why these methods fail to completely eliminate the sharpness of the minima, and whether there are modifications to these approaches that could be more effective?
