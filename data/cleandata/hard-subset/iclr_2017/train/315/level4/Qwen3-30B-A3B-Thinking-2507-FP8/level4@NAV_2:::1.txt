

This paper makes a valuable contribution by investigating the well-known generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning models. The authors provide compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods consistently converge to sharp minimizers of the loss landscape (characterized by numerous large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with many small eigenvalues), explaining the observed degradation in generalization performance. The authors present a novel computational metric for quantifying minimizer sharpness that is feasible for large networks, and they validate their findings across multiple network architectures including AlexNet and VGGNet configurations, with consistent results across different data sets and training approaches.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The work addresses a significant practical problem in deep learning training with strong empirical support, and the findings align with established theoretical understanding of the relationship between minimizer sharpness and generalization.

The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) The paper clearly identifies and rigorously investigates a major practical limitation of large-batch training methods that has been observed in practice but not thoroughly explained, and (2) The authors provide a well-designed experimental framework with multiple complementary metrics (parametric plots, sharpness metric, warm-starting experiments) that consistently support their central claim about the relationship between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization performance.

The supporting evidence is robust and comprehensive. The paper demonstrates a 1-2 order of magnitude difference in sharpness metric values between small-batch and large-batch methods across six different network configurations. The parametric plots in Figure 3 and the warm-starting experiments in Figure 5 provide particularly convincing visual evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharper regions of the loss landscape. The authors also appropriately acknowledge the limitations of their proposed sharpness metric and explore several potential remedies in Appendix E, even if these remedies do not fully resolve the issue.

For further improvement, the authors might consider: (1) Providing more detailed analysis of how the dimensionality of the sharp subspaces relates to network architecture and size, as mentioned in Section 2.2.2; (2) Including a more thorough discussion of how their sharpness metric correlates with the actual Hessian spectrum, particularly for larger networks where exact computation is infeasible; (3) Exploring the impact of different initialization strategies on the observed generalization gap, which could provide additional insights into the problem.

The following questions would help clarify and strengthen the paper: (1) Could you elaborate on the dimensionality of the sharp subspaces (e.g., the 5% mentioned in Section 2.2.2) and how this relates to the network architecture and number of parameters? (2) Given that the proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) reduce the generalization gap but don't eliminate sharp minimizers, what specific modifications might be necessary to fully address this issue? (3) How do your findings on the relationship between batch size and minimizer sharpness interact with recent theoretical work on the loss landscape of deep neural networks (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015)?
