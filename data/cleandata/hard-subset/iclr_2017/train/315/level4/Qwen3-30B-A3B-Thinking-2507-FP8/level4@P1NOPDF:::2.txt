

This paper presents an empirical investigation into two key claims: (1) that stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with smaller batch sizes converges to flatter minima, and (2) that flatter minima generalize better. While the novelty of the work is modest, its empirical rigor provides valuable insight into longstanding questions about generalization in deep learning.  

The study makes a meaningful contribution by empirically validating the connection between batch size, solution flatness, and generalization performance. This has clear implications: for theory, it helps identify plausible assumptions about real-world optimization landscapes that could inform future theoretical frameworks; for practice, it suggests a simple heuristic—adjusting mini-batch size—to potentially improve generalization in existing training pipelines.  

I previously raised concerns about the authors’ claim that their sharpness criterion is scale-invariant. I am pleased to note that the authors have appropriately withdrawn this claim in the revised manuscript, resolving the issue.  

The paper’s core argument holds merit, though the writing occasionally lacks precision. For instance, the term "flatter minima" should consistently refer to the *flatness* of minima (not "flatter minima" as a noun phrase), and the connection between flatness and generalization could be more explicitly tied to the authors’ specific sharpness metric. The experimental design is sound, but the discussion of why smaller batches yield flatter minima could be strengthened by addressing potential confounding factors (e.g., learning rate schedules or weight decay).  

In summary, while the work does not introduce groundbreaking theory, it offers a well-executed empirical analysis that clarifies an important practical relationship in deep learning. The revised manuscript addresses my earlier concerns, and the findings—though incremental—provide useful guidance for both theoretical development and practical model training. The paper is suitable for publication with minor revisions to sharpen the technical exposition.
