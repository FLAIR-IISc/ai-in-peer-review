- Paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), attributing it to LB converging to sharp minimizers while SB finds flat minima.
- Authors present numerical evidence (parametric plots) and propose a novel sharpness metric tied to Hessian eigenvalues to support their claim.
- Work is motivated by distributed deep learning and connects to literature on sharp vs. flat minima.
- Decision: Reject — interesting observation but insufficient theoretical and empirical support for the core claim.
- Main criticism: sharpness metric (Eq.4) lacks theoretical grounding in generalization bounds.
- Evidence shows correlation between batch size and sharpness but does not establish causation that sharpness causes worse generalization.
- Experiments do not adequately control for alternative explanations (e.g., overfitting, other training dynamics).
- Limited empirical validation: few architectures, datasets, and optimization algorithms tested (ADAM used for both regimes).
- Parametric plots show local sharpness along a line segment but do not prove global sharpness or its relevance to generalization.
- Authors acknowledge sharpness is local but do not justify why local sharpness should imply global generalization degradation.
- Additional suggestion: derive theoretical links between the sharpness metric and generalization or show metric correlates with generalization across varied experiments.
- Additional suggestion: run controlled experiments to isolate sharpness effects (e.g., match sharpness across batch sizes).
- Additional suggestion: broaden validation across architectures, datasets, and optimizers to test consistency of the sharpness–generalization relationship.
- Additional suggestion: clarify and justify the sharpness metric, addressing sensitivity to ε and random manifold dimension.
- Questions for authors: can the sharpness metric predict generalization across networks/datasets rather than merely correlate with batch size?
- Questions for authors: how do you reconcile the metric’s locality with the global nature of generalization — is there a theoretical link?
- Questions for authors: if sharpness occurs only along a small subspace, why would that selectively affect generalization performance?
