- Paper studies why large-batch SGD generalizes worse than small-batch SGD, showing empirically that large batches converge to sharp minima while small batches find flatter minima, and explores mitigation strategies.  
- Recommendation: reject due to insufficient novelty—the core claim repeats prior work rather than offering new theory or fundamentally new observations.  
- Sharpness metric (Eq.4) is presented as novel but is essentially a practical approximation of the Hessian's largest eigenvalue and its novelty/advantage is not clearly demonstrated.  
- Prior literature (e.g., Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997; Chaudhari et al. 2016) already links flat minima to better generalization, so the paper largely confirms existing findings.  
- Experiments are thorough but confirmatory; mitigation strategies like data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training are not shown to offer novel or strongly effective solutions.  
- The paper does not adequately disentangle batch size effects from confounding hyperparameters such as learning rate schedules and optimizer choice (ADAM usage complicates interpretation).  
- Authors should explicitly state the novel contribution relative to prior work and note if novelty is limited to specific architectures or experimental settings.  
- Authors should run ablation studies to isolate the effect of batch size from other hyperparameters (learning rate, optimizer, schedules).  
- A more rigorous theoretical account of why sharp minima generalize poorly is needed rather than relying solely on empirical evidence.  
- Clarify the relationship between the proposed sharpness metric and the full Hessian spectrum, and discuss its limitations in large-scale deep networks.  
- Question: Does the large-batch → sharp-minima observation hold when using the same learning rate schedule for both batch sizes?  
- Question: How do the authors ensure the neighborhood used in the sharpness metric is large enough to capture true curvature in high-dimensional parameter spaces?  
- Question: Could the reported generalization gap be partly explained by differences in training dynamics that proper early stopping or validation curves would reveal?  
- Question: How does warm-starting large-batch training with small-batch iterates compare, in computational cost and convergence speed, to simply using small batches throughout training?
