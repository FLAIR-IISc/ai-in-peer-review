

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**1. Summary**  
This paper presents a compelling investigation into the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning. The authors make a strong case that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods consistently find flat minimizers (with small Hessian eigenvalues), and this difference explains the observed degradation in generalization performance. The paper provides clear numerical evidence through parametric plots and a novel sharpness metric, demonstrating that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting but rather to the quality of the minimizers found. The work is well-motivated by practical concerns in distributed deep learning and connects thoughtfully to existing literature on sharp vs. flat minima.

**2. Decision**  
Reject. The paper makes an interesting observation but lacks sufficient theoretical justification and empirical validation to support its core claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers. The proposed sharpness metric is not rigorously connected to generalization performance, and the experiments do not conclusively rule out alternative explanations for the observed generalization gap.

**3. Supporting Arguments**  
The paper addresses the right problem, but the evidence falls short of establishing a causal relationship between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization. The key issue is that the authors' sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not theoretically grounded in generalization bounds, and the numerical evidence presented does not demonstrate that sharpness *causes* worse generalization. Instead, the paper merely observes a correlation between large batch sizes and higher sharpness values, without showing that this correlation persists when other factors are controlled.

The paper's main argument rests on the assumption that sharp minimizers generalize worse. However, the connection between sharpness (as defined by their metric) and generalization performance is not rigorously established. The authors cite several papers that discuss sharp minima in theory (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Rissanen, 1983), but these papers do not establish a direct link between sharpness (as measured by their metric) and generalization. The authors do not provide evidence that their sharpness metric correlates with generalization performance across different networks or tasks—only that it correlates with batch size.

Furthermore, the paper's experimental design does not adequately rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap. For example, the authors note that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting (as shown in Figure 2), but they do not demonstrate that the gap persists when the training process is carefully controlled to prevent overfitting. The paper also does not compare the sharpness metric across different network architectures, data sets, or optimization algorithms to establish whether the relationship is consistent.

The paper's claim that "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" is not sufficiently supported by the experiments. The parametric plots in Figure 3 show that LB minimizers appear sharper than SB minimizers along a single line segment, but this does not prove that the minimizers are globally sharper. The authors acknowledge that sharpness is a local property, but they do not provide evidence that the observed difference in sharpness is meaningful for generalization.

**4. Additional Feedback**  
The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the large-batch generalization gap, but the following improvements would strengthen it:

- **Theoretical Justification**: The authors should provide a stronger theoretical connection between their sharpness metric and generalization performance. This could include deriving bounds on generalization error in terms of the sharpness metric, or at least showing that the metric correlates with generalization across multiple experiments.

- **Controlled Experiments**: The paper should include experiments that isolate the effect of sharpness on generalization. For example, the authors could train networks to converge to minimizers with similar sharpness values but different batch sizes, and then compare generalization performance. This would help establish causality rather than just correlation.

- **Broader Validation**: The paper should test the sharpness-generalization relationship across a wider range of network architectures, data sets, and optimization algorithms. The current experiments are limited to a few network configurations and use ADAM for both SB and LB regimes, which may not capture the full picture.

- **Clarify the Sharpness Metric**: The authors should more clearly explain why their sharpness metric (Equation 4) is appropriate for measuring sharpness in the context of deep learning. They should address potential limitations of the metric, such as its sensitivity to the choice of ε and the dimension of the random manifold.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper claims that "the generalization gap is correlated with a marked sharpness of the minimizers." Can the authors provide evidence that the sharpness metric they propose correlates with generalization performance across different networks and data sets, rather than just with batch size?

2. The paper states that "large-batch methods are attracted to regions with sharp minimizers and that, unlike small-batch methods, are unable to escape basins of attraction of these minimizers." How do the authors explain the fact that the sharpness metric they use is a local property, while the generalization gap is a global property of the model? Is there a theoretical reason to believe that local sharpness should correlate with global generalization?

3. The paper notes that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." How does this observation relate to the authors' claim that sharp minimizers generalize poorly? If sharpness only occurs along a small subspace, why does this affect generalization performance?
