- Paper addresses the generalization gap: large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) training finds flat minima that generalize better.  
- Strong numerical evidence across architectures links sharpness (large Hessian eigenvalues) to poor generalization.  
- Authors introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) as an approximation of the largest Hessian eigenvalue.  
- Parametric plots (Fig. 3) and sharpness tables (Tables 3–4) consistently show LB produces much sharper minimizers than SB.  
- Warm-start experiments (Fig. 5) demonstrate LB can generalize well when initialized by SB, implicating initialization rather than LB algorithms per se.  
- Paper situates results within literature on flat vs. sharp minima and discusses theoretical implications (Section 4).  
- Authors explore potential remedies (Appendix E) but find data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization only partially mitigate the issue.  
- Decision: Accept — important practical problem with strong empirical support and solid literature placement.  
- Suggestion: Provide more detailed justification or empirical validation of how Equation 4 relates to actual Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Suggestion: More explicitly connect empirical findings to theoretical work on loss landscapes (e.g., Choromanska et al., Soudry & Carmon).  
- Suggestion: Analyze the warm-start “sweet spot” — quantify how many SB epochs are needed before switching to LB for good generalization.  
- Clarification Q1: How does the chosen manifold dimension p=100 in the sharpness metric affect reliability, and what happens for larger p (e.g., 500–1000)?  
- Clarification Q2: Explain why the ratio ‖x_s−x₀‖₂ / ‖x_l−x₀‖₂ is 3–10 and whether this varies across architectures or initializations.  
- Clarification Q3: After interventions (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization), which sharpness characteristics remain problematic and can they be quantified?
