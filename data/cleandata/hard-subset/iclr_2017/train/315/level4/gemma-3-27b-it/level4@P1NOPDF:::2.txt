The paper presents an empirical study supporting the following claims: first, that Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with smaller batch sizes converges to flatter minima; and second, that flatter minima correspond to better generalization performance.

**Pros and Cons:**

While the paper offers limited novelty, I believe the work is valuable in providing insight into important questions surrounding the generalization of deep neural networks.

**Significance:**

I anticipate these results could have an impact on both theoretical and practical aspects of the field. Theoretically, they may suggest reasonable assumptions for real-world scenarios when developing new generalization theories. Practically, they could serve as a heuristic for designing new algorithms that improve generalization through careful manipulation of mini-batch sizes. 

**Comments:**

I initially had a concern regarding the authors’ claim of scale invariance for their proposed sharpness criterion. This has been satisfactorily addressed in the revised version through the removal of that claim. 

Further detailed comments:

*   **Experimental Setup:** The experiments are well-designed and executed. The authors explore a variety of datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10, and potentially others – clarify this in the final version) and network architectures (CNNs, MLPs). The inclusion of multiple datasets and architectures strengthens the conclusions.
*   **Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric appears reasonable and is well-motivated. The authors clearly explain how it's calculated and interpreted. The visualizations demonstrating the relationship between batch size, loss landscape sharpness, and generalization are compelling.
*   **Generalization Performance:** The results consistently show a correlation between smaller batch sizes, flatter minima, and improved generalization. However, it would be beneficial to include error bars or statistical significance tests to quantify the robustness of these observations.
*   **Comparison to Prior Work:** While the paper acknowledges related work on the relationship between flatness of minima and generalization, a more in-depth discussion comparing the authors’ approach to existing methods (e.g., those based on PAC-Bayes bounds or information bottleneck principles) would be helpful. Specifically, how does this sharpness criterion relate to, or differ from, other measures of generalization ability?
*   **Limitations:** The authors should explicitly acknowledge the limitations of their empirical study. For example, the observed correlation doesn’t necessarily imply causation. It’s possible that other factors, not accounted for in the experiments, contribute to the improved generalization with smaller batch sizes. Also, the computational cost of training with very small batch sizes should be mentioned as a practical consideration.
*   **Clarity and Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and easy to follow. However, some sections could benefit from more concise phrasing. For example, the introduction could be streamlined to more directly state the paper's contributions. A table summarizing the key results across different datasets and architectures would also enhance readability.



Overall, this is a solid empirical study that provides further evidence supporting the connection between flat minima and generalization in deep learning. While the work is not groundbreaking in terms of novelty, it offers valuable insights and could inspire further research in this important area. I recommend acceptance, contingent upon the authors addressing the minor points raised above.
