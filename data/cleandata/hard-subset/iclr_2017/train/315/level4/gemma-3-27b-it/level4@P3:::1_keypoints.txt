- Paper studies why large-batch SGD degrades generalization, arguing large batches converge to sharp minima while small batches find flatter minima.  
- Well-motivated practical problem for scaling deep learning; clear articulation of the sharp-vs-flat hypothesis.  
- Empirical evidence (parametric plots and sharpness metric) provides compelling visual and quantitative support.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main weakness: analysis lacks sufficient rigor and a strong theoretical foundation linking large batches to sharp minima.  
- Experiments are limited to a small set of architectures and datasets, reducing generality of conclusions.  
- The proposed sharpness metric is heuristic; its relationship to Hessian eigenvalues and sensitivity to metric parameters is not established.  
- Remedies tested (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) show limited success and are not thoroughly analyzed.  
- Conclusion that remedies don't solve the problem is premature without deeper investigation.  
- Heavy reliance on empirical results without offering concrete theoretical directions; discussion acknowledges but doesn't address this gap.  
- Suggested improvements: justify and analyze the sharpness metric, including Hessian relation and ablation studies on metric parameters.  
- Suggested improvements: deeper analysis of why proposed remedies fail and conditions under which they might work.  
- Suggested improvements: pursue theoretical investigations into when/why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima.  
- Suggested improvements: broaden experiments across more architectures, datasets, and optimization algorithms; perform ADAM-parameter ablations.  
- Questions for authors: intuitive explanation linking batch size to curvature/step size; how remedies vary with batch size; effect of normalization techniques; computational cost and scalability of the sharpness metric.
