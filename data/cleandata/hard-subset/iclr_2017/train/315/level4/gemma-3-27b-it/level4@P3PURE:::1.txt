Interesting paper that definitely provides value to the community by discussing why large-batch gradient descent does not perform as well as expected. The authors investigate the relationship between batch size and generalization performance, identifying that large batches converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function, whereas small batches tend to find flat minimizers – and that flatness correlates with better generalization. 

The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and the experimental results are convincing. The core idea – connecting sharpness/flatness of the minima to generalization – is insightful and elegantly demonstrated. The ablation studies are particularly strong, systematically exploring the impact of different factors (learning rate scaling, optimizer choice) on the observed phenomena. I especially appreciated the visualization of the loss landscape; these figures effectively illustrate the difference between sharp and flat minima found with different batch sizes.

However, a potential limitation is the reliance on the specific datasets used (CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100). While these are common benchmarks, the observed effects might not generalize to all datasets. Further investigation on more diverse datasets would strengthen the conclusions. Additionally, the paper focuses primarily on fully connected networks and convolutional networks. Exploring the effect of batch size on generalization in other architectures (e.g., transformers, recurrent networks) could be valuable. 

Despite these minor points, this is a strong submission. The findings are significant and have practical implications for training deep neural networks. I recommend acceptance.
