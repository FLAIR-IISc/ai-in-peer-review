- Paper studies how batch size affects minima sharpness and generalization, claiming large-batch -> sharp minima with worse generalization, small-batch -> flat minima with better generalization.
- Reviewer recommends rejection in current form due to insufficient novelty and rigor for a top-tier venue.
- Empirical observations are interesting and consistent with existing understanding but lack deep theoretical justification.
- Paper presents clear, well-organized experiments across multiple architectures and datasets that convincingly show the correlation between batch size and sharpness.
- Authors introduce a sharpness metric (Equation 4) and use it to relate sharpness to generalization performance.
- Experimental setup is sound and includes attempts to mitigate the generalization gap (e.g., data augmentation, conservative training).
- Major weakness: reliance on empirical evidence with little new theoretical analysis or justification connecting sharpness metric to generalization bounds.
- Sharpness metric is not fully justified; connection to generalization and to the claimed Ritz-value approximation is insufficiently explained.
- Discussion of remedies for the generalization gap is superficial and lacks detailed quantitative comparisons, statistical tests, and computational cost analysis.
- Paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to related work on neural network loss landscapes.
- Question 1: Need sensitivity analysis of sharpness metric parameters (lambda and random matrix dimension p).
- Question 2: Require elaboration on why the Ritz-value approximation is sufficient and how it relates to generalization.
- Question 3: Ask whether findings hold across different optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum vs ADAM).
- Question 4: Request more details on mitigation experiments, quantitative results, significance testing, and computational costs.
- Question 5: Ask for clarification on how this generalization gap differs from standard overfitting and why early stopping wouldn’t address it.
