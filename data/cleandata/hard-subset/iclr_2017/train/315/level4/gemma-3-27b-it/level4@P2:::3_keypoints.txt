- Paper studies why large-batch SGD generalizes worse than small-batch SGD, attributing it to convergence to sharper minima.  
- Authors provide numerical evidence across networks/datasets that large-batch methods find sharper minimizers while small-batch methods find flatter ones.  
- Sharpness metric and parametric plots are introduced as visual and quantitative tools to characterize minima.  
- Paper explores remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) but finds limited success.  
- Reviewer recommends rejection: evidence is preliminary and proposed remedies are not convincingly effective.  
- Major critique: analysis is predominantly empirical and lacks rigorous theoretical underpinning.  
- Major critique: connection to existing theoretical work is acknowledged but not deeply explored.  
- Major critique: the proposed sharpness metric is imperfect and its correlation with generalization is not thoroughly validated.  
- Major critique: reported generalization gaps lack statistical significance testing.  
- Strength: problem is clearly articulated and practically relevant (performance drop when scaling batch sizes).  
- Strength: experiments span several configurations and provide initial support for the main claim.  
- Strength: attempts to address the problem (remedies) demonstrate effort toward practical solutions.  
- Suggestion: develop or adapt theoretical frameworks to explain the sharpness–generalization correlation.  
- Suggestion: validate and relate the sharpness metric to Hessian eigenvalues and consider alternative metrics.  
- Suggestion: deepen analysis of why proposed remedies fail and search for better hyperparameters or methods.  
- Suggestion: more thoroughly engage with and position the work against existing theoretical literature.  
- Suggestion: include statistical significance tests for reported performance differences.  
- Suggestion: explore dynamic batch-size schedules that combine small- and large-batch benefits.  
- Questions for authors: clarify limitations and improvement avenues for the sharpness metric.  
- Questions for authors: provide detailed analysis of warm-starting large-batch training with small-batch iterates and optimal strategies.  
- Questions for authors: identify loss-landscape properties, architectures, or datasets where large-batch sharp minima are more likely.  
- Questions for authors: report experiments combining large-batch training with weight decay or dropout.  
- Questions for authors: quantify computational cost of the sharpness metric and ways to reduce it.
