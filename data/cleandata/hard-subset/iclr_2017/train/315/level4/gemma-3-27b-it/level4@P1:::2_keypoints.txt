- Decision: Accept — compelling empirical investigation into the generalization gap with large batch sizes.  
- Problem identified: Scaling deep learning training degrades generalization with larger batch sizes.  
- Hypothesis 1: SGD with smaller batch sizes converges to flatter minima.  
- Hypothesis 2: Flatter minima correlate with better generalization performance.  
- Methods: Comprehensive numerical experiments across architectures/datasets using parametric plots and a sharpness metric.  
- Main finding: Large batches converge to sharper minima and exhibit poorer generalization; small batches find flatter minima and generalize better.  
- Sharpness metric: Used as a heuristic; provides convincing evidence but has limitations.  
- Robustness: Authors acknowledge and address alternative explanations, strengthening the argument.  
- Remedies: Tried data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization in the appendix, but these did not fully resolve the gap.  
- Minor improvements suggested: consistently define acronyms (e.g., SB, LB) for readability.  
- Metric discussion suggested: expand on limitations of the sharpness metric (Eq. 4), relation to Hessian spectrum, and biases from neighborhood size (epsilon) and manifold dimension (p).  
- Theory connection suggested: elaborate how findings link to existing theoretical work (e.g., MDL) and possibly refine frameworks.  
- Optimizer clarity: Explain rationale for using ADAM for both small and large batches and whether results generalize to other optimizers.  
- Appendix formatting: Improve consistency for better readability.  
- Question 1: Why did data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization fail individually or jointly to substantially close the generalization gap, and were there unexpected interactions with large batches?  
- Question 2: Beyond warm-start experiments, is there evidence that landscapes near sharp minima differ qualitatively (curvature, saddle points) from those near flatter minima?  
- Question 3: What is the computational cost of computing the sharpness metric (Eq. 4) and how does it scale with network size for practical use on larger models?
