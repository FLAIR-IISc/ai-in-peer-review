- Paper studies why large-batch SGD degrades generalization, arguing large batches converge to sharp minima while small batches find flatter minima, with numerical evidence and mitigation attempts.
- Recommendation: Reject due to incomplete analysis and weak theoretical foundation; empirical results don’t rule out other contributing factors.
- Strength: Clear problem statement linking batch size to loss landscape geometry and generalization.
- Strength: Well-designed numerical experiments across multiple architectures and datasets demonstrating correlation between batch size and sharpness.
- Strength: Useful comparisons (parametric plots) and a proposed sharpness metric that offer empirical insights.
- Strength: Extensive experimentation and preliminary attempts to mitigate the gap show thoroughness.
- Weakness: Claims are largely empirical without a formal theoretical proof linking large batches to sharp minima.
- Weakness: Proposed sharpness metric is heuristic; its relation to Hessian/eigenvalues and true curvature is not fully analyzed.
- Weakness: Mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) show limited success and failures are not deeply investigated.
- Weakness: Discussion lists open questions but offers no clear roadmap for future research.
- Suggestion: Strengthen theoretical justification by connecting to existing work on deep-network loss landscapes and providing formal arguments.
- Suggestion: Provide rigorous analysis of the sharpness metric and its relation to the Hessian and eigen-spectrum, including limitations.
- Suggestion: Perform a deeper failure analysis of mitigation strategies, including whether experiments used optimal hyperparameters.
- Suggestion: Explore alternative explanations (learning-rate schedules, optimizer choice) for the generalization gap.
