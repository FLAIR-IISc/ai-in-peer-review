The paper is an empirical study investigating the relationship between batch size, the sharpness of minima, and generalization in deep learning. Specifically, the authors aim to justify the following: 1) Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with smaller batch sizes converges to flatter minima, and 2) flatter minima exhibit better generalization performance.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling empirical investigation into a crucial issue in deep learning – the generalization gap observed with large batch sizes. The findings strongly suggest that large batches converge to sharper minima, which correlate with poorer generalization, and this is a valuable contribution to understanding this phenomenon.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The authors clearly identify a significant problem in scaling deep learning training: the degradation in generalization performance with larger batch sizes. Their central hypothesis – that this is due to the convergence to sharper minima – is well-motivated and supported by comprehensive numerical experiments across various network architectures and datasets. The use of both parametric plots and a sharpness metric (though acknowledged as a heuristic) provides convincing evidence for this correlation. The experiments demonstrate a consistent pattern: larger batches lead to sharper minima and lower generalization performance, while smaller batches find flatter minima and generalize better. The authors thoughtfully acknowledge and address potential alternative explanations for the observed results, strengthening their argument. The inclusion of attempted remedies in the appendix, while not fully successful, demonstrates a proactive attempt to address the problem and provides further insights.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is strong, several minor improvements could enhance its clarity and impact:

*   **Notation:** While generally clear, consistently defining acronyms (e.g., SB, LB) upon first use within each section would improve readability.
*   **Discussion of Sharpness Metric:** Expanding the discussion of the limitations of the sharpness metric (Equation 4) and its relationship to the Hessian spectrum could be beneficial.  Acknowledging potential biases introduced by the chosen neighborhood size (epsilon) and manifold dimension (p) would strengthen the analysis.
*   **Connection to Existing Work:** While the paper mentions relevant theoretical work, further elaboration on how the findings relate to and potentially refine existing theoretical frameworks would be valuable.  For example, a more detailed discussion of the connection to the Minimum Description Length (MDL) principle would be helpful.
*   **Clarity on ADAM:** The paper consistently uses ADAM as the optimizer for both small and large batch settings. It might be beneficial to briefly discuss the rationale for this choice and whether the observed results are specific to ADAM or generalize to other optimizers.
*   **Appendix Organization:** While the appendices are informative, a more consistent formatting style would enhance readability.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the limited success of the proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) in fully addressing the generalization gap. Could the authors elaborate on why these approaches, individually or in combination, failed to achieve substantial improvement? Were there any unexpected interactions between these techniques and large batch sizes?
2.  The authors observe that large batch methods appear to be "stuck" in the basin of attraction of sharp minimizers. Is there any evidence, beyond the warm-starting experiments, to suggest that the landscape near these sharp minima is qualitatively different than that near the flatter minima found by small-batch methods (e.g., different curvature properties, presence of saddle points)?
3.  Could the authors comment on the computational cost associated with calculating the sharpness metric (Equation 4) for the networks considered, and how this might scale with network size? This would help to understand the practical limitations of using this metric for larger, more complex models.
