- Recommendation: Reject.  
- Main finding: Large-batch SGD converges to sharper minima that generalize worse, while small-batch SGD finds flatter minima due to gradient noise.  
- Evidence: Extensive numerical experiments across networks/datasets show correlation between batch size, sharpness, and generalization.  
- Visualization: Parametric plots and sharpness metrics effectively quantify and visualize the phenomenon.  
- Empirical focus: Analysis is primarily empirical and demonstrates correlation rather than providing a rigorous causal explanation.  
- Limited remedies: Proposed fixes (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization; Appendix E) yield only marginal improvements.  
- Depth lacking: Paper lacks deeper theoretical understanding of why large batches favor sharp minima.  
- Future work weak: Conclusions list open questions but do not give compelling, concrete future research directions.  
- Related work: References are comprehensive but the connection to recent theoretical work and related literature is brief and underdeveloped.  
- Sharpness metric: Definition and interpretation need clarification, including relation to Hessian spectrum and metric limitations.  
- Computational concern: Need clarification on computational cost and scalability of sharpness metric for large models.  
- Overfitting claim: Reviewer asks for stronger evidence that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting (e.g., effect of early stopping).  
- Characterization of minima: Request for analysis of differences between minima beyond sharpness (e.g., curvature or other properties).  
- Dynamic batching: Reviewer requests elaboration on dynamic batch size adjustment and why it might be promising.  
- Suggested improvements: Provide theoretical justification, deeper analysis/tuning of remedies and combinations, explore architecture susceptibility, and clarify metric and related-work connections.
