- Paper studies how batch size affects minima sharpness and generalization: large batches → sharper minima and worse generalization; small batches → flatter minima and better generalization.
- Decision: Reject due to limited novelty.
- Main contribution is empirical validation of an already-known phenomenon rather than a novel finding or solution.
- Experimental setup is thorough but does not substantially advance prior investigations.
- Explored remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) show limited success and no compelling remedy.
- Sharpness analysis relies on perturbation-based metric, which is imperfect and needs firmer grounding.
- A stronger connection to the Hessian spectrum would bolster the conclusions.
- Authors addressed scale invariance earlier but should further discuss limitations of the sharpness metric.
- Related work section should better cover existing literature and recent theoretical results on loss surfaces.
- Provide more intuition and justification for the chosen sharpness metric (Equation 4) and its interpretation.
- Investigate and report correlations between the proposed metric and other flatness measures (e.g., Hessian eigenvalues).
- Remedy discussion should analyze why attempted methods fail to close the generalization gap.
- Include more experimental details, especially hyperparameter tuning for conservative training and robust optimization.
- Ask authors to explain specifically why the explored remedies failed to flatten minima.
- Ask for clarification on correlations between the sharpness metric and Hessian eigenvalues.
- Ask what dynamic batch-size schedules they recommend and what experiments were run.
- Ask what specific novel insight this work adds beyond prior studies and how it differs from existing literature.
