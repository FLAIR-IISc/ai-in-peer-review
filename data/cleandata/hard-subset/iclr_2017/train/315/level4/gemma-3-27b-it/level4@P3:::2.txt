## Review of “The drawbacks of large-batch methods”

This paper investigates the relationship between batch size, the sharpness of minima found during training, and generalization performance in deep learning. The authors present empirical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima, which correlate with poorer generalization, while small-batch methods converge to flatter minima that generalize better. This is attributed to the inherent noise in the gradient estimation of small-batch methods. The paper also explores potential strategies to mitigate the generalization gap observed with large batches.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents interesting empirical observations and aligns with commonly held beliefs regarding batch size and generalization, the work lacks significant novelty and the presented evidence, while suggestive, isn’t sufficiently strong to warrant acceptance. The connection between sharpness and generalization, though plausible, isn’t definitively established within the scope of this work.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary weakness lies in the largely observational nature of the study. The authors demonstrate a correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization gap across a set of network configurations and datasets. However, correlation does not equal causation. While the proposed sharpness metric offers a quantifiable measure, its direct link to generalization ability beyond the established theoretical intuition remains unproven. The experiments, while thorough in their exploration of different network architectures, don’t offer a convincing mechanism *why* large-batch methods gravitate towards sharper minima. 

Furthermore, the attempted remedies for the generalization gap – data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization – show limited success and are presented without detailed analysis of *why* they fail to fully resolve the issue. Simply stating they “do not correct the problem” is insufficient. The paper would be strengthened by a deeper investigation into the failure modes of these approaches. The idealized performance model presented in Appendix C feels tangential and doesn't substantially support the core arguments.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Theoretical Grounding:** While the paper rightly points to existing theoretical work on sharp and flat minima, it would benefit from a more in-depth discussion of relevant theory and how the empirical findings relate to established theoretical frameworks.
*   **Sharpness Metric Justification:** The authors acknowledge that the proposed sharpness metric is imperfect. A more rigorous justification of its relevance to generalization is needed, perhaps through comparisons to alternative measures or theoretical analysis.
*   **Mechanistic Explanation:** The paper would be substantially improved by attempting to explain *why* large-batch methods converge to sharper minima. Is it related to the loss landscape, the optimization dynamics, or other factors?
*   **Analysis of Remedies:** A more detailed analysis of why the proposed remedies fail to fully address the generalization gap is crucial. Were the parameters tuned appropriately? Are there specific network architectures or datasets where they are more effective?
*   **Comparison to Existing Work:** The introduction should more thoroughly discuss related work beyond the cited papers, particularly recent studies exploring batch size effects and generalization.
* **Statistical Significance:** While the authors report mean and standard deviation, further statistical testing to determine the significance of observed differences would strengthen the conclusions.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The authors mention that the sharpness metric approximates the Ritz value of the Hessian projected onto a random subspace. How was the dimensionality of this subspace (p=100) chosen, and what sensitivity analysis was performed to ensure the results are not overly dependent on this value?
2.  The paper demonstrates a correlation between sharpness and generalization. Can the authors speculate on possible mechanisms that might explain this relationship beyond the noise argument, perhaps relating to the properties of the loss landscape itself?
3.  The attempted remedies for the generalization gap did not fully succeed. What specific parameter tuning strategies were employed for these methods, and were any ablation studies performed to isolate the effects of different components?
4.  Can the authors elaborate on the connection between their findings and recent work on the geometry of loss landscapes in deep learning? Specifically, how do their observations relate to concepts like mode connectivity and barrier heights?
5.  The paper uses ADAM optimizer. Were any experiments conducted with other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum) and if so, did they yield similar results?
