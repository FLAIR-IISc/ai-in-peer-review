This paper investigates the degradation in generalization performance observed when using large batch sizes in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for training deep learning models. The authors present compelling numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function, which are known to generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flatter minimizers due to the inherent noise in gradient estimation. The paper further explores potential strategies to mitigate the generalization gap associated with large batches.

**Reject.** While the paper presents interesting observations and analysis, the conclusions are largely based on empirical evidence and lack a strong theoretical foundation. The core claim—that large batch sizes inherently lead to sharp minima—is plausible and supported by the experiments, but a more rigorous justification, perhaps with some theoretical backing or a more comprehensive exploration of the loss landscape, is needed to elevate the work beyond observation. Furthermore, the proposed solutions in Appendix E show limited success, which weakens the practical impact of the findings.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of generalization degradation in large-batch training and provides a reasonable explanation rooted in the sharpness of the minimizers found. The experimental setup appears well-designed, utilizing a variety of network architectures and datasets. The comparison between small-batch and large-batch methods is thoughtfully presented, and the parametric plots and sharpness metrics offer valuable insights into the characteristics of the obtained minima. The authors also appropriately contextualize their work within existing literature on optimization and generalization in deep learning. 

However, several aspects require further clarification and strengthening. Firstly, the connection between the observed sharpness of minima and generalization performance, while intuitively appealing, could be more rigorously demonstrated. For instance, the authors mention the minimum description length (MDL) theory but do not explicitly connect it to their experimental results. Secondly, the analysis focuses primarily on the Adam optimizer. While this is reasonable, exploring other optimization algorithms (e.g., SGD with momentum) under large-batch conditions might provide a more generalized understanding. Thirdly, the attempted remedies for the generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) show limited success. A more detailed analysis of *why* these methods fail to fully address the issue would be beneficial. Finally, the idealized performance model presented in Appendix C lacks sufficient detail regarding the assumptions made and the factors that might influence the actual speedups achievable in a real-world setting.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Theoretical Justification:** Explore potential theoretical explanations for why large batch sizes might favor sharp minima. Could the curvature of the loss landscape, or the properties of the gradient, play a role?
*   **Optimizer Sensitivity:**  Repeat key experiments with different optimizers, like SGD with momentum, to assess whether the observed phenomena are specific to Adam.
*   **Detailed Failure Analysis:** Provide a deeper analysis of why the proposed remedies fail to fully mitigate the generalization gap. Are there specific limitations to these techniques when applied to large-batch training?
*   **Sharpeness Metric Refinement:**  Investigate the sensitivity of the sharpness metric (Equation 4) to its parameters (e.g., the size of the neighborhood, the dimension of the random manifold).
*   **Performance Model Clarification:** Expand the performance model in Appendix C with more realistic considerations, such as communication overhead and synchronization costs.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What is the computational cost of calculating the sharpness metric for the networks used in your experiments? Is it feasible to apply this metric to larger, more complex models?
*   You mention that the parametric plots explore a linear slice of the function.  Does the sharpness observed in these slices hold true for other, more complex paths between the small-batch and large-batch minima?
*   Could you elaborate on the theoretical limitations of applying robust optimization techniques to large-batch training? What prevents scaling these methods to the sizes needed in your experiments?
*   You state that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting in the traditional sense. Have you considered investigating whether large-batch methods might be more susceptible to other forms of overfitting, such as memorization of the training data?
