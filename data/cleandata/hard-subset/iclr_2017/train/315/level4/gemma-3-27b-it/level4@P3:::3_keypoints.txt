- Paper studies generalization degradation with large-batch SGD, arguing large batches converge to sharp minima while small batches find flatter minima due to gradient noise.  
- Empirical evidence is compelling but the core claim lacks strong theoretical justification.  
- Proposed mitigation strategies (Appendix E) show limited success, reducing practical impact.  
- Experimental setup is well-designed, covering multiple architectures and datasets.  
- Parametric plots and sharpness metrics provide useful insights into minima characteristics.  
- Paper situates findings appropriately within existing optimization/generalization literature.  
- Connection between observed sharpness and generalization (e.g., via MDL) is not rigorously demonstrated.  
- Analysis focuses mainly on Adam; results may not generalize to other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum).  
- Remedies tested (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are insufficiently analyzed to explain their limited effectiveness.  
- Sharpness metric (Equation 4) sensitivity to parameters (neighborhood size, manifold dimension) needs investigation.  
- Idealized performance model (Appendix C) omits realistic factors like communication and synchronization costs.  
- Suggestion: develop theoretical explanations for why large batches favor sharp minima (loss curvature, gradient properties).  
- Suggestion: repeat experiments with other optimizers to assess optimizer sensitivity.  
- Suggestion: provide deeper failure analysis for mitigation strategies to understand why they fall short.  
- Suggestion: refine and probe sharpness metric sensitivity and robustness.  
- Suggestion: expand performance model with more realistic system-level considerations.  
- Question: what is the computational cost of computing the sharpness metric and is it feasible for larger models?  
- Question: do parametric linear slices reflect sharpness along more complex paths between minima?  
- Question: what theoretical limitations prevent scaling robust optimization techniques to large-batch regimes?  
- Question: have the authors investigated other forms of overfitting (e.g., memorization) as a cause of the generalization gap?
