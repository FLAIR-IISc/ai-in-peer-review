## Review of “The drawbacks of large-batch methods”

This paper investigates the degradation in generalization performance observed when using large batch sizes in stochastic gradient descent for deep learning. The authors present numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training and testing functions, which are known to generalize poorly. In contrast, small-batch methods consistently converge to flatter minimizers due to the inherent noise in gradient estimation. The paper also discusses several strategies to mitigate this generalization gap.

**Decision:** Reject. While the observations presented are interesting and the experiments well-executed, the paper lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance. The core argument – that large batch sizes lead to sharp minima and thus poorer generalization – is already well-established in the community, and this paper primarily provides further empirical support rather than a fundamentally new insight or solution.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper clearly articulates the problem of generalization degradation with large batches and positions it well within the existing literature. The experimental setup appears sound, employing a variety of network architectures and datasets. The visualizations and sharpness metrics provide compelling evidence correlating large batch sizes with sharper minima. The authors appropriately acknowledge related work and demonstrate familiarity with the current understanding of the loss landscape in deep learning. 

However, the paper’s main contribution is largely confirmatory. The link between sharp minima and poor generalization is not a novel finding. Furthermore, the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are presented as preliminary investigations with limited success, lacking a detailed analysis of *why* they fail to fully address the problem. The discussion of theoretical work, while relevant, remains at a high level and doesn't offer a substantial theoretical contribution.  The conclusion highlights open questions, which implicitly acknowledges the incomplete nature of the work.

**Additional Feedback:**

The paper is generally well-written, but some aspects could be improved for clarity. 

*   **Metric 2.1 (Sharpness):** The explanation of this metric is somewhat convoluted. Providing a more intuitive explanation of how it relates to the spectrum of the Hessian would be beneficial.
*   **Experiment Details:** While the appendices provide details on network architectures and datasets, more specifics about hyperparameter tuning (learning rate schedules, regularization parameters, etc.) would increase reproducibility and allow for a more thorough evaluation of the results.
*   **Warm-starting Experiments:** The results of the warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are intriguing but require further exploration.  What specific conditions (e.g., number of initial SB epochs) are necessary for the LB method to improve?  A more systematic investigation could provide valuable insights.
*   **Comparison to Existing Methods:**  The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison to existing methods designed to address the large-batch generalization problem, such as Layer-wise Adaptive Rate Scaling (LARS).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper states that the generalization gap isn’t due to overfitting. However, the training accuracy remains high even with large batches. Could the issue be a form of *implicit* regularization provided by the noise in small-batch training that is absent in large-batch training, rather than solely sharp minima?
2.  You mention the potential for dynamic batch size adjustment. Have you explored any specific scheduling strategies for increasing the batch size during training, and if so, what were the results?
3.  The sharpness metric relies on a limited neighborhood exploration. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the box size (parameter  in Equation 3) and the dimensionality of the random manifold (p=100)?
4.  The paper focuses primarily on ADAM. Have you observed similar behavior with other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum) when using large batch sizes?
