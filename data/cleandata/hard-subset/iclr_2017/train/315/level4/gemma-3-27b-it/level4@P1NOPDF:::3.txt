I find this paper to be quite interesting and potentially useful. 

The authors investigate the interplay between local and global optimization in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for non-convex optimization, specifically focusing on the benefits of sharp and broad minima. Their analysis provides valuable insights into why different optimization techniques, such as large-batch (LB) and small-batch (SB) SGD, exhibit contrasting behaviors. The theoretical findings are generally well-supported by the presented experimental results.

However, I believe the paper could benefit from further investigation into potential hybrid approaches. For example, introducing rescaled Gaussian noise to the gradients during the LB regime might allow one to leverage the advantages typically associated with the SB regime – potentially improving generalization and escaping unfavorable local minima. This is particularly relevant given the observed tendency of LB SGD to converge to sharp minima. 

More specifically, the paper demonstrates that LB SGD tends to find solutions residing in sharp minima, while SB SGD favors broader minima. This distinction is crucial as broader minima are hypothesized to generalize better to unseen data. The analysis of the loss landscape, particularly the curvature around stationary points, is a strong point of the paper. The authors clearly articulate how batch size influences the types of minima found and connect this to the theoretical properties of SGD.

My main concerns lie in the limited exploration of techniques to bridge the gap between the LB and SB regimes. While the paper convincingly argues *why* these regimes behave differently, it doesn’t delve into *how* to combine their strengths. The suggestion of gradient noise is just one potential avenue. Exploring adaptive batch size strategies, or incorporating techniques like sharpness-aware minimization (SAM) within the LB framework, could further strengthen the work.

Regarding the experimental validation, the results are generally convincing but could be more comprehensive. It would be beneficial to see performance comparisons on a wider range of datasets and network architectures, particularly those commonly used in the literature (e.g., ImageNet). Additionally, quantifying the sharpness of the minima found by different methods – perhaps through metrics like the Hessian’s largest eigenvalue – would provide a more direct validation of the theoretical claims.

The writing is generally clear, although some sections could benefit from more concise phrasing. For instance, the mathematical derivations, while correct, are occasionally dense and could be made more accessible with additional explanations. 

Overall, this is a solid contribution with a clear and well-defined research question. Addressing the suggestions above, particularly the exploration of hybrid optimization strategies and expanded experimental evaluation, would significantly enhance the paper's impact. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
