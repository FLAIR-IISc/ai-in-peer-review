This paper presents an empirical investigation into the relationship between stochastic gradient descent (SGD) with small batch sizes, the flatness of resulting minima, and generalization performance in deep neural networks. Specifically, the authors explore the hypothesis that smaller batch sizes lead to convergence at flatter minima, and that these flatter minima correlate with improved generalization ability.

While the work offers limited novelty in terms of algorithmic development, it provides valuable insights into fundamental questions surrounding deep network generalization. The experimental results contribute to a growing body of evidence supporting the connection between flatness of minima and generalization, and offer a practical perspective on this relationship. 

The potential impact of this work is substantial, spanning both theoretical and practical domains. From a theoretical standpoint, the findings can help refine assumptions used in the development of new generalization theories, ensuring they align with observed behavior in real-world training scenarios. Practically, the results suggest a potentially effective heuristic – manipulating mini-batch sizes – to improve generalization performance, inspiring the development of new algorithms or optimization strategies.

I initially raised a concern regarding the authors’ claim of scale invariance for their proposed sharpness criterion. However, this concern has been addressed in the revised manuscript through the removal of this claim, demonstrating responsiveness to feedback. 

Overall, the paper represents a solid empirical study that contributes to a better understanding of the interplay between optimization and generalization in deep learning. The clarity of the presentation and the thoroughness of the experiments are commendable.
