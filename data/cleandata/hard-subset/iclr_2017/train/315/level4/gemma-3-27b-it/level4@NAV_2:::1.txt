This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, positing that this stems from a tendency to converge to sharp minimizers of the loss landscape. The authors provide numerical evidence supporting this claim, demonstrating a correlation between batch size, the sharpness of the resulting minima, and generalization performance. The paper is well-motivated and addresses a practically relevant problem in deep learning optimization.

Reject. While the paper presents compelling evidence supporting its central hypothesis, the analysis lacks a deeper theoretical underpinning and the proposed remedies are not demonstrably effective. The empirical findings, while insightful, do not definitively establish causality – it remains possible that other factors contribute to the observed generalization gap.

The paper convincingly demonstrates that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharper minima than small-batch methods, and that these sharper minima are associated with poorer generalization performance. The use of parametric plots and a sharpness metric provides valuable insight into the characteristics of these minima. The experiments cover a reasonable range of network architectures and datasets. However, the paper relies heavily on empirical observations. A more rigorous theoretical analysis, even if preliminary, would strengthen the conclusions. Furthermore, the attempts to remedy the generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) showed limited success, and the reasons for their failure are not thoroughly investigated. The statement that these approaches "do not correct the problem" feels strong given the brief exploration.

Several questions remain. First, how sensitive are the results to the choice of optimizer (ADAM was used throughout)? Would different optimizers exhibit similar behavior with large and small batches? Second, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is presented as a computationally feasible approximation. How does this metric correlate with the actual spectrum of the Hessian? A more detailed analysis of the Hessian's eigenvalues, even on a smaller scale, would be beneficial. Third, the paper mentions that the sharp minima identified do not resemble a cone—that they are sharp only along a small dimensional subspace. Could this dimensionality be quantified or characterized further? Finally, the paper briefly touches upon the potential of dynamic batch size adjustment. A more detailed investigation of this approach, perhaps through additional experiments, would be valuable.
