- Paper studies the generalization gap with large mini-batches, hypothesizing LB methods converge to sharp minima while SB methods find flat minima that generalize better.  
- Experiments span six network architectures and multiple datasets to validate the hypothesis.  
- 1D parametric visualizations show loss is much steeper around LB solutions than SB solutions.  
- Introduces a practical sharpness metric (max loss in a neighborhood) that consistently finds LB solutions 1–2 orders of magnitude sharper than SB.  
- SB methods explore parameter space more and move farther from initialization; LB methods converge to nearer minima.  
- Warm-starting LB from an SB-found solution closes the generalization gap, suggesting LB methods get trapped in sharp basins rather than being inherently weak.  
- Data augmentation and robust/adversarial training give marginal gains but do not eliminate the sharpness problem.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is a strong empirical contribution.  
- Key reason 1: Addresses a critical, practical bottleneck in scaling deep learning to large batches.  
- Key reason 2: Provides strong, diverse empirical evidence combining visual and quantitative analyses.  
- Methodological strength: Rigorous, well-controlled experiments (same optimizer, multiple seeds) and effective visualizations.  
- Sharpness metric is a computationally feasible Hessian proxy and shows consistent sharpness differences in Tables 3–4.  
- Warm-start experiments indicate gradient noise from SB is essential for escaping sharp minima and guide future algorithmic directions.  
- Suggestion: Analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to hyperparameters (epsilon, random subspace dimension).  
- Suggestion: Strengthen connections to theory (e.g., MDL, Choromanska et al., Soudry & Carmon) to better ground empirical findings.  
- Suggestion: Bring promising remedies (e.g., dynamic batch-size scheduling) into the main conclusion as future directions.  
- Question: Have you characterized the low-dimensional “sharp subspace” (e.g., which layers or weights correspond to high-curvature directions)?  
- Question: How do adaptive optimizers (ADAM/Adagrad) interact with sharpness — does batch noise dominate their effect?  
- Question: Could alternative initialization strategies place x0 so LB methods are more likely to find flatter regions?
