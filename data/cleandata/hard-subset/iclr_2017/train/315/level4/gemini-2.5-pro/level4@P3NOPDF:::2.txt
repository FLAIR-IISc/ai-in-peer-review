This paper presents a comprehensive empirical investigation into the relationship between mini-batch size, the sharpness of the learned minima, and generalization performance in deep neural networks. The authors provide compelling evidence to support two widely held but less rigorously verified hypotheses: (1) that Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with smaller batch sizes tends to converge to flatter, wider minima, and (2) that these flatter minima are strongly correlated with better generalization.

**Strengths and Significance**

While the paper does not introduce a novel theoretical framework, its primary contribution and value are substantial. It provides a large-scale, systematic validation of phenomena that are often anecdotally observed or studied in more limited contexts. By meticulously demonstrating the connection between batch size, solution sharpness, and generalization across various models and datasets, the work sheds crucial light on the optimization landscape of deep networks.

The significance of these findings is twofold. For theorists, this study offers empirical grounding that can guide the formulation of more realistic assumptions for developing new generalization bounds and theories. For instance, the consistent observation that flatter minima generalize better supports research directions focused on the local geometry of the loss surface. For practitioners, the paper provides a clear rationale for the common heuristic of using small batch sizes and suggests potential avenues for algorithmic innovation, such as developing optimizers that explicitly seek out wider valleys in the loss landscape, perhaps through adaptive manipulation of the mini-batch size.

In essence, this work serves as a valuable bridge between empirical observation and theoretical inquiry, providing a solid foundation for future research in this area.

**Comments and Resolved Issues**

In a previous version of the manuscript, I had a concern regarding a claim about the scale-invariance of the proposed sharpness metric. I am pleased to see that the authors have addressed this by removing the claim in the revised version, which improves the precision and correctness of their work. This responsiveness to feedback is commendable.

**Conclusion**

In summary, this paper is a valuable contribution to the field. Despite its empirical nature, the work provides important insights and robust evidence that will be of great interest to both researchers and practitioners working on understanding and improving the generalization of deep neural networks. It clarifies important questions and provides a solid foundation for future theoretical and algorithmic development.
