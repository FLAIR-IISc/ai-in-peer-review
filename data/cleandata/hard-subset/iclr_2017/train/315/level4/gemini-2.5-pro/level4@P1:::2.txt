### Summary of Contributions

This paper provides a valuable empirical investigation into a widely-observed phenomenon in deep learning: the degradation in generalization performance when training with large mini-batches. The authors systematically explore the "generalization gap" between small-batch and large-batch training regimes. The central hypothesis, supported by extensive numerical experiments, is that this gap arises because large-batch methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers of the loss landscape, whereas small-batch methods, benefiting from the inherent noise in gradient estimates, are guided towards "flat" minimizers. The paper demonstrates that these flatter minima correspond to solutions with better generalization ability. The evidence is presented through compelling visualizations of the loss landscape and a proposed metric to quantify the sharpness of a minimum.

### Decision and Key Reasons

I recommend **Accept**.

This decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper provides a clear, rigorous, and much-needed empirical study of a practical and significant problem in deep learning. It offers a compelling explanation for why large-batch training often fails to generalize well.
2.  Although the paper does not propose a novel algorithm, its findings have significant implications for both practitioners and theorists, potentially guiding the development of new optimization methods and a better theoretical understanding of the deep learning optimization landscape.

### Supporting Arguments

The problem of scaling deep learning training is of paramount importance, and increasing the batch size is the most direct way to leverage parallel hardware. However, the resulting loss of generalization has been a major practical barrier. This paper tackles this issue head-on, not by proposing a fix, but by seeking to understand the underlying cause.

The authors' core contribution is the strong empirical evidence linking the generalization gap to the geometry of the minima found by different training regimes. The experiments are thorough, spanning several modern network architectures (including variants of AlexNet and VGG) and datasets. The use of 1D parametric plots effectively visualizes the difference in sharpness between the solutions found by small-batch (SB) and large-batch (LB) methods. Furthermore, the proposed sharpness metric, while a heuristic, provides a quantitative tool that reinforces the visual intuition: LB solutions consistently exhibit sharpness values that are orders of magnitude higher than SB solutions.

The value of this work lies in its potential to influence future research. For theorists, these results highlight the importance of modeling the geometry of the loss surface beyond simply finding zero-gradient points, suggesting that assumptions about the "flatness" of desirable minima are well-founded in practice. For practitioners and algorithm designers, the paper provides a clear target: to develop large-batch methods that can escape the basins of attraction of sharp minima. This could inspire new heuristics, such as adaptive batch-sizing strategies or novel regularization techniques aimed at promoting flatness.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

I would like to commend the authors for their responsiveness during the review process. An earlier version of the paper made a claim about the scale-invariance of the proposed sharpness metric, which I had concerns about. This claim has been removed in the revised manuscript, which I believe makes the work more precise and robust. This addresses my main concern with the initial submission and strengthens the paper's contribution.
