- Paper empirically investigates how SGD mini-batch size relates to loss landscape geometry and generalization.
- Validates two central hypotheses: smaller batches lead to flatter/wider minima, and flatter minima correlate with better generalization.
- Primary strength: clear, extensive, and well-executed experiments that systematically connect batch size, sharpness, and generalization.
- Provides concrete empirical grounding for commonly held intuitions in the deep learning community.
- Main weakness: limited theoretical novelty—serves as empirical confirmation rather than proposing new theory or algorithms.
- Prior literature has discussed sharpness, batch size, and generalization; this work’s contribution is experimental clarity rather than conceptual innovation.
- The findings can guide theory by identifying realistic loss-surface properties relevant for future analyses.
- Practical impact: emphasizes batch size as a crucial hyperparameter and could inspire adaptive training heuristics to seek flatter minima.
- Revision note: authors removed an incorrect claim about scale-invariance of the sharpness metric and addressed the reviewer’s primary technical concern.
