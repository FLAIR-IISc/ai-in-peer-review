- Paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training degrades generalization, hypothesizing LB converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) finds flatter, better-generalizing minima due to gradient noise.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Addresses an important, practical problem with a clear, testable hypothesis about minimizer geometry.  
- Provides strong, multi-faceted empirical evidence across architectures and datasets.  
- Shows the generalization gap holds across six architectures and four datasets and is not explained by simple overfitting or early stopping.  
- 1D parametric visualizations vividly illustrate the geometric difference between LB and SB solutions.  
- Introduces a practical sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) to avoid computing the full Hessian.  
- Sharpness metric reports a 1–2 order-of-magnitude difference between LB and SB solutions, supporting the visual evidence.  
- Warm-starting LB with SB iterates demonstrates SB’s exploratory phase helps locate flat basins that LB can exploit.  
- Connects empirical results to existing theory about loss surfaces and Minimum Description Length (MDL).  
- Attempts to remedy the problem (Appendix E) highlight the difficulty of the issue and warrant greater prominence in the main text.  
- Suggested follow-up experiment: add explicit artificial noise to LB gradients to test if noise narrows the generalization gap.  
- Questions/suggestions for authors: sensitivity of sharpness metric to subspace dimension (p=100); consider framing hybrid/schedule-based batch-size strategies and summarize appendix remedy results in the main discussion.
