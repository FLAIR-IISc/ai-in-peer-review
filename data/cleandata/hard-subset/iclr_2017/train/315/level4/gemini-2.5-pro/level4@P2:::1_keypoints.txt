- Central claim: Large-batch (LB) methods converge to sharp minimizers that generalize poorly, while small-batch (SB) noise leads to flat minimizers with better generalization.  
- Main contribution: Empirical evidence reframing the generalization gap as a loss-landscape geometry issue rather than conventional overfitting.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strong empirical support: Thorough experiments across six architectures and multiple datasets showing consistent behavior.  
- Multiple evidence types: Visual 1D parametric plots, a novel quantitative sharpness metric, and batch-size threshold analysis all support the thesis.  
- Not due to overtraining: LB test performance does not degrade over time, so early stopping does not explain the gap.  
- Quantitative sharpness result: Sharpness metric shows one-to-two orders of magnitude difference between SB and LB solutions.  
- Warm-starting experiment: LB can generalize well only if initialized after SB exploration, implying SB gradient noise helps escape sharp basins.  
- Remedies tested: Data augmentation and robust training were explored but do not fully solve the problem.  
- Suggestion 1: Provide sensitivity analysis of the sharpness metric (dependence on p and optimization iterations).  
- Suggestion 2: Include comparative results for other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum) to show phenomenon is optimizer-agnostic.  
- Suggestion 3: Analyze the low-dimensional sharp subspace (e.g., Hessian eigenvectors) to better characterize sharp directions.  
- Question 1: Can adding controlled noise to LB gradients (decoupling batch size from noise) close the generalization gap?  
- Question 2: When warm-started, does LB converge to the same flat minimizer or a different flat minimizer within the same basin (i.e., how large are these basins)?  
- Question 3: Why choose p=100 for the random manifold in the sharpness metric, and might this miss sharp directions in very high-dimensional problems?
