This paper presents a valuable and thought-provoking analysis of optimization dynamics, and the core ideas are both interesting and useful for the community. The distinction drawn between the large-batch (LB) and small-batch (SB) training regimes is particularly insightful.

While the current analysis is compelling, the paper's conclusions could be significantly strengthened by further investigation into the relationship between these two regimes. The authors convincingly argue that the SB regime possesses certain advantages, which are often attributed to the inherent noise in the gradient estimates. A natural and impactful extension would be to conduct an experiment that attempts to explicitly bridge the gap between the two settings.

Specifically, the authors might consider an experiment where appropriately rescaled Gaussian noise is added to the gradients during training in the LB regime. The central question would be whether this injection of artificial noise allows the LB optimization process to recover the beneficial dynamics and performance characteristics of the SB regime.

Successfully demonstrating this would provide powerful evidence for the paper's central thesis—that gradient noise is a key differentiating factor between the two regimes. Such an experiment would elevate the work from an insightful observational study to one that also validates a causal mechanism, thereby making the paper's contribution substantially more robust and conclusive.
