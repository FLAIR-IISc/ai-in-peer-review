- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima while SB finds flatter minima, supported by empirical evidence.

- Decision: Accept.

- Addresses an important practical problem: enabling effective large-batch training for scalable deep learning.

- Strong empirical support: extensive experiments across six network configurations and four datasets validate the central claim.

- Clear framing and plausible hypothesis that differences in minima geometry explain the generalization gap.

- Effective use of parametric visualizations (Goodfellow et al.) to qualitatively show sharp vs. flat minima.

- Introduces a practical sharpness metric (Eq.4) that quantifies landscape geometry without computing the Hessian.

- Sharpness metric results (Tables 3–4) show LB solutions are 1–2 orders of magnitude sharper than SB solutions.

- Warm-starting experiments indicate SB’s noisy early phase explores and locates flat basins, after which LB can converge without a gap.

- Suggestion: directly test the noise hypothesis by adding rescaled Gaussian noise to LB gradients to emulate SB variance.

- Suggestion: investigate interaction with learning rate schedules and optimizer scaling to rule out learning-rate artifacts.

- Suggestion: summarize Appendix E findings (data augmentation, conservative training, etc.) in the main text to show common fixes don’t fully resolve sharpness.

- Question: Have the authors tried adding artificial noise to LB gradients to isolate gradient-variance effects?

- Question: Does the observation that sharpness is confined to a low-dimensional subspace suggest second-order or curvature-aware algorithms or isotropy-promoting methods to steer LB toward flatter solutions?

- Question: How sensitive are the sharpness metric results to choices of hyperparameters, particularly epsilon and the random subspace dimension p?
