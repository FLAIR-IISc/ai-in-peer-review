- Decision: Accept.  
- Problem: Large-batch training often yields worse generalization than small-batch training despite similar training loss.  
- Main claim: Large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers while small-batch noise drives solutions to flatter minimizers that generalize better.  
- Contribution: Provides strong, systematic empirical evidence across multiple architectures and datasets supporting the sharp-versus-flat hypothesis.  
- Methodology: Rigorous experiments controlling confounders, including tests against simple explanations like early stopping.  
- Visualization: Uses intuitive 1D visualizations to illustrate geometric differences between minima.  
- Metric: Introduces a novel, tractable sharpness metric (Equation 4) suitable for high-dimensional networks.  
- Quantitative results: Sharpness metric shows orders-of-magnitude differences between small-batch and large-batch solutions (Tables 3 & 4).  
- Warm-start experiment: Demonstrates that large-batch training can succeed if initialized in flat regions found by small-batch methods.  
- Practical relevance: Work advances understanding needed to make large-batch training viable for scalable parallel training.  
- Suggestion: Perform sensitivity analysis of the sharpness metric with respect to epsilon and the random projection matrix A.  
- Suggestion: Expand discussion on why explored remedies (e.g., data augmentation, conservative training) only partially mitigate the problem.  
- Suggestion: Broaden final discussion to theorize about loss-surface properties (e.g., basin volume/density) underlying the observations.  
- Question: Was using ADAM critical—do SGD with momentum or other optimizers show the same sharp/flat dichotomy?  
- Question: Could the inexact inner maximization (10 L-BFGS-B steps) bias the sharpness measurement toward large-batch solutions?  
- Question: How do total parameter-update counts compare between small-batch and large-batch runs when reporting final performance?
