Hypothesis: Large-batch training converges to sharp minima causing a generalization gap, while small-batch noise guides optimization to flat minima—supported by visualizations and metrics.

Addresses an important practical trade-off between computational efficiency and generalization with respect to batch size.

Presents an intuitive, clear, and testable hypothesis linking batch size, sharp/flat minima, and generalization.

Provides strong empirical support: well-designed experiments and loss-landscape visualizations that correlate batch size, sharpness, and test accuracy.

Ambiguity in the definition and measurement of “sharpness”; needs discussion of metric choice, invariance to reparameterization, and robustness.

Correlation vs. causation not settled; suggest experiments that explicitly find flat minima with large batches to test causal claims.

Unclear generality beyond CNNs/CIFAR and SGD with momentum—requires evaluation on RNNs/Transformers, NLP/RL tasks, and adaptive optimizers like Adam/RMSprop.

Requires deeper discussion connecting the sharp/flat minima perspective to other explanations (e.g., SGD noise as implicit regularization).

Overall assessment: well-written, insightful, and empirically strong work; recommend acceptance.
