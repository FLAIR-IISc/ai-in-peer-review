- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch, arguing LB converges to sharp minima while SB finds flat minima, supported by loss visualizations and a sharpness metric across architectures/datasets.
- Recommendation: accept, motivated by a thorough empirical study of an important practical problem and insights likely to impact future theory and practice.
- Strength: systematic, extensive experiments that convincingly support the central claim linking flat minima to better generalization in modern deep nets.
- Strength: clear 1-D visualizations provide intuitive comparisons between SB and LB minima.
- Strength: proposed sharpness metric gives quantitative evidence (despite being heuristic) that complements visual findings.
- Key empirical findings: identification of a threshold batch size where generalization degrades and warm-starting results showing LB can succeed if initialized in a flat basin.
- Practical/theoretical impact: results can guide training strategies (e.g., manipulating batch size) and inform theory about loss landscape properties relevant to generalization.
- Suggestion: better situate empirical findings within prior theoretical/conceptual work on flat vs. sharp minima (e.g., Hochreiter & Schmidhuber 1997) to clarify contribution.
- Suggestion: discuss properties and limitations of the sharpness metric, such as sensitivity to parameter rescaling.
- Suggestion: briefly discuss in the main paper why appendix remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) are only partial solutions.
- Question: experiments use ADAM—could optimizer choice (e.g., SGD with momentum) interact with batch size and affect ability to escape sharp regions?
- Question: how sensitive are sharpness measurements to the random subspace chosen (variation with different projection seeds)?
- Question: is there a relationship between the critical batch-size threshold and network or dataset properties (depth, width, dataset size, number of classes)?
