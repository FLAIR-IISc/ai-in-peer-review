This paper presents an empirical study to support two primary claims: 1) Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) with smaller batch sizes converges to flatter minima, and 2) these flatter minima correspond to solutions with better generalization performance.

While the paper offers limited novelty in its core ideas, its contribution is significant. The work's primary value lies in providing extensive empirical evidence that helps solidify long-held intuitions about the generalization of deep networks. By doing so, it sheds considerable light on important questions in the field.

The findings have the potential to impact both theory and practice. On the theoretical side, these results can help guide the development of new theories by suggesting which assumptions about the loss landscape are valid in practical settings. On the practical side, the insights could be used heuristically to develop new optimization algorithms that achieve better generalization through the strategic manipulation of mini-batch sizes.

I had previously raised a concern regarding the authors' claim that their proposed sharpness criterion was scale-invariant. This issue has been satisfactorily resolved in the revised manuscript, as the authors have retracted this claim.
