- Central claim: large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minimizers while small-batch (SB) training finds flat minimizers, explaining LB's worse generalization.  
- Main contributions: extensive empirical evidence, visualizations of the loss landscape, and a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Importance: addresses a significant, practical problem for large-scale training and potential mitigation of the generalization gap.  
- Experimental thoroughness: experiments across six network configurations (fully-connected and convolutional) convincingly demonstrate the phenomenon.  
- Controls: authors rule out simple overfitting explanations by showing LB test accuracy does not degrade with more training.  
- Visualization evidence: 1D parametric plots consistently show LB minima are sharper and narrower than SB minima.  
- Quantitative evidence: the proposed sharpness metric shows a consistent 1–2 order of magnitude difference between SB and LB solutions.  
- Warm-start experiment: initializing LB from SB-trained parameters shows SB exploration is key to reaching flat, well-generalizing basins.  
- Suggested experiment: test whether adding correctly-scaled noise to LB gradients closes the gap to confirm the role of gradient noise.  
- Metric robustness question: request sensitivity analysis of Metric 2.1 to the choice of epsilon (why epsilon=1e-3).  
- Confounding factor: compare SB and LB with equal total parameter updates to isolate the effect of update count versus gradient noise.  
- Remedies: Appendix E experiments (data augmentation, conservative training) are promising but preliminary; recommend integrating and quantitatively comparing these results in the main text.
