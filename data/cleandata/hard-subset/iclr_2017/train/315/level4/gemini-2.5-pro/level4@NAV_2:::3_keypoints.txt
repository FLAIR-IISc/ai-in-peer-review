- Decision: Accept.

- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) despite similar training loss.

- Core claim: LB methods converge to sharp minima while SB methods find flatter minima; this explains the generalization gap.

- Strong empirical support via loss-landscape visualizations across architectures and datasets.

- Introduces a practical, computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify minima sharpness.

- Demonstrates SB explores parameter space more effectively; includes informative warm-start experiments.

- Explores strategies to mitigate the LB generalization gap and reports mixed success.

- Strength: clear, focused, and empirically rigorous investigation of an important practical problem.

- Contribution: thorough empirical validation of the flat vs. sharp minima hypothesis and a usable sharpness metric.

- Suggestion: run LB with added, properly scaled noise (e.g., Gaussian) to directly test whether SB gradient noise causes flat minima.

- Suggestion: analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to hyperparameters (perturbation size ε and random subspace dimension p) and justify choice p=100.

- Suggestion: evaluate sharpness along multiple random directions to quantify anisotropy of sharpness around minima.

- Observation: Appendix E shows data augmentation can close the generalization gap but still yield sharp minima, which may complicate the sharpness-generalization link and should be discussed in the main text.

- Question: Does the warm-start result imply SB noise mainly helps during early exploratory training rather than local convergence?

- Question: Have you tried explicitly adding noise to LB gradients to simulate SB and isolate the effect of gradient noise?

- Question: How was the fixed number of L-BFGS-B iterations chosen for the sharpness maximization, and would more iterations change the relative sharpness conclusions?
