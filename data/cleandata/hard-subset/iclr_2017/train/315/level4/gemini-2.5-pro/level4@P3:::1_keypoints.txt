- Paper studies the generalization gap: large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minima, small-batch (SB) training to flatter minima due to gradient noise.  
- Empirical evaluation spans multiple architectures (FC, AlexNet-like, VGG-like) and datasets (MNIST, CIFAR, TIMIT).  
- 1D visualizations along the line connecting SB and LB solutions show LB solutions lie in much sharper basins.  
- Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric (max loss in a small neighborhood) that finds LB minima 1–2 orders of magnitude sharper.  
- Warm-starting LB from an SB solution closes the generalization gap, implying SB performs a crucial exploration phase.  
- Auxiliary plots (sharpness vs loss during training) visualize divergent optimization paths for SB and LB.  
- Explored remedies (data augmentation, robust training); they help but do not fully eliminate LB’s tendency to find sharp minima.  
- Decision: Accept — paper offers clear insight and strong empirical support for an important practical problem.  
- Key strengths: clarity of explanation, methodological rigor, and consistency of results across settings.  
- The sharpness metric (Eq. 4) is a valuable, practical tool for probing loss geometry in high dimensions.  
- Paper is well-written and experiments are clearly described with multifaceted support for conclusions.  
- Suggestion: justify the unconventional form of constraint set C and the dependence on weight magnitude in the sharpness metric.  
- Suggestion: compare SB and LB after the same total number of parameter updates (iterations) to rule out under-training of LB.  
- Observation: data augmentation substantially narrows the gap but resulting minima may still be relatively sharp, hinting sharpness isn’t the sole factor.  
- Questions for authors: motivate constraint set C and sensitivity to projection dimension p; report comparisons after equal iterations; comment or provide experiments on hybrid strategies (gradually increasing batch size).
