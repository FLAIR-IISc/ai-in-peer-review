- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training worsens generalization and links it to optimization behavior.  
- Main contribution: empirical evidence that LB converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) finds flatter minima, and flatter minima generalize better.  
- Comprehensive experiments across datasets and architectures support the hypothesis.  
- Clear visualizations: 1D parametric plots (Figure 3) show LB solutions lie in much sharper regions than SB solutions.  
- Proposes a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify minima sharpness.  
- Baseline results (Table 2) establish a consistent generalization gap across models.  
- Sharpness metric results (Tables 3–4) show order-of-magnitude differences between SB and LB sharpness.  
- Authors removed an earlier questionable scale-invariance claim, strengthening confidence in the metric.  
- Additional experiments (Figure 4 threshold, Figure 5 warm-starting) support the role of gradient noise in escaping sharp basins.  
- Remedies like data augmentation and adversarial/robust training partially help but do not fully close the LB gap.  
- Decision: Accept — paper is a rigorous, timely empirical study with high potential impact.  
- Paper is well-motivated and well-placed in literature on flat/sharp minima and MDL-style generalization theories.  
- Limited direct methodological novelty, but high value as a thorough empirical contribution and target for future optimization work.  
- Suggestion: analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to hyperparameters (especially neighborhood size epsilon).  
- Suggestion: discuss the low-dimensional subspace of steep directions (which layers/parameters contribute) to inspire follow-ups.  
- Suggestion: summarize negative results (data augmentation, adversarial training failures) more prominently in the main text.  
- Question: clarify the mechanism by which SB noise helps escape sharp basins and whether LB initialized in SB flat regions remains there.  
- Question/concern: how reliable is the sharpness maximization using only 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B — did authors test more iterations to rule out artifacts?
