- Decision: Accept.

- Central claim: Large-batch (LB) methods converge to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) methods find flat minima, explaining the generalization gap.

- Problem importance: Addresses a practical, widely observed deep-learning issue and provides a testable geometric explanation.

- Empirical breadth: Strong, diverse experiments across fully-connected and convolutional architectures and datasets.

- Visual evidence: Compelling 1-D parametric plots and curvilinear paths show LB minima in narrow valleys vs. SB in wide basins.

- Quantitative metric: Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Eq.4) that quantifies the visual intuition.

- Quantitative results: Tables report 1–2 orders of magnitude differences in sharpness between SB and LB solutions.

- Warm-starting insight: Warm-starting with SB training guides LB methods into flatter basins, suggesting staged strategies work.

- Noise explanation: SB gradient noise enables exploration farther from initialization and escape from sharp, nearby minima.

- Remedies tested: Data augmentation and conservative training help partially but do not fully resolve convergence to sharp minima.

- Improvement suggestion 1: Analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to hyperparameter epsilon and random projection A (ablation study).

- Improvement suggestion 2: Clarify the relationship between sharp minima and overfitting—sharpness may be a form of overfitting to local geometry.

- Improvement suggestion 3: Summarize remedy findings (Appendix E) in the main text, especially data augmentation effects.

- Questions for authors: Could gradually increasing batch size (curriculum) bridge the gap; are sharpness directions concentrated in specific parameter subspaces (layers/types); and might the L-BFGS-B maximization bias sharpness estimates?
