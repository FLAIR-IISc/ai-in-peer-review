- Central claim: large-batch (LB) training converges to "sharp" minima while small-batch (SB) training, via gradient noise, finds "flat" minima that generalize better.  
- Paper argues the generalization gap is due to the geometry of minima, not conventional overfitting.  
- Strong empirical evidence across multiple architectures (fully-connected, AlexNet-like, VGG-like) and datasets.  
- 1D loss landscape visualizations (Figure 3) clearly differentiate SB vs. LB basins.  
- Introduces a practical sharpness metric (Equation 4) that avoids computing the Hessian.  
- Quantitative results (Tables 3–4) show an order-of-magnitude higher sharpness for LB solutions.  
- Warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) indicates SB performs exploratory search, after which LB can exploit a good basin.  
- Evaluated remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) only partially close the generalization gap.  
- Paper is well-written, with a clear, testable hypothesis and convincing experimental design.  
- Recommendation: Accept — problem is practically important and the work is thorough and convincing.  
- Suggested experiment: add calibrated artificial noise (e.g., rescaled Gaussian) to LB gradients to test whether noise alone reproduces SB benefits.  
- Question: Have you run noisy-LB experiments to isolate gradient noise effects?  
- Question: Have you analyzed the low-dimensional "sharp" subspace (e.g., its correlation with specific layers or parameter types)?
