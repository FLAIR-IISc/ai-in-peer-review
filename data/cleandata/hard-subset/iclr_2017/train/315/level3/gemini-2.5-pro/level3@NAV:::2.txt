This paper presents a valuable and comprehensive empirical study investigating why large-batch training of deep neural networks often leads to poorer generalization compared to small-batch training. The authors provide compelling evidence for the hypothesis that this "generalization gap" is due to the different geometric properties of the minima found by each method. Their experiments systematically show that large-batch methods tend to converge to "sharp" minima, characterized by high sensitivity to perturbations in weight space, while the stochasticity of small-batch methods enables them to find "flat" minima, which are associated with better generalization. The claims are supported by extensive experiments across various network architectures and datasets, using both visualizations and a quantitative sharpness metric.

I recommend to **Accept** this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons:
1.  The paper provides strong, systematic empirical evidence for a widely-held but not rigorously-proven belief about the relationship between batch size, loss geometry, and generalization.
2.  The findings are impactful, offering guidance to both practitioners seeking better model performance and theorists working to understand generalization in deep learning.

The strength of this work lies in its rigorous empirical approach. The authors demonstrate their core claims across a commendable range of network architectures and tasks, lending credibility and generality to their conclusions. The use of both 1D visualizations and a quantitative sharpness metric provides a multi-faceted and convincing analysis. I also commend the authors for their scientific diligence; the provided notes indicate they addressed a prior concern about their sharpness metric by removing a contested claim, which increases my confidence in their methodology and results. While the paper introduces limited methodological novelty, its contribution to illuminating the mechanisms of generalization is significant. It helps formalize practitioner heuristics and suggests that future theoretical work on generalization should account for the geometry of minima.

To further improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions for the authors' consideration:
-   The central argument is that the *noise* from small mini-batches is the causal factor for finding flat minima. Have the authors considered experiments to decouple batch size from noise, for example, by training with a large batch but injecting a controlled level of artificial gradient noise? This could further strengthen the paper's main claim.
-   How sensitive are the results of the sharpness metric (Equation 4) to the choice of the neighborhood size ε? A brief discussion on this sensitivity would add robustness to the quantitative analysis.
-   The paper notes that small-batch methods explore more of the weight space. It may be interesting to briefly connect this observation to related concepts, such as Langevin dynamics, to provide a richer theoretical context.
