This paper provides a compelling investigation into the well-known "generalization gap" observed when training deep neural networks with large batch sizes. The authors present strong numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that this gap arises because large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers, while small-batch (SB) methods find flatter minimizers that generalize better. The work contributes a clear empirical characterization of this phenomenon across several network architectures and introduces a practical metric for quantifying the sharpness of a minimum, making the core claim testable and concrete.

I recommend **Accepting** this paper. The primary reasons are:
1.  The work addresses a significant and highly practical problem in deep learning; improving large-batch training is crucial for leveraging parallel computation to reduce training times.
2.  The central claim is substantiated by a thorough and well-designed set of experiments that convincingly link the generalization gap to the sharpness of the converged solution.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear methodology and robust empirical support. By employing both qualitative visualization techniques (parametric plots) and their proposed quantitative sharpness metric, the authors build a powerful case. They systematically show that for various networks, LB methods consistently find solutions with higher sharpness values, which correlates with poorer test accuracy, despite achieving similar training loss. The experiments are methodologically sound, using multiple random initializations and exploring different network types. The investigation rightly dismisses over-fitting as the primary cause and instead provides strong evidence for the role of the loss landscape's local geometry.

To further improve the paper, I would offer the following suggestions. These are intended to be constructive and are not essential for my recommendation to accept. The authors convincingly argue that the inherent noise in SB gradients allows optimizers to escape sharp basins of attraction. A natural experiment to directly test this causal link would be to investigate whether adding appropriately scaled artificial noise (e.g., rescaled Gaussian noise) to the large-batch gradients can replicate the benefits of the SB regime. Success in such an experiment would provide powerful evidence for the paper's central thesis and point towards a practical solution for improving LB training.

Finally, I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  Following the suggestion above, if artificial noise were added to LB gradients, how do you believe its variance should be scaled relative to the gradient norm, and should this scaling adapt as training progresses?
2.  The warm-starting experiment is very insightful. Do you have evidence to suggest whether the LB method, when initialized from an SB iterate, is simply fine-tuning within the same flat basin or if it is guided to a different, yet similarly flat, minimum?
