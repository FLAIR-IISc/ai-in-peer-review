- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) training, attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima while SB finds flat minima due to gradient noise.  
- Authors provide strong empirical evidence across architectures and datasets supporting the sharp-vs-flat minima hypothesis.  
- Introduce a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric (Eq. 4) to quantify minima sharpness beyond visual inspection.  
- Use parametric 1D plots and tables showing 1–2 orders-of-magnitude sharpness differences between LB and SB solutions.  
- Warm-starting experiment shows LB can reach flat minima if SB first explores the basin, suggesting SB noise aids exploration rather than LB incapability.  
- Authors rule out simple overfitting as the cause, showing test accuracy does not degrade with more training.  
- Decision: Accept — the paper addresses an important, practical problem with a clear, testable hypothesis and convincing empirical support.  
- Significance: reframes the generalization gap in geometric terms (sharp vs. flat minima), offering a new lens for optimization in deep learning.  
- Empirical rigor: thorough experiments on multiple models and a quantitative sharpness metric strengthen the claims.  
- Suggested experiment: add scaled zero-mean Gaussian noise to LB gradients to test causality and potentially close the generalization gap.  
- Minor suggestion: report sensitivity of the sharpness metric to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations used in the inexact maximization.  
- Question: Have you tried explicitly adding scaled noise to LB gradients, and if so, does it close the gap or reveal additional structure in SB noise?  
- Question: How sensitive is the sharpness metric to the choice of random projection matrix A and the projection dimension p?  
- Question: Given that loss rises steeply only along a small subspace near LB solutions, can adaptive optimizers be modified to counteract sharp directions (e.g., directional regularization or step-size adjustment)?
