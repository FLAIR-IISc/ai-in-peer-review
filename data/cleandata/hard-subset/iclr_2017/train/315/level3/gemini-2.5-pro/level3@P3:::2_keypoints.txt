- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training, attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima while SB finds flat minima.  
- Central contribution is an extensive empirical study linking batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization gap.  
- Authors propose a quantitative sharpness metric (Eq. 4) and provide qualitative visualizations to compare minima.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Provides a clear, convincing, and well-supported empirical explanation for an important practical problem.  
- Reason 2: Limited methodological novelty but high practical value—batch size acts like a regularizer and has theoretical implications.  
- Broad experimental validation across six architectures (fully-connected, AlexNet-like, VGG-like) and multiple datasets (e.g., CIFAR, TIMIT) ensures findings are robust.  
- 1-D visualizations along the line between SB and LB solutions (Figure 3) give intuitive evidence of sharpness differences.  
- Sharpness metric shows consistent, order-of-magnitude differences between LB and SB minima (Tables 3 and 4).  
- Warm-start experiments (Figure 5) indicate SGD noise from small batches helps exploration and escaping sharp basins.  
- Authors removed an unsubstantiated scale-invariance claim after review, showing responsiveness and improving correctness.  
- Appendix shows negative results: standard regularizers (data augmentation, adversarial training) don’t fully close the LB generalization gap, motivating new approaches.  
- Suggestion: Perform sensitivity analysis of the sharpness metric to epsilon and inner maximization iterations to confirm robustness.  
- Suggestion: Expand the MDL-related discussion to better connect flat minima intuition with theoretical grounding.  
- Suggestion: Emphasize the finding that sharpness is concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace—LB minima lie in narrow canyons, which has optimization implications.  
- Question: Have you correlated your sharpness metric with Hessian-based curvature measures (e.g., top Hessian eigenvalues via Lanczos) to validate the heuristic?  
- Question: Have you tried a curriculum/batch-size schedule (start small, increase later) to retain flat minima while gaining LB efficiency?  
- Question: Why do techniques like data augmentation and adversarial training fail to fully close the generalization gap—do they only smooth locally without steering away from sharp regions?
