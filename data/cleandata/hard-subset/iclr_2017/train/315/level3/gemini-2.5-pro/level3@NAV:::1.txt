This paper presents an insightful and engaging empirical study into the well-known "generalization gap" observed when training deep neural networks with large batches. The authors provide compelling evidence for the hypothesis that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function, whereas small-batch (SB) methods find flatter minimizers that generalize better. The key contributions are a thorough experimental validation of this phenomenon across multiple network architectures and a novel, computationally feasible metric for quantifying the sharpness of a minimum, which is a valuable tool for analyzing high-dimensional loss landscapes where Hessian computation is intractable. This work provides a clear and intuitive explanation for a practical problem, offering significant value to the deep learning community.

I strongly recommend to **Accept** this paper. My decision is based on two key reasons: 1) The work addresses a critical and practical problem in scaling deep learning training, providing a well-supported explanation for why simply increasing batch size hurts performance. 2) The empirical investigation is rigorous and persuasive, using effective visualizations and a thoughtful new metric to robustly support its central claims.

The supporting arguments for this decision are clear from the paper's methodology. The authors systematically demonstrate the generalization gap and then use 1D parametric plots to visually confirm that LB solutions reside in much sharper basins of the loss landscape compared to SB solutions. The proposed sharpness metric reinforces these visualizations with quantitative data, showing a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sensitivity between the minima found by the two regimes. The experiments are conducted on a variety of network types, adding to the generality of the findings. The "warm-starting" experiment is a particularly clever demonstration of how the exploratory nature of SB methods is necessary to find a good basin of attraction before LB methods can effectively optimize within it.

To help improve the paper, I offer the following suggestions. The proposed sharpness metric is a core contribution but is heuristic. The paper would be strengthened by a brief discussion of its sensitivity to the choice of the random projection matrix A, the dimension p, and the number of L-BFGS iterations used for the maximization. Furthermore, the appendix contains valuable experiments on attempts to fix the LB problem (e.g., data augmentation). A brief mention in the main text that these methods reduce the gap but do not solve the underlying sharpness issue could strengthen the motivation for the paper's open questions.

Finally, I have two questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:
1.  Since the paper posits that noise in SB methods is key to finding flat minima, have you experimented with adding artificial gradient noise to LB methods to see if this can bridge the generalization gap and lead to flatter solutions?
2.  Your sharpness metric is calculated by inexactly solving a maximization problem. Could the observed difference in sharpness be an artifact of this inexactness—i.e., is it simply easier to find a direction of ascent from a sharp minimum in a few iterations?
