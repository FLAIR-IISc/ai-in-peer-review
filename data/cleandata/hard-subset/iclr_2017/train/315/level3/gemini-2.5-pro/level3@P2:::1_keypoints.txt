- Investigates why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) training, attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima while SB finds flat minima.
- Presents extensive empirical evidence across architectures and datasets supporting the sharp-vs-flat minima hypothesis.
- Uses 1D loss visualizations to qualitatively show LB solutions lie in sharper basins than SB solutions.
- Introduces a practical, computationally feasible sharpness metric that quantitatively shows LB solutions are orders of magnitude sharper.
- Argues that gradient noise in SB training helps escape sharp basins and discover flatter, more generalizable regions.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Addresses an important, practical problem for scaling deep learning.
- Key reason 2: Central hypothesis is well supported by rigorous, convincing experiments combining qualitative and quantitative evidence.
- Experiments are broad and robust: six network architectures, four datasets, repeated trials, and clear 1D parametric plots.
- Sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is a valuable contribution offering a Hessian-free way to measure landscape sharpness.
- Warm-start experiments show a few SB epochs can guide LB to flat minima, suggesting the early noise-driven phase is critical.
- Suggest clarifying the solution-dependent constraint set C in the sharpness metric and motivating that design choice.
- Ask whether sharpness differences persist when LB and SB are run for the same number of parameter updates rather than epochs.
- Recommend briefly mentioning in the main text that proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) are partially effective but incomplete.
- Ask if batch-size annealing (start small, then increase) was tried and whether the sharpness metric correlates with Hessian largest eigenvalues (e.g., via power method).
