- Empirical study showing large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) finds flatter minima that generalize better.  
- Introduces a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric for high-dimensional loss landscapes where Hessian is intractable.  
- Thorough experimental validation across multiple network architectures, with effective visualizations (1D parametric plots) confirming LB solutions lie in sharper basins.  
- Quantitative results: sharpness metric shows a 1–2 order-of-magnitude difference in sensitivity between LB and SB minima.  
- Warm-starting experiment demonstrates SB exploration is necessary to locate good basins before LB optimization performs well.  
- Strong accept recommendation based on addressing a practical scaling problem and providing rigorous, persuasive empirical evidence.  
- Core contribution: clear, intuitive explanation for the generalization gap that is valuable to the deep learning community.  
- Suggestion: discuss sensitivity of the sharpness metric to choice of random projection matrix A, projection dimension p, and number of L-BFGS iterations.  
- Suggestion: briefly mention in the main text that attempted fixes (e.g., data augmentation) reduce but do not eliminate the LB–SB gap (currently in appendix).  
- Question: have the authors tried adding artificial gradient noise to LB methods to see if that yields flatter solutions and bridges the generalization gap?  
- Question: could the observed sharpness difference be an artifact of inexactly solving the maximization—i.e., is it easier to find ascent directions near sharp minima in few iterations?
