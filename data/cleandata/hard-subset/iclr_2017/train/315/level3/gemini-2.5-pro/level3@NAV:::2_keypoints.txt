- Empirical study investigating why large-batch training often generalizes worse than small-batch training.  
- Main claim: the generalization gap arises from different geometry of minima found by each method.  
- Experiments show large-batch methods converge to sharp minima with high sensitivity to weight perturbations.  
- Stochasticity from small-batch methods helps find flat minima associated with better generalization.  
- Claims supported by extensive experiments across multiple architectures and datasets.  
- Analysis uses both 1D visualizations and a quantitative sharpness metric.  
- Reviewer recommends Accept based on strong, systematic empirical evidence and impact for practitioners and theorists.  
- Strength: rigorous empirical approach and breadth of experiments increase credibility and generality.  
- Strength: authors addressed a prior concern by removing a contested claim about the sharpness metric.  
- Limitation: limited methodological novelty, but significant contribution to understanding generalization.  
- Suggestion: run experiments that decouple batch size from noise (e.g., large-batch plus injected gradient noise).  
- Suggestion: discuss sensitivity of the sharpness metric to the neighborhood size ε.  
- Suggestion: connect small-batch exploration observations to concepts like Langevin dynamics for theoretical context.
