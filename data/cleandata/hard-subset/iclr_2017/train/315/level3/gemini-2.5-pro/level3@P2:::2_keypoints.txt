- Investigates why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) training, attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima while SB finds flat minima.  
- Provides extensive empirical evidence across architectures/datasets, visualizations of loss landscapes, and a novel sharpness metric.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strong empirical characterization linking batch size, sharpness of minima, and generalization performance.  
- Results have practical impact (batch-size tuning, scheduling) and theoretical implications (importance of loss-geometry assumptions).  
- Focused, well-executed empirical study addressing an important practical problem (enabling efficient LB training).  
- 1D parametric plots (Fig.3) give clear visual intuition for differences in loss landscape between LB and SB.  
- Proposed sharpness metric (Eq.4) is a useful heuristic showing order-of-magnitude sharpness differences between LB and SB.  
- Experiments cover multiple fully-connected and convolutional architectures (including AlexNet/VGG variants) and datasets, strengthening generality of findings.  
- Authors rule out simple overfitting/early-stopping explanations for the generalization gap.  
- Warm-start experiments suggest SB undergoes an exploration phase to find flatter regions before settling.  
- Suggest more analysis of sharpness metric sensitivity to perturbation size (epsilon) and random subspace dimension (p).  
- Note that data augmentation can close the generalization gap while still producing sharp minima—this nuance deserves discussion in the main text.  
- Highlight that LB sharpness appears concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace; recommend expanding this observation.  
- Question: Have you tried dynamic batch-sizing (start small, increase) and how does it affect final generalization and training efficiency?  
- Question: How was the low-dimensional sharp subspace measured, and does its dimensionality correlate with network size, batch size, or the generalization gap?  
- Question: Have you directly analyzed gradient-noise properties (e.g., variance) during SB “exploration” and settling to link gradient statistics to solution geometry?
