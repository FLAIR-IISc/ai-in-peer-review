### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates a significant and widely-observed problem in deep learning: the degradation in generalization performance when training with large mini-batches (LB) compared to small mini-batches (SB). The authors provide a compelling and well-supported empirical investigation, claiming that this "generalization gap" is not due to conventional overfitting but rather to the different types of minimizers found by each regime. The core contribution is the strong numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that LB methods tend to converge to *sharp minimizers* of the loss landscape, while SB methods, aided by the inherent noise in their gradient estimates, consistently find *flat minimizers*. The paper compellingly argues that these flat minima generalize better. To support this claim, the authors use visualization techniques (parametric 1D plots) and introduce a novel, computationally feasible metric for quantifying the "sharpness" of a minimum. The experiments are conducted across a variety of network architectures and datasets, lending significant weight to their observations.

### **Decision: Accept**

This is a clear Accept. The paper addresses a fundamentally important, practical, and still poorly understood issue in the optimization of deep neural networks. The central hypothesis is clear, well-motivated, and supported by a strong and convincing set of empirical results.

1.  **Significance and Clarity:** The work tackles the critical problem of how to scale deep learning training effectively. By moving the conversation from simply observing the generalization gap to providing a testable hypothesis about the geometry of the solution (sharp vs. flat minima), the paper provides a valuable new lens through which to view the problem.
2.  **Empirical Rigor:** The claims are backed by thorough experiments on multiple architectures (fully connected, AlexNet-like, VGG-like). The introduction of a quantitative sharpness metric is a key strength, allowing the authors to move beyond qualitative visual arguments and show a consistent order-of-magnitude difference between the solutions found by SB and LB methods.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strength lies in its focused and methodical approach to validating its central thesis. The observation that LB methods converge to sharp minima while SB methods find flat ones is not entirely new in spirit, but the quality and directness of the evidence presented here are exceptional. The parametric plots in Figure 3 provide a striking visual intuition for the phenomenon. More importantly, the sharpness metric defined in Equation 4 provides a quantitative tool that reinforces this visual intuition across all tested models. The results in Tables 3 and 4, showing a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness, are highly persuasive.

Furthermore, the "warm-starting" experiment (Figure 5) is a particularly clever piece of evidence. It suggests that LB methods are not inherently incapable of finding flat minima, but rather that they lack the exploratory capability to escape the basin of attraction of a nearby sharp minimum. When an SB method first performs this exploration and leads the iterates into the basin of a flat minimum, the LB method can then successfully converge to a high-quality solution. This elegantly supports the idea that the noise in SB gradients acts as a crucial exploration mechanism.

The authors also do a good job of ruling out simpler explanations, such as standard overfitting, by showing that test accuracy does not degrade with further training (Figure 2). The investigation into potential remedies in the appendix, while not fully successful, further strengthens the paper by showing the non-triviality of the problem and preemptively addressing obvious solutions like data augmentation.

### **Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper**

These suggestions are intended to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical for my decision to accept.

The paper's central thesis is that the inherent noise in SB methods is the key mechanism for finding flat minima and thus achieving better generalization. This suggests a very direct and powerful experiment to further test the hypothesis: artificially injecting noise into the large-batch gradient computations. A natural extension would be to add zero-mean Gaussian noise to the LB gradients, where the variance of this noise is scaled to approximate the gradient variance observed in the small-batch regime. If adding this rescaled noise to the LB method successfully closes the generalization gap and leads to flatter minima (as measured by your sharpness metric), it would provide near-conclusive evidence for your causal claim. This would transform the paper's conclusion from a strong correlation to a demonstration of causation, significantly increasing its impact. It would also provide a direct, practical strategy for improving LB training.

A minor point: The sharpness metric in Eq. 4 relies on an inexact maximization using L-BFGS-B. While practical, it would be useful to briefly comment on the sensitivity of the results to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations. Reassuring the reader that the core finding (orders-of-magnitude difference) is not sensitive to this hyperparameter would be valuable.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Following the suggestion above, have you experimented with adding explicit, scaled noise to the large-batch gradients to mimic the stochasticity of small-batch training? If this simple strategy works, it would be a powerful confirmation of your hypothesis. If it doesn't, what might that suggest about the nature of SB gradient noise that isn't captured by simple isotropic Gaussian noise?

2.  The sharpness metric in Eq. 4 is a key part of your quantitative evidence. Could you comment on the sensitivity of this metric to the choice of the random projection matrix `A` and the dimension `p`? Does the observed order-of-magnitude difference between SB and LB solutions hold robustly across different random projections?

3.  You note that the loss function near an LB solution rises steeply only along a small dimensional subspace. This is a fascinating observation. Does this suggest that adaptive optimization methods (like ADAM, which you used, or others) could be modified to specifically counteract this? For instance, could one use information about sharpness to increase regularization or decrease step sizes along these "sharp" directions?
