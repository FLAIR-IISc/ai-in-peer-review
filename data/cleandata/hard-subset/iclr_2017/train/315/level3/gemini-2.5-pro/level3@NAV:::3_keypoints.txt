- Central claim: the large-batch generalization gap occurs because LB methods converge to sharper minima while SB methods find flatter minima that generalize better.
- Strong numerical evidence provided across multiple network architectures supporting the sharp-vs-flat hypothesis.
- Introduces a practical quantitative metric for measuring the sharpness of a minimum, making the claim testable.
- Shows LB and SB achieve similar training loss but LB solutions have higher sharpness and worse test accuracy.
- Recommendation: Accept the paper.
- Reason 1: Addresses an important, practical problem—improving large-batch training for scalable parallel computation.
- Reason 2: The central claim is well substantiated by a thorough, well-designed experimental suite.
- Strengths: clear methodology, qualitative visualizations (parametric plots), the sharpness metric, multiple random initializations, and experiments on diverse network types; convincingly rules out overfitting as the primary cause.
- Suggested improvement: test causality by adding appropriately scaled artificial noise (e.g., rescaled Gaussian) to LB gradients to see if SB benefits can be replicated.
- Questions for authors: how should added noise variance be scaled relative to gradient norm and adapt over training; and does LB initialized from an SB iterate fine-tune within the same flat basin or move to a different similarly flat minimum?
