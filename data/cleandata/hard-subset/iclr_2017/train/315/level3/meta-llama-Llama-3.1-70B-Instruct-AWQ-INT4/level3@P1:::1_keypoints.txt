- Paper presents a compelling analysis of the drawbacks of large-batch methods in deep learning and explains the generalization gap.  
- Authors investigate causes of the generalization gap and provide insights into underlying mechanisms.  
- Work is well-motivated and follows a rigorous analytical approach.  
- Provides a clear, thorough explanation of SGD and its commonly used variants.  
- Claims are supported by numerical experiments across multiple deep learning architectures.  
- Uses visualizations (parametric plots) and introduces a heuristic sharpness metric to quantify minimizer sensitivity.  
- Main finding: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers with many large positive Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Small-batch methods tend to converge to flatter minimizers, which generalize better.  
- Noise in stochastic gradients helps escape basins of sharp minimizers and bias toward flatter solutions.  
- Explores mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) that help but do not fully resolve the problem.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reasons for acceptance: compelling analysis and clear community value in explaining the generalization gap.  
- Supporting points: rigorous methodology, thorough SGD exposition, and empirical evidence.  
- Suggested improvements: provide more experimental details (architectures, hyperparameters) and discuss practical implications.  
- Suggested future directions: explore additional remedies such as dynamic sampling or adaptive batch sizes.  
- Questions for authors: provide experimental setup details, practical application guidance, and ideas for other effective mitigation strategies.
