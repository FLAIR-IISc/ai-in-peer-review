- Paper studies why large-batch optimization generalizes worse than small-batch methods in deep learning.  
- Main claim: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers of training/testing functions, causing poorer generalization.  
- Accepted because the paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature.  
- Accepted because it provides strong numerical evidence supporting its claims.  
- Uses one-dimensional parametric plots and perturbation measures across multiple architectures as primary evidence.  
- Approach is appropriate, experiments are well-designed, and the paper is well-written and accessible.  
- Results are convincing; parametric plots and perturbation measures effectively illustrate sharp vs. flat minimizers.  
- Authors discuss implications and suggest potential remedies for poor large-batch generalization.  
- Major criticism: paper focuses mainly on numerical experiments with limited theoretical analysis.  
- Suggested experiment: test whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients in large-batch regimes recovers small-batch benefits.  
- Requests for clarification: why large-batch tends to find sharp minima, relation to prior loss-surface work (e.g., Choromanska et al., Soudry & Carmon), and potential applications beyond deep learning.
