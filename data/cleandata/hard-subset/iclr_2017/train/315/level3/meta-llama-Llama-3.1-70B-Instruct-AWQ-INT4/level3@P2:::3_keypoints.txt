- Paper studies why larger SGD batch sizes degrade model quality, focusing on convergence to sharp vs. flat minimizers.  
- Main finding: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (worse generalization), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers.  
- Authors attribute this difference to inherent noise in gradient estimation for small batches.  
- Evidence combines numerical experiments (parametric plots, sharpness metrics, test accuracy) and some theoretical analysis.  
- Experiments provide convincing numerical support for the convergence and generalization claims.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and well-placed within the existing SGD/optimization literature.  
- Limitation: analysis is primarily empirical with limited comprehensive theoretical proof of large-batch convergence to sharp minimizers.  
- Suggested improvement: test whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to large-batch gradients recovers small-batch benefits.  
- Questions for authors: provide more details on architectures and datasets used in experiments.  
- Questions for authors: assess whether results generalize to other optimizers (e.g., Adam, RMSProp).  
- Questions for authors: provide deeper theoretical insights linking large-batch convergence to sharp minimizers with existing optimization theory.  
- Overall decision: accept — paper is a valuable contribution to understanding SGD and deep learning generalization.
