- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch, showing large-batch converges to sharp minimizers while small-batch finds flat minimizers with better generalization.
- Decision: Accept.
- Paper is well-motivated, places work in context with a thorough literature review.
- Provides numerical evidence and theoretical insights supporting the sharp-vs-flat minimizer claim.
- Experiments are well-designed and results are clearly presented and analyzed.
- Discusses potential remedies for large-batch generalization issues (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization).
- Suggests future work: test adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients in large-batch regimes.
- Requests more details on computational cost (time and memory) of proposed methods.
- Asks for deeper theoretical discussion on the relationship between sharp minimizers and generalization.
- Question: Provide computational cost details (time/memory) for methods.
- Question: Have you tried adding rescaled Gaussian noise to large-batch gradients to mimic small-batch benefits?
- Question: Can you expand on theoretical implications linking sharp minima to poor generalization?
- Minor: Clarify why 10% of training data was chosen as the large-batch size in Section 2.2.1.
- Minor: Provide more details and hyperparameters for the warm-starting experiments in Section 3.
