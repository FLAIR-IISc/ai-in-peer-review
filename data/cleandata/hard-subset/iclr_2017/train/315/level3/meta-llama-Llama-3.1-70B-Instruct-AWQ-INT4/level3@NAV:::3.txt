**Paper Review**

**Summary**

This paper investigates the cause of the generalization gap in deep learning models trained with large-batch methods. The authors present numerical evidence that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training function, which leads to poor generalization. In contrast, small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers, which generalize better. The paper also explores several strategies to remedy the generalization problem of large-batch methods, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with the following key reasons:

1. The paper tackles a specific and important problem in deep learning, namely the generalization gap in large-batch methods.
2. The authors provide a well-motivated approach, including a thorough analysis of the literature and a clear explanation of their methodology.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is well-written and easy to follow, with a clear structure and concise language. The authors provide a thorough analysis of the literature on deep learning and stochastic gradient descent, and their approach is well-motivated by the need to understand the generalization gap in large-batch methods.

The numerical experiments presented in the paper are convincing and provide strong evidence for the authors' claims. The use of parametric plots and sharpness metrics is particularly effective in illustrating the differences between small-batch and large-batch methods.

**Additional Feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Consider investigating whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the large-batch regime can yield benefits of the small-batch regime. This could provide additional insight into the role of noise in generalization.
* Provide more details on the computational resources required for the experiments, as this could be relevant for practitioners who wish to replicate the results.
* Consider including more discussion on the implications of the results for practical applications of deep learning.

**Questions for the Authors**

* Can you provide more details on the choice of hyperparameters for the experiments, such as the learning rate and batch size?
* How do you think the results would change if different optimizers were used, such as SGD or RMSProp?
* Can you provide more insight into why the large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers, and whether this is a fundamental property of the loss landscape or a result of the optimization algorithm?
