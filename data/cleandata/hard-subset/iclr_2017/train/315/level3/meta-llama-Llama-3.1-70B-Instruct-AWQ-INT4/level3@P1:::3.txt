This paper tackles the specific question of why large-batch methods tend to generalize poorly compared to small-batch methods in deep learning. The authors investigate the cause of this generalization drop in the large-batch regime and present numerical evidence that supports the view that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the training and testing functions, which leads to poorer generalization.

I decide to accept this paper with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, addressing a significant problem in deep learning. Secondly, the paper provides strong numerical evidence to support its claims, including one-dimensional parametric plots and perturbation measures for a variety of deep learning architectures.

The authors' approach is well-motivated, and their use of numerical experiments to investigate the cause of the generalization drop is appropriate. The paper is also well-written and easy to follow, making it accessible to a wide range of readers.

The results presented in the paper are convincing, and the authors provide a clear explanation of their findings. The use of parametric plots and perturbation measures is effective in illustrating the difference between sharp and flat minimizers. The authors also provide a clear discussion of the implications of their findings and suggest potential remedies for the poor generalization of large-batch methods.

One potential criticism of the paper is that it focuses primarily on numerical experiments, and some readers may have preferred to see more theoretical analysis. However, the authors' use of numerical experiments is effective in illustrating the problem and providing insight into the cause of the generalization drop.
