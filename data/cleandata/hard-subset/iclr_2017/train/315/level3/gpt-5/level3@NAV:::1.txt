1. Summary of contributions
This engaging paper investigates why large-batch training degrades generalization. It offers a clear, empirically grounded explanation: large batches tend to converge to sharp minima, while small batches find flatter ones due to gradient noise facilitating exploration. The authors substantiate this with: (i) 1D and curvilinear loss-surface visualizations between SB/LB solutions, (ii) a computationally feasible sharpness proxy via local maximization in full space and random subspaces, showing 1–2 orders of magnitude differences, (iii) evidence of a batch-size threshold where generalization drops and sharpness rises, and (iv) warm-start experiments indicating that LB succeeds when SB first discovers a flat basin. The paper also discusses mitigations (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), and motivates the practical significance with a performance model. The work is well positioned relative to SGD literature, flat vs. sharp minima, and recent analyses of deep loss surfaces.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, practically important question with broad community relevance; (2) Strong, carefully designed empirical evidence supporting a plausible, well-motivated mechanism for the large-batch generalization gap.

3. Supporting arguments
- The problem is crisply formulated and placed within prior work (SGD stability, sharp/flat minima, visualization methods). The motivation for exploring sharpness as the mediator is compelling.
- Results are consistent across six architectures and multiple optimizers, with repeated trials. The warm-start and threshold experiments add mechanistic insight rather than mere correlation.
- The sharpness metric, while approximate, is justified and computed both in the ambient space and random subspaces to mitigate directionality artifacts; findings align with Hessian-spectral intuitions.
- The negative results on several remedies are valuable to the community, and the dynamic-batch suggestion is well supported by warm-start outcomes.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve, not central to the decision)
- Clarify confounders: match either number of parameter updates or total epochs across SB/LB; report learning-rate schedules, weight decay, and momentum/Adam hyperparameters and their tuning fairness.
- Analyze sensitivity of conclusions to ε and p in the sharpness metric; add scale-invariant flatness proxies (e.g., Hessian spectral norm via power iteration, PAC-Bayes-based measures) to address reparameterization concerns, especially with batch normalization.
- Report per-dataset absolute batch sizes (10% can be very large) and relate thresholds to gradient-noise scale.
- Consider additional modern architectures and datasets, and a gradual batch-size schedule experiment to directly test the proposed remedy.

Questions for the authors
- Did you equalize the number of update steps and/or tune learning-rate schedules separately for each batch size (e.g., linear scaling, warmup)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- How robust are sharpness gaps to ε, p, and the optimizer used in the inner maximization?
- Can you provide Hessian spectral norm or top-eigenvalue estimates at SB vs. LB solutions to corroborate Metric 2.1?
- How do batch normalization and weight rescaling affect your sharpness conclusions? Are results invariant under common reparameterizations?
- Can you quantify the batch-size “threshold” via a noise-scale metric and test dynamic batch-size growth end-to-end?
