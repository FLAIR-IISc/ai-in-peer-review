The paper tackles a timely and practically important question: why do large-batch (LB) training regimes often yield worse generalization than small-batch (SB) regimes, despite achieving similar training losses? It claims, and provides systematic empirical evidence, that LB methods tend to converge to sharper minima, while SB methods consistently find flatter minima—likely due to gradient noise enabling exploration and escape from basins of sharp minima. The authors propose a computationally tractable sharpness metric, use one-dimensional and curvilinear parametric plots to visualize loss geometry around LB and SB solutions, show a batch-size threshold beyond which generalization drops, and demonstrate that warm-starting LB from an SB trajectory can recover flat minima and good test accuracy. They also evaluate several remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), which narrow but do not close the gap, and suggest dynamic sampling as a promising direction.

Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (1) the paper addresses an important and widely observed phenomenon with careful, multi-architecture empirical study; (2) it offers a coherent explanation linking batch size, gradient noise, and loss landscape geometry, supported by multiple diagnostics (sharpness metric, path plots, warm-start and batch-size sweep experiments).

Supporting arguments:
- Problem specificity: The paper focuses squarely on the generalization gap in LB training, an issue that constrains parallel scalability and training efficiency. The question is clearly formulated.
- Motivation and placement: The work builds on known roles of gradient noise in SGD, recent analyses of nonconvex landscapes with many minima of similar value, and practical observations about batch-size thresholds. It usefully connects these strands by hypothesizing and testing the “sharp vs. flat minima” mechanism. The discussion of MDL/Bayesian perspectives on flatness adds intuition.
- Evidence and rigor: The experiments span multiple datasets and architectures (AlexNet/VGG-like CNNs and MLPs), use repeated trials, and report both training and test behavior. The parametric plots clearly exhibit sharper basins around LB solutions. The sharpness metric, while approximate, shows 1–2 orders-of-magnitude differences between LB and SB solutions in both full space and random subspaces, and the warm-start experiments are especially compelling in linking gradient noise to flat-minima discovery. The batch-size threshold plots further substantiate the mechanism.
- Claims vs. data: The paper does not overclaim theory; it presents numerical evidence consistent with the sharp-minima hypothesis and carefully explores alternative remedies, acknowledging their partial success.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper, not essential to the accept decision):
- Sharpness metric: Provide sensitivity analysis w.r.t. epsilon and the subspace dimension p; report variance across different random A matrices. Consider corroborating with top Hessian eigenvalue estimates via Hessian–vector products and Lanczos on smaller models to validate the proxy metric.
- Invariance concerns: The notion of sharpness can be affected by reparameterizations and per-layer rescaling (especially with BN). Discuss this limitation explicitly and consider per-layer normalized sharpness or scale-invariant alternatives.
- Optimizer confound: Most results use ADAM; although you note similar outcomes for other optimizers, adding explicit SGD-with-momentum curves and sharpness values would strengthen generality.
- Hyperparameters and schedules: Clarify learning-rate schedules, momentum, weight decay, and any warmup; LB performance can be sensitive to these choices. Reporting a fair, tuned LB baseline (e.g., learning-rate scaling with batch size and warmup) would make the gap quantification more robust.
- Noise injection as a remedy: Explore injecting calibrated Gaussian noise into LB gradients to mimic SB gradient variance. A simple scheme is to add η ~ N(0, σ^2 I) with σ scaled so that Var[g_LB + η] ≈ Var[g_SB], e.g., σ ∝ (1/√B_SB − 1/√B_LB) times a per-parameter estimate of gradient standard deviation. Anneal σ over training or couple it to the learning rate. This could combine LB parallelism with SB-like exploration.
- Characterizing sharp directions: You observe steep rise along a small subspace. Can you identify whether these directions concentrate in specific layers (e.g., classifier head) or parameters (e.g., BN scales)? This may inform architecture- or layer-wise remedies.

Questions for the authors:
1) How sensitive are your sharpness conclusions to the choice of epsilon and to p in the random subspace? Can you report distributions of φ across multiple A and show correlations with test accuracy?
2) Have you tried scale-normalized or per-layer normalized sharpness to mitigate reparameterization effects (e.g., invariance under weight scaling with BN)? Do your qualitative conclusions persist?
3) Can you provide explicit SGD-with-momentum baselines (accuracy and sharpness) for LB and SB across the same setups to rule out optimizer-specific behavior?
4) In the batch-size sweep, how thoroughly were learning rates and schedules tuned per batch size? Does linear LR scaling with batch size and warmup reduce the gap for your models?
5) For the warm-start experiments, can you quantify the “exploration” phase by showing how gradient variance, distance from initialization, and sharpness evolve with epochs? Is there a reproducible epoch threshold after which LB converges to flat minima?
6) Have you tested gradient noise injection in LB training? If so, does calibrating noise to match SB gradient variance close the gap without harming training speed?
7) What is the run-to-run correlation between your sharpness metric and test accuracy across seeds and architectures? A scatter plot with correlation coefficients would strengthen the sharpness–generalization link.
8) Your results suggest sharpness is concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace. Can you estimate its effective dimension (e.g., count of top eigenvalues above a threshold) and how it scales with model size and batch size?
9) Data augmentation improves LB generalization but leaves sharpness high. Is the residual sharpness predictive of sensitivity to distribution shift (e.g., corrupted or out-of-domain data)?
10) Could dynamic batch-size schedules (small early, large late) be formalized and shown to match SB accuracy with LB efficiency in your setups?

Overall, this is a well-executed empirical study on an important problem, with clear, actionable insights and promising directions for making LB training viable at scale.
