Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles a practically important and widely observed phenomenon: the drop in generalization when training deep networks with large mini-batches. It provides a clear empirical narrative that large-batch (LB) training tends to converge to sharp minima, while small-batch (SB) training tends to find flatter minima, and connects sharpness with poorer test performance.
- It offers multiple lines of evidence across architectures and datasets: (i) 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots that visually demonstrate sharp vs. flat minima; (ii) a computationally feasible “sharpness” metric based on local worst-case perturbations; (iii) batch-size sweep experiments showing a threshold beyond which accuracy degrades and sharpness rises; and (iv) warm-start experiments showing that LB training can succeed when initialized near a flat region located by SB.
- The paper also evaluates several practical mitigations (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) and reports that while they can narrow the gap, they generally do not eliminate sharpness or fully restore generalization, motivating future work (e.g., dynamic sampling).
Overall, the paper is engaging, carefully executed, and provides clear value to the community by diagnosing a key obstacle to scaling deep learning.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence for an important, practically consequential claim: LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima associated with poorer generalization, while SB methods find flatter minima.
- The study is well-motivated, well-situated in the literature, and the conclusions are supported by consistent results across multiple models/datasets and complementary diagnostics.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:
1) Specific question/problem: Why do large-batch training methods often generalize worse than small-batch methods, despite achieving similar training loss? The paper seeks both an explanation and potential remedies.
2) Motivation and placement: The work is motivated by the need to scale training through parallelism (which favors larger batches) and by long-standing empirical observations of a generalization gap. It is positioned within relevant strands: optimization properties of SGD, landscape analyses, flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber), MDL/Bayesian interpretations, and practical deep learning. The related work is appropriate and up-to-date for the venue.
3) Do the results support the claims? Yes, within the paper’s empirical scope. The parametric plots consistently show qualitatively sharper basins for LB solutions. The proposed sharpness metric, while heuristic, shows 1–2 orders of magnitude differences between SB and LB optima, in both full space and random subspaces. The warm-start and batch-size threshold experiments give a compelling operational story: gradient noise helps SB escape sharp basins; LB lacks this exploratory push unless started in flatter regions. Multiple architectures/datasets and optimizers were considered, and trends are robust.

Additional strengths:
- Clear articulation of hypotheses and careful elimination of simpler alternatives (e.g., generalization gap is not due to late overfitting).
- Practical implications are thoughtfully discussed, including a scalability model and the prospect of dynamic batch sizing.

Limitations and concerns (do not preclude acceptance):
- The sharpness metric is heuristic and depends on parameterization and the choice of neighborhood. While the authors strive for invariance and use both full-space and random-subspace versions, the measure can still be sensitive to reparameterizations (e.g., scaling symmetries with ReLU/BatchNorm) and to the particular box used and the inexact inner maximization (10 L-BFGS-B steps).
- Hyperparameter fairness remains a potential confound. It is not fully clear whether LB experiments used learning-rate schedules tuned for LB (e.g., scaling rules, warm-up), and whether the number of parameter updates vs. number of epochs was matched across regimes. The authors train “until the loss ceases to improve,” but differences in update counts can affect noise/injectivity and, thus, sharpness.
- While the paper evaluates ADAM primarily and notes similar trends with other optimizers, details and results for each alternative optimizer are limited in the main text. Given known optimizer-dependent generalization behaviors, a more thorough presentation would strengthen the case.
- The link between sharpness and generalization is argued via MDL/Bayesian intuition and empirical correlation, but there is no formal guarantee or causal test. This is understandable for a paper of this scope, but worth noting.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful feedback)
- Provide more detail on hyperparameter tuning per batch size: initial LR, LR schedule, momentum/Adam betas, weight decay, gradient clipping, and whether linear LR scaling or warm-up were tried for LB. Include ablations showing sensitivity to these choices.
- Report the number of parameter updates and total compute per regime; include a “matched updates” setting to disentangle exploration from step-count differences.
- Complement the sharpness metric with spectral estimates (e.g., top Hessian eigenvalue via power/Lanczos on a few models) to corroborate the conclusions and reduce sensitivity to the box constraint/optimizer in the inner maximization.
- Discuss parameterization effects explicitly (e.g., effects of BN scale/shift, ReLU rescaling symmetries) and whether sharpness is measured in function space vs. parameter space.
- For the remedies, detail the exact adversarial/stability training settings; consider dynamic sampling experiments directly (increasing batch size over training) given the warm-start results.
- Include broader comparisons across optimizers (SGD with momentum, RMSProp, etc.) in the main text with tabulated sharpness/accuracy to show the optimizer-robustness of the findings.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters and schedules: How were learning rates and schedules chosen for each batch size? Did you try linear LR scaling and/or warm-up for LB? Are results robust to these choices?
- Update parity: What happens if you fix the number of parameter updates (rather than epochs) across SB and LB, or vice versa? Does the generalization gap persist under matched-update budgets?
- Optimizer dependence: Could you provide more complete results for SGD with momentum and ADAGRAD in the main text (accuracy and sharpness), including any tuning you performed?
- Sharpness metric sensitivity: How sensitive are the sharpness results to the choice of ε, the dimension p of the random subspace, and the number of iterations used in the inner maximization? Can you report variance across different random A?
- Parameterization effects: Given BN and ReLU rescaling symmetries, have you examined whether simple reparameterizations around a solution materially change the sharpness value while leaving function values similar?
- Dynamic sampling: Based on the warm-start findings, have you tried a curriculum that gradually increases batch size during training? If so, how does sharpness evolve, and can LB eventually match SB generalization without SB pretraining?
