- Central claim: Large-batch (LB) training converges to sharper minima than small-batch (SB) training, and sharpness correlates with worse generalization.

- Contributions:
  - 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots showing sharper curvature near LB minima.
  - A computationally feasible sharpness metric (full-space and random-subspace) that separates LB vs SB by 1–2 orders of magnitude.
  - Systematic experiments across multiple architectures and datasets.
  - Warm-start experiments showing SB noise helps escape sharp basins and LB can work when initialized near flat minima.
  - Initial mitigation attempts (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial training) with only partial success.

- Importance: Timely, practically relevant problem with valuable framing and actionable insights (e.g., dynamic sampling, warm starts).

- Decision: Weak accept.

- Key reasons for decision:
  - Problem importance and a broad, carefully executed empirical study revealing a robust phenomenon (LB → sharper minima → generalization gap).
  - Convincing qualitative and quantitative evidence across models/datasets; hypothesis that gradient noise aids exploration is supported by warm-start and batch-size threshold experiments.

- Supporting arguments: Appropriate motivation/placement in literature; consistent evidence from parametric plots, sharpness metrics, warm-starts; multiple architectures and optimizers (Adam primary) strengthen external validity; training loss similar but test accuracy differs.

- Limitations: Sharpness metric is heuristic and sensitive to parameterization/scale (especially with batch norm); link to generalization is correlational not causal; some confounders not fully controlled (per-batch hyperparameter tuning, compute/update budget parity, optimizer choice).

- Control and ablation suggestions: Ensure fair compute/update budgets, tune LR/momentum/weight decay per batch size (consider linear scaling + warmup), and include SGD+momentum as a primary baseline with full results.

- Sharpness characterization suggestions: Test invariance to rescalings/reparameterizations (ReLU/BN symmetries), consider layer-wise normalization or function-space perturbations, compute partial Hessian/top eigenvalues and anisotropy, and quantify whether sharpness is concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace.

- Gradient-noise and dynamics suggestions: Measure gradient-noise scale (covariance/trace) vs batch size, explore dynamic batch sizing, and try injecting Gaussian noise into LB gradients matched to SB noise.

- Regularization/data suggestions: Test label smoothing, mixup, weight decay tuning across batch sizes; examine batch-norm variants (ghost/synchronized/none) and their interaction with sharpness.

- Reporting suggestions: Release code and exact protocols, report confidence intervals/statistical tests, and include more optimizer-specific results in the main text.

- Questions for the authors:
  1) How sensitive is the sharpness metric to layer-wise rescaling (ReLU/BN symmetries)?  
  2) Were LR/momentum/weight decay tuned per batch size, and how do LR scaling/warmup affect conclusions?  
  3) Are comparisons made under equal updates, equal gradient evaluations, or equal wall-clock time, and does the gap persist when matched?  
  4) Can you include full results for SGD+momentum with tuned schedules and weight decay?  
  5) Can you report gradient-noise covariance/trace across batch sizes during training and relate it to observed thresholds?  
  6) Have you injected Gaussian noise into LB gradients (variance matched to SB) and does it reduce sharpness/close the gap?  
  7) Do top Hessian eigenvalues and their mass differ markedly between SB and LB minima?  
  8) How do results change with ghost BN/synchronized BN or without BN? Could BN-scale effects impact sharpness measurements?  
  9) How far must SB training proceed before an LB switch consistently succeeds (in loss/distance/sharpness), and can dynamic batch schedules operationalize this?
