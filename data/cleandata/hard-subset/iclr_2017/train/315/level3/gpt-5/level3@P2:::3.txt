Summary and contributions
- The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training, despite achieving similar training loss. The central empirical claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers (high sensitivity of loss in a neighborhood), whereas SB methods converge to flatter minimizers, and that sharpness correlates with poorer generalization.
- The authors provide: (i) 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions showing sharper curvature near LB minima; (ii) a computationally feasible sharpness metric based on local worst-case perturbations (with both full-space and random-subspace variants) that differentiates SB vs LB solutions by 1–2 orders of magnitude; (iii) systematic experiments across several architectures (fully-connected, AlexNet-like, VGG-like) and datasets; (iv) warm-start experiments suggesting SB noise helps escape sharp basins and that LB can succeed when initialized near flat minima; and (v) initial attempts to mitigate the gap (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), with partial but insufficient improvements.
- The question is important, timely, and practically relevant for scaling deep learning. The paper’s framing, breadth of empirical evidence, and actionable insights (e.g., dynamic sampling, warm starts) are valuable to the community.

Decision: Weak accept
Key reasons:
1) Problem importance and broad, carefully executed empirical study that reveals a robust and practically meaningful phenomenon (LB → sharper minima → generalization gap).
2) Convincing qualitative and quantitative evidence across models/datasets, plus a clear hypothesis (gradient noise aids exploration) that is supported by warm-start and batch-size threshold experiments.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The work is well-motivated by systems/parallelism needs, and positioned relative to literature on SGD noise, saddle avoidance, loss landscape properties, and Bayesian/MDL perspectives on flat vs sharp minima. The discussion of related theory is appropriate for an empirical paper.
- Evidence and rigor: The parametric plots, sharpness metrics in full and random subspaces, and warm-start studies consistently align with the stated hypothesis. Using multiple architectures and optimizers (with Adam as primary) strengthens external validity. The observation that LB and SB achieve similar training loss but different test accuracy is documented clearly.
- Limitations: The sharpness metric is heuristic and may be sensitive to parameterization and scale (especially with batch normalization), and the link to generalization is correlational rather than causal. Some confounders are not fully controlled (e.g., per-batch-size hyperparameter tuning, compute/update budget parity, optimizer choice). Nonetheless, the consistency of the findings and the set of negative results for several attempted fixes make the main claim credible.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Control and ablations
  - Ensure fair compute/update budgets: compare SB vs LB under equal numbers of gradient evaluations or equal wall-clock targets; also report results under equal numbers of parameter updates (iterations).
  - Hyperparameter tuning per batch size: systematically tune learning rate, momentum/β, weight decay, and learning-rate schedules for each batch size; report sensitivity. Consider linear scaling + warmup for LB.
  - Optimizer breadth: complement Adam with SGD+momentum (commonly used in large-scale vision) as the primary baseline; include full results for ADAGRAD/SGD/adaQN mentioned.
- Sharpness characterization
  - Assess invariance of the sharpness metric to rescalings/reparameterizations (especially under ReLU/BN scaling symmetries). Consider normalizing by layer-wise scales or evaluating sharpness in function space (e.g., output perturbation).
  - Complement the heuristic metric with partial Hessian information (top eigenvalues via power iteration/Lanczos) and visualize anisotropy (eigenvalue spectra) at SB vs LB solutions.
  - Report whether sharp directions align with specific layers/parameters and whether sharpness concentrates in a low-dimensional subspace (the paper mentions ~5%; quantify this systematically).
- Gradient noise and dynamics
  - Measure the gradient noise scale (trace of covariance or norm variance) vs batch size during training; relate thresholds in Fig. 4 to noise magnitude.
  - Explore dynamic batch sizing more directly (gradually increasing batch size as loss decreases) and report generalization vs sharpness.
  - Investigate explicit noise injection in LB training: add rescaled Gaussian noise to the gradients with variance matched to the empirical SB gradient noise (e.g., proportional to 1/B or calibrated to the observed covariance). This could emulate SB’s exploratory benefits while retaining LB parallelism.
- Regularization and data
  - Include experiments with label smoothing, mixup, and weight decay tuning across batch sizes; analyze effects on sharpness.
  - Examine the role of batch normalization (per-batch statistics vs ghost batch norm) in LB settings and its interaction with sharpness.
- Reporting
  - Provide code and exact training protocols. Report confidence intervals and statistical tests where feasible. Include more optimizer-specific results in the main text.

Questions for the authors
1) Parameterization invariance: How sensitive is your sharpness metric to layer-wise rescaling (e.g., under ReLU/BN symmetries)? Have you tried scale-normalized variants or function-space perturbations?
2) Hyperparameter control: For each batch size, did you perform thorough learning rate/momentum/weight decay tuning? How do your conclusions change under LR scaling and warmup for LB?
3) Compute budget: Are comparisons made under equal numbers of updates, equal gradient evaluations, or equal wall-clock time? Does the generalization gap persist under matched budgets?
4) Optimizer effects: You state other optimizers show similar behavior; can you include full results for SGD+momentum with tuned schedules and weight decay?
5) Gradient noise: Can you measure and report the gradient noise covariance (or its norm/trace) across batch sizes and over training, and relate it to the observed thresholds?
6) Noise injection: Have you tried injecting Gaussian noise into LB gradients, with variance matched to the SB gradient noise (or scaled as lr/B)? Does this reduce sharpness and close the gap?
7) Hessian spectra: Beyond the heuristic metric, do top Hessian eigenvalues (and their mass) differ markedly between SB and LB minima in your settings?
8) Batch normalization: How do results change with ghost BN/synchronized BN or without BN? Could BN-induced scale effects impact sharpness measurements?
9) Warm-start: Can you quantify how far (e.g., in loss, distance, or sharpness) SB must progress before an LB switch consistently succeeds? Can dynamic batch schedules operationalize this?
