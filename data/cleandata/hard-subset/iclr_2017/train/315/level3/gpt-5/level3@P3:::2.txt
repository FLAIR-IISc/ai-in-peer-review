Summary of contributions
- The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse test accuracy than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training loss. The central empirical result is that LB methods tend to converge to sharper minima, while SB methods converge to flatter minima; flatter minima, in turn, correlate with better generalization.
- The study combines 1D/curvilinear loss visualizations and a computationally tractable sharpness metric to quantify sensitivity around solutions. It further documents a batch-size “threshold” beyond which generalization degrades and shows that warm-starting an LB run from an SB trajectory can recover flatter minima and better generalization.
- While methodological novelty is limited, the work provides timely, careful, and broadly applicable empirical evidence that helps illuminate generalization in deep networks, and suggests assumptions that may be legitimate when developing new theory.
- Practical implications include simple heuristics (e.g., tuning batch size over training, warm starts) for steering optimization toward flatter minima. The authors also explore data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial/robust training, finding partial improvements but persistent sharpness.
- Earlier concern: a reviewer noted the proposed sharpness criterion was not scale-invariant as initially claimed. Resolution: the authors removed the scale-invariance claim in revision, resolving the concern.

Decision
Accept. Reasons: (1) Strong, well-executed empirical evidence on a question of high practical and theoretical importance; (2) Clear implications for both algorithm design (batch-size manipulation) and the development of generalization theory, despite limited methodological novelty.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three review questions:
1) Specific question/problem: Why does increasing mini-batch size often harm generalization in deep learning? The paper asks whether the phenomenon is tied to the geometry of the attained minima, specifically sharp vs. flat minima.
2) Motivation and placement in literature: The problem is highly relevant due to parallelism constraints and the widespread observation of a “generalization gap” with large batches. The work is well-situated amid prior optimization and generalization literature (SGD/stability, saddle-point avoidance, loss landscape properties, MDL/Bayesian interpretations). Exploring sharp/flat minima is a natural, well-motivated lens, and the paper connects to related empirical visualization work.
3) Do the results support the claims? Yes, at the empirical level:
   - Multiple architectures/datasets show consistent accuracy gaps favoring SB, with LB converging to sharper minima per the proposed metric and 1D/curvilinear plots.
   - The warm-start and batch-size-threshold experiments are particularly compelling: they directly link gradient noise magnitude and the ability to escape sharp basins.
   - Attempts to “fix” LB training shift accuracy somewhat but leave minima relatively sharp, reinforcing the main thesis.
   - The earlier concern about “scale invariance” of the sharpness measure has been resolved by removing that claim.
Caveats/limits:
   - The sharpness metric depends on ε, the random subspace A, and the inexact inner maximization; results could be sensitive to these choices. The authors partly mitigate this by reporting across values and subspaces and by consistent qualitative outcomes.
   - The metric is not invariant to common reparameterizations (e.g., layer-wise rescaling, BatchNorm), so “flatness” is parameterization-dependent. The paper is careful not to over-claim after revision, but this remains a limitation.
   - Learning-rate schedules and optimizer hyperparameters (especially with Adam) can interact with batch size and noise; stronger controls and ablations would further strengthen causal claims.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not decisive for my recommendation)
- Sharpness metric:
  - Report sensitivity analyses for ε, number of L-BFGS-B steps, and p (subspace dimension). Include confidence intervals over different A draws.
  - Consider complementing the metric with approximate top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos to triangulate “sharpness.”
  - Discuss parameterization dependence more explicitly; consider normalized/scale-aware sharpness (e.g., filter-wise or layer-wise normalization) to reduce trivial rescaling effects.
- Experimental controls:
  - Clarify and systematize learning-rate schedules, weight decay, and momentum across batch sizes, and report whether linear LR scaling or noise-preserving LR adjustments were attempted.
  - Compare by equal number of parameter updates and by equal wall-clock budgets, not only by training-to-convergence, to separate optimization speed from generalization behavior.
  - Include results with plain SGD/momentum as the primary optimizer (Adam’s adaptivity can interact with batch size in nontrivial ways), ideally matching modern best practices.
- Theory connections:
  - Tighten the bridge to MDL/Bayesian arguments: e.g., PAC-Bayes-inspired flatness/complexity measures, or empirical posterior volume estimates.
  - Frame explicit assumptions (e.g., prevalence of many near-optimal minima with diverse curvature) that your results suggest are reasonable targets for future theory.
- Practical guidance:
  - Expand on dynamic batching (increase batch size over training) and warm starts with concrete recipes.
  - Summarize actionable heuristics in a short “practitioner’s box.”

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are your sharpness conclusions to ε and p in the sharpness metric, and to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations in the inner maximization? Can you provide quantitative robustness checks?
2) Did you attempt learning-rate scaling policies that preserve gradient-noise scale when changing batch size? If so, how did they affect sharpness and test accuracy?
3) How do results change under SGD with momentum and carefully tuned schedules vs. Adam? Are the sharp vs. flat trends preserved?
4) Can you report top-1/2 Hessian eigenvalues (via power iteration) at SB vs. LB solutions to corroborate the sharpness metric?
5) How do BatchNorm and other scale symmetries affect your conclusions? Have you tried scale-normalized sharpness measures?
6) In the warm-start experiments, what fraction of training (epochs/updates) is typically required before an LB run can “lock onto” a flat basin? Does a gradually increasing batch size reproduce this effect reliably?
7) Beyond data augmentation and conservative training, did you try weight decay schedules, label smoothing, or noise injection (e.g., additive gradient noise) to emulate SB noise with LB? How did these affect sharpness and generalization?
8) Can you provide code and detailed hyperparameter logs to improve reproducibility and facilitate broader testing across architectures/datasets?
