Summary and contributions
- The paper studies why training with large mini-batches often yields worse test performance than training with small mini-batches. The central claim is that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to sharp minima, while small-batch (SB) methods, due to gradient noise, tend to land in flatter minima; flatter minima generalize better.
- The authors provide multiple empirical probes: (i) 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions showing much sharper basins around LB solutions; (ii) a computationally tractable “sharpness” criterion based on maximization over a small neighborhood (full space and random subspaces); (iii) batch-size sweep revealing thresholds beyond which generalization degrades and sharpness increases; (iv) warm-start experiments showing that LB methods can achieve high test accuracy when initialized near flat minima found by SB; and (v) attempts to mitigate the gap (data augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial training), which help but do not remove sharpness.
- The work is timely and practically relevant. It offers a coherent empirical narrative linking mini-batch-induced noise, basin geometry, and generalization, and it suggests research directions (e.g., dynamic batch sizing, methods that bias toward flatness).

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
- Key reasons: (1) Clear empirical evidence across architectures and datasets that connects batch size to geometry and generalization; (2) The study is well motivated and fills an important gap despite limited methodological novelty. The earlier concern about a claimed scale-invariance property of the sharpness metric has been addressed; the claim was removed.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles a concrete and influential question in deep learning practice: why does generalization degrade with large batches? The motivation is strong (parallelization and training-time benefits) and well positioned in the literature (SGD theory, loss landscape studies, MDL/Bayesian perspectives).
- Empirical support: The evidence is consistent and multifaceted. The parametric plots provide an intuitive visualization; the sharpness metric shows 1–2 orders-of-magnitude differences between SB and LB solutions; warm-start experiments demonstrate a plausible mechanism whereby gradient noise enables escape from sharp basins before LB refines; and batch-size sweeps reveal thresholds consistent with a noise-exploration picture.
- Rigor and correctness: While the sharpness measure is heuristic and resource-limited (inexact inner maximization), the authors check both full-space and random-subspace variants, explore multiple epsilons, multiple networks/datasets, and optimizers. Importantly, they no longer claim scale invariance for the metric, resolving a correctness concern. The conclusions are appropriately framed as empirical evidence rather than formal proofs.
- Impact: The results offer guidance for both theory (supporting assumptions about wide valleys and generalization) and practice (heuristics like warm-starting and dynamic batch sizing; caution about using very large batches without biasing toward flatness).

Weaknesses and limitations
- Limited methodological novelty: The main contribution is empirical characterization; the metric is a practical proxy rather than a new theoretical tool.
- Metric sensitivity and parameterization dependence: The sharpness measure depends on epsilon, the random subspace, and network parameterization. Although the removed scale-invariance claim helps, more analysis of sensitivity and invariance would strengthen the argument.
- Hyperparameter fairness: Most results use ADAM with fixed batch sizes (SB=256, LB=10% of data). It remains unclear how results depend on optimizer (e.g., SGD with momentum), learning-rate/batch-size scaling rules, warmup schedules, weight decay, and batch norm settings. Without rigorous tuning parity, some confounding remains possible.
- Scope of experiments: Hessian spectra are not directly estimated; batch-size sweeps are shown for only two networks; and the mitigation strategies, while informative, are preliminary.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive; not all affect my decision)
- Provide sensitivity analyses: vary epsilon over orders of magnitude, report variance across random subspaces A, and examine the relationship to top Hessian eigenvalues via power iteration/Lanczos on selected layers to validate the proxy metric.
- Control for optimization confounders: compare ADAM vs SGD+momentum; apply batch-size–learning-rate scaling and warmup heuristics; report per-setting hyperparameter tuning and compute budgets. Consider equalizing total number of processed examples or wall-clock to control for training budget.
- Parameterization issues: discuss how sharpness changes under common rescalings (e.g., with batch norm or weight normalization). Consider normalized/scale-aware variations of the metric (e.g., per-layer rescaling or path-normalized measures).
- Extend batch-size sweeps and warm-starts: show the threshold phenomenon across all architectures; evaluate dynamic batch-size schedules more directly (not only via piggyback LB).
- Causality probes: add experiments that explicitly control gradient noise (e.g., small-batch with added Gaussian noise vs noise-reduced large-batch) to better isolate the role of noise in escaping sharp basins.
- Clarify the metric definition: the normalization (division by f(x)+) and the construction of the box using A+ could be explained and motivated more clearly; include ablations of the box design.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are your sharpness conclusions to the choice of epsilon and the dimension of the random subspace? Please provide variance across multiple A and a sweep over epsilon.
2. Did you evaluate SGD with momentum using batch-size–learning-rate scaling and warmup schedules? If so, do the sharpness and generalization trends persist?
3. How do weight decay and batch normalization hyperparameters affect the sharpness gap? Can you report results without batch norm to reduce scale-related confounds?
4. Can you approximate top Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., via power iteration) at SB vs LB minima to directly corroborate the sharpness proxy?
5. In your warm-start experiments, how far (in parameter distance) are the LB endpoints from the SB solutions? Does LB ever move from a flat basin to a nearby sharper basin during refinement?
6. How robust are your findings across different stopping criteria (fixed epochs, equal number of gradient evaluations, or early stopping on validation)?
7. The conservative (proximal) training improved accuracy but did not reduce sharpness. Could you combine it with explicit noise injection or entropy-regularized objectives (e.g., Entropy-SGD) and report whether LB can be steered to flat minima?
8. For data augmentation experiments, does the LB model remain sharper when measured on augmented validation distributions (i.e., does sharpness reduce when the effective data distribution widens)?

Overall, this is a valuable, careful empirical study that will interest the ICLR community and stimulate both theory and method development around batch size, landscape geometry, and generalization.
