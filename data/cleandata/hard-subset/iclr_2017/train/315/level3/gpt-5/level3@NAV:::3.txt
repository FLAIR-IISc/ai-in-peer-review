Summary and contributions
This paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training of deep networks often generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) training. It advances the thesis—supported by careful experiments across multiple architectures and datasets—that LB methods tend to converge to sharper minima (with larger local curvature) than SB methods, whose inherent gradient noise biases them toward flatter minima. The paper introduces a practical sharpness metric, uses parametric (linear and curvilinear) loss-surface visualizations, and runs warm-start experiments that suggest dynamic sampling as a remedy. Preliminary attempts (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) reduce but do not close the gap. The work is timely, clearly written, and useful to both practitioners and researchers interested in scalability.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper tackles an important and widely observed phenomenon with a well-motivated hypothesis grounded in prior literature and presents broad, coherent empirical evidence that is likely to influence practice and subsequent research.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity and motivation: The generalization gap in LB training is articulated crisply and placed in the context of SGD noise, MDL, and Bayesian perspectives, with appropriate citations.
- Evidence: Consistent findings across six networks and several optimizers; 1–2 orders-of-magnitude differences in the sharpness metric; parametric plots that are visually compelling; warm-start experiments that meaningfully probe causality and suggest dynamic batch-size schedules.
- Rigor and transparency: Experimental setups are described, multiple trials are run, and code is promised for key plots. While the sharpness metric is heuristic, its relationship to leading Hessian eigenvalues is discussed, and results align with theory that many minima have similar training loss but different geometry.

Suggestions to improve (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Investigate LB+noise: Add rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during LB training, calibrated to match the covariance or trace of SB gradient noise (e.g., SGLD/temperature schedules). Compare test accuracy and sharpness to SB, Entropy-SGD, and prior gradient-noise injection.
- Strengthen controls: Match optimization budget fairly (updates vs epochs vs examples), and report LR/momentum/weight-decay tuning for SB vs LB (including linear warmup and LR scaling).
- Geometry: Complement the sharpness metric with top-Hessian eigenvalues via power iteration/Lanczos to validate curvature differences.
- Invariance: Assess flatness measures that are more reparameterization-invariant (e.g., accounting for filter rescaling/BN) to guard against confounds.
- Reproducibility: Release full training scripts and hyperparameter sweeps.

Questions for the authors
- When SB and LB use matched total updates and carefully tuned LR schedules, how does the gap change?
- Does LB+rescaled Gaussian noise (matched to SB gradient covariance) recover SB-like generalization and flatness?
- How sensitive are results to weight decay, momentum, and BN statistics (train/eval) across batch sizes?
- Can you report top-1/2 Hessian eigenvalues at SB vs LB solutions to corroborate the sharpness metric?
- How does the “batch-size threshold” scale with model size and dataset size, and does dynamic sampling cross that threshold reliably?
