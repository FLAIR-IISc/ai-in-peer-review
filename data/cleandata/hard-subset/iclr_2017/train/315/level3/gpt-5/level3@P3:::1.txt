Summary of contributions
The paper tackles a timely and practically important question: why do large-batch (LB) training methods often yield worse generalization than small-batch (SB) methods in deep learning? The authors provide a clear, engaging empirical study that (i) documents a consistent generalization gap as batch size increases, (ii) links this gap to convergence to sharp minima via visualizations and a computable sharpness proxy, (iii) shows that SB noise appears to help escape basins leading to sharp minima while LB lacks this exploration, and (iv) probes several potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/robust training), finding partial but insufficient fixes. Additional insights include parametric and curvilinear loss landscape plots between LB and SB solutions, a warm-start experiment indicating that LB can succeed after an SB exploration phase, and evidence of batch-size thresholds where generalization degrades and sharpness rises. The work is well framed in existing literature (Goodfellow visualizations, MDL/Bayesian views, optimization theory) and delivers practical value to the community thinking about scalability and distributed training.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper offers a clear, well-motivated, and carefully executed empirical investigation that isolates a plausible mechanism (attraction to sharp minima) behind a widely observed and important phenomenon (LB generalization gap).
- The results are consistent across multiple architectures/datasets and lead to actionable guidance (e.g., dynamic sampling/warm starts), providing immediate value to practitioners and stimulating further theoretical work.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The core question—why LB training generalizes worse despite similar training loss—is crisply posed. The study disambiguates possible causes and provides evidence for the “sharp vs. flat minima” explanation.
- Motivation and placement: The need to scale training motivates LB; prior observations of generalization gaps and theoretical work on loss landscapes motivate the investigation. The paper situates itself well with references to SGD stability, saddle-point avoidance, MDL/Bayesian perspectives, and visualization techniques.
- Support for claims:
  - Parametric and curvilinear plots show steeper curvature around LB solutions relative to SB solutions for both train and test loss.
  - A sharpness metric (local sensitivity via constrained maximization in full space and random subspaces) exhibits a 1–2 order-of-magnitude difference between LB and SB minima across networks and datasets.
  - Warm-start experiments indicate that once SB discovers flatter regions, LB can converge there and close the generalization gap, consistent with the exploration-by-noise hypothesis.
  - Batch-size sweep experiments reveal thresholds beyond which generalization drops and sharpness rises, aligning with the proposed mechanism.
  - Attempts to mitigate (data augmentation, conservative proximal updates, adversarial/stability training) help but do not eliminate sharpness or fully close the gap, reinforcing the conclusion.
  - Scientific rigor: While the work is empirical rather than theoretical, it uses multiple, complementary diagnostics, multiple seeds, and varied architectures. The sharpness metric is justified and tied to Hessian spectra qualitatively, though not computed exactly due to cost.

Points limiting strength (do not overturn the main contribution):
- The sharpness proxy can be sensitive to choices of epsilon, the subspace dimension p, and reparameterizations; scale invariance (especially with batch norm) is not fully addressed.
- Most experiments use ADAM; broader coverage of optimizers and tuned learning-rate schedules for LB (e.g., scaling and warmup) would strengthen conclusions.
- Exact Hessian information (e.g., top eigenvalues via Lanczos/power iteration) is not reported to directly corroborate curvature differences.
- Some experimental controls (number of epochs vs. number of parameter updates, fixed regularization/weight decay, treatment of batch-norm statistics under different batch sizes) could be better standardized.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Report sensitivity analyses for the sharpness metric: vary epsilon, p, and the number of L-BFGS-B steps; discuss reparameterization effects (e.g., scaling layers, batch norm gamma/beta).
- Complement the proxy with estimates of the top few Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., stochastic Lanczos), and consider per-layer curvature to localize sharp directions.
- Include controlled comparisons with SGD + momentum under batch-size-aware learning-rate schedules and warmup; equalize the number of parameter updates and wall-clock budgets where feasible.
- Explore dynamic batch-size schedules (small-to-large) explicitly, beyond warm-starts, to test the proposed remedy more systematically.
- Examine how batch normalization’s running statistics are handled under SB vs. LB; mismatches can affect generalization independently of curvature.
- Provide more details and code for full reproducibility: exact hyperparameters, stopping criteria, data preprocessing, and the sharpness-optimization routine.
- Investigate whether sharp directions concentrate in specific layers (e.g., classification head) and whether regularizers (e.g., decoupled weight decay, label smoothing) reduce sharpness without hurting accuracy.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are your sharpness measurements to epsilon, subspace dimension p, and the number of inner optimization steps? Can you quantify variance across these settings?
- Did you evaluate the top Hessian eigenvalues (or trace) at SB and LB solutions to directly corroborate curvature differences?
- How were learning rates and schedules chosen for LB vs. SB, and did you try batch-size-aware scaling or warmup? Are results robust under such tuning?
- How are batch normalization statistics computed during training and evaluation for different batch sizes? Could mismatched BN statistics contribute to the gap?
- Are the conclusions consistent when matching the number of parameter updates (iterations) rather than epochs, and when standardizing weight decay and other regularizers?
- Do the sharp directions align with specific layers or parameter groups? Is sharpness reduced by per-layer adaptive regularization?
- In the warm-start study, can you quantify when SB transitions from exploration to convergence (e.g., via gradient-noise scale), and whether that transition predicts LB success?
- Have you measured sharpness on the test loss around the obtained minima as well, and does it track train-sharpness consistently?
