1) Summary
This paper presents a careful empirical study of the generalization gap between small- and large-batch training in deep networks. The central claim is that SGD with smaller batches tends to converge to flatter minima, while large-batch methods converge to sharper minima with worse generalization. The authors support this with multiple diagnostics: 1D/curvilinear loss slices through solutions, a computationally tractable sharpness proxy, warm-start experiments that reveal an exploration-to-flatness dynamic, and batch-size threshold studies. Although methodological novelty is limited, the work is valuable for clarifying which assumptions about optimization landscapes plausibly underlie generalization in deep learning and for suggesting practical heuristics around batch-size scheduling.

2) Decision
Accept. Reason: The paper offers compelling, reproducible empirical evidence linking batch size, flatness, and generalization, and provides actionable guidance for practice and for theory-building. The authors also addressed a prior correctness concern by removing an unsupported scale-invariance claim for their sharpness metric.

3) Supporting arguments
- Motivation and positioning: The question—why large batches generalize worse—is important for both scalability and theory. The paper is well-situated within optimization and deep learning literatures and contrasts multiple hypotheses (overfitting, saddle attraction, lack of exploration).
- Evidence quality: Experiments span several architectures/datasets/optimizers; confounders like classical overfitting are discussed and ruled out via training/testing curves. Warm-start and threshold experiments provide a coherent mechanism linking gradient noise to escape from sharp basins.
- Rigor: While the sharpness metric is heuristic, the authors now avoid over-claiming scale invariance, and they triangulate with complementary visualizations. The consistency of results across settings strengthens credibility and suggests legitimate assumptions for future theory.

4) Suggestions to improve (helpful, not decisive)
- Control and report training budgets across batch sizes (iterations/epochs/updates, total compute), learning-rate schedules (including linear scaling and warmup), weight decay, and normalization to rule out hyperparameter confounds.
- Calibrate sharpness with scale-aware or parameterization-invariant measures; consider Hessian spectral approximations (e.g., Lanczos top eigenvalues) and gradient-noise-scale estimates.
- Quantify the dimensionality of the “sharp subspace” more systematically.
- Evaluate dynamic batch scheduling and explicitly compare to flatness-seeking methods (e.g., Entropy-SGD, SAM) to test the proposed mechanism.
- Improve statistical reporting: variability across runs, significance tests, and ablations on optimizer choice.

Questions for authors
- How were compute budgets matched across SB/LB (same number of parameter updates vs epochs vs wall-clock)?
- Did you try LR scaling + warmup for LB? How does that affect sharpness/generalization?
- Can you report Hessian top eigenvalues (or power/Lanczos estimates) to validate the sharpness proxy?
- How sensitive are conclusions to weight decay, BN settings, and label smoothing?
- Can you quantify the low-dimensional sharp directions (e.g., rank-k curvature) more directly?
