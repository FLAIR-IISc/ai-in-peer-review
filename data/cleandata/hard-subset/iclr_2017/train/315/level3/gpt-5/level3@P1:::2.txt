Summary of contributions
- The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training. The central empirical finding is that LB methods converge to sharp minima, whereas SB methods converge to flatter minima, and flatter minima are associated with better generalization.
- The authors provide several forms of evidence: 1D and curvilinear parametric plots connecting LB and SB solutions; a heuristic sharpness metric that probes local neighborhoods; sharpness–loss trajectories; and warm-start experiments showing that LB can recover good generalization when initialized near a flat basin found by SB.
- The study spans multiple architectures and datasets, considers several optimizers (with ADAM as the main vehicle), and explores interventions (data augmentation, “conservative training,” adversarial training), finding partial mitigation but persistent sharpness for LB.
- The work is well positioned in the literature on SGD noise, generalization, and loss landscapes; it also connects to MDL and Bayesian intuitions about flatness. Although methodological novelty is limited, the empirical synthesis is clear and impactful. Importantly, an earlier concern about a claimed scale-invariance of the sharpness metric has been resolved by removing that claim in the revision.

Decision: Accept
- Key reasons: (1) Clear and carefully executed empirical study addressing a practically important and timely question; (2) Convincing, multifaceted evidence for a sharpness–generalization connection that is useful for both theory-building and practice. The authors resolved a correctness concern about the sharpness criterion.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a concrete, high-impact problem: LB training is attractive for parallelism but often harms test performance. Understanding the cause is essential for scalable deep learning.
- Placement in literature: The paper cites and builds on work on SGD noise, saddle-point avoidance, loss surface structure, and regularization. The MDL/Bayesian interpretations of flatness are apt, and the dynamic-batch/warm-start angle resonates with prior “dynamic sampling” ideas.
- Evidence and rigor: Across multiple networks and datasets, the reported generalization gap correlates with the sharpness metric by 1–2 orders of magnitude. Parametric plots consistently show sharper basins around LB solutions. Warm-start results provide especially compelling support: once SB exploration reaches flatter regions, LB optimization succeeds, suggesting a causal role of noise in escaping sharp basins. Negative results (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial training) are valuable and honest.
- Limitations and caveats: The sharpness metric is heuristic, dependent on the choice of neighborhood size and random subspace A, and not invariant to reparameterizations (e.g., scaling under BN or ReLU homogeneity). The inner maximization is inexact (10 L-BFGS-B steps), which may under-estimate sharpness unevenly. Batch-size effects may be entangled with hyperparameter tuning (learning rates, momentum/betas, schedules), total updates vs epochs, and regularization (weight decay, dropout, label smoothing). Most results use ADAM; broader optimizer coverage would strengthen generality. Nevertheless, the claims are framed as “numerical evidence,” and the patterns are consistent across several settings.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all part of the accept decision)
- Strengthen the connection to curvature by estimating top Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., Lanczos/power iteration with Hessian-vector products) and comparing to the sharpness metric across solutions and ε values.
- Analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to ε and to the choice/dimension of A; provide variance across random A’s and report stability ranges.
- Address reparameterization issues explicitly (e.g., invariance under BN scaling, ReLU positive homogeneity). Consider function-space measures (Fisher, PAC-Bayes flatness) or normalized Hessians to mitigate parameterization dependence.
- Control confounders: equalize (a) number of parameter updates, (b) total gradient evaluations, and (c) wall-clock budgets across SB/LB, and report whether gaps persist.
- Systematically explore batch-size schedules (dynamic sampling) beyond warm-starts; quantify the minimal SB “exploration” needed and whether smooth batch growth closes the gap.
- Report hyperparameter tuning details per batch size (LRs, momentum/betas, schedules, weight decay). Explore batch-dependent scaling rules and momentum damping.
- Provide ablations on regularization (weight decay, dropout, label smoothing) and their interaction with sharpness.
- Characterize the dimensionality of sharp directions (e.g., effective rank of Hessian near LB minima).
- Where feasible, add results for additional optimizers and larger-scale settings (e.g., ResNets/ImageNet) to test external validity.
- Improve reproducibility by releasing code, seeds, and configuration files for all experiments.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters: How were learning rates, momentum/Adam betas, and schedules tuned for each batch size? Did you try batch-size-dependent LR scaling or momentum adjustments? How sensitive are your conclusions to these choices?
- Metric sensitivity: How does the sharpness metric vary with ε and with different random subspaces A (dimension and draws)? Can you provide sensitivity curves or confidence intervals?
- Curvature corroboration: Did you compute top Hessian eigenvalues or use Lanczos to validate the sharpness differences? If so, how do these compare to your metric?
- Reparameterization: How do results change under parameter rescalings (e.g., with BN) or other equivalent reparameterizations? Can you propose or report an invariant variant of the metric?
- Budget matching: When controlling for the number of parameter updates or total gradient evaluations (rather than epochs), does the generalization gap remain?
- Dynamic batching: In your warm-start experiments, what is the minimal SB duration (epochs/updates) that consistently allows LB to reach flat minima? Does a gradually increasing batch schedule work as well or better?
- Regularization interactions: How do weight decay, label smoothing, and stronger data augmentation jointly affect both generalization and sharpness in the LB regime?
- Dimensionality of sharpness: Can you quantify the fraction of directions with high curvature near LB minima (e.g., via eigenvalue spectra or effective rank)?
- External validity: Have you observed the same patterns on larger datasets/architectures (e.g., ResNet on CIFAR-100/ImageNet), even if at reduced scale?
