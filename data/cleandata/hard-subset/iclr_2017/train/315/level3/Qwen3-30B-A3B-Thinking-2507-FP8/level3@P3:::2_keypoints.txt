- Main finding: Large-batch training converges to sharp minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) that generalize poorly, while small-batch training finds flatter minima with better generalization.  
- Empirical contribution: Comprehensive experiments across multiple architectures (e.g., AlexNet, VGG variants) support the main claim.  
- Sharpness metric: Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Eq. 4) that correlates with the Hessian spectrum without full eigenvalue computation.  
- Explanation of gap: Shows the generalization gap is not traditional overfitting but linked to the nature of minima found by different batch sizes.  
- Mitigation attempts: Explores data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization, which only partially reduce the large-batch generalization gap.  
- Revision note: Removed an earlier scale-invariance claim, strengthening credibility.  
- Decision: Recommend accept — valuable empirical contribution with practical and theoretical implications.  
- Literature placement: Well-motivated and connected to prior work on sharpness and theoretical developments.  
- Evidence quality: Extensive, well-designed experiments (parametric plots, sharpness metrics) that support the claims.  
- Suggestion 1: Add a more detailed comparison with related work on sharp minima (e.g., Chaudhari et al., Entropy-SGD).  
- Suggestion 2: Discuss computational cost of the sharpness metric versus direct Hessian computation.  
- Suggestion 3: Add discussion on how findings could inform new optimization algorithm design beyond batch-size changes.  
- Question 1: How do you distinguish the observed gap from other forms of overfitting that don’t show a clear test-accuracy peak-and-decline?  
- Question 2: How should we interpret the relationship between the proposed sharpness metric and the full Hessian spectrum in very high-dimensional networks?  
- Question 3: What theoretical loss-landscape properties explain why warm-starting large-batch from small-batch solutions improves generalization, and can this inspire new algorithms?
