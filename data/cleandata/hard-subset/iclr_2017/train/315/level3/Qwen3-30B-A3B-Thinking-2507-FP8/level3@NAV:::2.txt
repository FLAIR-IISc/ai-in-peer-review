

This paper presents a valuable empirical investigation into the relationship between batch size in SGD and the generalization performance of deep learning models. The authors demonstrate that smaller batch sizes consistently converge to flatter minima (characterized by numerous small eigenvalues in the Hessian), which correlate with better generalization, while larger batch sizes converge to sharper minima with poorer generalization. The study provides compelling numerical evidence across multiple network architectures and datasets, addressing an important practical concern in deep learning practice.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the generalization gap in large-batch training, which is a well-known but poorly understood phenomenon in the community. The authors effectively resolved a previous concern about scale invariance by removing that claim, strengthening the paper's scientific rigor. The findings have clear practical implications for algorithm design, suggesting that manipulating mini-batch sizes could be a simple heuristic to improve generalization.

The empirical evidence is robust, with multiple visualization techniques and sharpness metrics supporting the main claims. The paper successfully positions itself within the literature on flat vs. sharp minima and connects to related work on generalization. While the methodological novelty is limited, the study's value lies in its thorough empirical exploration of a critical practical issue, providing insights that could guide both theoretical developments and practical applications.

For improvement, the authors might consider expanding the discussion of how their sharpness metric relates to existing theoretical frameworks for generalization, particularly in relation to the recent theoretical work cited in Section 4. A brief analysis of the computational cost of their sharpness metric would also be helpful for practitioners. Additionally, while the paper mentions dynamic sampling as a potential remedy, a more concrete proposal for implementation would strengthen the practical impact.

I have no remaining concerns about the correctness of the core findings, and the resolution of the earlier scale-invariance issue provides confidence in the paper's scientific claims. The work provides a solid foundation for future research on generalization in deep learning.
