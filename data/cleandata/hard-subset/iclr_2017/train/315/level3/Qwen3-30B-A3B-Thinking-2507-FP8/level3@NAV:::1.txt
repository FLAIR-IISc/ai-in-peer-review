

The paper makes a significant contribution by investigating the fundamental reason behind the well-known generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods in deep learning. The authors compellingly argue that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by numerous large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers, and that sharp minima lead to poorer generalization performance. This explanation provides valuable theoretical insight into a practical problem that has been observed in the community but lacked a clear understanding.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The main reason is that the paper successfully addresses an important practical problem with clear relevance to deep learning practitioners, and the evidence supporting the main claim is robust and well-structured. A second key reason is that the paper provides clear value to the community by not only explaining the phenomenon but also exploring potential remedies.

The paper's strength lies in its thorough experimental validation across multiple network architectures (including AlexNet and VGGNet variants), multiple trials (5 runs from different initializations), and multiple metrics (parametric plots, sharpness metrics, and generalization performance). The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is computationally feasible and effectively demonstrates the key difference between SB and LB minimizers. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly convincing evidence that the SB method's exploration phase helps identify flatter minima that LB methods can then converge to.

For improvement, the authors might consider quantifying the percentage of directions in which the loss increases steeply around LB minimizers (beyond the statement that it's "5% of the whole space"). Additionally, a more detailed discussion of why the sharpness metric correlates with the Hessian spectrum would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

I would be interested to know: How sensitive is the observed sharpness difference to the choice of ε in the sharpness metric (Equation 4)? And could the authors clarify how they definitively ruled out overfitting as the cause of the generalization gap (Section 2.2), rather than the sharpness of the minima?
