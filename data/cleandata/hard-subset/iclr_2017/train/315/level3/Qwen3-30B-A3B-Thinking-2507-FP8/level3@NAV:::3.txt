

# Review

This paper makes a significant contribution by investigating the cause of the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers of the training function (characterized by high sensitivity in the loss landscape), while small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers (characterized by low sensitivity), which better generalize to unseen data. The paper is well-organized, thoroughly motivated, and provides extensive experimental validation across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, fully-connected networks) and datasets.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's core contribution is both interesting and useful, as it provides a clear mechanistic explanation for a well-known practical problem in deep learning. The main reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides robust empirical evidence supporting the sharpness hypothesis for the generalization gap, and (2) it effectively situates its work within the relevant literature on optimization and generalization in deep learning.

The paper supports its claims with multiple lines of evidence: parametric 1-D and curvilinear plots showing the sharpness difference between SB and LB solutions, the proposed sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) demonstrating consistent results across networks, and careful analysis of the relationship between batch size, sharpness, and generalization. The experiments are well-designed with multiple trials from different starting points, and the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations (e.g., the computational cost of Hessian-based sharpness metrics).

For further improvement, I suggest exploring the specific suggestion in the key points: investigating whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during large-batch training can yield benefits of the small-batch regime. This would directly address a potential remedy for the generalization gap. Additionally, the paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the theoretical implications of the sharpness metric and its relationship to the Hessian spectrum.

To clarify the paper's contribution, I'd like to ask: Have you considered whether the observed sharpness differences could be mitigated by modifying the optimization algorithm (beyond the conservative training and data augmentation approaches already explored), specifically by incorporating controlled noise into the large-batch training process as suggested in the key points?
