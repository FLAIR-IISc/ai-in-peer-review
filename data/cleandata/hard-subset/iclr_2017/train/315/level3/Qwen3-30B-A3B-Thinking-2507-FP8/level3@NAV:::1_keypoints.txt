- Paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training shows a generalization gap, attributing it to convergence to sharp minima vs small-batch (SB) convergence to flat minima.
- Claims LB minima are sharp (many large positive Hessian eigenvalues) and sharp minima lead to poorer generalization.
- Provides valuable theoretical insight into a practically observed phenomenon lacking clear prior explanation.
- Reviewer recommends acceptance due to practical relevance and robust evidence.
- Strong experimental validation across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG variants), multiple runs (5 initializations), and multiple metrics.
- Proposed sharpness metric (Eq. 4) is computationally feasible and effectively distinguishes SB and LB minimizers.
- Warm-starting experiments convincingly show SB exploration helps identify flatter minima that LB methods can then reach.
- Suggested improvement: quantify the percentage of directions with steep loss increase around LB minimizers (beyond the "5% of the whole space" statement).
- Suggested improvement: provide a more detailed discussion of why the sharpness metric correlates with the Hessian spectrum to strengthen theoretical grounding.
- Reviewer questions: how sensitive are results to the choice of ε in the sharpness metric (Eq. 4), and how was overfitting definitively ruled out as the cause of the generalization gap (Section 2.2)?
