1. Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch training.  
2. Empirical finding: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (large Hessian eigenvalues) that generalize poorly; small-batch methods find flat minimizers that generalize better.  
3. Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify sharp vs. flat minima.  
4. Explores several strategies to mitigate the large-batch generalization gap.  
5. Extensive experiments across architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants) support the results.  
6. Decision: Accept — significant, empirically supported contribution explaining a key practical problem.  
7. Strength: Thorough empirical investigation showing 1–2 orders-of-magnitude sharpness differences and informative parametric plots.  
8. Warm-starting experiments show small-batch training can guide large-batch methods toward better generalization.  
9. Paper is well-situated in prior sharp/flat minima literature and uses rigorous experimental methodology (multiple seeds, consistent architectures).  
10. Suggestion: Expand discussion of the sharpness metric’s limitations and relation to the true Hessian.  
11. Suggestion: Provide deeper analysis of the threshold batch size where generalization degrades.  
12. Suggestion: Discuss how warm-starting results could inform practical large-scale distributed training strategies.  
13. Question: How should rescaled Gaussian noise be properly scaled for large-batch training, and what supports this approach?  
14. Question: How does the observation that sharp minimizers are not cone-like relate to theoretical loss-landscape work (e.g., Choromanska et al.)?  
15. Question: Could the authors discuss theoretical barriers explaining why data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization only partially close the gap?
