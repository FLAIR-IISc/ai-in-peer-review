

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper investigates the well-documented phenomenon that large-batch training methods for deep learning often yield poorer generalization performance compared to small-batch methods, despite achieving similar training accuracy. The authors present compelling empirical evidence that this generalization gap arises because large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian), whereas small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers. The paper introduces a practical metric for measuring sharpness that avoids the prohibitive cost of computing the full Hessian, and demonstrates how this metric correlates with generalization performance across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, and fully-connected networks). The authors also explore several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap in large-batch training, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding a critical practical limitation in deep learning training. The core claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers, is well-supported by the evidence presented.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper effectively addresses the key question of why large-batch training suffers from a generalization gap. The authors demonstrate through multiple experimental setups that the sharpness metric correlates strongly with generalization performance, with large-batch methods consistently yielding sharper minima than small-batch methods. The parametric plots in Figure 3 and the sharpness metrics in Tables 3-4 provide clear visual and numerical evidence supporting their claim.

The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The paper builds on existing work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and connects to recent theoretical developments (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016). The authors clearly explain how their empirical findings align with and extend previous theoretical work, particularly regarding the relationship between minimizer sharpness and generalization.

The paper's claims are scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct thorough experiments across multiple network architectures, data sets, and training configurations. They provide statistical evidence (mean and standard deviation across 5 trials) and carefully address potential alternative explanations for the generalization gap (e.g., overfitting, saddle points). The resolution of the earlier concern about the scale-invariance property of their sharpness metric demonstrates the authors' responsiveness to reviewer feedback and commitment to scientific rigor.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper makes a strong contribution, there are a few areas where additional depth would enhance its impact:

- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of why flat minimizers generalize better from a theoretical perspective. While the MDL theory and Bayesian view are mentioned, a more concrete explanation would strengthen the connection between the empirical findings and theoretical understanding.

- The exploration of strategies to improve large-batch training (Section E) is somewhat brief. A more thorough analysis of why data augmentation and conservative training only partially mitigate the issue (rather than eliminating it) would be valuable for practitioners.

- The computational cost of the sharpness metric (Equation 4) could be discussed in more detail, particularly regarding how it scales to extremely large networks. The paper mentions that the metric is computationally feasible, but a brief analysis of the trade-offs would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Figure 5, the warm-starting experiment demonstrates that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when warm-started with small-batch training. How many epochs of small-batch training are typically required to achieve this effect, and does this number vary significantly across different network architectures or data sets?

2. The paper demonstrates that the generalization gap exists even when using different optimizers (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD) for large-batch training. How might the relationship between batch size, sharpness, and generalization change when using second-order optimization methods that explicitly incorporate curvature information?
