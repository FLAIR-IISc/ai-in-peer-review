

This paper makes a compelling contribution to understanding the generalization gap between large-batch and small-batch training in deep learning. The authors present strong empirical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers, and that sharp minimizers correlate with poorer generalization. Their numerical experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets consistently demonstrate this relationship, with sharpness metrics showing 1-2 orders of magnitude difference between the two regimes. The authors effectively characterize this phenomenon through parametric plots and the proposed sharpness metric, providing a clear visualization of the landscape differences between the two training approaches. The paper is well-motivated by practical challenges in deep learning parallelization and connects well to existing literature on flat vs. sharp minima.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning with clear empirical evidence, and the findings have significant implications for scalable training. The key strength is the systematic demonstration that the generalization gap is due to the nature of the minima found, not overfitting or other common explanations.

The supporting evidence is scientifically rigorous and well-presented. The authors carefully distinguish their findings from overfitting by showing that the generalization gap persists even when training to convergence without early stopping. Their analysis of the sharpness metric across multiple networks and datasets provides robust evidence for the main claim. The experiments with warm-started large-batch training (Figure 5) offer particularly insightful evidence that small-batch methods explore the landscape to find flat minima, which large-batch methods can then converge to when properly initialized. The paper's connection to theoretical work on loss surface properties (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016) further strengthens the context.

To improve the paper, consider the following suggestions: First, the authors should explicitly investigate whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the large-batch regime can replicate the benefits of small-batch training (as suggested in the key points). This would provide a direct experimental validation of their hypothesis that noise is what enables exploration of flat minima. Second, while the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is computationally feasible, the authors should provide more theoretical justification for why this metric correlates with the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian. Third, the paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the practical implications of their findings for distributed training systems.

The authors should clarify whether their sharpness metric (Equation 4) is truly invariant to dimensionality as claimed. The constraint set in Equation 3 appears to scale with the magnitude of the solution, but it's unclear how this affects comparisons across different network architectures with varying parameter scales. Additionally, the paper would be strengthened by a more systematic exploration of the relationship between batch size and the sharpness of the minimizers found, beyond the threshold behavior shown in Figure 4.

The authors should also address whether the sharp minima they identify are truly "sharp" in the theoretical sense (as defined in convex optimization literature), or if they are merely regions with high sensitivity to specific directions. Their observation that the loss increases steeply only along a small dimensional subspace (5% of the whole space) suggests these might be "directionally sharp" rather than globally sharp.

Finally, while the authors explore several strategies to improve large-batch generalization (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training), they should provide more detailed analysis of why these approaches fail to fully address the sharpness issue. A quantitative comparison of how much these methods reduce sharpness versus improving generalization would be valuable.

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding deep learning optimization dynamics, and with these minor revisions, it would be a strong addition to the literature. The findings have clear implications for both theoretical understanding and practical implementation of large-scale deep learning systems.
