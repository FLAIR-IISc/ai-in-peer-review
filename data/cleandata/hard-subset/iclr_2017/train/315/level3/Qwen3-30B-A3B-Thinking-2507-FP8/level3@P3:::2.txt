

# Review of "On Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning: Generalization Gap and Sharp Minima"

## 1. Summary

This paper makes a significant empirical contribution to understanding the generalization gap observed in deep learning when using large batch sizes. The authors demonstrate that large-batch training methods converge to sharp minima (characterized by large Hessian eigenvalues), which generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flatter minima with better generalization performance. Through comprehensive experiments across multiple network architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants), they provide compelling evidence that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting in the traditional sense, but rather stems from the nature of the minima found by different batch sizes. The authors introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) that effectively correlates with the Hessian spectrum, allowing them to quantify this phenomenon without the prohibitive cost of computing eigenvalues directly. They also explore several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap in large-batch training, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization, though these only partially address the issue.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a valuable empirical contribution to understanding generalization in deep learning, with well-supported claims that address an important practical issue in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper effectively answers the three key review questions:

1. **What specific question/problem does the paper tackle?** The paper identifies and investigates the generalization gap observed in large-batch training of deep neural networks, a well-known practical issue that has not been adequately explained.

2. **Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?** The paper is well-positioned in the literature, acknowledging previous work on sharpness (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and connecting to theoretical developments (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016). The motivation for exploring batch size effects is strong, given the practical importance of parallelization in deep learning.

3. **Does the paper support the claims?** The paper provides extensive empirical evidence across multiple architectures and data sets, with well-designed experiments (parametric plots, sharpness metrics) that consistently support the main claim. The authors appropriately addressed the reviewer's concern about scale invariance by removing that claim in the revision, which strengthens the paper's credibility.

The paper's main contribution is empirical, but its value lies in illuminating a fundamental aspect of deep learning generalization that has practical implications. While the methodological novelty is limited, the insights are valuable for both practitioners and theorists. The paper's findings suggest which assumptions about optimization are legitimate for developing new theories about generalization, directly addressing the theoretical impact point.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors might consider:

- Including a more detailed comparison with related work on sharp minima, particularly Chaudhari et al. (2016) which introduced Entropy-SGD, to clarify how this work complements or differs from prior research.
- Providing a brief discussion of the computational cost of their sharpness metric versus direct Hessian computation, to better justify their approach.
- Adding a section on how these findings might inform the design of new optimization algorithms beyond simple batch size manipulation, which would strengthen the practical impact.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting (with a testing accuracy peak followed by decline), but how do you distinguish this from other forms of overfitting where the model may still generalize poorly despite not showing a clear peak-and-decline pattern?

2. Your sharpness metric (Equation 4) is related to the Hessian eigenvalues, but for large deep networks, the Hessian is high-dimensional and complex. How do you interpret the relationship between your metric and the actual Hessian spectrum in these settings?

3. The warm-start experiments (Figure 5) suggest that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when started from small-batch solutions. What theoretical properties of the loss landscape would explain why this warm-starting approach works, and could this inspire new optimization algorithms?

This paper makes an important contribution to the understanding of generalization in deep learning, with findings that are both theoretically interesting and practically relevant. The empirical evidence is robust, the concerns raised by reviewers have been appropriately addressed, and the paper provides valuable insights that will inform both research and practice in deep learning.
