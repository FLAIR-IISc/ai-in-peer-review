

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper investigates the well-documented phenomenon that large-batch SGD methods often exhibit poorer generalization performance than small-batch methods in deep learning, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors make the compelling claim that this generalization gap arises because large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), which generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with many small eigenvalues), which generalize better. The paper provides extensive numerical evidence across multiple network architectures (including AlexNet and VGGNet variants) to support this claim, using novel visualization techniques and a computationally feasible sharpness metric. The authors also explore several strategies to address the generalization gap, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

## 2. Decision
I recommend acceptance. The paper addresses a significant practical problem in deep learning with a well-motivated and clearly articulated explanation, supported by rigorous empirical evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's central contribution is well-motivated and addresses a crucial practical concern in deep learning. The authors effectively connect a common empirical observation (large-batch generalization gap) with a theoretical concept (sharp vs. flat minima) that has been discussed in the literature (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016). The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly situating its contribution within the broader context of SGD, batch size effects, and generalization in deep learning.

The empirical evidence is robust and thorough. The authors employ multiple complementary approaches to validate their claim: (1) parametric plots showing the loss landscape along paths between SB and LB minimizers, (2) a novel sharpness metric (Equation 4) that quantifies the sensitivity of minimizers, and (3) a warm-starting experiment demonstrating how SB methods can guide LB methods toward better minima. The results consistently show that LB methods converge to significantly sharper minimizers with poorer generalization, while SB methods converge to flatter minimizers with better generalization. The experiments cover multiple network architectures (F1-F4, C1-C4), datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, CIFAR-10), and optimization methods (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD), strengthening the generality of their claims.

The paper also makes a clear contribution to the community by identifying a concrete mechanism behind a practical problem, which could guide future work on large-batch training. The authors' exploration of potential remedies, while preliminary, provides a useful foundation for future research.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the sharpness metric (Equation 4), particularly how it relates to the actual Hessian eigenvalues. While the authors note this relationship, a more thorough theoretical justification would strengthen their claim.
- It would be valuable to see more analysis of how the sharpness metric correlates with generalization performance across different architectures and datasets, rather than just comparing SB vs. LB regimes.
- The connection to recent theoretical work on the loss landscape of deep networks (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016) could be more explicitly developed, particularly regarding the density of sharp vs. flat minima.
- The paper mentions that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting" but does not provide sufficient evidence for this claim. A more detailed discussion of why the observed phenomenon differs from typical overfitting would be helpful.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." Could the authors elaborate on why this is the case, and how it relates to their sharpness metric (Equation 4)?
2. How do the authors interpret the relationship between the sharpness metric and the actual Hessian eigenvalues? Is there a theoretical foundation for why the metric would correlate with generalization performance?
3. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) show that LB methods benefit from being warm-started with SB methods. Could the authors discuss the computational cost of this approach and whether it would be practical for large-scale training?
