- Main contribution: attributes large-batch generalization gap to convergence to sharp minima vs. small-batch convergence to flat minima.  
- The explanation aligns with prior theory on flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997).  
- Strong empirical support: consistent results across six architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants) and multiple datasets.  
- Multiple lines of evidence shown: parametric 1D and curvilinear plots demonstrating relative sharpness (Figures 3, 7).  
- Introduces a practical sharpness metric (Equation 4) to measure minimizer sharpness.  
- Warm-start experiments (Figure 5) show large-batch methods can generalize well when initialized from small-batch iterates, supporting the core hypothesis.  
- Distinguishes the generalization gap from classical overfitting—it's a persistent performance difference, not just train-test divergence.  
- Experimental design is robust: multiple trials, careful analyses, and examination of batch-size vs. sharpness (Figure 4).  
- Recommends acceptance based on clarity, evidence, and significance of findings.  
- Suggests clarifying relationship between the proposed sharpness metric and Hessian eigenvalues where Hessian is computable.  
- Requests discussion of the computational cost of the sharpness metric for practitioners.  
- Asks for more details on the learning-rate schedule used for large-batch training, since it may affect gradient-noise dynamics.  
- Notes that large-batch minima are closer to initialization (ratio 3–10) and requests deeper analysis of this across networks.  
- Evaluates proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) as insightful but not fully effective per preliminary results.  
- Highlights dynamic batch-size increases (warm-starting) as a promising practical solution.  
- Emphasizes significant implications for distributed training if the generalization gap can be overcome.
