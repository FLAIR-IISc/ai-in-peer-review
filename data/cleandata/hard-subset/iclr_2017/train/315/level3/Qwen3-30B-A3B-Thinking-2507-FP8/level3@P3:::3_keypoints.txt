- Main contribution: Empirical evidence that large-batch training converges to sharp minima while small-batch training converges to flat minima, explaining worse generalization for large batches.  
- Decision: Accept — strong empirical contribution with well-designed experiments supporting the claim.  
- Motivation: Clearly addresses why large-batch methods generalize worse despite similar training loss.  
- Empirical scope: Experiments across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG variants), datasets, and random initializations.  
- Sharpness metric: Proposes a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Eq. 4) that captures the phenomenon for large networks.  
- Consistency of results: Large-batch methods show substantially higher sharpness (1–2 orders of magnitude) while achieving comparable training loss.  
- Literature connection: Builds on prior work on sharp vs. flat minima and recent loss-landscape theory.  
- Methodological rigor: Well-designed experiments and multiple lines of evidence (parametric paths, sharpness metric, warm-starting).  
- Remedies explored: Authors investigate potential fixes but they only partially mitigate the generalization gap.  
- Suggestion 1: More explicit theoretical link between the sharpness metric and Hessian eigenvalues is needed.  
- Suggestion 2: Test adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients in large-batch training to mimic small-batch noise.  
- Suggestion 3: Expand warm-start experiments to quantify how many small-batch epochs are needed to obtain flatness and the practical trade-off.  
- Question 1: How sensitive are sharpness results to the random-manifold dimension p (paper uses p=100)?  
- Question 2: Clarify how the observed phenomenon differs from traditional overfitting given sometimes lower training loss for large batches.  
- Question 3: Have you tried rescaled Gaussian gradient noise in large-batch regimes, and what were the results?  
- Question 4: How do these empirical findings relate to theoretical work on loss landscapes and the relative density of sharp vs. flat minima?
