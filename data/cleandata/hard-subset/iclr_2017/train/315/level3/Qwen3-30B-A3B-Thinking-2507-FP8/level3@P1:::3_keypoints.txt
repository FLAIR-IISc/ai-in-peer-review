- Main contribution: attributes the large-batch vs small-batch generalization gap to the nature of minima found (sharp vs flat).  
- Empirical claim: large-batch methods converge to sharp minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch methods find flat minima, and sharpness correlates with poorer generalization.  
- Experimental scope: consistent numerical evidence across multiple architectures and datasets showing 1–2 orders of magnitude difference in sharpness.  
- Visualization and metric: parametric plots and the proposed sharpness metric effectively illustrate landscape differences between training regimes.  
- Motivation and context: well-motivated by parallelization challenges and tied to relevant theoretical work (e.g., Choromanska et al., Lee et al.).  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Overfitting ruled out: generalization gap persists even when training to convergence without early stopping.  
- Warm-start insight: warm-started large-batch experiments show small-batch exploration can locate flat minima that large-batch can reach when properly initialized.  
- Suggested experiment 1: test whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients in large-batch training reproduces small-batch benefits.  
- Suggested analysis 2: provide theoretical justification why the sharpness metric (Eq.4) correlates with the largest Hessian eigenvalue.  
- Practical implications: include a more detailed discussion of consequences for distributed/large-scale training systems.  
- Metric invariance concern: clarify whether the sharpness metric is truly invariant to dimensionality and parameter scale across architectures.  
- Batch-size relationship: perform a more systematic study of how sharpness changes with batch size beyond the shown threshold behavior.  
- Definition of sharpness: clarify if identified minima are globally “sharp” or only “directionally sharp” along a small subspace.  
- Mitigation strategies: provide quantitative analysis of why data augmentation, conservative, and robust training do not fully resolve sharpness and how much they reduce sharpness versus improve generalization.
