

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper addresses a crucial practical issue in deep learning: the observed generalization degradation when using large-batch training methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors present compelling empirical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) that generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with small eigenvalues) that generalize better. The paper introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify this phenomenon and explores several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap for large-batch methods. The findings are supported by extensive experiments across multiple network architectures (including AlexNet and VGGNet variants) and provide valuable insights into the relationship between optimization dynamics and generalization performance.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a clear, empirically supported explanation for a well-known practical problem in deep learning. The core insight that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers while small-batch methods find flat minimizers is well-motivated and addresses an important gap in our understanding of deep learning optimization.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's strength lies in its thorough empirical investigation. The authors demonstrate a consistent 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between small-batch and large-batch solutions across multiple network architectures (Tables 3-4). The parametric plots (Figure 3) provide intuitive visual evidence of the sharpness difference, and the warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) offer a particularly insightful analysis of how small-batch methods can "guide" large-batch methods toward better generalization.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, connecting to prior work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) while providing concrete evidence for the specific context of deep learning. The authors clearly articulate why this problem matters, noting the potential for massive scalability gains if large-batch training could achieve the same generalization performance as small-batch methods. The paper's methodology is scientifically rigorous, with careful attention to experimental design (multiple random seeds, consistent architecture choices, and thorough analysis of results).

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is strong overall, but the following points could further enhance it:

- Consider expanding the discussion of the sharpness metric's limitations. While the authors note it's approximate, a more detailed analysis of how it relates to the true Hessian would strengthen the claims.

- The paper mentions a threshold batch size beyond which generalization degrades (Figure 4), but doesn't thoroughly analyze why this threshold exists. A deeper exploration of this phenomenon would provide additional insights.

- The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are particularly valuable. Consider adding a brief discussion of how these results could inform practical training strategies for large-scale distributed systems.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper suggests that adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during large-batch training might help replicate the beneficial effects of small-batch noise. Could you elaborate on how this noise might be properly scaled and what theoretical or empirical evidence supports this approach?

- The authors note that sharp minimizers identified in experiments do not resemble cones (i.e., function increases rapidly only along a small dimensional subspace). How does this observation relate to recent theoretical work on the geometry of deep learning loss landscapes (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015)?

- In Appendix E, the authors explore data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization to address the generalization gap. Given that these approaches only partially improve generalization while maintaining sharp minimizers, could you discuss potential theoretical barriers to completely resolving this issue?
