- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch, arguing large-batch converges to sharp minima while small-batch finds flat minima, and proposes a practical sharpness metric evaluated across several architectures.

- Decision: Accept — significant contribution with well-supported core claim.

- Empirical evidence: Sharpness metric correlates strongly with generalization; parametric plots and tables show large-batch yields sharper minima than small-batch.

- Context: Builds on prior work (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber; Choromanska et al.; Lee et al.) and connects empirical findings to recent theory.

- Experimental rigor: Thorough experiments across networks/datasets, statistical reporting (mean/std over 5 trials), and responses to alternative explanations and scale-invariance concerns.

- Improvement area: Expand theoretical explanation for why flat minima generalize better (beyond MDL/Bayesian mentions).

- Improvement area: Provide deeper analysis of mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training) and why they only partially work.

- Improvement area: Discuss computational cost and scaling trade-offs of the proposed sharpness metric for very large networks.

- Question for authors: How many epochs of small-batch warm-starting are needed for large-batch to achieve good generalization, and does this vary by architecture/dataset?

- Question for authors: How would second-order optimization methods that incorporate curvature affect the relationship between batch size, sharpness, and generalization?
