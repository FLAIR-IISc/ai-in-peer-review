

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the generalization gap observed in large-batch deep learning training. The authors investigate why large-batch methods (with batch sizes >512) consistently yield poorer generalization performance compared to small-batch methods, despite achieving similar training loss. They convincingly argue that the core issue lies in the tendency of large-batch methods to converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function, whereas small-batch methods naturally converge to flat minimizers due to the inherent noise in gradient estimation. This explanation aligns with theoretical work on flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and provides a clear, empirically supported framework for understanding this practical phenomenon.

I recommend acceptance. The paper's main contribution is its clear identification of sharpness as the key differentiator between small-batch and large-batch training outcomes, supported by extensive experimental evidence. The authors present multiple lines of evidence: parametric 1D and curvilinear plots (Figures 3, 7) showing the relative sharpness of minima, a well-motivated sharpness metric (Equation 4) that enables practical measurement of this property, and consistent results across six network architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants) on multiple datasets. The distinction between the generalization gap and overfitting is particularly important, as the authors correctly note that the phenomenon manifests as a consistent performance difference rather than the typical training-testing accuracy divergence seen in overfitting.

The paper is well-motivated and places its work appropriately in the literature, acknowledging prior observations of the generalization gap while providing a new explanatory framework. The experimental design is robust, with multiple trials, careful attention to the distinction between generalization gap and overfitting, and thorough analysis of the relationship between batch size and minimizer sharpness (Figure 4). The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly compelling evidence that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when started from small-batch iterates that have already explored to flat minima, supporting the authors' core hypothesis.

For improvement, the authors might clarify the relationship between their sharpness metric (Equation 4) and the actual Hessian eigenvalues, especially for cases where Hessian computation is feasible. A brief discussion of the computational cost of their sharpness metric would also help practitioners. Additionally, while the paper mentions that large-batch methods converge to minimizers closer to the initialization point (ratio 3-10), a more detailed analysis of this relationship across different networks would strengthen the argument.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify two points: First, how does their sharpness metric (Equation 4) compare to the actual largest eigenvalue of the Hessian in cases where the Hessian can be computed (e.g., for smaller networks)? Second, could they provide more details on the learning rate schedule used for large-batch training, as this might interact with the noise characteristics they discuss?

The paper's discussion of potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) is insightful, though the preliminary results suggest these approaches don't fully solve the sharpness problem. The authors' suggestion of dynamic batch size increases (warm-starting) as a practical solution is particularly promising and aligns with their experimental findings.

Overall, this paper provides a clear, well-supported explanation for a fundamental practical issue in deep learning. The findings have significant implications for the design of distributed training systems, as large-batch methods could potentially reduce training time by orders of magnitude if the generalization gap can be overcome. The paper is well-written, the experiments are thorough, and the claims are well-supported by the evidence presented, making it a strong contribution to the field.
