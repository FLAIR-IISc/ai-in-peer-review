- Problem and hypothesis: Explains why large-batch training generalizes worse, hypothesizing LB converges to sharp minima while SB finds flat minima that generalize better.  
- Empirical evidence: Presents strong numerical evidence across multiple architectures (including AlexNet/VGG variants) supporting the sharp vs. flat minima hypothesis.  
- Methods: Uses parametric plots and a novel sharpness metric to quantify sharpness differences between SB and LB solutions.  
- Decision: Reviewer recommends Accept due to addressing a widely observed problem with thorough empirical support.  
- Experimental rigor: Tests multiple networks (F1–F4, C1–C4) and datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants), providing robust, diverse evaluations.  
- Key result (plots): Parametric plots show LB solutions are significantly sharper than SB solutions along the connecting line.  
- Key result (metric): Sharpness metric reports 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sharpness for LB vs. SB (Tables 3–4).  
- Warm-start insight: Warm-starting LB with SB finds better-generalizing solutions, implying SB’s exploration is crucial for flat minima.  
- Remedies tested: Explores data augmentation, conservative and robust training with limited success, offering practical directions.  
- Caution in claims: Authors appropriately frame results as numerical evidence and acknowledge metric approximations.  
- Suggestion 1: Quantify computational cost of the sharpness metric versus standard training and full Hessian computations.  
- Suggestion 2: Relate findings to implicit regularization literature in SGD (e.g., Hardt et al., 2015).  
- Suggestion 3: More explicitly discuss dynamic batch-size scheduling or hybrid SB/LB strategies as practical remedies.  
- Question 1: How does the sharpness metric correlate with the true largest Hessian eigenvalue where computable?  
- Question 2: Provide details on the reported ||x_s^* - x_0||^2 / ||x_l^* - x_0||^2 ratio across specific networks and its correlation with generalization gap.
