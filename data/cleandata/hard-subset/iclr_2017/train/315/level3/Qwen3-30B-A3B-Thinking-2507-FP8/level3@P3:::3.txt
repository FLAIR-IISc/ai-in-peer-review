

# Review of "Train Faster, Generalize Better: Stability of Stochastic Gradient Descent" (ICLR 2017)

## 1. Summary

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the generalization gap between large-batch and small-batch training in deep learning. The authors provide compelling empirical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity of the loss function in a small neighborhood), while small-batch methods consistently converge to flat minimizers. This explanation aligns with the well-known observation that flat minima tend to generalize better than sharp minima. The paper presents multiple lines of evidence including parametric plots along paths between solutions, a computationally feasible sharpness metric, and experiments across various network architectures. The authors also explore potential remedies for the generalization gap, though these only partially address the core issue. The work is particularly valuable as it provides a clear, empirically grounded explanation for a practical problem that has been observed in deep learning for years.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper makes a strong empirical contribution to an important practical problem in deep learning, with well-designed experiments that consistently support the main claim.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a well-known practical issue in deep learning: the generalization gap between large-batch and small-batch training. The main question tackled is clearly articulated: "Why do large-batch methods for training deep learning models tend to generalize worse than small-batch methods, despite achieving similar training loss?" The authors' explanation—that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers—is well-motivated and supported by extensive empirical evidence.

The approach is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and connecting to recent theoretical work on the loss landscape of deep networks (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016). The paper's methodology is rigorous, with experiments conducted across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants), multiple datasets, and multiple runs from different initializations. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is well-designed to be computationally feasible for large networks while still capturing the essence of the phenomenon. The results are consistent across all experiments, with large-batch methods consistently showing higher sharpness values (1-2 orders of magnitude higher) than small-batch methods, while achieving comparable training loss.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:

1. The connection between the proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) and the Hessian eigenvalues could be more explicitly discussed. While the authors note that the metric relates to eigenvalues, a more detailed theoretical explanation would help readers understand the relationship.

2. The paper suggests exploring noise injection as a potential remedy (in Section 4), but doesn't actually investigate this. The authors should consider adding an experiment that directly tests whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during large-batch training can yield benefits similar to small-batch methods. This would directly address the key suggestion in the prompt and provide a more complete picture of potential solutions to the generalization gap.

3. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are particularly insightful. The authors could expand on how many epochs of small-batch training are needed to achieve the desired flatness, and whether this represents a practical trade-off between the two approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the sharpness metric (Equation 4), how sensitive are the results to the choice of dimension p for the random manifold? The paper uses p=100, but it would be valuable to see how results change with different p values.

2. The paper mentions that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting in the traditional sense, but rather to the nature of the minima found. Could you clarify how this differs from traditional overfitting, especially given that large-batch methods often have lower training loss than small-batch methods?

3. The authors suggest that adding noise to large-batch training might help. Have you explored adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the large-batch regime to mimic the noise in small-batch methods? If so, what were the results, and could this be a promising direction for future work?

4. How do your findings about the nature of minima found by large-batch vs. small-batch methods relate to recent theoretical work on the loss landscape of deep neural networks (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016)? Do your experiments provide any insights into the relative density of sharp vs. flat minima?
