- Investigates the generalization gap in large-batch training, showing large-batch methods converge to sharp minima while small-batch methods find flat minima that generalize better.
- Well-organized, thoroughly motivated, and provides extensive experimental validation across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG variants, fully-connected) and datasets.
- Recommends acceptance; views the core contribution as both interesting and practically useful.
- Main reasons for acceptance: robust empirical evidence supporting the sharpness hypothesis and effective positioning within optimization/generalization literature.
- Provides multiple lines of evidence: parametric 1-D and curvilinear plots, the proposed sharpness metric (Metric 2.1), and analysis linking batch size, sharpness, and generalization.
- Experiments are well-designed with multiple trials from different initializations; authors acknowledge limitations like the computational cost of Hessian-based metrics.
- Suggests investigating whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients in large-batch training can replicate small-batch benefits.
- Recommends a deeper discussion of the theoretical implications of the sharpness metric and its relationship to the Hessian spectrum.
- Asks whether modifying the optimization algorithm (e.g., controlled noise injection) beyond conservative training and data augmentation was considered to mitigate sharpness differences.
