- Paper studies why large-batch SGD generalizes worse than small-batch SGD, arguing large-batch converges to sharp minima while small-batch finds flat minima, supported by experiments and a sharpness metric.  
- Recommendation: accept — addresses an important practical problem with strong empirical support.  
- Main contributions: links large-batch generalization gap to sharp vs. flat minima and situates work within prior literature on minima geometry.  
- Strong empirical evidence across multiple architectures (AlexNet/VGG variants), datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, CIFAR-10), and optimizers (SGD, ADAM, ADAGRAD).  
- Methods used: parametric loss-path plots, a novel sharpness metric (Eq.4), and warm-start experiments showing SB can guide LB to better minima.  
- Paper proposes preliminary remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) to reduce the large-batch generalization gap.  
- Needs a more detailed discussion of limitations of the sharpness metric and its relationship to Hessian eigenvalues; theoretical justification is lacking.  
- Would benefit from analysis correlating the sharpness metric with generalization across different architectures and datasets, not just SB vs. LB comparisons.  
- Should more explicitly relate findings to recent theoretical work on loss landscapes (e.g., Choromanska et al., Soudry & Carmon) regarding density of sharp vs. flat minima.  
- The claim that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting requires more evidence and clearer differentiation from standard overfitting phenomena.  
- Questions for authors: explain why sharp minimizers are not conical and how that relates to Eq.4; clarify theoretical basis linking the sharpness metric to Hessian eigenvalues and generalization; discuss computational practicality of warm-starting LB with SB for large-scale training.
