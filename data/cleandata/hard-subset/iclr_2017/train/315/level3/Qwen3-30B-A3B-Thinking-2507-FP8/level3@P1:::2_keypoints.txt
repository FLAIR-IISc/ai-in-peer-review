- Paper studies the generalization gap when using large-batch SGD for deep networks.  
- Empirical finding: large-batch methods converge to sharp minima while small-batch methods converge to flat minima that generalize better.  
- Introduces a practical sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify flatness/sensitivity of minima.  
- Supports claims with parametric plots and experiments across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG) and datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, CIFAR-10).  
- Explores remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) that only partially mitigate the large-batch generalization gap.  
- Recommendation: accept — paper provides valuable empirical insights into a practical, underexplained deep learning issue.  
- Strength: thorough and consistent empirical evidence across architectures and datasets.  
- Strength: authors addressed reviewer concern about scale-invariance of the sharpness metric in the revision.  
- Limitation: methodological novelty is limited; contribution is primarily empirical.  
- Improvement requested: quantify the relationship between batch size and the sharpness metric (how sharpness changes with batch size).  
- Improvement requested: strengthen the theoretical discussion linking observed phenomena to prior flat-minima frameworks.  
- Improvement requested: elaborate limitations and failure modes of the sharpness metric and its relation to the largest Hessian eigenvalue.  
- Question 1: clarify the mechanism by which warm-starting with small-batch training helps large-batch phases converge to flatter minima.  
- Question 2: specify which data augmentation strategies most effectively mitigate sharp minima and why they do not fully resolve the issue.  
- Overall: findings are timely and practically important for balancing computational efficiency (large batches) and model performance (generalization).
