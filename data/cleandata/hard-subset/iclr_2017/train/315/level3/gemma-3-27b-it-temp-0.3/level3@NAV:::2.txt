## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon of generalization performance degradation when using large batch sizes in SGD training. The authors present empirical evidence supporting the hypothesis that large batches converge to sharper minima, which are known to generalize poorly, while smaller batches find flatter minima. The study systematically compares the sharpness of minima found by large and small batch methods across several network architectures and datasets. 

I recommend **Accept** this paper. While the findings aren’t entirely novel – the connection between sharpness and generalization has been explored – the paper provides a valuable, well-executed empirical investigation that reinforces this connection in the context of deep learning and offers a clear explanation for the observed generalization gap. The resolution of the reviewer’s concern regarding scale invariance further strengthens the work.

The paper effectively addresses the question of *why* large batch sizes lead to poorer generalization. The approach is well-motivated by the practical observation of this issue and is appropriately placed within the existing literature on generalization in deep networks. The empirical results, including the parametric plots and sharpness metric analysis, strongly support the claim that large batches converge to sharper minima. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their sharpness metric and provide a clear definition and justification for its use. The inclusion of attempts to mitigate the issue with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization, even if unsuccessful, demonstrates a thorough investigation.

However, the paper could be improved with further clarification. Specifically, the relationship between the observed sharpness and the specific optimization landscape (e.g., the role of saddle points) could be discussed in more detail. It would also be beneficial to see a more detailed analysis of the impact of different learning rate schedules on the sharpness of the minima found by large and small batches. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions that the sharpness metric is related to the eigenvalues of the Hessian. Could you elaborate on how the choice of the random matrix *A* in Equation (4) affects the approximation of the Ritz value and the resulting sharpness measure?
2.  While the attempts to improve generalization with techniques like data augmentation were unsuccessful, did you observe any *qualitative* differences in the minima found with these techniques (e.g., a slight reduction in sharpness)?
3.  The paper focuses on ADAM. Have you explored the impact of different optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum) on the observed relationship between batch size and sharpness? Would different optimizers alter the findings?
