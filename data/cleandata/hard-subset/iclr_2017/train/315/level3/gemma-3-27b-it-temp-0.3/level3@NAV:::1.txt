## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper tackles the important question of why increasing batch size in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) often leads to degraded generalization performance in deep learning. The authors investigate this phenomenon, proposing that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers of the loss function, which are known to generalize poorly compared to the flatter minima found by small-batch methods. The work is engaging and clearly motivated by a practical problem frequently encountered by practitioners.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The analysis provides a compelling explanation for a well-known empirical observation, and the numerical evidence presented strongly supports the central hypothesis. The paper offers valuable insight into the optimization landscape of deep neural networks and contributes to a better understanding of the trade-offs involved in choosing batch sizes. 

The approach is well-motivated, building upon existing literature regarding the relationship between sharpness of minima and generalization. The authors appropriately frame their work within the context of recent theoretical investigations into the loss surfaces of deep networks. The paper supports its claims through a series of experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets, utilizing both parametric plots and a sharpness metric to quantify the characteristics of the minima found by small and large-batch methods. The results consistently demonstrate a correlation between large batch sizes and sharper minima.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification. Specifically, the connection between the observed sharpness and the inherent noise in small-batch gradients could be more explicitly articulated. While the authors mention this connection, a more detailed explanation of *how* the noise prevents convergence to sharp minima would strengthen the argument. Additionally, the limitations of the proposed sharpness metric should be discussed more thoroughly. While computationally feasible, it is an approximation, and acknowledging its potential shortcomings would enhance the paper’s rigor. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   Could you elaborate on why the proposed sharpness metric is a reasonable proxy for the spectrum of the Hessian, particularly in high-dimensional spaces?
*   The paper mentions attempts to remedy the generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) but finds limited success. Could you provide more detail on *why* these approaches did not fully address the problem, and what specific limitations were observed?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of optimizer (ADAM) and learning rate? Were other optimizers and learning rate schedules explored?
