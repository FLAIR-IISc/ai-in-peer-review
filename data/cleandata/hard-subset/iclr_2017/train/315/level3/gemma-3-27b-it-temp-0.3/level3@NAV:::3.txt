## Review of “The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods”

This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models. The authors convincingly argue, through numerical experiments, that large-batch methods tend to converge to *sharp* minimizers of the loss function, which are known to generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to *flat* minimizers. The paper clearly articulates the problem, provides supporting evidence, and explores potential remedies, making it a valuable contribution to understanding the behavior of stochastic gradient descent.

**Accept** – I recommend accepting this paper. The problem addressed is significant and practically relevant, and the paper provides compelling evidence supporting its central claim. The analysis is well-conducted, and the findings align with existing theoretical understanding of generalization in deep learning.

The strength of this paper lies in its clear presentation of the connection between batch size, the sharpness of minima, and generalization performance. The authors effectively use parametric plots and a sharpness metric to demonstrate this relationship across various network architectures and datasets. The exploration of potential remedies, while preliminary, demonstrates a thoughtful approach to addressing the identified issue. The inclusion of an idealized performance model in the appendix further strengthens the motivation for addressing the large-batch generalization gap.

However, the paper could benefit from further investigation into the underlying mechanisms driving the convergence to sharp minima. While the authors suggest noise in the gradient as a key factor for small-batch methods, a more detailed explanation of *why* large-batch methods are drawn to sharp minima would be beneficial. 

I have a few questions for the authors:

*   **Noise Scaling:** You mention investigating whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the LB regime can yield benefits of the SB regime. Could you elaborate on the rationale behind this approach and any preliminary results you may have? What scaling strategy would be employed?
*   **Landscape Visualization:** While the parametric plots are insightful, could you provide visualizations of the loss landscape in higher dimensions (e.g., using dimensionality reduction techniques) to provide a more comprehensive understanding of the differences between SB and LB minima?
*   **Initialization Sensitivity:** How sensitive are your findings to the initialization scheme? Do different initialization strategies affect the sharpness of the minima found by LB methods?



These suggestions are intended to further strengthen an already strong paper and are not critical for acceptance. Overall, this is a well-written and insightful contribution that will be of interest to the deep learning community.
