## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper tackles the important question of why increasing batch size during training degrades generalization performance in deep learning, a commonly observed but not fully understood phenomenon. The authors present a compelling argument that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers of the loss landscape, which are known to generalize poorly compared to the flatter minima favored by small-batch stochasticity. The work is engaging, clearly written, and provides a valuable contribution to the community by shedding light on this practical challenge.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The analysis is well-motivated by the practical limitations of large-batch training and builds upon existing literature regarding the relationship between flatness of minima and generalization. Crucially, the paper supports its claims with numerical experiments demonstrating a correlation between batch size, sharpness of minima, and generalization gap across various network architectures and datasets. The presentation of parametric plots and a sharpness metric, while not without limitations (acknowledged by the authors), provides convincing evidence for the central hypothesis.

The strength of this paper lies in its empirical investigation. The thoroughness of the experimentation – varying network configurations, optimizers, and providing a clear metric for sharpness – strengthens the validity of the conclusions. The acknowledgement of limitations, such as the computational cost of sharpness calculation and the inexact maximization process, is commendable. The discussion section effectively situates the work within the broader context of recent theoretical advancements in understanding loss surfaces. 

To further enhance the paper, I suggest the authors address the following points:

*   **Sharpeness Metric Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of parameter ‘λ’ in the sharpness metric (Equation 4)? A sensitivity analysis would build confidence in the robustness of the findings.
*   **Alternative Sharpness Measures:** Are there other, potentially more robust, measures of minimizer sharpness that could be explored?
*   **Impact of Initialization:** How does the initial weight initialization influence the observed tendency of large-batch methods to converge to sharp minima? 
*   **Elaborate on Augmentation:** Can the authors provide more specific details on the augmentation techniques used, particularly how they were tailored to each dataset and network architecture?



I would appreciate the authors elaborating on the choice of ADAM as the primary optimizer. Was ADAM specifically chosen because it exhibits the described behavior more strongly than other optimizers, or was it simply a convenient choice for experimentation? Additionally, could they comment on how the results might differ with other optimization algorithms like SGD with momentum?
