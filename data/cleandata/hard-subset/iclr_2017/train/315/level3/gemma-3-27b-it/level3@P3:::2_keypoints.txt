- Investigates relationship between batch size, minima sharpness, and generalization; finds large batches converge to sharper minima with poorer generalization.  
- Empirical study offers valuable insights despite no new algorithmic contribution.  
- Well-motivated, relevant question addressing a practical deep learning problem.  
- Methodology compares minima sharpness across batch sizes using a proposed sharpness metric.  
- Experiments across multiple architectures and datasets consistently show large-batch methods find sharper minima.  
- Results presented with parametric plots, sharpness metric, multiple trials, and reported standard deviations.  
- Authors removed a problematic scale-invariance claim in response to reviewer feedback, improving rigor.  
- Sharpness metric is reasonable but needs more discussion of limitations and sensitivity to epsilon and manifold dimensionality.  
- Paper briefly references theoretical work; stronger speculative/theoretical connections explaining why large batches yield sharper minima would help.  
- Warm-starting experiment is promising but requires analysis of whether large-batch methods escape different minima or refine small-batch solutions.  
- Practical implications are underdeveloped; remedies in Appendix E had limited success and more guidance on batch-size selection is needed.  
- The practical significance of the observed sharpness differences should be quantified in terms of test accuracy impact.  
- Question: how sensitive is the sharpness metric to epsilon and the dimensionality of the random manifold?  
- Question: why does warm-starting from small-batch help large-batch—escaping vs refining minima?  
- Question: beyond Appendix E, what other mitigation strategies (architecture changes, regularization) were considered?
