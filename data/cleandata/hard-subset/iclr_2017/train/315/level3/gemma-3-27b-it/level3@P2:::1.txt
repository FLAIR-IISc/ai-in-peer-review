This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for training deep learning models. The authors present numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers of the loss function, which are associated with poorer generalization performance, while small-batch methods converge to flatter minimizers. The paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important practical problem.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper presents a compelling analysis of the relationship between batch size, sharpness of minimizers, and generalization performance. The experimental results are convincing, and the arguments are well-supported. This work contributes valuable insights into the behavior of large-batch training and provides a foundation for future research aimed at mitigating its drawbacks.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper successfully identifies a potential root cause for the generalization gap in large-batch training: the tendency to converge to sharp minimizers. This explanation is intuitively appealing and supported by the presented experiments. The parametric plots (Figure 3 and Appendix D) visually demonstrate the difference in sharpness between minimizers found by small and large-batch methods. The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), while acknowledged as a heuristic, provides a quantifiable measure that correlates with generalization performance. 

The experiments cover a reasonable range of network architectures (Table 1) and datasets (Table 5), strengthening the generality of the findings. The analysis goes beyond simply observing the generalization gap; it attempts to characterize the nature of the minimizers and relates it to existing theoretical concepts like flat vs. sharp minima. The discussion of related work (Section 4) appropriately contextualizes the contributions of this paper within the broader literature. The inclusion of an idealized performance model (Appendix C) highlights the potential benefits of overcoming the limitations of large-batch training.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper provides strong evidence for the link between sharpness and generalization, it would benefit from further exploration of *why* large-batch methods tend to converge to sharper minimizers.  Is it solely due to the reduced noise in the gradient estimates? Are there properties of the loss landscape that favor sharp minima for large-batch optimization? Addressing these questions would further solidify the understanding of the phenomenon.

The attempts to remedy the generalization problem (Appendix E) are valuable, even though they don't fully solve it. It would be helpful to discuss the limitations of these approaches in more detail. For example, why does data augmentation not completely alleviate the issue? What are the trade-offs involved in conservative training?

Consider adding a discussion of the computational cost associated with evaluating the sharpness metric. While acknowledged as a heuristic, quantifying the computational burden would be useful for practitioners considering its application.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that the sharpness metric relates to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian (when A = In) and approximates the Ritz value when A is randomly sampled. Could you elaborate on the practical implications of this connection? Is there a way to estimate the eigenvalue spectrum without explicitly computing the Hessian, and how would this relate to the proposed sharpness metric?

2.  The paper focuses on the ADAM optimizer. Have you investigated the behavior of other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum) in the large-batch regime? Do different optimizers exhibit different tendencies towards sharp or flat minima?

3.  In Appendix E, you explore several techniques to improve generalization with large batches. Have you considered techniques that directly modify the batch update rule to encourage exploration of flatter regions of the loss landscape, such as adding noise to the gradients or using a stochastic weight averaging scheme?

4.  The paper mentions the ratio of distances from initial point to SB vs LB minima. Could you present the distribution of this ratio over multiple runs and networks? This would give a better understanding of how consistently this observation holds.
