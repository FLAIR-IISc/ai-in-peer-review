This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when increasing batch size in deep learning, attributing it to a shift from flat to sharp minimizers. The authors provide numerical evidence supporting this claim, demonstrating that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharper minima compared to small-batch methods, and these sharper minima correlate with poorer generalization performance. The work is well-motivated, addressing a practical challenge in scaling deep learning training, and aligns with recent theoretical explorations of the loss landscape.

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling argument supported by empirical results. The connection made between sharpness of minima and generalization gap is insightful and relevant to the deep learning community. However, the supporting evidence could be strengthened with more detailed analysis and exploration of potential mitigation strategies.

The core argument – that large batch sizes lead to sharper minima and thus worse generalization – is convincingly supported by the parametric plots (Figures 3 & 7) and the sharpness metric (Tables 3 & 4). The observed consistent difference in sharpness between SB and LB methods across various network configurations adds weight to the claims. The ablation studies attempting to remedy the issue (Appendix E), while not fully successful, demonstrate an honest attempt to address the identified problem. The analysis in Section 3, relating the success of SB methods to noise in the gradient, is reasonable and connects well to existing understanding of SGD.  The performance model in Appendix C, though idealized, provides a useful context for the potential benefits of overcoming the generalization gap.

However, the paper lacks a deeper dive into *why* LB methods converge to sharper minima. While noise is posited as a key factor for SB methods, the precise mechanism driving LB methods towards sharpness remains somewhat unclear. The metric used for sharpness, while computationally feasible, is acknowledged as an approximation, and a discussion of its limitations is warranted. Furthermore, the attempts to remedy the problem in Appendix E seem somewhat cursory.  More detailed analysis of the failure modes of each approach, and exploration of parameter sensitivity, would be beneficial. Finally, the discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advancements in understanding loss landscape topology. 

Here are some questions for the authors:

1.  **Mechanism for Sharpness:** Could the authors elaborate on the potential mechanisms driving large-batch methods towards sharper minima? Is it simply the lack of noise, or are there other factors, such as the smoothness of the loss landscape at different scales, that contribute to this phenomenon?
2.  **Sharpness Metric Sensitivity:**  How sensitive are the conclusions to the choice of parameters in the sharpness metric (Equation 4), specifically *epsilon* and the dimensionality *p* of the random manifold?  Were any sensitivity analyses performed?
3.  **Remedy Analysis Depth:** For the methods attempted in Appendix E (Data Augmentation, Conservative Training, Robust Training), can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of *why* they did not fully resolve the generalization gap? Specifically, were any hyperparameter sweeps performed, and are there any indications of the parameter regimes where these methods show the most promise?
4.  **Initialisation Impact**: You mention initialization as a potential avenue for future work. Have you observed any systematic differences in the final sharpness of minima based on different initialization schemes?
5. **Alternative Sharpness Measures**: Were other methods of measuring sharpness (e.g., spectral analysis of the Hessian) considered but deemed computationally infeasible? If so, why?
