- Study links large-batch SGD generalization gap to convergence to sharp minimizers versus small-batch convergence to flatter minimizers.  
- Decision: Accept — compelling analysis with convincing experimental support.  
- Paper is well-motivated, clearly written, and tackles an important practical problem.  
- Numerical evidence and parametric plots (Fig. 3, App D) visually demonstrate sharpness differences between minima found by small and large batches.  
- Proposed sharpness metric (Eq. 4) is heuristic but correlates with generalization and relates to the largest Hessian eigenvalue / Ritz value.  
- Experiments cover multiple network architectures (Table 1) and datasets (Table 5), supporting the generality of findings.  
- Analysis connects empirical observations to theoretical concepts of flat vs. sharp minima and situates work within related literature (Sec. 4).  
- Appendix C provides an idealized performance model illustrating benefits of addressing large-batch limitations.  
- Appendix E reports attempts to mitigate the problem (e.g., data augmentation, conservative training) but they don’t fully solve it; limitations and trade-offs need deeper discussion.  
- Suggest further investigation into why large-batch methods favor sharp minima (e.g., reduced gradient noise vs. loss landscape properties).  
- Recommend quantifying the computational cost of computing the sharpness metric for practical applicability.  
- Questions for authors: clarify practical implications of the sharpness–Hessian connection and eigenvalue estimation without explicit Hessian; report behavior of other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum); consider update-modifying techniques (gradient noise, SWA); provide distribution of SB vs LB distance ratios across runs/networks.
