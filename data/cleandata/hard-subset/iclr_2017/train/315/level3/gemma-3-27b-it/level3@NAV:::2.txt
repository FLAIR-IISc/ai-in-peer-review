## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper investigates the well-documented phenomenon of generalization performance degradation when using large batch sizes in SGD training of deep networks. The authors present empirical evidence supporting the hypothesis that large batches converge to sharper minima, which are known to generalize poorly, while smaller batches converge to flatter minima exhibiting better generalization. The study utilizes established visualization techniques and proposes a sharpness metric to quantify this observation across several network architectures and datasets.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While lacking significant methodological novelty, the work provides valuable insights into the relationship between optimization trajectory, minima characteristics, and generalization in deep learning. The empirical results are well-executed and clearly demonstrate a correlation between batch size, minima sharpness, and testing performance. This understanding is crucial for developing more robust and effective training strategies.

The paper is well-motivated, placing the study within the context of the practical limitations of scaling SGD to larger batches. It correctly identifies a key open question in deep learning: why do larger batches often perform worse despite faster computation? The experimental setup is sound, employing a variety of networks and datasets, and the results are presented with sufficient detail to allow for replication. The authors address my earlier concern regarding the scale-invariance of their sharpness criterion by removing the problematic claim, demonstrating responsiveness to feedback. The connection to existing theoretical work on loss surface characteristics is also appropriately highlighted.

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion regarding the limitations of the proposed sharpness metric. While it provides a computationally feasible approximation, its relationship to the full Hessian spectrum isn't fully explored. Additionally, the attempts to remedy the generalization gap with techniques like data augmentation and robust training, while valuable explorations, are presented briefly and don't yield conclusive results. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive are the observed results to the choice of learning rate and optimizer (specifically, ADAM)? Were different learning rate schedules explored for large vs. small batches?
*   Could the authors provide a more intuitive explanation of *why* larger batches gravitate towards sharper minima? Is it purely a consequence of the reduced noise in the gradient estimates, or are other factors at play?
*   The paper mentions the possibility of dynamic batch size scheduling. Could the authors elaborate on specific strategies or preliminary results in this direction?
*   Regarding the sharpness metric, what are the implications of its sensitivity to the chosen manifold dimension (parameter 'p' in Equation 4)? How does varying 'p' affect the conclusions drawn from the experiments?
