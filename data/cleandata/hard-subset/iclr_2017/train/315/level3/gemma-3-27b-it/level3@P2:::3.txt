This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, attributing it to a tendency to converge to sharp minimizers of the loss landscape. The authors provide numerical evidence supporting this claim, demonstrating that large-batch methods indeed converge to sharper minima than small-batch methods, which align with known properties of generalization performance. The paper also explores potential remedies, although initial attempts show limited success.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles an important and practical problem in deep learning – the degradation of generalization performance with larger batch sizes. The approach is well-motivated, builds upon existing observations, and the experimental results provide strong support for the core hypothesis regarding the sharpness of minima.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The motivation for this work is clear and relevant. The observation that large batch sizes lead to poorer generalization, despite potentially faster training times, is a significant hurdle in scaling deep learning. The paper’s hypothesis – that this stems from converging to sharp minima – is a plausible and well-articulated explanation, grounded in the existing literature on generalization and optimization. 

The numerical experiments are reasonably comprehensive. The authors tested several network configurations (AlexNet, VGGNet variants) on different datasets, providing evidence that the observed phenomenon isn't specific to a particular architecture. The use of parametric plots and the proposed sharpness metric provides a quantitative way to compare the nature of minima found by large and small batch methods. The inclusion of results from various optimizers (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD) further strengthens the validity of their findings. The comparison of the distances from initial starting points to the final minima adds an interesting dimension to the analysis. 

The paper’s connection to recent theoretical work, such as research on the loss surface of deep neural networks, is also valuable. Acknowledging the relationship to existing theory helps contextualize the empirical results and suggests avenues for future theoretical investigation.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper provides strong evidence for its core hypothesis, the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) yielded limited success. Expanding on these investigations, perhaps exploring a wider range of regularization techniques or parameter settings, would be beneficial. 

The sharpness metric, while a practical approximation, is acknowledged as imperfect. Further analysis exploring its relationship to the actual eigenvalue spectrum of the Hessian would strengthen the analysis.

The idealized performance model in Appendix C, while useful for illustrating the potential benefits of scaling, lacks detailed justification of the assumed parallel efficiency. Providing a more nuanced discussion of the potential bottlenecks in real-world parallel training environments would improve its relevance.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention in the conclusion that “conservative training” and “robust optimization” did not correct the problem. Could you elaborate on the specific parameter settings or hyperparameter searches performed during these experiments? Perhaps a more extensive hyperparameter sweep might reveal settings where these methods are more effective.
2. The paper suggests that the noise in small-batch methods helps escape sharp minima. Would adding rescaled Gaussian noise to the gradients during the large-batch regime yield similar benefits, effectively mimicking the stochasticity of small-batch methods?
3.  The sharpness metric relies on maximizing the loss function within a defined neighborhood. How sensitive are the results to the choice of the neighborhood size (parameter ‘’) and the dimension of the random manifold (p = 100)? Did you perform any sensitivity analysis on these parameters?
4.  The parametric plots show the loss function along a line segment (or curve) between the SB and LB minima. Is there any indication that the loss landscape differs qualitatively *between* the basins of attraction of the SB and LB minima, beyond just the sharpness of the minima themselves? For instance, are there more saddle points or other local minima in the LB basin?
