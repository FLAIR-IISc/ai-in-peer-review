- Paper studies generalization degradation with large SGD batch sizes.  
- Provides empirical evidence that large batches converge to sharper minima and small batches to flatter minima with better generalization.  
- Uses visualization techniques and proposes a computational sharpness metric across multiple architectures and datasets.  
- Recommends accepting the paper.  
- Lacks significant methodological novelty but offers valuable insights into optimization trajectories and generalization.  
- Empirical results are well-executed and clearly show correlation between batch size, minima sharpness, and test performance.  
- Well-motivated in the context of practical limits of scaling SGD and addresses an important open question.  
- Experimental setup is sound, varied, and presented with sufficient detail for replication.  
- Authors addressed prior concern about scale-invariance by removing the problematic claim.  
- Paper appropriately connects findings to existing theoretical work on loss surfaces.  
- Limitation: relationship between the proposed sharpness metric and the full Hessian spectrum is not fully explored.  
- Limitation: remedial attempts (data augmentation, robust training) are briefly presented and inconclusive.  
- Questions posed: sensitivity of results to learning rate and optimizer (e.g., ADAM) and differing LR schedules for large vs small batches.  
- Questions posed: need for a more intuitive explanation for why large batches favor sharper minima beyond reduced gradient noise.  
- Questions posed: request for elaboration on dynamic batch size scheduling strategies or preliminary results.  
- Questions posed: implications of sharpness metric’s sensitivity to manifold dimension parameter 'p' and how varying 'p' affects conclusions.
