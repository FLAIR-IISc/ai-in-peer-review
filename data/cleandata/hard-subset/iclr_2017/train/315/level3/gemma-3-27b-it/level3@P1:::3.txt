## Review of "The drawbacks of large-batch methods"

**1. Summary**

This paper investigates the observed degradation in generalization performance when using large batch sizes for training deep learning models. The authors present numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers of the loss landscape, which are known to generalize poorly. Conversely, small-batch methods tend to converge to flatter minima, benefiting from the inherent noise in gradient estimation. The paper explores this connection through parametric plots and a sharpness metric and briefly discusses potential remedies, finding limited success with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.  The work provides valuable insight into the interplay between batch size, optimization dynamics, and generalization ability in deep learning.

**2. Decision**

**Reject**. While the paper identifies a compelling and well-known phenomenon and provides supporting numerical evidence, the analysis feels somewhat descriptive rather than deeply analytical or offering a practical solution. The connection between sharpness and generalization, while intuitively plausible, isn’t rigorously demonstrated, and the proposed remedies show limited effectiveness.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of the paper lies in clearly articulating a potential cause—sharp minima—for the generalization gap observed with large-batch training. The experiments, performed across several network configurations and datasets, consistently show a correlation between batch size and the sharpness of the resulting minimizers. The use of parametric plots to visualize the loss landscape and the proposed sharpness metric are helpful in illustrating this difference. 

However, the paper lacks a deeper investigation into *why* large-batch methods gravitate towards sharper minima. While the authors acknowledge the existing theoretical work on loss surfaces, they do not integrate these findings to provide a more comprehensive explanation.  The attempts to remedy the issue—data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization—are presented briefly and yield underwhelming results. This suggests the problem is more complex than initially presented or that the chosen approaches are not well-suited to address it. The metric for sharpness, while useful, also feels somewhat heuristic and could benefit from further justification or connection to established measures in optimization. 

Furthermore, the paper relies heavily on empirical observations. While these observations are valuable, a theoretical analysis or more rigorous mathematical framework would significantly strengthen the claims. 

**4. Additional Feedback**

This is a valuable contribution to our understanding of large-batch training, and with some revisions, it could become a strong publication. Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Theoretical grounding:** Explore the connection to existing theoretical work on loss landscapes and generalization. Can the observed behavior be explained through established theoretical frameworks?
*   **Deeper analysis of remedies:**  A more thorough exploration of potential remedies is needed. Why did data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization fail to fully address the issue? Are there alternative strategies worth investigating?
*   **Sharpness metric justification:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the proposed sharpness metric and discuss its limitations. How does it relate to other measures of curvature or Hessian eigenvalues?
*   **Alternative optimizers:** Explore the use of different optimizers, particularly those designed to escape sharp minima or encourage exploration of the loss landscape.
*   **Batch size schedule:** Investigate dynamic batch size schedules, starting with a small batch size and gradually increasing it during training. This could potentially combine the benefits of both approaches.
*   **Investigate the role of learning rate:** Perform a more systematic study of the interplay between batch size and learning rate.
*   **Consider adding rescaled Gaussian noise:** Investigate whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to the gradients during the LB regime can yield similar benefits to those observed with SB. This could help inject more exploration into the optimization process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you elaborate on why you chose ADAM as the primary optimizer for your experiments? Have you explored other optimizers and, if so, what were the results?
*   The paper states that the sharpness metric approximates the Ritz value of the Hessian. Could you provide a more detailed explanation of this connection and how the choice of the matrix *A* impacts the accuracy of this approximation?
*   In the discussion of remedies, you mention that they “do not completely remedy the problem.” Can you quantify the extent to which these methods improve generalization performance and provide a more detailed analysis of their limitations?
*   Do you observe any differences in the sharpness of the minima when using different activation functions (e.g., ReLU, sigmoid, tanh)?
*   Could you discuss the computational cost associated with calculating the sharpness metric and whether it is feasible to apply it to very large networks or datasets?
