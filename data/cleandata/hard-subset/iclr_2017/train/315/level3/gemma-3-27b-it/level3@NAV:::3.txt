## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper investigates the degradation in generalization performance observed when employing large-batch stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for deep learning. The authors convincingly argue, through numerical experiments, that large-batch methods tend to converge to *sharp* minima of the loss landscape, which are known to generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to *flat* minima. The work is well-motivated, addressing a significant practical challenge in scaling deep learning training. The connection made to the sharpness of minima and its impact on generalization is a valuable contribution.

I **accept** this paper. The problem tackled is relevant and important, and the empirical evidence presented strongly supports the central claim regarding the relationship between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization performance. The paper provides a clear and compelling narrative explaining a common observation in deep learning practice.

The approach is well-motivated, building upon established concepts like sharp vs. flat minima and the limitations of parallelization with small batches. The authors appropriately position their work within the existing literature, referencing relevant theoretical and empirical studies. The experiments are well-designed, utilizing multiple network architectures and datasets, and the presentation of results – including parametric plots and the proposed sharpness metric – is clear and informative. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work and discuss potential avenues for future research, demonstrating a thorough understanding of the problem.

However, the paper would benefit from further investigation into the potential for mitigating the generalization gap. While the authors explore data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization, these approaches don’t fully resolve the issue. A particularly intriguing direction, hinted at in the results regarding warm-starting, is the investigation of *dynamically* adjusting the batch size during training. Specifically, I’d like to see the authors explore whether adding rescaled Gaussian noise to gradients during the LB regime can yield benefits analogous to the SB regime. The noise could potentially act as a regularizer, guiding the optimization process away from sharp minima. 

Further, it would be helpful to quantify the impact of different network architectures on the observed phenomenon. Are certain architectures more susceptible to converging to sharp minima with large batches than others? Finally, a theoretical analysis of why large batches favor sharp minima would significantly strengthen the contribution. Is there a relationship between the batch size, the curvature of the loss landscape, and the dynamics of gradient descent?
