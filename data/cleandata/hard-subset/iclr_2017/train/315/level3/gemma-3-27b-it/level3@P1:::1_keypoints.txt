- Paper studies why large-batch SGD yields worse generalization, arguing large batches converge to sharp minima while small batches find flat minima, and provides numerical evidence plus initial remedy attempts.  
- Decision: Reject — interesting analysis but empirical evidence is limited and lacks statistical rigor; proposed remedies are underdeveloped.  
- Hypothesis is plausible and well-motivated, coherently connected to prior theory on flat minima and generalization.  
- Experiments are limited in scope (few architectures/datasets) and other mentioned experiments lack detail.  
- The employed sharpness metric is heuristic and its direct link to generalization is not fully established.  
- The paper lacks rigorous statistical testing to show the observed sharpness differences are significant rather than due to randomness.  
- Remedies tested (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are briefly reported and do not convincingly address the problem.  
- Paper is well-written and concepts are clearly explained.  
- Recommendation to expand experimental evaluation: more architectures, datasets, optimizers, learning-rate schedules, and regularization techniques.  
- Recommendation to include rigorous statistical analyses (t-tests/ANOVA, confidence intervals, p-values).  
- Recommendation to deepen analysis of why remedial approaches fail, including loss-landscape and optimizer behavior studies.  
- Suggest strengthening connections to theoretical work on loss landscapes and critical-point properties.  
- Suggest testing sensitivity to alternative sharpness metrics (e.g., Hessian spectrum).  
- Question: How sensitive are results to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations in the sharpness maximization and was convergence analyzed?  
- Question: Please provide details on experiments with other networks/initializations and whether they confirm the findings.  
- Question: Is there evidence that basins of attraction for sharp minima are systematically larger or deeper for large-batch methods?  
- Question: Could observed sharpness differences result from different effective learning rates; were adaptive learning-rate scalings explored?  
- Question: For remedial approaches, what hyperparameters and tuning procedures were used, and were ablation studies performed?
