- Investigates generalization gap with large-batch training and attributes it to convergence to sharp minima.  
- Provides numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima than small-batch methods, explaining poorer generalization.  
- Experiments across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG variants) and datasets show the phenomenon is not architecture-specific.  
- Uses parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric to quantitatively compare minima found by LB and SB methods.  
- Includes results with multiple optimizers (SGD, ADAM, ADAGRAD) to bolster the findings’ robustness.  
- Compares distances from initialization to final minima, highlighting trajectory differences between LB and SB.  
- Connects empirical results to recent theoretical work on neural network loss surfaces, framing future theory directions.  
- Explores remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) but reports limited success so far.  
- Suggests expanding remedy investigations with broader regularization techniques and more extensive hyperparameter sweeps.  
- Notes the sharpness metric is a practical but imperfect approximation and recommends relating it to the Hessian eigenvalue spectrum.  
- Criticizes the idealized performance model in Appendix C for lacking justification of assumed parallel efficiency and calls for discussion of real-world bottlenecks.  
- Raises author questions: detail hyperparameter searches for conservative/robust training; whether adding gradient noise could mimic SB stochasticity; sensitivity of sharpness metric to neighborhood size and manifold dimension; and whether LB vs SB basins differ qualitatively beyond sharpness.
