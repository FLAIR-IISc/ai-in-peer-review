- Decision: Accept — paper makes a significant, novel contribution to understanding large-batch training limitations.

- Main finding: Large-batch gradient descent tends to converge to sharp minimizers, harming generalization, while small-batch noise helps escape sharp minima.

- Evidence: Robust numerical experiments across architectures using parametric plots, sensitivity measures, and sharpness metrics support the claims.

- Proposed remedies: Data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization reduce but do not fully close the large-batch generalization gap.

- Motivation and positioning: Work is timely, well-situated in literature, and connects observed behavior to loss landscape geometry and MDL/Bayesian theory.

- Scientific rigor: Empirical methodology is thorough and computationally feasible; some sharpness metrics are innovative.

- Theoretical gap: The paper lacks strong theoretical justification or formal models explaining why large-batch methods gravitate to sharp minima.

- Remedy evaluation gap: Proposed mitigation strategies are not deeply compared or analyzed; more detailed evaluation and future directions are needed.

- Practical implications: The paper could better articulate broader impacts, e.g., on distributed training and scalability.

- Question — theory: Can authors provide formal proofs or models for the batch-size–sharpness correlation and landscape properties that cause it?

- Question — dynamics: Were dynamic batch-size strategies beyond warm-starting explored, and how do they compare to other remedies?

- Question — metrics: Have alternative sharpness metrics (e.g., Hessian spectral analyses) been considered and compared to the heuristic used?
