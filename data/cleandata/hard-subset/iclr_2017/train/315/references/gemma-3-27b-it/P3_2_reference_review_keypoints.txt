- Empirical claim: large-batch training converges to sharper minima (higher concentration of large Hessian eigenvalues) linked to worse generalization.
- Decision: Reject — interesting observations but not sufficiently convincing or conclusive.
- Main weakness: correlation between sharpness and generalization is shown but causality is not established.
- Sharpness metric is ad hoc, lacks theoretical grounding, and depends on a random matrix A and parameter ε whose effects are unexplored.
- The choice p=100 in the metric is unexplained and no sensitivity analysis over p is provided.
- Mitigation attempts (data augmentation and conservative training) were unsuccessful, reducing the paper’s practical impact.
- Paper is heavily empirical with little theoretical analysis or bounds connecting sharpness to generalization error.
- Presentation is overly descriptive, spending space on known notation and restating known results.
- Suggested experiment: force convergence to flat minima (e.g., gradient clipping or curvature penalties) to test causality.
- Suggested refinement: analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to A and ε and consider alternative Hessian-spectrum–based metrics.
- Suggested mitigation exploration: try spectral normalization, robust optimization, and other techniques beyond augmentation/conservative training.
- Suggested addition: include a theoretical discussion relating results to existing loss-landscape literature and theoretical predictions.
- Suggested writing change: streamline content to focus on novel empirical findings and contributions.
- Question: clarify how the sharpness metric relates to the Ritz values of the Hessian and how A affects accuracy.
- Question: justify p=100 and report sensitivity analysis across different p values.
- Question: provide examples where the generalization gap persists despite aggressive augmentation or conservative training.
- Question: do other optimizers (e.g., L-BFGS) show similar sharpness behavior, and what optimizer properties cause the effect?
- Question: does the choice of linear vs. curved parametric path significantly affect observed sharpness differences?
