This paper investigates the impact of batch size on the generalization performance of deep learning models, positing that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers, leading to a generalization gap. The authors present empirical evidence supporting this claim, observing that large-batch training results in sharper minima compared to small-batch training. They explore potential remedies in the appendix, without finding a complete solution.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a potentially important issue – the connection between batch size, sharpness of minima, and generalization – the presented evidence, while suggestive, is not sufficiently compelling to warrant acceptance. The experiments, while numerous, lack a rigorous comparison and control for confounding factors. The paper primarily *observes* a correlation rather than *demonstrating* a causal link.

The paper’s core argument relies on the observation that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima. However, several factors could contribute to this, and the paper does not adequately disentangle them. The use of ADAM as the optimizer for both small and large batch sizes is a significant limitation. It's unclear if the observed behavior is specific to ADAM or generalizes to other optimizers.  Furthermore, the choice of batch sizes (256 vs 10% of the training data) is somewhat arbitrary and doesn't explore a smooth transition between the two regimes. The paper lacks a systematic investigation of the impact of learning rate scaling with batch size, which is a known best practice and could significantly influence the sharpness of the minima achieved. The metric for sharpness, while computationally feasible, is a heuristic and its validity as a true measure of minimizer sharpness isn’t thoroughly established. While comparing it to eigenvalue analysis, the paper does not *calculate* eigenvalues, leaving the reader without a strong understanding of its correlation to the underlying Hessian. The attempts at remedies in Appendix E are brief and do not offer substantial improvements, but a more thorough exploration could have strengthened the paper.

The parametric plots, while visually appealing, are limited to 1D slices and curves, which may not fully represent the high-dimensional loss landscape. The discussion section acknowledges existing theoretical work but doesn't offer a clear connection between the empirical findings and these theories, nor does it propose new theoretical directions. The presentation of the results is often descriptive without sufficient statistical rigor. For example, the claim of a "1-2 order of magnitude difference" in sharpness metrics needs to be substantiated with statistical tests.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Optimizer Control:** Conduct experiments with multiple optimizers (SGD, Momentum, etc.) for both small and large batch sizes to determine if the observed behavior is specific to ADAM.
*   **Learning Rate Scaling:** Implement and evaluate learning rate scaling strategies (e.g., linear scaling rule) for large-batch training.
*   **Systematic Batch Size Variation:** Explore a wider range of batch sizes, including intermediate values, to observe a smoother transition in performance and sharpness.
*   **Eigenvalue Analysis:** If computationally feasible for smaller networks, perform eigenvalue analysis of the Hessian to validate the sharpness metric and establish a stronger connection to the underlying loss landscape.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Include statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to support the claims about differences in sharpness and generalization performance.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** Elaborate on the relationship between the empirical findings and existing theoretical work on the loss landscape of deep neural networks.
*   **More Robust Remedies:** Investigate the proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, etc.) in greater depth, potentially exploring different hyperparameters and combinations of techniques.
*   **Clarify Sharpness Metric:** Provide a more detailed justification for the chosen sharpness metric and its limitations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the observed results to the choice of optimizer? Have you experimented with SGD and other optimization algorithms?
2.  Have you implemented learning rate scaling strategies for large-batch training, and if so, how did they affect the results?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the limitations of the sharpness metric and its correlation with the eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix?
4.  Were any efforts made to estimate the computational cost of eigenvalue calculation for the networks used, to justify not including it in the analysis?
5.  Why was the specific batch size ratio (256 vs. 10% of the dataset) chosen, and how would different ratios affect the conclusions?
