This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, attributing it to the sharpness of the minimizers found by these methods. The authors provide numerical evidence supporting this claim, showing that large-batch training tends to converge to sharp minimizers with larger eigenvalues in the Hessian, while small-batch methods converge to flatter minima. They also explore potential remedies, such as data augmentation and conservative training, but find limited success in fully addressing the generalization gap.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of sufficient novelty and rigor in establishing a causal link between minimizer sharpness and generalization. While the observation that large-batch methods lead to sharper minima is interesting and aligns with existing literature, the paper doesn’t advance significantly beyond what’s already known. The experiments are descriptive and correlational; they demonstrate a connection but don’t convincingly *prove* that sharpness is the root cause of the generalization gap. Additionally, the sharpness metric used (Equation 4) is a heuristic and its direct correlation to generalization performance isn't fully justified or validated.

Further, the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) are standard techniques, and the reported limited success lacks detailed analysis. The paper states these don't "completely remedy the problem," but doesn’t offer a nuanced explanation of *why* they fail to fully close the gap, beyond reiterating the sharpness issue.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear presentation of the problem and empirical observations, but it lacks the theoretical depth or significant empirical advancement required for publication at ICLR. The connection to existing literature is acknowledged but doesn’t present a substantial contribution that builds upon or challenges the current understanding.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Lack of Causal Proof:** The paper establishes a correlation between sharpness and generalization, but doesn’t offer a rigorous proof of causality. It's possible other factors, not accounted for, contribute to the generalization gap.
* **Heuristic Metric:** The sharpness metric is presented as computationally feasible, but its relationship to generalization performance is not firmly established. A more robust metric, even if computationally expensive, would strengthen the claims. The justification for using the specific parameters in the sharpness metric (e.g., the value of epsilon) is lacking.
* **Limited Novelty in Remedies:** The explored remedies are well-known techniques, and the paper doesn't present novel insights into why they fail to fully resolve the issue for large-batch training.
* **Descriptive Nature of Experiments:** The experiments primarily *show* the correlation rather than actively *testing* the hypothesis that sharpness causes the generalization gap. Controlled experiments, perhaps involving artificially shaping the loss landscape, would provide stronger evidence.
* **Missing Error Bars:** Many tables present values without accompanying standard deviations. This makes it difficult to assess the statistical significance of the findings.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Theoretical Justification:** Expand on the theoretical arguments linking sharp minima to poor generalization, drawing more heavily on existing literature (MDL, Bayesian perspectives).
* **Metric Validation:** Provide a more thorough validation of the sharpness metric. Compare its performance to alternative metrics, and explore its sensitivity to various network architectures and datasets.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more detailed ablation studies on the remedies. For instance, systematically vary the data augmentation parameters or the regularization strength in conservative training to understand their individual contributions.
* **Loss Landscape Visualization:** Explore more sophisticated techniques for visualizing the loss landscape, beyond the 1D parametric plots, to better understand the geometry of the minima.
* **Alternative Optimizers:** Explore the effect of different optimization algorithms (e.g., second-order methods) in both small-batch and large-batch settings.
* **Explore Initializations:** How does different initialization strategies affect the sharpness of minima found by large-batch methods?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. What theoretical guarantees, if any, support the claim that sharpness, as measured by your metric, directly impacts generalization performance?
2. Why was the specific value of epsilon chosen for the sharpness metric (Equation 4)? What is the sensitivity of the results to this parameter?
3. What further analyses were performed to understand *why* the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) did not fully close the generalization gap? Were there any indications of diminishing returns or other limiting factors?
4. Were any attempts made to directly manipulate the sharpness of the minima during training, for example, through a penalty term added to the loss function?
5. Could you clarify the statement "the loss function landscape of deep neural networks is such that large-batch methods are attracted to regions with sharp minimizers"? Is this a property of the landscape itself, or a consequence of the optimization algorithm used (ADAM)?
