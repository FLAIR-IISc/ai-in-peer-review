- Paper claims large-batch training converges to sharp minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) causing worse generalization than small batches which find flatter minima.  
- Decision: Reject — empirical results interesting but insufficient theoretical grounding and unsuccessful remedies.  
- Empirical focus with limited theoretical grounding: correlation shown but causality not established, leaving the paper incomplete.  
- Sharpness metric concerns: maximization step is computationally expensive and sensitive to epsilon and choice/dimensionality of A, with robustness not fully validated.  
- Limited novelty: findings (large-batch → poorer generalization / sharp minima) largely corroborate prior work rather than being a breakthrough.  
- Unsuccessful remedial approaches: data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization provided limited improvement without explanation for failures.  
- Lack of comparative analysis with existing techniques: insufficient comparisons to large-batch mitigation methods like LARS or normalization strategies.  
- Theoretical exploration suggested: investigate SGD dynamics and batch-size effects on Hessian spectral properties to explain observed correlation.  
- Robustness analysis suggested: perform sensitivity studies on epsilon and A’s dimensionality and consider alternative sharpness measures.  
- Broader experimental scope suggested: test more datasets and modern architectures (e.g., Transformers).  
- Compare to state-of-the-art: evaluate against recent large-batch training techniques explicitly.  
- Deeper analysis of failed remedies: analyze tuning, limitations, and reasons those approaches did not improve generalization.  
- Clarify relation to prior work: better articulate how this study builds on and differs from existing literature.  
- Q1: Have you tested other optimizers (e.g., LARS) to see if results hold beyond ADAM?  
- Q2: How was the sharpness maximization performed and how sensitive are results to epsilon and A’s dimensionality?  
- Q3: Can you provide evidence (e.g., trajectory visualizations) that large-batch methods stay near initialization?  
- Q4: Why did the remedial approaches fail—insufficient tuning or fundamental limitations?  
- Q5: Could different learning-rate schedules mitigate the sharpness/generalization issue for large batches?
