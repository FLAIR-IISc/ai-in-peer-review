This paper investigates the relationship between batch size and generalization performance in deep learning, positing that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima, leading to a generalization gap. The authors present numerical experiments on several network configurations and datasets to support this claim, comparing the sharpness of minima found by small-batch and large-batch training. They also explore potential remedies like data augmentation and conservative training, finding limited success.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding the sharpness of minima and its correlation with generalization is potentially insightful, the paper suffers from a multitude of methodological shortcomings, a lack of rigorous analysis, and insufficient justification for key design choices. The numerous minor flaws accumulate to a point where accepting the paper would set a problematic precedent for ICLR.

The primary concern is the lack of robust experimental validation. The choice of ADAM as the sole optimizer for both small and large batch regimes feels restrictive and unsubstantiated. It's unclear why other optimizers weren’t considered for both scenarios, particularly given the claim that the *method* (batch size) is the primary driver of the observed effect, not the optimizer itself.  Furthermore, the batch sizes used (10% of the training data for large batch, 256 for small batch) are rather arbitrary. The paper asserts these choices without adequate justification, and doesn't explore a wider range of batch sizes to assess the robustness of the observed trends.  The limited exploration of hyperparameters beyond the batch size weakens the conclusions. The fixed learning rate for ADAM is a particularly concerning omission.

The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is also problematic. While acknowledging its imperfection, the authors fail to adequately assess its validity or sensitivity. The reliance on a randomly sampled matrix *A* with a fixed dimension (100) is ad-hoc and lacks theoretical grounding.  The number of L-BFGS iterations (10) is also arbitrary and could significantly affect the computed sharpness.  A more thorough analysis of the metric's correlation with actual generalization performance on unseen data would be required.  The fact that the metric, as presented, approximates the Ritz value raises concerns about its interpretability and applicability.

Several aspects of the presentation are concerning. The organization is somewhat disjointed, with the notation section feeling unnecessary given its limited scope. The frequent reference to appendices (A, B, C, E) disrupts the flow of the main text and suggests that crucial details are relegated to supplementary material. The repeated emphasis on the "generalization gap" (defined as up to 5% in the introduction) seems disproportionate given the lack of a comprehensive statistical analysis demonstrating its significance. Tables 3 and 4 present results with two different values of ' ', without a clear explanation for the choice of these values.

Finally, the discussion of related work feels superficial. While acknowledging existing literature on flat vs. sharp minima, the paper fails to adequately position its contribution within this context or to critically analyze the limitations of previous approaches.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Optimizer Choice:** Justify the exclusive use of ADAM. Conduct experiments with other optimizers (SGD, RMSprop) for both small and large batch sizes.
*   **Batch Size Sweep:** Perform a more comprehensive sweep of batch sizes to determine the optimal threshold for each network architecture.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** Systematically tune hyperparameters (learning rate, weight decay) for both small and large batch regimes.
*   **Sharpness Metric Validation:** Provide a more rigorous analysis of the sharpness metric, including its correlation with generalization performance and its sensitivity to the choice of parameters (e.g., the dimension of *A*, the number of L-BFGS iterations).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Perform statistical tests to assess the significance of the observed differences in sharpness and generalization performance.
*   **Clearer Presentation:** Streamline the organization of the paper, moving essential details from the appendices into the main text.
*   **Detailed Related Work:** Provide a more in-depth discussion of related work, including a critical analysis of existing literature on flat vs. sharp minima.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the theoretical justification for choosing a dimension of 100 for the matrix *A* in the sharpness metric? How sensitive are the results to this choice?
2.  How were the initial starting points for the training runs selected, and how was the random seed chosen?
3.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the conservative training approach failed to improve generalization performance, despite its theoretical underpinnings?
4.  How does the performance of the proposed sharpness metric compare to other metrics for characterizing the geometry of the loss landscape (e.g., Hessian eigenvalues)?
5.  Was the observed 5% generalization gap consistent across different random seeds and hyperparameter settings? If not, what was the variability observed?
