This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models. The authors hypothesize and present numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers of the loss function, which exhibit poorer generalization performance compared to the flatter minimizers found with small-batch methods. The work explores this phenomenon across several network architectures and datasets, utilizing parametric plots and a sharpness metric to characterize the minimizers.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting observation and provides supporting empirical evidence, but the analysis lacks sufficient depth and rigor to warrant acceptance. The core claim – a direct link between large batch size and sharper minima leading to poor generalization – is plausible, but not conclusively demonstrated. The provided "sharpness" metric, while computationally feasible, feels somewhat *ad hoc* and its direct correlation to generalization ability isn't firmly established. The paper also lacks a thorough discussion of limitations and potential confounding factors.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Weakly Defined Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a heuristic. While the authors acknowledge this and relate it to the spectrum of the Hessian, its validity as a reliable indicator of generalization performance is not convincingly argued. The sensitivity of the metric to hyperparameters (e.g., the size of the neighborhood ‘epsilon’ and the dimension of the random subspace ‘p’) is not adequately discussed. Does varying these parameters significantly alter the conclusions? 

2. **Limited Scope of Experiments:** The experiments, while extensive, primarily focus on variations of AlexNet and VGGNet. Generalizing the observed phenomenon to other network architectures (e.g., Transformers, more recent architectures) and datasets is not demonstrated. The authors mention results on other networks are similar, but providing those results directly would significantly strengthen the paper.

3. **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper leans heavily on empirical observations. While acknowledging relevant theoretical work, it doesn’t offer any theoretical insight into *why* large-batch methods gravitate towards sharper minima. A theoretical exploration, even a preliminary one, would significantly enhance the contribution. 

4. **Insufficient Comparison to Related Work:** While the introduction cites relevant work on generalization, sharpness, and optimization, the paper doesn’t deeply engage with these studies. For example, the relationship to recent work on the role of flat minima in generalization (e.g., studies of mode connectivity) isn’t fully explored.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Sensitivity Analysis of Sharpness Metric:** Conduct a sensitivity analysis of the sharpness metric to its hyperparameters and demonstrate its robustness.
* **Explore Different Architectures & Datasets:** Include results from a wider range of network architectures and datasets to assess the generalizability of the findings.
* **Investigate Alternative Sharpness Measures:** Compare the proposed metric to other established measures of curvature (e.g., using eigenvalue distributions of the Hessian).
* **Theoretical Exploration:** Attempt to provide a theoretical explanation (even a simplified one) for why large batch sizes might favor sharp minima. Consider exploring the impact of batch size on the Fisher Information Matrix.
* **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to isolate the impact of different factors (e.g., optimizer, learning rate schedule) on the observed phenomenon.
* **Discuss Limitations:** Explicitly acknowledge the limitations of the study (e.g., the heuristic nature of the sharpness metric, the limited scope of the experiments).




**Questions for the authors:**

1. How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (epsilon and p) in the sharpness metric (Equation 4)? Can you provide a quantitative analysis of this sensitivity?
2. Have you observed similar behavior on more recent network architectures, such as Transformers? If so, can you include those results?
3. Can you provide any intuition, even speculative, regarding *why* large batch sizes tend to converge to sharper minima? Is it related to the noise level of the gradient, the exploration properties of the optimizer, or something else?
4. Have you considered investigating the Fisher Information Matrix as a measure of sharpness, and how does it correlate with your proposed metric and the generalization gap?
5. The paper mentions attempted remedies in Appendix E. Can you expand on the results of these remedies in the main paper, detailing why they were not completely effective?
