- Paper claims large-batch (LB) methods yield sharper minima correlated with worse generalization, while small-batch (SB) methods find flatter minima.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Key contribution is largely observational and reiterates previously reported LB→sharper minima phenomenon (limited novelty).  
- Empirical evaluation is narrow (few architectures/datasets) with incomplete hyperparameter details, harming reproducibility and generality.  
- Proposed sharpness metric is heuristic and not shown to outperform or correlate better than existing metrics.  
- Parametric 1D loss plots are suggestive but insufficient to characterize the full loss landscape.  
- Paper lacks a solid theoretical framework explaining why LB converges to sharper minima (e.g., role of gradient variance or curvature).  
- Remedies are briefly treated and inconclusive, offering no promising mitigation strategy.  
- Recommendation: narrow focus (e.g., rigorously validate the sharpness metric or one remedy) to increase impact.  
- Recommendation: expand experiments across more architectures (including transformers), datasets, optimizers, and include sensitivity analyses/confidence intervals.  
- Recommendation: develop theoretical analysis linking batch size to sharpness and generalization.  
- Recommendation: use richer landscape analyses (beyond 1D slices) and compare sharpness metric to Hessian-based and other established measures.  
- Questions for authors: provide validation vs. training loss plots (early stopping evidence), quantify computational cost/scalability of the sharpness metric, and report sharpness comparisons across optimizers beyond ADAM.
