- Paper studies the generalization gap with large-batch training and hypothesizes large batches converge to sharp minima with worse generalization.  
- Authors provide numerical evidence across architectures/datasets that large-batch methods find minima with large positive Hessian eigenvalues while small-batch methods find flatter minima.  
- Experiments support the correlation between sharpness and poorer generalization but offer no compelling mechanistic explanation for why large batches favor sharp minima.  
- Decision: Reject — observations are interesting but the work is incomplete and lacks sufficient rigor for acceptance.  
- Main critique: the paper is descriptive rather than explanatory, relying on intuitive arguments without formal grounding.  
- Limited novelty: sharpness/flatness and their link to generalization are well-studied; the proposed sharpness metric is heuristic and acknowledged as limited.  
- Remedies section is underdeveloped: attempted fixes (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are briefly reported and don’t fully resolve the issue.  
- Statistical rigor is lacking: no explicit significance tests provided for differences in sharpness or generalization metrics.  
- Presentation is uneven and reads like a research report; introduction should be more concise and focused on contributions.  
- Recommendation: develop even a preliminary theoretical framework explaining why large batches gravitate to sharp minima, possibly relating to curvature or optimizer properties.  
- Suggest deeper analysis of remedies to understand why they fail and more thorough exploration of their limitations.  
- Advise adding statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate robustness of findings.  
- Recommend expanding related-work discussion and clearly positioning contributions relative to theoretical analyses of generalization and loss landscapes.  
- Suggest investigating optimizer dependence (e.g., beyond ADAM) and the interaction between learning rate and batch size.  
- Questions for authors include clarifying the MDL connection, Hessian computation costs and approximations, reasons remedies underperformed, relation between threshold batch size and curvature, and limitations/biases of the sharpness metric.
