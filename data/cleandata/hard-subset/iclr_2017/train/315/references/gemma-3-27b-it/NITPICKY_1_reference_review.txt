This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, positing that this gap is correlated with the sharpness of the minima found by these methods. The authors present numerical evidence supporting this claim, using various network configurations and datasets, and explore the differences in minima found by small and large-batch training. They also briefly discuss potential remedies for the generalization issue and present an idealized performance model suggesting potential speedups with large-batch training.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding the correlation between sharpness of minima and generalization gap is intuitively plausible and supported by empirical results, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and depth to warrant acceptance. The analysis feels superficial, and the experimental setup, while broad in scope, suffers from a lack of critical detail and justification. The writing is also somewhat repetitive and lacks conciseness.

The primary argument rests on empirical observations. However, the metrics used to quantify “sharpness” (Equation 4) feel ad-hoc and lack a strong theoretical justification. While the authors acknowledge this, they don’t fully explore the limitations or potential biases introduced by this metric. The sensitivity analysis using different values of 'lambda' (Table 3 & 4) is minimal. Furthermore, the claim that the observed phenomena align with theoretical work (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; MDL theory; Bayesian learning) is asserted rather than demonstrated. There is no attempt to connect these theoretical frameworks to the empirical findings in a meaningful way. 

The experimental details are also concerning. The choice of batch sizes (10% of the training data and 256) appears somewhat arbitrary. Why were these specific values chosen, and how sensitive are the results to these choices? The authors state that experiments were conducted five times with different initializations, but the standard deviations reported in the tables are often surprisingly small, raising questions about the robustness of the results. The convergence criteria are not specified – the networks were trained “until the loss function ceased to improve,” which is vague and subjective. The justification for using ADAM as the optimizer for all experiments (even when contrasting small and large batch methods) is weak. Different optimizers might exhibit different behavior with varying batch sizes, confounding the analysis. The analysis in Section 3 regarding the success of small-batch methods feels descriptive rather than analytical. The claim about SB methods pushing iterates *out* of sharp minima is not convincingly demonstrated. 

Finally, the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are presented without sufficient detail or analysis. The results indicate that these approaches offer limited improvement, but the authors don’t delve into the reasons *why* they fail to fully address the problem.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Sharpeness Metric:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the chosen sharpness metric or explore alternative, more theoretically grounded measures. Analyze the sensitivity of the results to the choice of metric.
*   **Experimental Rigor:** Provide detailed experimental settings, including the specific convergence criteria, optimizer parameters, and justification for the chosen batch sizes. Report more comprehensive statistical analysis (e.g., confidence intervals) to support the claims.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** Strengthen the connection between the empirical findings and the cited theoretical work. Explain *how* the theoretical frameworks support the observed phenomena.
*   **Optimizer Choice:** Justify the use of ADAM consistently throughout the experiments. Consider exploring the impact of different optimizers on the observed behavior.
*   **Remedies Analysis:** Conduct a more in-depth analysis of the attempted remedies. Investigate the reasons for their limited success and explore potential modifications to improve their effectiveness.
*   **Clarity and Conciseness:** Streamline the writing to eliminate redundancy and improve clarity.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of batch sizes (10% and 256)? Have you explored a wider range of batch sizes?
2.  What specific convergence criteria were used to terminate the training process?
3.  Could you elaborate on the choice of ADAM as the optimizer and explain why you believe it doesn’t confound the analysis?
4.  What is the justification for choosing a random matrix A in equation (4)? Could other matrix constructions lead to different or more informative results?
5.  Regarding the remedies explored, were any hyperparameter sweeps performed to optimize their performance? If so, please provide details.
