- Summary: Paper shows large-batch training tends to converge to sharper minima (more large positive Hessian eigenvalues) correlated with worse generalization, supported by empirical results and a proposed sharpness metric.
- Decision: Reject — interesting observation but analysis lacks rigor, causality, and a compelling solution.
- Main critique — Correlational not causal: evidence links sharpness and generalization but does not prove sharpness causes the gap; other factors may explain results.
- Sharpness metric concerns: proposed metric is ad-hoc, relies on random matrix A and limited L-BFGS iterations, with no rigorous link to true Hessian eigenvalues.
- Limited exploration of remedies: data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization were tried superficially and conclusions about their insufficiency are premature.
- Lack of theoretical grounding: paper is mainly empirical and does not meaningfully extend or connect to existing theory on loss landscapes and flatness.
- Suggested experiment — Manipulate sharpness (e.g., regularizer that encourages flatness) to test causal effect on generalization.
- Suggested robustness checks — sensitivity analysis of sharpness metric to A, its dimensionality, and L-BFGS iterations; compare with full Hessian eigenvalues on small networks.
- Suggested remedy studies — systematic exploration, parameter sweeps, combinations, and ablation studies for proposed fixes.
- Theoretical suggestion — attempt to explain observed sharpness differences via existing theory or propose needed theoretical modifications.
- Clarify batch-size choices: justify using 10% of data for LB and 256 for SB.
- Report computational costs: quantify cost of computing the sharpness metric.
- Question 1: Clarify how "low precision" flat minimizers relate to model sensitivity to input perturbations.
- Question 2: Report sensitivity to random initialization: results across multiple seeds and runs?
- Question 3: Show whether early stopping affects the generalization gap to rule out overfitting explanations.
- Question 4: How does choice of random matrix A (dimensionality/elements) affect sharpness estimates; have other approximation methods been tried?
- Question 5: Provide details on the data augmentations used and rationale per dataset.
- Question 6: Discuss limitations: architectures or datasets where sharpness–generalization relationship might fail.
