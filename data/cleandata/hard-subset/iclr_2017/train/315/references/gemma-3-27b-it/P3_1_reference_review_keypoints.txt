- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse, hypothesizing large batches converge to sharper minima and proposing a sharpness metric.
- Decision: Reject — interesting observations but insufficient causal evidence and limited novelty.
- Main finding: Large-batch methods correlate with sharper minima and worse generalization.
- Correlation vs. causation: Evidence is correlational; sharpness may not be the causal factor.
- Limited novelty: Sharp vs. flat minima is known; metric and context are incremental and partly ad-hoc.
- Weak remediation attempts: Data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization are tried but results are underwhelming and not deeply analyzed.
- Lack of theoretical grounding: Empirical results aren’t rigorously connected to existing theoretical frameworks on loss landscapes.
- Presentation issues: Paper reads descriptive rather than analytical; some tables and results are not well integrated into the narrative.
- Suggestion: Strengthen causal claims by testing interventions that actively flatten minima and measure resulting generalization.
- Suggestion: Build connections to theoretical work to elevate empirical findings to a foundational contribution.
- Suggestion: Analyze in depth why attempted remedies fail in the large-batch regime rather than only reporting negative results.
- Suggestion: Perform sensitivity analysis on the sharpness metric and compare to other measures (e.g., Hessian eigenvalues).
- Suggestion: Broaden experiments across architectures, datasets, and optimizers to test robustness of findings.
- Suggestion: Quantify and analyze the role of SGD noise and its relation to batch size and sharpness.
- Question: Provide details on the computational cost of the proposed sharpness metric for large networks.
- Question: Have you analyzed curvature (Hessian) around minima and its relation to the sharpness metric?
- Question: Why was ADAM chosen as the primary optimizer; were alternatives like SGD with momentum tested and did results vary?
- Question: Explain in detail why data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization did not mitigate the large-batch generalization gap.
- Question: How was overfitting ruled out beyond early stopping — were learned features or decision boundaries analyzed?
