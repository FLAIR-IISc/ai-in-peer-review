## Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models. The authors hypothesize and provide empirical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to *sharper* minimizers of the loss landscape compared to small-batch methods, which find *flatter* minima. They argue that this sharpness contributes to poorer generalization performance. The paper presents a series of experiments across different network architectures and datasets, using metrics to quantify the sharpness of the found minima and demonstrating a correlation between sharpness and generalization gap. They also explore potential remedies, finding limited success with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper identifies an interesting and important phenomenon – the link between large batch training, sharp minima, and generalization – the analysis lacks sufficient rigor and the evidence presented isn’t compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The paper feels more like exploratory data analysis rather than a conclusive study.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Lack of Novelty in Observation:** The observation that large batch sizes can lead to poorer generalization is well-known in the deep learning community. The novelty lies in linking this to the sharpness of minima. However, the paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate *causation*; it shows correlation.
*   **Weakness of Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) relies on maximizing the loss function within a constrained neighborhood.  This maximization is performed inexactly with only a limited number of L-BFGS-B iterations, raising concerns about its reliability and sensitivity. The authors acknowledge this, but the metric remains a significant weakness. There's insufficient justification for why this specific metric is a good proxy for the true Hessian eigenvalues or the curvature of the loss surface.
*   **Limited Exploration of Remedial Strategies:** While the authors attempt to mitigate the generalization gap, the methods employed (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are relatively standard and don't demonstrate substantial improvements. The analysis of *why* these methods fail to resolve the issue is shallow.
*   **Insufficient Theoretical Grounding:** The paper relies heavily on empirical observations. While acknowledging related theoretical work, it doesn’t develop a theoretical framework to explain the observed phenomenon.  A stronger theoretical foundation would significantly strengthen the argument.
*   **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is somewhat repetitive, especially in sections 2.2 and 3, and lacks a concise summary of key findings. The organization could be improved for better flow.

**4. Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Robustness of Sharpness Metric:** The authors should thoroughly investigate the sensitivity of their results to the parameters of the sharpness metric (ε and the matrix A). Perform ablation studies to demonstrate that the conclusions are not driven by specific choices of these parameters. Compare their metric to other curvature-based measures (e.g., using Hessian approximations).
*   **Causality Demonstration:**  More direct methods to demonstrate a causal link between sharpness and generalization would be valuable. For example, could they project a solution found with a large batch onto a flatter minimum using a small batch, and observe improved generalization?
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Explore theoretical explanations for why large-batch methods might gravitate towards sharper minima. Could the characteristics of the stochastic gradient noise play a role?
*   **Impact of Network Architecture:** How does the observed phenomenon vary with different network architectures (e.g., residual networks, transformers)? Do some architectures exhibit a stronger correlation between batch size, sharpness, and generalization?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  How sensitive are the results to learning rate, optimizer choice, and other hyperparameters?

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  You mention the difficulty of computing the full Hessian. Have you considered using computationally cheaper approximations of the Hessian (e.g., Fisher Information Matrix) to further validate your findings?
2.  The sharpness metric relies on maximizing the loss function in a neighborhood. How confident are you that the inexact maximization performed doesn't introduce significant bias in the results? Did you try different solvers or more iterations for the maximization step?
3.  The paper suggests SB methods “escape” sharp minima. Can you characterize this escape mechanism more precisely? Is it related to the magnitude of the stochastic gradient noise, or other factors?
4.  In the warm-starting experiments, how does the convergence speed of the large-batch method change as the number of initial SB epochs increases?  Is there a point of diminishing returns?
5.   While you showed that the generalization gap isn’t due to overfitting in the traditional sense, have you considered whether large-batch methods might be converging to solutions that are more sensitive to perturbations in the input data?



In conclusion, while the paper raises a relevant question, the current presentation and evidence are insufficient to justify acceptance. Significant revisions addressing the concerns outlined above are needed to strengthen the scientific rigor and impact of the work.
