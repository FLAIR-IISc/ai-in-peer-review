- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse, linking it to convergence to sharper minima with more large positive Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Authors introduce a pragmatic sharpness metric and run experiments across architectures/datasets, with limited discussion of remedies.  
- Decision: Reject due to insufficiently robust empirical evidence, limited theoretical grounding, and weak remediation results.  
- Observation of large-batch generalization gap is not novel; paper doesn’t prove sharpness is the primary cause amid other factors (LR schedules, optimizer, architecture).  
- The proposed sharpness metric lacks rigorous validation and may be sensitive to the choice of matrix A and limited L-BFGS iterations.  
- Remediation experiments (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are brief and show limited improvement without explaining why.  
- Paper is overly empirical and needs a theoretical framework linking batch size, sharpness, and generalization error.  
- Presentation is redundant and disjointed; writing and organization need tightening.  
- Need more detailed comparisons of Hessian eigenvalue distributions between small and large batches and low-dimensional loss landscape visualizations.  
- Require ablation studies on sharpness metric components (neighborhood size, choices of A, optimization iterations).  
- Explore more advanced regularization/optimization methods and learning rate schedules tailored to large-batch training.  
- Attempt to connect findings to theoretical work (e.g., flat minima, PAC-Bayes bounds) to strengthen contributions.  
- Explicitly state study limitations (datasets, architectures, task-dependence) and scope of conclusions.  
- Question: Is there a “critical” batch size threshold where the generalization gap consistently appears across models/datasets?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to matrix A and number of L-BFGS iterations, and how does the metric compare to other sharpness measures?  
- Question: Can authors rule out alternative causes like different optimization trajectories or distinct local minima?  
- Question: Why did remediation strategies fail—what specific challenges prevent addressing sharpness in large-batch training?  
- Question: How might observed sharpness relate to basin-hopping or mode connectivity phenomena in high-dimensional loss landscapes?  
- Conclusion: Topic is important but current work lacks rigor and depth; addressing empirical, theoretical, and experimental gaps would improve acceptance chances.
