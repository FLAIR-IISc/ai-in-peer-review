This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when increasing batch size in deep learning. The authors hypothesize and provide empirical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima in the loss landscape, which negatively impacts generalization performance, while small-batch methods find flatter minima. They explore this phenomenon through parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and briefly investigate potential remedies like data augmentation and conservative training.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding the sharpness of minima and its correlation with generalization is interesting and aligns with existing intuition, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance. The work primarily *demonstrates* a phenomenon already recognized in the field rather than offering a truly novel insight or solution. The empirical validation is limited and lacks a comprehensive comparison to well-established baselines beyond ADAM with different batch sizes.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of a strong theoretical grounding. The paper relies heavily on empirical observations and loosely connects them to existing theoretical concepts like MDL. While the proposed sharpness metric is a reasonable proxy, it's not rigorously justified and its correlation with generalization is demonstrated rather than proven. Simply showing sharper minima correlate with poorer generalization isn’t particularly surprising; the critical question is *why* large batch methods gravitate towards these minima, and the paper doesn’t offer a compelling answer beyond stating it as an observation. 

Furthermore, the experimental setup feels somewhat ad-hoc. The choice of networks and datasets, while common, isn't justified with respect to the specific hypothesis. The limited exploration of different optimizers (only ADAM consistently) is a concern, and the attempts at remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) feel cursory and lack a detailed analysis of *why* they fail to fully address the problem. The results are presented descriptively without statistical significance testing, hindering confidence in the findings. The claim of a 5% generalization gap in smaller networks is mentioned without providing supporting details.

Additional feedback:

*   **Theoretical justification:** Provide a more solid theoretical rationale for the observed connection between sharpness and generalization. Explore existing theories (e.g., PAC-Bayes bounds, information bottleneck) and attempt to connect them to the findings.
*   **Baseline comparison:** Compare the performance of large-batch methods with and without the proposed remedies against stronger regularization techniques (e.g., weight decay, dropout) and other optimization algorithms known for good generalization (e.g., SGD with momentum).
*   **Statistical significance:** Include statistical significance testing (e.g., t-tests) to validate the observed differences in sharpness and generalization performance.
*   **Metric validation:** Provide a more thorough justification for the proposed sharpness metric and its relationship to the Hessian eigenvalues. Explore alternative metrics and compare their performance.
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameters, such as learning rate, regularization strength, and batch size.
*   **Explain the choice of 10% for large batch size:** Why this specific percentage? How does this affect generalization?

Questions for the authors:

*   What specific properties of large-batch optimization lead to convergence to sharper minima? Is it purely a matter of gradient noise, or are there other factors at play?
*   Could the observed effect be mitigated by adaptive learning rate schedules that adjust based on the curvature of the loss landscape?
*   Have you explored the impact of different network architectures on the observed sharpness of minima?
*   Is there evidence to suggest that different datasets exhibit varying degrees of sensitivity to the sharpness of minima?
*   Beyond data augmentation and conservative training, what other strategies might be effective in steering large-batch methods towards flatter minima?
