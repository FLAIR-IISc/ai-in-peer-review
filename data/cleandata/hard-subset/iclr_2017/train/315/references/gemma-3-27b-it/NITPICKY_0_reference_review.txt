This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, attributing it to the sharpness of the minima found by these methods. The authors present numerical experiments across several network configurations to support this claim, demonstrating that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima characterized by larger eigenvalues in the Hessian, while small-batch methods find flatter minima. They also explore potential remedies, like data augmentation and conservative training, but find limited success.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding the sharpness of minima and its potential link to generalization is interesting and aligns with recent work, the paper suffers from a multitude of methodological shortcomings and lacks sufficient rigor to warrant acceptance. The pervasive lack of detail, combined with inconsistencies and a surprisingly superficial exploration of established concepts, leads to a lack of confidence in the presented results and conclusions.

Here’s a detailed breakdown of the concerns:

1. **Lack of Rigor in Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) relies on maximizing the loss within a defined box. The choice of the box size parameter, ‘’, is arbitrary and lacks justification. More importantly, the maximization is performed using only 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B. This is wholly inadequate for a high-dimensional non-convex problem and renders the sharpness measure unreliable. The authors acknowledge this limitation but dismiss it as a practical necessity. A more robust or at least better-justified metric, or at least a sensitivity analysis on the metric’s parameters, is essential.

2. **Inconsistent Batch Size Definition:** The paper defines “large-batch” and “small-batch” but uses batch sizes (10% of training data and 256) that are highly dataset-dependent. A batch size of 10% of the MNIST dataset is vastly different from 10% of ImageNet. This makes comparisons across different networks (Table 1) and even within the same network with different datasets difficult to interpret. A more consistent and relative definition (e.g., relative to the total dataset size or model parameter count) is needed.

3. **Limited Exploration of Optimizer Parameters:** The authors consistently use the ADAM optimizer with unspecified hyperparameters.  The sensitivity of results to ADAM’s hyperparameters (learning rate, betas) is not investigated, yet these can significantly impact convergence and the resulting minima.

4. **Insufficient Statistical Power:** While the authors mention conducting experiments five times, the presentation of results (e.g., Tables 3 & 4) lacks proper statistical significance testing. Simply reporting means and standard deviations is insufficient to draw firm conclusions. Statistical tests are needed to demonstrate that the observed differences in sharpness are statistically significant.

5. **Superficial Discussion of Related Work:** The discussion of related work feels perfunctory. The paper references relevant papers (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, Chaudhari et al.), but the connection to their findings is often weak. The authors don't sufficiently engage with the existing literature on flat vs. sharp minima or the implications of their findings for these areas.

6. **Formatting & Notation Issues:** The document contains several minor formatting inconsistencies (e.g., inconsistent spacing, odd line breaks). The notation, while generally understandable, could be more consistent and standardized (e.g., using boldface for vectors).

7. **Weak Attempts to Remedy the Problem:** The investigated methods (data augmentation, conservative training) are presented without sufficient details or a clear rationale for their selection. The lack of significant improvements suggests these approaches may not be suitable and warrants a more thorough exploration, or an admission that solving the problem is beyond the scope of this paper.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the motivation:** Why is scaling to larger batch sizes specifically important *now*? What bottlenecks are being hit with current methods? The idealized performance model (Appendix C) is vague and doesn’t convincingly establish the need for this work.
*   **Provide more visualization:** Beyond the parametric plots, visualize the loss landscape using dimensionality reduction techniques (e.g., t-SNE) to better understand the difference between the basins of attraction of small-batch and large-batch methods.
*   **Investigate the influence of network architecture:** How does the sharpness of minima vary with different network architectures (e.g., residual networks, transformers)?
*   **Consider alternative metrics:** Explore other metrics for characterizing the flatness/sharpness of minima, such as the Hessian's condition number or the participation ratio.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you justify the choice of the parameter ‘’ in your sharpness metric and demonstrate its impact on the results?
*   How sensitive are your results to the choice of ADAM's hyperparameters? Have you performed a hyperparameter search?
*   What statistical tests were used to assess the significance of the differences in sharpness between small-batch and large-batch methods?
*   Given the limitations of the attempted remedies, what are your thoughts on alternative strategies for mitigating the generalization gap with large-batch methods?
*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the idealized performance model in Appendix C, quantifying the potential speedups achievable with large-batch methods?
