This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, positing that this gap is correlated with the sharpness of the minimizers found. The authors present numerical evidence supporting this claim, comparing the sharpness of minima obtained via small-batch and large-batch training across several network architectures. They also explore potential remedies, finding limited success with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding the correlation between sharpness and generalization is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper suffers from a lack of rigor, incomplete analysis, and a somewhat superficial exploration of potential solutions. The numerous minor flaws collectively outweigh the novelty of the observation, ultimately hindering confidence in the conclusions drawn.

The primary concern lies with the methodology and presentation of the sharpness metric (Equation 4). The reliance on a randomly sampled matrix 'A' to approximate the Ritz value introduces significant uncertainty and lacks justification for the chosen dimension (p=100). The sensitivity of the results to the choice of 'lambda' in the conservative training section, and its lack of thorough exploration, is another concern. Furthermore, the comparison between small and large batch methods isn't entirely fair; the batch sizes used (10% of the training data for LB vs. 256 for SB) are drastically different in scale, potentially confounding the results. The choice of ADAM as the sole optimizer used for both SB and LB regimes also limits the generalizability of the findings. The paper states that results on other optimizers for LB experiments yielded similar results, but does not include that data. 

The discussion of related work feels cursory. While acknowledging relevant theoretical papers, the authors don't fully engage with how their empirical findings contribute to or challenge existing theoretical understanding. The exploration of potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) feels underdeveloped. The authors acknowledge their limited success but offer little insight into *why* these techniques failed to adequately address the issue. The idealized performance model in Appendix C is overly simplistic and lacks concrete grounding. The repetition of results and the lack of a concise summary of findings further detract from the paper’s clarity. Finally, numerous minor formatting issues and inconsistencies (e.g., differing citation styles, unclear figure captions) detract from the professionalism of the manuscript.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Sharpeness Metric:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the chosen sharpness metric and the selection of parameters like 'lambda' and the dimensionality of matrix 'A'. Conduct a sensitivity analysis to demonstrate the robustness of the results to variations in these parameters.
*   **Batch Size Scaling:** Investigate the impact of different batch size ratios between SB and LB. Normalize batch sizes by the size of the dataset.
*   **Optimizer Choice:** Explore the impact of different optimizers for both SB and LB methods to determine if the observed results are specific to ADAM.
*   **Theoretical Connection:** Deepen the discussion of related theoretical work, exploring how the empirical findings align with or challenge existing models of generalization in deep learning.
*   **Remedy Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of why the attempted remedies failed, potentially investigating alternative strategies or combinations of techniques.
*   **Conciseness and Clarity:** Streamline the presentation, reducing redundancy and improving the overall clarity of the writing. Address the numerous minor formatting issues.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the conclusions regarding sharpness to the choice of the dimension 'p' in the matrix 'A' used in the sharpness metric?
2.  Why was ADAM chosen as the sole optimizer for both small-batch and large-batch experiments, and have you considered other optimizers? If so, what were the results?
3.  How do you explain the lack of improvement observed with the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization)? Are there specific hyperparameters that you believe could have yielded better results?
4.  Can you elaborate on the limitations of the idealized performance model presented in Appendix C and the assumptions that underpin it?
5.  The paper mentions that LB minima do not resemble a cone. Can you quantify this statement and elaborate on the dimensionality of the subspace along which the function rises steeply?
