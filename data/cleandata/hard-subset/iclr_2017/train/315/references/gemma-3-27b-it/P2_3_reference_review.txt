## Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the well-known generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes. The authors present numerical evidence suggesting that this gap is correlated with the *sharpness* of the minimizers found by large-batch methods, as opposed to the *flatness* of those found by small-batch methods. They hypothesize that large-batch methods get stuck in sharp minima, hindering generalization performance, while small-batch methods explore the loss landscape more effectively and converge to flatter minima. The paper explores this relationship through parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and briefly discusses potential remedies.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a potentially important observation – the correlation between sharpness and generalization gap – the analysis lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The presented evidence is largely descriptive and relies heavily on empirical observations without a strong theoretical underpinning or compelling novelty in methodology.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Novelty:** The observation that larger batch sizes can lead to sharper minima and poorer generalization has been previously reported in the literature (though not always framed exactly this way). The core insight isn’t entirely new.  The paper doesn’t present a novel algorithm, theoretical advance, or significantly more insightful empirical analysis that moves the field forward substantially.
* **Weak Empirical Rigor:** The experiments are conducted across a limited number of network architectures and datasets. The parameter tuning details (beyond batch size and optimizer) aren’t thoroughly described, making it difficult to assess the reproducibility and generalizability of the results. The metric for "sharpness" is heuristic and its correlation with generalization isn't demonstrably stronger than other existing metrics. While the parametric plots are suggestive, they only explore a 1D slice of the loss landscape and might not accurately represent the broader picture.
* **Lack of Theoretical Justification:** The paper primarily relies on empirical observation and cites existing theoretical work without establishing a clear connection between the observed sharpness and the underlying theoretical principles. A stronger theoretical framework explaining *why* large batches converge to sharper minima would significantly strengthen the claims.  
* **Insufficient Exploration of Remedies:** The section on attempting to remedy the problem is very brief and the reported results are inconclusive. It doesn't offer a promising path forward for mitigating the generalization gap with large batches.

**4. Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

* **Sharpen the Focus:** The paper attempts to cover a lot of ground – observation, exploration of causes, and attempted remedies. Focusing on a more specific aspect, such as a rigorous analysis of the sharpness metric or a more in-depth investigation of a specific remedy, would strengthen the impact.
* **More Robust Empirical Evaluation:** Expand the experiments to include a wider range of network architectures (including transformers), datasets, and optimizers. Perform a sensitivity analysis of hyperparameters. Consider reporting confidence intervals on the key metrics.
* **Develop a Theoretical Framework:**  Investigate the theoretical reasons why larger batches might lead to sharper minima. Is it related to the variance of the gradients? The curvature of the loss landscape? A theoretical connection would significantly elevate the paper.
* **Analyze the Landscape:** Go beyond 1D parametric plots. Consider visualizing the loss landscape using techniques like t-SNE or UMAP to get a better understanding of the distribution of minima and their corresponding sharpness.
* **Comparative Analysis:** Compare the proposed sharpness metric with other established metrics for evaluating the quality of minima (e.g., Hessian eigenvalues).

**Questions for the Authors:**

*  You mention that the generalization gap isn’t due to overfitting. Could you provide a more detailed analysis showing that early stopping does not improve performance? A plot of validation loss vs. training epochs for both SB and LB would be helpful.
*  What is the computational cost of calculating the proposed sharpness metric for larger networks? Is it scalable enough to be used for hyperparameter tuning or model selection?
*  You mention that the observed behavior is consistent across different optimizers (ADAGRAD, SGD, adaQN). Could you provide a quantitative comparison of the sharpness values obtained with different optimizers for both SB and LB methods?
*  The paper heavily relies on ADAM optimizer. Have you investigated whether the observed sharpness difference exists across different optimizers? If so, could you discuss how different optimizers might influence the shape of the minima?



In conclusion, while the paper touches upon a relevant and important problem, it lacks the depth, novelty, and rigor necessary for acceptance at a conference like ICLR. Addressing the feedback outlined above could significantly strengthen the paper and make it a more valuable contribution to the field.
