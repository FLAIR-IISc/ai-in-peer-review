- Claim: Large-batch training converges to sharp, high-curvature minima that generalize worse, while small-batch finds flatter, better-generalizing minima.  
- Empirical finding: Experiments across architectures/datasets show a correlation between batch size, sharpness, and generalization gap; attempted remedies were inconclusive.  
- Decision: Reject — insufficient novelty and rigor for ICLR.  
- Lack of novelty: Observations largely confirm prior community intuition and earlier work (e.g., Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, MDL).  
- Empirical rigor concerns: Statistical analysis is limited, trial count is small (5), and significance of differences is unclear.  
- Sharpness metric critique: The proposed metric is heuristic and not rigorously tied to generalization or other curvature measures.  
- Remedies underdeveloped: Data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization experiments show little benefit and lack depth.  
- Presentation issues: Paper is verbose, unfocused, and contains tangential sections (e.g., Appendix C).  
- Suggestion: Narrow scope to a specific contribution (e.g., deep analysis of the sharpness metric or one remedy).  
- Suggestion: Increase trials, report statistical significance, provide stronger error bars and robustness checks.  
- Suggestion: Justify the sharpness metric and compare it to Hessian eigenvalues or other curvature measures.  
- Suggestion: Add more theoretical grounding linking results to existing loss-landscape theory.  
- Suggestion: More candidly discuss limitations (heuristic metric, limited architectures/datasets).  
- Suggestion: Systematically explore adaptive batch-size schemes, as warm-starting looked promising.  
- Question: Please justify the choice of the sharpness metric (Eq. 4) and its relation to Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Question: Could the generalization gap reflect different learned features rather than not being due to overfitting, and how was that ruled out?  
- Question: Given limited success of remedies, which avenues do authors consider most promising and what else are they exploring?  
- Question: How sensitive are results to random seeds, and would more trials change the conclusions?
