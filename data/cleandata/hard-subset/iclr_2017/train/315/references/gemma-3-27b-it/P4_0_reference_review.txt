This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes. The authors present numerical evidence suggesting this gap is correlated with the sharpness of the minimizers found by large-batch methods, contrasting them with the flatter minimizers obtained with small-batch methods. The core claim is that large-batch methods get stuck in sharp minima, hindering generalization, while small-batch methods explore a wider range of flatter minima.

**Reject**

The paper, while presenting an interesting observation and relevant background, lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance. The empirical evidence, though extensive in terms of network configurations, doesn't convincingly establish a causal link between sharpness and generalization. The methodology relies heavily on heuristics (like the sharpness metric) and doesn't offer a strong theoretical justification for the observed phenomena. Furthermore, the attempted remedies for the large-batch problem demonstrate limited success and lack detailed analysis.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of novelty. The observation that large batch sizes can lead to different minima than small batch sizes is well-known in the deep learning community, and the connection to sharpness has been explored in prior work (though not necessarily framed in the same way). The paper doesn’t substantially advance the existing understanding or propose a demonstrably effective solution. The core contribution seems to be a detailed empirical exploration of a known problem, rather than a breakthrough insight.

Secondly, the presented ‘sharpness’ metric, while acknowledged as a heuristic, feels insufficiently validated. The paper states the metric relates to eigenvalues of the Hessian, but a more rigorous connection would strengthen the analysis. The sensitivity of the results to the parameters used in defining the metric (particularly λ in Equation 4 and the choice of matrix A) is not adequately explored. Additionally, the impact of different optimizers (beyond ADAM) on the observed sharpness is not thoroughly investigated.

Finally, the discussion of attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) feels cursory. The reported lack of success requires a more in-depth analysis. Why did these approaches fail to alleviate the generalization gap? What limitations do they have in the context of large-batch training?

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper would benefit significantly from a more rigorous theoretical foundation. Exploring the connection to existing theoretical work on loss landscape properties would be valuable.
*   **Sharpness metric validation:**  Provide a stronger justification for the chosen sharpness metric. A comparison with other measures of flatness/sharpness (e.g., based on Hessian eigenvalue analysis, even if approximate) would be useful. Conduct sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of metric parameters on the results.
*   **Optimizer exploration:** Extend the experimental evaluation to include a broader range of optimizers beyond ADAM.
*   **Remedy analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the attempted remedies. Investigate the reasons for their limited success and explore potential modifications.
*   **Visualization:** Consider visualizing the loss landscape in a more intuitive way. While the parametric plots are helpful, other techniques (e.g., contour plots) could provide additional insights.
*   **Clarification of batch size selection:** Explain the rationale behind choosing 10% of the training data as the batch size for large-batch experiments and 256 for small-batch experiments. How sensitive are the results to these choices?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How does the observed correlation between sharpness and generalization gap change with different network architectures or datasets? Are there specific types of networks where this correlation is stronger or weaker?
2.  You mention the sharpness metric relates to Hessian eigenvalues. Can you provide a more quantitative estimate of this relationship, or explain how the choice of matrix *A* influences the approximation?
3.  Why do you believe the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) failed to significantly improve generalization with large-batch methods? Are there specific limitations that prevent these techniques from effectively addressing the sharpness problem?
4.  Have you explored any other methods for mitigating the generalization gap with large-batch methods, such as techniques based on second-order optimization or regularization?
5.  The paper focuses on the *correlation* between sharpness and generalization. Do you have any evidence suggesting a *causal* relationship? For example, have you tried actively flattening the minima (e.g., through regularization) and observed improved generalization?
