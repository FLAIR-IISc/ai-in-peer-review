- Paper summary: Shows correlation between large-batch training, convergence to sharper minima, and worse generalization; small-batch noise helps find flatter minima; uses parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric; attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) were insufficient.  
- Decision: Reject — descriptive study lacking rigorous causal evidence and relying on an ad-hoc sharpness metric.  
- Main critique: Work is mostly observational and does not provide a strong theoretical/mechanistic explanation for why large batches find sharp minima.  
- Sharpness metric concerns: The metric is defined via a maximization approximated with few L-BFGS steps, raising sensitivity and reliability questions (parameters λ, p).  
- Hessian connection unclear: Paper claims relation to Hessian eigenvalues but does not rigorously connect the metric to true Hessian spectra.  
- Remedies underexplored: Data augmentation and conservative training were tested briefly and found insufficient, suggesting the investigation of solutions is not thorough.  
- Literature engagement lacking: The paper does not deeply integrate or contrast its findings with existing theoretical work (e.g., MDL, Bayesian approaches, Hochreiter & Schmidhuber).  
- Request for theory: Authors should provide stronger theoretical justification (e.g., role of gradient variance scaling with batch size) for large-batch behavior.  
- Metric validation needed: Evaluate sensitivity to λ and p, and compare metric values to actual Hessian eigenvalues on small models/datasets.  
- Explore other remedies: Investigate techniques designed to flatten loss landscapes (e.g., Sharpness-Aware Minimization) and more comprehensive interventions.  
- Clarify flatness: Provide a precise, operational definition of “flatness” rather than an informal description.  
- Architecture effects: Systematically study how network architecture influences the batch-size/sharpness/generalization relationship.  
- Question on Hessian validation: Can the authors detail the connection to the largest Hessian eigenvalue and how the metric approximates the Ritz value?  
- Question on computational cost: What is the cost of computing the full Hessian for the studied networks, and can it validate the metric on smaller scales?  
- Question on gradient variance: Could gradient variance scaling with batch size explain the observed trapping in sharp minima?  
- Question on remedial efficacy: Were there any specific cases where data augmentation or conservative training provided measurable improvement?  
- Question on warm-starting: What is the optimal number of warm-start epochs when initializing large-batch from small-batch, and what trade-offs exist between initial small-batch training and final large-batch fine-tuning?
