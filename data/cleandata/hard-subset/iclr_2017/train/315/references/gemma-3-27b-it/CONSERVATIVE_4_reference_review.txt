This paper investigates the relationship between batch size and generalization performance in deep learning, positing that large batch sizes lead to convergence to sharper minima, resulting in a generalization gap. The authors present empirical evidence supporting this claim across several network architectures and datasets, comparing the sharpness of minima obtained with small and large batch training. They also explore potential remedies, finding limited success with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding the sharpness of minima correlating with batch size is interesting and aligns with existing intuition, the paper lacks the rigor and depth needed for acceptance at ICLR. The empirical validation, while extensive, feels largely descriptive and doesn’t provide a compelling causal link. The attempted remedies are also superficially explored and lack convincing evidence of improvement. 

The core argument relies heavily on the "sharpness" metric (Equation 4), which is acknowledged as a heuristic. While a computationally feasible proxy is necessary, the paper doesn’t sufficiently justify why this specific metric captures the relevant aspects of minima sharpness impacting generalization. Furthermore, the sensitivity analysis of this metric (varying epsilon) is not thoroughly presented, making it difficult to assess its robustness. The conclusions drawn from the parametric plots (Figure 3 & 7) are also visually suggestive but lack quantitative support. Simply *showing* sharper minima isn't enough; the authors need to demonstrate *how* this sharpness translates to measurable differences in generalization beyond the observed testing accuracy gap.

The comparisons to existing theoretical work are superficial. Mentioning connections to the MDL principle or Bayesian learning doesn’t constitute a rigorous theoretical grounding. The authors themselves acknowledge a need for proving the link between batch size and sharp minima convergence – a significant gap in the current work.  The exploration of potential remedies feels like a preliminary investigation rather than a comprehensive attempt to address the identified issue. The results are inconclusive and lack detailed ablation studies. The claim that these approaches "do not completely remedy the problem" is a weak conclusion without a more granular analysis of their impact.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Sharpeness Metric Justification:** Provide a more detailed justification for the chosen sharpness metric. Relate it to the Hessian spectrum more concretely. Explore alternative metrics and compare their results. Perform a sensitivity analysis demonstrating the robustness of the findings to the choice of epsilon.
*   **Causality:** Strengthen the causal link between sharpness and generalization. Beyond observing a correlation, attempt to manipulate the loss landscape (e.g., through regularization techniques specifically targeting flatness) and measure the impact on both sharpness and generalization performance.
*   **Theoretical Grounding:**  Expand the discussion of relevant theoretical work. Could existing theory be adapted or extended to provide a formal explanation for the observed phenomenon?
*   **Remedy Exploration:**  Conduct more thorough investigations of the proposed remedies. Include ablation studies to isolate the impact of each technique. Explore a wider range of strategies, such as sharpness-aware minimization (SAM).
*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for the observed differences in sharpness and generalization performance.
*   **Code Release:** Release the code used for the experiments to facilitate reproducibility and allow others to build upon this work.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Beyond the chosen sharpness metric, what other measures of minima characteristics (e.g., condition number, eigenvalue distribution) were considered, and why were they ultimately discarded?
2.  How sensitive are the observed results to the choice of optimizer (ADAM) and its hyperparameters? Have you explored other optimizers and learning rate schedules?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* large batch sizes are believed to be attracted to sharper minima? Is it solely a consequence of the noisier gradient or are there other contributing factors?
4.  What is the computational cost of evaluating the sharpness metric for the largest networks used in the experiments? Would more accurate (but more expensive) methods for estimating sharpness yield different results?
5.  The paper mentions a threshold batch size where generalization starts to degrade. How does this threshold vary across different network architectures and datasets? What factors appear to influence its value?
