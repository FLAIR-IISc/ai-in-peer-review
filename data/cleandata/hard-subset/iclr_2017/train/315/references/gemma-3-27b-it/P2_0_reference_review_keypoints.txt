- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse, hypothesizing and empirically linking large batches to sharper minima and a corresponding generalization gap.  
- Decision: Reject — interesting phenomenon but analysis lacks rigor and is not sufficiently convincing.  
- Observation itself (large batches hurt generalization) is known; novelty claimed is linking it to sharpness but causation is not demonstrated.  
- The sharpness metric (Eq. 4) is weak: maximization is done inexactly with few L-BFGS-B iterations, raising reliability and sensitivity concerns.  
- Insufficient justification that the proposed metric approximates true curvature or Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Remedial strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are standard and show limited success, with shallow analysis of why they fail.  
- Paper is empirically heavy and lacks a stronger theoretical framework explaining why large batches find sharper minima.  
- Presentation issues: repetitive sections and lack of a concise summary of key findings; organization and clarity need improvement.  
- Recommendation: perform sensitivity/ablation studies on sharpness metric parameters (ε, A) and compare with other curvature measures (e.g., Hessian approximations).  
- Recommendation: provide more direct causal evidence (e.g., projecting large-batch solutions to flatter minima with small-batch updates and measuring generalization).  
- Recommendation: develop theoretical analysis, possibly relating SGD noise characteristics to attraction to sharp minima.  
- Recommendation: study how the phenomenon varies across architectures (ResNets, transformers) and with hyperparameters (learning rate, optimizer).  
- Question: Have you considered cheaper Hessian approximations (e.g., Fisher Information) to validate sharpness findings?  
- Question: How confident are you that the inexact maximization for the sharpness metric doesn’t bias results — did you try different solvers or more iterations?  
- Question: Can you more precisely characterize the mechanism by which small-batch methods “escape” sharp minima (e.g., role of stochastic gradient noise)?  
- Question: In warm-start experiments, how does large-batch convergence speed change with more initial small-batch epochs — is there a point of diminishing returns?  
- Question: Have you tested whether large-batch solutions are more sensitive to input perturbations, beyond traditional overfitting explanations?
