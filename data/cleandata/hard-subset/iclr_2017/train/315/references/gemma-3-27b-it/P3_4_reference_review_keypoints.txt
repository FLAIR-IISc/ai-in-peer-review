- Paper studies the generalization gap for large-batch training and finds large-batch methods converge to sharper minima correlated with worse generalization.
- Authors introduce a pragmatic sharpness metric and show its correlation with generalization across architectures and datasets.
- Decision: Reject — strong empirical description but lacks theoretical explanation and effective remedies, so it doesn’t solve the problem.
- Main critique: descriptive evidence is strong but the paper fails to explain why large batches find sharper minima (interaction of batch size, gradient noise, curvature).
- Sharpness metric concern: results sensitive to choice of epsilon and subspace dimensionality p; robustness and validation against Hessian eigenvalues are needed.
- Remedies evaluated (data augmentation, conservative training) yield only marginal improvements and do not resolve the core issue.
- Related work coverage is incomplete; recent theoretical advances on implicit regularization, PAC-Bayes, and flatness should be better integrated.
- Presentation could be tightened: reduce redundancy, emphasize core findings, and clarify notation (confusing repeated x subscripts).
- Suggestion: include a simple theoretical analysis (e.g., quadratic model) to illustrate batch-size effects on minima.
- Suggestion: perform Hessian or approximate Hessian analysis on a subset of the network to validate the sharpness metric.
- Suggestion: investigate other remedies like SAM or adaptive batch-size schedules.
- Suggestion: analyze whether architectural choices (residuals, skip connections) affect the sharpness-generalization relationship.
- Suggestion: provide more intuitive visualizations of the loss landscape (2D/3D contour plots) to illustrate sharp vs flat minima.
- Question: what is the computational cost and approximations used to compute the sharpness metric for large nets (e.g., VGG)?
- Question: can the authors characterize the structure of sharp minima (are they localized in specific weight subspaces)?
- Question: what direction do the authors consider most promising to overcome the large-batch generalization gap?
- Question: could sharp minima be an implicit form of regularization that improves training loss but harms transfer to unseen data?
