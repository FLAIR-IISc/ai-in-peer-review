This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes. The authors posit and provide numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers of the loss function, leading to poorer generalization performance compared to small-batch methods which find flatter minima. They explore this through parametric plots and a novel sharpness metric, and briefly discuss potential remedies.

**Reject.** While the observation regarding sharpness and generalization is intuitively appealing and aligns with existing theoretical work, the paper falls short of providing a convincing and rigorous demonstration. The numerous minor methodological flaws and inconsistencies, coupled with a lack of depth in the analysis, undermine the core claim. 

The primary weakness lies in the lack of statistical rigor and detail in the experimental setup. The choice of ADAM as the optimizer for both small and large batch settings, while pragmatic, limits the generalizability of the conclusions. A comparison across different optimizers would be crucial. The batch sizes (10% of training data vs 256) feel somewhat arbitrarily chosen without sufficient justification; a more systematic exploration of batch size effects is needed. The sharpness metric, while a pragmatic approach, requires further validation and comparison to established measures like Hessian eigenvalues. The number of trials (5) seems limited, and the presentation of results lacks proper statistical error bars beyond a simple mean and standard deviation. Furthermore, the claim about the generalization gap being *not* due to overfitting is weakly supported – demonstrating a lack of the typical overfitting curve doesn’t negate the possibility of other forms of overfitting at play. The experiments themselves aren't described with sufficient precision to enable replication, and the link between the observed sharpness and the actual generalization gap feels more correlational than causal. The attempted remedies in Appendix E are presented without sufficient evaluation – stating they “do not completely remedy the problem” lacks quantitative evidence. The discussion section feels superficial, merely acknowledging relevant literature without deeply engaging with it.

Further issues include:

*   **Inconsistent notation:** The definition of Bk changes between the introduction and section 2.1.
*   **Formatting inconsistencies**: Figure references within the text do not consistently follow a standardized format.
*   **Unnecessary detail/redundancy**: The detailed descriptions of network architectures in Appendix B, while thorough, contribute little to the central argument and could be streamlined.
*   **Vague language**: Phrases like “significant number of large positive eigenvalues” lack quantitative precision.
*   **Missing error analysis**: What types of errors do the models make? Are there differences in the nature of the errors between small and large batch training?

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Statistical Significance:** Provide comprehensive statistical analyses (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to support all claims. Report confidence intervals and p-values.
*   **Optimizer Analysis:** Repeat the experiments with different optimizers (SGD, Adagrad) for both small and large batch sizes.
*   **Batch Size Sweep:** Systematically vary the batch size and analyze the resulting generalization gap and sharpness metrics.
*   **Hessian Analysis:** Perform a limited Hessian eigenvalue analysis on a subset of models to validate the sharpness metric.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to isolate the impact of individual factors (e.g., data augmentation, regularization) on the generalization gap.
*   **Error Analysis**: Detailed error analysis of the models trained with small and large batch sizes.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the ADAM optimizer, and what modifications were attempted with other optimizers?
2.  Why were the specific batch sizes of 256 and 10% of the training set chosen, and how were they determined to be representative?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed justification and validation of the proposed sharpness metric, including a comparison with more traditional eigenvalue-based measures?
4.  Beyond a lack of improvement, what specifically happened with the attempted remedies in Appendix E? Were there any trends, even if not statistically significant, that provide further insight?
5.  Can you provide the raw data used to generate the plots and tables, to enable independent verification of the results?
