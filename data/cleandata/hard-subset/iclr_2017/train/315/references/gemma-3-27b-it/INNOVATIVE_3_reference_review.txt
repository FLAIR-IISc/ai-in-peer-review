This paper investigates the connection between large batch training, sharp minimizers, and generalization performance in deep learning. The authors convincingly argue, through empirical evidence, that large batch methods converge to sharper minima than small batch methods, and that this sharpness correlates with poorer generalization. They present a sharpness metric and demonstrate its difference between the two training regimes across several network architectures. The work is well-motivated, addressing a known practical issue in scaling deep learning training.

**Reject.** While the observations presented are interesting and the empirical work is reasonably thorough, the paper feels incomplete. The core claim – that sharpness directly *causes* the generalization gap – isn't definitively proven, and the proposed remedies (described in the Appendix) are largely unsuccessful. More crucially, the paper doesn't offer a clear path forward or a novel algorithmic solution to address the identified problem. The value lies primarily in insightful empirical analysis, rather than a groundbreaking advance.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

The authors effectively demonstrate a correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization. The parametric plots (Figure 3 & D) are compelling visualizations. The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), while heuristic, seems reasonable and allows for quantitative comparison. The experiments across different network architectures and datasets strengthen the validity of the findings. The observation that small batch methods appear to “escape” sharp minima while large batch methods get stuck is particularly insightful and aligns with intuition about the role of noise in optimization. 

However, the paper's weakness lies in its lack of prescriptive guidance. Identifying the problem is valuable, but the attempted solutions in Appendix E are not compelling. Data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization show little improvement, and the authors acknowledge this.  A stronger contribution would involve proposing a novel training strategy specifically designed to mitigate the issue of sharp minima in large-batch settings – perhaps a dynamic batch size adjustment schedule or a modified optimization algorithm. 

The discussion section acknowledges relevant theoretical work but doesn't fully integrate it with the empirical findings. A more in-depth exploration of the theoretical underpinnings of sharpness and generalization would enhance the paper's impact. The connection to minimum description length (MDL) is mentioned, but could be expanded upon.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Sharper Definition of Sharpness:** While the proposed metric is useful, its limitations should be discussed more thoroughly. How sensitive is the metric to the choice of ‘?’ in Equation 4? A sensitivity analysis would be valuable.
*   **Explore Alternative Regularization Techniques:** Beyond the methods presented in the appendix, investigate other regularization techniques, such as weight decay or spectral normalization, to see if they can influence the sharpness of the minima found by large-batch methods.
*   **Theoretical Investigation:** Even a preliminary theoretical analysis exploring the relationship between batch size, gradient noise, and the curvature of the loss landscape would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Clarify the Role of Initialization:**  How does the initialization scheme affect the sharpness of the minima found by the different batch sizes? Are certain initializations more prone to converging to sharp minima with large batches?
*   **Elaborate on Warm-Starting Results:**  The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are promising. Why does a period of small-batch training appear to be necessary to prime the large-batch method for good performance?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   You mention that the sharpness metric approximates the Ritz value of the Hessian. Can you elaborate on the computational cost of directly estimating the Ritz value and why your heuristic is a reasonable trade-off?
*   The paper focuses on the *sharpness* of the minima. Could the *basin size* around the minima also be a factor in generalization performance? Have you considered measuring basin size and correlating it with generalization?
*   You state that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting. Could it be a form of *implicit regularization* induced by the different batch sizes, rather than solely a consequence of minimizer sharpness?
