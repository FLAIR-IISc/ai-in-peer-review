This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, positing that this gap is correlated with the sharpness of the minima found by these methods. The authors present empirical evidence supporting this claim across several network configurations and datasets, comparing the sharpness of minima obtained with large and small batches. They explore potential remedies but find limited success.

**Reject.** While the observed phenomenon – a correlation between batch size, minima sharpness, and generalization – is interesting and aligns with existing intuition, the paper lacks the rigor and thoroughness expected of a strong ICLR submission. The evidence presented feels preliminary, and the conclusions are drawn with insufficient statistical power and comparative analysis.

The primary weakness lies in the lack of comprehensive baselines and controlled experiments. The authors primarily compare large-batch ADAM to small-batch ADAM. While a reasonable starting point, it fails to disentangle the effects of batch size *from* the specific optimizer used. A comparison against established, well-tuned small-batch optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum, Nesterov accelerated gradient) is crucial.  Furthermore, the choice of ADAM as the large-batch optimizer is not sufficiently justified; other optimizers might behave differently. The claim that the generalization gap isn’t due to overfitting is made without exploring standard overfitting mitigation techniques (weight decay, dropout) *in conjunction with* large-batch training. The attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are presented with minimal detail and lack convincing results. Simply stating they "do not completely remedy the problem" isn't sufficient; the extent of improvement (or lack thereof) needs quantitative analysis.

The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is heuristic and lacks a strong theoretical justification. While acknowledged by the authors, its validity as a reliable measure of minima sharpness is questionable. A more rigorous analysis relating this metric to the Hessian eigenvalue spectrum would strengthen the argument. The parallel performance model in Appendix C feels overly optimistic and lacks concrete details regarding hardware and communication costs. The experiments are limited in scope; the network architectures tested are relatively standard, and the datasets used are well-studied. Extending the experiments to more complex architectures and datasets would provide a broader understanding of the observed phenomenon. The discussion of related work is superficial, and the connections to theoretical results are tenuous.  The claim about the "idealized" scaling potential of large-batch methods feels unsupported by the empirical evidence presented.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Establish stronger baselines:** Compare large-batch training to well-tuned small-batch SGD with momentum and Nesterov acceleration.
*   **Optimizer comparison:** Test the effects of different optimizers (e.g., SGD, L-BFGS) with large batches.
*   **Overfitting exploration:** Systematically investigate the impact of regularization techniques (weight decay, dropout) in conjunction with large-batch training.
*   **Sharpeness metric justification:** Provide a more theoretical justification for the proposed sharpness metric or explore alternative, more robust measures.
*   **Statistical significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for all experimental results.
*   **Scaling model refinement:** Provide more details and realistic assumptions for the parallel performance model.
*   **Expanded experiments:** Conduct experiments with a wider range of network architectures, datasets, and hyperparameters.
*   **Deeper theoretical connection:** Elaborate on the connections between the observed results and recent theoretical work on loss surface landscapes.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Why was ADAM chosen as the primary optimizer for both small and large-batch experiments? Were other optimizers considered, and if so, why were they excluded from the main results?
2.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of evaluating the sharpness metric for larger networks?
3.  What specific regularization techniques (e.g., weight decay, dropout) were explored in conjunction with large-batch training, and what were the results?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the parameter 'λ' in the conservative training approach?
5.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of the parallel performance model presented in Appendix C, and what factors might affect its accuracy in a real-world setting?
