## Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon of generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep neural networks. The authors hypothesize and provide numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to *sharp* minimizers of the loss landscape, characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian, while small-batch methods converge to *flat* minimizers. They argue that this sharpness is correlated with poorer generalization performance. The paper presents experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets to support this claim, and briefly explores potential remedies.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the observations presented are insightful and align with common empirical experience, the paper feels incomplete and lacks sufficient rigor to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The primary reason for rejection is the lack of a compelling mechanistic explanation *why* large-batch methods favor sharp minima, beyond simply observing the correlation.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Descriptive, not Explanatory:** The paper largely *describes* a correlation between batch size, sharpness of minima, and generalization gap. It doesn't delve deeply into *why* this correlation exists. The arguments presented (e.g., noise in SGD pushing out of sharp minima) are intuitive but lack formal grounding. It feels more like an empirical study than a theoretical contribution.
*   **Limited Novelty:** The concept of sharpness and flatness of minima and their relation to generalization has been discussed in prior work (referenced by the authors). The paper does not significantly advance the theoretical understanding of this phenomenon. The 'sharpness' metric proposed is a heuristic and its limitations are acknowledged.
*   **Insufficient Exploration of Remedies:** The attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are briefly mentioned, and the reported results indicate they don’t fully solve the problem. This section feels underdeveloped and lacks sufficient analysis.
*   **Lack of Statistical Rigor:** The paper mentions reporting mean and standard deviation, but the statistical significance of the observed differences (e.g., sharpness values) isn’t explicitly tested. This makes it harder to assess the robustness of the findings.
*   **Presentation:** The paper reads somewhat like a research report rather than a polished conference submission. The introduction could be more concise and focus more on the specific contribution.

**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

*   **Develop a Theoretical Framework:**  Even a preliminary theoretical model explaining why large batches might gravitate towards sharper minima would greatly strengthen the paper. Is it related to the curvature of the loss landscape?  Are there properties of large-batch optimizers that exacerbate this issue?
*   **More Rigorous Analysis of Remedies:** Investigate the remedies in more detail. Analyze *why* they don't fully resolve the issue.  Provide more insightful discussion on their limitations.
*   **Statistical Testing:** Include statistical tests (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the observed differences in sharpness and generalization performance.
*   **Compare to Related Work:** Expand the discussion of related work, specifically focusing on theoretical analyses of generalization and loss landscape properties.  Clearly position the contribution of this work within the existing literature.
*   **Investigate Different Optimizers:** While ADAM is used, explore whether the observed effects are optimizer-dependent.
*   **Address the Role of Learning Rate:** The learning rate is a crucial hyperparameter. How does it interact with batch size in influencing the sharpness of the minima reached?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention the connection to minimum description length (MDL) theory.  Can you elaborate on how MDL specifically explains the generalization gap observed with large batches, beyond the general intuition of flat minima requiring less precision?
2.  What is the computational cost of calculating the exact Hessian for the networks you experimented with?  Could a more efficient approximation of the Hessian spectrum be used to validate your sharpness metric?
3.  Why do you believe the attempted remedies did not significantly improve generalization? Were there any specific challenges in applying these techniques in the large-batch setting?
4.  You observe a threshold batch size beyond which generalization drops. Is there any relationship between this threshold and the curvature of the loss landscape?
5. Could you provide a more detailed discussion about the limitations of your proposed sharpness metric and potential sources of bias?



In conclusion, while the paper raises an important and relevant question, the lack of a mechanistic explanation and the limited novelty prevent me from recommending it for acceptance at ICLR. Addressing the feedback provided could significantly improve the paper's contribution and rigor.
