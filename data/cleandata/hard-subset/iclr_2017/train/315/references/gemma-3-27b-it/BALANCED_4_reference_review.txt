This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models. The authors posit that this gap is correlated with the sharpness of the minimizers found by large-batch methods, as opposed to the flatter minimizers found by small-batch methods. They support this claim with numerical experiments across various network configurations and datasets, demonstrating that large-batch methods indeed converge to sharper minima as measured by a proposed sharpness metric. The work is well-motivated, addressing a practical problem in deep learning optimization and drawing connections to existing theoretical ideas around generalization and loss surface properties.

**Reject** – While the observations presented are interesting and the experimental setup is reasonable, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and depth to warrant acceptance at ICLR. The core finding – that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharper minima – is largely consistent with existing intuition and prior work (e.g., Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and doesn’t present a fundamentally new insight. The exploration of remedies in Appendix E is brief and inconclusive, and the discussion section primarily summarizes existing work rather than proposing future research directions grounded in the presented findings.

The paper's support for its claims is adequate but not entirely convincing. The sharpness metric, while computationally feasible, feels somewhat ad-hoc and its correlation with generalization performance is asserted rather than rigorously demonstrated. While the parametric plots and numerical results provide evidence of sharper minima, they don't establish a causal link between sharpness and the generalization gap. For instance, the paper does not explore whether artificially sharpening minima obtained via small-batch training also leads to reduced generalization performance. The warm-starting experiments offer a glimpse of potential solutions, but the explanation provided – relating to SB methods exploring flat minima – feels speculative. The lack of statistical significance testing in comparing sharpness values is a notable weakness. The presentation of results, particularly in the tables, could be improved with confidence intervals and p-values. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper should more clearly articulate its novel contribution beyond simply observing and quantifying a known phenomenon. Perhaps focusing on a particular aspect of the sharpness/generalization relationship, or proposing a more refined metric for sharpness, would strengthen the novelty.
*   **Theoretical grounding:** The paper frequently references existing theoretical work but doesn’t attempt to connect the empirical findings to these theories in a substantial way. A more rigorous theoretical analysis, even if limited in scope, would elevate the work.
*   **Remedies exploration:** The section on remedies feels underdeveloped. Expanding on these initial attempts, perhaps with a more systematic hyperparameter search or a deeper analysis of why they fail, would be beneficial.
*   **Sharpness metric justification:** A more detailed justification for the proposed sharpness metric is needed. Why is this metric a good proxy for generalization performance? How does it compare to other potential metrics (e.g., based on the Hessian spectrum)?
*   **Control experiments:** Include control experiments to demonstrate the *causal* role of minimizer sharpness. Can one artificially sharpen minima found by SB training and observe a corresponding drop in generalization performance?
*   **Batch Size Thresholds:** A more comprehensive analysis of the batch size thresholds discussed in Section 3 is warranted.  What features of the network or dataset influence the location of this threshold? Is there a predictive model for this threshold?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The sharpness metric relies on a maximization step. How sensitive are the results to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations used in this maximization?  Have you experimented with different optimization algorithms for the maximization step?
2.  You mention that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting. Could you provide more detail on the criteria you used to rule out overfitting?
3.  The paper highlights the correlation between sharpness and generalization. However, it is plausible that other factors, correlated with batch size, could also contribute to the generalization gap. How have you attempted to control for these confounding factors?
4.  Given the limitations of the remedies explored in Appendix E, what are the most promising avenues for future research aimed at improving the generalization performance of large-batch methods?
5.  How do the observed results change with different network architectures, activation functions, or data sets? Are there specific types of networks or datasets where the generalization gap is more or less pronounced?
