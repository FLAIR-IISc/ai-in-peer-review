## Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the well-known generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes. The authors present numerical evidence suggesting that this gap is correlated with the *sharpness* of the minima found by large-batch methods, as opposed to the *flatness* of minima found by small-batch methods. They argue that large batch methods get stuck in sharp minima due to a lack of exploration, while small batches, with their noisy gradients, can escape these regions and find flatter, better generalizing solutions. The paper explores this phenomenon through parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and briefly discusses potential remedies like data augmentation and conservative training, finding them insufficient to fully resolve the issue.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a potentially important connection between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization, the work feels largely descriptive and lacks sufficient rigor to convincingly establish a causal link. The empirical analysis, while extensive, doesn’t strongly demonstrate that sharpness *causes* the generalization gap, and the proposed sharpness metric feels somewhat ad-hoc. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Descriptive rather than explanatory:** The paper primarily *observes* a correlation between sharpness and generalization. It proposes plausible explanations, but doesn’t offer a strong theoretical or mechanistic justification for *why* large batches converge to sharper minima.  Why are large batches more prone to getting trapped in sharp minima? What properties of the loss landscape contribute to this? 
* **Ad-hoc Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) relies on a maximization problem, which is approximated with a limited number of L-BFGS iterations. The sensitivity of results to the choice of parameters (λ, p) and the inexact nature of the maximization raise concerns about its reliability. Furthermore, the paper acknowledges it’s related to the Hessian eigenvalues, but doesn’t provide a detailed connection, leaving it feeling less theoretically grounded.
* **Limited Scope of Remedial Approaches:** The explored attempts to improve large-batch training (data augmentation, conservative training) are presented very briefly and found to be ineffective. This raises the question of whether the investigation into potential solutions was sufficient.
* **Lack of Comparison to Existing Theoretical Work:** While the paper briefly mentions related theoretical work (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, MDL theory, Bayesian learning), it doesn’t deeply engage with it.  How do these existing theories explain or contradict the observed results? Can the findings be framed within these existing theoretical frameworks?



**4. Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

* **Theoretical Justification:** The authors should strive to provide a stronger theoretical justification for *why* large batches converge to sharper minima. Could the scaling of the gradient variance with batch size be a contributing factor? 
* **Sharpness Metric Validation:**  A more thorough validation of the sharpness metric is needed.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of parameters (λ, p)?  Is there a strong correlation between the metric and the actual Hessian eigenvalues (even on a small subset of the data)?
* **Expanded Remedial Investigation:** The attempted remedies should be investigated more thoroughly. Consider exploring techniques specifically designed to flatten the loss landscape (e.g., sharpness-aware minimization – SAM).
* **Deeper Connection to Literature:** The paper should engage more deeply with existing theoretical work on the loss landscapes of deep neural networks. How do the findings relate to concepts like mode connectivity and the barrier height between minima?
* **Clearer Definition of "Flatness":** The concept of "flatness" is somewhat vague. Providing a more precise definition would be helpful.
* **Impact of Architecture:** The paper mentions varying network configurations, but doesn’t systematically investigate how the architecture affects the relationship between batch size, sharpness, and generalization.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* You mention the sharpness metric is related to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of this connection?  How does the metric approximate the Ritz value?
* What is the computational cost of calculating the full Hessian for the networks you have experimented with? Could the full Hessian be used to validate the sharpness metric on a smaller scale?
* Could you comment on the potential role of gradient variance scaling with batch size in driving the observed behavior?
* You found that data augmentation and conservative training didn’t fully resolve the generalization gap.  Were there any specific cases where these techniques showed some improvement?
*  You state that warm-starting a large-batch method from a small-batch solution can improve performance. What is the optimal number of warm-starting epochs?  Is there a trade-off between the initial small-batch training and the final large-batch fine-tuning?
