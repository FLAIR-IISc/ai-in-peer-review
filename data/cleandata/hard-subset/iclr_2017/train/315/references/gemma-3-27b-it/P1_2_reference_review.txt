## Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

**1. Summary:**

This paper investigates the well-known issue of generalization performance degradation when using large-batch training in deep learning. The authors present numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to *sharper* minimizers of the loss landscape, characterized by a greater concentration of large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian, compared to the *flatter* minimizers found by small-batch methods. They hypothesize that this sharpness is a key factor contributing to the observed generalization gap, linking it to concepts like minimum description length and Bayesian learning. The paper provides empirical results across several network architectures and datasets, supporting this claim through parametric plots and a novel sharpness metric. They also briefly explore potential remedies, finding limited success.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper identifies an interesting phenomenon and provides some supporting evidence, the analysis lacks sufficient rigor and the connection between sharpness and generalization remains correlational rather than demonstrably causal. The novelty is incremental, and the presented work doesn't offer a compelling solution to the large-batch generalization problem.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Correlational, Not Causal:** The core argument hinges on the correlation between sharpness of minimizers and generalization gap. While the presented data demonstrates this correlation, it does not *prove* that sharpness *causes* the generalization issue. Other factors, like the composition of the batch itself or the optimization dynamics in high-dimensional space, could be at play.  The paper acknowledges this, but doesn't provide further analysis to address it.
* **Sharpness Metric Concerns:** The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) feels somewhat ad-hoc and its relationship to true Hessian eigenvalues is only asserted, not rigorously proven. The reliance on a random matrix *A* for approximating the Ritz value is concerning. The sensitivity of results to the choice of  and the number of L-BFGS iterations is not thoroughly investigated.  A more robust and theoretically grounded measure of sharpness would strengthen the argument.
* **Limited Exploration of Remedies:** The attempts to mitigate the generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) were not deeply investigated. The conclusion that they are insufficient feels premature.  More extensive experimentation and ablation studies are needed to understand their potential.
* **Lack of Theoretical Grounding:**  The paper leans heavily on empirical observation.  While referencing relevant theoretical work (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, MDL theory, Bayesian learning), it doesn't offer new theoretical insights or connect the observations to existing theory in a significant way.  



**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Stronger Causal Argument:** Conduct experiments designed to *manipulate* the sharpness of the minimizers and observe the effect on generalization.  For example, could you introduce a regularization term that explicitly encourages flatness?
* **Robustness of Sharpness Metric:** Perform a sensitivity analysis on the sharpness metric. How do results change with different values of  and different numbers of L-BFGS iterations?  Compare results with direct (though computationally expensive) Hessian eigenvalue calculations on smaller networks.
* **More In-Depth Remedy Investigation:** Systematically explore the parameter space of the proposed remedies. Consider combinations of techniques and perform ablation studies to isolate their individual contributions.
* **Theoretical Connection:**  Explore existing theoretical work on loss landscape properties and flatness.  Can the observed sharpness difference be explained by existing theory? If not, what modifications to existing theory are needed?
* **Clarify Batch Size Selection:**  The paper mentions batch sizes of 10% of the training data for LB and 256 for SB. This is fine, but the justification for these choices should be included.
* **Discuss Computational Costs:** Provide a clear indication of the computational cost of calculating the sharpness metric.




**Questions for Authors:**

1.  You mention the importance of flat minimizers requiring "low precision." Can you elaborate on how this precision relates to the sensitivity of the model to perturbations in the input?
2.  How sensitive are your results to the random initialization of the network weights?  Did you perform multiple runs with different random seeds, and were the results consistent?
3.  You state that the generalization gap isn't due to overfitting. Could you demonstrate that early stopping *does not* reduce the gap, perhaps by showing results with and without early stopping?
4.  The sharpness metric relies on a random matrix *A*.  How does the choice of *A* (its dimensionality and elements) affect the results?  Have you explored other methods for approximating sharpness?
5.   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of data augmentations that were used and how they were chosen for the specific datasets?
6.  What are the limitations of the presented approach? Are there specific network architectures or datasets where the observed relationship between sharpness and generalization might not hold?



In conclusion, while the paper raises an interesting observation, the lack of a strong causal argument, limitations in the sharpness metric, and insufficient exploration of remedies lead me to recommend rejection. With significant revisions addressing these points, the work could potentially be resubmitted for further consideration.
