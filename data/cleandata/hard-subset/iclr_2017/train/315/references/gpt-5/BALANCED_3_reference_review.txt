Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training despite similar training loss. It hypothesizes that LB methods converge to “sharp” minima (high curvature), whereas SB methods find “flatter” minima that generalize better.
- It proposes a practical sharpness metric based on local worst-case perturbations of f(x) constrained to a box and optionally a low-dimensional random subspace. It complements this with 1D/curvilinear parametric plots between SB/LB solutions.
- Extensive empirical evidence across six architectures (FC and CNN variants of AlexNet/VGG on multiple datasets) shows: (i) clear generalization gaps when using very large batches, (ii) large differences (1–2 orders of magnitude) in the proposed sharpness between SB and LB solutions with similar training losses, (iii) a “batch-size threshold” beyond which test accuracy degrades and sharpness grows, and (iv) warm-starting LB with a partially trained SB model can recover good generalization and reduce sharpness.
- The paper evaluates several remediation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/stability training), reporting partial improvements but persistent sharpness with LB training.
- It situates results within known theory suggesting many near-equivalent local minima and discusses implications for scalable training.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) The work addresses a timely and important practical question with broad impact and provides clear, reproducible empirical evidence across multiple architectures. (2) The analyses (sharpness metric, parametric plots, batch-size thresholds, warm-start experiments) coherently support the central claim and offer actionable insights that have already influenced follow-up work.

Supporting arguments
- Significance and novelty: The paper isolates a concrete mechanism—curvature/sharpness—as a likely contributor to the LB generalization gap and provides multiple, mutually reinforcing diagnostics. The warm-start experiment is particularly illuminating about the role of gradient noise and basin exploration.
- Breadth and robustness: Results are shown on multiple datasets and networks, with several optimizers tried for LB (though Adam is the main focus), and repeated across five random initializations with means/standard deviations reported.
- Methodological clarity: The sharpness metric is defined transparently and explored in both full space and random subspaces; visualization along paths between solutions is easy to interpret and compelling.
- Relation to literature: The study complements theoretical work on loss landscapes and contributes practical evidence that empirically aligns with the idea of many near-equivalent minima with different generalization behavior.

Caveats and limitations (do not change my decision but temper claims)
- The sharpness metric is heuristic and not invariant to common reparameterizations (e.g., scaling in ReLU/BN networks). While the authors aim for dimension/sparsity invariance, scale invariance is not addressed; this can confound curvature comparisons across solutions or architectures. Validation against actual top Hessian eigenvalues on smaller models would strengthen the connection.
- Optimizer/baseline confounds: Using Adam as the primary optimizer may affect generalization differently from SGD with momentum. The LB/SB comparison could be sensitive to learning-rate schedules, weight decay, and training budgets. LB training often benefits from schedule changes (e.g., warmup, linear scaling); the paper does not systematically sweep these.
- Statistical strength: Five runs per setting are helpful but modest; some claims rely on 1D path plots and inexact inner maximization (10 L-BFGS-B steps). While the effects are large, more thorough statistical analysis would increase confidence.
- Interpretability of parameter distance: Distances from initialization can be misleading due to parameter symmetries and scale; representational measures would be more informative.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions)
- Provide a systematic hyperparameter study for LB vs SB: learning rate (including linear scaling and warmup), momentum/Adam betas, weight decay, label smoothing, and data augmentation. Report whether careful tuning reduces the gap and how sharpness responds.
- Include results with SGD+momentum as the mainline optimizer to decouple Adam-specific effects. Also consider decoupled weight decay.
- Validate the sharpness metric: on small networks compute the top Hessian eigenvalues (power/Lanczos) and correlate with the metric; compare across different ε values and subspace dimensions p; report variance across random A.
- Consider scale-invariant curvature proxies: e.g., normalized sharpness, Fisher eigenvalues, or PAC-Bayes/flatness measures that account for reparameterization.
- Quantify gradient-noise scale vs batch size and relate it to the observed threshold. Connecting noise magnitude to escape from sharp basins would strengthen the explanation.
- Clarify training budgets: match number of optimizer updates and/or total epochs across SB/LB, and report wall-clock considerations separately from generalization outcomes.
- Explore representational similarity (e.g., CKA) to compare SB/LB solutions beyond parameter distance.
- Extend the warm-start study with dynamic batch-size schedules (gradual increase) to show whether a principled schedule reliably achieves flat minima with LB efficiency.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive are your conclusions to optimizer choice? Do the main results (gap and sharpness) hold under SGD with momentum across the same architectures with matched/tuned schedules?
2. How did you choose ε and p in the sharpness metric? How robust are rankings across different ε, p, and random A? Can you report correlations with top Hessian/Fisher eigenvalues on smaller models?
3. Did you try linear learning-rate scaling and warmup for LB? If so, how did test accuracy and sharpness change relative to your baseline LB?
4. Is the batch-size “threshold” consistent across datasets/architectures when controlling for effective gradient noise (e.g., batch size normalized by dataset size or gradient variance)?
5. In the warm-start experiments, is the improvement explained by entering a flat basin, or could it be reproduced by injecting noise into LB gradients? What happens if you artificially add noise to LB updates?
6. How do weight decay and label smoothing affect the sharpness metric for LB solutions? Do these regularizers narrow the gap?
7. Given BN and ReLU scale invariances, have you explored sharpness measures that account for filter-wise rescaling? Do your conclusions persist under such normalization?

Overall, this paper provides clear empirical evidence of a practically important phenomenon, offers useful diagnostics, and opens a productive research direction. Despite limitations in the sharpness metric and possible optimizer/baseline confounds, the contribution is strong and of high interest to the community.
