- Paper's claim: large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) finds flatter minima, explaining LB’s worse generalization.  
- Evidence presented: 1D/curvilinear loss plots, a heuristic local “sharpness” metric optimized with L-BFGS-B, experiments across models/datasets, and warm-start experiments showing LB can generalize if initialized near SB solutions.  
- Reported negative/partial results for data augmentation, conservative/adversarial/robust training not resolving the gap.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Empirical evidence does not control known confounders (learning-rate schedules, weight decay, training duration, linear scaling/warmup, optimizer choice), so causality LB→sharp→poor generalization is not supported.  
- Main reason 2: The sharpness metric is ad hoc and not invariant to common reparameterizations, so sharp vs flat conclusions are not robust.  
- Incomplete hyperparameter control: LB requires different LR schedules, warmup, and longer training; paper fixes SB/LB setups without fair per-regime tuning.  
- Optimizer dependence: most experiments use ADAM, but generalization behavior differs from SGD-momentum; optimizer–batch interactions may drive effects.  
- Insufficient experimental reporting: final training losses and loss distributions are not fully reported; extreme LB sizes and lack of intermediate/compute-matched protocols limit interpretation.  
- Sharpness metric issues: maximizes loss in a local box with only 10 L-BFGS-B steps and depends on parameter scaling/parameterization (problematic with ReLU/BatchNorm).  
- Reparameterization fragility: simple weight rescalings leave function unchanged but alter Hessian/“sharpness,” undermining metric validity.  
- Limited curvature evidence: no Hessian spectrum measurements (top-k eigenvalues, trace) or Fisher/Gauss–Newton proxies are reported.  
- 1D/curvilinear plots are suggestive but may be misleading due to symmetries (neuron permutations, scaling) and high dimensionality; straight-line paths can cross high-loss regions even within same basin.  
- Alternative explanations not disentangled: effective learning rates, implicit regularization, and gradient-noise-scale relationships could explain differences rather than escaping sharp basins.  
- Recommendation: use fair state-of-the-art LB baselines (SGD-momentum, linear scaling, warmup, tuned weight decay/label smoothing, compute-matched training).  
- Recommendation: adopt scale-/reparameterization-aware flatness measures (layer-normalized, path-normalized, PAC-Bayes–inspired metrics).  
- Recommendation: report Hessian spectral approximations (Lanczos top-k, trace via Hutchinson) and compare with Fisher spectra.  
- Recommendation: measure gradient covariance and noise scale over training and correlate with sharpness/generalization.  
- Recommendation: strengthen path analyses using mode connectivity (low-loss connecting curves) to test basin isolation.  
- Recommendation: broaden optimizer coverage (SGD-momentum, decoupled weight decay for Adam, Entropy-SGD, SAM, SWA/SWAG) and test explicit noise injection or flatness-seeking methods.  
- Recommendation: clarify and fully report training protocols (LRs, schedules, weight decay, epochs/updates, seeds) and report mean±std for train loss and test error.  
- Recommendation: test invariances (layer rescaling, Ghost BatchNorm) to check robustness of sharpness conclusions.  
- Key questions for authors: how were LRs/schedules/weight decay chosen per batch size; was linear scaling/warmup tried; were LB runs trained longer or matched by updates?  
- Key question: provide final training cross-entropy values (not just accuracy) per model for SB vs LB.  
- Key question: how stable are sharpness measures across reparameterizations and random seeds; does SB–LB gap persist under transformations?  
- Key question: can you provide curvature evidence beyond the heuristic metric (e.g., Lanczos, Hutchinson, Fisher eigenvalues)?  
- Key question: do SAM or SWA close the LB generalization gap and reduce the sharpness metric; does gradual batch-size increase replicate warm-start benefits?  
- Key question: how does generalization vary over a dense grid of batch sizes under compute-matched settings, and does sharpness correlate monotonically with test error?
