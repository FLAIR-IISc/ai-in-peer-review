Summary
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training of deep networks tends to generalize worse than small-batch (SB) training. The central claim is empirical: LB methods converge to sharp minima (high local sensitivity of the training loss), while SB methods converge to flatter minima, and this difference explains the generalization gap. The paper supports this with (i) 1D/curvilinear parametric plots of the loss between SB and LB solutions, (ii) a heuristic “sharpness” metric based on a local worst-case perturbation solved approximately with L-BFGS-B, (iii) experiments across several architectures/datasets, and (iv) warm-start experiments showing that LB can recover good generalization when started near flat minima found by SB. The authors also report negative or partial results from data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial/robust training.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical evidence, while suggestive, does not sufficiently control for known confounders in batch-size studies (learning-rate/epoch schedules, weight decay, training duration, linear scaling/warmup, optimizer choice), making the causal claim (LB→sharp minima→poor generalization) insufficiently supported.
- The sharpness metric is ad hoc and not invariant to common reparameterizations of neural networks, so conclusions about “sharp vs flat” minima are not robust.

Supporting arguments
- Incomplete hyperparameter control across batch sizes. It is now well understood that large-batch training requires different learning-rate schedules (e.g., linear scaling with batch size and warmup), potentially longer training, and carefully tuned weight decay/momentum to maintain generalization. The paper fixes SB at 256 and LB at 10% of the dataset without convincingly demonstrating fair optimization budgets or best-effort tuning per regime. The reported generalization gap may partly reflect suboptimal LB hyperparameters rather than an intrinsic phenomenon.
- Optimizer dependence and representativeness. Most experiments use ADAM. Generalization behavior of ADAM differs from SGD with momentum; many large-batch successes in vision rely on SGD-momentum and specific schedules. Claims about “LB methods” should be supported across optimizers, especially SGD, or at least demonstrate that optimizer–batch interactions are not driving the effect.
- Experimental design and reporting. It is stated that both regimes reach similar training loss, but exact training losses (not only accuracy) and loss distributions should be reported to ensure comparability. The LB batch is extremely large (e.g., 10% of CIFAR-10 ≈ 5k), which may reduce gradient noise dramatically and change the effective optimization dynamics. There is limited exploration of intermediate batch sizes and of compute-matched protocols (e.g., equal number of optimizer updates vs equal epochs vs equal wall-clock budgets).
- Sharpness metric and reparameterization issues. The metric maximizes the loss in a local box around the solution, approximated with only 10 L-BFGS-B steps, and depends on parameter scaling and parameterization. For ReLU networks with BatchNorm, simple weight rescalings leave the function unchanged but can arbitrarily change Hessian eigenvalues and local sensitivity measures. Without a scale- or reparameterization-invariant notion of flatness, the sharp/flat conclusion is fragile. This is especially salient given BatchNorm and Dropout in the architectures.
- Limited curvature evidence. The paper asserts many large positive Hessian eigenvalues at LB solutions but does not measure spectra (even approximations). Modern tools (Lanczos, stochastic Lanczos quadrature, Hutchinson trace, Fisher/Gauss–Newton proxies) could provide direct and scale-aware curvature evidence.
- 1D/curvilinear plots are suggestive but not conclusive. Due to symmetries (e.g., neuron permutations, scaling) and the high dimensionality, a line between two parameter vectors may traverse high-loss regions even if both endpoints lie on the same flat basin in function space. Additional diagnostics (e.g., mode connectivity curves that minimize loss along the path) would strengthen the interpretation.
- Interpretation vs alternative explanations. The paper interprets SB’s “noise” as escaping sharp basins. This is plausible but not disentangled from: (i) different effective learning rates/updates per epoch; (ii) implicit regularization differences; (iii) the known relationship between gradient noise scale and generalization. Direct measurements of gradient noise statistics and their correlation with the sharpness metric would clarify causality.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Ensure fair and state-of-the-art LB baselines: use SGD-momentum with linear scaling rule, warmup, tuned weight decay/label smoothing, and train longer to equalize the total number of parameter updates (or compare compute-matched budgets). Report sensitivity to these choices.
- Replace or complement the sharpness metric with scale-/reparameterization-aware measures:
  - Normalize by layer-wise scales or use path-normalized sharpness/PAC-Bayes–inspired flatness.
  - Report Hessian spectral approximations: top-k eigenvalues via Lanczos, Hessian trace, and compare Fisher (empirical) spectra to decouple loss geometry from parameterization.
  - Evaluate gradient covariance and the “noise scale” over training; relate it to batch size and generalization.
- Strengthen path analyses with mode connectivity: find low-loss connecting curves between SB and LB solutions to test whether LB is isolated or connected to flat basins.
- Expand optimizer coverage: compare ADAM vs SGD-momentum; include decoupled weight decay for ADAM; test Entropy-SGD, SAM (sharpness-aware minimization), SWA/SWAG, and explicit noise injection to LB to see whether “flatness-seeking” algorithms mitigate the gap without SB warm starts.
- Clarify training protocols: learning rates, schedules, weight decay, number of epochs/updates, early stopping criteria, and convergence thresholds; report mean±std over seeds for both train loss and test error.
- Investigate invariances: show whether sharpness conclusions persist under simple re-scalings of layers (with compensatory inverse scaling in subsequent layers) or with Ghost BatchNorm.

Questions for the authors
1. How were learning rates, schedules, momentum/weight decay chosen for each batch size and optimizer? Did you try linear scaling and warmup for LB? Did you train LB longer to match the number of parameter updates?
2. Can you report final training cross-entropy (not only accuracy) for SB vs LB, per model, to ensure comparable empirical risk?
3. How stable are sharpness measurements across random reparameterizations (e.g., layer rescaling) and across seeds? Does the SB–LB gap persist under such transformations?
4. Can you provide curvature evidence beyond the heuristic metric—e.g., top eigenvalues/traces via Lanczos/Hutchinson; Fisher eigenvalues—to substantiate the “sharpness” claim robustly?
5. Does SAM or SWA close the LB generalization gap while also reducing your sharpness metric? If warm-starting LB from SB helps, does gradual batch-size increase (dynamic sampling) replicate that without SB pretraining?
6. How does the generalization gap vary over a dense grid of batch sizes under a compute-matched setting (equal updates), and does your sharpness metric correlate monotonically with test error across that grid?
