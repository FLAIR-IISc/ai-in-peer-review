- Main problem: Large-batch (LB) training often generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) training; authors attribute this to LB converging to sharper minima while SB finds flatter, better-generalizing minima.
- Approach: Empirical study across architectures/datasets using 1D and curvilinear interpolation, a practical sharpness metric, batch-size threshold analysis, and warm-start experiments.
- Key contribution 1: Empirical characterization linking the generalization gap to sharp vs. flat minima.
- Key contribution 2: A heuristic, computationally feasible sharpness metric showing 1–2 orders-of-magnitude SB/LB differences.
- Key contribution 3: Warm-start results suggesting SB “exploration” helps find flat regions that LB can exploit.
- Key contribution 4: Performance analysis arguing that resolving LB generalization would significantly improve scalability.
- Decision: Accept.
- Primary reason for accept: Important, timely question with coherent, cross-architecture empirical evidence and insightful warm-start experiments.
- Importance/motivation: Batch size is central to scaling deep learning; the generalization gap is widely observed but poorly understood.
- Methodology strengths: Multiple network types, datasets, five random restarts, consistent phenomena, and informative interpolation plots.
- Evidence: Sharpness metric and interpolation indicate LB minima are sharper and correlate with worse test accuracy; warm-start experiments offer a plausible mechanism.
- Framing: Authors present findings as empirical correlations rather than formal causal claims, which is appropriate given the scope.
- Reproducibility: Clear writing, appendix details, and code pointers for key plots.
- Limitation — metric: Sharpness measure is ad hoc, depends on parameterization, scaling choices, and may be affected by batch norm; not invariant to reparameterizations.
- Limitation — hyperparameters: SB and LB may require different LR schedules, BN momentum, and regularization; the paper lacks systematic sweeps to rule out confounds.
- Limitation — compute budget: Different numbers of parameter updates between SB/LB could confound results; budgets/epochs not equalized.
- Limitation — optimizer: Experiments focus on Adam; SGD with momentum, known to differ in generalization, is insufficiently explored.
- Limitation — batch-size selection: Use of very large LB (e.g., 10% of dataset) and limited batch-size sweep reduce external validity.
- Suggested improvement: Report top Hessian eigenvalues (power/Lanczos) and spectra in random subspaces to strengthen curvature evidence.
- Suggested improvement: Use parameterization-invariant or normalized sharpness measures and analyze BN/rescaling effects.
- Suggested improvement: Repeat comparisons with fixed parameter updates, matched wall-clock, and tuned LB schedules (linear LR scaling, warmup); try ghost batch norm.
- Suggested improvement: Ablate sharpness metric sensitivity to ε, L-BFGS-B steps, and subspace dimension p and show distributions across seeds.
- Suggested improvement: Include SGD+momentum experiments with tuned schedules to test optimizer dependence.
- Suggested improvement: Study dynamic/gradually increasing batch-size schedules motivated by warm-start findings.
- Key questions for authors: How were hyperparameters chosen for SB vs LB, and were linear LR scaling/warmup tried?
- Key question: Can results be replicated when equalizing total parameter updates or epochs?
- Key question: How sensitive are sharpness results to ε and subspace dimension p, and can you provide robustness curves?
- Key question: How does sharpness behave under weight rescalings that preserve function (parameterization invariance)?
- Key question: Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues for SB vs LB on smaller networks?
- Key question: Did you try ghost batch norm or fixing BN statistics to reduce the gap?
- Key question: Do the phenomena persist with SGD+momentum under tuned schedules on CIFAR/ImageNet-scale tasks?
