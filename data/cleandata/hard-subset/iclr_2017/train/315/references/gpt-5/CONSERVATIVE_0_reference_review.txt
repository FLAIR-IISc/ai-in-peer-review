Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles an important and practical question: why do large-batch (LB) training regimes often yield worse test performance than small-batch (SB) methods, despite achieving similar training loss? The central claim is that LB converges to “sharp” minima (high curvature), whereas SB tends to “flat” minima, and that this correlates with a generalization gap.
- Evidence provided includes: (i) 1D and curvilinear interpolations between SB and LB solutions showing steeper loss growth near LB solutions; (ii) a heuristic “sharpness” metric based on local maximization in a small box (optionally projected to a random subspace), showing 1–2 orders of magnitude difference between SB and LB; (iii) warm-start experiments where LB succeeds only after SB has “explored” towards flatter regions; (iv) negative/partial results from data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training.
- The paper positions the phenomenon within existing literature (SGD stability, saddle-point avoidance, MDL/Bayesian intuitions) and argues that improving LB generalization would unlock much better parallel scalability.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The evidence for “sharpness causes poor generalization” rests on a non-standard, parameterization-dependent sharpness proxy and 1D visualizations, without adequate controls for known confounders (optimizer, learning-rate scaling/warmup, regularization, and scale invariances introduced by BN/ReLU). 2) Experimental scope is limited (moderate-scale datasets, mostly Adam) and hyperparameter tuning across batch sizes is insufficient to rule out better LB configurations or alternative explanations.

Supporting arguments
- Sharpness definition and invariances: The proposed metric is ad hoc, depends on a box radius and A, uses only 10 steps of L-BFGS-B, and is not demonstrably invariant to reparameterizations (e.g., layer-wise rescalings in BN/ReLU nets) known to arbitrarily affect Hessian spectra. Without a parameterization-invariant measure or explicit normalization for scale symmetries, the reported “1–2 orders” differences could be confounded by representation scale rather than intrinsic flatness.
- Limited optimizer and hyperparameter controls: Most experiments use Adam for both SB and LB; while some LB runs reportedly used other optimizers, there is no systematic hyperparameter sweep (learning rate, momentum/βs, weight decay, label smoothing, LR warmup, gradient clipping) tailored to batch size. Established practice is to adjust learning rate with batch size (and often use warmup) and to re-tune regularization; the paper’s claim that LB inherently finds sharp minima is not convincing without thorough tuning per regime.
- Dataset/scale and external validity: Results are on MNIST/CIFAR-like vision tasks and TIMIT, with LB often set to 10% of the training set. This leaves open whether the phenomenon persists (or can be mitigated) in larger-scale settings and modern training recipes; the paper’s performance-model arguments would benefit from evidence at scale.
- Causality vs correlation: The warm-start results are suggestive but do not establish that gradient noise is the causal factor steering toward flat minima, as opposed to effective learning rate or schedule effects. No direct measurements of gradient noise scale/variance are reported, and the exploration vs exploitation narrative remains speculative.
- Evaluation methodology: 1D and curvilinear plots are illustrative but insufficient for characterizing curvature in very high dimensions. If curvature is the core explanatory variable, more direct, better-controlled spectral approximations (e.g., Lanczos/KFAC-based top-eigenvalue estimates, Hessian trace via Hutchinson) would strengthen the case.
- Fairness of comparison: It is unclear whether training budgets, decay schedules, and total parameter updates are matched in a way that fairly compares SB and LB. “Train until the loss ceases to improve” is not reproducible and may differentially favor regimes.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not necessarily tied to the decision)
- Provide a parameterization-aware flatness metric, or normalize sharpness to account for layer-wise rescaling invariances (especially with BN). Consider PAC-Bayes–style flatness (posterior volume) or noise-injection–based measures that are less sensitive to reparameterization.
- Systematically re-tune hyperparameters per batch size and optimizer, including LR scaling, warmup, momentum, weight decay, label smoothing, and data augmentation. Report sensitivity curves and best-of-sweep results for each batch size.
- Replace or complement the heuristic sharpness proxy with spectral diagnostics: top Hessian eigenvalues via Lanczos, Hessian trace, or spectral density approximations; report across multiple random seeds with confidence intervals.
- Control for training budget: match number of steps and/or total compute; report wall-clock; compare test accuracy at equal updates and equal epochs.
- Measure and report gradient noise scale (variance) across batch sizes; if noise is the mechanism, the relationship between noise scale, curvature, and escape from sharp basins should be made quantitative.
- Extend to larger datasets and stronger baselines (e.g., ImageNet-scale, modern regularization). Demonstrate whether the phenomenon persists under best practices for LB (LR warmup, tuned WD, etc.).
- Clarify and justify the construction of the box C and the role of A and A+. Analyze sensitivity of the metric to epsilon, A’s dimensionality, and the number of inner optimization steps.
- Release full code and scripts to reproduce all experiments, not just the interpolation plots.

Questions for the authors
1) How does your sharpness metric behave under layer rescalings (especially with batch norm)? Can you demonstrate invariance or provide a normalization to address known symmetries?
2) Did you tune learning rates, momentum/βs, weight decay, and schedules separately for each batch size and optimizer? Please report the best-found settings and ablation tables.
3) How sensitive are your conclusions to LR warmup and linear LR scaling with batch size?
4) Can you provide Hessian spectral estimates (top eigenvalue/trace) to corroborate the sharpness differences beyond 1D slices and the heuristic proxy?
5) Can you quantify gradient noise scale across SB/LB and correlate it with curvature and escape behavior, rather than relying on qualitative warm-start evidence?
6) Are your results reproducible on larger datasets and with stronger regularization (e.g., label smoothing, mixup/cutout) commonly used in high-performance LB training?
7) How were training budgets matched between SB and LB (epochs, updates, wall-clock)? Could different stopping criteria bias the comparison?
