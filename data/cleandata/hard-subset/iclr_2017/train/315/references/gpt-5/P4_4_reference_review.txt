1. Summary (claims and contributions)
- The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite reaching similar training loss. Across several feedforward and convolutional architectures and datasets, the authors empirically observe a generalization gap for LB methods.
- The central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers (large positive Hessian eigenvalues, high local sensitivity), whereas SB methods tend to find flatter minimizers. The paper supports this by: (i) 1D and curvilinear interpolation plots between SB and LB solutions showing sharpness differences; (ii) a heuristic “sharpness” metric based on local max of f in a small neighborhood (full-space and random low-dimensional subspaces); (iii) experiments showing a batch-size threshold beyond which sharpness increases and test accuracy drops; and (iv) “warm-start” experiments indicating that LB can generalize well when initialized near a flat minimizer located by SB.
- The paper also reports preliminary attempts to mitigate the LB generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative proximal training, adversarial/robust training). These improve accuracy but do not eliminate sharpness. The paper discusses connections to MDL/Bayesian views of flat minima and to theoretical work on loss landscapes.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The evidence for the core causal claim (LB converges to sharp minima which generalize worse) is suggestive but not sufficiently rigorous; the sharpness metric is heuristic, ambiguously defined, and only inexactly optimized, with limited sensitivity analysis.
- The study does not adequately control for known confounders in LB training (learning-rate scaling/schedules, warmup, momentum/Adam hyperparameters, BatchNorm statistics, weight decay) and lacks comparisons to strong LB baselines; thus the generalization gap may be partly due to suboptimal LB training protocols rather than an intrinsic property.

3. Supporting arguments
- Clarity and definition of sharpness: The sharpness metric φx,f(ε,A) is defined via a box C built from A and A+, but the expression for C and the normalization in Eq. (4) are ambiguous (typesetting suggests a “+ f(x) × 100” term). The use of 10 L-BFGS-B iterations for the inner maximization is ad hoc; there is no convergence check, nor analysis of sensitivity to the box size ε, to the subspace dimension p, or to the random seed of A. Without these, the metric’s reliability across models and settings is unclear.
- Limited direct curvature evidence: The narrative links sharpness to Hessian spectra, but no top eigenvalues or spectral approximations (e.g., Lanczos/power iteration) are reported, even on smaller models where this is practical. This weakens the claim that results reflect curvature rather than other sources of sensitivity.
- Experimental controls: The LB configuration is fixed at 10% of the training set, which is unusually large and may not reflect common LB practice. It is unclear if learning rates, schedules, and optimizer hyperparameters were tuned per batch size (e.g., linear scaling, warmup, momentum correction), or if BN statistics and regularization (weight decay, dropout) were adjusted appropriately for LB. The paper notes ADAM is used for both regimes but omits critical training details; ADAM’s behavior can vary substantially with batch size. Without such controls, the generalization gap could be a training protocol artifact.
- Scope and representativeness: While multiple networks are considered, most results are on relatively modest datasets and models; no large-scale setting (e.g., ImageNet-scale) or modern LB recipes are tested. The reported batch-size thresholds and warm-start results are interesting, but causality remains speculative.
- Positioning in the literature: The connection between flat minima and generalization has prior art; the novelty is the linkage to batch size through empirical evidence. However, the work does not sufficiently situate itself against strong LB training techniques or assess whether those protocols reduce sharpness and the gap.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all part of the decision)
- Clarify and correct the sharpness metric: precisely define C, A+, normalization, and units; justify invariance claims; provide algorithmic details for the inner maximization; add sensitivity analyses over ε, p, number of inner iterations, and random A. Release code to reproduce sharpness computations.
- Provide direct curvature measurements: on smaller models, compute the top-k Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., via Lanczos) at SB/LB solutions. Report directional curvature distributions in random directions and along principal axes.
- Control for optimization confounders: explicitly tune per-batch learning rates, momentum/Adam betas, weight decay, schedules (include warmup), and BN handling; report the exact settings; compare SGD with momentum to ADAM. Match training budgets fairly (number of optimizer updates vs epochs) and report robustness to these choices.
- Strong LB baselines: include dynamic batch size schedules, linear scaling, warmup, label smoothing, and other known practices. Evaluate whether these reduce sharpness and the generalization gap.
- Expand empirical scope: add larger-scale datasets and architectures; report multiple seeds; quantify the correlation between sharpness and test error across many runs.
- Causality analysis: consider interventions that explicitly bias towards flatness (e.g., entropy-SGD, sharpness-aware minimization variants) and test whether they remove the LB gap. Ablate the warm-start protocol to quantify how far into training SB must proceed before LB can take over.

Questions for the authors
- Sharpness metric details: Please provide the exact formula for φ, including the normalization term in Eq. (4), the definition and role of A+, and the rationale for C’s bounds ((|(A+ x)i| + 1)). How sensitive are results to ε, p, the number of L-BFGS-B steps, and the random seed of A?
- Hyperparameter tuning: Were learning rates, schedules (including warmup), momentum/Adam parameters, weight decay, and dropout tuned separately for each batch size? How were BatchNorm statistics handled across SB/LB? Can you share the exact training protocols?
- Fairness of training budgets: Are SB and LB matched by number of optimization updates, epochs, or wall-clock? Do conclusions hold under different matching criteria?
- Curvature measurements: Can you report top eigenvalues (or spectral density estimates) of the Hessian at SB vs LB solutions for smaller models to corroborate the heuristic sharpness metric?
- Batch-size threshold: How consistent is the threshold across datasets/seeds? Does progressively increasing batch size (dynamic sampling) avoid sharp minima without SB warm-start?
- Remedies: For the augmentation and conservative/adversarial training experiments, were hyperparameters tuned for LB specifically? Do these methods reduce sharpness under your metric, and by how much relative to improved accuracy?
