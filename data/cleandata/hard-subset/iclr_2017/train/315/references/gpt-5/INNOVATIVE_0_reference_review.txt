1) Summary of contributions
- The paper tackles the long-observed generalization gap of large-batch (LB) training in deep networks. It advances a concrete hypothesis: LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima (large positive Hessian eigenvalues), which generalize worse than the flatter minima typically reached by small-batch (SB) methods.
- It provides multiple empirical probes supporting this view:
  - 1-D (and curvilinear) interpolations between SB and LB solutions showing much sharper basins around LB solutions.
  - A practical sharpness metric based on local worst-case loss increase within a small box (with optional random low-dimensional subspace projections).
  - Warm-start experiments where LB succeeds only after SB has “explored” towards flatter regions; batch-size threshold phenomena; distance-from-initialization differences.
- It tests several attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/robust training). These help somewhat but do not remove the sharpness gap, suggesting the issue is structural rather than a simple regularization deficit.
- Practical implication: naive LB training is attractive for parallelism, but the noise properties of SB appear critical for finding flat minima; dynamic sampling or warm-start from SB may be viable paths.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: 
- The paper makes a clear, novel, and bold conceptual contribution that reframes a widely reported phenomenon (LB generalization gap) in terms of sharp vs. flat minima, and provides simple, reproducible diagnostics to study it. 
- Even with some methodological imperfections, the evidence is consistent and the work opens a consequential line of inquiry with direct implications for scalable training.

3) Supporting arguments
- Novelty and significance: While flat vs. sharp minima has precedent, tying batch size to sharpness and carefully documenting its empirical effects across architectures/datasets is new and influential. The work offers a crisp explanatory lens that has already inspired follow-ups (e.g., noise injection, dynamic batch sizing, biasing toward flatness).
- Breadth and coherence of evidence: The interpolation plots, sharpness metric (full space and random subspaces), warm-start experiments, and batch-size threshold studies tell a consistent story across multiple models and data.
- Practical impact: Identifying simple protocols (e.g., warm-starting, dynamic sampling) to mitigate LB failure modes is immediately actionable for distributed training and systems work.
- Honest negative results: The attempted remedies that did not fully work are carefully documented, which is valuable to the community.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Sharpness metric and parameterization: The proposed metric is intuitive and practical, but can be sensitive to parameterization (e.g., weight scaling with ReLU/BatchNorm). Please:
  - Empirically test invariance by rescaling layers (especially with BN) and report how sharpness changes at the same function value.
  - Complement with parameterization-lean probes: top Hessian eigenvalue estimates via power/Lanczos at the found minima; or flatness measures in function space (e.g., perturb outputs or margins).
- Hyperparameter/tuning fairness for LB:
  - Report learning rate schedules, momentum, weight decay, and any warmup for SB vs LB. Prior work suggests LR scaling and warmup matter greatly; an ablation (constant steps, constant epochs, linear scaling of LR) would strengthen claims that the gap is intrinsic, not tuning.
  - Consider noise-injection baselines for LB (e.g., SGLD-style Gaussian noise or Entropy-SGD) to test the “gradient noise drives flatness” hypothesis directly.
- Scope of experiments:
  - Include at least one larger-scale setting (e.g., ImageNet-scale) with SGD to assess generality beyond Adam and smaller datasets; your current Adam-centric results could be confounded by optimizer-specific dynamics.
  - Report the fraction and principal directions of the “sharp subspace” more rigorously (e.g., top-k eigenvalues/vectors, or randomized sketching) to quantify dimensionality of sharpness.
- Metric details and robustness:
  - Justify the construction of C and the use of A+; clarify the claimed dimension/sparsity invariance. Provide sensitivity analyses over epsilon, p (subspace dimension), and number of L-BFGS-B steps to ensure conclusions are stable.
  - Since the maximization is inexact (10 L-BFGS-B steps), report convergence diagnostics or bounds to ensure comparable sharpness values across runs.
- Causal evidence:
  - Where possible, control for the number of parameter updates vs. epochs to disentangle “fewer noisy updates” from “larger batches.”
  - Quantify correlations between measured sharpness and test error across many runs to strengthen the flatness-generalization link.

5) Questions for the authors
- Learning rate and regularization: Did you try linear LR scaling with batch size and warmup? How were weight decay and momentum tuned for LB vs SB? Can you provide ablations showing the gap persists under carefully tuned LB schedules?
- Parameterization dependence: How does your sharpness metric change under simple rescalings of layer weights (with and without BN)? Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues to corroborate the metric?
- Noise hypothesis: If you add calibrated Gaussian noise to LB gradients (matching SB gradient variance), do LB methods still converge to sharp minima? Conversely, does reducing SB noise (via larger batches late in training) increase sharpness?
- Dynamic batch sizing: Your warm-start results are compelling. Can you show a full dynamic-batch curriculum (gradually increasing batch size) that matches SB generalization without SB pretraining?
- Threshold effects: What determines the batch-size threshold where sharpness/test accuracy change abruptly? Is it predictable from gradient variance or Hessian spectrum near initialization?
- Subspace sharpness: You mention sharpness concentrates in a low-dimensional subspace. Can you estimate the dimensionality and alignment of these directions across runs?
- Reproducibility: Will you release code, exact configs, and seeds to allow others to reproduce the sharpness metric and plots?

Overall, despite some methodological gaps and room for deeper analysis, the paper presents a compelling, novel narrative with practical implications. I recommend acceptance.
