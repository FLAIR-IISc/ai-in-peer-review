Summary and contributions
- The paper studies the well-known “generalization gap” observed when training deep networks with large mini-batches. It documents that, across several architectures and datasets, large-batch (LB) training achieves comparable training loss but worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training.
- The central claim is that LB training tends to converge to “sharp” minima (high sensitivity of f in a small neighborhood), while SB training finds “flatter” minima; the authors argue this difference explains the generalization gap.
- Evidence includes: 1D and curvilinear interpolation plots between SB and LB solutions; an ad hoc sharpness metric based on local maximization in a small box (full-space and random low-dimensional subspaces); batch-size sweep identifying a threshold after which test performance degrades; and warm-start experiments suggesting that SB noise helps escape basins of sharp minima and that LB methods can succeed when initialized near flatter regions.
- The paper also evaluates several remedies (data augmentation, a proximal “conservative training” inner loop, and adversarial/stability training). These sometimes reduce the gap but still lead to relatively sharp solutions, according to the metric. The paper frames open questions and relates to flatness/MDL/Bayesian arguments.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The core causal claim (LB → sharp minima → worse generalization) is insufficiently supported by rigorous, controlled experiments and relies on a sharpness metric with questionable validity. (2) Critical baselines and confounders (hyperparameter tuning per batch size, regularization, learning-rate schedules/warmup, optimizer choice, and normalization effects) are not systematically controlled, making the conclusions fragile.

Supporting arguments
- Sharpness metric validity. The proposed “sharpness” measure is an approximate local adversarial maximization solved by 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B, with a box defined via A+ and random subspaces. This is ad hoc, depends on several arbitrary choices (box size, projection dimension, inexact inner loop), and is not shown to be stable across these choices. There is no direct validation against more principled curvature measures (e.g., spectral norm via power iteration or Hessian-vector products) on even small models. The metric may not be invariant to reparameterizations or rescalings, which undermines its interpretability as a general property of a solution rather than a parameterization artifact.
- Experimental controls. The paper uses ADAM in both SB and LB regimes but does not report careful tuning of hyperparameters per batch size (learning rate, learning-rate schedule/warmup, β1/β2, weight decay, gradient clipping). It is well established that LB training is highly sensitive to these choices. Defining LB as “10% of the dataset” conflates datasets of very different sizes and does not align with common practice; batch sizes should be studied in absolute terms with learning-rate scaling/warmup and regularization tuned per size.
- Fairness of comparison. It is unclear whether experiments control for equal compute, equal number of epochs, or equal number of optimization steps. The criterion “train until the loss ceased to improve” is ambiguous and potentially favors one regime. Without standardized training budgets, conclusions about generalization are hard to attribute to batch size alone.
- Confounders: normalization and architecture. Several architectures use batch normalization and dropout. BN’s statistics are batch-size dependent; very large batches can change the effective regularization and the signal-to-noise of BN’s estimates. The paper does not disentangle BN’s effect from batch size (e.g., by comparing LayerNorm/GroupNorm or disabling BN).
- Breadth and statistical strength. Only six configurations with five seeds are reported. Results are summarized but raw numbers (variance, effect sizes) are not consistently presented, and correlation between sharpness and test error across runs is not quantified. The evidence is more anecdotal than conclusive.
- Causality versus correlation. The paper repeatedly adopts a causal narrative (LB “converge to sharp minima, hence poor generalization”) but the experiments support correlation at best. For example, after data augmentation, generalization improves but “sharpness” remains high by the proposed metric, weakening the central causal link.
- Remedy evaluation. The exploration of remedies is shallow: adversarial training/stability training “did not help,” but no details on hyperparameters, strength of attacks/noise, or ablations. Conservative training is only tried with a fixed λ and few inner steps. The dynamic sampling suggestion is only indirectly supported via warm-starting, without a direct controlled schedule study.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Replace or validate the sharpness metric. For small models, compute approximate top Hessian eigenvalues (power iteration with Hessian-vector products or stochastic Lanczos). Study metric sensitivity to its hyperparameters. Discuss parameterization invariance and scaling issues.
- Stronger baselines. For each batch size, properly tune learning rate, warmup schedule, momentum/Adam β’s, weight decay (including decoupled weight decay), label smoothing, gradient clipping, and data augmentation. Report grid or Bayesian search ranges and best settings found per size.
- Fair evaluation protocol. Compare under matched training budgets: (a) fixed total number of epochs; (b) fixed number of optimizer steps; and (c) fixed total examples seen. Report all three or justify one. Ensure identical augmentations and regularization.
- BN confounders. Repeat key comparisons with architectures that do not use batch statistics (e.g., GroupNorm/LayerNorm) or evaluate BN with ghost batch normalization to decouple batch size from BN behavior.
- Quantify correlation. Across many seeds and checkpoints, report correlation coefficients (and confidence intervals) between sharpness and test error. Show scatter plots to substantiate the relationship beyond a few exemplars.
- Larger-scale and modern setups. If feasible, add experiments on a standard large-scale benchmark (e.g., ImageNet with ResNet-like models), where LB training is most relevant, and where known training recipes exist.
- Remedies. Evaluate dynamic batch-size schedules directly and systematically. For adversarial training, specify attack strength, method, and compute budget; consider TRADES-style stability if the aim is robustness to perturbations in weight space or data space.

Questions for the authors
1) How were optimizer hyperparameters chosen per batch size? Did you tune learning rate, warmup, β1/β2, weight decay, and schedule separately for each batch size? Please provide search details and best settings.
2) Are your conclusions robust when controlling for equal number of epochs, equal steps, and equal examples seen? Which protocol do your current results follow?
3) How sensitive is your sharpness metric to the choice of ε/box size, number of L-BFGS-B steps, and projection dimension p? Can you show stability analyses?
4) Did you validate the sharpness metric against top Hessian eigenvalues (via power iteration) on smaller networks?
5) What is the effect of batch normalization? Do the trends persist with GroupNorm/LayerNorm or without normalization?
6) Can you quantify the correlation between your sharpness metric and test error across many runs (e.g., Pearson/Spearman with CIs)?
7) In the data augmentation experiments, generalization improved but the metric still indicates “sharp” minima. How do you reconcile this with the proposed causal story?
8) Did you try learning-rate scaling and warmup specifically tailored for very large batches, and ghost BN for large batches? Please report results.
