- Central claim: Large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) training finds flatter minima, explaining LB’s poorer generalization.  
- Loss-path visualizations: Linear and curvilinear paths between SB and LB solutions show LB minima are much sharper.  
- Sharpness metric: A practical local adversarial weight-perturbation metric (full space and random subspaces) shows 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sharpness for LB across architectures/datasets.  
- Batch-size threshold: There is a batch-size threshold beyond which test accuracy drops and measured sharpness increases.  
- Warm-start result: Starting training with SB iterations then switching to LB recovers good generalization and lower sharpness, motivating dynamic batch sizing.  
- Negative/partial remedies: Data augmentation, conservative/proximal training, and adversarial training improve accuracy somewhat but still yield sharper minima for LB.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason 1: Addresses an important practical question and supplies clear, reproducible empirical evidence linking batch size, sharpness, and generalization.  
- Key reason 2: Opens avenues for algorithmic improvement (dynamic batch sizing, warm starts) and provides useful diagnostics (sharpness metric, path visualizations).  
- Literature placement: Well situated within prior work on flat minima, visualization, stability, and landscape studies; MDL/Bayesian rationale is consistent with prior theory.  
- Evidence quality: Experiments run across multiple architectures (CNNs, FC nets), datasets (vision, speech), and replicated across five runs; path visualizations and warm-starts are compelling.  
- Practicality of metric: Sharpness metric is computationally feasible, correlates with test accuracy, and subspace variant adds robustness.  
- Low-dimensional concentration: Sharpness often concentrates along a low-dimensional subspace, useful for diagnostics.  
- Broader impact: Proposes a simple, testable narrative linking batch size, gradient noise, and generalization; suggests “explore-then-exploit” strategies.  
- Limitation — parameterization sensitivity: Sharpness metric is not invariant to reparameterizations (e.g., scale transforms with ReLU/BatchNorm) and can be sensitive to parameter scaling.  
- Limitation — optimization confounds: Use of ADAM and limited hyperparameter tuning/learning-rate schedules for LB may confound conclusions; proper LB recipes (lr scaling, warmup) need systematic checking.  
- Limitation — Hessian evidence missing: Sharpness inferred via function sensitivity rather than direct top-Hessian eigenvalue estimates; Hessian-based validation would strengthen claims.  
- Limitation — scope: Experiments are on moderate-scale datasets/architectures; results on modern residual networks and larger datasets would improve external validity.  
- Suggestion: Provide full training details per setting (optimizers, lr schedules, weight decay, batch-norm behavior) and report results under standard LB recipes.  
- Suggestion: Add curvature diagnostics (top-k Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos with HVPs) to validate the sharpness metric.  
- Suggestion: Investigate parameterization-invariant sharpness measures (weight-normalized coordinates, Fisher-Rao, PAC-Bayes).  
- Suggestion: Report sharpness measured on test loss as well as training loss.  
- Suggestion: Strengthen statistical reporting (confidence intervals, tests) and show robustness/sensitivity of inner maximization procedure.  
- Suggestion: Expand dynamic batch-size experiments beyond warm starts (explicit schedules, comparisons to pure SB/LB).  
- Questions for authors — tuning: How were learning rates and schedules tuned across batch sizes/optimizers; were linear/sqrt lr scaling and warmup tried?  
- Questions for authors — Hessian: Can you provide top Hessian eigenvalue estimates for SB vs LB via power/Lanczos and do they align with the sharpness metric?  
- Questions for authors — invariances: How does the sharpness measure behave under layer rescalings or BN-induced invariances; results under weight-normalized parameterizations?  
- Questions for authors — test-side sharpness: Do sharpness-generalization correlations persist when measuring sharpness on test loss?  
- Questions for authors — threshold stability: Is the batch-size threshold stable across seeds/hyperparameters and does it move with lr scaling or regularization?  
- Questions for authors — modern architectures: Can you include results on residual architectures or larger datasets to test generality?  
- Questions for authors — inner maximization details: How often does L-BFGS-B hit box boundaries and how sensitive are sharpness values to epsilon and the 10-iteration budget?  
- Overall: A well-motivated, carefully executed empirical study with actionable insights and clear diagnostics.
