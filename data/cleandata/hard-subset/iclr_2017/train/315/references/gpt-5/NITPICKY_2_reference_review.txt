Summary and contributions
- The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training loss. The central claim is that LB tends to converge to sharp minima (characterized heuristically by large curvature), while SB gravitates toward flatter minima; sharpness correlates with poorer generalization.
- Evidence provided includes: (i) 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions; (ii) a computable “sharpness” metric defined as a local worst-case increase in the loss within a small box (full-space and random 100-d subspaces), showing order-of-magnitude differences between SB and LB minima; (iii) batch-size threshold experiments showing a sharp drop in test accuracy and increase in sharpness beyond a certain batch size; (iv) warm-start experiments in which LB can recover good generalization if started from SB iterates believed to be nearer flat minima; and (v) preliminary attempts (data augmentation, “conservative training,” adversarial/stability training) that reduce but do not eliminate the LB generalization gap/sharpness.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Confounding factors and inadequate experimental controls undercut causal claims; (2) the proposed sharpness metric is ad hoc, sensitive to parameterization, and insufficiently validated; (3) the paper’s claims (e.g., LB yields minimizers with many large Hessian eigenvalues) are not directly supported by measurements; (4) presentation and citation formatting issues are numerous.

Supporting arguments
1) Experimental confounds and insufficient controls
- BatchNorm confound: Many architectures use Batch Normalization. Changing batch size changes the BN statistics and its regularization behavior, which is known to affect generalization. The study attributes effects to batch size per se without controlling for BN (e.g., using GhostBN, freezing BN stats, or removing BN). This is a major confound.
- Learning-rate and schedule: The paper does not clearly specify per-regime learning-rate scaling, warmup, decay schedule, or weight decay. For LB training, standard practice (linear scaling, warmup, LARS/LAMB) is critical. Using the same Adam hyperparameters across disparate batch sizes can unfairly disadvantage LB and inflate “generalization gap.”
- Update budget vs. epoch budget: It is unclear whether runs were matched on number of parameter updates, total epochs, wall-clock, or convergence to the same training loss. LB often sees far fewer updates per epoch; comparing end-of-training test accuracy without controlling for update counts can bias results against LB.
- Choice of “LB” as 10% of the dataset: This yields very different absolute batch sizes across datasets; only two networks are explored over a batch-size sweep. The claims would be stronger with systematic batch sweeps across all tasks.
- Optimizer breadth: While the text states that SGD/Adagrad/adaQN showed similar behavior, the main results focus on Adam; there is no rigorous, side-by-side evidence for other optimizers.

2) Sharpness metric and claims about curvature
- The sharpness metric (Eq. (4)) is heuristic and lacks invariance to reparameterizations that preserve the function (e.g., layerwise rescalings in ReLU nets). The use of A+ x inside the box definition (Eq. (3)) and the stated “invariance to dimension and sparsity” is not justified. The dependence on ε and the arbitrary choice of a 100-d random subspace are not sensitivity-tested.
- The metric is computed via 10 L-BFGS-B iterations; no convergence checks are reported. This could bias measured “max” values and the SB/LB comparison.
- The paper asserts LB minima have “many large positive eigenvalues” but does not compute spectra or even top eigenvalues (e.g., via Lanczos/power iteration). Without direct Hessian information or a validated proxy (e.g., spectral norm over random directions) the spectral claims are speculative.

3) Overstated causal narrative
- Statements about “basins of attraction,” saddle-point behavior, and the mechanism of “noise pushing iterates out of sharp basins” are plausible but not empirically isolated. The warm-start experiment is suggestive but does not rule out the aforementioned confounds (BN behavior, LR schedule, update count).
- The conclusion that early stopping is irrelevant is not convincingly supported; shown curves for two models are insufficient to generalize broadly.

4) Presentation and reproducibility issues
- Numerous citation and formatting errors (e.g., escaped LaTeX strings in references; Srivastava et al. year listed as 1929; inconsistent “2014a/2014b” labeling). These detract from clarity.
- Several critical details are missing or ambiguous: exact LR/decay schedules, Adam β parameters, weight decay, data preprocessing for each dataset, and stopping criteria (“until the loss ceased to improve” is underspecified).

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Control for BatchNorm: Repeat key experiments with BN removed or with consistent effective BN (e.g., GhostBN) across batch sizes, or freeze BN statistics after a warmup; report whether the gap persists.
- Fair LB baselines: Include LB training with established practices (linear LR scaling, warmup, LARS/LAMB), and match number of optimizer steps or training loss across regimes.
- Hessian information: Report top-k Hessian eigenvalues or spectral norm at SB and LB minima using Lanczos/power iteration; analyze directional curvature distributions. This would substantiate curvature claims beyond the heuristic metric.
- Metric validation: Provide sensitivity analyses over ε, subspace dimension p, and optimizer iterations inside the maximization. Discuss lack of invariance to reparameterization and consider function-space measures or scale-invariant flatness proxies.
- Broader batch-size sweeps: For each architecture/dataset, show test accuracy and sharpness across a wide range of batch sizes, with error bars, matched updates, and consistent hyperparameters.
- Modern regularization: Test whether mixup, label smoothing, or SAM/ASAM reduce the gap while quantifying sharpness under your metric and via Hessian spectra.
- Clarify the performance model assumptions and relate to realistic parallel efficiency/communication regimes.

Questions for the authors
1) How were learning rates, schedules, weight decay, and Adam β parameters chosen per batch size? Did you use linear LR scaling and warmup for LB?
2) How did you control for the number of optimization steps? Are the results robust when matching SB and LB by updates or by final training loss?
3) What is the effect of BatchNorm on your conclusions? Do the gaps persist with BN removed or with BN statistics controlled uniformly across batch sizes?
4) How sensitive is your sharpness metric to ε, the choice of A, and the number of inner optimizer iterations? Please provide sensitivity plots.
5) Can you report top eigenvalues or spectral norms of the Hessian at SB and LB solutions to directly substantiate curvature claims?
6) Do your conclusions hold for SGD with momentum under strong LB baselines (e.g., LARS/LAMB) on CIFAR/ImageNet with standard data augmentation and label smoothing?
7) The constraint set in Eq. (3) includes A+ x; please justify its role and the claimed invariance properties. How does the metric behave under layerwise rescaling transformations that leave the function invariant?
