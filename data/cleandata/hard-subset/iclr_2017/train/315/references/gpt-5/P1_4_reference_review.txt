Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles an important and practical question: why do large-batch (LB) training methods often exhibit worse test performance than small-batch (SB) methods despite achieving similar training loss? The central claim is that LB tends to converge to sharp minima (high local sensitivity, large positive curvature) that generalize poorly, while SB converges to flatter minima that generalize better.
- The authors support this with: (i) 1D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions showing stark differences in local landscape; (ii) a computationally tractable “sharpness” metric based on maximizing the loss within a small box (full space and random low-dimensional subspaces); (iii) experiments across several architectures (fully connected and CNNs inspired by AlexNet/VGG) and datasets; (iv) analyses of batch-size thresholds and warm-start experiments indicating that initial SB “exploration” helps LB ultimately reach flatter regions; and (v) preliminary attempts to mitigate the gap (data augmentation, conservative training via proximal updates, adversarial/stability training), which reduce but do not eliminate the sharpness and generalization gap.
- The work connects to MDL/Bayesian intuitions for why flat minima may generalize better, and to parallel/distributed training motivation where LB would be more scalable if it did not harm generalization.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper provides a clear, well-motivated empirical study of a phenomenon of immediate relevance to the community, with multiple converging pieces of evidence (plots, metric, warm-start/batch-size thresholds) and practical implications for scalable training.
2) While not theoretically definitive and with limitations in the sharpness metric and experimental controls, the results are compelling, broadly reproducible, and have influenced subsequent lines of work on generalization and curvature.

Supporting arguments
- Importance and timeliness: Understanding the generalization gap with large minibatches is crucial for parallelizing deep learning. The paper situates the problem well and connects to prior optimization and generalization literature.
- Methodological variety: The combination of visualization, a sensitivity-based sharpness metric (both full-space and random subspaces), and training protocol experiments (batch size sweeps, warm-start) offers triangulation rather than reliance on a single diagnostic.
- Breadth of empirical coverage: Multiple architectures and datasets are considered; results are consistent across optimizers for LB (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD, adaQN).
- Clarity of the main empirical finding: The 1D/curvilinear plots and sharpness tables consistently show LB minima are sharper by 1–2 orders of magnitude using the proposed metric; warm-start experiments indicate SB noise may help escape sharp basins, and LB subsequently exploits flatter regions.

Caveats and limitations:
- The sharpness metric is heuristic and optimization-limited (10 L-BFGS-B iterations). It depends on parameterization; modern networks (with batch normalization and ReLU) admit reparameterizations and scalings that can alter perceived sharpness without changing function, raising questions of invariance despite the box normalization. A spectral estimate (e.g., power/Lanczos for top Hessian eigenvalues) or gradient-noise-scale analysis would strengthen claims.
- Hyperparameter fairness: The paper largely uses ADAM for both SB/LB; ADAM is known to have different generalization characteristics than SGD with momentum. Learning rates, weight decay, and schedules may need to be tuned differently for LB (e.g., linear scaling with warmup). It is not fully clear that LB was given an equally strong hyperparameter budget as SB across settings.
- The evidence remains correlational: the causal link “LB → sharp minima → worse test accuracy” is plausible, but not proven. Some of the proposed remedies change both effective noise and regularization simultaneously, complicating interpretation.
- Scope: Architectures are AlexNet/VGG-like and MLPs; inclusion of modern residual/skip architectures would broaden impact.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive)
- Provide full hyperparameter details per experiment (learning-rate schedules, weight decay, ADAM betas/epsilon, gradient clipping, number of epochs/updates) and describe the tuning protocol for SB vs LB to ensure fairness.
- Complement the sharpness metric with inexpensive spectral diagnostics: top-1 to top-10 Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos on mini-batch approximations; or curvature proxies such as Fisher diagonal or empirical NTK sharpness.
- Analyze parameterization invariance: discuss how batch-norm scale/shift and layer rescalings affect the metric; consider weight normalization or fixing BN statistics at evaluation; report sharpness under weight rescalings to assess robustness of conclusions.
- Report results with SGD + momentum (and tuned LR/decay) in both SB and LB, and include LR warmup/linear scaling for LB. Also try adding explicit noise to LB updates to test the “noise as exploration” hypothesis.
- Expand to ResNet-like architectures and larger-scale datasets; consider label smoothing and modern regularization to test whether sharpness differences persist.
- Where possible, equalize compute budgets (updates or epochs) and also compare at matched training loss to decouple optimization speed from generalization.
- Clarify the sensitivity of the sharpness metric to the box size, the A subspace dimension p, and the number of L-BFGS-B steps; provide ablations.

Questions for the authors
1) Hyperparameters: What learning-rate schedules, weight decay, and ADAM betas/epsilon were used for SB vs LB? Were these tuned separately per batch size? Did you try LR linear scaling with warmup or momentum SGD baselines?
2) Fairness/compute: Were SB and LB compared at matched number of epochs, updates, or matched training loss? How sensitive are results to training duration and early stopping?
3) Sharpness metric: How sensitive are φx,f(ε,A) to ε, p, and the number of inner maximization steps? Can you provide variance across random A and seeds? How do results change under simple layer rescalings (e.g., scaling weights prior to BN)?
4) Curvature corroboration: Have you estimated top Hessian eigenvalues via power iteration near SB vs LB minima to validate the metric?
5) Noise hypothesis: Does injecting isotropic gradient noise into LB (or using SGLD/cyclical LR) reduce sharpness and close the gap? Conversely, does reducing noise for SB (very large batch) increase sharpness?
6) Architectures: Do you observe the same phenomena in ResNets or other modern architectures and with label smoothing or cosine decays?

Overall, this is a valuable empirical study of a central question in deep learning optimization and generalization, and merits acceptance after clarifications and enhancements along the lines above.
