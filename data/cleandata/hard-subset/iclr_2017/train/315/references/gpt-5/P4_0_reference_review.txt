1. Summary of contributions
The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training of deep networks often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite reaching similar training loss. The central claim is that LB methods are attracted to sharp minima of the training objective, whereas SB methods tend to find flatter minima; sharpness is associated with poorer generalization. The paper supports this with:
- Visualization along paths between SB and LB solutions showing LB basins are sharper.
- A practical sharpness metric defined via local worst-case perturbations in full space and random low-dimensional subspaces, showing 1–2 orders-of-magnitude higher sharpness at LB solutions across multiple architectures/datasets.
- Empirical characterization of a batch-size threshold beyond which test accuracy drops and sharpness grows.
- Warm-start experiments: SB pre-training followed by LB converges to flatter minima with good generalization, suggesting dynamic sampling could work.
- Preliminary attempts at remedies (data augmentation, “conservative” proximal updates, adversarial/robust training) improve accuracy somewhat but do not eliminate sharpness.

The problem is important for scaling deep learning; the study is broad across several standard architectures/datasets and the narrative is clear and well-motivated.

2. Decision
Accept, primarily because:
- The paper addresses a practically significant and timely question and proposes a clear, testable hypothesis (sharp vs. flat minima) with consistent empirical support.
- The approach is generally well-motivated and placed in the literature; the experiments are diverse and the conclusions are actionable (e.g., warm-start/dynamic batching).

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:

1) What problem is tackled?
- Why LB training degrades generalization, and whether this correlates with the geometry of the found minima (sharp vs. flat). This is clearly stated and important.

2) Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature?
- The paper situates the work within SGD theory, parallelism, prior observations about generalization gaps, landscape visualizations, and notions of flatness (MDL/Bayesian perspectives). The motivation for the proposed sharpness metric (computational tractability vs. exact Hessian) and for warm-starting/dynamic sampling is reasonable.

3) Does the paper support the claims with rigorous results?
- The evidence is largely empirical and correlational but consistent across multiple models/datasets and seeded runs. The 1D/curvilinear visualizations and the random-subspace sharpness metric align with the hypothesis; warm-start experiments further support the mechanistic explanation. While not a formal proof, the results substantiate the main claims within an empirical study.

Caveats (do not undermine acceptance but temper claims):
- The sharpness metric is heuristic and depends on parameterization; there is no explicit treatment of reparameterization invariance (e.g., scaling symmetries in ReLU nets, batch norm), which can confound flatness/sharpness measured in weight space.
- Hyperparameter fairness is not fully specified: LR schedules, weight decay, and optimizer choices for LB vs. SB are critical. Using ADAM for both regimes and a fixed very large LB (10% of data) might bias outcomes; best practices for LB (e.g., linear LR scaling, warmup) are not systematically evaluated.
- The local maximization for sharpness uses only 10 L-BFGS-B iterations; while pragmatic, convergence quality and sensitivity to epsilon and the random subspace dimension are not analyzed.
- The claims are correlational; causal evidence (e.g., controlling geometry while keeping batch fixed) is limited.

Despite these limitations, the qualitative picture is coherent, reproducible in spirit, and practically useful.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not strictly part of the decision)
- Provide full training details for SB and LB: optimizer hyperparameters, learning-rate schedules (and whether LR scaling/warmup were tuned for LB), weight decay, number of updates vs. epochs, and batch norm statistics handling. Consider matching compute budgets or number of optimization steps across regimes.
- Analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to:
  - Reparameterizations (e.g., layer-wise rescaling, weight normalization), and report whether conclusions are stable under such transformations.
  - The choice of epsilon, subspace dimension p, and the stopping criterion of the inner maximization.
- Complement the heuristic metric with estimates of top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos at converged solutions to strengthen the link to curvature.
- Include results with SGD+momentum (and decoupled weight decay) for both SB and LB, as ADAM’s adaptive nature may interact with batch size and curvature differently.
- Expand the warm-start/dynamic-batch experiments with more seeds and schedules (e.g., gradual batch growth), and quantify variance.
- For the attempted remedies, provide fuller details (e.g., exact adversarial training settings: norm, epsilon, method) and more comprehensive comparisons.

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates and schedules selected for SB vs. LB? Did you try linear LR scaling and warmup for LB, and if so, how did this affect sharpness and test accuracy?
- Are results robust to replacing ADAM with SGD+momentum (with tuned weight decay) in both regimes?
- How do batch normalization statistics differ between SB and LB training (per-batch vs. moving averages), and could this contribute to the observed gaps?
- Can you report estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalues at converged SB and LB solutions (e.g., via power iteration)? Do these corroborate the sharpness metric?
- How sensitive are your sharpness measurements to layer-wise rescaling (e.g., scaling weights in one layer and inversely scaling the next) and to weight normalization? Does the SB/LB ordering persist?
- In the warm-start experiments, how many seeds were used and what is the variance? Does warm-starting consistently lead LB to flatter regions across architectures?
- For adversarial/robust training experiments, what adversary (FGSM/PGD), norm, and epsilon were used? Could stronger adversaries or TRADES-like formulations change the outcome?
- Did you control for the number of parameter updates vs. epochs when comparing SB and LB? If you equalize the number of updates, does the generalization gap persist?
