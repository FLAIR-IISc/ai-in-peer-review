1. Summary of claimed contributions
The paper empirically studies why large-batch (LB) training of deep networks often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite reaching similar training loss. Its main claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima—characterized by large positive curvature—while SB methods converge to flatter minima, and that this correlates with the observed generalization gap. The authors:
- Provide 1D and curvilinear parametric plots of the loss between SB and LB solutions, showing LB solutions lie in sharper basins.
- Propose a computationally feasible sharpness metric based on local worst-case loss increase under bounded perturbations in full space and in random low-dimensional subspaces, showing 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sharpness for LB solutions across several architectures and datasets.
- Show that SB iterates travel farther from initialization than LB iterates, and that warm-starting LB from intermediate SB iterates can recover good generalization once SB has reached a flat basin, suggesting a threshold phenomenon linked to gradient noise.
- Evaluate several mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial/stability training), finding partial improvements but persistent sharpness, and suggesting dynamic batch-size schedules as a promising direction.
The work is positioned against prior literature on SGD properties, loss landscapes, MDL/Bayesian intuitions for flatness, and practical observations about LB generalization.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper provides clear, consistent, and multi-faceted empirical evidence linking batch size, sharpness, and generalization across diverse architectures/datasets, with a novel, practical sharpness metric and insightful warm-start experiments.
- The problem is important for scalable training; the observations are actionable (e.g., motivate dynamic batching) and well-placed in the literature.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity:
- The paper is clearly written, defines notation, and describes experimental setups and architectures (with appendices for datasets and models). The visualization methodology and the proposed sharpness metric are explained sufficiently for reproduction, and the limitations (e.g., inexact inner maximization) are acknowledged.

Motivation and placement:
- The generalization gap with LB is a known practical bottleneck. Connecting it to sharpness provides an interpretable and testable hypothesis grounded in prior ideas (MDL, Bayesian priors, loss landscape properties). The work is timely and relevant to both practitioners and theorists.

Technical soundness and rigor:
- The claims are framed as empirical observations supported by multiple diagnostics: parametric plots (linear and curved paths), a sensitivity-based sharpness measure in both full and random subspaces, batch-size threshold studies, and warm-start experiments.
- The sharpness metric is computationally tractable and relates to top Hessian eigenvalues when the perturbation radius is small; using random subspaces mitigates artifacts from narrow directions. The use of multiple networks/datasets and five random seeds helps robustness.
- Limitations are present: the evidence is correlational; the sharpness metric may be sensitive to parameterization/scale; inner maximization is inexact; hyperparameter/budget matching across batch sizes is not exhaustively controlled. The authors partially address optimizer variation and mitigation strategies but do not fully close these gaps. Nonetheless, the results are consistent and strong enough to support the paper’s main empirical claims.

Novelty and impact:
- The systematic link between batch size and sharp vs. flat minima with a quantitative sharpness measure and warm-start analysis appears novel and has clear implications for scalable training (e.g., dynamic batch sizing, curricula, or noise injection strategies). The work is likely to influence practice and spur theoretical developments.

4. Additional feedback (for improvement; not all are decision-critical) and questions for authors
Constructive suggestions:
- Parameterization/scale invariance: The sharpness metric may depend on reparameterizations (e.g., scaling in layers, interactions with batch normalization). Consider reporting a normalized or functionally invariant flatness measure (e.g., layer-wise rescaling-invariant metrics, spectral normalization, or Sharpness-Aware Minimization–style normalized perturbations), and/or ablations showing robustness of your conclusions under simple reparameterizations.
- Hyperparameter fairness: Clarify and, if feasible, standardize training budgets (epochs, updates, wall-clock) and learning rate schedules across batch sizes. Large-batch training often requires linear LR scaling and warmup; include ablations showing whether such practices alter your conclusions.
- Optimizer coverage: While you note similar LB behavior with SGD/Adagrad/adaQN, please include representative results (accuracy and sharpness) in the appendix to strengthen generality.
- Sharpness metric details: Provide sensitivity analyses on the perturbation radius, the choice/dimension of random subspace p, and the number of L-BFGS-B iterations. Include convergence diagnostics for the inner maximization and show that trends are stable to these choices.
- Hessian evidence: Where computationally feasible, approximate the top Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., power iteration) at SB vs. LB minima to cross-validate the sharpness metric.
- Threshold characterization: Systematically sweep batch sizes to quantify the “threshold” across all models and relate it to gradient noise magnitude, curvature, and distance from initialization.
- Subspace sharpness: You note sharpness is concentrated in a small subspace. Quantify this by reporting explained variance of top Hessian eigenvalues or growth of the sharpness metric vs. subspace dimension.
- Reproducibility: Consider releasing full training code/configs (not only plotting scripts) to facilitate replication.

Questions for authors:
- How were learning rate schedules chosen for SB vs. LB? Did you use linear LR scaling with batch size and warmup for LB? If not, how sensitive are results to these standard LB practices?
- How do results change if training time (or number of parameter updates) is matched rather than “until loss ceases to improve”? Does extending LB training with tuned schedules close any of the gaps?
- Can you demonstrate that the sharpness differences persist under simple layer-wise rescalings (particularly in networks with batch normalization), or when using a scale-invariant sharpness metric?
- What are the top few Hessian eigenvalues at SB vs. LB solutions for a subset of models, and do they corroborate the proposed metric?
- In the warm-start experiments, how many SB epochs are necessary before LB consistently finds flat minima across seeds? Can you couple this with a dynamic batch-size schedule to achieve similar accuracy with fewer total steps?
- You note LB minima are “closer to initialization” in parameter space. Do you observe a consistent relation between distance traveled, sharpness, and test accuracy after controlling for parameterization (e.g., via path-norm or function-space metrics)?
- Among the mitigation methods, which yielded the largest accuracy recovery for LB? Can you provide a clearer ranking and discuss computational overheads?

Overall, the paper addresses a significant practical problem with careful empirical methodology and yields insights with actionable implications.
