Summary and contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training for deep neural networks. The central empirical observation is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima—characterized by high sensitivity of the training loss in a neighborhood—while SB methods tend to find flatter minima that generalize better. The authors (i) document the generalization gap across several architectures and datasets, (ii) visualize loss landscapes along linear and curvilinear paths between SB and LB solutions, (iii) propose a computable “sharpness” proxy based on local worst-case increase in the loss within a small box (optionally restricted to a random low-dimensional subspace), and (iv) present two key phenomena: a batch-size threshold beyond which test accuracy drops and a warm-start effect where SB pretraining allows LB methods to subsequently reach flatter minima and good generalization. They also report preliminary attempts at remedies (data augmentation, proximal/conservative training, adversarial/robust training), which help partially but do not eliminate sharpness.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The paper addresses a timely and practically important question—why LB training underperforms—and provides consistent, multi-faceted empirical evidence linking batch size, sharpness, and generalization, together with actionable insights (e.g., warm-start/dynamic batching).
2) The work is well situated in the literature on optimization and deep learning landscapes, and offers a simple, computationally feasible sharpness diagnostic that can be reproduced and used by practitioners.

Supporting arguments
- Importance and clarity: The LB generalization gap is a critical bottleneck for scalable training. The paper is clearly written, the hypothesis is well articulated, and the visualizations make the core claim intuitive.
- Breadth of evidence: Experiments span multiple architectures (fully connected and convolutional variants inspired by AlexNet/VGG) and datasets, using multiple random seeds, showing: (a) similar training loss but worse test accuracy for LB; (b) 1D and curved path plots where the LB solution sits in a much sharper basin; (c) a sharpness metric that differs by 1–2 orders of magnitude between SB and LB; (d) a batch-size threshold at which test accuracy degrades and sharpness rises; (e) warm-start experiments demonstrating that once SB has escaped sharp basins, LB can converge to flatter regions.
- Motivation and placement: The discussion relates to MDL/Bayesian intuitions (flat minima require fewer bits), to recent theory on nonconvex loss surfaces with many minima of similar training loss, and to the role of gradient noise in exploration, tying the phenomena to known properties of SGD.
- Practical implications: The warm-start/dynamic batching suggestion is valuable. The performance model highlights when LB could be competitive if generalization can be preserved.

Limitations and concerns (do not preclude acceptance but should be addressed)
- Causality vs correlation: The paper establishes a strong correlation between LB and sharp minima but does not prove causality. The interpretation that gradient noise aids escape from sharp basins is plausible but not directly measured (e.g., via gradient noise statistics).
- Sharpness metric: Although pragmatic, the metric depends on several design choices (ε, subspace dimension p, the definition of the box, the number of LBFGS-B steps) and may be sensitive to parameterization and scaling (especially with batch normalization and ReLU rescalings). A discussion and ablations on invariances and robustness of this metric would strengthen the claims.
- Hyperparameter fairness: Details on optimizer hyperparameters (learning-rate schedules, weight decay, momentum, LR scaling with batch size) are crucial for fair SB/LB comparisons. Using ADAM for AlexNet/VGG-like models is nonstandard; although the authors state similar findings with SGD/Adagrad/adaQN, quantitative results are not shown. The choice of LB as 10% of the dataset is very large; sensitivity curves across a wide range (with appropriate LR scaling rules) would be informative.
- Optimization budget: “Trained until the loss ceased to improve” may allocate different compute/updates to SB vs LB. Reporting matched budgets (e.g., same number of epochs, same number of parameter updates, or same wall-clock) would clarify whether the gap is due to optimization dynamics vs capacity.
- Curvature measurement: The link between the sharpness metric and Hessian spectrum is suggestive but indirect. Even approximate top eigenvalue/eigenvector computations (power/Lanczos) at SB/LB solutions on smaller models would make the curvature story more concrete.

Suggestions to improve the paper (to help, not all are decision-critical)
- Provide full training details (optimizer, LR schedules, LR/batch scaling, weight decay, BN settings, early stopping criteria) and ablate their effects on the generalization gap and sharpness.
- Report results with momentum SGD (the standard for these CNNs) and include quantitative comparisons beyond “similar conclusions.”
- Analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric: vary ε and p; test invariance under simple reparameterizations (e.g., rescaling layers/BN) and report correlations with Hessian top eigenvalues on small models.
- Quantify gradient noise (variance of stochastic gradients vs batch size) and relate it to the observed threshold and warm-start phenomena.
- Control for optimization budget: compare SB and LB under fixed number of parameter updates and under fixed number of epochs.
- Explore dynamic batch-size schedules more systematically (e.g., batch-size growth vs epoch) as a practical remedy inspired by your warm-start result.
- Where possible, release code for sharpness computation and plotting to aid reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
1) Sharpness metric:
- Please clarify Equation (4) precisely (there is a formatting ambiguity) and justify the dependence of the box C on A+ x. How sensitive are the results to ε, p, and the number of LBFGS-B iterations?
- How does the metric behave under layer rescalings (especially with BN)? Did you attempt any normalization to improve parameterization invariance?

2) Hyperparameters and fairness:
- What learning-rate and decay schedules were used for SB vs LB? Did you apply linear LR scaling or warmup for larger batches? How were weight decay and momentum tuned?
- For optimizers other than ADAM, can you provide quantitative results (test accuracy and sharpness) to substantiate “similar behavior”?

3) Optimization budget and noise:
- Are SB and LB compared under matched numbers of parameter updates/epochs? If not, how sensitive are your conclusions when budgets are matched?
- Did you measure gradient noise magnitude as a function of batch size and relate it to the observed threshold?

4) Curvature:
- Have you tried estimating top Hessian eigenvalues/vectors (power/Lanczos) at SB vs LB solutions on smaller networks to corroborate the sharpness proxy?

5) Remedies:
- The warm-start result is compelling. Can you provide a simple dynamic batch-size schedule that reliably reproduces the good generalization, and quantify its performance across architectures?

Overall, this is a timely, well-motivated empirical study that delivers clear insights with practical implications. Addressing the above points would substantially strengthen the paper.
