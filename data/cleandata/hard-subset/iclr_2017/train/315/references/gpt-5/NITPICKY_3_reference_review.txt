1) Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training loss. The central claim is that LB methods converge to sharp minima—regions with high curvature—while SB methods converge to flatter minima that generalize better. The authors support this with (i) 1D and curvilinear interpolations between SB/LB solutions illustrating sharper neighborhoods for LB solutions, (ii) a heuristic “sharpness” metric defined via constrained local maximization of the loss in a small neighborhood, showing 1–2 orders-of-magnitude larger values for LB solutions, and (iii) experiments indicating a batch-size threshold beyond which generalization degrades and sharpness increases. They also show that warm-starting LB from sufficiently progressed SB runs can recover good generalization, suggesting LB lacks the exploratory ability of SB. Finally, they report preliminary attempts (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial robustness) that reduce but do not eliminate the sharpness/generalization gap.

2) Decision
Reject. Key reasons:
- The experimental methodology has substantial confounders and underspecified hyperparameter control (optimizer choice, learning rate/schedule, weight decay, BatchNorm behavior, batch-size scaling), making the causal attribution to “sharpness” insufficiently supported.
- The sharpness metric is ambiguously defined, poorly justified, and optimized inexactly; the evidence for sharpness (and its link to generalization) is thus not rigorous enough.

3) Supporting arguments
- Confounded comparison and missing hyperparameter tuning:
  - SB uses batch size 256; LB is fixed at 10% of the dataset (e.g., 5,000 on CIFAR-10). No learning-rate scaling (e.g., linear scaling with warmup), optimizer retuning, or weight decay adjustments per batch size are described. ADAM’s effective step size and bias correction interact with batch size and must be tuned; otherwise, results may reflect suboptimal hyperparameters rather than inherent LB limitations.
  - “Trained until the loss ceased to improve” is vague. Stopping criteria, patience, and schedules (for LR decay, etc.) are not specified. LB can plateau earlier under poor schedules.
- Choice of optimizer and architectural confounds:
  - Most results rely on ADAM, which often generalizes differently from SGD and is sensitive to batch size and β parameters. Claims of similar behavior with other optimizers are anecdotal—details, settings, and results are not provided.
  - Batch Normalization statistics depend on batch size. Changes in BN noise/stats with LB are known to affect generalization and curvature; this is a major confound not disentangled (e.g., using Ghost BN, group norm, or frozen BN for evaluation).
- Sharpness metric and analysis concerns:
  - The definition (Eq. 4) is ambiguous: the denominator appears as “+ f(x) × 100” without clear parentheses; likely intended [(max−f(x))/(1+f(x))]×100. Please disambiguate.
  - The constraint set C uses A+ (pseudo-inverse) in bounds “(|(A+x)i|+1)”; dimensions are unclear and likely a typo—should it be |(A+ x)i|?
  - Using L-BFGS-B for only 10 iterations on a nonconvex maximization with random A yields an under-optimized and noisy proxy; no sensitivity/variance across A or with respect to ε is reported.
  - No direct curvature evidence: no top-eigenvalue/eigenvector estimation (e.g., Lanczos/Power method), no spectrum estimates, no per-parameter Hessian diagonal approximations. Yet the paper claims “significant number of large positive eigenvalues.”
  - The reported “only ~5% directions are steep” is asserted but not quantitatively substantiated (method and thresholds absent).
- Evidence scope and robustness:
  - Parametric plots sample only 1D curves between a single (or a few) SB/LB solution pairs. Robustness across runs, seeds, and different interpolants is not systematically quantified.
  - Limited ablations: No experiments with (i) LR scaling and warmup for LB, (ii) matching number of optimizer steps vs. epochs, (iii) weight decay sweeps, (iv) BN alternatives, (v) SGD with momentum as the primary optimizer.
  - Claims that early stopping does not help overfitting are supported by two example curves; broader coverage is needed.
- Presentation and reproducibility:
  - Many typos, inconsistent notation, and unclear equations (e.g., Eq. (3), (4)); repeated/ambiguous figure references (Figure 7 used for multiple appendices).
  - Architectural section B.4 appears to mislabel networks (C2 vs C3).
  - References contain numerous formatting errors (years wrong, placeholders like “? \Q2016\E,” etc.), missing venues/pages, and duplicated entries.
  - Code availability is only for parametric plots, not full training/metrics; difficult to reproduce.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (independent of the decision)
- Clearly specify all training details: optimizer (including β1, β2), learning-rate schedule, weight decay, momentum, batch norm and dropout configurations, number of steps/epochs, and stopping criteria. Report per-dataset absolute batch sizes.
- Control for BN by using alternatives (GroupNorm/LayerNorm) or Ghost BN; clarify train/eval modes when computing sharpness and parametric plots.
- Provide fair LB baselines with tuned hyperparameters, e.g., linear LR scaling with warmup, cosine decay, weight decay sweeps, label smoothing, data augmentation on baseline, and SGD with momentum. Compare equal-steps, equal-epochs, and equal-sample regimes.
- Strengthen curvature analysis: estimate top Hessian eigenvalues/eigenvectors (e.g., Lanczos), Hessian trace/trace approximations, per-layer spectra proxies, and visualize alignment of sharp directions with parameter subspaces.
- Formalize the sharpness metric: fix notation, remove ambiguities, justify normalization, report sensitivity to ε and A, and include confidence intervals over multiple random A realizations and seeds.
- Expand warm-start/dynamic batch-size experiments into a systematic schedule (e.g., gradually increasing batch size) and compare to baselines with identical total compute.
- Clean up writing, figures, and references; ensure consistent network naming and correct figure numbering.

Questions for the authors
- What exact learning-rate schedules, weight decay, and ADAM β parameters were used for SB vs LB? Were they tuned separately per batch size? Did you try linear LR scaling with warmup for LB?
- How did you handle BatchNorm during training and evaluation for sharpness computation and parametric plots? Did you test GroupNorm or Ghost BN to decouple BN effects?
- Can you report results with SGD + momentum as the primary optimizer (with tuned hyperparameters) across batch sizes?
- Please clarify Eq. (3)–(4): the role of A+, dimensional consistency, and the exact normalization. Can you provide variance across random A and sensitivity to ε?
- Can you provide estimates of top Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., power/Lanczos) to directly substantiate curvature claims?
- How many independent seeds/runs were used for each result in Tables 3–4, and what are the confidence intervals?
- Did you try label smoothing, weight decay sweeps, and LR warmup in the LB setting, and how did they affect sharpness and generalization?
