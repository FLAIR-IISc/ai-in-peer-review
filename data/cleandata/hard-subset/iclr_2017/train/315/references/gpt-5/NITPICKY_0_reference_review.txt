Summary and contributions
- Problem: The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training of deep networks often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite similar training loss. The central claim is that LB converges to “sharp” minima (high curvature), which generalize poorly, whereas SB finds “flat” minima.
- Contributions:
  - Empirical observation across several architectures and datasets that LB solutions have higher “sharpness” (via a proposed sensitivity metric) and worse test accuracy than SB.
  - 1-D and curvilinear parametric path visualizations between SB and LB solutions showing LB minima appear sharper.
  - A heuristic sharpness metric based on local worst-case perturbations (with optional projection to a random low-dimensional subspace).
  - Warm-start experiments suggesting SB noise helps escape sharp basins early; LB can succeed when initialized near a flat region.
  - Negative results for several mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/stability training).
Overall, the paper presents a useful, well-communicated empirical narrative about the LB generalization gap and its relation to sharpness.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Methodological and definitional issues in the central “sharpness” metric and experimental controls (batchnorm, optimizer, schedules, stopping) create confounds that undermine the main causal claim.
2) Numerous specification/formatting errors (including ambiguous/malformed equations central to the contribution) and missing experimental details prevent reproducibility and clear interpretation.

Supporting arguments
- Sharpness metric ill-specified:
  - Eq. (3) appears to omit the ε scaling entirely despite φx,f(ε, A) depending on ε; the text says ε “controls the size of the box,” but ε does not appear in C. This is critical.
  - Eq. (4) is ambiguous: φx,f(ε, A) := (maxy∈C f(x+Ay) − f(x)) + f(x) × 100. It likely intends a normalization by (1 + f(x)) times 100, but as written it is mathematically unclear. This directly affects the main quantitative comparisons.
  - Parameterization sensitivity: The metric is not invariant to common reparameterizations (e.g., scale symmetries in ReLU, BatchNorm). The claim of “invariance to problem dimension and sparsity” via the box definition does not address these reparameterization issues; thus comparing sharpness across runs may be misleading.
  - Inner maximization is solved by 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B, which is heuristic and likely finds a direction of steepest local increase rather than a robust worst case; no convergence or stability analysis is provided. Results could be highly sensitive to A, ε, and the number of inner steps. No ablations on p, ε, or repeats over A are reported.
- Experimental confounds and insufficient controls:
  - BatchNorm behavior strongly depends on batch size. Using LB dramatically changes the stochasticity in BN statistics, which itself is a known regularizer. No controls (e.g., ghost BN, frozen BN statistics, GroupNorm/LayerNorm) are reported. The observed gap could be a BN effect, not a general LB property.
  - ADAM is used in both regimes. ADAM’s effective step size and implicit regularization depend on gradient variance; simply changing batch size alters these substantially. There is no evidence of thorough per-regime hyperparameter tuning (learning rate, beta parameters, weight decay) or scaling rules for comparability.
  - Fairness of training budgets: “trained until the loss function ceased to improve,” but training time/updates differ drastically between SB and LB; no matched compute, matched epochs, or matched number of parameter updates are enforced. Warm-start experiments confound optimization phases and schedules; no matched learning-rate schedules are described.
  - Optimizer breadth: Results are mostly with ADAM; the claim “similar with SGD, Adagrad, adaQN” is asserted but not detailed (hyperparameters, schedules). SGD with momentum is a standard baseline; it should be a primary comparator with tuned schedules for both SB and LB.
- Evidence strength and scope:
  - Claims of “not overfitting” rely on two example curves; broader statistical evidence (confidence intervals over runs, early stopping on validation vs training loss monitoring) is lacking.
  - Path visualizations are suggestive but 1-D slices can be misleading in high dimensions; no Hessian spectrum approximations (e.g., top eigenvalues via power iteration/Lanczos) or Fisher-based curvature measures are provided to substantiate “many large positive eigenvalues.”
  - The “threshold batch size” phenomenon is interesting, but quantitative analysis is limited to two networks; no systematic study across architectures/datasets with controlled hyperparameters.
- Presentation and reproducibility issues:
  - Numerous citation and formatting errors (e.g., malformed years “? \Q1929\E”, inconsistent author-year references, typos “which and are often referred”), undefined or misplaced tables/figures in the main text (Tables 1–4, Figures 3–7), GitHub link relegation for a single plot, and unclear stopping criteria. These hinder clarity and reproducibility.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Fix the definitions of C and φ rigorously. Include ε explicitly in Eq. (3), clarify normalization in Eq. (4), and report sensitivity to ε, p, A, and number of inner iterations. Provide per-run seeds and distributions over sharpness estimates.
- Control for BatchNorm: evaluate with LayerNorm/GroupNorm or ghost BN; compare “frozen BN stats” during training; report whether the gap persists. This is essential.
- Use SGD with momentum as a primary optimizer; tune learning rates and weight decay per batch size using standard scaling rules; report learning-rate schedules and update counts. Provide matched-update and matched-compute comparisons.
- Add curvature estimates via top Hessian eigenvalues or Fisher eigenvalues (power iteration/Lanczos) at solutions; report train/test curvature.
- Ensure parameterization invariance where possible (e.g., weight normalization) or discuss its effect; compare functional flatness metrics (PAC-Bayes, noise stability).
- Expand the “threshold batch size” analysis across all networks and datasets; quantify test accuracy and sharpness vs batch size with statistical confidence.
- Clarify stopping: use a held-out validation set and consistent early stopping criteria.
- Release full code and configs for all experiments.

Questions for the authors
1) Please correct and precisely define Eqs. (3) and (4). How exactly does ε enter C? What is the denominator in φ? How sensitive are your conclusions to ε, p, the random A, and the number of inner L-BFGS-B steps?
2) How did you set and tune ADAM hyperparameters across SB and LB? Did you apply any learning-rate scaling with batch size? What weight decay was used?
3) How were BatchNorm statistics handled in LB? Did you try ghost BN or freezing stats? Do the results hold with LayerNorm/GroupNorm or without BN?
4) Are results robust under SGD with momentum with tuned schedules for each batch size? Please report those experiments in detail.
5) Are sharpness measurements performed on the test loss as well as the training loss? Do the qualitative conclusions hold on test curvature?
6) How many parameter updates and total compute were used in SB vs LB runs? Can you provide matched-update and matched-compute comparisons?
7) Can you provide Hessian top-eigenvalue estimates at the converged points to corroborate the sharpness metric?
