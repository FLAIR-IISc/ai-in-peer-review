1) Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite similar training loss. The central claim is that LB methods converge to sharp minima (characterized by many large positive Hessian eigenvalues), which generalize poorly, whereas SB methods converge to flatter minima.
- Evidence provided includes: (i) 1D and curvilinear loss-surface visualizations between SB and LB solutions; (ii) an empirical “sharpness” metric based on local maximization of the loss within a small box in parameter space, either in the full space or in a random low-dimensional subspace; (iii) experiments across multiple datasets and architectures (fully connected and CNNs resembling AlexNet/VGG); (iv) warm-start experiments suggesting that SB “exploration” helps LB find flatter minima; (v) preliminary attempts to mitigate the LB generalization gap via augmentation, “conservative” proximal steps, and adversarial training.
- The paper positions the observations within MDL/Bayesian intuitions and recent theory on nonconvex loss surfaces.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The empirical methodology does not control for critical confounders in comparing SB vs LB (learning rate, schedule, number of updates, normalization effects, weight decay, etc.), making the central causal claim (LB → sharp minima → worse generalization) insufficiently supported.
- The proposed sharpness metric is ad hoc, sensitive to parameterization and scaling (especially under BatchNorm and layer rescalings), and lacks validation and sensitivity analyses; many implementation choices (ε, subspace dimension p, inner optimizer) are only weakly justified.
- Numerous presentation/formatting inconsistencies and errors reduce confidence in the execution and reproducibility.

3) Supporting arguments
Experimental design and confounding:
- Batch size selection: LB is fixed at 10% of the dataset (e.g., 5k on CIFAR-10) while SB is 256. No control for the number of parameter updates vs epochs, nor for the linear-scaling/warmup rules known to be crucial for LB training. Using ADAM without re-tuning step sizes per batch size conflates batch size with effective learning rate and noise scale.
- Optimizer choice: Results are primarily with ADAM; although authors say other optimizers yielded “similar results,” details, tuned hyperparameters, and curves are missing. ADAM’s interaction with batch size and β parameters is nontrivial.
- Regularization and normalization: The setups employ BatchNorm and Dropout, but the paper does not specify weight decay, momentum, learning-rate decay schedules, or BN statistics handling under LB (which is known to matter). Data augmentation is termed “aggressive” but its impact on SB baselines is not fully quantified and ablated.
- Stopping criteria: “Trained until the loss ceased to improve,” but not standardized across regimes. This can disadvantage high-variance SB or LB differently.

Sharpness metric and interpretation:
- Definition: φx,f(ε, A) uses a bound box whose size depends on |(A+ x)i|+1. This couples the box size to parameter magnitudes, which are not invariant under common reparameterizations (e.g., with BN, rescaling one layer and counter-rescaling another leaves the function unchanged but alters parameter magnitudes). Claim of invariance to dimension/sparsity is unsubstantiated; at minimum, scale invariance is not demonstrated.
- Sensitivity analyses missing: No study of how φ varies with ε (only two small values), with p (subspace dimension 100 is arbitrary), or with different parameterizations. No comparison against Hessian-based proxies (e.g., power iteration for top eigenvalue) even on smaller models to validate the metric.
- Inner maximization: 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B are unlikely to approximate the max well; the stopping criterion is arbitrary and could systematically inflate differences. No assessment of robustness to the number of inner steps or initialization.

Evidence and claims:
- 1D/curvilinear visualizations are illustrative but not diagnostic of local curvature or generalization; slicing directions can be misleading, and the shown examples appear anecdotal.
- Warm-start experiment is intriguing but still confounded (e.g., SB changes effective learning rates and BN running stats before LB begins). The observed “threshold” batch sizes are model/dataset dependent and could reflect learning-rate mis-tuning rather than intrinsic sharpness.
- The assertion that LB converges to minima “closer to initialization” is based on Euclidean distances in parameter space; with BN and scale invariances, such distances can be meaningless without canonicalization.

Presentation issues that impair trust/reproducibility:
- Numerous typos and inconsistencies: e.g., “which and are often,” “Appedix,” “Figure 31,” mismatches in section B.4 (C2 vs C3), and citation artifacts (\Q2016\E; Srivastava et al., year shown as 1929). Table/figure cross-references are occasionally incorrect or missing.
- Lacking precise hyperparameters, schedules, seeds, and data preprocessing details for full reproduction beyond selected plots.

Positioning in literature:
- The discussion largely omits alternative explanations and subsequent advances on LB training stability and generalization (e.g., learning rate scaling and warmup, trust-ratio methods, layerwise adaptive rate scaling, noise scale theory, and results questioning the sharp/flat dichotomy’s sufficiency). While some are post-2016, the paper’s framing would benefit from engaging with these directions conceptually.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (constructive, not all part of decision)
- Control the confounds: for each batch size, tune learning rate, decay, and warmup; compare at fixed number of parameter updates and fixed compute; report full hyperparameters. Include SGD with momentum and weight decay as primary baselines in addition to ADAM.
- Validate the sharpness metric: (i) show correlation with top Hessian eigenvalue via power iteration on smaller models; (ii) perform sensitivity analyses over ε and p; (iii) demonstrate robustness across reparameterizations or normalize per-layer scales; (iv) ablate the dependence on A+ x in the box size.
- Expand evidence: include spectra estimates (e.g., Lanczos) on small networks; evaluate along random directions and principal curvature directions; quantify the “small subspace” where sharpness manifests.
- Compare to strong LB recipes: linear scaling rule, warmup, LARS/LAMB-like trust-ratio methods, label smoothing, stochastic weight averaging, and modern augmentation; show whether the gap persists.
- Standardize stopping criteria and report learning curves, not just end-point accuracies; include multiple seeds with statistical tests.
- Fix all formatting/citation errors and cross-references; ensure tables/figures are complete and consistent.

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates and schedules chosen for each batch size and optimizer? Did you use linear scaling and warmup? Please provide full hyperparameters per setting and per dataset.
- How sensitive are your sharpness conclusions to ε, to p, and to the choice of A? Can you show results for a range of ε and p, and with A = I vs random orthonormal A?
- Can you validate φ against approximate top Hessian eigenvalues (power iteration) on smaller models? Do the two measures correlate across runs and checkpoints?
- How do BN moving averages and per-batch statistics interact with your LB settings? Did you use ghost BN or other stabilization?
- In the warm-start experiments, if you resume with the same optimizer/hyperparameters except for batch size, do you observe similar improvements when controlling for LR and BN stats?
- The box C depends on A+ x. Why this choice, and how do you address sensitivity to reparameterizations that change parameter magnitudes without changing the function?
- Can you provide results with momentum SGD + weight decay (well tuned) as the main baseline, and include ImageNet-scale experiments or stronger CIFAR baselines with modern recipes?

Overall, the paper raises an important question and offers suggestive evidence, but the current experimental design and metric leave too many uncontrolled factors to support the strong causal claim.
