- Central claim: Large-batch (LB) training converges to sharper minima than small-batch (SB) training, and sharpness correlates with worse generalization, supported by interpolations, a sharpness metric, systematic experiments, warm-starts, and partial remedies.

- Decision: Accept — timely, practically important, and empirically convincing across multiple settings; warm-start experiment is especially informative.

- Importance/clarity: Clear narrative that LB reduces gradient noise → less exploration → higher chance of landing in sharp minima.

- Breadth: Experiments span multiple architectures (FC/CNN variants), datasets (MNIST/CIFAR/TIMIT), and optimizers (ADAM default; some evidence for others).

- Methodological variety: Uses landscape slices, a local worst-case “sharpness” metric (full and subspace), batch-size sweeps, and warm-start analyses to triangulate the phenomenon.

- Practical insight: Dynamic sampling/warm-start (start SB then switch to LB) is actionable and consistent with noise-as-regularizer intuition.

- Sharpness metric caveat: Metric depends on parameterization; rescalings (ReLU homogeneity, BN) can change Hessian magnitudes without changing function — test invariance under simple layer rescalings.

- Hessian corroboration: Recommend reporting top Hessian eigenvalues (power iteration/Lanczos) at SB and LB solutions to strengthen the sharpness claim.

- Metric sensitivity: Provide sensitivity analysis of the sharpness metric to epsilon choices, A construction (full vs random subspace), and the limited inner optimization budget (10 L-BFGS-B steps).

- Hyperparameter control: Ensure fair SB vs LB comparisons by controlling total parameter updates or wall-clock compute; “trained until loss ceased to improve” is ambiguous and potentially biased.

- LR/WD retuning: LB typically needs learning-rate scaling (e.g., linear scaling, warmup) and weight decay retuning — report thorough LB hyperparameter sweeps.

- Optimizer dependence: ADAM can generalize differently than SGD+momentum — include carefully tuned SGD+momentum LB results to rule out optimizer-specific effects.

- Batch-size realism: Using 10% of the dataset as LB is atypical; extend batch-size sweeps and emphasize moderate, practical LB sizes (e.g., 1k–8k for CIFAR/ImageNet-scale tasks).

- Overfitting vs implicit regularization: Disentangle effects by reporting final train accuracies/losses and ensuring matched update counts; clarify role of noise scale, update count, and weight decay coupling.

- Remedies need depth: Remedies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal steps, adversarial/stability training) are preliminary — provide more detailed ablations (e.g., proximal lambda, inner iterations) and specification of adversarial threat model/strength.

- Dynamic batch schedule: Given warm-start success, analyze explicit dynamic batch-size schedules (start small, ramp up) and explain when/why they work.

- Q: Sharpness invariance — how stable is the metric under reparameterizations like scaling consecutive layers with ReLU/BN?

- Q: Did you try linear LR scaling with batch size, LR warmup, and retuning weight decay for LB; what were effects on generalization and sharpness?

- Q: Can you report the top 1–3 Hessian eigenvalues at SB and LB solutions to corroborate sharpness?

- Q: In batch-size threshold plots, what was held constant (epochs, updates, total examples), and how sensitive are thresholds to those choices?

- Q: In warm-start experiments, does LB actually converge to the same basin as SB (e.g., via low-loss interpolation/mode connectivity)?

- Q: For adversarial/stability training, what inner attack strength and steps were used, and would stronger adversaries alter sharpness/generalization outcomes?
