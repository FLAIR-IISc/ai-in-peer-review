1) Summary of contributions
- Problem: The paper investigates why training with large mini-batches leads to worse generalization than small-batch training in deep networks, despite reaching similar training loss.
- Main claim: Large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to sharp minima that generalize poorly, whereas small-batch (SB) methods converge to flatter minima with better generalization.
- Evidence:
  - Parametric 1-D and curvilinear plots along paths between SB and LB solutions show much sharper loss landscapes near LB solutions on both train and test metrics.
  - A computationally tractable sharpness metric (maximization of loss within a small neighborhood, full space and low-dimensional random subspaces) shows 1–2 orders-of-magnitude higher sensitivity at LB solutions across six architectures/datasets.
  - Warm-start experiments: starting LB optimization from intermediate SB iterates leads to improved test accuracy and reduced sharpness once SB has “found” flatter regions, suggesting the role of gradient noise in escaping sharp basins.
  - Batch-size threshold phenomena: beyond a certain batch size, test accuracy drops and sharpness rises.
  - Attempts to remedy (data augmentation, conservative proximal steps, adversarial/stability training) provide partial improvements but do not eliminate sharpness or the generalization gap.
- Context: The work connects to views from MDL/Bayesian perspective (flat minima need fewer bits; generalize better) and aligns with recent theory that many local minima have similar loss but potentially different generalization. It also discusses implications for the scalability of DL training.

2) Decision
Weak accept. Key reasons: (1) The paper tackles a practically important and timely problem with clear implications for scalable training. (2) It offers novel and reasonably convincing empirical evidence linking batch size, sharpness, and generalization, and provides actionable insights (e.g., warm-start/dynamic sampling).

3) Supporting arguments
- Importance and novelty: Large-batch parallelism is central to scaling deep learning; diagnosing its generalization gap is impactful. The focus on sharp vs flat minima as the operative mechanism is a compelling and broadly relevant hypothesis.
- Breadth of evidence: Results span multiple network families (fully connected, AlexNet/VGG-like convnets) and datasets (vision, speech), reducing the risk of overfitting conclusions to a niche setup. The use of both line and curved path visualizations strengthens the sharpness observation qualitatively.
- Quantitative metric: While heuristic, the sharpness measure is computationally feasible at scale and consistently differentiates SB/LB across settings (full parameter space and random subspaces). The observation that sharpness concentrates in a low-dimensional subspace is interesting and actionable.
- Mechanistic insight: The warm-start experiment convincingly supports the “SGD noise helps escape sharp basins” narrative, and suggests dynamic batch-size schedules as a practical remedy.
- Negative results: Reporting partial success/failures with common remedies is valuable and honest, and it informs future work.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Hyperparameter fairness and baselines: Please clarify per-batch-size tuning. Common practice scales the learning rate with batch size and uses warmup; these choices can substantially affect LB generalization. Including momentum SGD with standard LR decay, weight decay, label smoothing, and modern data augmentation would strengthen the baseline suite. Reporting results with linear scaling + warmup would be useful.
- Batch Normalization confounders: Many architectures use BN. BN statistics depend on batch size and affect both optimization noise and implicit regularization. Please detail how BN was handled (synchronized across devices or per-replica), and, if possible, provide results with alternatives (e.g., GroupNorm or removing BN) to decouple BN effects from batch-size effects.
- Compute/epoch budget: Ensure controlled comparisons by equalizing either total number of optimization steps, number of epochs, or total compute. The paper says training continues until loss ceases to improve; please report the number of steps/epochs and wall-time for SB and LB to reach comparable training loss and test accuracy.
- Sharpness metric analysis: The metric may be sensitive to parameterization and scaling symmetries of ReLU nets. Discuss this limitation explicitly and consider complementary measures: top Hessian eigenvalues via Lanczos/power iteration, trace of Fisher information, or normalized measures invariant to weight rescalings. Sensitivity analyses over epsilon and subspace dimension p would increase confidence.
- Statistical rigor: Expand the number of seeds beyond five where feasible and report confidence intervals. Show correlations between sharpness and test error across runs, not only class averages.
- Larger-scale benchmarks: Results on larger datasets (e.g., ImageNet) would enhance external validity given the paper’s motivation in large-scale training.
- Link to theory: Briefly discuss reparameterization issues and how they might affect the flatness–generalization relationship; consider situating your metric relative to PAC-Bayes flatness or margin-based viewpoints.

5) Questions to authors
- Hyperparameters: How were learning rate, momentum/Adam betas, weight decay, and LR schedules chosen for each batch size? Did you try linear LR scaling with batch size and warmup?
- BN details: Are BN statistics computed on the same batch used for gradient computation? Were they synchronized across devices? How do results change with GroupNorm/LayerNorm or without BN?
- Compute control: Did SB and LB use the same number of optimization steps or epochs before stopping? Please report steps/epochs to reach a fixed training loss and the corresponding test accuracy.
- Optimizer dependence: Do the core findings hold for SGD with momentum and cosine/step decay schedules, not just Adam? Any qualitative differences?
- Sharpness metric: How sensitive are conclusions to epsilon and the choice of p in the subspace? Can you provide estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalues at SB vs LB minima via power/Lanczos to corroborate the heuristic metric?
- Dynamic batching: Your warm-start suggests batch growth over time could work. Did you try explicit dynamic batch-size schedules, and does this close the generalization gap without SB pretraining?
- Proximity to initialization: You note LB solutions are closer to initialization. Is this explained by fewer effective noisy steps or inherent basin attraction? Can you report path length or distance traveled for controlled step counts?

Overall, this is a timely, carefully executed empirical study with meaningful insights and practical implications, albeit with some confounders (hyperparameters, BN, parameterization) that should be addressed or discussed more thoroughly.
