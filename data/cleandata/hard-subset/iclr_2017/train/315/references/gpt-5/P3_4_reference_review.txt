1) Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why very large mini-batches often harm generalization in deep learning, despite achieving similar training loss as small-batch training. The central claim is empirical: large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to sharp minima, whereas small-batch (SB) methods find flatter minima; sharpness correlates with poorer test performance. The authors support this with:
- 1D and curvilinear loss landscape visualizations interpolating between LB and SB solutions.
- A practical “sharpness” metric based on local worst-case increase in the loss under small parameter perturbations, computed either in full parameter space or in random subspaces.
- Systematic experiments across several architectures and datasets, showing consistent generalization gaps and sharper LB minima.
- A warm-start experiment showing that running SB for some epochs lets subsequent LB training land in flatter basins.
- Initial attempts at remedies (data augmentation, “conservative” proximal steps, adversarial/stability training) that partially reduce the gap but still yield sharp LB solutions.

Overall, the paper tackles a highly relevant scalability question and provides compelling, if largely empirical, evidence connecting batch size, sharpness, and generalization.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper addresses a timely, practically important question with clear, broadly applicable insights.
- The empirical evidence is convincing across multiple architectures and datasets, and the warm-start experiment is particularly informative about the role of stochasticity as implicit exploration/regularization.

3) Supporting arguments
- Importance and clarity: The work directly targets the current bottleneck in distributed DL—scaling via large batches—and explains a widely observed generalization gap. The narrative is crisp: LB reduces gradient noise, which reduces exploration, which increases propensity to converge to sharp minima.
- Breadth: Experiments span multiple architectures (fully-connected and convolutional variants akin to AlexNet/VGG), datasets (MNIST/CIFAR/TIMIT), and optimizers (ADAM as default, with brief evidence that trends persist for others).
- Methodological variety: The paper combines multiple diagnostic tools—landscape slices, a sharpness metric (both full and subspace), batch-size sweep with observed thresholds, and warm-start analysis—to triangulate the phenomenon.
- Practical insight: The dynamic sampling/warm-start result is actionable and consistent with the intuition that noise helps avoid or escape sharp basins early, after which LB can be effective.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Sharpness metric and invariances:
  - The proposed metric is pragmatic but depends on the parameterization of the network. Rescalings (e.g., due to ReLU homogeneity, batch norm) can change Hessian magnitudes without changing function behavior. Although you attempt some normalization in the box definition, please test and document invariance under simple layer rescalings (e.g., scale a layer by c and inversely scale the next).
  - Consider reporting top Hessian eigenvalues via power iteration/Lanczos at the SB and LB solutions to corroborate the sharpness claim with a more standard proxy. Even a few top eigenvalues would strengthen the case.
  - Provide a sensitivity analysis of the metric to the epsilon choices and the A construction (full, random subspaces) and to the limited inner optimization budget (10 L-BFGS-B steps).

- Hyperparameter control and training budgets:
  - Ensure fair comparisons across SB/LB by controlling either the total number of parameter updates or the total wall-clock compute. The statement “trained until the loss ceased to improve” is ambiguous and may bias LB vs SB differently.
  - Large-batch training typically requires retuning learning-rate schedules (e.g., linear scaling with batch size, warmup) and weight decay. Please report thorough sweeps for LB, including learning-rate schedules tailored to the larger batch size. Clarify whether LR scaling/warmup were attempted.
  - Because ADAM can have different generalization behavior than SGD with momentum, include a more complete set of LB results with SGD+momentum, carefully tuned for LB (LR, momentum, weight decay), to rule out optimizer-specific artifacts.

- Scope and realism of batch sizes:
  - Using 10% of the dataset as the LB is very large and can be atypical. The batch-size sweep showing a “threshold” is useful but only shown for a subset of models. Please extend the sweep, and emphasize results at more moderate but practically relevant LB sizes (e.g., 1k–8k on CIFAR/ImageNet-scale tasks), to better position the findings for real-world settings.

- Overfitting vs implicit regularization:
  - While you argue the gap is not classical overfitting, it remains important to disentangle implicit regularization effects (noise scale, update count, weight decay coupling with batch size). Reporting final train accuracies/losses alongside test metrics and ensuring matched update counts would help.

- Remedies:
  - The remedy experiments are appreciated but somewhat cursory. For adversarial/stability training, specify the threat model/inner-loop strength. For “conservative” training, provide ablations on the proximal parameter lambda, inner iterations, and effect on sharpness beyond test accuracy.
  - Given the warm-start success, an explicit dynamic batch-size schedule (start small, ramp up) with analysis of when/why it works would be valuable.

Questions for the authors
- How invariant is your sharpness metric to simple reparameterizations (e.g., scaling of weights in consecutive layers with ReLU/BN)? Can you provide experiments demonstrating stability of the metric under such transforms?
- Did you try linear learning-rate scaling with batch size and warmup for LB, and retuning weight decay specifically for LB? If so, what were the outcomes on both generalization and measured sharpness?
- Can you report the top 1–3 Hessian eigenvalues (via power iteration) at SB and LB solutions to corroborate the sharpness claim?
- In the batch-size threshold plots, what is held constant (epochs, updates, total examples seen), and how sensitive are the thresholds to these choices?
- In the warm-start experiments, is the LB method converging to the same basin as the SB solution (e.g., via low-loss interpolation without barriers)? Any evidence from mode connectivity analysis?
- For adversarial/stability training, what inner attack strength and steps were used? Could stronger adversaries change the sharpness and generalization outcomes?

This paper delivers a clear, impactful empirical story on a central question in scalable deep learning. Addressing the above points would further solidify its conclusions and practical guidance.
