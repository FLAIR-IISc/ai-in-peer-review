- Paper studies why very large minibatches degrade test accuracy despite similar training loss, hypothesizing LB → sharp minima and SB → flat minima that generalize better.  
- Evidence includes 1D/curvilinear loss plots, a computational “sharpness” proxy (local worst-case loss increase in a small box), empirical trends across architectures/datasets, and warm-start experiments.  
- Reports that common remedies (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial training) improve accuracy somewhat but LB solutions remain relatively sharp; proposes dynamic batch sizing.  
- Decision: Weak accept — important problem, coherent/testable explanation, and compelling empirical evidence likely to influence the community.  
- Strength: Clear problem framing and practical relevance for scalable training.  
- Strength: Breadth and consistency across multiple architectures, datasets, and preliminary optimizer checks.  
- Strength: Multiple complementary diagnostics (parametric slices, sharpness proxy in full/random subspaces, warm-start experiments).  
- Strength: Qualitative narrative linking gradient noise to exploration aligns with theoretical perspectives (MDL, Bayesian, landscape results).  
- Concern: Sharpness metric is sensitive to parameterization and scale (batch norm, layer rescaling), raising questions about intrinsic vs. artifact differences.  
- Concern: The maximization used to compute sharpness (10 L-BFGS-B steps) may be an unreliable approximation; sensitivity to box size ε and tolerances not characterized.  
- Concern: Training protocol confounds—LB often needs different LR schedules, warmup, and regularization; unclear whether hyperparameters were fairly tuned across batch sizes.  
- Concern: Correlation vs. causation—paper shows correlations between LB, sharpness, and generalization but lacks interventions establishing causality.  
- Concern: 1D loss slices are limited and potentially misleading; stronger Hessian-based probes (top eigenvalues, trace) are missing.  
- Concern: Compute normalization and thresholds—using LB as 10% of data is extreme; need controls for total updates and examples processed and systematic noise-scale mapping.  
- Suggestion: Test reparameterization invariance (layer rescaling, batch norm) and consider normalized/Fisher-weighted or PAC-Bayes-inspired flatness measures.  
- Suggestion: Estimate top Hessian eigenvalues (power/Lanczos) and trace (Hutchinson) at SB and LB solutions and correlate with test accuracy.  
- Suggestion: Control training protocols—tune LB with linear LR scaling and warmup, report SGD+momentum and ADAM results, and supply full schedules/hyperparameters.  
- Suggestion: Control for total updates vs. total epochs and show sensitivity plots across these axes.  
- Suggestion: Study effects of label smoothing, mixup/cutmix, and weight decay on sharpness and generalization for LB.  
- Suggestion: Perform sensitivity analyses varying ε, number of L-BFGS-B steps, and subspace dimension p and report stability.  
- Suggestion: Provide a single-run dynamic batch-size schedule (noise annealing) and compare it to the warm-start two-phase procedure.  
- Question: How were learning-rate schedules chosen per batch size, and was linear scaling with warmup tried? How sensitive are conclusions to schedules?  
- Question: Can you show the sharpness metric is not driven by trivial parameter rescalings (e.g., with/without batch norm) or provide a reparameterization-invariant proxy?  
- Question: Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues and/or trace via Lanczos/Hutchinson near SB and LB solutions?  
- Question: If you equalize number of parameter updates or total examples seen, how do generalization gaps change?  
- Question: Beyond reported augmentations, were label smoothing, mixup/cutmix, or stronger weight decay tested for LB, and do they reduce sharpness and close the gap?  
- Question: In the warm-start experiments, at what training stage (loss/sharpness) does LB begin to generalize well, and can a continuous dynamic-batch schedule replicate this behavior?  
- Question: How do results vary with intermediate batch sizes between 256 and 10% of the dataset, and can you relate the transition to an estimate of the gradient noise scale?
