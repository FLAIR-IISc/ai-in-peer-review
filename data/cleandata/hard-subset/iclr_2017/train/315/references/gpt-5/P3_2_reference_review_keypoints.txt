- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), attributing it to LB converging to "sharp" minima vs SB finding "flat" minima.
- Evidence includes 1-D/curvilinear interpolation plots, an empirical sharpness metric (neighborhood loss maximization), multi-architecture experiments, warm-starting experiments, and attempts at mitigation (augmentation, conservative/adversarial training).
- Paper argues SB gradient noise aids exploration and escaping sharp basins, suggesting dynamic sampling as a remedy.
- Decision: Reject.
- Central critique: sharpness–generalization claim relies on a metric and protocol not invariant to common reparameterizations, undermining the causal claim.
- Major confound: optimizer, learning-rate and batch-size coupling, and batch norm are not controlled, so batch-size effects may be conflated with optimization “temperature.”
- Baselines insufficient: no strong LB baselines (e.g., SGD+momentum with LR scaling + warmup, tuned weight decay, ghost BN, LARS/LAMB) to show LB cannot match SB.
- Batch-size choices and budgets problematic: using 10% of dataset as LB is atypical and may make optimization overly deterministic; compute/epoch matching unclear.
- Sharpness metric concerns: ad hoc, weakly normalized, and sensitive to layerwise rescalings (Dinh et al. issues); batch norm complicates scaling arguments.
- Missing reparameterization-invariant diagnostics: no Hessian/Fisher spectral estimates or layerwise-normalized curvature are reported.
- Optimization temperature not equalized: paper varies batch size but keeps optimizer/LR schedules fixed, conflating reduced noise with batch-size effects.
- Optimizer choice bias: heavy reliance on Adam, which behaves differently across batch sizes and is not the standard LB baseline.
- Evidence quality: 1-D plots are limited; sharpness maximization uses few L-BFGS-B steps and arbitrary settings; results may be underexplored and sensitive to choices.
- Lack of direct Hessian spectral estimates (power/Lanczos, top eigenvalues, trace approximations) despite feasibility.
- Causal overreach: paper extrapolates from correlations to LB → sharp minima → poor generalization without holding noise scale or reparameterization fixed.
- Suggested metric improvements: report top-k Hessian eigenvalues, Fisher/Gauss–Newton spectra, or layerwise normalized curvature to mitigate reparameterization issues.
- Suggested LB baselines: SGD+momentum with LR scaling and warmup, tuned weight decay, ghost BN, LARS/LAMB, and LR/momentum/weight-decay sensitivity curves per batch size.
- Suggested controls: equalize noise scale across batch sizes, compare constant examples vs constant updates, and sweep batch sizes under tuned hyperparameters.
- Reparameterization sensitivity tests recommended: apply layerwise scalings that preserve function to test sharpness stability.
- Additional diagnostics requested: distance-to-init, weight norms, margin distributions, calibration, and top Hessian eigenvalues during training.
- Reproducibility concerns: clarify LR schedules, weight decay, momentum, batch norm settings, augmentations, early stopping, seeds, and provide code.
- Key questions for authors include LR scaling/warmup usage, sensitivity to layerwise rescalings, reporting Hessian spectra, equalizing noise scale effects, rationale for 10% LB choice, SGD+momentum comparisons, warm-start confounds, and sensitivity/ablations for the sharpness metric.
