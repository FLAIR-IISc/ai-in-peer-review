1) Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training tends to generalize worse than small-batch (SB) training. Across six architectures and several datasets, the authors present empirical evidence that LB methods often converge to “sharp” minima, while SB methods find “flatter” minima. The paper supports this claim with: (i) 1-D and curvilinear loss landscape visualizations between SB and LB solutions, (ii) a practical sharpness metric based on local worst-case loss increase within a small box (full space and random low-dimensional subspaces), (iii) batch-size sweeps showing a “threshold” beyond which generalization degrades and sharpness rises, and (iv) warm-start experiments indicating that SB noise can help escape sharp basins and that LB can then refine towards flatter solutions. The paper also documents several attempted remedies (data augmentation, proximal “conservative” steps, adversarial/stability training), finding partial improvements but persistence of sharp minima. The problem is important, the experiments are extensive and well presented, and the practical implications (e.g., dynamic batch sizing) are valuable.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The central evidence relies on an ad-hoc sharpness metric and 1-D/curvilinear visualizations that are not invariant to common reparameterizations (e.g., layer scaling with ReLU/BatchNorm), leaving ambiguity about whether “sharpness” as measured is causally tied to generalization.
- Important confounders in comparing SB vs LB are insufficiently controlled (optimizer choice, learning-rate scaling, effective number of updates, weight decay and regularization), weakening the causal interpretation that batch size per se leads to sharp minima and worse generalization.

3) Supporting arguments
- On the question/problem: The paper tackles a timely and specific question—explaining the LB generalization gap—and frames it clearly. The motivation (parallelism and time-to-accuracy) and the potential impact are strong.
- On placement in the literature: The paper cites relevant optimization and deep learning works and articulates hypotheses (flat vs sharp minima) with links to MDL/Bayesian interpretations. However, it does not adequately engage with reparameterization invariance concerns or alternative explanations (e.g., number of parameter updates, learning-rate–batch-size scaling), which have been discussed in subsequent and contemporary work.
- On empirical support: The experiments consistently show a generalization gap and larger “sharpness” for LB solutions under the chosen settings. The warm-start and batch-size-threshold studies are insightful. Nonetheless:
  - The sharpness metric depends on an arbitrarily chosen box and uses a scaling involving A+ and |(A+ x)i| + 1; it is not demonstrated to be invariant to layer-wise rescaling or affine reparameterizations (a serious issue with ReLU/BatchNorm networks). As a result, “flat vs sharp” may partly reflect parameterization, not inherent geometry relevant to function-space generalization.
  - The study uses Adam for both SB and LB. Adam’s generalization behavior differs from SGD with momentum, and standard LB best practices (linear learning-rate scaling, warmup, longer training or matching the number of parameter updates, appropriate weight decay) are not systematically explored. Without such controls, it is difficult to ascribe the gap to batch size rather than optimization hyperparameters or total update budget.
  - Visualizations along 1-D/curvilinear paths are illustrative but can be misleading in high dimensions. More direct curvature estimates (e.g., top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos, Hessian trace via Hutchinson) would strengthen the claims.
  - The paper claims the sharpness is concentrated in a small subspace; quantifying this (e.g., spectrum decay, intrinsic dimensionality) would be valuable but is only qualitatively discussed.
- Attempts to remedy: The negative results on adversarial/stability training and the partial success of data augmentation and conservative steps are useful, but the breadth of regularization/optimization knobs is limited. The warm-start result is compelling and supports dynamic batch-size strategies, but this is not explored systematically.

4) Additional feedback to help improve (not necessarily part of the decision)
- Invariance and metric design:
  - Provide a careful analysis of the sharpness metric’s invariances. Demonstrate empirically that conclusions hold under layer-wise rescaling, weight normalization, or BatchNorm parameter changes. Consider function-space or scale-invariant flatness measures (e.g., normalized sharpness, PAC-Bayes-inspired flatness, Fisher-Rao norms).
  - Complement the box-perturbation metric with Hessian-based estimates: top eigenvalues (power/Lanczos), spectral density (Hutch++), and layer-wise curvature to quantify “sharp subspace” dimensionality.
- Control confounders:
  - Compare SB vs LB using SGD with momentum and weight decay alongside Adam. Systematically sweep learning-rate schedules with linear scaling and warmup, and match either wall-clock compute, epochs, or the number of parameter updates to isolate batch-size effects.
  - Report sensitivity to label smoothing, mixup/cutmix, dropout rates, data augmentation strength, and training duration (train-longer hypotheses).
- Broaden empirical scope:
  - Include modern benchmarks/architectures (e.g., ResNets, Wide-ResNets on CIFAR-10/100; ImageNet if feasible) to increase external validity.
  - Report more seeds and confidence intervals; release code to reproduce all experiments (only partial code is linked).
- Analysis of noise and escape:
  - Quantify gradient noise scale vs batch size and relate it to escape from sharp basins; test explicit noise injection in LB updates to mimic SB behavior.
  - Explore dynamic batch-size schedules and weight averaging (e.g., SWA) as principled ways to find flatter solutions in LB regimes.
- Clarifications:
  - The definition of the constraint set C and the role of A+ in Equation (3) are unusual; justify the form and show ablations on epsilon, box shape, and p (subspace dimension).

Questions for the authors
1) How invariant is your sharpness metric to layer-wise rescalings and BatchNorm parameterization? Can you provide experiments demonstrating that the SB–LB sharpness gap persists under reparameterizations that leave the function unchanged?
2) Did you try SGD with momentum, linear learning-rate scaling with batch size, warmup, and matching the number of parameter updates between SB and LB? How do the sharpness metrics and test accuracies change under these controls?
3) Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues or a spectral approximation at SB and LB solutions to corroborate the perturbation-based sharpness metric?
4) In the warm-start experiments, what are the exact learning-rate schedules and stopping criteria for the LB phase? Does dynamic batch-size scheduling without explicit warm-start (e.g., gradually increasing batch size) reproduce the effect?
5) Your results suggest sharpness is concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace. Can you quantify this (e.g., how many top eigen-directions account for most curvature), and does SB vs LB differ in that intrinsic dimensionality?
6) How sensitive are your findings to the choice of Adam vs SGD, weight decay, label smoothing, and aggressive data augmentation? Could the observed gap be reduced by longer training or weight averaging for LB?

Overall, this is a timely and thoughtful empirical study. Addressing the metric invariance and experimental confounders would substantially strengthen the causal claims and impact.
