1) Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training tends to generalize worse than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training losses. The central claim is that LB methods converge to sharp minima—characterized by large positive curvature—while SB methods, aided by gradient noise, find flatter minima that generalize better. The authors:
- Provide multiple empirical signatures: 1-D and curvilinear interpolations between SB/LB solutions, a warm-start study showing LB can succeed once SB has navigated to flatter regions, and batch-size “threshold” phenomena.
- Propose a practical sharpness metric via a bounded worst-case perturbation (solved approximately by L-BFGS-B) both in the full space and low-dimensional random subspaces.
- Demonstrate the correlation between sharpness and generalization gap across several architectures and datasets, and show that common remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/stability training) help but do not eliminate sharp minima for LB.
- Suggest dynamic sampling as a promising direction, supported by the warm-starting results.

Overall, the paper offers a clear and compelling conceptual framing—sharp vs. flat minima as an explanation for generalization gaps with LB—and a simple, reproducible diagnostic.

2) Decision: Accept
Rationale:
- Novelty/impact: The paper advances a bold, influential hypothesis linking batch size to solution geometry and generalization, backed by diverse empirical evidence and a usable sharpness metric. This is a new direction rather than an incremental tweak.
- Breadth and clarity: Results span multiple architectures/datasets and triangulate the phenomenon via several complementary analyses.

3) Supporting arguments
- Well-motivated problem: Scaling DL requires larger batches; practitioners observe generalization gaps. The paper squarely addresses this practical/important question.
- Evidence: The 1-D and curvilinear plots cleanly visualize sharpness differences; the sharpness metric, though heuristic, consistently shows 1–2 orders-of-magnitude gaps between LB and SB solutions. Warm-start experiments substantiate the role of gradient noise in escaping sharp basins and connect to dynamic batch strategies.
- Literature placement: The paper situates its findings within MDL/Bayesian intuitions and relates to recent work on loss surface geometry and saddle-point avoidance.
- Robustness: The phenomenon appears across networks and optimizers, and persists under several attempted remedies. The batch-size threshold plots further support the “noise as exploration” narrative.

4) Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions; not all are decision-critical)
- Parameterization sensitivity of sharpness: The proposed metric depends on the coordinate system and scaling (e.g., rescaling layers/weights can change curvature). Please discuss invariances and consider normalized definitions (per-parameter or per-layer scaling, filter-wise normalization, or path-norm/BN-invariant measures). Even a brief ablation (e.g., weight rescaling) would strengthen claims.
- Tuning and optimizer confounds: Much of the study uses Adam for both SB and LB. Provide a careful SGD study with batch-size-aware learning-rate/momentum scaling and weight decay, and report whether the gap persists. Clarify learning-rate schedules and total optimization budgets to ensure fairness across batch sizes.
- Larger-scale benchmarks: Including a larger-scale dataset (e.g., ImageNet-like) would increase external validity. If not feasible, at least provide more details on data sizes and effective epoch counts for LB vs. SB.
- Sharpness computation details: The metric uses 10 L-BFGS-B steps and two epsilons. Please analyze sensitivity to epsilon, number of inner iterations, and the choice/dimension of random subspace A. Confidence intervals for the sharpness estimates would help.
- Additional diagnostics: Consider estimating top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos or Hutchinson-based spectral summaries to corroborate the metric. Reporting gradient noise scale vs. batch size and the distance from initialization (already hinted) with statistics across runs would round out the story.
- Batch normalization effects: BN couples the loss to batch statistics. Discuss whether BN exacerbates LB sharpness (e.g., due to reduced stochasticity) and provide ablations with/without BN or using ghost-batch normalization.
- Dynamic sampling: Since warm-starting works, include a simple progressive batching schedule and show whether it matches SB generalization while attaining LB efficiency.

Questions for the authors
- How sensitive are your conclusions to optimizer and hyperparameters? Specifically, with SGD + momentum and batch-size-aware learning-rate scaling, does LB still converge to sharper minima?
- How does the sharpness metric behave under simple reparameterizations (e.g., layer-wise scaling, weight normalization, BN rescaling)? Can you propose or test a scale-invariant variant?
- What is the relationship between your sharpness metric and actual Hessian spectra at solutions? Have you measured top eigenvalues or trace approximations to validate the metric?
- Can you quantify the gradient noise scale across batch sizes and relate the observed threshold to the noise needed to exit sharp basins?
- In the warm-start experiments, how many epochs (or what loss/curvature regime) are sufficient before LB consistently finds the flat basin? Can you turn this into a practical dynamic batch-size policy?
- Do your conclusions hold on larger datasets or tasks (e.g., ImageNet-scale vision, larger speech corpora), where LB is most attractive for throughput?

This paper makes a strong, novel conceptual contribution with convincing multi-angle evidence and opens an impactful research direction. Despite some methodological limitations and open questions about metric invariance and tuning, I believe it merits acceptance.
