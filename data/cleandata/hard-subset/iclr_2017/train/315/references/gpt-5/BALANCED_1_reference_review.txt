1) Summary of contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training loss. Across several architectures and datasets, the authors report a consistent “generalization gap” for LB. Their main empirical observation is that LB converges to “sharp” minima (high local sensitivity), whereas SB tends to find “flatter” minima. They support this with:
- 1-D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions showing sharper basins near LB solutions.
- A computationally feasible sharpness metric based on local maximization in a box (in full parameter space and random subspaces).
- Warm-start experiments indicating that initializing LB from later SB iterates mitigates the gap, consistent with the SB noise enabling escape from sharp basins.
- Batch-size threshold studies showing a point beyond which test accuracy degrades and sharpness rises.
- Preliminary attempts (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) that reduce but do not eliminate the gap or sharpness.

Overall, the paper makes a clear, well-motivated empirical claim, provides multiple lines of consistent evidence, and frames open questions and potential remedies.

2) Decision: Reject (borderline)
Key reasons:
- The central claim rests on an ad hoc sharpness measure and limited curvature diagnostics that are not invariant to common reparameterizations and may confound interpretation.
- The experimental protocol does not sufficiently control for strong LB baselines and hyperparameter sensitivities (e.g., optimizer choice, learning-rate scaling/warmup, momentum, weight decay), making it hard to conclude that LB per se leads to sharp minima rather than suboptimal tuning.

3) Supporting arguments
- Sharpness metric and invariance. The proposed sharpness (max within a box) depends on the choice of ε, random subspace A, and the parameterization. Without scale- or reparameterization-invariant controls (e.g., Fisher-scaled norms, layer-wise rescaling invariance, path-norm, or function-space measures), sharpness can change under simple weight rescalings (particularly with BatchNorm). This weakens the causal interpretation “LB → sharp minima → poor generalization.”
- Curvature estimation. The metric is optimized inexactly (10 L-BFGS-B steps); no validation via top Hessian eigenvalue (e.g., power iteration) or Hessian trace/diagonal approximations is provided. Given claims about “many large positive eigenvalues,” direct spectral evidence would strengthen the results.
- Optimization baselines. Most experiments are with Adam; LB generalization is known to be sensitive to optimizer, learning-rate schedules, momentum, and weight decay. While the paper mentions similar outcomes with SGD/Adagrad, details are insufficient (hyperparameters, tuning budget, schedules). Absent strong LB baselines (e.g., linear learning-rate scaling with warmup, momentum tuning, weight-decay scaling), the gap may reflect suboptimal LB tuning rather than an intrinsic property.
- Protocol confounds. The paper trains “until the loss ceased to improve,” but does not equate training budgets (e.g., total examples seen, wall-clock, epochs) across SB/LB. Differences in optimization dynamics and early stopping criteria may bias which basins are visited. Also, using 10% of data as LB for all datasets can be extremely large (e.g., ~5k for CIFAR), which may not reflect practical LB regimes; exploring the full batch-size spectrum with careful tuning is important.
- Correlation vs causation. Although sharpness and generalization gap correlate in these settings, the paper does not convincingly rule out alternative explanations (e.g., implicit regularization differences due to optimizer/learning-rate noise, weight decay interaction, label smoothing, data augmentation strength). The warm-start experiments are suggestive but not decisive.

Despite these reservations, the work is thoughtfully executed, clearly written, and focuses on an important practical problem.

4) Additional feedback and questions to help improve the paper (not all part of my decision)
Constructive suggestions:
- Use scale- and reparameterization-aware flatness measures. Consider Fisher-weighted norms, normalized sharpness (e.g., per-parameter scaling invariance), path-norms, spectral norms of weight matrices, or function-space curvature proxies. Validate the sharpness metric against top Hessian eigenvalues (power iteration) and/or trace approximations.
- Strengthen LB baselines. For SGD with momentum, include linear learning-rate scaling with warmup, momentum and weight-decay tuning per batch size, label smoothing, and stronger data augmentation. Report thorough hyperparameter sweeps for SB and LB with comparable tuning budgets.
- Equalize training budgets. Compare SB vs LB under fixed total examples processed, fixed epochs, and matched wall-clock where possible. Report sensitivity to stopping criteria.
- Report broader curvature–generalization scatter. Across many random seeds and multiple minima, show correlations between sharpness (invariant metrics) and test accuracy, not just exemplar pairs.
- Measure gradient noise scale. Quantify how gradient noise decreases with batch size in your setups and relate it to the “exploration” hypothesis and the observed thresholds.
- Provide distance and basin analyses. Systematically report distances traveled from initialization and connect to basin geometry. Consider linear mode connectivity between SB and LB solutions.
- Expand remedies. Include dynamic batch-size growth experiments (with schedules) and analyze how sharpness evolves. Try explicit regularizers that penalize curvature (e.g., spectral norm penalties) and report whether LB can be steered to flatter regions.

Questions for the authors:
- How were learning rates, momentum/β parameters, weight decay, and warmup tuned per batch size and per optimizer? Can you provide comprehensive tuning details and ablations to ensure the LB baselines are strong?
- How does your sharpness metric behave under simple rescalings of layers (especially with BatchNorm)? Can you demonstrate invariance or provide normalized variants and show the main conclusions hold?
- Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues (or approximations) at SB and LB solutions for a subset of models, and compare with your metric?
- If you match total number of examples seen and use the best-tuned schedules for each batch size, do the generalization gap and sharpness differences persist?
- Beyond Adam, what are the full results with SGD + momentum under common large-batch tricks (linear scaling, warmup), and with modern strong augmentation? Do you still see the same sharpness–gap pattern?
- Your experiments suggest sharpness concentrates in a low-dimensional subspace. Can you quantify this (e.g., spectrum decay, effective rank) and relate it to generalization?

Overall, this is an important and timely investigation. With stronger invariance-aware curvature measurements and more rigorous LB baselines and controls, the paper could make a compelling and durable contribution.
