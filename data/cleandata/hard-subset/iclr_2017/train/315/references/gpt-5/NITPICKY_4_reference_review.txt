1) Summary and claimed contributions (positive and generous)
The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often exhibits a generalization gap relative to small-batch (SB) training. The central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima (large positive Hessian eigenvalues), whereas SB methods (with noisier gradients) converge to flatter minima, and that this difference explains the generalization gap. The paper supports this claim with:
- Empirical comparisons across six architectures/datasets (fully connected and CNNs).
- 1D and curvilinear interpolation plots between SB and LB solutions showing the LB basin appears sharper.
- A practical “sharpness” proxy (a constrained local max of the loss around a solution, optionally in a random subspace) that shows 1–2 orders-of-magnitude larger values for LB solutions.
- Observations of a “batch-size threshold” beyond which test accuracy degrades and sharpness increases.
- Warm-start experiments showing that starting LB from SB iterates after an initial “exploration phase” leads LB to reach flatter regions with improved generalization.
- Attempts to mitigate the gap (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), which improve accuracy somewhat but still yield sharper solutions, suggesting the core phenomenon persists.
Overall, the paper provides an appealingly simple narrative (gradient noise as implicit regularization steering away from sharp minima) with multiple empirical probes across setups.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Methodological shortcomings and insufficient controls for fair LB vs SB comparisons (optimizer/hyperparameters, normalization layers, training protocols) make the core causal claim not adequately supported.
- The proposed sharpness metric is ad hoc, insufficiently justified, and likely not invariant to reparameterization (e.g., BatchNorm/scale symmetries), making the central evidence fragile; critical computational details are missing or ambiguous.

3) Supporting arguments
Fairness and tuning:
- LB uses 10% of the dataset as batch size across tasks; this absolute choice interacts strongly with dataset size, optimizer settings, and learning-rate schedules. There is no clear tuning or learning-rate scaling/warmup strategy per batch size, nor a report of hyperparameter sweeps to ensure LB is not disadvantaged. Using the same ADAM hyperparameters across batch sizes can confound conclusions (LB often requires different base LR, beta parameters, decay, and schedule).
- The training stop rule “until the loss ceased to improve” is vague; criteria and patience windows are not specified. No matched compute budget or matched epoch/step protocols are reported, making comparisons ambiguous.
- Regularization (weight decay, dropout rates), data preprocessing, and augmentation differ in their interaction with batch size; the paper does not systematically control for these interactions or report comprehensive ablations.

Sharpness metric and interpretation:
- The sharpness proxy in Eq. (4) is not demonstrably invariant to common reparameterizations (e.g., layer scaling with ReLU/BatchNorm). The ad hoc box C and dependence on A+ raise concerns; the intended invariance rationale is not persuasive. Without reparameterization-invariant measures, the comparison between SB and LB minima is questionable.
- Only 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B are used to estimate the local worst-case perturbation; convergence or stationarity of this inner maximization is not verified, and sensitivity to the chosen epsilon values (10^-3, 5·10^-4) is not analyzed.
- The relationship to Hessian spectra is claimed but not measured. Modern Hessian-vector product approximations (power iteration/Lanczos) could estimate top eigenvalues at scale; not attempted.
- Interpolation plots with BatchNorm: interpolating parameters across solutions with BN requires careful handling of running statistics; it is not stated whether BN stats were recomputed at each interpolated point. Without this, plots can be misleading.
- Dropout presence complicates evaluation; it is unclear whether dropout was disabled at test time consistently during interpolations/metric evaluation.

Evidence scope and reporting:
- Only 5 random seeds are used; reported means/standard deviations for the key metrics/tables are referred to but not fully shown here. Confidence intervals and effect sizes are not systematically discussed.
- The batch-size “threshold” is shown for two networks; the phenomenon may be task/architecture dependent. A more systematic sweep with controlled LR schedules per batch size is necessary.
- The mitigation attempts (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial/stability training) are under-specified (hyperparameters, adversarial perturbation norms/steps, exact procedures). The negative result on adversarial/stability training may be due to suboptimal configurations.

Clarity/formatting issues (numerous minor flaws):
- Multiple typos/grammar (“which and are often referred…”, “which a MaxPool…”), inconsistent section/figure references (Figure 31 vs Figure 3; Figure 7 used for different content), and LaTeX artifacts in references (e.g., “? \Q2016\E”).
- Notational ambiguities: Eq. (3) appears to intend |(A^+ x)_i| but is typeset as |(A+x)_i|; define all symbols unambiguously. Clarify A generation, dimensions, and whether A is resampled per run.
- Tables 2–6 referred to but not fully specified; ensure complete reporting.

Given the central causal claim hinges on a fragile metric and insufficiently controlled comparisons, I do not find the evidence rigorous enough for acceptance.

4) Suggestions for improvement (helpful, not all tied to the decision)
- Ensure fair LB tuning: adopt batch-size-aware LR scaling and warmup; sweep LR/decay/schedule/momentum/Adam betas per batch size and report best validated settings; match training budgets.
- Use reparameterization-robust sharpness measures or control for BN/scale symmetries; at minimum, recompute BN statistics for interpolated points and report both parameter-space and function-space flatness (e.g., PAC-Bayesian-inspired normalized flatness).
- Directly estimate top Hessian eigenvalues via Hessian-vector products; report spectra/traces/subspace structure to substantiate “few sharp directions.”
- Report gradient noise variance across batch sizes to directly link noise to exploration/flatness.
- Provide comprehensive ablative studies: effect of weight decay, label smoothing, data augmentation strength, architecture depth/width, and batch normalization placement.
- Specify and justify the epsilon and box construction for sharpness; do sensitivity analyses; verify inner maximizer convergence.
- Improve writing, fix references/figures/tables, and release code for full experiments (not only interpolation plots).

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates, schedules, and Adam betas chosen for each batch size? Did you use LR scaling/warmup? Please report per-setting hyperparameters and validation curves.
- How are BatchNorm running statistics handled in interpolation and sharpness evaluations? Are stats recomputed at each point on the path and for perturbed parameters?
- What is the rationale for Eq. (3) (the box C) and the use of A+? Please clarify the formula (is it |(A^+ x)_i|+1?) and demonstrate invariance claims empirically.
- How sensitive are your sharpness results to epsilon, to the number of L-BFGS-B steps, and to the choice/dimension p of A?
- Can you provide Hessian top-eigenvalue estimates (power iteration) at SB and LB solutions to corroborate the sharpness claim?
- Did you equalize training budgets across SB and LB (epochs or updates)? What is the exact “ceased to improve” criterion?
- For the “threshold” experiment, were hyperparameters re-tuned per batch size? Please provide full curves and settings.
- In the mitigation studies, what exact adversarial/stability training configurations were used (norms, step sizes, iteration counts)? Could suboptimal settings explain the negative results?
