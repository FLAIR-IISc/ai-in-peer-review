1) Summary and claimed contributions
- The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often exhibits a generalization gap relative to small-batch (SB) training. The central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima (characterized by many large positive Hessian eigenvalues) that generalize worse, whereas SB methods, due to gradient noise, tend to find flatter minima.
- The authors provide empirical evidence across several feedforward and convolutional architectures on standard datasets. They use 1D and curvilinear interpolation plots between SB/LB solutions and propose a heuristic “sharpness” metric computed by maximizing the loss in a small neighborhood (full space and random subspaces).
- They explore warm-starting LB from SB iterates, observe a batch-size “threshold” phenomenon, and attempt remedies (data augmentation, conservative proximal steps, adversarial training), finding partial improvements but persistent sharpness.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Methodological rigor is insufficient to substantiate the causal claim that LB → sharp minima → poorer generalization. The evidence is largely correlational, with substantial confounders not controlled.
- The proposed sharpness metric is ad hoc, sensitive to parameterization and scaling, and not convincingly validated; the optimization used to estimate it is inexact and its dependence on hyperparameters is not probed.
- The experimental protocol for LB training appears under-tuned relative to SB, with missing controls (learning-rate scaling, schedules, normalization effects), making it hard to conclude that the observed gap is intrinsic to batch size rather than suboptimal LB procedure.

3) Supporting arguments
- Confounding factors in LB vs SB comparisons:
  - Learning-rate and schedule: There is no clear accounting of appropriate learning-rate scaling with batch size, warmup, or momentum tuning. Using the same optimizer and nominal hyperparameters across drastically different batch sizes is known to bias results against LB.
  - Number of updates vs epochs: “Trained until the loss ceased to improve” can result in very different numbers of parameter updates for SB and LB. Generalization depends on the number of updates; this is not controlled.
  - Normalization: Most networks use batch normalization, which interacts materially with batch size. BN statistics’ quality and training dynamics differ under SB vs LB, confounding conclusions about curvature and generalization.
  - Optimizer choice: Most results emphasize ADAM, whose effective step sizes depend on gradient variance. Claims of “similar results” with SGD/Adagrad are not presented with comparable tuning rigor and detailed hyperparameters.
- Sharpness metric:
  - The metric depends on a box size parameter and a random subspace A; only 10 L-BFGS-B iterations are performed, so the max is likely far from optimal and uneven across runs.
  - The metric is not invariant to simple reparameterizations (e.g., rescaling layers in ReLU networks), making cross-solution comparisons questionable. This is a known issue for “flat vs sharp” measured in parameter space.
  - No sensitivity analysis is shown for epsilon, p, choice of A, or effect of weight rescaling; the purported relation to the top Hessian eigenvalues is asserted but not empirically validated (e.g., via power/Lanczos for top-eigs).
- Evidence is largely qualitative:
  - 1D/curvilinear interpolations can be illustrative but are not definitive. Similar plots can be misleading due to path choice and parameterization.
  - Only 5 seeds are used; standard deviations are not consistently reported alongside all key metrics.
  - The batch-size choice (10% of training set) is extreme and dataset-dependent; conclusions may not transfer to practically relevant “moderately large” batches.
- Remedies and warm-start:
  - The warm-start result is interesting but does not isolate whether batch-size per se or hyperparameter dynamics (e.g., effective noise from LR schedules) drive the effect. Dynamic batch sizing is suggested but not rigorously tested.

4) Feedback to improve the paper (to help the authors)
- Strengthen experimental protocol:
  - For each batch size, tune learning rates, momentum, weight decay, and schedules (with warmup) to best validation performance. Report full sweeps and selected best settings.
  - Control for number of parameter updates and total training compute. Compare SB and LB under fixed update budgets and under matched wall-clock goals.
  - Evaluate with SGD+momentum as primary (ADAM has its own confounds) and provide thorough ablations; include AdamW if used.
  - Probe the role of BN: repeat with alternative normalizations (e.g., GroupNorm) or fixed BN statistics to decouple normalization effects from batch size.
- Improve the curvature characterization:
  - Complement the heuristic metric with top-k Hessian eigenvalue estimates via power/Lanczos at final solutions; report eigenspectrum tails or trace approximations (e.g., Hutchinson).
  - Provide sensitivity analyses of the sharpness metric to epsilon, p, the number of inner optimization steps, and simple layer rescalings demonstrating (non-)invariance.
  - Consider function-space or output-space flatness measures (e.g., margin-based, PAC-Bayes-inspired metrics) that are less sensitive to reparameterizations.
- Expand scope and robustness:
  - Include larger-scale settings and stronger baselines (e.g., ImageNet-scale architectures/datasets) where LB training is practical.
  - Report confidence intervals over more seeds and show per-run variability.
  - When claiming partial remedy via augmentation or proximal steps, ensure fair retuning and quantify whether generalization parity is achieved under matched compute.
- Clarify and release: Provide all hyperparameters, code, and seeds for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates, momentum/betas, weight decay, and schedules selected for each batch size? Did you apply batch-size-aware scaling and warmup? Please provide full hyperparameter grids and chosen values.
- How many parameter updates did SB vs LB perform until “loss ceased to improve”? Can you reproduce results under matched numbers of updates?
- What is the effect of removing BN or replacing it with GroupNorm/LayerNorm on the sharpness and generalization trends?
- Can you report top-1/2 Hessian eigenvalues via power iteration at SB and LB solutions and compare them to your sharpness metric?
- How sensitive are your sharpness scores to epsilon, p, and the number of L-BFGS-B steps? Do simple layer rescalings change the metric while leaving function values unchanged?
- Do your findings persist with SGD+momentum tuned per batch size and with standard regularization (label smoothing, stronger weight decay, Mixup/CutMix)?
- In dynamic sampling, can you show a controlled schedule where batch size increases over time that matches SB generalization without warm-starting?
