Summary and contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often exhibits worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training loss. The central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to “sharp” minima—characterized by large positive curvature—while SB methods find “flat” minima, and that this sharpness correlates with poorer generalization. The authors provide:
- Empirical evidence across several architectures/datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100, TIMIT; AlexNet/VGG-style and MLPs).
- 1D and curvilinear parametric plots connecting SB and LB solutions that visualize sharper basins for LB.
- A heuristic “sharpness” metric based on local maximization of the loss within a small box (full space and random subspaces).
- Warm-start experiments suggesting LB can succeed after an SB “exploration phase.”
- Preliminary attempts to remedy LB generalization (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), with partial improvements.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: The empirical evidence does not sufficiently control for well-known confounders in large-batch training, and the sharpness metric is not robust to reparameterization or rigorously validated against curvature. As a result, the main causal claim (LB → sharp minima → poor generalization) is not convincingly established.

Supporting arguments
- Hyperparameter confounding: The LB regime is set to 10% of the dataset, which results in very large absolute batch sizes (e.g., 5k on CIFAR-10). There is no systematic tuning per batch size (e.g., linear scaling of learning rate, warmup, schedule/decay, momentum tuning, weight decay, label smoothing). The literature shows that many “generalization gap” effects diminish or vanish with appropriate large-batch training protocols. Without matching training budgets (updates), LR schedules, and regularization per batch size, the comparisons are not apples-to-apples.
- Choice of optimizer: Most results are with Adam, which is known to have different generalization behavior than SGD with momentum commonly used in large-batch imaging tasks. Although the paper states some additional experiments, the core evidence and narrative depend heavily on Adam. A thorough treatment requires systematic experiments with momentum SGD across batch sizes with proper scaling, otherwise the conclusions are limited.
- Sharpness metric validity: The proposed metric relies on inexact local maximization within a box whose definition depends on A+ and an ad hoc bound (|A+ x| + 1). It is not invariant to reparameterizations (notably problematic with BatchNorm and ReLU scaling), and the paper acknowledges only a hand-wavy link to the Hessian spectrum. Prior work has shown that “sharpness” in weight space can be arbitrarily manipulated by rescaling without changing generalization. Without scale-invariant measures or careful control of parameterization, the observed differences may be artifacts.
- Missing curvature baselines: No direct estimates of top Hessian eigenvalues (via power/Lanczos and Hessian-vector products), Fisher/empirical Fisher, or Gauss–Newton approximations are provided to corroborate the sharpness metric. The claim that LB solutions have many large positive eigenvalues is not directly supported.
- Limited breadth of evidence: The datasets and architectures are modest and do not reflect contemporary best practices where large batches are used successfully. The remedies section is preliminary; dynamic batch size is suggested but not thoroughly evaluated.
- Causality vs correlation: The experiments show correlation between LB and larger values under the chosen sharpness metric, but do not convincingly rule out alternative explanations (e.g., suboptimal LR schedules leading to under-training in function space, or different implicit regularization due to Adam vs SGD).

Additional feedback for improvement (to help the authors)
- Control training protocols across batch sizes:
  - Use SGD with momentum and Adam as separate, fully tuned baselines.
  - Apply linear LR scaling with batch size and warmup; tune weight decay, label smoothing, data augmentation strength, and training duration so that the number of parameter updates and/or training loss are matched fairly.
  - Report per-batch-size hyperparameter tuning and validation.
- Strengthen curvature analysis:
  - Estimate top Hessian eigenvalues via power iterations and Hessian-vector products; compare with the proposed metric to validate it.
  - Consider scale-invariant sharpness measures or measures in function space to address reparameterization invariance (especially with BatchNorm).
  - Report sensitivity of your sharpness metric to ε, p (subspace dimension), number of L-BFGS-B steps, and the construction of A. Show stability across these choices.
- Expand batch-size sweeps and ablations:
  - Provide accuracy vs batch size curves with properly tuned settings (including schedules) and show the threshold phenomena across multiple datasets/architectures.
  - Compare Adam vs SGD, with and without BatchNorm, to separate optimizer and normalization effects.
- Remedies:
  - Evaluate dynamic batch schedules (small-to-large) more systematically, given the warm-start result.
  - Consider recent flatness-promoting algorithms (e.g., sharpness-aware minimization) as a control and assess whether they mitigate the LB gap under matched training setups.
- Presentation:
  - Clarify stopping criteria (“until the loss ceased to improve” is ambiguous across batch sizes).
  - Provide multiple random seeds and error bars for all key plots, not just tables.
  - Discuss parameterization dependence explicitly, and the implications for interpreting sharpness.

Questions for the authors
1. How were learning rates, momentum/β parameters, weight decay, and schedules chosen per batch size? Did you use linear scaling and warmup? Please provide a detailed tuning protocol and whether results persist under best-practice LB settings.
2. Why prioritize Adam for the main comparisons? Do your sharpness and generalization conclusions hold when using SGD with momentum and properly tuned schedules across batch sizes?
3. How sensitive is your sharpness metric to ε, the choice/dimension of A, and the number of L-BFGS-B steps? Can you demonstrate rank-order stability across these variations?
4. Can you report estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalues (or empirical Fisher spectral norm) at SB vs LB solutions to directly support the curvature claim?
5. Given BatchNorm and known reparameterization issues, how do you ensure your sharpness comparisons are meaningful and not artifacts of scaling?
6. In the warm-start experiments, can you quantify how the noise scale/effective step size evolves and whether a dynamic batch schedule reproduces the same effect without an explicit SB pre-phase?
