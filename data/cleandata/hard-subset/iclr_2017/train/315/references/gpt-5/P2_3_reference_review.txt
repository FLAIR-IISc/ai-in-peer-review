1) Summary of contributions
- Problem: The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse test accuracy than small-batch (SB) training, despite achieving similar training loss. The central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minima, which generalize worse, while SB methods converge to flatter minima that generalize better.
- Approach: The authors provide empirical evidence on multiple architectures and datasets. They use 1D and curvilinear interpolation between SB and LB solutions, propose a computationally feasible “sharpness” metric based on local worst-case perturbations, study batch-size thresholds, and perform warm-starting experiments showing that SB “exploration” precedes successful LB convergence to flat regions. They also attempt several remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), finding partial but insufficient improvements.
- Contributions: (i) A clear empirical characterization of the generalization gap in terms of sharp vs flat minima, (ii) a heuristic but practical sharpness metric exhibiting 1–2 orders-of-magnitude differences between SB and LB solutions, (iii) compelling warm-start results suggesting a mechanistic explanation of SB’s advantage, and (iv) a performance analysis motivating why solving the LB generalization issue would be impactful for scalability.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- The paper tackles an important and timely question with high practical and scientific relevance, and presents a coherent, well-motivated empirical study across several architectures.
- The evidence is compelling that LB training tends to find sharper solutions than SB, and that this correlates with worse generalization. The warm-start experiments are particularly insightful and actionable.

3) Supporting arguments
- Importance and motivation: Scaling deep learning is central to practice; batch size is a primary lever for parallelism. The generalization gap has been widely observed but poorly understood. The paper is well-placed in the literature and connects to multiple theoretical perspectives (MDL, Bayesian views, recent loss landscape works).
- Methodology: Multiple networks (fully-connected and convnets akin to AlexNet/VGG), several datasets, 5 random restarts per condition, and consistent phenomena across settings. The interpolation plots provide qualitative evidence of sharper basins for LB minima. The sharpness metric, despite being heuristic, shows large and consistent differences between SB and LB solutions in both full space and random subspaces. The batch-size threshold and warm-start experiments provide a plausible mechanism: SB noise helps escape sharp basins; once a flat region is found, LB can home in without suffering generalization degradation.
- Claims vs evidence: The paper appropriately frames its core claims as empirical observations and correlations rather than formal causation. The evidence supports the main claims convincingly within the scope studied. The negative results on attempted remedies are also informative.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is clearly written, with architecture and dataset details in the appendix and code pointers for key plots. The narrative is easy to follow.

Limitations and threats to validity (do not outweigh contributions but merit acknowledgement):
- The sharpness metric is ad hoc and depends on parameterization, scaling choices (e.g., ε, matrix A), and potentially batch normalization. It is not invariant to simple reparameterizations, which complicates comparing “flatness” across models. Stronger spectral evidence (e.g., top Hessian eigenvalues via Lanczos) would strengthen the claim.
- Hyperparameter confounds: LB and SB training might require different learning rate schedules (e.g., linear scaling rule, warmup), batch norm momentum, and regularization. The paper does not systematically control for or sweep these settings, which could partially explain the gap.
- Compute budget confound: Training “until the loss ceases to improve” may result in very different numbers of parameter updates across SB/LB. Equalizing total updates or total epochs could disentangle optimization speed from generalization behavior.
- Optimizer choice: Experiments focus on Adam for both regimes (with some brief checks for others). Given known generalization differences between Adam and SGD with momentum, a thorough SGD-with-momentum study would be valuable.
- Batch size selection: Using 10% of the dataset as LB can be very large (e.g., 5k on CIFAR-10). A more systematic sweep including practically used large batches (e.g., 1k–16k with appropriate schedule tuning) would increase external validity.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Strengthen curvature evidence: On smaller models, report top Hessian eigenvalues/eigen-gaps via power/Lanczos, and compare to the proposed sharpness metric. Include spectra in random subspaces for consistency.
- Address parameterization dependence: Report “normalized sharpness” or flatness measures that are invariant to layer rescaling (e.g., path-norm, normalized sharpness), and discuss BN effects. Analyze how sharpness changes under simple rescalings that preserve function.
- Control for training budgets: Repeat key comparisons with fixed number of parameter updates and matched wall-clock where possible. Include linear learning-rate scaling and warmup for LB, tune BN momentum, weight decay, label smoothing, and data augmentation. Consider ghost batch norm to decouple normalization statistics from global batch size.
- Ablate the sharpness metric: Show sensitivity to ε, number of L-BFGS-B steps, and subspace dimension p. Provide distributions across seeds.
- Broaden optimizer coverage: Include SGD with momentum and report whether the same sharp/flat patterns persist under tuned schedules.
- Explore dynamic batching: Given the warm-start results, include a systematic study of gradually increasing batch size and compare to constant LB.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters: How were learning rates, Adam betas, weight decay, BN momentum chosen for SB vs LB? Did you try linear LR scaling with batch and warmup? How sensitive are your conclusions to these choices?
- Training budget: Can you replicate the main results when equalizing the total number of parameter updates or epochs between SB and LB?
- Sharpness metric sensitivity: How do results vary with ε and subspace dimension p? Can you provide curves showing robustness of the SB–LB gap across these settings?
- Parameterization invariance: How does your sharpness compare under simple weight rescalings (including compensating BN/activation rescaling) that leave the function unchanged? Do generalization and sharpness remain aligned?
- Spectral corroboration: On smaller networks, can you report the top few Hessian eigenvalues at SB and LB minima to substantiate the sharpness claim directly?
- BN effects: Did you test ghost batch norm or fixing BN batch size while increasing global batch? Does that reduce the gap?
- Optimizer dependence: Do you observe the same phenomena with SGD+momentum under tuned schedules on CIFAR/ImageNet-scale tasks?

Overall, this is a clear, timely, and influential empirical study that addresses an important practical problem and offers a plausible, well-supported explanation. Despite limitations, the contribution merits acceptance.
