1) Summary of contributions
- Problem: Why do large-batch (LB) training methods often yield worse generalization than small-batch (SB) methods despite achieving similar training loss?
- Claim: LB tends to converge to sharp minima (large positive Hessian eigenvalues or high local sensitivity), which correlates with poorer generalization; SB tends to find flatter minima.
- Evidence: 
  - 1D and curvilinear interpolation plots between SB and LB solutions consistently show LB minima are sharper.
  - A computationally tractable “sharpness” metric (max loss increase within a small box, in full space and random subspaces) is 1–2 orders of magnitude larger for LB solutions across several architectures/datasets.
  - Warm-start experiments: running SB for some epochs before switching to LB allows LB to reach flatter minima and close the generalization gap, consistent with the “exploration via noise” hypothesis.
  - Batch-size sweep suggests a threshold beyond which generalization drops and sharpness stabilizes at a high level.
- Negative results: Data augmentation, “conservative training” via proximal subproblems, and adversarial/stability training help somewhat but do not eliminate sharpness or the generalization gap.
- Broader context: Discusses scalability motivation for LB and connects with literature on loss landscapes and many equivalent minima in deep nets.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- The paper tackles an important, practically relevant problem (scalable training without harming generalization) and provides coherent, cross-architecture empirical evidence that isolates a plausible mechanism (sharp minima).
- The study offers diagnostic tools (visualizations and a computable sharpness metric) and actionable training insights (warm-start/dynamic batching), likely to influence practice and follow-up research.

3) Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Motivation and placement: The need to scale training is well motivated; the generalization gap in LB is a widely observed pain point. The paper connects to optimization and generalization theory (MDL/Bayesian intuitions, saddle-point literature) and to empirical loss-surface explorations.
- Breadth and consistency: Multiple architectures (FCNs and CNNs, AlexNet/VGG-like variants) and datasets; repeated trials; consistent qualitative patterns across settings.
- Methodological clarity: Simple, interpretable experiments (parametric plots; heuristic sharpness) that, while approximate, are computationally feasible and reproducible enough to make the main point clear.
- Insightful ablation: Warm-starting and batch-size thresholds make the “noise helps escape sharp basins” narrative compelling.

Limitations (do not outweigh the contribution but should be acknowledged)
- Causality vs correlation: The link between sharpness and generalization is largely correlational; the paper does not fully rule out confounders (e.g., hyperparameter regimes interacting with batch size).
- Hyperparameter control: SB and LB use the same optimizer (ADAM) and fixed batch sizes (256 vs 10% of data), but there is limited exploration of batch-size-aware tuning (e.g., learning-rate/momentum scaling, weight decay, warmup). “Trained until loss ceased to improve” is under-specified and could bias outcomes.
- Sharpness metric: Heuristic, dependent on chosen box size and parameterization; only approximate inner maximization (10 L-BFGS-B steps). While the authors attempt invariance via scaling in the constraint set and random subspaces, reparameterization issues can still affect conclusions. Reporting top Hessian eigenvalues (e.g., via power iteration) would strengthen the link.
- Scope: Most experiments are on smaller vision/speech tasks; while likely indicative, results on larger-scale datasets/models would increase external validity.

Overall, despite these limitations, the paper convincingly documents a robust empirical phenomenon, provides a plausible mechanism, and suggests practical remedies (e.g., dynamic batching via warm starts).

4) Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily gating)
- Experimental controls:
  - Match computational budgets: compare methods at equal numbers of training examples seen, equal wall-clock budgets, and equal step counts.
  - Systematically tune learning rate, momentum/β parameters, weight decay, and learning-rate schedules per batch size (including warmup and linear scaling rules).
  - Report sensitivity analyses to these hyperparameters.
- Sharpness measurement:
  - Complement the heuristic metric with top Hessian eigenvalue(s) via power iteration/Lanczos, and relate values to generalization at comparable training losses.
  - Discuss potential dependence of sharpness on reparameterization (e.g., BatchNorm scale parameters) and clarify how your metric mitigates it.
- Broader coverage:
  - Include SGD with momentum as a primary optimizer; ADAM behaves differently with respect to noise and curvature.
  - Evaluate on at least one larger-scale dataset/model to test robustness of the conclusions.
- Remedies:
  - Systematically evaluate dynamic batch-size schedules (e.g., gradual increase) and report when/why they succeed relative to warm-started LB.
  - Consider reporting results with label smoothing, stochastic depth, or additional regularizers that may affect flatness.

Questions for the authors
- Hyperparameters: How were learning rates, β1/β2, weight decay, and schedules chosen for SB vs LB? Did you try batch-size-aware scaling and warmup? How sensitive are your conclusions to these choices?
- Budget matching: Do the SB and LB comparisons hold when matched on total gradient evaluations or wall-clock time? If not, how do the trends change?
- Optimizer choice: Do the sharpness and generalization trends persist with SGD+momentum and tuned schedules? Any notable differences from ADAM?
- Sharpness metric:
  - How sensitive are your sharpness rankings to the choice of ε, the random subspace dimension p, and the number of L-BFGS-B steps?
  - Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues (even approximately) and correlate them with your metric?
- Reparameterization: Given BatchNorm and scaling symmetries, how invariant is your sharpness metric across equivalent parameterizations?
- Dynamic batching: Can you formalize the warm-start threshold at which LB begins to benefit? How does this threshold depend on model/data and on the noise level implied by SB?
