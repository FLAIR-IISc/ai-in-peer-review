Summary and contributions
- The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training for deep networks often yields worse test accuracy than small-batch (SB) training despite similar training loss. The central claim is empirical: LB converges to sharp minima (large positive curvature), which generalize worse, while SB converges to flatter minima. 
- Evidence includes: 1D/curvilinear interpolation plots between SB/LB solutions; a heuristic “sharpness” metric based on local maximization in a neighborhood (full space and random subspaces); batch-size sweeps indicating a threshold beyond which test accuracy drops and sharpness rises; warm-start experiments where LB succeeds if initialized by SB after an exploration phase; preliminary attempts to mitigate (data augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial training).
- The work aims to motivate scalable training without sacrificing generalization and frames open questions relating LB behavior, landscape geometry, and possible remedies.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The empirical evidence for “LB → sharp minima → poor generalization” is not sufficiently controlled or rigorous; alternative explanations and confounders are not ruled out. 
2) The proposed sharpness metric is ad hoc, parameterization-dependent, and optimized inexactly, making the central causal claim tenuous.

Supporting arguments
- Optimizer and regularization confounds: Most experiments use ADAM for both SB and LB. ADAM’s generalization behavior differs from SGD with momentum and is sensitive to weight decay and learning-rate schedules. Without a strong baseline using well-tuned SGD+momentum across batch sizes, it is hard to attribute gaps to batch size rather than optimizer-specific effects. Using a single optimizer for both regimes does not eliminate the confound—interaction effects between batch size and adaptivity are known.
- Hyperparameter tuning fairness: LB training typically requires different learning-rate schedules (e.g., scaling rules, warmup), weight decay, and momentum parameters. The paper does not convincingly document a thorough, per-batch-size hyperparameter search or matched-compute comparisons (same number of updates vs same epochs). Under-tuned LB runs can systematically underperform and land in sharper minima, making the observed gap ambiguous.
- Sharpness measure: The metric depends on parameterization and scaling (e.g., layer rescalings in networks with ReLU/BN), and is computed by a highly inexact inner maximization (10 L-BFGS-B iterations). The chosen box C, dependence on A and its pseudo-inverse, and fixed epsilon values are arbitrary. As such, it is unclear whether the reported 1–2 order-of-magnitude differences reflect intrinsic geometry or artifacts of parameterization and the maximization heuristic. A more direct and standard curvature proxy (e.g., estimating top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos or Fisher spectrum) would make the claim more convincing.
- Interpolation plots: 1D/curvilinear visualizations are informative but slice-dependent; they do not establish global sharpness differences or causality with generalization. They can be heavily affected by scaling and reparameterizations and do not reveal curvature in directions orthogonal to the slice.
- Batch-size design: Using “10% of the dataset” as the LB regime mixes absolute batch-size effects across datasets and architectures. A systematic sweep over absolute batch sizes with per-size tuning is more appropriate. The “threshold” phenomenon is suggestive but lacks a principled definition or analysis of gradient-noise scale versus curvature.
- Mitigations: Data augmentation narrows accuracy gaps; other methods partly help. However, given the metric’s limitations, the conclusion that “sharpness remains” is not compelling. Stronger baselines (e.g., tuned schedules, explicit gradient noise injection, per-layer adaptive clipping, ghost batch norm, etc.) were not explored.
- Theoretical framing: The MDL/Bayesian narrative is speculative here. No formal link between the proposed sharpness metric, Hessian spectrum, and generalization is provided. Without stronger empirical controls, causal claims remain unsubstantiated.

Additional feedback (to improve the work; not all critical to my decision)
- Provide rigorous, per-batch-size hyperparameter searches for each optimizer. Include SGD+momentum with tuned learning-rate scaling/warmup, weight decay, and batch-norm settings. Report compute-matched comparisons (same updates and same epochs).
- Replace or complement the sharpness metric with scale- and parameterization-robust curvature proxies: top Hessian eigenvalues or spectral density via Lanczos/power iteration; Fisher information spectrum; function-space perturbation measures; or PAC-Bayes-style flatness evaluations. Clarify sensitivity of your metric to layer rescalings and choice of epsilon, A, and inner-solver budget.
- Quantify the dimensionality of “sharp” subspaces more directly (e.g., number of eigenvalues above thresholds; principal curvature directions). 
- Expand batch-size sweeps with principled analyses of gradient-noise scale vs curvature. Align batch sizes across datasets in absolute terms and report noise scale estimates.
- Strengthen mitigations: include dynamic batch-size schedules, explicit gradient noise injection for LB, tuned label smoothing/weight decay, ghost batch normalization, and learning-rate warmup. Analyze how these affect curvature and test performance.
- Improve reproducibility: release full training code and all hyperparameters, schedules, and seeds.

Questions for the authors
1) What hyperparameter tuning was done per batch size and optimizer (learning rates, schedules, weight decay, momentum/Adam betas, BN settings)? Are results stable under thorough tuning for LB?
2) Do the core findings hold with SGD+momentum baselines that are considered strong for generalization? How do results change with learning-rate warmup and linear scaling?
3) How sensitive is your sharpness metric to reparameterization (e.g., layer scalings/BN) and to epsilon/A/inner-iterations? Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues to corroborate?
4) In warm-start experiments, is the improvement explained by moving to a region with different curvature, or simply by better LR/regularization late in training? Can you disentangle?
5) Can you provide compute-matched comparisons (same updates) and show whether LB still converges to sharper minima under fair training budgets?
6) Do results extend to larger-scale settings (e.g., ImageNet) and modern architectures with stronger regularization?
