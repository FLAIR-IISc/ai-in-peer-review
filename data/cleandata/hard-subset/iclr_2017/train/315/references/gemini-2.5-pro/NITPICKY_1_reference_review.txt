### **Review**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the widely observed empirical phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large mini-batches often results in poorer generalization performance compared to training with small mini-batches, even when both achieve similar training loss. The authors' central thesis is that this "generalization gap" arises because large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers of the loss function, whereas small-batch (SB) methods are guided by gradient noise toward "flat" minimizers, which exhibit better generalization. The authors support this claim with a comprehensive set of numerical experiments across six different network architectures and several datasets. Key evidence includes 1-D visualizations of the loss landscape showing the qualitative difference in sharpness, and a novel proposed metric for quantifying the sharpness of a minimizer. The paper also explores the behavior of SB methods, identifying a batch size threshold beyond which performance degrades, and investigates several potential remedies for improving LB training, such as data augmentation and conservative training.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The central empirical evidence relies on a novel sharpness metric that is inadequately justified, containing arbitrary and non-standard design choices that call its validity into question.
2.  The paper is marred by a substantial number of methodological inconsistencies, missing details, and significant formatting errors, which collectively undermine the reader's confidence in the scientific rigor of the work.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

While the paper addresses an important problem and presents an intuitive hypothesis, the execution is plagued by numerous flaws.

**Regarding the Sharpness Metric:** The paper's quantitative claims hinge on Metric 2.1 (Equation 4). This metric, however, is built on a poorly motivated foundation.
*   **Constraint Set Definition (Eq. 3):** The constraint set `C` is defined in a highly unusual manner: `C = {z ∈ Rp : − (|(A+x)i|+ 1) ≤ zi ≤ (|(A+x)i|+ 1)}`. The dependence on the solution `x` itself means the size and shape of the exploration neighborhood change for each minimizer being evaluated, complicating direct comparisons. The term `+ 1` is entirely arbitrary. Furthermore, the use of the pseudo-inverse `A+` for a randomly generated, potentially non-square matrix `A` is not explained or justified; its inclusion seems both computationally suspect and theoretically opaque. A standard `l∞`-norm ball (`||z||∞ ≤ ε`) would have been a simpler, more interpretable, and better-justified choice.
*   **Normalization (Eq. 4):** The sharpness value is normalized by `(ε + f(x))`. Since `f(x)` is a loss value that approaches zero, this normalization is unstable and heavily influenced by the final training loss. A small difference in `f(x)` between two minimizers could lead to a large, artificial difference in their reported sharpness. Reporting the raw value `max f(x+Ay) - f(x)` would have been more direct and less ambiguous.
*   **Inexact Maximization:** The maximization subproblem is solved inexactly with only 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B. There is no analysis to show this is sufficient to find a reasonable approximation of the maximum, or how sensitive the final sharpness values are to this choice. The results could be biased by the optimizer's behavior in the first few steps.

**Regarding Methodological and Experimental Flaws:**
*   **Inconsistent Batch Sizes:** The SB size is fixed at 256, while the LB size is set to "10% of the training data." This leads to widely varying LB sizes across experiments (e.g., 6000 for MNIST, 5000 for CIFAR-10, but ~1900 for TIMIT). This inconsistency makes it difficult to attribute observed effects solely to the "large vs. small" dichotomy, as the ratio of LB to SB size changes from one experiment to the next.
*   **Dismissal of Overfitting:** The authors claim the generalization gap is "not due to over-fitting," based on a narrow definition that requires the testing accuracy curve to peak and then decay. A persistent gap between training and testing accuracy, which is clearly visible in their results (Table 2), is the standard definition of overfitting. The argument presented is a semantic distraction that misrepresents a key concept.
*   **Missing Performance Data:** The primary motivation for using large batches is to reduce training time via parallelism. The paper provides no wall-clock timing results to show how the number of epochs or total training time differs between the SB and LB regimes. The "idealized performance model" in Appendix C is not a substitute for empirical evidence and relies on the unrealistic assumption of perfect parallel efficiency.

**Regarding Presentation and Formatting:** The paper suffers from a striking lack of polish, which suggests a rushed preparation.
*   **Citations:** The reference list is improperly formatted with BibTeX artifacts (e.g., `Bengio et al\.,? \Q2016\E`). The citation for Srivastava et al. (Dropout) is listed with the year "1929", a clear and significant error.
*   **Figure/Appendix Confusion:** The reference "Figure 31" in the main text is confusing. In Appendix E.2, the text refers to results in "Figure 7," but Figure 7's caption and its reference from the main text are located in Appendix D, creating a disjointed reading experience.

#### **4. Additional Feedback**

The underlying idea of connecting the generalization gap to the geometry of the loss landscape is valuable and worthy of publication. To strengthen the paper, I suggest the following:
*   Redefine the sharpness metric using a standard, well-motivated neighborhood (e.g., an `l_p`-norm ball) and remove the problematic normalization. A thorough sensitivity analysis of its parameters is needed.
*   Standardize the experimental protocol. For instance, maintain a fixed ratio between large and small batch sizes across all experiments to ensure more controlled comparisons.
*   Include empirical measurements of training time (epochs and wall-clock time) to properly ground the work in its practical motivation.
*   Revisit the discussion on overfitting to align with standard definitions in the machine learning literature.
*   The entire manuscript requires a meticulous proofreading pass to correct the numerous citation, formatting, and cross-referencing errors.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a detailed justification for the specific functional form of the constraint set `C` in Equation 3? Specifically, what is the motivation for the solution-dependent term `|(A+x)i|`, the addition of `+ 1`, and the use of the pseudo-inverse `A+`?
2.  How did you verify that 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B were sufficient for the maximization subproblem in your sharpness metric? How much do the reported sharpness values change if you use, for example, 100 or 1000 iterations?
3.  The text states that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting. Could you clarify how you distinguish your observations from the standard definition of overfitting, which is characterized by a significant gap between training and test performance?
