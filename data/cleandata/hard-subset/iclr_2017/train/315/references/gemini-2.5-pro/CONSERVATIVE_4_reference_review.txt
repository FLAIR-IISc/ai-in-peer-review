### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large mini-batches often results in poorer generalization performance compared to training with small mini-batches, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors' central claim is that this "generalization gap" is attributable to the geometry of the minima found by the respective methods. Specifically, they posit that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, which are characterized by high sensitivity to perturbations in the parameter space, while small-batch (SB) methods are guided toward "flat" minimizers that generalize better. The paper supports this claim with extensive empirical evidence across six different network architectures and four datasets. The evidence includes 1D visualizations of the loss landscape, a proposed heuristic metric for quantifying sharpness, and experiments showing the effect of batch size on test accuracy and sharpness.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **The central empirical claims rely heavily on a novel, unproven heuristic for measuring "sharpness".** The methodological soundness of this new metric (Metric 2.1) is not sufficiently established. The metric has several hyperparameters (e.g., the perturbation size ε, the projection matrix A, the inexactness of the inner maximization) whose influence on the conclusions is not analyzed, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the core findings.
2.  **The paper provides strong correlational evidence but falls short of establishing causality.** While the connection between large batches, high values of the proposed sharpness metric, and poor generalization is demonstrated, the paper does not convincingly prove that sharpness *causes* poor generalization. It is equally plausible that both sharpness (as measured) and poor generalization are symptoms of a more fundamental underlying dynamic of LB optimization, such as insufficient exploration of the parameter space.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in deep learning optimization. The observation that LB methods converge to different solutions than SB methods is important, and the hypothesis linking this to sharpness and flatness is a compelling line of inquiry. The breadth of the experiments is commendable. However, the rigor of the central arguments is undermined by a reliance on methods that are not sufficiently validated.

**Critique of the Sharpness Metric:** The paper's quantitative argument rests almost entirely on Metric 2.1. This metric is defined as the maximum increase in the loss function within a small, ε-sized box around a solution `x`. This approach presents several methodological issues:

*   **Arbitrariness of Hyperparameters:** The metric's value is dependent on the choice of ε. The paper uses two small values (10⁻³, 5·10⁻⁴) but does not justify these choices or provide a sensitivity analysis. Would a different choice of ε lead to different conclusions? Furthermore, the inner maximization problem is solved inexactly with only 10 L-BFGS-B steps. The degree of this inexactness could systematically bias the results, and this is not controlled for.
*   **Lack of Grounding:** The proposed metric is heuristic. While computing the Hessian spectrum is expensive, established stochastic approximation methods (e.g., Lanczos method) exist to estimate the top eigenvalues, which are the standard measure of local curvature. The paper does not compare its proposed metric against these more standard measures, even on smaller models where it might be feasible. Without such a comparison, it is difficult to trust that the new metric is a reliable proxy for sharpness.
*   **Dimensionality of Subspace:** For the projected sharpness, a random subspace of dimension p=100 is chosen. This choice seems arbitrary. The observation that the function rises steeply only along a "small dimensional subspace" is interesting but is not rigorously quantified beyond this single choice of `p`.

**Correlation vs. Causation:** The 1D parametric plots (Figure 3) are visually striking but can be misleading in high-dimensional spaces. A line segment between two points is an extremely limited view of the landscape. The paper successfully demonstrates a strong correlation: LB training leads to solutions that have high scores on their sharpness metric and perform poorly on the test set. However, the causal link is assumed rather than proven. The authors' own experiment on warm-starting (Figure 5) suggests an alternative explanation: SB methods perform an "exploration" phase that finds a "good" basin of attraction, which LB methods, lacking sufficient gradient noise, are unable to find on their own. In this view, finding a flat minimum is the *result* of successful exploration, not necessarily the *mechanism* of generalization itself. The core issue might be the exploratory property conferred by gradient noise, with sharpness being merely a feature of the suboptimal basins that LB methods get stuck in.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Strengthen the Methodological Foundation:** To make the claims more convincing, the authors should validate their sharpness metric. This could involve: (a) comparing its output to Hessian eigenvalue estimates on smaller networks; (b) performing a sensitivity analysis with respect to its hyperparameters (ε, L-BFGS-B steps); (c) providing a stronger theoretical motivation for its specific formulation, perhaps linking it more formally to generalization bounds from the literature (e.g., PAC-Bayes).
*   **Deepen the Analysis of Optimization Dynamics:** The paper notes that SB iterates travel further from the initialization point. This is a valuable observation that could be expanded. Quantifying the "exploration" of SB vs. LB methods (e.g., by analyzing the trajectory variance or autocorrelation of gradients) could provide a more direct explanation for their different behaviors, potentially framing the "sharpness" observation as a consequence rather than a cause.
*   **Discuss Limitations of Visualization:** While visualizations are helpful, the authors should be more forthright about their limitations in high-dimensional spaces and avoid drawing overly strong conclusions from them.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  How were the specific values for ε in Metric 2.1 chosen? How do the reported sharpness values, and the conclusions drawn from them, change as ε varies over a wider range (e.g., from 10⁻⁵ to 10⁻²)?
2.  Could you provide a comparison between your proposed sharpness metric and a more standard measure, such as the top eigenvalue of the Hessian (approximated via the power method or Lanczos algorithm), for at least one of the smaller networks (e.g., F1)? This would help validate whether your heuristic is capturing true local curvature.
3.  The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) is very insightful. It suggests that once an SB method has found a "good region," an LB method can successfully optimize within it. Does this not suggest that the primary advantage of SB methods is their ability to escape poor initial basins of attraction, rather than an inherent ability to flatten a minimum they are already in? How do you disentangle the "exploration" effect of gradient noise from the "flatness" of the final solution?
