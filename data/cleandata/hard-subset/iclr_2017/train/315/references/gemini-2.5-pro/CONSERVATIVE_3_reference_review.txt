### **ICLR Review**

**Summary of Claims:**

This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large batches often leads to poorer generalization compared to small-batch training, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors' central claim is that this "generalization gap" is attributable to the nature of the minimizers found by the different training regimes. Specifically, they posit that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, which are characterized by high sensitivity to perturbations in the parameter space. In contrast, small-batch (SB) methods, benefiting from the inherent noise in gradient estimates, are able to find "flat" minimizers, which generalize better. The paper supports this claim with empirical evidence across several network architectures and datasets. The evidence includes 1-D visualizations of the loss landscape, which show LB solutions residing in steeper basins, and a novel heuristic metric designed to quantify the "sharpness" of a minimizer. This metric consistently shows values that are orders of magnitude higher for LB solutions than for SB solutions.

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **The central claim relies on correlation rather than demonstrated causation.** The paper provides compelling evidence that large-batch training is correlated with sharp minima and poor generalization. However, it does not rigorously establish that sharpness is the *cause* of the generalization gap, rather than another symptom of large-batch dynamics.
2.  **The main quantitative evidence rests on a novel, unvalidated heuristic for sharpness.** The proposed sharpness metric is central to the paper's argument, but its properties and sensitivity to its own hyperparameters (e.g., ε, the choice of manifold A) are not explored. Without a more thorough validation or grounding in established measures of curvature, it is difficult to assess the rigor of the findings that depend on it.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The paper tackles an important and practical problem. The hypothesis that the geometry of the loss landscape, specifically the flatness of minimizers, is key to generalization is an intuitive and long-standing idea in the machine learning community. The experiments are well-documented and the visualizations in Figure 3 are illustrative.

However, the methodological soundness of the core argument is questionable. My primary concern is the reliance on the proposed sharpness metric in Equation (4). This metric is presented as a computationally feasible proxy for the Hessian's spectrum, but this connection is asserted rather than proven. The metric itself involves an inexact maximization using a fixed number of L-BFGS-B steps, and depends on a parameter ε and a random projection matrix A. The paper does not provide any analysis on how the choice of these parameters affects the sharpness values. Are the reported orders-of-magnitude differences robust to changes in ε or the dimension `p` of the random manifold? How does this measure compare to more established, albeit expensive, methods for estimating the top eigenvalues of the Hessian on a smaller-scale problem where direct computation is feasible? Without such analysis, the metric feels ad-hoc, and the conclusions drawn from it are not as rigorous as they need to be.

Furthermore, the paper's argument frames sharpness as the cause of poor generalization. An alternative hypothesis is that both the observed sharpness and the generalization gap are consequences of the optimization trajectory itself. Small-batch methods introduce significant noise, which is known to have an implicit regularizing effect. This noise might allow the optimizer to traverse flat, high-volume regions of the parameter space, which may be more robust and generalize better. In this view, the "flatness" of the final solution is a property of the region discovered by the noise-driven search, not an independent feature that is sought out. The paper observes that LB methods stay closer to the initialization, which supports the idea that they perform a more "direct" descent into the nearest basin, which happens to be sharp. This does not prove that if an LB method were somehow forced into a flat basin, it would generalize well. The correlation is strong, but the causal link required to make a strong claim is not sufficiently supported.

Finally, the attempts to remedy the problem in Appendix E are presented as preliminary. While data augmentation shows some promise in closing the accuracy gap, the authors note that the minimizers remain sharp. This finding seems to partially contradict the main thesis: if sharpness is the problem, then a method that improves generalization should also reduce sharpness. The fact that it doesn't suggests the relationship is more complex than presented.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   **Metric Validation:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by a section dedicated to validating the proposed sharpness metric. This could include:
    *   A sensitivity analysis with respect to its parameters (ε, `p`, number of L-BFGS-B iterations).
    *   A comparison with approximations of the top Hessian eigenvalue (e.g., via the Lanczos method) on a smaller network to demonstrate that the proposed metric is a reliable proxy.
*   **Strengthening the Causal Link:** To move beyond correlation, consider experiments designed to isolate sharpness. For example, could one devise an optimization algorithm that explicitly penalizes sharpness (using the proposed metric or a Hessian-based regularizer)? If such an algorithm, when used in a large-batch setting, successfully finds flatter minima *and* recovers the generalization performance of small-batch methods, the causal argument would be much more convincing.
*   **Optimizer Diversity:** The experiments primarily use ADAM. While the authors state that other optimizers yielded similar results, it would be beneficial to include a comparison with a non-adaptive method like SGD with momentum in the main paper. The dynamics of adaptive and non-adaptive methods are different, and showing the phenomenon holds for both would make the claims more general.
*   **Dynamic Batch Sizing:** The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) is one of the most interesting parts of the paper. It strongly suggests that a curriculum strategy, starting with small batches and gradually increasing the size, could be a viable solution. This seems like a more promising direction than the methods in the appendix and could be a more central focus of the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding Metric 2.1, how did you choose the values for ε? How sensitive are the reported sharpness differences in Tables 3 and 4 to this choice? A very small ε might only capture local curvature, while a larger one might measure the width of the entire basin.
2.  The warm-starting experiment in Section 3 suggests that SB methods first perform an "exploration" to find a good basin, after which LB methods can effectively "exploit" it. Does this not suggest that the primary issue is the lack of exploration in LB methods, and that sharpness is merely a characteristic of the suboptimal basins they find by default?
3.  In Appendix E.1, data augmentation closes the generalization gap to a large extent but the LB minima remain "sharp." How do you reconcile this with the central thesis that sharpness is the primary cause of the generalization gap? Could it be that your sharpness metric is capturing a property that is not perfectly aligned with generalization?
