### Review of "On Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning: Generalization Gap and Sharp Minima"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper investigates the commonly observed phenomenon that deep learning models trained with large mini-batches often exhibit a "generalization gap"—poorer performance on test data—compared to models trained with small batches, even when achieving similar training loss. The authors' central claim is that this gap is attributable to the geometry of the solutions found by the respective methods. They posit that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers of the loss function, while small-batch (SB) methods, aided by gradient noise, are able to locate "flat" minimizers. The paper supports this claim with a series of numerical experiments across six different network architectures. The evidence includes 1D visualizations of the loss landscape between SB and LB solutions and a novel, computationally efficient metric proposed to quantify the sharpness of a minimizer. The authors also present experiments exploring the dynamics of SB methods and briefly discuss the limited success of several strategies (e.g., data augmentation, robust training) aimed at improving LB training.

**2. Decision: Reject**

While the paper addresses an important and relevant problem in deep learning optimization and presents an intuitive central hypothesis, I recommend rejection. The decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant Methodological Ambiguity and Lack of Rigor:** The paper's core quantitative evidence relies on a novel sharpness metric that is poorly motivated, unclearly defined, and dependent on arbitrary hyperparameters. This lack of rigor in the central measurement tool undermines the credibility of the experimental conclusions.
2.  **Numerous Technical and Formatting Flaws:** The manuscript is replete with errors, ranging from notational inconsistencies and grammatical mistakes to a demonstrably incorrect numerical example in the appendix. The sheer volume of these minor flaws suggests a lack of careful preparation and calls the overall reliability of the work into question.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's interesting premise is unfortunately let down by a pattern of imprecision and error.

*   **Critique of the Proposed Sharpness Metric:** The quantitative backbone of the paper is Metric 2.1, but its formulation is problematic.
    *   The definition of the constraint set *C* in Equation (3) is highly unusual and its motivation is absent. The size of the search box is made dependent on the solution `x` itself through the `(A+x)i` term, but the authors provide no justification for this specific, non-standard choice. The notation `(A+x)i` is itself ambiguous.
    *   The maximization subproblem in Equation (4) is solved inexactly with a fixed number of L-BFGS-B iterations (10). This makes the sharpness metric dependent on an arbitrary hyperparameter. There is no analysis to show that the results are insensitive to this choice or that 10 iterations are sufficient for the optimizer to reveal the local sharpness. The conclusions could potentially be an artifact of this experimental design choice.
    *   The authors' claim that the metric "relates to the largest eigenvalue" or "approximates the Ritz value" is stated without formal proof or citation, making it a hand-wavy assertion rather than a grounded connection to established theory.

*   **Insufficient Experimental Detail and Unsupported Claims:**
    *   The training protocol is not reproducible. The stopping criterion is vaguely stated as "until the loss function ceased to improve," which is not a concrete or standard condition.
    *   A key claim in Section 2.2.2 states that the loss function rises steeply "only along a small dimensional subspace (e.g. 5% of the whole space)." This is a powerful statement, but it is presented without any empirical evidence or description of the methodology used to arrive at this specific percentage.

*   **Demonstrable Errors and Inconsistencies:**
    *   The performance model in Appendix C contains a clear calculation error. With the given parameters (`fs(P) = 0.2`, `Bs/B` = 0.1`), the required iteration ratio `I_l/I_s` should be `< 0.02`. The authors incorrectly state it is `0.5` ("half as many iterations"). This fundamental error in a supporting analysis is a major concern.
    *   The manuscript suffers from numerous typos and formatting issues. For instance, there is a grammatical error in the introduction ("which and are often referred to"), a likely typo in "Figure 31", and conflicting references to "Figure 7" in Appendices D and E. The mathematical notation is frequently sloppy (e.g., `1 M` instead of `1/M`, undefined `x?s` notation).
    *   The reference list is unformatted and contains obvious errors, such as dating Srivastava et al. (2014) to "1929" and failing to distinguish between two "Goodfellow et al., 2014" papers cited as `a` and `b` in the text.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

Despite my recommendation, the core idea is valuable and could form the basis of a strong paper with substantial revision.

*   I strongly encourage the authors to develop a more principled sharpness metric. This could involve either a more thorough theoretical justification for the current metric or replacing it with a more standard approach from the optimization literature, perhaps adapted for computational feasibility. A sensitivity analysis regarding the metric's hyperparameters is essential.
*   For reproducibility, please provide complete experimental details, including precise stopping criteria, learning rates, and optimizer hyperparameters (`β1`, `β2`, `ε` for ADAM) for both SB and LB regimes.
*   The claims in Appendix E that various remedies do not solve the problem are interesting negative results. These would be more impactful if presented with the same rigor as the main experiments, including tables showing the test accuracy and sharpness values for each method.
*   The entire manuscript requires meticulous proofreading to correct all grammatical, notational, and formatting errors before resubmission.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide a detailed justification for the specific functional form of the constraint set `C` in Equation (3)? Why was this particular form chosen over simpler, more standard definitions of a neighborhood like an `l_∞` or `l_2` ball of a fixed radius?
2.  How do the reported sharpness values in Tables 3 and 4 change if the number of L-BFGS-B iterations is varied (e.g., to 5, 20, or 50)? Is it possible that the observed 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference is an artifact of the inexact maximization?
3.  Could you please elaborate on the methodology used to conclude that the minimizers are sharp only in a "5% subspace"? What analysis was performed to arrive at this number?
