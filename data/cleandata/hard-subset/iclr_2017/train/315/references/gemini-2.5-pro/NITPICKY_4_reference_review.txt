### Review

**Summary of Contributions:**

This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that deep learning models trained with large batches often exhibit poorer generalization performance compared to those trained with small batches, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors' central claim is that this "generalization gap" arises because large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers of the loss function, while small-batch (SB) methods, aided by gradient noise, find "flat" minimizers. The paper supports this claim through a series of numerical experiments across six different network architectures. The evidence includes 1-D parametric plots visualizing the loss landscape between LB and SB solutions, and a novel heuristic metric designed to quantify the "sharpness" of a minimizer. The authors also explore the behavior of iterates during training and conduct preliminary experiments on potential remedies, such as data augmentation and conservative training.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  The primary quantitative evidence supporting the paper's central claim rests on a novel "sharpness" metric (Metric 2.1) that is poorly justified and methodologically questionable. Its complex and unusual definition raises concerns about its validity and interpretability, undermining the core results presented in Tables 3 and 4.
2.  The manuscript suffers from a significant number of formatting errors, typographical mistakes, and instances of imprecision. While individually minor, their sheer quantity detracts from the paper's professionalism and erodes confidence in the meticulousness of the research itself.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core of this paper is the empirical connection between large batches and sharp minima. While the visualizations in Figure 3 are compelling, the quantitative argument hinges entirely on Metric 2.1. This metric is problematic for several reasons:

*   **Unclear Motivation for the Constraint Set `C`:** The definition of the constraint set in Equation (3) is highly unorthodox. The bounds `±(|(A+x)i|+ 1)` depend on the solution `x` itself. This makes it difficult to compare sharpness values across different points in the parameter space in a principled manner. Why is the size of the neighborhood in which sharpness is measured a function of the minimizer's location? Furthermore, the addition of the constant `1` is arbitrary and unexplained. This non-standard definition requires a much stronger justification than is provided.
*   **Inexact Maximization:** The maximization problem within the sharpness metric is solved inexactly using only 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B. Given the non-convexity of the function `f`, it is unclear if 10 iterations are sufficient to find a meaningful local maximum. The reported sharpness values could be artifacts of this highly approximate inner-loop optimization, rather than true measures of the landscape's sensitivity.
*   **Unsupported Claims:** The paper concludes Section 2.2.2 with the claim that the function rises steeply "only along a small dimensional subspace (e.g. 5% of the whole space)". This is a specific and interesting claim, but it is presented without any supporting methodology or evidence. How was this 5% figure determined? This unsubstantiated statement is symptomatic of a lack of rigor in the presentation of the results.

Beyond the central methodological issue, the paper's presentation is unacceptably careless for a top-tier conference submission. The accumulation of minor errors suggests a rushed preparation process.

*   **Formatting and Citation Errors:** The reference list is riddled with errors that appear to stem from a malfunctioning BibTeX compilation. For example, entries include extraneous characters like `\.,? \Q2016\E,`. The citation for Srivastava et al.'s dropout paper is incorrectly listed as 1929. The reference for LeCun et al. (1998) appears twice for two different papers. Such errors are easily caught with careful proofreading.
*   **Typographical and Grammatical Errors:** The text contains numerous typos and grammatical mistakes, such as "which and are often referred to as" (Section 1), "This phenomenon manifest themselves" (Section 2.2), "Appedix E" (Section 2.2.2), "Such and algorithm" (Appendix C), "which a MaxPool(2)" (Appendix B.4), and "co-ordinate descent" (Appendix E.2).
*   **Inconsistent and Confusing References:** In Section 2.2.1, the text refers to "Figure 7 in Appendix D" for curvilinear plots. Later, in Appendix E.2, the text refers to results being presented in "Figure 7". However, the provided manuscript does not seem to contain a figure with this number in Appendix E. This makes the paper difficult to follow.

While the research question is important and the high-level hypothesis is plausible, the execution and presentation fall short of the standards expected for ICLR. The reliance on a questionable metric and the pervasive lack of polish in the manuscript make it impossible to confidently accept the paper's conclusions.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   The core idea of linking batch size to the geometry of the found minima is valuable. To strengthen the paper, the sharpness metric needs to be either rigorously justified or replaced with a more standard and interpretable measure. For example, you could use approximations of the leading eigenvalue of the Hessian (e.g., via the Lanczos method) or analyze the trace of the Hessian, which can be estimated stochastically.
*   The definition of "large batch" as 10% of the training data is ambiguous without providing the absolute dataset sizes. For instance, 10% of MNIST is ~6000, while 10% of ImageNet is >100,000. A table clearly mapping each network (C1, F2, etc.) to its dataset, training set size, SB size, and LB size would greatly improve clarity and reproducibility.
*   The manuscript requires a thorough proofreading pass to correct all formatting, citation, and grammatical errors. This is a critical step for ensuring the work is presented professionally.
*   The attempts to remedy the LB problem in Appendix E are interesting but feel preliminary. These could be strengthened with more analysis or perhaps be framed as future work to maintain focus in the main paper. The results for adversarial training were particularly surprising and could be elaborated on.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you please provide a detailed justification for the specific functional form of the constraint set `C` in Metric 2.1, particularly the dependence on `x` and the addition of `1`? Have you considered how this metric behaves under simple transformations, such as scaling the network weights?
2.  How did you arrive at the conclusion that the loss function near an LB solution is sharp only in a "small dimensional subspace (e.g. 5%)"? Could you describe the experiment that led to this specific quantitative claim?
