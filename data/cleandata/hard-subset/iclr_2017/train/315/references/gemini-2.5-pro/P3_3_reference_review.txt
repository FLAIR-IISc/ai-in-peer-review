### Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the widely observed empirical phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large batch sizes often leads to poorer generalization compared to small-batch training, even when both achieve similar training loss. The authors' central contribution is to provide compelling evidence for the hypothesis that this "generalization gap" is caused by the different characteristics of the minima found by each training regime. Specifically, they argue and demonstrate that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, which are located in steep, narrow valleys of the loss landscape. In contrast, small-batch (SB) methods, aided by the inherent noise in their gradient estimates, are able to escape these sharp basins and find "flat" minimizers, which lie in wide, flatter regions. The paper posits that these flat minimizers generalize better. The authors support their claims with a thorough set of experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets, using two primary forms of evidence: 1) 1-D visualizations of the loss landscape between LB and SB solutions, and 2) a novel, computationally feasible metric for quantifying the "sharpness" of a minimizer.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Clear Hypothesis with Strong Empirical Support:** The paper tackles a significant and practical problem in deep learning. Its central hypothesis—that the generalization gap is explained by convergence to sharp vs. flat minima—is clear, intuitive, and rigorously tested. The empirical evidence provided, from the parametric plots to the proposed sharpness metric, is extensive and consistently supports the main claim across a variety of settings.

2.  **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The experimental design is sound. The authors carefully control for confounding factors, such as ensuring that the generalization gap is not due to simple overfitting. The introduction of a quantitative sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is a valuable contribution, providing a practical tool for analyzing the geometry of learned solutions that is more tractable than direct Hessian analysis for large models.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its focused and methodical investigation.

First, the problem formulation is excellent. The authors clearly distinguish the observed generalization gap from classical overfitting, noting that early stopping would not remedy the issue (Figure 2). This correctly frames the problem as one of optimization dynamics and the quality of the resulting solution, rather than simply a matter of training duration.

Second, the empirical evidence is convincing. The 1-D parametric plots (Figure 3) provide a powerful qualitative visualization of the core claim. The observation that the basin around the LB solution is dramatically sharper than the one around the SB solution is striking. While this is only a 1-D slice of a high-dimensional space, the authors strengthen this by also considering a random subspace projection for their sharpness metric.

The proposed sharpness metric itself is a key strength. Characterizing the local geometry of solutions for high-dimensional models is notoriously difficult. Direct computation of the Hessian is infeasible. The proposed metric, which measures the maximal increase in loss within a small neighborhood, is an elegant and practical proxy. The fact that this metric shows a 1-2 order of magnitude difference between SB and LB solutions (Tables 3 and 4) provides strong quantitative backing for the visual intuition from the parametric plots.

Finally, the analysis in Section 3 and Appendix E adds significant depth. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) is particularly insightful. It suggests that SB training performs an "exploration" phase to locate a good basin (a flat one), after which LB training can effectively "exploit" that basin to converge quickly. This provides a nuanced view of the role of gradient noise. Furthermore, the investigation of potential remedies like data augmentation and conservative training, even though they were only partially successful, demonstrates scientific diligence and helps delineate the boundaries of the problem.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further improve it.

*   **Sensitivity of the Sharpness Metric:** The sharpness metric in Equation 4 depends on several hyperparameters, notably `epsilon` and the number of optimization steps (10 iterations of L-BFGS-B) used to find the maximum. The choice of 10 iterations seems quite low for a non-convex maximization problem and might lead to underestimation of the true sharpness. It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion or ablation study on the sensitivity of the results to these choices to bolster confidence in the reported sharpness values.
*   **Role of the Optimizer:** The experiments exclusively use ADAM. While the authors state that other optimizers yielded similar results, ADAM's adaptive per-parameter learning rates might interact with the loss landscape's curvature in a unique way. A brief comparison with a non-adaptive method like SGD with momentum in one of the experiments could provide valuable insight into whether this phenomenon is optimizer-agnostic or if adaptivity plays a specific role in seeking out sharp minima.
*   **Connection to Hessian Eigenvalues:** The paper correctly notes the connection between sharpness and the Hessian's eigenvalues. It would be very compelling to supplement the proposed metric with a direct estimation of the top eigenvalue of the Hessian (e.g., via the Lanczos or power method), at least for one of the smaller models where this is computationally feasible. This would serve as a powerful validation of the proposed sharpness metric and directly link the findings to the formal definition of curvature.
*   **Bringing Appendix E Forward:** The experiments in Appendix E, which explore potential solutions, are valuable "negative" or "partially successful" results that significantly strengthen the paper's conclusions. The main paper would benefit from a more prominent summary of these findings, perhaps in the Discussion section, as they effectively rule out several simple fixes and motivate the need for more fundamental solutions.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of using only 10 iterations for the inner maximization loop in your sharpness metric? Have you investigated whether using more iterations changes the qualitative conclusions, i.e., does the relative gap in sharpness between LB and SB methods remain consistent?
2.  Your warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) is very insightful. It suggests a hybrid approach where one starts with SB and switches to LB. Have you experimented with a simple schedule like this as a potential remedy? Does it successfully combine the exploration of SB with the speed of LB while achieving good generalization?
3.  The paper focuses on the final converged solutions. Did you observe any differences in the *trajectory* of the sharpness metric during training? For example, do LB iterates immediately enter a sharp region and stay there, while SB iterates explore regions of varying sharpness before settling in a flat one, as suggested by Figure 6?
