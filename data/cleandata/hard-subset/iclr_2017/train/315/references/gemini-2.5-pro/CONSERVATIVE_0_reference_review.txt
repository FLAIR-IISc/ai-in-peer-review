### Review

**Summary of the paper**

This paper investigates the commonly observed "generalization gap," where deep learning models trained with large mini-batches exhibit poorer test performance than those trained with small mini-batches, despite achieving comparable training loss. The authors' central hypothesis is that this phenomenon is due to the nature of the minimizers found by the different training regimes. They posit that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, characterized by high sensitivity to perturbations in the parameter space, while small-batch (SB) methods, aided by the noise in the gradient estimates, are able to locate "flat" minimizers that generalize better. The paper supports this claim with extensive numerical experiments across six different network architectures and four datasets. The evidence presented includes 1D visualizations of the loss landscape and a novel, computationally feasible metric proposed by the authors to quantify the sharpness of a minimizer. The results consistently show that LB solutions are located in significantly sharper regions of the loss surface compared to their SB counterparts.

**Decision: Reject**

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **The paper's central claims rest on a new, unproven heuristic for measuring sharpness.** The proposed metric (Metric 2.1) is not adequately justified or validated against established measures of curvature, such as the Hessian spectrum. Its behavior is dependent on an inexact optimization procedure and choices of hyperparameters that are not thoroughly analyzed, making the quantitative conclusions less reliable.
2.  **The work establishes a strong correlation but fails to provide conclusive evidence of a causal link between sharpness and poor generalization.** While the experiments are compelling, they remain observational. The inconclusive results of the remedy attempts in the appendix, particularly the case where data augmentation closes the generalization gap without reducing sharpness, challenge the main hypothesis that sharpness is the primary causal factor.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper tackles an important and practical problem in deep learning. The empirical investigation is comprehensive, and the visualizations in Figure 3 provide a clear, intuitive illustration of the authors' hypothesis. The experiments comparing the distance traveled from initialization and the warm-starting procedure provide interesting insights into the differing dynamics of LB and SB methods.

However, the methodological foundations of the quantitative analysis are questionable. The paper's main evidence beyond the 1D plots comes from Metric 2.1, a novel measure of sharpness. As a reviewer who values methodological soundness and established techniques, I find several issues with this metric:

*   **Lack of Validation:** The metric is presented as a computationally feasible proxy for the local curvature (related to the Hessian's eigenvalues), but this connection is informal. A more rigorous evaluation, for instance by comparing the metric's output to the true maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian on a smaller model where it is tractable to compute, is necessary to validate it. Without this, it is difficult to be confident that the metric is not capturing some other artifact of the optimization process.
*   **Arbitrary Formulation and Hyperparameters:** The definition of the constraint set C in Eq. (3) is ad-hoc. The choice to scale the box size by `|(A+x)i|+ 1` is not well-motivated and seems unusual. Furthermore, the results depend on the choice of `epsilon`, and a sensitivity analysis of this crucial hyperparameter is missing. The maximization in Eq. (4) is performed inexactly with only 10 L-BFGS steps, which may not be sufficient and could introduce its own biases.
*   **Correlation vs. Causation:** The paper demonstrates that LB training is correlated with sharp minima and poor generalization. However, it does not rule out alternative hypotheses. For example, it is possible that the noise in SB-SGD acts as a powerful regularizer, and finding a flat minimum is a byproduct of this regularization, not the direct cause of good generalization itself. The investigation in Appendix E, while commendable, casts further doubt on the causal claim. The finding that data augmentation can close the generalization gap while the LB solutions remain sharp is particularly problematic for the central thesis. If sharpness is the cause, why does a solution that is still sharp now generalize well? This suggests that sharpness, at least as measured by Metric 2.1, may be a symptom rather than the root cause.

While the paper provides a good characterization of an interesting phenomenon, it reads more like an empirical report with a hypothesis than a paper presenting a rigorously tested theory. The lack of a successful remedy based on the hypothesis further weakens its standing. For a venue like ICLR, a higher standard of methodological rigor is expected, especially when the core claims depend on a newly proposed measurement.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   I would encourage the authors to provide a more thorough justification and analysis of their sharpness metric. A section exploring its theoretical properties or, at minimum, an empirical validation against the Hessian spectrum on a smaller problem would significantly strengthen the paper's main argument.
*   The results in Appendix E are very interesting and should perhaps be integrated more centrally into the paper's narrative. The apparent contradiction raised by the data augmentation experiment warrants a deeper discussion in the main text, as it directly impacts the interpretation of the paper's core claim.
*   The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) is one of the most compelling pieces of evidence. Expanding on this could be fruitful. For example, what happens if the process is reversed (warm-starting SB with an LB solution)?

I would like to ask the authors the following questions to clarify my understanding:

1.  Regarding Metric 2.1, could you provide the rationale behind the formulation of the constraint set C, particularly the `+1` term and the dependency on the weights x? Have you experimented with a simpler constraint, such as a hypercube of fixed side length centered at x, and do the conclusions still hold?
2.  Your sharpness calculation relies on an inexact maximization. Is it possible that the optimization landscape around LB solutions is "easier" to maximize within a few steps than around SB solutions, thus artificially inflating the measured sharpness for LB methods?
3.  How do you interpret the result that data augmentation closes the generalization gap for LB methods but leaves the minima "sharp"? Does this suggest that the proposed sharpness metric is not capturing the geometric property that is truly relevant for generalization in this case?
