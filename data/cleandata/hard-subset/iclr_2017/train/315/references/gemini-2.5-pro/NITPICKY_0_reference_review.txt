### **ICLR Review**

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the widely observed empirical phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large mini-batches often leads to poorer generalization compared to small-batch training, even when both achieve similar training loss. The authors' main contribution is to provide substantial numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that this "generalization gap" is due to the different types of minima found by the respective training regimes. Specifically, the paper claims that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, characterized by large eigenvalues of the Hessian, while small-batch (SB) methods are guided by gradient noise toward "flat" minimizers. The authors support this claim with extensive experiments across six different network architectures. They visualize the loss landscape using 1D parametric plots and introduce a novel, computationally feasible metric to quantify the sharpness of a minimum. The work provides a compelling empirical characterization of a significant problem in large-scale deep learning.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The central quantitative evidence of the paper relies on a novel sharpness metric that is poorly defined, methodologically questionable, and potentially flawed. Ambiguities in its formulation and execution cast significant doubt on the reliability of the core numerical results presented in Tables 3 and 4.
2.  The paper is replete with formatting errors, incorrect citations, typographical mistakes, and a demonstrably flawed analysis in an appendix. This pervasive lack of attention to detail undermines confidence in the scientific rigor of the work as a whole and makes it fall short of the quality standards expected for publication at ICLR.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's core thesis is compelling, but its execution is plagued by a concerning number of minor and major issues that, in aggregate, warrant rejection.

1.  **Flaws in the Proposed Sharpness Metric (Metric 2.1):** The paper's quantitative argument hinges on this metric, but its formulation is problematic.
    *   **Equation (3) is ill-defined:** The constraint set `C` is defined as `{z ∈ Rp : − (|(A+x)i|+ 1) ≤ zi ≤ (|(A+x)i|+ 1)}`. This definition is highly unconventional. First, it appears to be missing the parameter `ε`, which is a critical component of the subsequent metric definition in Equation (4) and the discussion. Without `ε`, the size of the box is not controlled by a small perturbation value. Second, the size of the box for each dimension `i` depends on the value of `(A+x)i`. This dependency is not justified and is unusual; it means the shape of the search neighborhood changes depending on the solution `x`. A standard approach would be a simple norm ball, e.g., `||z||_∞ <= ε`. The term `+ 1` is also arbitrary and unexplained.
    *   **Inexact Maximization:** The maximization in Equation (4) is solved with only "10 iterations of L-BFGS-B". This is a very rough approximation for a non-convex optimization subproblem. The reported sharpness values could be highly sensitive to this choice. The paper provides no analysis of how the results might change with more iterations, leaving the reader to question whether the reported values are artifacts of an incomplete search.
    *   **Denominator in Equation (4):** The metric is normalized by `ε + f(x)`. Normalizing by the function value `f(x)` can be problematic, as cross-entropy loss can approach zero, potentially making the metric unstable. A justification for this specific normalization choice is missing.

2.  **Pervasive Lack of Care in Presentation:** While minor typos can be forgiven, their sheer volume here suggests a hastily prepared manuscript.
    *   **Incorrect Citations:** The reference to Srivastava et al. for Dropout is dated "1929", which is clearly incorrect. The bibliography is riddled with formatting errors (e.g., "Bengio et al\.,? \Q2016\E"), indicating a failure to properly compile or check the references.
    *   **Typographical Errors:** Numerous typos detract from the paper's professionalism, such as "Appedix E" instead of "Appendix E", a reference to "Figure 31" which should be "Figure 3", and grammatical errors like "which and are often referred to as...".
    *   **Flawed Performance Model:** The performance model in Appendix C contains a significant error. The inequality `I` B` P < Is Bs Pfs(P )` is presented. For the time-to-solution analysis, the number of processors `P` should be in the denominator of the total time calculation. This error invalidates the subsequent derivation and conclusion, reflecting a lack of rigor in the theoretical justification for the work's importance.

3.  **Inconsistencies in Experimental Conclusions:** In Appendix E.1, data augmentation is shown to significantly reduce the generalization gap (Table 6). However, the authors state that "the sharpness of the minima still exists". This finding potentially contradicts the paper's main thesis. If the generalization gap can be closed without eliminating sharpness (as measured by their metric), it implies that either (a) sharpness is not the primary cause of the gap, or (b) the proposed sharpness metric is not a reliable indicator of the geometric properties that lead to poor generalization. This crucial tension is not addressed.

While the paper's overarching idea is valuable and has since become influential, this specific manuscript, in its current form, does not meet the standards for publication due to the combination of a questionable core methodology and a profound lack of scholarly diligence.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The definition of the sharpness metric must be clarified and rigorously justified. I recommend using a more standard definition, such as the maximum value in an L2 or L-infinity ball of radius `ε` around the solution, and providing a clearer explanation for any normalization factors.
*   The maximization procedure for computing the sharpness needs to be more thorough. A sensitivity analysis showing the effect of the number of optimization steps (e.g., 10 vs. 100 vs. 1000) on the final sharpness value would significantly strengthen the results.
*   The entire manuscript requires meticulous proofreading to correct all typographical, grammatical, and citation errors. The bibliography must be properly formatted.
*   The analysis in Appendix E should be brought into the main paper and expanded upon. The potential conflict between the results of data augmentation and the main hypothesis needs to be discussed and reconciled explicitly.
*   It would be beneficial to include results with a non-adaptive optimizer like SGD with momentum to delink the findings from the specific behavior of ADAM, which might influence the landscape geometry through its adaptive preconditioning.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you please provide the exact, corrected formulation for the constraint set C in Equation (3)? What is the theoretical or empirical justification for making the search neighborhood's size dependent on the solution `x`?
2.  How do the reported sharpness values in Tables 3 and 4 change if the number of L-BFGS-B iterations for the inner maximization problem is increased from 10 to a much larger number, for instance, 100 or until convergence?
3.  Regarding Appendix E.1: If data augmentation closes the generalization gap while the minima remain "sharp" according to your metric, how do you reconcile this with your central claim that sharpness is the primary cause of the generalization gap? Does this not suggest a potential decoupling of the two phenomena?
