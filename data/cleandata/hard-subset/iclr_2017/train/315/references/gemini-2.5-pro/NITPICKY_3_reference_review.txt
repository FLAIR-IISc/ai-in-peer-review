### **Review**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that deep learning models trained with large batches often exhibit poorer generalization performance compared to those trained with small batches. The authors' primary contribution is to present compelling numerical evidence linking this "generalization gap" to the geometry of the loss landscape. They posit that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, which are characterized by high sensitivity to perturbations in the parameter space. In contrast, they argue that the stochastic noise inherent in small-batch (SB) methods enables them to find "flat" minimizers, which are more robust and generalize better. To support this claim, the paper provides 1D visualizations of the loss function between SB and LB solutions and introduces a novel heuristic metric to quantify sharpness. The experiments span multiple network architectures and datasets, consistently showing that LB solutions are orders of magnitude sharper than their SB counterparts.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  The central quantitative claims of the paper rely on a novel but ad-hoc and poorly justified metric for "sharpness." The mathematical definition of this metric is ambiguous and appears to contain significant typographical errors (Equation 4), and the construction of the neighborhood for evaluation (Equation 3) is non-standard and lacks theoretical grounding.
2.  The manuscript is beset by a high density of typographical, formatting, and referencing errors, which severely detract from its scientific credibility and suggest a lack of careful preparation. These errors range from malformed equations and grammatical mistakes to incorrect citations and figure cross-references.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

My decision to recommend rejection, despite the paper's interesting premise, is based on a meticulous examination of its methodological and presentational details.

**Methodological Weaknesses:**

*   **The Sharpness Metric (Metric 2.1) is Problematic:** The paper's core evidence rests on this metric. However, its definition is fragile.
    *   Equation (3), which defines the constraint set `C`, is highly unconventional. The size of the search box is made dependent on the solution `x` via `|(A+x)i|`. The authors' claim that this ensures "invariance... to problem dimension and sparsity" is presented without any justification or proof, leaving the reader to question its validity. A standard `l_inf` or `l_2` ball would be a more conventional and interpretable choice.
    *   Equation (4) is presented with a critical typographical error in the provided text: `(max...)- f(x) / f(x) + f(x) × 100`. This expression is mathematically nonsensical. Assuming it is intended to be a normalized difference, e.g., `(max f - f(x)) / |f(x)|`, this should be stated clearly and correctly. The ambiguity undermines the reproducibility and credibility of the results in Tables 3 and 4.
    *   The maximization within the metric is performed inexactly using only 10 iterations of L-BFGS-B. This is a crucial detail. The reported sharpness value is therefore a function of this arbitrary hyperparameter. It is plausible that the optimizer is simply more effective at making progress in steep regions within a fixed iteration budget, making the measurement itself an artifact rather than a pure property of the minimum.

*   **Lack of Experimental Precision:**
    *   The training termination condition is described as "until the loss function ceased to improve." This is too vague for a rigorous scientific study. A precise criterion (e.g., patience on a validation set, gradient norm threshold) is necessary to ensure that the SB and LB methods are compared at equivalent stages of convergence.
    *   The choice of LB size as 10% of the training data is an extreme setting. While Figure 4 shows results for intermediate sizes, the main quantitative results in the tables only compare the two extremes (256 vs. 10%), which may exaggerate the observed effect.

**Presentational Flaws:**

The manuscript suffers from a distracting number of errors that should have been caught in proofreading.
*   **Formatting Errors:** Equations (1) and (2) incorrectly use spaces instead of slashes for division (e.g., `1 M` instead of `1/M`). There are grammatical errors, such as in the sentence "...which and are often referred to as...".
*   **Citation Errors:** The reference list appears to be corrupted by BibTeX parsing errors, with entries like `Bengio et al\.,? \Q2016\E`. More alarmingly, the reference for Srivastava et al. on Dropout is listed with the year 1929, a clear and significant error.
*   **Incorrect Cross-Referencing:** Section 2.2.1 refers to "Figure 31" which is a typo for Figure 3. Appendix E.2 refers to Figure 7 for results on conservative training, but Figure 7 in Appendix D contains curvilinear plots. These errors make the paper difficult to follow and verify.

While any single one of these points might be considered minor, their sheer number collectively creates an impression of haste and a lack of scholarly diligence, which is unacceptable for a top-tier conference like ICLR.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The core hypothesis of this paper is valuable and has since become influential in the field. The work is certainly worth refining for a future submission.
*   The sharpness metric needs to be put on a more solid footing. Please provide a detailed justification for the design of Equation (3) or replace it with a standard definition of a neighborhood. Please correct and clarify Equation (4). Furthermore, analyze the sensitivity of the measured sharpness to the number of iterations used in the inner maximization procedure.
*   Strengthen the connection to established theory. While the link to Hessian eigenvalues is mentioned, it would be beneficial to compute these directly for a smaller model where it is feasible, to validate that your proposed metric indeed correlates with the maximum eigenvalue.
*   A thorough and comprehensive proofreading of the entire manuscript is essential. Correcting all typographical, grammatical, and referencing errors will significantly improve the paper's quality and reception.
*   The experimental section should use precise language, particularly for the stopping conditions.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To better assess your work, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:
1.  Could you provide a theoretical motivation for the specific form of the constraint set `C` in Equation (3), particularly its dependence on the solution `x`? How does this definition lead to the claimed invariance properties?
2.  What is the correct mathematical expression for the sharpness metric in Equation (4)?
3.  Could you provide the exact stopping criteria used for training all models? Specifically, how was it determined that the loss function had "ceased to improve"?
4.  You argue that the generalization gap is not due to standard overfitting. Was the test accuracy monitored throughout training, and did you ever observe it decrease after peaking for either SB or LB methods if training was allowed to continue for an extended period?
