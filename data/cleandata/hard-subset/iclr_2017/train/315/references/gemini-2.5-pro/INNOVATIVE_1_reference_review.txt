### **Review**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large batches results in poorer generalization compared to small-batch training, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors' central thesis is that this "generalization gap" is caused by the different geometric properties of the minima found by the two training regimes. Specifically, they posit that large-batch (LB) methods converge to "sharp" minimizers, which are sensitive to perturbations in the weight space, while the stochastic noise inherent in small-batch (SB) methods enables them to find "flat" minimizers, which are more robust and generalize better.

To support this claim, the paper presents a compelling empirical study across a variety of network architectures and datasets. The evidence is threefold:
1.  **Visualization:** 1D parametric plots along the line connecting SB and LB solutions vividly illustrate that the loss landscape is significantly sharper around the LB minimum.
2.  **A Novel Sharpness Metric:** The authors propose a computationally feasible metric to quantify sharpness by measuring the maximum loss value in a small neighborhood around a solution. This metric consistently shows that LB solutions are one to two orders of magnitude sharper than SB solutions.
3.  **Analysis of Optimizer Trajectories:** Experiments demonstrate that SB methods explore more of the parameter space and that LB methods, even when warm-started with an SB solution, can fail to retain good generalization unless the SB method has already found a flat basin.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper is a clear accept. It provides a simple, powerful, and empirically well-grounded explanation for a critical and vexing problem in deep learning.

1.  **Significant Conceptual Contribution:** The paper offers a highly intuitive and valuable conceptual framework—the dichotomy between sharp and flat minima—to understand the practical trade-offs of batch size selection. This is not an incremental improvement but a fundamental insight into the optimization landscape and its relationship with generalization. It has the potential to reframe how the community approaches large-scale training.

2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Evidence:** The central claim is backed by a series of clear, well-designed experiments. The visualizations are striking, and the proposed sharpness metric, while a heuristic, provides compelling quantitative evidence for a qualitative difference between the solutions found by large- and small-batch methods.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary strength of this paper is its boldness and clarity in tackling a well-known but poorly understood issue. While the concepts of sharp and flat minima are not new, this work is one of the first to so directly and convincingly link them to the batch size dilemma in modern deep learning. The investigation is thorough, covering multiple architectures (FC, AlexNet-like, VGG-like) and datasets, which lends credibility to the generality of the findings.

The paper excels in its scientific storytelling. The argument is built layer by layer, starting with the baseline observation of the generalization gap, moving to visual intuition via parametric plots, and then solidifying the claim with the proposed sharpness metric. The analysis in Section 3, particularly the warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) and the plot of sharpness versus loss (Figure 6), provides insightful evidence for the different behaviors of SB and LB optimizers—namely, that SB methods perform a crucial exploration phase to escape sharp regions that initially attract optimizers.

While the proposed sharpness metric is not a perfect measure (e.g., it relies on an inexact maximization), it is a pragmatic and clever choice for models where Hessian computation is intractable. The fact that it shows such a consistent and dramatic difference between the two regimes makes it a valuable tool and a strong piece of evidence. This is a perfect example of prioritizing a novel direction over methodological purity, leading to significant new insights. The attempts to remedy the problem in the appendix, though preliminary, further strengthen the paper by showing that the issue is non-trivial and not solved by standard regularization techniques.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.
*   The sharpness metric defined in Equation 4 uses a constraint set `C` that depends on the magnitude of the weights `x`. This is an interesting choice, but the motivation could be explained more clearly. A brief discussion comparing this choice to a simpler L-infinity ball would be beneficial.
*   The experiments rely heavily on the ADAM optimizer. While the authors state that other optimizers produced similar results, including a key result (e.g., a sharpness vs. loss plot for one network) for SGD with momentum in the appendix would make the paper's claims even more robust.
*   The observation that the function is sharp only along a low-dimensional subspace is fascinating. This is a powerful insight that is mentioned almost as an aside. If possible, a brief experiment or visualization supporting this could make the paper even more impactful. For instance, could the "sharp" direction found by the inexact maximization be used to show this property?

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**
*   In your warm-starting experiment (Figure 5), the LB method begins to achieve good generalization only after the SB method has run for a sufficient number of epochs. Have you investigated whether this transition point correlates with any specific event in the SB training, such as a learning rate drop or the point where the training loss begins to plateau?
*   You mention that adversarial training did not improve generalization for LB methods. This is somewhat surprising, as adversarial training is often thought to encourage robustness and could plausibly lead to flatter solutions. Could you elaborate on this finding? Did adversarial training also fail to reduce the sharpness metric for the LB solutions?
