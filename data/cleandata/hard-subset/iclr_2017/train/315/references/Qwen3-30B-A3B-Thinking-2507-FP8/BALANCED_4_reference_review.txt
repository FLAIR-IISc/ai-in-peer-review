

# Review

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions
The paper investigates the well-documented generalization gap between small-batch and large-batch training methods for deep learning. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity of the loss function in certain directions), while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers with better generalization properties. They introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify this phenomenon and present numerical evidence across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, and fully-connected networks) on various datasets. The paper also explores several attempts to mitigate this problem, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training, though these approaches are only partially successful.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper makes an interesting empirical observation but lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and has significant methodological concerns that undermine the strength of its claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision
The paper's main contribution is descriptive rather than explanatory, failing to provide the theoretical foundation needed to support its claims. While the empirical evidence is generally well-presented, several critical issues undermine the paper's scientific rigor:

First, the paper lacks theoretical justification for its main claim. The authors acknowledge they provide only "numerical evidence" rather than theoretical proof that large-batch methods typically converge to sharp minimizers (Section 4). Without theoretical analysis, the paper cannot establish causality between batch size and sharpness, leaving open the possibility of alternative explanations for the observed generalization gap.

Second, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is poorly justified. The authors claim it relates to the Hessian eigenvalues but provide no rigorous connection or analysis of how well it approximates the true Hessian-based sharpness measure. The metric's dependence on arbitrary parameters (ε, A, and the number of L-BFGS-B iterations) is not thoroughly explored, making it unclear whether the observed differences in sharpness are robust to these choices.

Third, the experimental design contains significant inconsistencies. The "large-batch" size is defined as 10% of the training set, which varies substantially across datasets (e.g., 5,000 for CIFAR-10, 10,000 for TIMIT). This is not comparable to standard large-batch settings in the literature (typically 1,024-4,096 samples). The comparison between "small-batch" (256 samples) and "large-batch" (10% of training set) methods is therefore not a fair comparison of small vs. large batch sizes.

Finally, the paper's claim that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting (Section 2.2) is not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The training-testing curves in Figure 2 do not clearly distinguish between overfitting and sharpness-related phenomena, and the authors provide no statistical analysis to confirm their interpretation.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

- Provide theoretical analysis explaining why large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers. This could include a simple analysis of how batch size affects the convergence properties of the optimization algorithm.
- Rigorously justify the sharpness metric by establishing its relationship to the Hessian eigenvalues and analyzing how its parameters affect the results.
- Standardize the batch size comparison using fixed batch sizes (e.g., 256, 1024, 4096) rather than percentage-based batch sizes that vary across datasets.
- Include more detailed statistical analysis of the sharpness differences between small and large batches, including confidence intervals and significance tests.
- Clarify the relationship between their sharpness metric and existing measures in the literature (e.g., Chaudhari et al.'s entropy-SGD).

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How do you justify the choice of ε = 10^-3 and 5×10^-4 in your sharpness metric (Equation 4)? Would different choices of ε affect your conclusions about the sharpness difference between small and large batches?
2. The paper claims that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers, but doesn't provide evidence of convergence to these minimizers. Can you provide evidence that large-batch methods actually converge to these sharp minimizers rather than just reaching a point with high sharpness at some intermediate iteration?
3. How do your results compare to recent work by Li et al. (2018) on the relationship between batch size and sharpness, which found that larger batch sizes can lead to flatter minima under certain conditions?
4. The paper states that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting, but the training-testing curves in Figure 2 don't show the typical overfitting pattern. Can you provide more evidence to distinguish between the sharpness explanation and the overfitting explanation?
5. How does the sharpness of minimizers vary with the learning rate, and how does this interaction with batch size affect generalization performance?
