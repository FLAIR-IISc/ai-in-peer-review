- Paper studies the large-batch vs small-batch generalization gap and claims it arises because large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minima while small-batch methods find "flat" minima.  
- Decision: Reject — interesting empirical findings but insufficient evidence for causation between sharpness and poor generalization.  
- Empirical results (parametric plots, proposed sharpness metric, tables) show a clear correlation: large-batch converges to sharper minima.  
- The proposed sharpness metric is not rigorously connected to Hessian eigenvalues, undermining its claim to measure standard sharpness.  
- Authors' dismissal of overfitting as the cause is not well supported and lacks theoretical justification.  
- Warm-start experiments show SB iterates improve LB generalization but do not prove sharpness is the causal factor.  
- The paper needs a theoretical argument explaining why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima rather than only empirical evidence.  
- The sharpness metric should be validated against actual Hessian eigenvalues on small networks to establish correspondence.  
- A more rigorous overfitting analysis is required (e.g., training vs testing loss at convergence, capacity analysis).  
- The paper should explicitly consider alternative explanations for the correlation (e.g., landscape prevalence or initialization effects).  
- Broaden experiments to additional architectures (e.g., ResNets, transformers) to test generality beyond AlexNet/VGG variants.  
- Questions for authors: does Equation 4 correlate with Hessian eigenvalues; why is overfitting implausible; does initialization determine sharpness; does SB noise actively push toward flatter regions?
