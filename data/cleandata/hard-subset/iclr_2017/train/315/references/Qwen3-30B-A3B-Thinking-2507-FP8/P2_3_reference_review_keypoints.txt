- Paper claims large-batch training converges to "sharp" minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch finds "flat" minima, explaining the generalization gap, supported by 1D plots, a sharpness metric, and noise-based analysis.

- Decision: Reject, with strong recommendation to resubmit after addressing major concerns.

- Main rejection reasons: central sharp-vs-flat claim is insufficiently supported and the sharpness metric lacks theoretical grounding.

- Sharpness metric (Eq.4) is poorly justified; no demonstrated correlation with actual Hessian spectrum in deep networks.

- Confounding factors (number of iterations, learning rate schedules) are not properly controlled and may explain the generalization gap instead.

- Interpretation of "sharp" vs. "flat" minima is problematic since experiments rely on a single metric and do not directly analyze Hessian eigenvalues.

- Experimental design issues: "large-batch" sizes (~10% of data) are unrealistically large and not representative of practical large-batch regimes where the gap is observed.

- Paper omits comparisons to recent large-batch optimization methods (e.g., LAMB, LARS) that target this problem.

- Recommendation: provide theoretical grounding for the sharpness metric, including tests on models with computable Hessians.

- Recommendation: use more realistic batch sizes (e.g., 1,000–4,000) and proper learning-rate scaling across batch sizes.

- Recommendation: explicitly control for iterations and learning-rate schedules to avoid confounding batch-size effects.

- Recommendation: directly compute or approximate the Hessian spectrum at minima to validate sharpness claims.

- Recommendation: compare results with modern large-batch training methods to demonstrate practical relevance.

- Key questions for authors include: correlation of metric with Hessian eigenvalues; apparent contradiction about overfitting; why training accuracy matches but test differs; relation between distance from initialization and flatness; and whether learning-rate schedules explain the gap.
