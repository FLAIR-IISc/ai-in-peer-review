

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper addresses a well-documented practical issue in deep learning: the "generalization gap" observed when using large-batch training methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors make the compelling claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) that generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with many small eigenvalues) that generalize better. 

The paper provides multiple lines of empirical evidence to support this claim, including:
- Parametric 1D and curvilinear plots showing the sharpness difference between minimizers
- A computationally feasible sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) that measures sensitivity to small perturbations
- Warm-starting experiments demonstrating that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when started from small-batch solutions
- Exploration of potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training)

The contribution is significant because it provides a concrete explanation for a common practical observation in deep learning, potentially guiding future work on large-batch training that could dramatically improve scalability.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. 

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning that has been observed by practitioners but not well-explained theoretically.
2. The empirical evidence across multiple network architectures and experimental setups is robust and well-presented.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the generalization gap in large-batch training. The core observation—linking poor generalization to sharp minimizers—is well-supported by multiple experimental approaches.

The paper's strength lies in its comprehensive empirical investigation. The authors examine six different network configurations (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, fully-connected networks), use both 1D and curvilinear parametric plots, and employ a novel sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) that is computationally feasible for large networks. Their warm-starting experiments provide particularly convincing evidence that the issue is about the nature of the minimizer rather than the optimization algorithm itself.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, correctly citing relevant work on sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) and theoretical results on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016). The connection between their findings and these theoretical works is appropriately made.

While the paper doesn't provide theoretical guarantees (which would be difficult for such a complex problem), the empirical evidence is scientifically rigorous. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their sharpness metric (not being a precise measure of the Hessian spectrum) and demonstrate that their results are consistent across multiple experiments and network architectures. The fact that they show similar behavior across different networks and optimizers (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD) strengthens their claims.

The paper's contribution is particularly valuable because it provides a concrete explanation for a practical problem that affects real-world deep learning applications, potentially guiding future work on scalable training methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following:

1. **Clarify the relationship between sharpness and generalization:** While the paper assumes that sharp minima generalize poorly, it would be helpful to provide more direct evidence that the sharpness metric correlates with generalization performance across different models. For example, could the authors show that models with similar sharpness metrics but different generalization performance exist?

2. **Address the computational cost of the sharpness metric:** The paper mentions that computing the Hessian is prohibitive, but the sharpness metric itself requires multiple L-BFGS-B iterations. It would be valuable to quantify how much more expensive the sharpness metric is compared to standard training, as this affects the practicality of using it for model selection.

3. **Expand on the warm-starting experiments:** The warm-starting results are very promising. It would be helpful to explore how many small-batch epochs are needed before warm-starting large-batch methods becomes effective, and whether this number scales with network size or complexity.

4. **Consider the connection to other generalization theories:** The paper mentions MDL theory and Bayesian learning as alternative explanations. It would strengthen the argument to explicitly connect the sharpness metric to these theoretical frameworks.

5. **Include more details about the optimization process:** How does the sharpness metric change during training? Does it peak at the same time as the generalization gap?

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." Can you elaborate on how many directions exhibit sharp behavior (e.g., what percentage of the eigenvalues are large), and how this relates to the generalization performance?

2. The paper mentions that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training." However, the training and testing accuracy curves in Figure 2 show a difference between training and testing accuracy. Could you clarify whether this difference is related to the generalization gap (which is the difference between SB and LB methods) or is a separate phenomenon?

3. In the warm-starting experiments (Figure 5), it appears that the sharpness metric decreases as the number of warm-starting epochs increases. Can you provide more details about how the sharpness metric evolves during the warm-starting process, and at what point does it become sufficiently flat?

4. The paper uses a fixed sharpness metric with ε = 10^-3 and 5×10^-4. How sensitive are the results to the choice of ε, and what is the theoretical justification for these specific values?

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the practical challenges of large-batch training in deep learning. With the minor clarifications suggested above, it would be a strong contribution to the ICLR community.
