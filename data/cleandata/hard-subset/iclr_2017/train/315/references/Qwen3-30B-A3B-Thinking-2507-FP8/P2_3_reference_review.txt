

# Review of "Large-Batch Training of Deep Learning Models"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes compared to small batch sizes. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with smaller eigenvalues), and that this difference in minimizer geometry directly explains the generalization gap. The paper provides multiple lines of evidence: parametric 1D plots along solution paths, a computationally feasible sharpness metric, and analysis of how small-batch methods "escape" sharp minimizers through gradient noise. The authors also explore several mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) but find them insufficient to fully resolve the problem. This work effectively connects empirical observations with theoretical concepts from optimization and statistical learning theory, providing valuable insights for practitioners and researchers.

## 2. Decision

**Reject** (with a strong recommendation for resubmission after addressing key concerns)

**Key reasons for rejection:** 
1) The paper's central claim about sharp vs. flat minima being the cause of the generalization gap is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided, and the proposed sharpness metric lacks theoretical grounding.
2) The experimental design and analysis have several methodological limitations that undermine the strength of the conclusions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core claim - that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers which cause the generalization gap - is not rigorously supported. While the authors present interesting numerical evidence, several critical issues undermine their conclusions:

First, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is poorly justified. The authors acknowledge it's "imperfect" and computationally feasible, but fail to establish a clear theoretical connection between their metric and the actual Hessian spectrum. The metric is defined as a ratio of function values over a neighborhood, but the paper doesn't demonstrate how this correlates with the actual Hessian eigenvalues for deep neural networks. Without this, the "sharpness" measurements remain ambiguous.

Second, the paper doesn't adequately address the possibility of confounding factors. The generalization gap could be due to other batch-size effects, such as the number of iterations (large batches converge faster but may require more iterations to reach optimal performance), or the learning rate schedule (which is often adjusted with batch size). The paper doesn't properly control for these factors.

Third, the interpretation of "sharp" vs. "flat" minima is problematic. The paper claims that sharp minima have "a significant number of large positive eigenvalues," but the experiments only measure a single metric (the sharpness metric) and don't directly analyze the Hessian spectrum. The claim that "the function rises steeply only along a small dimensional subspace" is interesting, but without direct Hessian analysis, it's difficult to verify.

The paper's experimental design also has limitations. The batch sizes used for "large-batch" methods (10% of training data) are extremely large (e.g., 50,000 samples for CIFAR-10), which is not typical in practice. The paper doesn't show results for more realistic batch sizes (e.g., 1,000-4,000 samples) where the generalization gap is known to occur. Additionally, the paper doesn't compare with more recent large-batch training methods (like LAMB or Layer-wise Adaptive Rate Scaling) that have been designed to address this exact problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper significantly, I recommend the following:

1. **Theoretical grounding of the sharpness metric:** The authors should rigorously establish the relationship between their metric and the Hessian spectrum. This could involve testing the metric on simple models where the Hessian is computable, and showing that their metric correlates with actual eigenvalues.

2. **Improved experimental design:** The authors should use more realistic batch sizes for their "large-batch" experiments (e.g., 1,000-4,000 samples) rather than 10% of the training data, which is not representative of typical large-batch training in practice. They should also include proper learning rate scaling for different batch sizes, as this is a critical factor in large-batch training.

3. **Control for confounding factors:** The paper should explicitly control for the number of iterations and learning rate schedule, as these are confounded with batch size in their current experiments. A fair comparison should use the same number of iterations for both small and large batch methods, or adjust learning rates appropriately.

4. **Hessian analysis:** The authors should directly compute and analyze the Hessian spectrum at the found minima (or use an approximation) to confirm their claims about sharp vs. flat minima. This would significantly strengthen their central claim.

5. **Comparison with recent large-batch methods:** The authors should compare their results with recent methods specifically designed for large-batch training (e.g., LAMB, LARS) to demonstrate the relevance of their findings to current practice.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do your sharpness metric results correlate with direct Hessian eigenvalue computations on the same models? Have you performed any such analysis?

2. The paper claims that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training," but the training curves in Figure 2 show similar behavior to overfitting (training accuracy continues to rise while testing accuracy plateaus). Could you clarify this apparent contradiction?

3. In Table 2, the training accuracy for large-batch methods is nearly identical to small-batch methods, yet the testing accuracy differs significantly. How do you explain this when the loss function values are similar?

4. The paper states that "the ratio of ‖x?s − x0‖2 and ‖x?` − x0‖2 was in the range of 3–10." This suggests small-batch methods move farther from the initialization. How does this relate to the "flatness" of the minima they converge to?

5. Have you considered the possibility that the generalization gap is primarily due to the need for different learning rate schedules for different batch sizes, rather than the minimizer geometry itself?

While the paper addresses an important and well-known problem in deep learning, the current evidence does not sufficiently support the authors' central claim about sharp vs. flat minima as the cause of the generalization gap. With the suggested improvements, particularly in establishing the theoretical connection between their metric and the Hessian spectrum, and with more careful experimental design, this paper could make a valuable contribution to the field. As it stands, the claims are overstated relative to the evidence provided.
