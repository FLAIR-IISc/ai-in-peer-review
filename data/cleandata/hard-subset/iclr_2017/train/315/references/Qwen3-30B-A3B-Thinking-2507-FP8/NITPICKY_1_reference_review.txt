

# Review of "The Generalization Gap in Large-Batch Training"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper investigates the well-documented phenomenon of the "generalization gap" observed when training deep learning models with large-batch methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors propose that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (characterized by small Hessian eigenvalues). The authors support this claim with numerical experiments, including parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), and explore potential remedies such as data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains significant methodological flaws and presentation issues that undermine its main claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers that generalize poorly is not adequately supported by the evidence provided. Several critical issues prevent us from accepting this claim:

**Methodological flaws:**
- The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not properly justified or validated against standard Hessian-based measures of sharpness. The constraint set C in Equation (3) contains an error: "− (|(A+x)i|+ 1) ≤ zi ≤ (|(A+x)i|+ 1)" should be "− (|(A^+x)i|+ 1) ≤ zi ≤ (|(A^+x)i|+ 1)" (where A^+ is the pseudo-inverse), but the paper does not clarify this. Without proper validation, the metric cannot be trusted to measure sharpness.
- The paper uses ADAM for both small and large batch methods, but ADAM's behavior is known to change with batch size, which confounds the results. The paper fails to address how the optimizer's behavior might contribute to the observed differences.
- The paper claims the generalization gap is "as high as 5%," but fails to provide sufficient statistical evidence to support this claim. The results in Table 2 lack standard deviations or confidence intervals for the accuracy values.
- The paper states that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting," but the evidence provided (Figure 2) does not clearly distinguish between overfitting and sharp minima as the cause of the performance difference.

**Presentation and formatting issues:**
- The paper contains numerous formatting errors, such as inconsistent notation (|B_k| vs. B_k), mathematical errors in equations (Equation 1: "1 M M∑ i=1 fi(x)" should be "1/M ∑_{i=1}^M f_i(x)"), and typographical errors (e.g., "reinforced" instead of "reinforced" in the abstract).
- The paper references "Figure 1" for a hypothetical illustration of sharp vs. flat minima, but no such figure is provided in the text.
- The paper uses inconsistent terminology (e.g., "sharp minimizers" vs. "sharp minima") throughout.
- The references contain numerous formatting errors, with some entries containing "?" and inconsistent citation styles.

**Theoretical and conceptual issues:**
- The paper misrepresents the relationship between flat minimizers and precision: "flat minimizers can be specified with lower precision than to sharp minimizers" is incorrect; flat minimizers require lower precision to specify, not higher.
- The paper fails to properly place its work in the context of more recent literature, such as the Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) approach by Foret et al. (2020), which directly addresses the sharp minima problem.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:
- Rigorously validate their sharpness metric against standard Hessian-based measures.
- Use a more appropriate experimental design that controls for optimizer behavior across batch sizes.
- Provide sufficient statistical evidence to support their claims about the generalization gap.
- Correct all formatting and typographical errors throughout the paper.
- Properly contextualize their work within the current literature on sharp minima.
- Use standard benchmark datasets (e.g., CIFAR-10, ImageNet) rather than custom networks to ensure results are generalizable.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How did you validate that your sharpness metric (Equation 4) correlates with the largest Hessian eigenvalue, which is the standard measure of sharpness in the literature?
2. Why did you choose 10% of the training data as the batch size for large-batch experiments, and how does this compare to standard practice in the field?
3. How do you explain the fact that your "large-batch" experiments (using 10% of training data) still use a batch size that is relatively small for many applications (e.g., ImageNet training typically uses batches of 256-1024 on a single GPU)?
4. How do your findings align with the recent SAM (Sharpness-Aware Minimization) approach, which directly addresses the sharp minima problem and has been shown to improve generalization for large-batch training?
