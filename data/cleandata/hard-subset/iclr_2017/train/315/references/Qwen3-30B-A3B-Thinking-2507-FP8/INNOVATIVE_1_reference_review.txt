

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper makes a significant contribution by identifying and empirically validating a key mechanism behind the well-known generalization gap in large-batch deep learning training. The authors propose that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) with poor generalization performance, while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers with better generalization. This insight goes beyond merely documenting the phenomenon to providing a concrete explanation rooted in the geometry of the loss landscape. The paper offers a novel sharpness metric (Equation 4) that enables practical assessment of this phenomenon, supported by thorough empirical evidence across multiple network architectures and datasets. The work effectively bridges theoretical concepts (sharp vs. flat minima) with practical deep learning challenges, opening new research directions for scalable training.

## 2. Decision

Accept. This paper presents a novel, well-motivated insight that significantly advances our understanding of large-batch training. The core claim—large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers causing generalization gaps—is compelling, well-supported by evidence, and represents a meaningful conceptual contribution to the field. While the paper doesn't provide a complete solution to the problem, it identifies the fundamental mechanism behind the phenomenon, which is more valuable than incremental improvements to existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its novel conceptual contribution that reinterprets an established practical problem through a new theoretical lens. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a practical, computationally feasible approach to measuring the phenomenon, making the theoretical concept applicable to real deep learning scenarios. The empirical evidence is robust and comprehensive, showing consistent results across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants), datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, image classification), and optimization approaches (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD). 

The warm-starting experiments (Section 3) are particularly compelling as they demonstrate that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when "guided" by small-batch exploration, directly supporting the authors' sharpness hypothesis. This provides a clear path forward for future work. While the paper doesn't provide a theoretical proof of convergence to sharp minimizers, the empirical evidence is sufficiently strong to make the claim credible and valuable to the community.

The paper successfully positions itself within the literature by connecting to prior work on sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and recent theoretical results on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015), while making a clear, specific contribution to a practical problem that affects deep learning practitioners.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:
1. A more detailed discussion of the relationship between the sharpness metric (Equation 4) and the actual Hessian eigenvalues, including a brief analysis of the approximation error.
2. A clearer explanation of why the sharpness metric works well despite being a heuristic measure (e.g., why it correlates with Hessian eigenvalues in practice).
3. A more comprehensive discussion of related work on batch size effects, particularly recent papers that have addressed this topic (e.g., Smith et al., 2017 on learning rate scaling).
4. A brief discussion of how the sharpness metric might be incorporated into training algorithms to actively avoid sharp minima.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that sharp minimizers "do not resemble a cone" (Section 2.2.2), with the function rising steeply only along a small dimensional subspace. Could you elaborate on how this observation relates to the dimensionality of the loss landscape and whether this is a universal property of sharp minima in deep learning?

2. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) suggest that after a certain number of small-batch epochs, large-batch methods can achieve good generalization. What is the precise relationship between the number of small-batch epochs and the quality of the warm-started large-batch solution? Is there an optimal number of warm-start epochs?

3. In Appendix E, the authors attempt to address the generalization gap through data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training. While these approaches show some improvement, they don't fully solve the problem. Could you speculate on why these approaches fail to eliminate sharp minima, and what additional modifications might be necessary to fully address the problem?
