

# Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims/contribution
The paper claims to investigate why large-batch training methods in deep learning suffer from a generalization gap compared to small-batch methods. The authors propose that this gap is due to large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with small Hessian eigenvalues). They present numerical evidence using a new sharpness metric (Equation 4) and parametric plots to support this claim, suggesting that this insight could lead to more scalable training approaches.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper fails to provide rigorous evidence for its central claim, and the proposed sharpness metric lacks theoretical grounding. The authors do not adequately compare with established approaches that have been shown to mitigate the generalization gap.

## 3. Supporting arguments
The paper's main contribution is the claim that the generalization gap is caused by convergence to sharp minimizers. However, the evidence provided is insufficient for several critical reasons:

First, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is neither standard nor rigorously justified. The authors state it "is closely related to the spectrum of ∇²f(x)" but provide no theoretical justification or empirical evidence for this relationship. They claim it approximates Ritz values of the Hessian but never demonstrate this on a model where Hessian computation is feasible. Without this connection, the metric is merely a heuristic without established meaning in optimization theory.

Second, the paper fails to compare against established baselines that address the generalization gap. The standard approach for large-batch training is to scale the learning rate with batch size (Smith et al., 2017), which eliminates the generalization gap without any need for sharpness-based modifications. The authors' experiments use a fixed learning rate for both small and large batch methods, which is not standard practice. This design flaw makes it impossible to determine whether the observed gap is due to batch size or simply an inappropriate learning rate for large batches.

Third, the paper claims the generalization gap "is not due to over-fitting" but provides no evidence for this. The observed behavior (high training accuracy, lower testing accuracy) is precisely the hallmark of overfitting, especially when using an unscaled learning rate with large batches. The authors dismiss this possibility without proper analysis.

Finally, the paper does not provide evidence that sharpness is the *cause* of the generalization gap rather than a *correlate*. If the gap disappears when using learning rate scaling (which is known to be effective), this would strongly suggest that sharpness is not the fundamental issue.

## 4. Additional feedback
To improve this paper, the authors should:

1. Compare against established methods for large-batch training, particularly learning rate scaling (Smith et al., 2017), which is the standard approach to mitigate the generalization gap. The paper's claim that "a training algorithm that employs large batches without sacrificing generalization performance would have the ability to scale to a much larger number of nodes" is not supported when the standard solution is simply to adjust the learning rate.

2. Provide a rigorous theoretical justification for their sharpness metric. If they claim it relates to Hessian eigenvalues, they should demonstrate this relationship on smaller models where Hessian computation is feasible.

3. Acknowledge and engage with the broader literature on batch size effects, particularly the work by Smith et al. (2017), You et al. (2017), and others who have shown the generalization gap can be addressed through learning rate scaling.

4. Conduct experiments with learning rate scaling to determine whether the sharpness difference persists when using standard large-batch training practices.

5. Clarify whether the observed sharpness difference is a cause or consequence of the generalization gap. If the gap disappears with learning rate scaling but sharpness remains, this would undermine the paper's central claim.

## Questions for authors
1. Why did you not scale the learning rate with batch size, as is standard practice in large-batch training? How would this change your results?

2. How do you justify the relationship between your sharpness metric (Equation 4) and the Hessian eigenvalues? Can you provide evidence for this relationship on a model where Hessian computation is feasible?

3. Have you compared your approach with learning rate scaling (Smith et al., 2017), which has been shown to eliminate the generalization gap without any need for sharpness-based modifications? If so, what were the results?

4. How do you reconcile your claim that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting with the standard interpretation of high training accuracy and lower testing accuracy?

5. Have you considered that the sharpness difference might be a consequence of using an inappropriate learning rate for large batches rather than the batch size itself being the fundamental cause?
