

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper investigates the well-documented generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning compared to small-batch methods. The authors propose a compelling hypothesis: large-batch methods converge to "sharp minimizers" characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian, while small-batch methods converge to "flat minimizers" with small eigenvalues. They provide multiple lines of empirical evidence to support this claim, including parametric 1D plots, a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4), and warm-starting experiments demonstrating that small-batch methods can guide large-batch methods toward better generalizing minima. The paper also explores several potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) but finds they only partially address the issue.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable empirical contribution to understanding a well-known phenomenon in deep learning, and the evidence provided is scientifically rigorous for the claims made.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper addresses a significant practical problem in deep learning with clear empirical evidence. The three key strengths supporting my recommendation are:

First, the paper is exceptionally well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly articulate the problem (generalization gap with large batches), cite relevant prior work (LeCun et al., 2012; Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016), and connect their findings to recent theoretical work on loss landscapes (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016). The problem statement is clear, important, and relevant to the deep learning community.

Second, the empirical evidence is robust and multi-faceted. The parametric 1D plots (Figure 3) visually demonstrate the sharpness difference between SB and LB minima. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) provides quantitative evidence of a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness, with consistent results across multiple network architectures (F1-F4, C1-C4) and data sets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants). The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly compelling evidence by showing that small-batch methods can guide large-batch methods toward flatter minima with better generalization.

Third, the paper acknowledges limitations appropriately. The authors correctly note that their sharpness metric is a computationally feasible proxy (not a direct Hessian computation), and they thoroughly explore potential remedies while documenting their partial success. This honesty and thoroughness strengthen the paper's credibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) could benefit from more theoretical justification. The authors note it relates to Hessian eigenvalues but could provide a more precise connection to the spectrum of ∇²f(x), especially since the paper is fundamentally about curvature. A brief analysis of how the metric relates to the actual Hessian properties would strengthen the methodology.

2. The paper could better address the relationship between batch size and sharpness. The threshold behavior (Figure 4) is interesting but not fully explored. A systematic investigation of how sharpness changes as batch size increases (beyond the threshold) would provide more complete understanding.

3. The paper should clarify the scope of their findings. While they note the results hold for multiple networks, they should explicitly state which architectures and tasks they believe their findings generalize to, and which might be exceptions.

4. The warm-starting approach (Figure 5) is promising but the paper doesn't fully explore its practical implications. A brief discussion of how much computational benefit this might provide in real-world scenarios would strengthen the "performance model" argument in Section 1.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and ensure confidence in the assessment:

1. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is computed using L-BFGS-B iterations. How sensitive are the results to the number of iterations used in this maximization? Would more iterations lead to significantly different sharpness values?

2. The paper states that "the solutions obtained by a large-batch method defines points of larger sensitivity of the training function." How do the authors reconcile this with the observation that the sharpness is concentrated in only a small subspace (5% of the whole space) as mentioned in Section 2.2.2? Does this mean the sharpness metric is capturing something different from the full Hessian spectrum?

3. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) show that after a certain number of small-batch epochs, large-batch methods can achieve good generalization. How does the number of warm-start epochs relate to the network architecture and data set? Is there a general rule of thumb for this threshold?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the generalization gap in deep learning. The evidence is compelling, the methodology is sound, and the findings are important for practitioners seeking to scale deep learning training. I recommend acceptance with the suggested minor revisions to further strengthen the paper.
