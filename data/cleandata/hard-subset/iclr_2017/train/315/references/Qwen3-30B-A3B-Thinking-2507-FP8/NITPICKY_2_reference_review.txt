

# Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

## Summary
This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when training deep neural networks with large-batch methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors propose that this gap stems from large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with numerous small eigenvalues). The paper presents numerical evidence using parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric to support this claim. The authors also explore potential remedies including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection

1. **Numerous formatting and technical inconsistencies**: The paper contains multiple formatting errors in mathematical notation (e.g., ∇2f(x) instead of ∇²f(x)), inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., "\Q2016\E" instead of "2016"), and unclear methodological choices (e.g., using ADAM optimizer for large-batch training without justification). These issues significantly undermine the paper's credibility and scientific rigor.

2. **Lack of sufficient evidence for key claims**: The paper claims "the drop in generalization (also called generalization gap) to be as high as 5% even for smaller networks" but fails to provide specific examples or data to support this claim. The paper also asserts that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting" but provides insufficient evidence to substantiate this claim.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper has several critical methodological and presentation issues that prevent me from being confident in the validity of its claims:

1. **Inconsistent batch size reporting**: The paper states "For all experiments, we used 10% of the training data as batch size for the large-batch experiments" without specifying the actual dataset sizes. This makes it impossible to determine the absolute batch size used in experiments, which is crucial for reproducibility. Different datasets would yield vastly different absolute batch sizes (e.g., 10% of 10,000 vs. 10% of 1,000,000 training examples).

2. **Unjustified use of ADAM for large-batch training**: The paper states "We used the ADAM optimizer for both regimes" but ADAM was specifically designed for small-batch stochastic optimization. The authors provide no justification for using ADAM with large batches, which is a significant methodological flaw. The paper's claims about large-batch methods are undermined by using an optimizer not designed for this regime.

3. **Inadequate statistical evidence**: The paper claims "a 1–2 order-of-magnitude difference between the values of our metric for the SB and LB regimes" but fails to provide statistical tests to confirm these differences are significant. Without proper statistical analysis, the claimed difference could be due to random variation.

4. **Misleading sharpness metric**: The paper defines a sharpness metric (Equation 4) but fails to establish its validity. They claim it "relates to the largest eigenvalue of ∇²f(x)" but provide no theoretical or empirical evidence to support this relationship. The metric's sensitivity to the choice of parameters (like the size of the neighborhood) is not adequately explored.

5. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for batch size (sometimes |Bk|, sometimes Bk), which creates confusion about what is being measured. This is a fundamental issue in mathematical writing that undermines the paper's clarity.

6. **Unsubstantiated claims**: The paper makes several claims without evidence, such as "the generalization gap is as high as 5% even for smaller networks" and "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone." These claims require specific evidence to be credible.

## Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide clear, consistent mathematical notation throughout the paper (e.g., use ∇²f(x) instead of ∇2f(x)).

2. Justify their choice of optimizer for large-batch training. If they used ADAM for large batches, they should explain why it was appropriate and how it differs from standard practice.

3. Report absolute batch sizes for all experiments (not just percentages of training data) to enable reproducibility.

4. Provide statistical evidence (e.g., p-values, confidence intervals) for all claims about significant differences between SB and LB methods.

5. Clarify the relationship between their sharpness metric and the Hessian eigenvalues. They should either provide theoretical justification or empirical validation of this relationship.

6. Remove unsupported claims like "the generalization gap is as high as 5% even for smaller networks" unless they can provide specific data to back this up.

7. Address the inconsistency in reference formatting (e.g., "\Q2016\E" should be "2016" or "2016" in the bibliography).

## Questions for Authors

1. What specific datasets and network sizes were used for the claim that "the drop in generalization (also called generalization gap) to be as high as 5% even for smaller networks"? Please provide specific examples.

2. Why was ADAM chosen as the optimizer for large-batch training, given that it was designed for small-batch optimization? How does this choice affect the validity of your conclusions about large-batch methods?

3. What statistical tests were performed to confirm that the observed differences in sharpness metric between SB and LB methods are statistically significant?

4. How does your sharpness metric (Equation 4) relate to the Hessian eigenvalues in a mathematically rigorous way? Can you provide theoretical justification or empirical validation of this relationship?

5. In your warm-start experiments (Figure 5), what was the exact number of epochs used for warm-starting before switching to large-batch training? This detail is critical for reproducibility.
