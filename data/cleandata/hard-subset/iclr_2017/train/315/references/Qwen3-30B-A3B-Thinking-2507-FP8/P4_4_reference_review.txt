

1. Summary: The paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models. The authors claim that this gap is caused by large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (characterized by small eigenvalues). They support this claim through numerical experiments using parametric plots and a heuristic sharpness metric (Equation 4), which measures the sensitivity of the loss function in a local neighborhood. The paper also explores attempts to mitigate the problem through data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training, though these approaches only partially address the issue.

2. Decision: Reject

Key reasons:
- The paper lacks theoretical justification for the observed phenomena.
- The sharpness metric is a heuristic proxy without rigorous connection to the Hessian eigenvalues.

3. Supporting arguments:
The paper's main contribution is empirical, but it fails to establish a rigorous theoretical link between the observed phenomena and the sharpness metric. While the authors note that "Metric 2.1 is closely related to the spectrum of ∇²f(x)" and explain it as approximating Ritz values, they provide no mathematical proof or analysis demonstrating this relationship. The metric is defined as φˣ,ᶠ(ε, A) = (maxᵧ∈ᶜ f(x+Ay) - f(x))/f(x) × 100, but the paper does not rigorously show how this relates to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian or how it correlates with generalization performance across different architectures.

Furthermore, the empirical evidence is limited in scope. The experiments are conducted on only six network configurations (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, and fully-connected networks) across a small number of datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, and image classification datasets). The paper does not establish whether the observed sharpness phenomenon generalizes to more complex architectures, larger datasets, or other optimization algorithms beyond ADAM. The claim that "the generalization gap is correlated with a marked sharpness of the minimizers" is supported only by a narrow set of experiments without proper statistical analysis of the significance of the observed differences.

The paper also fails to address whether the observed sharpness is a property of the loss landscape itself or a consequence of the optimization algorithm. The authors note that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone," but this observation doesn't clarify whether the sharpness is inherent to the problem or induced by the optimization process.

4. Additional feedback:
- The authors should provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis connecting the sharpness metric to the Hessian eigenvalues. This could include a mathematical proof of the relationship or at least a thorough empirical validation across multiple architectures and datasets.
- The paper would benefit from a more comprehensive analysis of the sharpness metric's reliability. For example, they could compare the metric's values with actual Hessian eigenvalue computations for smaller networks where this is feasible.
- The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are promising but under-analyzed. A more detailed exploration of the relationship between the number of warm-start epochs and the final sharpness/generalization performance would strengthen the paper's claims.
- The paper should clarify whether the sharpness phenomenon is specific to ADAM or generalizes to other optimizers like SGD, as the authors note they used ADAM for both SB and LB methods but don't explore other optimizers' behavior.

Questions for authors:
1. Can you provide a theoretical justification for why the sharpness metric (Equation 4) correlates with the largest Hessian eigenvalue? What mathematical conditions are necessary for this correlation to hold?
2. How do your results generalize to other optimization algorithms beyond ADAM (e.g., SGD with momentum)? 
3. Why do conservative training and data augmentation only partially address the generalization gap? Is there a fundamental limitation to these approaches?
4. How do your findings relate to recent theoretical work on the implicit bias of optimization algorithms in deep learning (e.g., Chizat & Bach, 2020; Li et al., 2019)?
