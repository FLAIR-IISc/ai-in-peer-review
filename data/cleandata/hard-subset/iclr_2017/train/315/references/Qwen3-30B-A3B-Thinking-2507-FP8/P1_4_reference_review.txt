

# Peer Review: "On Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a compelling contribution to understanding the generalization gap observed between large-batch and small-batch training methods in deep learning. The authors present a clear hypothesis: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity to small perturbations in the loss function) that generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers with better generalization properties. The paper supports this hypothesis with thorough empirical evidence across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet, and others) using two key approaches: parametric 1D plots of the loss landscape along paths between SB/LB minimizers, and a novel sharpness metric (Equation 4) that quantifies the sensitivity of minimizers. The authors also explore the implications of these findings for scalability and propose several remediation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training), while demonstrating that none fully eliminate the sharpness issue. The work is well-positioned within the literature on deep learning optimization and generalization, and provides valuable insights for both practitioners and researchers.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding a fundamental practical limitation in deep learning training, and the empirical evidence is robust and well-presented. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper identifies and rigorously characterizes a previously observed but poorly understood phenomenon in deep learning; (2) the evidence presented is scientifically rigorous with multiple network architectures, multiple trials, and appropriate statistical reporting.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated by the widespread empirical observation of the generalization gap in large-batch training (LeCun et al., 2012), which has been a practical barrier to scaling training to more compute resources. The authors successfully distinguish between possible explanations for this gap and provide compelling evidence supporting the sharpness hypothesis.

The empirical methodology is particularly strong. The parametric plots (Figure 3 and Figure 7) provide intuitive visual evidence of the sharpness difference, while the sharpness metric (Equation 4) offers a quantitative measure that is computationally feasible for large networks. The 1-2 order of magnitude difference in sharpness values between SB and LB methods (Tables 3-4) is statistically significant and consistent across multiple network architectures.

The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly convincing evidence that the issue is not with the large-batch method itself, but with the basin of attraction it converges to. This demonstrates that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization if started from a flat minimizer discovered by small-batch methods, which strongly supports the authors' hypothesis.

The paper's discussion of related work is well-placed, acknowledging both the theoretical work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016) and practical work on large-batch training (Zhang et al., 2015; Das et al., 2016). The connection to MDL theory and Bayesian learning (MacKay, 1992) provides a theoretical foundation for the empirical observations.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the relationship between sharpness and generalization**: The paper states that sharp minimizers generalize poorly, but the causal relationship is not fully established. It would strengthen the paper to discuss whether sharpness *causes* poor generalization or is merely correlated with it. Some theoretical justification for why sharpness would lead to poor generalization (beyond the MDL argument) would be valuable.

2. **Expand on the computational cost of the sharpness metric**: The paper notes that computing eigenvalues of the Hessian is prohibitive, but doesn't fully address the computational cost of their metric. How many function evaluations does the L-BFGS-B maximization require? How does this scale with network size?

3. **Clarify the warm-starting experiment**: The warm-starting approach (Figure 5) is very promising but the paper doesn't fully explain why SB methods need to "explore" before LB methods can succeed. A more detailed explanation of the exploration phase would help readers understand the dynamics.

4. **Address the "curse of dimensionality" concern**: The paper notes that sharp minima only rise steeply along a small subspace (5% of the whole space). This is important but should be more explicitly connected to why this is a problem for generalization.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the solutions obtained by a large-batch method defines points of larger sensitivity of the training function." Could you clarify whether this sensitivity is measured with respect to the training set or the generalization performance? Is there evidence that the sharpness metric correlates with generalization error across different datasets?

2. In Section 3, you note that "the ratio of ‖x?s − x0‖2 and ‖x?` − x0‖2 was in the range of 3–10." Could you provide more detail about how this distance ratio relates to the sharpness of the minimizers? Does this suggest that flat minimizers are farther from the initialization than sharp minimizers?

3. The paper mentions that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training." Could you elaborate on how you determined that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting? Specifically, what evidence did you examine to rule out overfitting as the cause?

4. The paper states that "adversarial training did not improve generalization." Given that adversarial training is designed to make the model less sensitive to input perturbations (which seems related to the sharpness hypothesis), why do you think it failed to address the sharpness issue? Is there a theoretical reason for this?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to understanding a key practical limitation in deep learning. The empirical evidence is compelling and the insights have clear implications for the scalability of deep learning training. With minor revisions to address the points above, this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
