

# Review of "On the Generalization of Large-Batch Training in Deep Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon of the "generalization gap" in deep learning, where large-batch training methods achieve similar training accuracy as small-batch methods but significantly worse testing accuracy. The authors claim that this gap is not due to overfitting but rather stems from large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with numerous small eigenvalues). They support this claim through parametric plots of loss landscapes and a proposed "sharpness metric" (Equation 4) that quantifies the sensitivity of the loss function around minima. The paper also explores attempts to mitigate the problem through data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training, though the authors find these approaches only partially address the issue.

## 2. Decision: Reject

**Key reasons for rejection:** The paper's main claim (that the generalization gap is due to sharp minimizers) is not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented. The proposed sharpness metric lacks rigorous validation against the actual Hessian properties it claims to measure, and the experimental evidence is limited in scope and depth.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

The paper's central claim—that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima that generalize poorly—relies on a metric (Equation 4) that is not properly validated. The authors acknowledge this metric is "imperfect" and "computationally feasible" but fail to demonstrate that it actually correlates with the Hessian eigenvalues that define sharp minima. They state: "Metric 2.1 is closely related to the spectrum of ∇²f(x)" but provide no evidence for this relationship. Without this validation, the entire empirical argument collapses, as the paper's conclusions depend entirely on this metric.

The experimental evidence is also insufficiently rigorous. The authors test only 6 network architectures (with limited variation in data sets and tasks), which is inadequate to make broad claims about deep learning. Their sharpness metric shows a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference between small-batch and large-batch methods, but they don't demonstrate that this difference is causally related to generalization performance. The paper lacks ablation studies to rule out alternative explanations, such as the fact that large-batch methods might simply require different hyperparameter tuning to achieve good generalization.

Furthermore, the paper fails to address a critical counterargument: the generalization gap might be due to hyperparameter mismatch rather than the nature of the minima. The authors use ADAM for both regimes but don't systematically vary learning rates for large-batch methods, which is known to significantly impact performance. If the generalization gap disappears with appropriate hyperparameter tuning, the sharpness hypothesis is undermined.

The attempts to mitigate the problem (Section E) are also insufficiently evaluated. The authors state that "data augmentation" leads to "comparable accuracy" but fail to quantify how much the sharpness metric improves (or whether it's eliminated), which is the core issue they're addressing.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper tackles an important practical problem in deep learning, it would benefit from several key improvements:

1. **Rigorous validation of the sharpness metric**: The authors should demonstrate that their metric correlates with actual Hessian eigenvalues on simpler models (e.g., small networks or linear models) where Hessian computation is feasible. Without this, the paper's central claim lacks scientific grounding.

2. **More comprehensive experiments**: The paper should include a wider range of architectures, data sets, and optimization methods. The current experiments are limited to a small subset of standard networks (AlexNet, VGGNet variants) and use only ADAM, which doesn't provide sufficient evidence for general claims.

3. **Hyperparameter sensitivity analysis**: The authors should systematically vary learning rates and other hyperparameters for large-batch methods to determine if the generalization gap can be closed without altering the optimization dynamics (i.e., without changing the batch size).

4. **Theoretical grounding**: While the paper references prior work on sharp vs. flat minima, it lacks a clear theoretical framework explaining why sharp minima generalize poorly. The authors should provide a more detailed explanation of how their metric relates to generalization, beyond citing MDL theory and Bayesian views.

5. **Clarify the relationship to prior work**: The paper mentions similar work (e.g., Chaudhari et al., 2016) but doesn't clearly distinguish their contribution. A more precise positioning of their work relative to existing literature would strengthen the paper.

## Questions for the Authors

1. Can you provide evidence that your sharpness metric (Equation 4) actually correlates with the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian? Without this, your main claim lacks empirical support.

2. Have you systematically varied learning rates for large-batch methods to see if the generalization gap disappears with appropriate hyperparameter tuning? This is a critical control for your hypothesis.

3. How do you account for the possibility that the generalization gap is due to hyperparameter mismatch rather than the nature of the minima? For example, large-batch methods often require smaller learning rates to converge well.

4. Why did you not test with SGD (which is mentioned in the introduction but not extensively explored in the experiments)? SGD with large batches has been shown to generalize better than ADAM with large batches in some prior work.

The paper addresses an important practical issue in deep learning, but the evidence provided to support its central claim is insufficiently rigorous. The proposed sharpness metric lacks validation, the experiments are limited in scope, and the paper fails to address critical alternative explanations. These shortcomings prevent the paper from making a compelling contribution to the field.
