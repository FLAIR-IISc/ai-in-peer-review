

# Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods for Training Deep Learning Models"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers with better generalization performance. They support this claim with numerical experiments, including parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric. The paper makes a potentially valuable contribution by identifying a key mechanism behind the generalization gap in large-batch training.

## Decision
Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection
1. The paper contains numerous formatting inconsistencies and technical inaccuracies that undermine the scientific rigor of the work.
2. The experimental details are insufficiently described, making the results difficult to verify and reproduce.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper suffers from a significant number of minor but critical formatting and technical errors that collectively indicate a lack of attention to detail. For instance:

- Equation (1) is incorrectly formatted as "min x∈Rn f(x) := 1 M M∑ i=1 fi(x)" instead of the correct "min_{x∈R^n} f(x) := (1/M) ∑_{i=1}^M f_i(x)".
- Equation (2) has similar formatting issues with "1 |Bk|" instead of "1/|B_k|".
- The notation "x?s" and "x?`" is used inconsistently throughout the paper instead of proper mathematical notation like "x_s^*" and "x_l^*".
- The sharpness metric in Equation (4) is formatted poorly with a line break in the middle of the equation.
- The reference list contains inconsistent formatting like "Bengio et al.,? \Q2016\E" instead of standard citation format.

The experimental section lacks critical details necessary for scientific rigor. The authors state that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training," but fail to provide specific evidence beyond "This is not what we observe in our experiments." They claim "both sharp and flat minimizers have very similar loss function values," but don't provide numerical evidence to support this assertion.

The paper's experimental methodology is inadequately described. For example:
- They state "we randomly chose a pair of SB and LB minimizers from the 5 trials," but don't explain why this random choice is appropriate or how it affects the results.
- They mention "10% of the training data" for large-batch experiments but don't specify the total training data size, making it impossible to understand what "10%" means.
- They claim "a 1–2 order-of-magnitude difference" between sharpness metrics but don't provide actual values or statistical significance.
- The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not properly justified mathematically, and they don't explain why their approximation is valid.

The paper also makes unsupported claims about their metric's relationship to the Hessian spectrum without providing mathematical justification. In Section 2.2.2, they state "Metric 2.1 is closely related to the spectrum of ∇²f(x)" but provide no proof or evidence for this relationship.

## Additional Feedback

1. Please fix all formatting inconsistencies in equations and notation throughout the paper. Proper mathematical notation is essential for scientific communication.

2. Provide complete experimental details, including:
   - Total training dataset sizes
   - Specific values for all hyperparameters (not just "10%" and "256")
   - Statistical significance testing for all results
   - Complete description of how random choices were made

3. Clarify the relationship between your sharpness metric and the Hessian spectrum with mathematical justification.

4. Provide numerical evidence to support your claim that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting.

5. Explain why you chose ADAM for both small and large batch regimes without justification.

6. Include Table 2 as promised in Section 2.2, and provide the actual numbers you reference.

7. For the GitHub repository link, provide a specific commit hash or version number for reproducibility.

## Clarifying Questions

1. Why did you choose ADAM as the optimizer for both small and large batch methods without comparing to other optimizers like SGD or ADAGRAD?

2. What was the total training dataset size for each experiment, so that "10% of the training data" can be properly interpreted?

3. How did you determine the appropriate value of ε for your sharpness metric (Equation 4)? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis?

4. Can you provide statistical significance tests for the 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness metrics between SB and LB methods?

5. How does your sharpness metric relate to the actual Hessian spectrum in practice? Can you provide evidence that your metric correlates with the largest eigenvalue?

The paper presents an interesting hypothesis about the generalization gap in large-batch training, but the numerous technical inconsistencies and insufficient experimental details prevent me from being confident in the validity of the results. A more rigorously written paper with complete experimental details would be appropriate for ICLR.
