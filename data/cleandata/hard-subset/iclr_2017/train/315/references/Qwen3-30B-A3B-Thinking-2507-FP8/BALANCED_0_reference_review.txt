

# Review of "Sharp Minima and Generalization in Large-Batch Training"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the well-known generalization gap between small-batch and large-batch training in deep learning. The authors make the compelling empirical observation that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity in a neighborhood, as measured by their proposed metric) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers. They support this claim through extensive numerical experiments across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants), providing parametric plots and sharpness metrics that consistently show a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between the two regimes. The paper also explores several potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), though these only partially mitigate the problem. The authors position their work as filling a gap between practical observations and theoretical understanding of why large-batch training suffers from poor generalization.

## 2. Decision

Reject

Key reasons: (1) The paper lacks a theoretical foundation explaining why large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers, which is central to their claim; (2) The sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is not rigorously connected to the theoretical definition of sharpness (largest Hessian eigenvalue), undermining the core argument.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the empirical observation linking batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization performance. This observation is interesting and aligns with prior work (e.g., Keskar et al. 2016), but it falls short of a significant theoretical contribution. The lack of a theoretical explanation for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima is a fundamental weakness. Without this, the paper remains descriptive rather than explanatory, limiting its scientific impact.

The sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is a significant vulnerability. While the authors note it's "closely related" to the largest Hessian eigenvalue, they provide no evidence that their metric actually correlates with this theoretical measure. The paper doesn't compute the largest Hessian eigenvalue (which would be computationally prohibitive for deep networks) nor does it validate their metric against approximate Hessian computations. Without this connection, the entire argument that "sharp" minimizers cause the generalization gap lacks scientific rigor.

The paper also makes an unsubstantiated claim that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting." The evidence provided (training-testing curves in Figure 2) shows a consistent gap rather than the typical overfitting pattern (testing accuracy declining after a peak), but the authors don't provide a rigorous statistical analysis to support this distinction.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper significantly, the authors should:

1. Provide a theoretical analysis of why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima. This could involve examining the optimization dynamics (e.g., how gradient noise affects convergence) or relating to recent theoretical work on loss landscapes.

2. Rigorously validate their sharpness metric. At minimum, they should compute approximate largest Hessian eigenvalues (using power iteration or other methods) for a subset of their experiments and demonstrate correlation with their metric.

3. Address the causal relationship between sharpness and generalization more directly. The paper shows correlation but doesn't provide evidence that sharpness is the *cause* of the generalization gap (e.g., by showing that sharpness changes precede generalization changes during training).

4. Clarify the relationship between their metric and the theoretical definition of sharpness. The paper mentions "in the convex optimization literature, the term sharp minimum has a different definition" but doesn't explain why their metric is appropriate for non-convex deep learning.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How do you know that your sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) accurately reflects the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian? Can you provide evidence (e.g., correlation with approximate Hessian eigenvalues computed via power iteration) to support this claim?

2. What theoretical justification do you have for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima rather than flat ones? The paper lacks a theoretical explanation for this key observation.

3. In Figure 6, you observe that small-batch methods initially maintain constant sharpness before reducing it, while large-batch methods rapidly increase sharpness. Can you explain the mechanism behind this difference in sharpness evolution?

4. In Appendix E, you note that conservative training improves generalization but doesn't eliminate sharpness. How do you explain this partial success? Is the remaining sharpness sufficient to explain the persistent generalization gap?
