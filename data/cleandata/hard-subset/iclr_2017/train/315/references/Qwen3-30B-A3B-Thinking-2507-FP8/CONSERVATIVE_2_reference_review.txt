

# Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

## 1. Summary of Claims

This paper investigates the well-known generalization gap observed when using large-batch training for deep learning models. The authors claim that the primary cause of this gap is that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian matrix), whereas small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with smaller eigenvalues), and that this difference in minimizer sharpness directly explains the generalization performance disparity. They propose a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify this phenomenon and present empirical evidence across multiple network architectures. The paper also attempts to address the problem through data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training, though the authors acknowledge these only marginally improve generalization.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient empirical validation of its key claims and lacks the methodological rigor required for ICLR. The proposed sharpness metric is not adequately justified, and the paper does not demonstrate how its analysis leads to a practical solution for the generalization gap problem.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the identification of sharp minimizers as the cause of the generalization gap in large-batch training. However, this claim is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The key issues are:

1. **Lack of rigorous validation of the sharpness metric**: The paper admits Metric 2.1 is "imperfect" but provides no evidence that it actually correlates with the true Hessian eigenvalues or generalization performance. The metric measures sensitivity in a limited neighborhood (Equation 4), but the paper doesn't demonstrate how this relates to the actual Hessian spectrum, which would be required to claim that the metric captures "sharpness" as defined in the literature. Without this connection, the entire analysis is built on a shaky foundation.

2. **Inadequate comparison to established approaches**: The paper only compares small-batch vs. large-batch methods using ADAM, but doesn't compare to established techniques for large-batch training that have been shown to mitigate the generalization gap (e.g., linear scaling of learning rates with batch size, which has been demonstrated to work well for large-batch training). The paper's claim that "the lack of generalization ability is due to the fact that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" is not supported by comparison to methods that successfully use large batches while maintaining good generalization.

3. **Marginal improvements from proposed remedies**: The attempts to "remedy" the problem (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) only show marginal improvements in generalization performance, not a fundamental solution to the problem. The paper itself admits these approaches "do not completely remedy the problem," which undermines the paper's claimed contribution.

4. **Lack of theoretical grounding**: The paper relies on empirical observations without providing any theoretical justification for why sharp minimizers generalize poorly. While it cites Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) and Chaudhari et al. (2016), it doesn't clearly connect its metric to these theoretical frameworks or provide new theoretical insights.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide rigorous justification for the sharpness metric (Equation 4) and demonstrate its correlation with actual Hessian eigenvalues through direct computation on smaller networks where this is feasible.

2. Compare their findings to established large-batch training methods (e.g., linear scaling of learning rates) to determine whether the sharpness explanation is necessary to understand the generalization gap.

3. Include more diverse experiments across different optimizers (SGD, Adam, etc.) and network architectures to show the generality of their findings.

4. Explicitly state how their analysis leads to a practical solution for large-batch training that maintains good generalization, rather than just characterizing the problem.

5. Address the relationship between their sharpness metric and previous work on sharpness (e.g., Chaudhari et al., 2016) more thoroughly.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you justify Equation 4 as a valid proxy for the Hessian-based sharpness definition? What evidence do you have that it correlates with actual Hessian eigenvalues?

2. Why did you not compare your results to established large-batch training methods like linear scaling of learning rates, which has been shown to maintain good generalization with large batches?

3. In Figure 5, you show that warm-starting with small-batch methods improves large-batch generalization. How does this relate to the sharpness explanation you propose? Does it suggest that the sharpness metric is not the fundamental cause?

4. The paper claims "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" but doesn't provide evidence that this is a necessary condition for the generalization gap. Can you provide an example of a large-batch method that achieves good generalization without converging to a sharp minimizer?

5. The paper states that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting" but doesn't provide a clear alternative explanation for why large-batch methods generalize worse. How do your findings reconcile with the fact that large-batch methods often achieve similar training accuracy to small-batch methods?
