

1. Summary: The paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning compared to small-batch methods. The authors claim that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) rather than flat minimizers (with many small eigenvalues), which generalize better. The paper provides comprehensive numerical evidence across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, fully-connected networks) using parametric plots and a practical sharpness metric (Equation 4). The authors also explore potential remedies like data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training, though they find these only partially effective at reducing the generalization gap.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper makes a clear, well-supported empirical contribution to understanding the generalization gap in large-batch training. The evidence presented is strong, the methodology is sound, and the findings are significant for the deep learning community.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper effectively demonstrates that large-batch methods converge to minimizers with significantly higher sharpness metrics (Tables 3, 4) compared to small-batch methods. The parametric plots (Figures 3, 7) visually confirm that LB minimizers are sharper than SB minimizers. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide compelling evidence that SB methods can guide LB methods toward better generalizing minima. The paper thoroughly addresses alternative explanations for the generalization gap (overfitting, saddle points, proximity to initial point) and rules them out through careful experimentation. The methodology is robust: multiple networks, multiple runs, and multiple metrics consistently support the main claim. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is well-motivated as a computationally feasible approximation to the Hessian-based sharpness measure, with the authors clearly explaining its relationship to the actual Hessian spectrum.

4. Additional feedback: The paper could strengthen its contribution by providing more detail on the relationship between the proposed sharpness metric and the actual Hessian spectrum. A more thorough analysis of how the metric's parameter affects the results would enhance the paper's technical depth. The authors might also consider discussing how the sharpness metric could be practically applied in model selection or hyperparameter tuning for large-scale training. A comparison with recent work on sharpness-aware optimization would further position the paper within the current literature.

5. Questions for authors:
- How sensitive are the sharpness metric values to the choice of the parameter ε in Equation (4)? Could the authors provide results across a range of ε values to demonstrate robustness?
- The paper states that sharp minimizers "do not resemble a cone" (Section 2.2.2). Could the authors elaborate on their methodology for determining this and whether it holds consistently across different network architectures and datasets?
- The warm-starting experiments in Figure 5 show that SB methods need to run for a certain number of epochs before LB methods can benefit. Is there a clear threshold or is the improvement gradual, and what is the relationship between the number of SB epochs and the resulting LB generalization performance?
- The paper claims the generalization gap is "not due to over-fitting or over-training" (Section 2.2). How do the authors reconcile this with previous work showing that SGD has implicit regularization properties that improve generalization (e.g., Hardt et al., 2015)?
