

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a compelling empirical case for the cause of the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning compared to small-batch methods. The authors claim that the primary reason for this gap is that large-batch methods converge to "sharp minimizers" (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to "flat minimizers" (with many small eigenvalues). They introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify this phenomenon and provide extensive numerical evidence across six different network architectures (F1-F2, C1-C4) and multiple datasets.

The paper makes several important contributions: (1) it provides a clear empirical explanation for the generalization gap; (2) it offers a new metric to measure minimizer sharpness; (3) it demonstrates that warm-starting large-batch training with small-batch iterates can achieve good generalization; and (4) it systematically evaluates several proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training), showing they only partially address the problem.

The paper is well-motivated by the potential benefits of large-batch training (improved parallelism, reduced training time), which makes the problem it addresses highly relevant to the deep learning community. The authors provide a thorough literature review that places their work in the context of prior work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and related theoretical work (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016).

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** with the following key reasons:
1) The paper provides a compelling, well-designed empirical investigation that consistently supports the sharpness hypothesis as the primary cause of the generalization gap.
2) The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding a well-known practical problem in deep learning that affects both research and industry practice.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's main strength is its thorough empirical investigation. The authors conduct extensive experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets, using both parametric plots (Figures 3, 7) and quantitative sharpness metrics (Tables 3-4) to demonstrate the sharpness difference between small-batch and large-batch minimizers. The 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness metrics between the two regimes is particularly convincing.

The paper also effectively addresses alternative explanations for the generalization gap. They rule out overfitting as the primary cause (Figure 2) and provide evidence that the difference is due to the nature of the minimizers themselves. Their warm-start experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly strong evidence: when large-batch training is initialized with small-batch iterates that have already explored flat regions, it achieves good generalization.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with appropriate citations to key prior work. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their purely empirical approach, which is appropriate for the question they're addressing. The connection to potential scalability benefits (Appendix C) is well-motivated and makes a strong case for the importance of their work.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper further, I suggest the following improvements:

1. **Sharpen the connection between the sharpness metric and Hessian eigenvalues**: While the authors mention this relationship, a more formal explanation would strengthen their methodological contribution. How exactly does the metric approximate the largest eigenvalues or Ritz values?

2. **Quantify the "small dimensional subspace" claim**: The paper states that sharp minimizers rise steeply along only a small dimensional subspace (5% of the whole space). Providing specific measurements of this dimensionality across different networks would add significant value.

3. **Clarify the warm-start threshold**: In Figure 5, there appears to be a threshold number of small-batch epochs before warm-starting large-batch training works well. Quantifying this threshold (e.g., "after 20 epochs of SB training") would be helpful.

4. **Add computational cost analysis**: The paper states the sharpness metric is feasible for large networks, but including actual timing measurements (e.g., "computing sharpness for ResNet-50 takes X seconds on a single GPU") would strengthen this claim.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In the warm-start experiments (Figure 5), how many small-batch epochs are typically required before large-batch training achieves good generalization? Is there a clear threshold value?

2. The paper claims that the sharp minimizers identified rise steeply only along a small dimensional subspace (5% of the whole space). Can you provide more details on how this percentage was calculated, and does it vary across different network architectures or datasets?

3. The authors state the generalization gap is not due to overfitting. How do they definitively distinguish between the sharpness hypothesis and a form of overfitting that manifests differently than typical overfitting?

4. Could the authors provide a more formal justification for why their sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a good proxy for the Hessian eigenvalues, particularly in non-convex settings where the Hessian may not be positive definite?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to our understanding of deep learning optimization. While it is purely empirical, the quality of the experiments and the consistency of the results across multiple networks and datasets provide strong evidence for the authors' claims. The paper is well-written, well-motivated, and addresses a problem of significant practical importance. With the suggested improvements, it would make an excellent contribution to the ICLR conference.
