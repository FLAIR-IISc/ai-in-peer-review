- Empirical contribution: extensive experiments show large-batch training converges to "sharp" minima while small-batch finds "flat" minima, linked to worse generalization.
- Novelty: introduces a sharpness metric (Eq.4) and parametric 1D plots to quantify minima geometry across architectures and datasets.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main rejection reason 1: Paper shows correlation between sharpness and poor generalization but does not establish causation.
- Main rejection reason 2: The proposed sharpness metric lacks a rigorous connection to standard Hessian-based sharpness, weakening theoretical grounding.
- Supporting critique: Experiments demonstrate the phenomenon but do not explain why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima (no mechanism/theory).
- Remedies critique: Proposed fixes (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial training) only partially mitigate the gap and lack systematic analysis.
- Recommendation 1: Perform controlled experiments that manipulate minima sharpness to establish causality (e.g., via regularization).
- Recommendation 2: Rigorously connect or replace the sharpness metric with Hessian-based measures (estimate largest eigenvalues).
- Recommendation 3: Develop and test a theoretical hypothesis for why large-batch optimization leads to sharp minima.
- Recommendation 4: Better clarify and differentiate the work from related methods (e.g., Entropy-SGD / prior flat-minima literature).
- Questions for authors: reconcile with Chaudhari et al. (2016) flat-minima claims; report computational cost of the sharpness metric at scale; explain "not resemble a cone" remark; discuss similarity of loss values for sharp and flat minima relative to prior loss-surface theory.
