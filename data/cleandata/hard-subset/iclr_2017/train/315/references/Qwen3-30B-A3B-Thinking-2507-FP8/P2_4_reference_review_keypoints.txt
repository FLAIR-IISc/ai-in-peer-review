- Paper addresses the LB vs SB generalization gap, attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima and SB to flat minima, supported across architectures and datasets.
- Authors provide parametric 1D plots, a computationally feasible sharpness metric, and warm-start experiments showing SB can guide LB to better minima.
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.
- Key reasons: tackles an important practical problem and presents rigorous, multi-architecture empirical evidence.
- Parametric plots (Figures 3 and 7) show LB minima are sharper than SB minima along the line between solutions.
- Sharpness metric (Tables 3 and 4) reports 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sharpness for LB methods across networks.
- Warm-start experiments (Figure 5) indicate SB’s exploration phase enables LB to reach better minima.
- Paper situates itself well in prior literature and acknowledges limitations (no theoretical convergence proof).
- Main weakness: absence of a theoretical foundation for the empirical observations.
- Recommend stronger justification of Metric 2.1 and its theoretical connection to sharp minima in high dimensions.
- Recommend discussion of the computational cost of using L-BFGS-B for the sharpness maximization in large networks.
- Recommend explaining why SB requires a certain number of epochs before warm-starting benefits LB (clarify exploration dynamics).
- Recommend clearer differentiation between the studied generalization gap and standard overfitting.
- Recommend reporting absolute batch sizes used (not just percentages) across datasets.
- Question: How does Metric 2.1 scale to very large networks (e.g., ResNet-152)?
- Question: Is there a correlation between the ratio ‖x_s - x0‖₂ / ‖x_l - x0‖₂ (reported 3–10) and the magnitude of the generalization gap?
- Question: What is meant by “sharp minimizers don't resemble a cone” and why is this significant?
- Question: How many warm-start epochs are typically needed, and does this vary by architecture or dataset?
