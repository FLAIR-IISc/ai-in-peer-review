

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper addresses a significant practical problem in deep learning: the generalization gap observed when training with large batch sizes compared to small batch sizes. The authors make a compelling case that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (characterized by small eigenvalues). The paper supports this claim with multiple lines of evidence across various network architectures (including AlexNet and VGGNet variants) and datasets. The authors present parametric 1D plots showing the sharpness difference, introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric, and conduct warm-start experiments demonstrating that small-batch methods can guide large-batch methods toward better minima. The paper also explores several remediation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) but finds limited success in fully resolving the generalization gap.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons: 
1) The paper addresses a well-known practical problem in deep learning with clear relevance to the community.
2) The evidence presented is scientifically rigorous, with multiple lines of support across various architectures and datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding a fundamental empirical observation in deep learning. The main claim is well-supported by the evidence presented:

- The parametric 1D plots (Figures 3 and 7 in the appendix) clearly show that LB minima are sharper than SB minima along the line between them.
- The sharpness metric (Tables 3 and 4) shows a consistent 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between SB and LB methods across multiple networks.
- The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide compelling evidence that SB methods can guide LB methods to better minima, supporting the authors' claim about the exploration phase of SB methods.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, correctly citing and building upon relevant work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) and recent theoretical work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016; Lee et al., 2016). The authors acknowledge the limitations of their work (no theoretical proof of convergence to sharp minima), which adds credibility.

The paper's main weakness is the lack of a theoretical foundation for their empirical observations, but this is explicitly acknowledged as an open question in the conclusion. The authors have provided sufficient empirical evidence to support their claims within the scope of the paper.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Sharpness metric justification**: The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why Metric 2.1 is appropriate for measuring sharpness in high-dimensional spaces. While the authors note it relates to Hessian eigenvalues, a more rigorous connection to the theoretical definition of sharp minima would strengthen their methodology.

2. **Computational cost**: The paper mentions using L-BFGS-B for the maximization in the sharpness metric, but doesn't discuss the computational cost. For large networks, this could be prohibitively expensive, which would limit the metric's practical utility. A brief discussion of computational considerations would be helpful.

3. **Warm-starting explanation**: The paper observes that SB methods need to run for a certain number of epochs before LB methods can benefit from warm-starting (Figure 5), but doesn't explain why. A brief discussion of the exploration phase of SB methods would clarify this important observation.

4. **Generalization gap vs. overfitting**: The paper states the generalization gap isn't due to overfitting, but the explanation could be clearer. A more explicit differentiation between the generalization gap they're studying and standard overfitting would help readers.

5. **Batch size details**: The paper states "10% of the training data as batch size for large-batch experiments" but doesn't specify the absolute batch sizes across different datasets. Providing these numbers would help readers understand the scale of the experiments.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) scale with network size? Would it be feasible to apply this metric to very large networks like ResNet-152?

2. The paper mentions "the ratio of ‖xₛ - x₀‖₂ and ‖xₗ - x₀‖₂ was in the range of 3-10." Could you elaborate on how this ratio relates to the generalization gap? Is there a correlation between this ratio and the magnitude of the generalization gap?

3. The paper states that sharp minimizers don't "resemble a cone" (meaning the function doesn't increase rapidly in all directions). Could you elaborate on what this means and why it's significant for understanding the generalization gap?

4. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) show that after sufficient warm-starting, LB methods achieve good accuracy. Could you provide more insight into how many epochs of warm-starting are typically needed, and whether this number varies across different architectures or datasets?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding a fundamental empirical observation in deep learning. The evidence presented is scientifically rigorous, and the paper is well-positioned within the literature. With minor revisions addressing the points above, this paper would make a strong contribution to the ICLR community.
