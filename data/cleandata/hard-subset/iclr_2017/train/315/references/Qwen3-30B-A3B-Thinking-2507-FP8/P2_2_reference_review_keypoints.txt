- Main claim: Large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minimizers that generalize poorly, while small-batch (SB) training converges to flat minimizers that generalize better.  
- Empirical evidence: Parametric 1D and curvilinear plots show sharpness differences between LB and SB minimizers.  
- Sharpness metric: Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) to measure sensitivity to small perturbations.  
- Warm-starting: Warm-starting LB from SB solutions reduces sharpness and improves LB generalization, suggesting the minimizer, not the optimizer, causes the gap.  
- Remedies explored: Experiments examine data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training as potential fixes.  
- Contribution significance: Provides a concrete, practically relevant explanation for the LB generalization gap that can guide scalable training research.  
- Decision: Recommend accept with minor revisions.  
- Key reasons for acceptance: Tackles an important practical problem and presents robust empirical evidence across multiple architectures and setups.  
- Experimental thoroughness: Evaluates six network configurations, multiple optimizers (SGD, ADAM, ADAGRAD), and both 1D/curvilinear analyses, strengthening claims.  
- Literature placement: Correctly cites and connects to prior work on sharp minima and loss-surface theory.  
- Limitations: No theoretical guarantees provided; sharpness metric is not a precise Hessian-spectrum measure but is empirically consistent.  
- Suggested clarification 1: Provide more direct evidence linking the sharpness metric to generalization (e.g., counterexamples with similar sharpness but different generalization).  
- Suggested clarification 2: Quantify the computational cost of the sharpness metric versus standard training to assess practicality.  
- Suggested clarification 3: Expand warm-starting results to detail how many SB epochs are needed and how this scales with model size/complexity.  
- Suggested clarification 4: Explicitly connect the sharpness findings to other generalization theories (MDL, Bayesian) to strengthen theoretical context.  
- Suggested clarification 5: Report how the sharpness metric evolves during training and whether its dynamics align with the generalization gap.  
- Questions for authors: How many directions/eigenvalues are “sharp” and how does that percentage relate to generalization?  
- Questions for authors: Is the train/test accuracy difference shown separate from the LB vs SB generalization gap, and how?  
- Questions for authors: In warm-starting, how does sharpness decrease over epochs and when is it “sufficiently flat”?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are results to the choice of ε in the sharpness metric and why were ε = 1e-3 and 5e-4 chosen?
