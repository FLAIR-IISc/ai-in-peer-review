

# Review

## Summary
This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon of the "generalization gap" observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning compared to small-batch methods. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to "sharp minimizers" (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat minimizers" (with smaller eigenvalues), and that this difference in minimizer sharpness explains the observed generalization gap. They present numerical evidence through parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), and explore potential remedies including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training.

## Decision
Reject. The paper lacks sufficient empirical validation and theoretical grounding to support its main claims. The proposed sharpness metric is not rigorously connected to the Hessian spectrum, and the evidence provided does not conclusively establish causality between minimizer sharpness and generalization performance.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's central claim—that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers which cause the generalization gap—lacks sufficient scientific rigor. The authors propose a sharpness metric (Equation 4) that they admit is "imperfect" but computationally feasible, yet they fail to establish a rigorous connection between this metric and the true Hessian eigenvalues that would validate their claim. Without this connection, their entire analysis rests on an unverified metric.

The paper does not adequately address the most important question: is minimizer sharpness the *cause* of the generalization gap, or merely a *correlate*? The authors acknowledge that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training" but provide no causal evidence that sharpness *causes* poor generalization rather than being a symptom of the same underlying issue. The paper's experiments show correlation but not causation.

The paper also fails to compare against established approaches that have already addressed the large-batch generalization problem. For example, Smith et al. (2017) demonstrated that scaling the learning rate with batch size can largely eliminate the generalization gap, yet this paper does not engage with this important prior work. The authors' proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training) are not shown to be significantly better than established methods.

The paper's methodological approach is insufficiently rigorous. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is applied without sufficient justification of its properties, and the paper does not provide statistical significance testing for the reported differences in sharpness between small-batch and large-batch methods. The experiments are conducted with limited network configurations (only 6 networks) and with a fixed batch size ratio (10% vs. 256), without exploring the full range of batch sizes where the generalization gap is observed.

## Additional Feedback

1. The paper would benefit from a more thorough literature review that properly situates its work within the existing body of knowledge about large-batch training. The work of Smith et al. (2017) on learning rate scaling for large-batch training is particularly relevant and should be addressed.

2. The authors should provide a rigorous theoretical connection between their proposed sharpness metric and the true Hessian spectrum. Without this, their metric is just a heuristic with unclear relationship to the claimed phenomenon.

3. The paper should include more comprehensive experiments that test whether their sharpness metric is predictive of generalization performance across a wider range of networks, datasets, and training configurations.

4. The paper should conduct ablation studies to determine whether the observed sharpness is the cause of the generalization gap or merely a correlate. For example, they could train models to converge to sharp minimizers using a different method (not large-batch training) and test whether these models generalize poorly.

5. The paper should address the possibility that the observed sharpness is a result of the training process rather than a cause of the generalization gap. For instance, could the large-batch training process itself be creating these sharp minimizers, which would mean the sharpness is a consequence rather than a cause?

## Clarification Questions

1. The authors claim that the generalization gap is "not due to over-fitting," yet the observed behavior (training accuracy high, testing accuracy lower) is textbook overfitting. How do they reconcile this with standard machine learning theory?

2. The paper states that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." This seems to contradict the definition of sharp minimizers in Hochreiter & Schmidhuber (1997) and the literature. Can the authors clarify this apparent contradiction?

3. The paper claims that their proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) relates to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian, but they do not provide any theoretical justification for this claim. Can the authors provide a mathematical derivation showing this relationship?

4. The authors state that "the generalization gap is correlated with a marked sharpness of the minimizers," but they do not provide any statistical evidence that this correlation is significant. What statistical tests were performed to establish the significance of the correlation between sharpness and generalization gap?
