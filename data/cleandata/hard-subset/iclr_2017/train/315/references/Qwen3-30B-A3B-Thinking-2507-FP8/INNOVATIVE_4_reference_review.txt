

# Review

## Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution by identifying a specific mechanism behind the well-known generalization gap in large-batch deep learning training: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers. The authors provide a practical metric for measuring sharpness (equation 4) and demonstrate through extensive experiments across multiple network architectures that this sharpness correlates strongly with the generalization gap. They also systematically explore several approaches to mitigate this problem (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) and show that while these improve generalization, they don't fully resolve the sharpness issue. The paper's central insight—that the geometry of the loss landscape (specifically sharp vs. flat minima) explains the generalization gap—represents a novel and important conceptual contribution to deep learning theory.

## Decision

Accept

## Key Reasons for Acceptance

1. **Novel Mechanism for a Long-Standing Problem**: The paper identifies a specific, testable mechanism (sharp minimizers) for the generalization gap in large-batch training, moving beyond the common but vague explanation that "large-batch training overfits." This is a significant conceptual advance that provides a clear direction for future research.

2. **Bold Approach and Clear Direction**: The authors don't just observe the problem—they propose a concrete, measurable property (sharpness) that explains it, and they've developed a practical metric to measure it at scale. This gives the field a new lens through which to view the large-batch training problem and provides a clear path for future work.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is not merely incremental—it fundamentally reframes how we understand the large-batch training problem. While the generalization gap has been observed before (LeCun et al., 2012), the authors provide a specific, measurable mechanism that explains it. Their sharpness metric (equation 4) is a practical innovation that enables empirical validation of this mechanism across multiple architectures. The experiments are well-designed, showing consistent results across different networks (AlexNet, VGGNet, etc.) and data sets.

The paper also makes a bold claim that is scientifically testable: "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers." This is not just another empirical observation—it's a hypothesis about the underlying optimization dynamics that could guide future algorithm design. The authors don't stop at identifying the problem; they also attempt to address it with several promising approaches (data augmentation, conservative training), even if these don't fully solve the problem.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novel concepts over methodological perfection, I'm willing to overlook the paper's lack of theoretical proof that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima (only empirical evidence) and its somewhat limited exploration of theoretical connections to existing work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015). The empirical contribution here is substantial enough to warrant acceptance.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Theoretical Connections**: While the paper mentions recent theoretical work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016), it would be valuable to more explicitly connect these theories to the authors' empirical findings. For instance, do the theoretical models predict the existence of sharp minima that large-batch methods converge to? This would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the paper.

2. **Sharpness Metric Justification**: The authors could provide more detailed justification for their choice of sharpness metric (equation 4) versus alternatives. Specifically, how does it relate to the theoretical concept of sharpness in the optimization literature (Ferris, 1988)? While they note this difference, a more thorough discussion would help readers understand the limitations of their metric.

3. **Warm-Starting Analysis**: The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are particularly compelling. It would be valuable to explore the relationship between the number of initial small-batch epochs and the final generalization performance in more detail. This could lead to a practical training strategy that combines the benefits of both approaches.

## Clarification Questions

1. The paper claims "the lack of generalization ability is due to the fact that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers." Could the authors provide additional evidence that the sharpness is causing the generalization gap rather than being a correlated phenomenon? For example, could they show that when they modify the loss function to reduce sharpness (without changing the batch size), generalization improves?

2. The authors note that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." How does this observation relate to recent work on the geometry of loss landscapes (e.g., Chaudhari et al., 2016; Soudry & Carmon, 2016)? Does this suggest that the concept of "sharpness" needs refinement to account for this directional nature?

3. The paper states that conservative training (equation 5) "does not solve the problem of sensitivity." Given that conservative training is designed to better utilize a batch, could the authors speculate why this approach doesn't address the sharpness issue? Is it because the batch size is still too large, or is there a deeper issue with the optimization dynamics?
