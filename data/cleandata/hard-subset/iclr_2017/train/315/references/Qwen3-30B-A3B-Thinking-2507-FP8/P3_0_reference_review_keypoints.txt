- Paper claims large-batch training generalization gap arises because LB methods converge to sharp minima while SB methods find flat minima.
- Authors provide extensive empirical evidence (parametric 1D plots and a sharpness metric) across multiple architectures and datasets.
- They test mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative/robust training) but find only partial success.
- Recommendation: weak accept (minor revisions) due to solid empirical contribution but methodological/theoretical gaps.
- Main strength: thorough, consistent empirical results across architectures (F1–F4, C1–C4) and datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants).
- Major concern: weak theoretical grounding—no explanation for why batch size influences convergence to sharp vs flat minima.
- Major concern: sharpness metric (Eq.4) is ad-hoc and not validated or shown to correlate with Hessian-based sharpness.
- Major concern: evidence that the gap is “not due to overfitting” is inadequate; more rigorous validation is needed.
- Suggestion: add theoretical analysis of SGD noise or other mechanisms explaining why SB avoids sharp minima (warm-start experiments are promising).
- Suggestion: validate sharpness metric on simple models where Hessian eigenvalues can be computed to show correlation.
- Suggestion: clarify how this work extends or differs from prior sharp-minima literature (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber; Chaudhari et al.).
- Suggestion: discuss limitations of the sharpness metric and how non-conical minima affect conclusions.
- Requested detail: quantify the relationship between warm-start epochs and final sharpness/accuracy, and whether a threshold exists.
- Requested evidence: reconcile findings with results like Soudry & Carmon (2016) and provide validation-set/early-stopping experiments to support the “not overfitting” claim.
