

# Peer Review: "The Generalization Gap in Large-Batch Training of Deep Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper investigates the well-documented generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes compared to small batch sizes. The authors make a compelling claim that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with many small eigenvalues). They provide extensive empirical evidence using parametric 1D plots and a novel sharpness metric across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants) and datasets. The paper also explores several mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) but finds them only partially effective. The authors position their work as providing crucial insights into the optimization landscape of deep learning and potential pathways to make large-batch training viable without sacrificing generalization.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak accept** (with minor revisions). The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding a significant practical issue in deep learning optimization. The empirical evidence is thorough and well-presented, and the findings align with recent theoretical work on loss surfaces. However, the paper would benefit from more rigorous theoretical grounding and addressing some methodological concerns.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's main contribution is well-motivated and addresses a significant practical problem in deep learning. The empirical evidence supporting the sharpness hypothesis is impressive in its breadth, covering multiple network architectures (F1-F4, C1-C4), datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants), and using both parametric plots and a sharpness metric. The consistency of the results across these different configurations is particularly strong.

However, there are several concerns that prevent a stronger recommendation:

1. **Theoretical grounding is weak**: The paper claims the generalization gap "is due to the fact that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers," yet provides no theoretical explanation for *why* this happens. While the paper references work on sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016), it fails to build a theoretical argument explaining the convergence difference between batch sizes. This is a significant gap given the paper's central claim.

2. **Sharpness metric lacks validation**: The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is ad-hoc and not rigorously justified. While the authors note it's related to the largest Hessian eigenvalue when A=I_n, they don't provide evidence that this metric actually correlates with the Hessian-based sharpness. For a paper making such a central claim about sharpness, this lack of validation is concerning. The paper would be stronger if they demonstrated this correlation for simpler models where Hessian computation is feasible.

3. **Inadequate justification for "not overfitting" claim**: The paper states the generalization gap isn't due to overfitting, but the evidence (training-testing accuracy curves not peaking and decaying) is insufficient. The paper should provide more rigorous evidence, such as comparing validation performance during training or showing that the gap persists even with early stopping.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- **Strengthen theoretical foundation**: Even a brief exploration of how the noise in small-batch SGD might prevent convergence to sharp minima (through an analysis of the stochastic gradient noise) would significantly strengthen the paper. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) suggest a promising direction for theoretical analysis.

- **Validate sharpness metric**: The authors should compute the sharpness metric for a simpler model (e.g., a small MLP) where they can also compute the Hessian eigenvalues directly, and show the correlation between the two measures. This would provide much stronger justification for their metric.

- **Clarify relationship to prior work**: The paper references work on sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016), but doesn't clearly distinguish its contribution. The authors should more explicitly state how their findings build on or differ from these earlier works.

- **Discuss limitations more thoroughly**: The paper should acknowledge that their sharpness metric is a heuristic and discuss its limitations. For example, they mention sharp minima "do not resemble a cone," but don't explore how this relates to their metric or what implications this has for their conclusions.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you reconcile your findings with the recent theoretical work by Soudry and Carmon (2016) that shows "no bad local minima" for deep networks? Do your results suggest that the "good" minima are flat while the "bad" minima are sharp?

2. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) suggest a promising approach. Could you provide more details about the exact relationship between the number of warm-starting epochs and the final sharpness/accuracy? Is there a threshold number of warm-starting epochs beyond which the LB method consistently finds flatter minima?

3. The paper claims the generalization gap "is not due to over-fitting," but the evidence provided is limited to the shape of training-testing curves. Could you provide additional evidence, such as comparing the performance on validation sets during training for SB and LB methods, or showing that the gap persists even with early stopping?

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding a critical practical issue in deep learning optimization. With the suggested revisions—particularly strengthening the theoretical foundation and validating the sharpness metric—it would be an excellent contribution to the field.
