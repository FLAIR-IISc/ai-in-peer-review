

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper investigates a well-documented empirical phenomenon in deep learning: the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that this gap is correlated with the "sharpness" of the minimizers found by these methods. They propose a computationally feasible metric to measure sharpness (Equation 4) and demonstrate that large-batch methods converge to significantly sharper minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) than small-batch methods, which converge to flatter minimizers. The paper explores several approaches to mitigate this problem (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) but finds they do not fully solve the issue. The work provides important empirical insights into the behavior of different batch size training regimes, which has significant implications for scalable deep learning training.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The main reasons for this recommendation are:
1. The paper addresses a significant, well-known empirical problem in deep learning that has practical implications for scalable training.
2. The empirical evidence presented is robust, consistent across multiple network architectures, and supported by multiple complementary metrics (parametric plots, sharpness measurements).

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a strong empirical case for its central claim. The parametric 1-D plots (Figure 3) clearly illustrate the sharpness difference between small-batch and large-batch solutions. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is well-designed to be computationally feasible for large networks, and Tables 3 and 4 provide quantitative evidence of the orders-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between small-batch and large-batch methods.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, citing key works on optimization (Bottou, Sutskever), generalization (Hardt et al.), and the concept of flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, Chaudhari et al.). The discussion of related work in Section 4 is thorough and appropriately contextualizes the contributions.

The authors carefully distinguish between overfitting and the generalization gap, demonstrating that the observed phenomenon is not due to overfitting (Figure 2). The experiments are well-designed with multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants), multiple trials (5 runs per experiment), and appropriate baselines.

The paper also makes an important practical contribution by presenting a performance model (Appendix C) that illustrates the potential benefits of large-batch training if the generalization gap could be resolved, which is particularly relevant for the deep learning community.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

- **Theoretical justification**: While the empirical evidence is strong, the paper would benefit from more theoretical insight into why large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers. The authors mention that "noise in the gradient pushes the iterates out of the basin of attraction of sharp minimizers," but this could be more rigorously explained.

- **Optimizer comparison**: The paper uses ADAM for both small and large batch sizes, but it's worth noting that SGD is more commonly used for large-batch training. It would be valuable to see if the results hold with SGD as the optimizer for large-batch methods.

- **Sharpness metric clarification**: The paper mentions that the sharp minimizers identified do not resemble a cone, but this claim could be better supported with more detailed analysis of the directional sensitivity of the loss function.

- **Warm-starting explanation**: The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are particularly interesting, but the paper could better explain why warm-starting with small-batch iterations helps large-batch methods find flatter minima.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "large-batch methods are attracted to regions with sharp minimizers." Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim beyond the parametric plots? For instance, could you analyze the convergence paths to better understand how the optimization trajectory leads to sharp minima?

2. In Table 3, the sharpness metric shows a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference between SB and LB methods. Could you elaborate on why the difference is consistent across networks despite their different architectures and data domains?

3. The paper uses ADAM for both SB and LB methods, but SGD is typically used for large-batch training. How would your results change if you used SGD for large-batch training? Is the sharpness difference dependent on the optimizer used?

4. In Section 4, the authors mention "dynamic sampling" as a potential remedy. How do your warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) inform the optimal dynamic sampling strategy, and what would be the most effective way to implement this in practice?

5. The paper states that conservative training and data augmentation "improve the generalization of large-batch methods but still lead to relatively sharp minima." Could you provide more insight into why these approaches don't fully address the root cause of the sharpness issue?

This paper makes an important contribution to our understanding of batch size effects in deep learning training, and the evidence presented is compelling. With the suggested improvements, the paper would be even stronger and more impactful for the deep learning community.
