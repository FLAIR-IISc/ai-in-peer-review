

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper addresses a well-known practical challenge in deep learning: the generalization gap observed when training with large-batch methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors present compelling numerical evidence suggesting that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with smaller eigenvalues). This observation aligns with prior work on the relationship between minimizer geometry and generalization. The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing concrete visualizations (parametric plots) and a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify this phenomenon across multiple network architectures. The authors also thoughtfully explore potential remedies, including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training, though these show limited success in fully resolving the issue.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting hypothesis supported by reasonable numerical evidence, but the theoretical foundation is insufficiently developed, and the empirical claims require more rigorous validation. The sharpness metric lacks a strong theoretical connection to the Hessian spectrum, and the experimental evidence is limited in scope.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution is the observation that large-batch training leads to sharper minima, which correlates with poorer generalization. This is a valuable insight that connects to prior work on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and recent theoretical advances (Choromanska et al., 2015). The parametric plots in Figures 3 and 7 provide compelling visual evidence of the sharpness difference.

However, the paper falls short on two critical dimensions:

First, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not rigorously connected to the Hessian spectrum. The authors note that "Metric 2.1 is closely related to the spectrum of ∇²f(x)" but provide no mathematical justification for this claim. The metric computes the maximum function value in a neighborhood, which is not equivalent to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian (which would be the limit as the neighborhood size approaches zero). This is a critical gap because the paper's entire thesis depends on the relationship between the metric and the Hessian.

Second, the experimental evidence is limited to just six network configurations (Table 1) across a small number of datasets (Table 5). The paper claims "Results on other networks and using other initialization strategies, activation functions, and data sets showed similar behavior," but provides no evidence for this assertion. The generalization gap is well-known in the community, and the paper's contribution would be significantly strengthened by a more comprehensive experimental evaluation across diverse architectures and datasets.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this work for future publication, the authors should:

1. Provide a rigorous mathematical connection between their sharpness metric (Equation 4) and the Hessian spectrum. This could include theoretical bounds or a proof that their metric approximates the largest eigenvalue under certain conditions.

2. Expand the experimental evaluation to include a wider range of network architectures (including modern transformers, ResNets, etc.), datasets, and optimization methods beyond ADAM. The current experiments focus heavily on ADAM and two network types (AlexNet/VGG variants).

3. Include a more detailed analysis of the relationship between batch size and sharpness. The paper mentions "a threshold" after which generalization degrades (Section 3), but doesn't provide a systematic study of how sharpness changes with batch size.

4. Better contextualize their sharpness metric with existing approaches. For example, how does their metric compare to the one used in Chaudhari et al. (2016) or Keskar et al. (2016)? A comparison would strengthen their claims.

5. Address the causality question more directly: Is sharpness the *cause* of poor generalization, or is it merely a correlate? The paper suggests sharpness causes poor generalization, but the experiments don't rule out alternative explanations (e.g., large-batch methods simply have different convergence properties).

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The authors claim that "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" (Section 2.1). Can you provide a more formal statement of this claim, and if possible, a theoretical argument for why this occurs (beyond just empirical observation)?

2. In Table 3 and 4, the sharpness metric shows a 1-2 order of magnitude difference between SB and LB methods. How does this magnitude compare to the sharpness differences observed between different models trained with the same batch size? For example, how does the sharpness of a poorly generalizing small-batch model compare to a well-generalizing one?

3. The paper states that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training" (Section 2.2). However, the training curves in Figure 2 show that both SB and LB methods eventually reach similar training accuracy. How can you be certain the gap isn't due to overfitting that occurs during the final convergence phase, rather than the fundamental nature of the minima?

4. The warm-starting experiment in Figure 5 suggests that LB methods can achieve good generalization if warm-started with SB methods. Does this imply that the fundamental issue is not with the LB method itself, but with the initial conditions of the optimization process? If so, how does this change the interpretation of the paper's main claim?
