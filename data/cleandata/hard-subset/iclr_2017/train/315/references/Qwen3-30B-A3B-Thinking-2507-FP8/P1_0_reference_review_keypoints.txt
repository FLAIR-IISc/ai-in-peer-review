- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), attributing it to LB converging to "sharp" minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) and proposes a practical sharpness metric (Eq.4).
- Empirical evidence provided across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG variants) and datasets, including parametric plots showing sharpness differences.
- Authors explore partial remedies and relate work to prior theory on sharp/flat minima and loss landscapes.
- Recommendation: Accept, due to importance of the problem and thorough empirical validation.
- Strength: Comprehensive experiments across six network configurations and datasets supporting the central claim.
- Strength: Parametric plots (Figures 3 and 7) give clear visual evidence that LB minima are significantly sharper than SB minima.
- Strength: Sharpness metric (Eq.4) is a computationally feasible proxy for the largest Hessian eigenvalue; Tables 3–4 show 1–2 orders of magnitude difference between SB and LB.
- Strength: Authors address alternative explanations (overfitting, saddle points); warm-start experiment (Figure 5) shows LB can generalize if initialized from SB flat minima.
- Improvement: Quantify the generalization gap more precisely with a table of exact percentages across networks.
- Improvement: Clarify in more detail how the sharpness metric approximates the Hessian's largest eigenvalue.
- Improvement: Expand on the warm-starting experiment and its practical implications for LB training.
- Improvement: Discuss the computational cost of computing the sharpness metric (use of L-BFGS-B) and its practicality.
- Question: Provide exact generalization gap numbers for each network configuration tested.
- Question: How sensitive are sharpness measurements to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations used?
- Question: Can the authors demonstrate causation (sharpness causes poor generalization) rather than mere correlation, or rule out confounding factors?
- Question: What is the computational cost of warm-starting compared to standard LB training, and does it negate parallelization benefits?
- Question: How do the authors distinguish the reported generalization gap from standard overfitting seen in training/testing accuracy curves?
