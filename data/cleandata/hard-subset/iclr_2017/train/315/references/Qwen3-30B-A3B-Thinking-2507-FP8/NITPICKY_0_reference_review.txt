

# Review of "Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper investigates the generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes compared to small batch sizes. The authors propose that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with small Hessian eigenvalues). They support this claim with numerical experiments using parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and explore potential remedies including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training. The paper makes a potentially valuable contribution to understanding a well-known practical challenge in deep learning optimization.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and formatting issues that undermine the credibility of the results and prevent confident assessment of the claims. The authors fail to properly present key results, and the technical details of their methodology are insufficiently justified.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's most significant flaws fall into three categories: missing critical information, technical inconsistencies, and inadequate justification.

First, the paper references key tables (3 and 4) and figures (5 and 7) that are not included in the manuscript. Without these, the reader cannot verify the claimed "1-2 order-of-magnitude difference" in sharpness metrics between small-batch and large-batch methods. The absence of these critical visualizations severely limits the ability to assess the empirical evidence.

Second, there is a critical technical inconsistency in the sharpness metric definition (Equation 4): "φx,f ( , A) := (maxy∈C f(x+Ay))− f(x) + f(x) × 100." This expression is mathematically incorrect as written, suggesting the metric is (max f(x+Ay) - f(x) + f(x) × 100), which would be nonsensical. It should be (max f(x+Ay) - f(x)) / f(x) × 100. The paper does not clarify this, and this error casts doubt on all sharpness measurements presented.

Third, the paper makes unsupported claims about the nature of the generalization gap. The statement that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training" is presented as fact but lacks empirical justification. The paper observes that the test accuracy doesn't peak and then decline (as in typical overfitting), but this is not sufficient to rule out generalization issues as a form of overfitting. The paper provides no evidence to distinguish between the two phenomena.

Additionally, the paper contains multiple formatting errors in equations (e.g., missing spaces, inconsistent notation in Equation 1), and uses the term "generalization gap" without clear definition. The choice of batch sizes (10% for large-batch, 256 for small-batch) is not justified relative to dataset size, and the paper doesn't explain why these specific values were chosen.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. Provide complete figures and tables referenced in the text (Tables 3, 4, Figure 5, Figure 7). Without these, the empirical claims cannot be verified.

2. Correct the sharpness metric definition in Equation 4 to be mathematically precise. Clarify whether the metric is (max f(x+Ay) - f(x)) / f(x) × 100 or something else.

3. Justify the batch size choices relative to dataset size. For example, clarify whether 10% is considered "large" for the specific datasets used.

4. Provide evidence to distinguish between generalization gap and overfitting. The paper's current argument that "this is not what we observe" is insufficient.

5. Address the technical inconsistency in Equation (4) by providing a clear mathematical definition and justification for the metric's use.

6. Include a more precise definition of "generalization gap" early in the paper to avoid confusion.

7. Clarify the relationship between the sharpness metric and the Hessian eigenvalues, as the paper claims this connection but doesn't provide supporting evidence.

8. Provide more details on the number of L-BFGS-B iterations used for the sharpness calculation and why 10 iterations was deemed sufficient.

The paper has an interesting hypothesis that merits investigation, but the current presentation lacks the rigor and completeness required for publication in ICLR. The missing key results and technical inconsistencies prevent confident assessment of the claims. I would welcome a revised version that addresses these issues before reconsideration.
