

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper investigates the generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large-batch methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) that generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (characterized by small eigenvalues) that generalize better. They support this claim through numerical experiments across multiple deep learning architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, etc.), using parametric plots and a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify the difference between minimizers found by small-batch and large-batch methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant empirical contribution to understanding a well-known practical issue in deep learning. The main reasons for acceptance are the well-designed experiments that provide compelling evidence for the sharpness-generalization connection, and the thorough exploration of multiple network architectures and batch sizes.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Technical Soundness and Methodology:** The paper's methodology is sound and well-justified. The authors carefully address the computational challenges of directly measuring Hessian eigenvalues by proposing a practical sharpness metric (Equation 4) that approximates the relationship between the metric and the Hessian's spectrum. They validate this metric through both 1D parametric plots and more complex curvilinear paths (Appendix D), providing consistent evidence across different visualization techniques. The experimental setup is robust, with multiple trials (5 runs from different starting points), multiple network architectures (6 total), and appropriate baselines.

**Literature Placement and Motivation:** The paper is well-placed in the literature, correctly situating itself within the established discussion of flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997) and the practical problem of generalization gaps in large-batch training (LeCun et al., 2012). The motivation is strong as it addresses a fundamental practical concern in deep learning that impacts real-world training efficiency. The paper also thoughtfully connects to recent theoretical work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016).

**Empirical Evidence:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The sharpness metric consistently shows a 1-2 order of magnitude difference between small-batch and large-batch solutions across all experiments (Tables 3-4). The parametric plots (Figures 3 and 7) visually demonstrate the sharpness difference in a compelling way. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) provides particularly strong evidence that small-batch methods can guide large-batch training toward flatter minimizers, directly linking the exploration phase of small-batch methods to the quality of large-batch solutions.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed explanation of why the sharpness metric (Equation 4) correlates with generalization performance. While the connection to the Hessian spectrum is noted, a more formal discussion of this relationship would strengthen the theoretical foundation.

- The paper mentions that the sharp minimizers identified in experiments "do not resemble a cone" (Section 2.2.2), but this is not fully supported by the presented evidence. A more thorough analysis of the directional sensitivity (e.g., which specific directions exhibit sharpness) would help clarify this point.

- The discussion of related work (Section 4) could be expanded to better connect to recent theoretical work on sharp minima, particularly the work by Chaudhari et al. (2016) on Entropy-SGD, which also explores flat minima and generalization.

- The paper could provide more context about the computational cost of the sharpness metric, especially for very large networks, to help readers understand its practical applicability.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In Section 2.2.2, you state that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone." What evidence do you have that the sharpness is not uniform across all directions? How did you determine the specific dimensionality of the sharp subspace (e.g., the 5% mentioned)?

2. The paper uses ADAM for both small-batch and large-batch experiments. How would the results differ if you used SGD with momentum for the large-batch experiments, given that the authors of (Zhang et al., 2015) suggest that large-batch training with SGD may have different properties than with adaptive methods?

3. In Appendix E.2, you mention that conservative training improves testing accuracy but doesn't solve the sensitivity problem. Could you provide more details on how the regularization parameter λ was selected, and whether the improvement is consistent across all network architectures?
