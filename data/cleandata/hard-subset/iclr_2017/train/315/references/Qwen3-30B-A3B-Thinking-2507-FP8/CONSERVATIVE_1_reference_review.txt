

**Review**

1. Summary of paper's claims/contribution: The paper claims to investigate the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods in deep learning, asserting that this gap is due to large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with small eigenvalues), which generalize better. The authors propose a sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify this phenomenon and present empirical evidence from 6 network configurations. They also describe preliminary attempts to mitigate the problem using data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training.

2. Decision: Reject. The paper lacks sufficient scientific rigor to support its main causal claim, and the evidence provided is correlational rather than causal. The sharpness metric used is not properly validated against the Hessian eigenvalues, and the experimental design does not adequately rule out alternative explanations.

3. Supporting arguments:
- The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is described as "imperfect" but is used as the primary evidence without establishing its validity as a proxy for Hessian eigenvalues. The connection between the metric and the Hessian is only tangential, and the paper fails to demonstrate that the metric actually correlates with the relevant Hessian properties in a meaningful way. The authors note "the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions" but claim the minimizers are sharp, creating a contradiction that isn't addressed.
- The paper reports a "generalization gap" of up to 5% but provides no statistical evidence to confirm this is not due to random variation. The results are presented as averages across 5 trials but without confidence intervals or statistical tests to validate the significance of the observed differences.
- The paper fails to adequately engage with the existing literature, particularly Keskar et al. (ICLR 2017), which presents nearly identical results and claims. This suggests the authors did not properly contextualize their work in the literature, raising concerns about novelty.
- The experiments are limited in scope (only 6 network configurations) and do not explore the parameter space thoroughly. The claim "we have observed that the loss function landscape of deep neural networks is such that large-batch methods are attracted to regions with sharp minimizers" is presented as fact without sufficient evidence.
- The attempts to remedy the generalization gap (Section E) are described as "preliminary findings" without sufficient detail to evaluate their effectiveness. The paper claims these approaches "do not completely remedy the problem" but doesn't provide clear evidence of why they fail or how they compare to established methods.

4. Additional feedback:
- The authors should rigorously validate their sharpness metric against Hessian eigenvalues using smaller networks where Hessian computation is feasible. Without this, the metric's validity as a proxy for "sharpness" remains unproven.
- The paper should conduct proper statistical analysis of the reported generalization gap, including confidence intervals and p-values, to confirm it's not due to random variation.
- The authors should clarify how their work differs from Keskar et al. (ICLR 2017) and explain why this paper represents a significant advance over existing work.
- The paper should explore whether the generalization gap persists when using appropriate learning rate scaling for large-batch methods, as suggested by Smith et al. (2017), which is a well-established approach to address this issue.
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed exploration of alternative explanations for the generalization gap, such as differences in hyperparameters (learning rates, weight decay) between small-batch and large-batch methods.

Questions for authors:
1. How did you validate that your sharpness metric (Equation 4) actually correlates with the Hessian eigenvalues? Have you tested this on smaller networks where Hessian computation is feasible?
2. Can you provide statistical evidence (p-values, confidence intervals) for the generalization gap you report? The 5% drop is mentioned but not statistically validated.
3. How does your work differ from Keskar et al. (ICLR 2017), which presented nearly identical results and claims?
4. Did you test whether the generalization gap persists when using appropriate learning rates for large-batch methods? Previous work suggests that with proper learning rate scaling, large-batch methods can achieve similar generalization to small-batch methods.
5. How do you reconcile your claim that LB methods converge to sharp minimizers with your observation that "the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions"? This appears contradictory to the definition of a sharp minimizer.

The paper presents an interesting observation about the generalization gap, but the evidence provided is insufficient to support the causal claim that sharp minimizers are the cause of the gap. The authors need to provide much stronger empirical and theoretical justification for their claims before this work can be considered for publication at ICLR.
