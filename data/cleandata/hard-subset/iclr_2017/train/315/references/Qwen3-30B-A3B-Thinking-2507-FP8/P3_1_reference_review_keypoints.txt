- Paper's claim: large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch (SB) converges to flatter minima, explaining LB's worse test performance; introduces a practical sharpness metric and presents experiments on multiple networks/datasets.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main rejection reason 1: Paper presents correlation, not a demonstrated causal link between sharpness and poor generalization.  
- Main rejection reason 2: The proposed sharpness metric (Eq. 4) lacks theoretical validation as a proxy for the Hessian spectrum and for predicting generalization.  
- Metric concerns: Dependence on arbitrarily chosen epsilon and random matrix A makes the metric subjective; no formal connection to generalization error is provided.  
- Visualization concern: Parametric plots (e.g., Fig. 3) show function along a single path, which may not represent overall landscape sharpness; curved-path analyses are not thoroughly discussed.  
- Causation gap: Authors suggest gradient noise causes SB to find flat minima but provide no direct evidence isolating noise as the mechanism; warm-start results are suggestive but not conclusive.  
- Remedies: Explored fixes (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) show limited effectiveness, which is informative but not sufficient.  
- Suggested experiments: Manipulate sharpness directly (e.g., modify loss) to test causal impact on generalization and isolate gradient noise effects from other hyperparameters.  
- Suggested theory work: Provide theoretical justification for Eq. 4, potentially linking it to generalization bounds (PAC-Bayes, uniform convergence).  
- Suggested empirical expansion: Test more diverse architectures and datasets (e.g., transformers) to assess generality.  
- Questions for authors: reconcile with Dinh et al. (2017) on flat minima generalization counterexamples; provide details on warm-start epoch requirements and plateau; clarify distinction between studied generalization gap and standard overfitting; report statistical significance (std. dev.) of sharpness values across runs.
