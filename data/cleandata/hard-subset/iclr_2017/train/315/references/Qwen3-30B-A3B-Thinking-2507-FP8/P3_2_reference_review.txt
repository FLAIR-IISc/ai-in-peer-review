

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims and Contribution

This paper makes a valuable empirical contribution to understanding the well-known generalization gap observed when training deep learning models with large batch sizes versus small batch sizes. The authors propose that this gap stems from large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity to input perturbations) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (characterized by low sensitivity). They support this claim with extensive numerical experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets, using both parametric 1D plots and a novel sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify the sharpness of minima. The paper also explores potential remedies for the generalization gap, including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training, though these approaches only partially mitigate the problem.

The paper is well-motivated and places itself within the context of existing work on the generalization gap (LeCun et al., 2012), sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), and recent theoretical analyses of deep learning loss landscapes (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016). The authors' focus on the geometric properties of minima as an explanation for the generalization gap represents a significant contribution to the field.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

**Key reasons for rejection:**
1. The causal relationship between sharp minima and poor generalization is not definitively established; the paper shows correlation but not causation.
2. The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not rigorously connected to the standard Hessian-based measure of sharpness, limiting the theoretical foundation of the claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary contribution is the empirical evidence linking large-batch training to sharp minima. While the parametric plots (Figure 3) and sharpness metric results (Tables 3-4) are compelling, they suffer from a critical limitation: they demonstrate correlation but not causation. The authors observe that large-batch methods converge to points with higher sharpness values, and these points generalize worse, but they do not establish that the sharpness *causes* the poor generalization. It's equally plausible that some other factor (e.g., the specific optimization trajectory) causes both the sharp minima and the poor generalization.

More fundamentally, the paper's sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not properly connected to the standard definition of sharpness in optimization. While the authors note that their metric relates to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian, they don't rigorously establish this relationship or demonstrate how their metric compares to the standard Hessian-based measure. This weakens the theoretical foundation of their claims and makes it difficult to interpret their results within the broader context of optimization theory.

The paper also fails to provide a theoretical explanation for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima. The authors observe this phenomenon but don't explain the underlying mechanism, which is a significant gap for a paper claiming to explain a fundamental phenomenon in deep learning.

The attempted remedies (Section E) are also problematic. While the authors report modest improvements with data augmentation and conservative training, they don't demonstrate a clear path to completely resolving the generalization gap. The paper would be stronger with a more systematic analysis of why these remedies only partially work.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper significantly, the authors should:

1. **Establish causality:** Conduct controlled experiments where they explicitly manipulate the sharpness of minima (e.g., through regularization techniques) and measure the resulting generalization performance. This would move the paper from correlation to causation.

2. **Refine the sharpness metric:** Either provide a rigorous theoretical connection between their metric and the Hessian-based measure, or replace it with a more standard measure. The paper would be much stronger if they directly computed or estimated the largest eigenvalues of the Hessian (or a good approximation) for their minima.

3. **Provide a theoretical explanation:** Develop a hypothesis for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima, and support it with additional experiments or analysis. Without this, the paper remains an empirical observation without a mechanism.

4. **Clarify the relationship to prior work:** While the paper cites relevant literature, it doesn't sufficiently distinguish its contribution from recent work like "Entropy-SGD" (Chaudhari et al., 2016) that also connects flat minima to generalization.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you reconcile your findings with the "flat minima" hypothesis proposed by Chaudhari et al. (2016)? Your results suggest that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima, but Chaudhari et al. claim that SGD (with noise) finds flat minima. Is there a fundamental difference in how "flat" is defined?

2. Could you provide more details about the computational cost of your sharpness metric? Given that you're using L-BFGS-B for 10 iterations to solve the maximization problem, how expensive is this for larger networks (e.g., ResNet-50)?

3. The paper states that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone" (Section 2.2.2). Could you elaborate on what this means and how it relates to the standard understanding of sharp minima in optimization?

4. You mention in Section 4 that "both sharp and flat minimizers have very similar loss function values." How does this align with the theoretical work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016) which suggests that many local minima have similar loss values?

The paper makes an important observation that could help guide future work on scalable deep learning training, but it currently lacks the theoretical rigor and causal evidence needed to be accepted at ICLR. With the suggested revisions, particularly addressing the causality issue and refining the sharpness metric, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
