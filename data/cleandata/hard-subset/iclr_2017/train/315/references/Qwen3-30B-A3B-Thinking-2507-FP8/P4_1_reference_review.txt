

1. Summary: The paper investigates the generalization gap observed in large-batch training of deep learning models. The authors claim that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with small eigenvalues). They introduce a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify this difference and provide empirical evidence across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants) and datasets. The paper also explores several approaches to mitigate the generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) but finds they only partially address the problem.

2. Decision: Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing thorough empirical evidence linking sharp minimizers to the generalization gap in large-batch training, which is a well-known practical problem in deep learning. The methodology is sound, and the results are consistently supported across multiple experimental settings.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper addresses a critical practical problem in deep learning with broad implications for scalable training. The authors provide compelling evidence through parametric 1D plots (Figure 3) and their sharpness metric (Tables 3-4), showing consistent 1-2 order-of-magnitude differences in sharpness between small-batch and large-batch minimizers. The sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is well-justified as a computationally feasible alternative to Hessian computation, and the authors demonstrate its correlation with generalization performance across six different network configurations. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly insightful evidence that small-batch exploration is necessary to reach flat minimizers that large-batch methods can then converge to. The paper's approach is well-placed in the literature, building on prior work about sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) while providing new empirical evidence specific to deep learning.

4. Additional feedback: 
- The paper could strengthen its theoretical grounding by more thoroughly connecting the sharpness metric to existing theories of generalization (MDL, Bayesian learning). While the paper mentions these theories, a more detailed explanation of how they specifically relate to the observed sharpness-generalization relationship would enhance the contribution.
- The claim that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting" could be better substantiated with additional evidence beyond the statement that training-testing curves don't show the typical overfitting pattern. A brief supplementary figure showing these curves would clarify this important point.
- The paper uses ADAM for both small and large batch experiments. It would be valuable to see if the sharpness-generalization relationship holds for other optimizers (like SGD with momentum) to demonstrate the generality of the findings.

Questions for authors:
1. The paper states that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting, but doesn't provide detailed evidence beyond noting the absence of a typical overfitting curve. Could you provide additional evidence (e.g., training-testing accuracy curves for both regimes) to more clearly demonstrate this distinction?
2. The paper mentions that experiments with other optimizers (ADAGRAD, SGD, adaQN) for large-batch methods led to similar results but doesn't present these results. Could you share these results to demonstrate the generality of your findings beyond ADAM?
3. In Section 3, the warm-starting experiments show that after a certain number of small-batch epochs, large-batch methods can achieve good generalization. What is the minimum number of small-batch epochs required for this effect to manifest, and how does this vary across different network architectures?
