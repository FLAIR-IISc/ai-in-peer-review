

# ICLR Peer Review

## Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper addresses a significant and well-documented empirical observation in deep learning: large-batch training methods often yield poorer generalization performance compared to small-batch methods, despite achieving similar training loss. The authors make a compelling case that this "generalization gap" stems from large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minima (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) rather than "flat" minima (with many small eigenvalues) that small-batch methods converge to. The paper provides comprehensive empirical evidence across six different network architectures (including AlexNet and VGGNet variants), using both parametric 1D plots of the loss function and a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify the sharpness of minima. The authors further demonstrate that warm-starting large-batch methods with small-batch iterations can significantly improve generalization, suggesting that the issue lies not with the large-batch method itself but with the basin of attraction it converges to. This work makes a valuable contribution to understanding the fundamental mechanisms behind the generalization gap in deep learning.

## Decision

**Accept**

**Key Reasons for Acceptance**
1. The paper addresses a significant, well-known problem in deep learning with a clear hypothesis that is well-motivated by existing literature.
2. The empirical evidence provided is robust, consistent across multiple network architectures, and includes multiple complementary metrics to support the core claim.

## Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper's central hypothesis—that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima that generalize poorly while small-batch methods converge to flat minima that generalize well—is well-supported by the evidence. The parametric plots (Figure 3) clearly show that large-batch solutions are significantly sharper than small-batch solutions along linear paths between minima. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) provides a computationally feasible way to quantify this difference, with Tables 3 and 4 demonstrating a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between the two regimes across all networks.

The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are particularly compelling, as they demonstrate that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization performance when initiated from small-batch solutions. This provides strong evidence that the issue is with the basin of attraction rather than the large-batch method itself. The authors also effectively distinguish their findings from overfitting, noting that the training-testing curves don't exhibit the typical overfitting pattern (where validation performance peaks and then declines).

The paper is well-placed in the literature, citing relevant work on sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), the generalization gap (LeCun et al., 2012), and related theoretical work (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016). The methodology is clearly described, and the experiments are conducted with appropriate controls (multiple trials, different starting points, various network configurations).

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. **Theoretical Context**: The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of how the authors' findings relate to recent theoretical work on loss landscapes (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016). Specifically, the authors might discuss whether their empirical observations align with or challenge the theoretical models of the loss surface.

2. **Sharpness Metric Nuances**: The authors acknowledge that their sharpness metric is an approximation of the Hessian spectrum. They could provide more detail about the limitations of this metric—particularly how it behaves when the Hessian has negative eigenvalues or when the loss surface has complex curvature.

3. **Comparison with Entropy-SGD**: The paper cites Chaudhari et al. (2016) on "Entropy-SGD" (which aims to find flat minima), but doesn't discuss how their findings relate to this approach. A brief comparison would help situate their work more clearly in the literature.

4. **Reproducibility**: The GitHub link mentioned in Section 2.2.1 appears to be broken ("https://github.com/keskarnitish/large-batch-training"). Ensuring this link works would significantly improve reproducibility.

## Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and strengthen the paper, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

1. The paper states that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training." Can you provide more specific evidence for this claim? For example, could you show training and validation loss curves over time to demonstrate that the gap appears even before the training loss has stabilized?

2. In Section 3, you mention that "the ratio of ‖x_s^* − x_0‖₂ and ‖x_l^* − x_0‖₂ was in the range of 3–10." How does this ratio vary across different networks and initialization methods? Is there a correlation between this ratio and the generalization gap?

3. You note that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." What is the typical dimensionality of the subspace along which the function increases rapidly? Does this dimensionality correlate with the generalization gap?

4. In Appendix E.3, you state that adversarial training "did not improve generalization." Could you elaborate on why these approaches failed to improve generalization, given that they are designed to improve robustness? Is there a theoretical reason why robust optimization might not address this specific issue?

The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding a fundamental problem in deep learning practice. The empirical evidence is strong, the methodology is sound, and the implications for future work are clear. I believe this paper will be of significant interest to the deep learning community and should be accepted for publication at ICLR.
