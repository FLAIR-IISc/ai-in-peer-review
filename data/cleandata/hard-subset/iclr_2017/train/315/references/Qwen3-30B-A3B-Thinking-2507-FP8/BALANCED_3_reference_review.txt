

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper investigates the well-documented generalization gap between small-batch and large-batch training methods for deep learning. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity to weight perturbations, with many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with many small eigenvalues). They support this claim with numerical evidence using parametric plots and a proposed heuristic sharpness metric. The paper also attempts to address this problem with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization, though these approaches only partially mitigate the generalization gap.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes a plausible claim about the relationship between batch size and sharpness of minima, but the evidence provided is insufficient to support the main claim. The paper lacks theoretical justification and the empirical evidence is limited in scope.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary weakness of the paper is that it relies solely on numerical evidence without providing theoretical justification for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima. While the authors present interesting numerical results using their sharpness metric, they fail to establish this metric as a reliable proxy for the actual sharpness of minima. The metric (Equation 4) is described as "computationally feasible" but is not validated against established measures of sharpness. Without this validation, the main evidence for the paper's central claim is questionable.

The paper also lacks theoretical grounding. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima, but provide no theoretical argument for this phenomenon. This is a significant gap, as the paper's central claim rests entirely on this observation. The authors cite related theoretical work (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016), but these works do not specifically address why large-batch methods would converge to sharp minima rather than flat ones.

The empirical evidence is limited to only 6 network configurations across a small number of datasets. The authors claim their results are broadly applicable to deep learning, but the limited scope of experiments makes this claim difficult to support. Additionally, the paper's assertion that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting is not sufficiently supported. The authors present Figure 2 showing training-testing curves, but don't provide evidence to rule out overfitting as an explanation (e.g., showing that test accuracy continues to improve with more training rather than peaking and declining).

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more rigorous theoretical foundation for their claims. At minimum, they should prove (or provide a compelling argument) that large-batch methods are more likely to converge to sharp minima under certain conditions.

2. Validate their sharpness metric against established measures. They should compare their metric with direct computation of Hessian eigenvalues on smaller networks to demonstrate its reliability.

3. Expand the empirical evaluation to include more diverse architectures (e.g., RNNs, transformers) and datasets (e.g., NLP tasks) to demonstrate broader applicability.

4. Provide a more thorough analysis of the overfitting explanation. The paper should explicitly compare training and testing accuracy curves to show that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting (e.g., by demonstrating that test accuracy continues to improve with additional training).

5. Clarify the relationship between their sharpness metric and the true Hessian. The current description is vague, and it's unclear how well their metric approximates the actual sharpness of minima.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you justify that your sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a reliable proxy for the sharpness of minima? Have you compared it with direct computation of Hessian eigenvalues on smaller networks?

2. Can you provide theoretical evidence (or at least a more compelling argument) for why large-batch methods would converge to sharp minima, rather than relying solely on numerical evidence?

3. Have you tested your claims on more diverse network architectures (e.g., RNNs, transformers) or datasets (e.g., natural language processing tasks)?

4. How do you address the possibility that the generalization gap is simply due to overfitting, which would be consistent with the training and testing accuracy curves you present in Figure 2?
