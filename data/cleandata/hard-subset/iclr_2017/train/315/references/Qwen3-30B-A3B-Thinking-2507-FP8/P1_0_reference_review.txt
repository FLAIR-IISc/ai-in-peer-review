## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

This paper addresses a significant practical challenge in deep learning: the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors present compelling empirical evidence that this gap is due to large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minima (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) rather than "flat" minima, which generalize better. They support their claims through extensive numerical experiments across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants) and datasets, including parametric plots demonstrating the difference in sharpness and a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) that quantifies this difference. The paper also explores potential remedies for the generalization gap, though the authors find these approaches only partially effective. The work is well-placed in the literature, connecting to relevant theoretical work on sharp/flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) and the loss landscape of deep neural networks (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016).

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The main reasons for this decision are:

1. The paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning with significant implications for parallelization and scalability of training.
2. The empirical evidence supporting the authors' claims is thorough, well-structured, and presented across multiple architectures and datasets.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to understanding why large-batch training methods suffer from a generalization gap. The main strength is its comprehensive empirical investigation across six different network configurations and multiple datasets. The parametric plots (Figures 3 and 7) provide clear visual evidence that LB minima are significantly sharper than SB minima, which strongly supports the authors' central claim.

The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a clever and practical solution to the computational challenge of directly computing Hessian eigenvalues. The authors provide a clear explanation of how this metric relates to the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian, which is theoretically sound. The results in Tables 3 and 4 consistently show a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between SB and LB regimes, reinforcing the authors' claim.

The paper thoughtfully addresses potential alternative explanations for the generalization gap (e.g., overfitting, saddle points) and provides evidence against them. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) is particularly compelling, demonstrating that LB methods can achieve good generalization if started from an SB solution that has already found a flat minimizer. This provides strong evidence that the issue is specifically related to the sharpness of the minima rather than other factors.

The paper is well-motivated by the practical observation that large-batch training is desirable for parallelization but suffers from a generalization gap, and the authors situate their work well within the existing literature.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be improved:

1. **Quantify the generalization gap more precisely**: The paper mentions "the drop in generalization (also called generalization gap) to be as high as 5% even for smaller networks" but doesn't provide specific numbers. Adding a table with exact generalization gap percentages across different networks would strengthen the paper.

2. **Clarify the relationship between the sharpness metric and the Hessian**: While the authors note the connection to the largest eigenvalue, a more detailed explanation of how the metric approximates this eigenvalue would help readers understand its validity.

3. **Expand on the warm-starting experiment**: The warm-starting approach (Figure 5) is one of the most compelling results, but it's only briefly mentioned. A more detailed description of this experiment and its implications for practical large-batch training would be valuable.

4. **Address the computational cost of the sharpness metric**: The paper mentions using L-BFGS-B to solve the maximization problem but doesn't discuss the computational cost of this metric, which is important for understanding its practicality.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify my understanding and provide additional evidence for my assessment, I have the following questions:

1. The paper claims "the generalization gap is as high as 5% even for smaller networks." Can the authors provide specific numbers for this claim, perhaps in a table showing the exact generalization gap for each network configuration tested?

2. In the sharpness metric (Equation 4), the authors use "10 iterations of L-BFGS-B" to solve the maximization problem. How sensitive are the sharpness measurements to the number of iterations used? Would using more iterations lead to different results?

3. The paper states that "the solutions obtained by a large-batch method defines points of larger sensitivity of the training function." Can the authors provide evidence that this sensitivity is indeed the cause of the generalization gap, rather than a correlation? For example, could there be a third factor causing both sharp minima and poor generalization?

4. In Figure 5, the warm-starting experiment shows that after a certain number of epochs, LB methods achieve good generalization. What is the computational cost of this approach compared to standard large-batch training? Would this approach be feasible in practice, or does it negate the parallelization benefits of large-batch training?

5. The paper mentions that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training." How do the authors distinguish between the generalization gap observed in their experiments and the typical overfitting that occurs in training-testing accuracy curves (Figure 2)?
