

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper addresses a well-documented but poorly understood phenomenon in deep learning: the generalization gap between large-batch and small-batch training methods. The authors claim that this gap arises because large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity in the loss landscape), while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers that generalize better. The paper provides compelling numerical evidence supporting this claim through parametric 1D plots and a proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), demonstrating that large-batch solutions consistently exhibit higher sharpness values across multiple network architectures. The authors also explore potential remedies, including data augmentation and conservative training, though they find these approaches only partially mitigate the problem. The paper is well-motivated and connects to prior work on loss surface geometry, making a valuable contribution to understanding deep learning optimization.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**. While the paper presents interesting empirical observations, it lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and contains methodological concerns that prevent it from making a strong contribution to the field. The key issue is that the paper establishes correlation but fails to provide sufficient evidence for causation regarding sharp minima causing the generalization gap.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's main weakness is its inability to establish causation between sharp minima and poor generalization. The authors observe a correlation between sharp minima and poor generalization but fail to rule out alternative explanations. For instance, the paper claims the generalization gap isn't due to overfitting, yet it doesn't provide evidence against this alternative explanation (e.g., showing training loss continues to decrease while testing loss increases for LB methods). The observed "generalization gap" could equally be explained by LB methods simply learning a model with higher complexity (not necessarily sharp minima) that overfits the training data.

The paper's sharpness metric (Equation 4) is also problematic. The authors acknowledge it's "imperfect" but don't justify why it's a valid proxy for the eigenvalue spectrum of the Hessian, which is the standard measure of sharpness. The metric's dependence on the choice of matrix A (random vs. identity) and the box size parameter ε introduces significant ambiguity. The paper doesn't compare this metric with other established sharpness measures (e.g., the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian, or the "flatness" measure used in Chaudhari et al., 2016), making it difficult to assess the validity of their conclusions.

Furthermore, the paper uses ADAM for both SB and LB training, but ADAM's behavior is batch-size dependent, potentially confounding the results. The paper doesn't explore whether the phenomenon persists with SGD (which is more commonly used with large batches in practice), making the findings less generalizable to typical large-batch training scenarios.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The authors should provide a theoretical argument or proof that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima (not just empirical evidence). This would strengthen the causal claim.

2. **Alternative Explanations**: The paper should more thoroughly address alternative explanations for the generalization gap (e.g., overfitting, different convergence rates) and provide evidence against them.

3. **Sharpness Metric**: The authors should compare their metric with established sharpness measures and demonstrate its validity through theoretical or empirical analysis.

4. **Optimizer Comparison**: The paper should test with SGD (not just ADAM) for large-batch training, as this is the standard practice in the field.

5. **Mechanism Explanation**: The paper should provide a more detailed explanation of *why* large-batch methods converge to sharp minima (e.g., through a formal analysis of the noise in the gradient).

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How would the results change if you used SGD (not ADAM) for large-batch training? Given that ADAM is not typically used with large batches in practice, this is a critical question.

2. Can you provide evidence that the observed generalization gap isn't simply due to overfitting (e.g., by plotting training and validation loss curves for both SB and LB methods)?

3. How does your sharpness metric (Equation 4) relate to the eigenvalue spectrum of the Hessian? Can you provide theoretical justification for using this metric as a proxy?

4. The paper claims that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training as commonly observed in statistics." However, this is a key claim that needs stronger evidence. What specific evidence do you have that rules out overfitting as the cause?

5. In Section 3, you describe a warm-starting experiment where LB methods started from SB iterates improve. Does this suggest that the fundamental issue is the *path* taken to reach the minimum (not the minimum itself)? If so, how does this reconcile with your main claim about sharp minima?

In conclusion, while the paper presents interesting empirical observations, it fails to establish the causal relationship it claims. The methodological concerns regarding the sharpness metric and the lack of theoretical grounding prevent this from being a strong contribution to the field. Addressing these issues would substantially strengthen the paper.
