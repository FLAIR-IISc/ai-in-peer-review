

# Peer Review: "Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the generalization gap in large-batch deep learning training. The authors present compelling empirical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flatter minimizers. This observation provides a concrete explanation for the well-documented phenomenon where large-batch training achieves similar training accuracy but significantly worse test performance compared to small-batch methods. The paper introduces a practical sharpness metric (Equation 4) that allows for efficient assessment of minimizer properties without computing the full Hessian, and provides thorough numerical experiments across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, fully-connected networks) and datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, CIFAR-10).

The paper's key contribution lies in connecting the empirical observation of the generalization gap to the geometric properties of the loss landscape, providing a clear hypothesis that can guide future research. The authors also explore potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) and demonstrate their limited effectiveness, which is valuable for the community.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

**Key Reasons:**
1. The paper fails to establish a causal relationship between sharp minima and generalization performance, relying solely on correlation.
2. The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is not sufficiently validated as a proxy for the Hessian spectrum, and the paper does not address how this metric relates to generalization theoretically.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

While the paper presents interesting empirical observations, it falls short of establishing a scientifically rigorous causal link between sharp minima and generalization performance. The paper states: "The lack of generalization ability is due to the fact that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers," but the evidence provided is correlational rather than causal. The authors show that LB methods converge to sharper minima and have worse generalization, but they do not demonstrate that the sharpness *causes* the poor generalization. It's possible that both phenomena are caused by some third factor (e.g., different optimization trajectories).

The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a critical component of the paper's argument, yet its theoretical justification is weak. The authors note that it "relates to the largest eigenvalue of ∇²f(x)" but provide no formal connection to generalization. The metric is defined as a sensitivity measure in a neighborhood of the solution, but the paper doesn't establish how this measure correlates with generalization error across different networks or datasets. The metric's dependence on the choice of (which is set arbitrarily to 10⁻³ and 5×10⁻⁴) and the random matrix A introduces significant subjectivity.

Furthermore, the paper's interpretation of the parametric plots (Figure 3) is questionable. The authors claim LB minima are "sharper" but the plots only show the function along a single path between SB and LB solutions. It's unclear whether this single path is representative of the overall landscape. The authors acknowledge in Appendix D that they also plot along curved paths, but they don't discuss whether the sharpness is consistent across different paths.

The paper also fails to address a critical question: Why do small-batch methods converge to flatter minima? The authors suggest that noise in the gradient helps escape sharp minima, but they don't provide evidence that this noise is actually sufficient to cause the observed behavior. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) suggests that after sufficient exploration, LB methods can converge to flatter minima, but this doesn't prove that the noise is the cause—it could be that the initial exploration phase simply moves the optimizer to a region where LB methods can converge to flat minima.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper has strong empirical evidence but needs stronger theoretical grounding. I recommend:

1. **Establish a causal link**: Conduct controlled experiments where the sharpness of minima is manipulated (e.g., through loss function modification) while holding other factors constant, and observe the impact on generalization.

2. **Validate the sharpness metric**: Provide theoretical justification for Equation 4 as a proxy for generalization, perhaps by connecting it to known generalization bounds (e.g., PAC-Bayesian bounds, uniform convergence).

3. **Expand the analysis**: Include more diverse architectures (e.g., transformers, vision transformers) and datasets to strengthen the claim that this phenomenon is general across deep learning.

4. **Clarify the role of noise**: Perform experiments to isolate the effect of gradient noise from other factors (e.g., different learning rates, weight decay) to establish whether noise is indeed the mechanism driving the difference between SB and LB methods.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How do you reconcile your findings with the recent work by Dinh et al. (2017) that showed flat minima can have high generalization error? Does your sharpness metric capture the relevant properties that distinguish good and bad flat minima?

2. In Figure 5, you show that warm-starting LB methods with SB iterations leads to better generalization. Can you provide more details on how many epochs of SB training are required for this effect, and whether the improvement plateaus after a certain number of epochs?

3. The paper states that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training." However, Figure 2 shows a clear difference in training and testing accuracy curves. How do you distinguish between the generalization gap you're studying and the standard overfitting phenomenon?

4. In Table 3 and 4, the sharpness metric shows large differences between SB and LB methods. However, the absolute sharpness values are not reported in terms of standard deviations across trials. Could you provide this information to assess the statistical significance of the differences?

This paper presents an interesting observation that could guide future research on large-batch training, but the current evidence does not sufficiently establish a causal relationship between sharp minima and generalization performance. With stronger theoretical justification and more rigorous experimental validation, this work could become a valuable contribution to the field.
