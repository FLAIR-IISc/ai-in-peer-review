- Paper addresses the generalization gap: large-batch methods converge to sharp minima and generalize worse than small-batch methods.  
- Authors provide empirical evidence across six architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants).  
- Use of parametric 1D loss plots and a computational sharpness metric (Eq. 4) to quantify sharp vs. flat minima.  
- Sharpness metric shows 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sharpness for large-batch solutions (Tables 3–4).  
- Parametric plots (Figure 3) demonstrate large-batch solutions are significantly sharper along linear paths between minima.  
- Warm-starting large-batch training with small-batch iterations improves generalization, implicating basin of attraction.  
- Authors argue the generalization gap is not due to overfitting or over-training (training/testing curves do not show classic overfitting).  
- Paper is well-situated in related literature and uses sound methodology with multiple trials and controls.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Suggest adding discussion connecting results to theoretical loss landscape work (Choromanska et al., Soudry & Carmon).  
- Recommend clarifying limitations and behavior of the sharpness metric, especially with negative Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Suggest comparing findings to Entropy-SGD and other flat-minima-seeking methods.  
- Reproducibility issue: provided GitHub link appears broken and should be fixed.  
- Question: provide training/validation loss curves to support claim that gap precedes loss stabilization.  
- Question: how does the ratio ‖x_s*−x_0‖₂ / ‖x_l*−x_0‖₂ vary across networks/initializations and correlate with the generalization gap?  
- Question: what is the dimensionality of the subspace where loss increases rapidly, and does it correlate with the generalization gap?  
- Question: why did adversarial/robust training fail to improve generalization in these experiments; is there a theoretical explanation?
