- Paper hypothesis: large-batch (LB) methods converge to sharp minimizers (large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch (SB) methods find flatter minima, explaining LB generalization gap.  
- Decision: Recommend acceptance with minor revisions.  
- Strength 1: Well-motivated and well-situated in prior literature, clearly articulating the LB vs SB generalization problem.  
- Strength 2: Robust, multi-faceted empirical evidence (parametric 1D plots, sharpness metric, consistent results across architectures/datasets, warm-starting experiments).  
- Strength 3: Authors transparently acknowledge limitations and use a computationally feasible sharpness proxy while exploring remedies.  
- Improvement 1: Provide stronger theoretical justification connecting the sharpness metric (Eq.4) to the Hessian spectrum.  
- Improvement 2: Systematically investigate how sharpness varies with increasing batch size and characterize the observed threshold behavior.  
- Improvement 3: Clarify the scope of generalization — explicitly state which architectures/tasks findings are expected to hold or not.  
- Improvement 4: Expand discussion of practical implications of warm-starting, including potential computational benefits in real-world scenarios.  
- Question 1: How sensitive are sharpness metric results to the number of L-BFGS-B iterations used in the maximization?  
- Question 2: How do authors reconcile LB solutions’ concentrated sharpness in a small subspace (~5%) with the sharpness metric’s relation to the full Hessian spectrum?  
- Question 3: How does the required number of warm-start SB epochs depend on network architecture and dataset; is there a general rule of thumb?
