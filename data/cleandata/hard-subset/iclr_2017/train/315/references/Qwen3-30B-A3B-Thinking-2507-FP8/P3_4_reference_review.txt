

# ICLR Review

## Summary of Contributions
This paper investigates the well-known generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning compared to small-batch methods. The authors make the compelling claim that this gap is not due to overfitting, but rather stems from large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with small Hessian eigenvalues). They support this claim through parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and explore several mitigation strategies including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training. The paper makes a valuable contribution by providing empirical evidence that connects the generalization gap to the geometry of the loss landscape.

## Decision
Reject. While the paper presents interesting empirical observations, it fails to establish causation between sharp minima and poor generalization, which is central to its main claim. The evidence presented only demonstrates correlation, not causation.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary contribution is the empirical observation that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima than small-batch methods, as measured by their proposed metric (Equation 4). The parametric plots (Figure 3) and sharpness metrics (Tables 3-4) show a clear correlation between large-batch training and sharper minima. However, the paper's main claim—that sharp minima *cause* the generalization gap—lacks sufficient evidence.

The authors acknowledge that their sharpness metric is not rigorously connected to the actual Hessian eigenvalues, noting it "approximates the Ritz value of ∇²f(x) projected onto the column-space of A." This is a significant limitation, as the literature on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) is based on Hessian eigenvalues, not their proposed metric. Without establishing this connection, the paper cannot claim to be measuring the standard definition of sharpness.

Furthermore, the paper's dismissal of overfitting as an explanation for the generalization gap is not well-supported. The authors claim the generalization gap "is not due to over-fitting or over-training as commonly observed in statistics," but their Figure 2 shows training and testing curves that don't exhibit the typical overfitting pattern (testing accuracy declining after a peak). However, they don't provide a theoretical justification for why this pattern would be absent in deep learning, nor do they rule out other forms of overfitting that might occur without this specific pattern.

The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are interesting, showing that LB methods warm-started with SB iterates achieve better generalization. However, this only demonstrates that SB iterates can lead to better generalization, not that sharpness *causes* the generalization gap. It's equally plausible that warm-starting with SB iterates gives LB methods a better initial point in the loss landscape, rather than addressing sharpness directly.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Theoretical justification**: The paper needs to provide a theoretical argument for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima, not just empirical evidence. Without this, the paper remains an empirical observation rather than a theoretical contribution.

2. **Sharpness metric validation**: The authors should validate their sharpness metric against actual Hessian eigenvalues for small networks where computation is feasible. This would strengthen the connection between their metric and the standard definition of sharpness.

3. **Overfitting analysis**: The paper should provide a more rigorous argument against overfitting as an explanation for the generalization gap. This could include comparing the training and testing losses at the point of convergence, or analyzing the model's capacity relative to the training data.

4. **Causation vs. correlation**: The paper should explicitly discuss alternative explanations for the correlation between sharp minima and poor generalization. For example, it's possible that sharp minima are simply more common in the loss landscape, and large-batch methods happen to find them more often.

5. **Architecture analysis**: The paper uses a limited set of network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants). It would be valuable to see if the phenomenon holds across a wider range of architectures, including more modern ones like ResNets or transformers.

## Questions for Authors

1. How do you know your sharpness metric (Equation 4) is measuring the same concept as the standard definition of sharpness based on Hessian eigenvalues? Can you provide evidence that your metric correlates with actual Hessian eigenvalues?

2. The paper claims the generalization gap is not due to overfitting, but doesn't provide a strong theoretical argument for why the typical overfitting pattern (testing accuracy declining after a peak) is absent. Can you explain why overfitting isn't a plausible explanation?

3. In your warm-starting experiments (Figure 5), you observe that LB methods warm-started with SB iterates achieve better generalization. Does this suggest that the sharpness of the final solution depends on the initial point, rather than being solely a property of the training algorithm?

4. How do you explain the fact that small-batch methods converge to flatter minima? Is this a property of the loss landscape, or does the noise in small-batch gradients actively push the optimization toward flatter regions?

While the paper presents an interesting empirical observation, it falls short of establishing the causal relationship it claims. The evidence presented supports a correlation between sharp minima and poor generalization, but not causation. Without this crucial link, the paper's main contribution is limited to an interesting observation rather than a meaningful theoretical insight. The paper would need substantial additional theoretical justification and validation to merit acceptance.
