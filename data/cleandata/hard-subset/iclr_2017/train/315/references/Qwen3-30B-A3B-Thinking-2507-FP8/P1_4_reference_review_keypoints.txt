- Hypothesis: large-batch (LB) training converges to sharp minimizers that generalize poorly, while small-batch (SB) training finds flatter minimizers with better generalization.  
- Empirical support via parametric 1D loss plots and a novel sharpness metric (Equation 4) across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG, etc.).  
- Sharpness metric shows a consistent 1–2 order of magnitude higher sharpness for LB vs SB minima (Tables 3–4).  
- Warm-start experiments indicate LB methods can generalize well if initialized from SB-found flat minima, implicating basin-of-attraction differences.  
- Proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative/robust training) were explored but did not fully eliminate the sharpness/generalization gap.  
- Methodological strengths: multiple networks, multiple trials, appropriate statistics, and strong positioning within related theoretical and practical work.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions due to the paper’s significance and robust empirical evidence.  
- Requested clarification on whether sharpness causally leads to poor generalization or is merely correlated, and need for additional theoretical justification beyond MDL/Bayesian arguments.  
- Ask for more detail on computational cost and scalability of the sharpness metric (e.g., number of L-BFGS-B function evaluations).  
- Request clearer explanation of the warm-starting/exploration dynamics and why SB must “explore” before LB can succeed.  
- Suggest explicitly connecting the observation that sharpness rises in a small subspace (~5%) to why this harms generalization.  
- Question whether the sensitivity measure refers to training loss or generalization performance and evidence of correlation across datasets.  
- Ask how the reported distance ratio (‖x_s − x0‖2 vs ‖x_l − x0‖2 of 3–10) relates to sharpness and whether flat minima are farther from initialization.  
- Request elaboration on evidence used to rule out overfitting/over-training as the cause of the generalization gap.  
- Ask why adversarial training, despite reducing input sensitivity, failed to improve generalization with respect to the sharpness hypothesis.
