

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper makes an important empirical contribution by investigating the generalization gap between small-batch and large-batch training methods in deep learning. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to "sharp" minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers, and that this sharpness correlates with poorer generalization performance. The paper offers a well-structured analysis with clear parametric plots, a novel sharpness metric, and thoughtful exploration of potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization). The connection to prior work on sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) is well-placed, and the warm-starting experiments provide particularly insightful evidence.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper makes an interesting empirical observation, the claims lack sufficient theoretical grounding and the evidence, though compelling, is limited in scope. The paper does not sufficiently establish why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima (rather than just observing that they do), and the sharpness metric, while computationally feasible, lacks rigorous theoretical justification.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is the empirical observation that large-batch methods converge to sharper minimizers than small-batch methods. This observation is well-supported by the parametric plots and the sharpness metric (Equation 4) across multiple network architectures. However, the paper fails to adequately address the key question: *why* do large-batch methods converge to sharp minima? The authors observe the phenomenon but do not provide a theoretical explanation for it, which is a significant gap for a paper making such a fundamental claim about optimization behavior.

The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is practical for large networks but lacks rigorous connection to the Hessian. While the authors note the relationship to eigenvalues, the metric itself is ad hoc and not fully validated against Hessian computations (which would be prohibitively expensive). This weakens the paper's core claim about "sharp" vs. "flat" minima.

The experiments are limited in scope: only 6 network configurations are tested, and the results are presented for specific batch sizes (10% of training data for large-batch, 256 for small-batch). The paper does not explore the full range of batch sizes or test on more diverse architectures (e.g., transformers, GNNs), making it difficult to claim generalizability.

Finally, while the warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) are insightful, they only demonstrate a correlation between SB exploration and LB performance, not causation. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to establish that the sharpness of the minimizer is the *cause* of the generalization gap, rather than a correlated factor.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this paper for future submission, the authors should:
1. Provide a theoretical argument for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima, possibly by analyzing the stochasticity in the gradient updates (e.g., showing how noise in small batches acts as a regularizer).
2. Conduct more rigorous validation of the sharpness metric by comparing it with direct Hessian computations on smaller networks where this is feasible.
3. Expand the experimental scope to include more diverse architectures, batch sizes, and optimization algorithms (e.g., SGD with momentum, which is commonly used in practice).
4. Address the causal relationship between sharpness and generalization more thoroughly by conducting ablation studies that isolate the sharpness factor from other variables.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide a theoretical explanation for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima, rather than just empirically observing this phenomenon? How does the batch size affect the properties of the stochastic gradient that lead to this behavior?

2. The paper notes that the sharp minima identified in experiments "do not resemble a cone" (Section 2.2.2), meaning the function increases rapidly only along a small subspace. How does this observation relate to the sharpness metric (Equation 4), and how might this inform the development of a more theoretically grounded sharpness measure?

3. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) show that LB methods initialized with SB iterates achieve better generalization. Can you quantify the minimum number of SB epochs required to achieve this improvement, and does this number correlate with the sharpness metric?
