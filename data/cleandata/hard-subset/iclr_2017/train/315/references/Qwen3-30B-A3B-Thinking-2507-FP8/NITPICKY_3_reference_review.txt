## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper investigates the "generalization gap" observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning, claiming that this gap is due to large-batch methods converging to "sharp" minimizers (with many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to "flat" minimizers (with many small eigenvalues). The authors support this claim with parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric, and they explore several attempts to remedy the generalization problem for large-batch methods.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper contains multiple methodological inconsistencies and insufficiently justified claims that undermine the scientific rigor of the empirical results, despite the interesting problem being addressed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's central claim - that the generalization gap is due to large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers - is not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. Several critical methodological flaws undermine the validity of the conclusions:

First, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) has significant mathematical and conceptual issues. The metric is defined as:
"φx,f ( , A) := (maxy∈C f(x+Ay))− f(x) + f(x) × 100"

This formulation contains a critical mathematical error: it should be (maxy∈C f(x+Ay) - f(x)) / f(x) × 100, but the way it's written would be interpreted as (maxy∈C f(x+Ay) - f(x) + f(x)) × 100 = maxy∈C f(x+Ay) × 100. This would produce nonsensical results and invalidate all sharpness measurements reported in Tables 3-4.

Second, the paper claims "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting" but provides no evidence to rule out this common explanation. The authors state "this is not what we observe in our experiments" but don't provide the necessary training-testing accuracy curves to demonstrate this. Without showing the typical overfitting behavior (where test accuracy peaks and then declines), the claim is unsubstantiated.

Third, the experimental setup contains arbitrary choices without justification. The authors state they used "10% of the training data as batch size for the large-batch experiments" but don't explain why 10% was chosen. This arbitrary choice makes the results difficult to interpret and reproduce. Similarly, the use of ADAM for both small and large batch methods is not justified, as ADAM is typically associated with small batches.

Fourth, the paper contains multiple minor formatting inconsistencies that suggest a lack of attention to detail: inconsistent spacing in mathematical notation (Equations 1 and 2), typo in "Appedix E" (should be "Appendix E"), and the reference to "Figure 1" without its appearance in the provided text.

These methodological flaws collectively undermine the paper's central claim and the validity of the reported results. While the problem being studied is important, the evidence provided is insufficient to support the conclusions drawn.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the mathematical error in the sharpness metric definition (Equation 4) and provide a proper justification for the metric's formulation.

2. Provide training-testing accuracy curves to demonstrate that the observed behavior is not due to overfitting.

3. Justify the choice of 10% as the large-batch size and explain why this specific value was chosen rather than a more standard value like 50% or 100%.

4. Justify the use of ADAM for large-batch training rather than other optimizers that might be more appropriate for this regime.

5. Clarify the relationship between the sharpness metric and the actual eigenvalues of the Hessian, as the paper claims this connection but doesn't provide evidence.

6. Address the issue that the sharpness metric might be influenced by the choice of optimizer (ADAM) rather than the batch size itself.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide the specific training-testing accuracy curves for the networks mentioned in Section 2.2 that would demonstrate the absence of overfitting behavior, as claimed in Section 2.2?

2. Why was ADAM chosen as the optimizer for large-batch training, given that it's typically used with small batches? What evidence is there that ADAM is appropriate for large-batch methods?

3. Can you provide a more detailed justification for the constraint set C in Equation (3), particularly why it's defined using the pseudo-inverse of A?

4. In Table 2, you report testing accuracy but not training accuracy. Why is training accuracy not reported, given that it's crucial for understanding the generalization gap?

5. The paper claims "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone," but provides no evidence for this claim beyond a general statement. Could you provide specific evidence or analysis to support this observation?
