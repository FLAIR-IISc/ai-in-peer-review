- Paper claims large-batch training converges to sharp minimizers with many large positive Hessian eigenvalues, while small-batch training finds flatter minimizers that generalize better.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Lacks rigorous theoretical framework; relies mainly on empirical evidence that may not generalize.  
- Main reason 2: Fails to sufficiently explore alternative explanations (e.g., gradient-noise effects, regularization).  
- Methodology concern: Experiments use varied architectures but are limited to a narrow set of hyperparameters, raising generalization doubts.  
- Related-work gap: Inadequate discussion and comparison to prior work on flat vs. sharp minima (e.g., Hochreiter & Schmidhuber).  
- Sharpness metric issue: Introduces a new sharpness metric but does not analyze its properties or limitations thoroughly.  
- Suggested improvement: Provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of large-batch convergence and its effect on generalization.  
- Suggested improvement: Investigate alternative explanations like batch-size-induced gradient noise and regularization impacts.  
- Suggested improvement: Explore a broader hyperparameter range (batch sizes, learning rates, regularization) to test robustness.  
- Suggested improvement: Compare to other generalization-improving methods (batch norm, dropout, data augmentation).  
- Question for authors 1: Provide a detailed analysis of the sharpness metric’s properties and limitations.  
- Question for authors 2: How do you address the concern that results may be specific to the studied architectures and tasks?  
- Question for authors 3: Can you more thoroughly explore alternative explanations (gradient noise, regularization) for generalization degradation?  
- Question for authors 4: How will you extend this work to other architectures and tasks?
