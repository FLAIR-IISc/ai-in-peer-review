- Paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), claiming LB finds sharp minima while SB finds flat ones.
- Provides extensive numerical evidence across architectures/datasets supporting the sharp-vs-flat hypothesis.
- Uses parametric loss-landscape plots and introduces a sharpness metric to quantify minima differences.
- Evaluates mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) and finds them only partially effective.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary reason: lacks a comprehensive theoretical foundation and no robust solution to the LB generalization gap.
- Strength: Addresses an important, well-known problem relevant to the community.
- Strength: Thorough and consistent numerical experiments across multiple models and datasets.
- Strength: Sharpness metric and visualizations give useful insights into loss landscape differences.
- Weakness: Insufficient theoretical grounding explaining why LB converges to sharp minimizers.
- Weakness: Proposed mitigation strategies are not convincingly effective, limiting practical impact.
- Weakness: Does not sufficiently explore alternative explanations (optimization dynamics, initialization).
- Suggestion: Provide rigorous theoretical analysis (e.g., role of gradient noise or landscape geometry).
- Suggestion: Develop or test training algorithms that steer LB toward flat minima (e.g., dynamic batch sizing).
- Suggestion: Expand experiments to more optimizers, regularizers, and initialization schemes; justify sharpness metric hyperparameter choices.
- Questions for authors: Can you prove why LB converges to sharp minima; did you study initialization effects; can dynamic or hybrid batch strategies fully mitigate the gap?
- Conclusion: Valuable empirical insights but needs stronger theory and practical solutions before acceptance.
