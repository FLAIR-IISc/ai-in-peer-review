Summary and contributions
- The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training despite achieving similar training loss. It advances the hypothesis that LB methods converge to sharp minima, whereas SB methods (due to gradient noise) find flatter minima that generalize better.
- It provides supporting empirical evidence across several architectures and datasets: 1-D and curvilinear interpolations between SB/LB solutions show markedly sharper basins around LB solutions; a “sharpness” metric based on local worst-case perturbations differs by 1–2 orders of magnitude between LB and SB; and a warm-start experiment suggests SB’s early noisy exploration can guide LB to flatter regions.
- It evaluates attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial training), finding partial but insufficient improvements, and points to dynamic sampling as a promising direction.
- The work ties results to existing theory on nonconvex landscapes (multiple minima with similar training loss) and gives a performance motivation for LB training.

Decision: Weak accept.
Key reasons: (1) The paper addresses an important and timely question with broad practical impact and provides compelling, if empirical, evidence for a plausible mechanism (sharp vs. flat minima). (2) The experimental design contains several thoughtful probes (interpolations, warm-start) that triangulate the hypothesis and will influence follow-up work.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Clear articulation of the generalization gap in LB training, with consistent empirical trends across multiple architectures (fully connected and convnets) and datasets.
- The parametric plots and warm-start experiments provide intuitive, model-agnostic evidence that SB noise can help escape sharp basins, whereas LB gets trapped.
- The sharpness proxy, while heuristic, is computationally tractable and systematically distinguishes SB vs. LB solutions under the paper’s settings.
- The study goes beyond mere observation by probing remedies and discussing system-level implications.

Weaknesses and threats to validity
- Sharpness definition and invariance. The proposed metric depends on the weight parameterization. For ReLU/Bn networks, trivial layer rescalings can alter local curvature without changing the function. The attempt at normalizing via the box constraints (|(A+x)i|+1) is not obviously invariant to such reparameterizations. Without an invariance check (e.g., rescale one layer and inversely rescale the next), the link between “sharpness” in weight space and generalization remains fragile.
- Optimizer and hyperparameter confounds. Most results use ADAM, whose effective step sizes depend on batch size. Although Appendix E claims similar outcomes for other optimizers, the main narrative should control for SGD with momentum (the de facto baseline in vision) with batch-size-appropriate learning-rate/momentum/weight-decay schedules. It is unclear whether LB runs were equivalently tuned (e.g., LR scaling, decay schedule) and trained for comparable numbers of updates.
- Compute and training protocol parity. SB and LB differ in number of parameter updates per epoch. Training “until the loss ceases to improve” does not equalize either updates or total examples processed, potentially confounding generalization. Results should be reported under matched budgets (fixed number of updates, passes, and/or wall-clock compute).
- Heuristic maximization. The sharpness proxy uses 10 L-BFGS-B steps; its values may vary with epsilon, number of iterations, and the random subspace dimension p. Sensitivity analyses are limited, and statistical uncertainty across seeds is modest (n=5).
- Scope. Datasets and models are relatively small; generality to modern deeper/wider nets is suggested but not convincingly demonstrated here.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Reparameterization test: Construct functionally equivalent networks via layer rescaling; report whether the sharpness metric is stable and whether the SB/LB gap persists.
- Curvature estimates: Complement the proxy with top Hessian eigenvalue(s) via power/Lanczos (even on subsets or blocks) to corroborate sharpness differences.
- Hyperparameter controls: Provide thorough sweeps for LR, momentum, and weight decay per batch size, and report best-of-sweep results. Include SGD+momentum as primary.
- Protocol parity: Report results when (i) the total number of parameter updates is matched, and (ii) the total number of processed examples is matched; show whether the gap persists.
- Sensitivity: Vary epsilon, p, and L-BFGS-B iterations in the sharpness metric; include uncertainty bands to quantify robustness.
- Correlation analysis: Scatter plots of test accuracy vs. sharpness across seeds and settings would strengthen the claimed association.
- BatchNorm effects: Because BN depends on batch statistics, it can confound batch-size effects. Consider freezing BN statistics or using group norm/weight norm to isolate effects.
- Additional remedies: Try explicit gradient noise injection for LB (annealed Gaussian noise), label smoothing, or noise in activations to mimic SB stochasticity.
- Public code and exact training scripts would aid reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates, momentum/β1-β2, and weight decay selected for each batch size and optimizer? Did you apply batch-size-dependent LR scaling or schedule tuning to ensure fair comparisons?
- For SGD+momentum, do the qualitative conclusions (parametric plots and sharpness gap) hold across well-tuned settings? Please include full tables/plots.
- How sensitive is the sharpness metric to epsilon, the number of L-BFGS-B steps, and the subspace dimension p? Can you report variance across these choices?
- Does the SB/LB sharpness gap persist under explicit layer rescalings that leave the function unchanged? If the metric changes, how do you reconcile this with the generalization claim?
- Under matched numbers of parameter updates, and separately under matched total examples processed, does the generalization gap remain?
- Can you provide estimates of the top Hessian eigenvalue at SB and LB solutions (e.g., via power iteration) to validate the proxy?
- What happens when BatchNorm statistics are fixed during evaluation/training for LB? Does the gap reduce?
- In the warm-start experiments, how many epochs are required before LB consistently converges to flat minima? Is this threshold correlated with any measurable property (e.g., gradient noise scale)?

Overall, the paper presents a timely and influential empirical study. Addressing the invariance and protocol confounds would significantly strengthen the case and the long-term utility of the conclusions.
