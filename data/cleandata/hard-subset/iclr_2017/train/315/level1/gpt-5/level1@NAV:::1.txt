Summary and contributions
The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training in deep learning often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training. It hypothesizes that LB methods preferentially converge to sharp minima, while SB methods—benefiting from gradient noise—find flatter minima that generalize better. The authors provide a heuristic but practical sharpness metric, 1D and curvilinear parametric loss visualizations between SB/LB solutions, experiments across multiple architectures and datasets, and warm-start studies showing that initializing LB from SB iterates can recover flatter minima. They also evaluate several remedies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal training, adversarial training) and discuss why they only partially mitigate the gap. The work is well framed in the literature on loss landscapes, MDL/Bayesian arguments for flat minima, and recent theory on nonconvex optimization.

Decision: Accept
Reason: The paper addresses a practically important and widely observed phenomenon with a clear, testable hypothesis and supplies consistent, multi-faceted empirical evidence across models. The diagnostic tools (sharpness metric, parametric plots, warm-start experiments) are likely to influence future research and practice.

Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: The question—why LB harms generalization—is crisply posed and impactful for scalable training.
- Motivation and placement: The link between flatness and generalization is well motivated via MDL/Bayesian perspectives and connected to literature on loss surfaces and SGD noise.
- Evidence: Results are coherent across architectures (AlexNet/VGG variants, fully connected, speech/image tasks), show 1–2 orders-of-magnitude differences in the proposed sharpness metric, and reveal batch-size thresholds and distance-to-initialization effects. Warm-start experiments compellingly support the “noise enables escape to flat basins” mechanism. Multiple attempted fixes strengthen the claim by showing the gap is nontrivial to close.
- Rigor: While the sharpness metric is heuristic and the maximization is inexact, the authors use both full-space and random-subspace variants and consistent settings, which triangulate the effect.

Feedback to improve (helpful, not prerequisites for acceptance)
- Control confounders: Report learning rate schedules, momentum/weight decay, and ensure fair training budgets (same updates/epochs). Analyze sensitivity to these choices and to optimizer (SGD+momentum), as ADAM’s generalization may confound results.
- Metric robustness: Discuss scale/reparameterization invariance (especially with batch norm) and how epsilon and subspace dimension p affect conclusions; include ablations and, where feasible, Lanczos-based top-eigenvalue estimates.
- Quantify correlations: Provide correlations between sharpness and test accuracy across seeds, and distributions for “distance from initialization.”
- Noise characterization: Measure gradient noise scale versus batch size to directly connect noise magnitude to basin transitions.
- Remedies: Include dynamic batch-size schedules (motivated by your warm-start results) as a concrete experiment.

Questions for the authors
- How do results change under LR scaling/warmup tuned specifically for LB? Are conclusions robust under SGD+momentum?
- Is your sharpness measure invariant under weight rescaling/BN reparameterizations? Can you normalize to reduce such effects?
- Do you observe a strong monotonic correlation between your sharpness metric and test error across runs?
- Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues (approximate) to validate the metric?
- How were training budgets matched across SB/LB (updates, epochs, wall-clock), and does the gap persist under strict controls?
- What is the gradient noise scale across batch sizes, and where is the observed “threshold” relative to that scale?
