Summary and contributions
- The paper tackles an important and timely question: why do large-batch (LB) training methods in deep networks often yield worse generalization than small-batch (SB) methods? It posits that LB tends to converge to sharp minimizers (high curvature) while SB, due to gradient noise, finds flatter minima, and that this explains the generalization gap.
- The authors provide empirical evidence across six networks (fully-connected and CNNs) and multiple datasets. Evidence includes: (i) 1D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions that visually suggest sharper basins around LB solutions; (ii) a “sharpness” metric based on local worst-case increases in the loss over small neighborhoods (both full space and random subspaces) showing 1–2 orders-of-magnitude larger values for LB solutions; (iii) batch-size thresholds at which generalization degrades and sharpness increases; (iv) warm-start experiments where initial SB training helps LB converge to flatter minima and better generalization.
- The paper also explores mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial/stability training) and observes partial improvements in test accuracy but persistent sharpness.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The central claim relies on a sharpness proxy that is not theoretically grounded, not demonstrably invariant to common reparameterizations, and is computed inexactly; thus, the link between “sharpness” and generalization is insufficiently validated.
2) Experimental controls are incomplete: hyperparameters, learning-rate/batch-size scaling, training budgets (epochs vs updates), and optimizer choices are not carefully matched across batch regimes, leaving confounding explanations for the generalization gap.

Supporting arguments
- On the sharpness metric: The proxy (Eq. 4) depends on a box around x with a nonstandard normalization (|(A+x)i|+1), uses random subspaces and only 10 L-BFGS-B steps for the inner maximization. The relation to the Hessian’s top eigenvalues is asserted but not established; in nonconvex settings, local max search quality matters. Without assessing sensitivity to ε, A, optimization depth, and reparameterizations, the metric’s robustness is unclear. In ReLU networks, simple layer rescalings can arbitrarily change curvature without affecting function or generalization; the metric does not appear to address such invariances.
- Visualization is qualitative: 1D and curved interpolations are informative but do not constitute strong evidence of basin geometry in high dimensions; they can be misleading and depend on the interpolation path.
- Hyperparameter confounds: LB and SB use the same optimizer (ADAM) but it is unclear if learning-rate schedules, β parameters, weight decay, and batch-norm settings were tuned per batch size. Known practice (and later literature) suggests learning rate/scale adjustments, warmup, and momentum tuning are critical for LB. The paper trains LB with batch size ≈10% of training data (e.g., 5k on CIFAR-10), which is extreme, and may require substantially different hyperparameters. The stopping criterion (“until loss ceased to improve”) may produce different numbers of parameter updates between SB and LB, conflating exploration time with generalization.
- Limited ablations: Results are reported for ADAM but only briefly for other optimizers; there is no study of the effect of batch-norm, dropout, weight decay, label smoothing, or data augmentation strength on sharpness and generalization. The warm-start experiments are compelling, but do not exclude alternative explanations (e.g., optimization path dependence unrelated to curvature properties).
- Claims vs evidence: While correlations are shown between LB, higher sharpness, and worse test accuracy, causality remains speculative. The paper does not measure actual Hessian spectra (e.g., top eigenvalues via Lanczos) at SB/LB solutions nor provide PAC-Bayes/MDL-based flatness-complexity estimates. Without a parameterization-invariant measure, the conclusion that “sharpness causes poor generalization” is not sufficiently supported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all part of the decision)
- Provide stronger, parameterization-aware curvature measures: estimate top Hessian eigenvalues or spectral norm via Lanczos/power iteration at SB and LB solutions; report distribution of eigenvalues or trace approximations; study sensitivity to rescaling transformations that leave the function unchanged (e.g., layer-wise scaling in ReLU networks).
- Control training protocols: compare SB vs LB under matched numbers of parameter updates, matched wall-clock compute, and matched epochs; evaluate linear learning-rate scaling and warmup for LB; tune weight decay separately per batch size; report comprehensive hyperparameter sweeps.
- Robustness of the sharpness proxy: vary ε over orders of magnitude; vary the number of inner maximization steps; compare full-space vs subspace results; report variance across random A’s; ablate the normalization (|(A+x)i|+1).
- Beyond ADAM: include tuned SGD with momentum for both regimes; in some settings Adam’s adaptivity interacts with batch size and batch norm in nontrivial ways.
- Broader metrics: include margin-based measures, PAC-Bayes bounds, and width/flatness metrics that are invariant to simple reparameterizations; relate to generalization bounds where possible.
- Extend warm-start/dynamic-batch experiments: test curricula that gradually increase batch size; quantify when the switch preserves generalization and how sharpness evolves.
- Reproducibility: release full training scripts, hyperparameters, and seeds; report exact architectures, data preprocessing, and schedules.

Questions for the authors
1) How were learning rates, momentum/β parameters, weight decay, and batch-norm statistics tuned for LB versus SB? Did you explore linear scaling of learning rate with batch size and warmup? How sensitive are your conclusions to these choices?
2) Can you report the number of parameter updates taken in SB and LB runs “until loss ceased to improve”? If matched on updates rather than epochs, do the results persist?
3) How sensitive is the sharpness metric to ε, to the choice of A and its dimension p, and to the number of inner L-BFGS-B iterations? Please provide variance across multiple A’s and ε values.
4) Have you examined the top Hessian eigenvalues at SB and LB solutions using power/Lanczos to corroborate the proxy? If not feasible on full models, can you do it on smaller networks?
5) How does your metric behave under simple layer rescalings that preserve function (e.g., scaling a layer’s weights and inversely scaling the subsequent layer)? Does the SB/LB sharpness gap remain?
6) In warm-start experiments, what is the minimal SB pretraining (in updates/epochs) needed for LB to recover SB-like generalization? Does gradual batch-size increase achieve similar outcomes?
7) Do the results hold for tuned SGD with momentum and weight decay (without Adam), and for stronger regularization (label smoothing, stronger augmentation, mixup/cutout-like methods)?
