- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), hypothesizing LB converges to sharper minima while SB finds flatter minima aided by gradient noise.  
- Empirical evidence across six architectures and multiple datasets supports the sharp-vs-flat hypothesis.  
- Parametric one-dimensional and curvilinear plots show sharper loss landscapes around LB solutions.  
- Introduces a practical “sharpness” metric via local maximization in small boxes and random low-dimensional subspaces; LB shows 1–2 orders of magnitude higher sharpness.  
- Warm-start experiments show LB can generalize well if initialized from an SB trajectory near a flat region, implying SB noise aids exploration.  
- Batch-size threshold experiments reveal marked drops in test accuracy and increases in sharpness beyond certain batch sizes.  
- Evaluates remedies (data augmentation, conservative/adversarial/stability training) finding partial gains but persistent sharpness; proposes dynamic sampling as promising.  
- Decision: Accept — problem is clear, practically relevant, and well-motivated.  
- Main strengths: convincing, consistent empirical support across architectures and a feasible diagnostic sharpness measure with informative visualizations.  
- Experiments are systematic with multiple restarts, optimizers, and coherent alignment of results to the central hypothesis.  
- Warm-start results provide a mechanistic explanation that stochasticity helps escape sharp basins.  
- Limitation: The sharpness metric is heuristic and dependent on parameterization, not invariant to reparameterizations (e.g., ReLU/BatchNorm scale).  
- Limitation: Hyperparameter tuning parity across batch sizes is not fully addressed; LR schedules, warmup, and momentum scaling could confound results.  
- Limitation: Hessian spectra are argued but not directly measured (e.g., via Lanczos/Krylov methods).  
- Limitation: Some LB regimes use very large batches (≈10% of data), which may not reflect common practice; wider ablations and tuned regimes would help.  
- Suggested: Discuss reparameterization invariance and consider alternative flatness measures (PAC-Bayes, normalized Hessian traces, path-normalized).  
- Suggested: Provide sensitivity analysis of sharpness metric to ε, subspace dimension p, and L-BFGS-B budget; report gradient norms and local quadratic fits.  
- Suggested: Fully detail and tune hyperparameters per batch size (LR scaling, warmup, momentum, weight decay, BatchNorm) and control for update count vs. epoch count.  
- Suggested diagnostics: measure gradient noise covariance vs. batch size, report Hessian top eigenvalues or spectra, and examine regularization/BatchNorm effects and accumulated BN stats.  
- Suggested: Show correlations between sharpness and test accuracy within batch sizes across runs to strengthen causal claims.  
- Remedies suggestions: expand dynamic sampling experiments, provide schedules, and consider explicit noise injection or label smoothing to mimic SB noise in LB training.  
- Questions for authors: How were learning rates/schedules selected per batch size and was linear LR scaling with warmup explored?  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive are sharpness measurements to ε, p, and L-BFGS-B iterations; can robustness plots be provided?  
- Questions for authors: Were Hessian spectral properties (top eigenvalues or trace) measured at converged SB/LB solutions?  
- Questions for authors: How do BatchNorm statistics differ between SB and LB and could BN mismatches explain some of the gap?  
- Questions for authors: Are results controlled for number of parameter updates (not just epochs), and is the batch-size threshold predictable from measured gradient noise variance?  
- Questions for authors: Within a fixed batch size, do lower-sharpness solutions consistently generalize better, strengthening causality?
