- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), hypothesizing LB converges to sharper minima while SB’s gradient noise finds flatter ones.
- Empirical evidence across six networks/datasets: 1D/curvilinear interpolations, a “sharpness” metric (full-space and random subspaces), batch-size thresholds, and warm-start experiments.
- Explored mitigation strategies (augmentation, conservative/proximal updates, adversarial/stability training) which partially improve accuracy but sharpness remains.
- Decision: Reject.
- Key reason 1: The sharpness proxy is not theoretically grounded, may not be invariant to reparameterizations, and is computed inexactly.
- Key reason 2: Experimental controls are incomplete—hyperparameters, LR/batch-size scaling, training budgets, and optimizer choices are not properly matched across regimes.
- Sharpness metric concerns: uses a nonstandard normalization, random subspaces, only 10 L‑BFGS‑B inner steps, and its relation to Hessian eigenvalues is unestablished.
- Reparameterization issue: in ReLU networks simple layer rescalings can change curvature without changing function or generalization—metric doesn’t account for this.
- Visualizations are qualitative: 1D and curvilinear interpolations are informative but insufficient to characterize high-dimensional basin geometry.
- Hyperparameter confounds: unclear per-batch tuning (LR schedules, β, weight decay, batch‑norm), extreme LB sizes used, and stopping criteria may cause unequal numbers of updates.
- Limited ablations: mostly ADAM results, little study of optimizer alternatives, batch‑norm, dropout, weight decay, or augmentation strength; warm-starts don’t rule out alternative optimization-path explanations.
- Causality vs correlation: paper shows correlation between LB, higher sharpness, and worse test accuracy but does not provide causal evidence (no Hessian spectra or invariant flatness measures).
- Suggestion: report top Hessian eigenvalues/spectra via Lanczos/power iteration and test sensitivity to layer-wise rescaling.
- Suggestion: rigorously match training protocols (updates, wall-clock, epochs), apply LR scaling and warmup, and tune weight decay per batch size.
- Suggestion: test sharpness-proxy robustness across ε, inner-optimization depth, subspace choices, normalization, and multiple random A’s.
- Suggestion: include well-tuned SGD with momentum and stronger regularizers/augmentations (label smoothing, mixup, cutout).
- Suggestion: use parameterization-invariant generalization metrics (margin-based, PAC-Bayes bounds) and relate to theoretical bounds where possible.
- Suggestion: extend warm-start/dynamic-batch curricula experiments and quantify when/why batch-size switches preserve generalization.
- Suggestion: improve reproducibility by releasing training scripts, hyperparameters, seeds, architectures, and preprocessing details.
- Question: How were LR, momentum/β, weight decay, and batch‑norm stats tuned for LB vs SB? Was linear LR scaling and warmup explored?
- Question: How many parameter updates did SB and LB runs take “until loss ceased to improve”? Do results hold when matched on updates?
- Question: How sensitive is the sharpness metric to ε, choice/dimension of A, and number of inner L‑BFGS‑B iterations; what is the variance across A/ε?
- Question: Have you measured top Hessian eigenvalues at SB/LB solutions (or on smaller models) to corroborate the proxy?
- Question: How does the metric behave under layer rescalings that preserve function; does the SB/LB sharpness gap persist?
- Question: In warm-starts, what is the minimal SB pretraining (updates/epochs) needed for LB to recover SB-like generalization; does gradual batch-size increase work similarly?
- Question: Do the results hold for tuned SGD with momentum and weight decay, and under stronger regularization/augmentation techniques?
