This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when increasing batch size in deep learning, attributing it to the convergence to sharper minima with larger batches compared to the flatter minima found with smaller batches. The authors support this claim with numerical experiments across several network configurations and datasets, using parametric plots and a sharpness metric to characterize the minima. They also briefly discuss potential remedies, finding limited success.

**Reject**

The paper presents an interesting observation and initial exploration, but lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance at ICLR. While the empirical evidence *suggests* a correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization, the paper falls short of convincingly establishing a causal relationship or providing a particularly insightful explanation beyond commonly held beliefs. The proposed “sharpness” metric, while computationally feasible, feels somewhat ad-hoc and its connection to generalization is not thoroughly explored. Furthermore, the attempted remedies are briefly discussed with minimal detail and do not offer a significant advancement.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Rigor in Establishing Causality:** The paper demonstrates a *correlation* between large batch size and sharp minima, but doesn’t definitively prove that sharpness *causes* the generalization gap. It’s possible that other factors correlated with batch size contribute to the observed effect. A more controlled experimental setup, such as explicitly projecting gradients onto flat or sharp minima and observing generalization performance, would be necessary to strengthen this claim. The authors acknowledge this in the discussion section, stating they only provide numerical evidence.

2. **Limited Novelty:** The observation that large batch sizes lead to different minima than small batch sizes and that sharp minima generalize poorly is not entirely new. Several prior works (referenced in the paper) have explored similar ideas. The contribution of this paper largely lies in consolidating and empirically demonstrating this phenomenon across multiple networks, but the insights gained are incremental. The “sharpness” metric, while a practical approach, isn't a significantly novel contribution to the field.

3. **Weakness of Proposed Remedies:** The section on attempts to improve LB methods (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training) feels underdeveloped. The authors mention limited success, but provide minimal details on the experimental setup or specific results. The failure of these methods isn't particularly surprising, and doesn’t detract from the main observation, but it weakens the overall impact of the paper as a potential solution-oriented work.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Justification of Network Architectures:** The choice of network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet, etc.) is justified as "popular configurations," but a more explicit rationale for selecting these specific architectures for this investigation would be helpful.
* **Parameter Sensitivity of Sharpness Metric:** The paper mentions the use of two values for the parameter in the sharpness metric. A sensitivity analysis examining how the results change with different parameter values would strengthen the reliability of the findings.
* **Theoretical Connection:** While the authors acknowledge relevant theoretical work, a deeper discussion of how these theoretical results relate to their empirical findings would be beneficial. Could existing theoretical frameworks predict or explain the observed relationship between batch size and minimizer sharpness?
* **More Detailed Results for Remedies:** Provide more details (tables, figures) for the experiments with data augmentation, conservative training, and robust training. Quantify the improvement (or lack thereof) in generalization performance for each method.
* **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost associated with calculating the sharpness metric, especially for larger networks, would be helpful for assessing the practicality of this approach.

**Questions for Authors:**

* Can you provide a more rigorous argument, beyond correlation, to support the claim that sharp minima *cause* the generalization gap observed with large batch sizes?
* What is the theoretical justification for the chosen sharpness metric? How does it relate to the Hessian spectrum or other measures of curvature?
* The paper mentions warm-starting LB methods from SB solutions. Could you explore this strategy more thoroughly and discuss its limitations?
* What specific hyperparameter tuning was performed for the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust training), and how sensitive were the results to these hyperparameters?
* What is the computational cost of calculating the sharpness metric for a network with millions of parameters?
