## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper investigates the degradation in generalization performance observed when using large batch sizes in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for training deep learning models. The authors convincingly argue, through numerical experiments, that large-batch methods tend to converge to *sharp* minima of the loss function, which are known to generalize poorly. Conversely, small-batch methods converge to *flat* minima due to the inherent noise in the gradient estimation. The paper provides a clear and well-motivated exploration of this phenomenon, linking it to the broader discussion of loss landscape characteristics and generalization in deep learning.

**Reject** - While the paper presents valuable insights and a clear analysis, the conclusions are largely observational. The core claim – that large batch sizes *cause* convergence to sharp minima – lacks a rigorous theoretical underpinning.  The paper does a good job *showing* the correlation, but doesn't sufficiently *prove* the causality, which limits its contribution to the field.

The supporting arguments are strong in that the experimental results consistently demonstrate a correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization gap. The authors consider multiple network architectures and present compelling visualizations (parametric plots) and quantitative metrics (sharpness measure) to support their claims. The exploration of potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) provides context and highlights the difficulty in addressing this issue. However, these attempts are presented as preliminary and do not offer definitive solutions. The link to existing literature on loss landscape properties is also well-established.

Additional feedback:

*   **Clarify the sharpness metric:** While Equation (4) is provided, a more intuitive explanation of how this metric relates to the eigenvalues of the Hessian would be helpful. Specifically, how does it approximate the Ritz value?
*   **Justification of ADAM:** Why was ADAM specifically chosen as the optimizer? Would the observations hold with other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum)?
*   **Robustness to initialization:** How sensitive are the results to the initialization scheme? Do different initializations lead to different types of minima?
*   **Scaling with network size:** The experiments focus on relatively small networks. Do the observations hold as network size increases significantly?
*   **Computational cost:**  The paper mentions the computational cost of calculating the Hessian. Can this cost be reduced through approximations or sampling techniques to enable more detailed analysis?
