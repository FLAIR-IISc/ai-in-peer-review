This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large batch sizes in deep learning, attributing it to the convergence to sharper minimizers. The authors provide numerical evidence supporting this claim, showing that large-batch methods tend to find sharp minima while small-batch methods converge to flatter ones, correlating flatness with better generalization. They also explore preliminary strategies to mitigate the generalization gap, finding limited success.

**Reject**

The core argument—that large batch sizes lead to sharper minima and thus worse generalization—is plausible and aligns with existing literature. However, the paper lacks the depth of analysis and rigor needed for acceptance at ICLR. The empirical evaluation, while broad in terms of network architectures, feels superficial and doesn’t convincingly demonstrate causality. The observed correlation between batch size, sharpness, and generalization doesn't rule out other contributing factors. Furthermore, the attempted remedies are not thoroughly investigated, and the lack of conclusive results weakens the overall contribution.

The paper’s justification for its choices is sometimes weak. For example, choosing a fixed batch size ratio (10% for large batch, 256 for small batch) across different network architectures and datasets feels arbitrary. The justification for using ADAM as the sole optimizer, even when exploring large batch sizes where other optimizers might be more appropriate, is insufficient. While the authors acknowledge limitations and suggest avenues for future research, the current state of the work does not present a compelling or novel enough insight to warrant acceptance. The claims, while interesting, are not sufficiently *supported* by the presented evidence, falling short of the scientific rigor expected at ICLR. The presentation, while clear, relies heavily on referencing prior work without providing significant novel analytical contributions.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a heuristic and its limitations should be more clearly acknowledged. How sensitive are the conclusions to the parameters used in this metric (e.g., , p)? A more thorough analysis of its validity and comparison to other sharpness measures (e.g., eigenvalue analysis, even if approximate) would strengthen the argument.
*   **Causality:** The paper establishes a correlation but doesn't strongly demonstrate a causal link between sharpness and generalization. Experiments that explicitly manipulate the flatness of minima (e.g., through regularization techniques) and observe the effect on generalization would be more convincing.
*   **Optimizer Choice:** Justify the use of ADAM as the sole optimizer. Explore the impact of different optimizers, particularly for large batch sizes.
*   **Batch Size Ratio:** The fixed batch size ratio feels arbitrary. Investigate the impact of varying this ratio and potentially relating it to the network size or dataset characteristics.
*   **Remedy Evaluation:** The attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are presented briefly. A more detailed analysis, including hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies, would be valuable. What specific aspects of these remedies failed to fully address the issue?
*   **Theoretical Connection:** The paper briefly mentions related theoretical work. A deeper engagement with these theories and an attempt to connect the empirical findings to existing theoretical frameworks would be beneficial.
*   **Visualization:** While the parametric plots are informative, explore other visualization techniques to better understand the loss landscape and the differences between small and large batch training.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  How sensitive are the conclusions regarding sharpness and generalization to the choice of the parameters in your sharpness metric (Equation 4)? Have you explored alternative sharpness metrics?
2.  You mention that the batch size ratio was chosen somewhat arbitrarily. Have you experimented with different ratios and observed any changes in the results?
3.  Why did you choose to only use ADAM as the optimizer? Did you consider other optimizers, and if so, what were the results?
4.  The paper establishes a correlation between sharpness and generalization. What experiments did you consider, or would you consider, to establish a stronger causal link between these two factors?
5.  Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) and why they did not fully address the generalization gap?
6.  How do your empirical observations relate to existing theoretical work on the loss landscape of deep neural networks (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016)?
