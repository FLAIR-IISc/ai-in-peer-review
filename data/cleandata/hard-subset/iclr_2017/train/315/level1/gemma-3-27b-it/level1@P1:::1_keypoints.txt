- Paper studies the large-batch generalization gap, attributing it to convergence to sharper minima and provides numerical evidence linking sharpness to worse generalization.  
- Authors show large-batch methods tend to find sharp minima while small-batch methods find flatter minima; remedies explored have limited success.  
- Recommendation: Reject — the work lacks sufficient depth, rigor, and novel contribution for ICLR.  
- Empirical evaluation is broad in architectures but superficial and does not convincingly establish causality.  
- Correlation between batch size, sharpness, and generalization is shown but other contributing factors are not ruled out.  
- Attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are under-investigated and inconclusive.  
- Justification for experimental choices is weak; fixed batch size ratio (10%/256) appears arbitrary across architectures/datasets.  
- Use of ADAM as the sole optimizer is insufficiently justified, especially when different optimizers may behave differently for large batches.  
- Claims are interesting but not sufficiently supported by evidence or novel analytical contributions.  
- Sharpness metric (Eq. 4) is heuristic; its parameter sensitivity (e.g., epsilon, p) and limitations must be acknowledged and compared to other measures (e.g., eigenvalue analysis).  
- Causality is not established; experiments that explicitly manipulate flatness (e.g., via regularization) are needed to show effect on generalization.  
- Explore optimizer diversity and the impact of different optimizers on large-batch training outcomes.  
- Investigate the effect of varying batch size ratios and relate choices to network size or dataset characteristics.  
- Remedy evaluation needs deeper analysis: hyperparameter tuning, ablation studies, and clear identification of which aspects failed.  
- Strengthen connection to existing theoretical work on loss landscapes and attempt to relate empirical findings to theory.  
- Improve visualization techniques beyond parametric plots to better characterize loss landscapes and differences between small and large-batch training.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of sharpness metric and exploration of alternatives; experiments with different batch ratios; rationale for using ADAM only; plans to establish causality; detailed limitations of the remedies; relation of observations to existing theoretical works.
