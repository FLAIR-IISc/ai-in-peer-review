- Main finding: Large-batch SGD tends to converge to sharp minima that generalize poorly, while small-batch SGD converges to flat minima due to gradient noise.  
- Empirical support: Numerical experiments across multiple architectures show consistent correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization gap.  
- Presentation: Clear, well-motivated exploration with compelling visualizations (parametric plots) and a quantitative sharpness measure.  
- Contribution limitation: Conclusions are largely observational; paper shows correlation but lacks rigorous theoretical proof of causality.  
- Remedies explored: Data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization are considered but only as preliminary, non-definitive solutions.  
- Literature context: Good linkage to existing work on loss landscape properties and generalization.  
- Clarify metric: Need a more intuitive explanation of the sharpness metric and how it relates to Hessian eigenvalues / approximates the Ritz value.  
- Optimizer choice: Justification required for using ADAM and whether results hold for other optimizers (e.g., SGD with momentum).  
- Initialization sensitivity: Request analysis of robustness to different initialization schemes and their effect on minima types.  
- Scaling concern: Experiments focus on relatively small networks; need to test whether observations scale to much larger models.  
- Computational cost: Question whether Hessian computation cost can be reduced via approximations or sampling to enable more extensive analysis.  
- Recommendation: Reject (insightful but limited by lack of theoretical causality and incomplete remedies).
