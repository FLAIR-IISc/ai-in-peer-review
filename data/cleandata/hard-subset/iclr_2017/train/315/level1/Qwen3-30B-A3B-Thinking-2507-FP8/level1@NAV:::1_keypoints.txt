- Paper attributes the small-batch vs large-batch generalization gap to large-batch methods converging to sharp minima while small-batch methods find flatter minima.  
- Introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Equation 4) to quantify solution sensitivity.  
- Empirical results show 1–2 orders-of-magnitude difference in sharpness across multiple network architectures (F1–F4, C1–C4) and datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants).  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1: Addresses an important practical problem with hypotheses grounded in prior sharp vs. flat minima literature.  
- Reason 2: Robust, consistent empirical evidence supported by parametric plots and the proposed sharpness metric.  
- Provides clear characterization of the phenomenon and explores mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training).  
- Warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) indicate small-batch exploration can help large-batch methods reach flatter minima, strengthening the argument.  
- Suggestion: Add detail on why the sharpness metric correlates with Hessian eigenvalues and relate to theoretical work (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015).  
- Suggestion: Emphasize the warm-starting approach as a promising practical solution.  
- Question: What explains the relationship between batch size and the threshold where generalization begins to degrade (Figure 4)?  
- Question: Would the sharpness metric correlate with generalization across different optimizers (e.g., SGD vs. ADAM), or is the phenomenon optimizer-specific?
