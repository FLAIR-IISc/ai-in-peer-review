

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper investigates the cause of the generalization gap observed when using large-batch methods for training deep learning models, as opposed to small-batch methods. The authors claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by large eigenvalues in the Hessian) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers, and that this difference in minimizer sharpness explains the observed generalization drop. They support this claim with extensive empirical evidence across multiple network architectures (including AlexNet and VGGNet variants), introducing a practical sharpness metric that measures the sensitivity of the loss function in a neighborhood around a minimizer. The authors also explore several strategies to mitigate the generalization gap, including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training.

## 2. Decision
Reject.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes an important observation about the relationship between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization, but fails to provide sufficient novel evidence to justify a new contribution. The main claim that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers is not new, as the authors themselves acknowledge (citing Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997 and Chaudhari et al., 2016). The paper's contribution is primarily empirical, but the evidence provided does not convincingly establish causation or rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap.

The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is an approximation of Hessian-based sharpness, but the paper fails to rigorously establish how well this metric correlates with the true Hessian spectrum. The authors note that "when A = In, the value (4) relates to the largest eigenvalue of ∇²f(x)" but provide no quantitative evidence of this relationship. Without this connection, the metric's validity as a measure of minimizer sharpness is questionable.

The paper also fails to adequately rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap. For example, they dismiss overfitting as a cause but provide only weak evidence (observing that testing accuracy doesn't peak and decay) that doesn't conclusively rule out overfitting. The generalization gap could still be due to the large-batch method not learning the training data sufficiently well, which would be a form of underfitting rather than overfitting.

The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) suggests that the problem may be with the optimization path rather than the batch size itself, which contradicts the paper's main claim that large-batch methods inherently converge to sharp minimizers. This nuance is not adequately addressed in the paper.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical connection between the proposed sharpness metric and the Hessian-based sharpness definition. A comparison with direct Hessian eigenvalue calculations on smaller networks would strengthen the metric's validity.
- The authors should more thoroughly rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap, particularly overfitting, by providing evidence that the large-batch method achieves similar or better training accuracy than the small-batch method (which would rule out underfitting as the cause).
- The nuance about sharp minima being directional (not cone-shaped) is important but currently buried in the text. This should be emphasized as it significantly impacts the interpretation of the results.
- The paper should clarify whether the generalization gap persists with different optimizers beyond ADAM, as the current experiments only use ADAM for both small and large batch sizes.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How do you reconcile your findings with recent theoretical work (Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016) that shows deep learning loss functions have many local minima with similar loss values? If sharp minima exist, why don't they have significantly higher loss values?

2. Can you provide quantitative evidence that your sharpness metric (Equation 4) correlates with the Hessian's largest eigenvalue? A comparison with direct Hessian calculations (on smaller networks) would strengthen your claims.

3. The paper claims "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training" based on the absence of a testing accuracy peak followed by decay. However, this observation doesn't rule out overfitting in other forms. How would you distinguish between overfitting and the sharpness hypothesis in your experiments?

4. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) suggests that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when started from small-batch solutions. Does this imply that the problem is with the optimization path rather than the batch size itself, and how does this align with your main claim about large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers?
