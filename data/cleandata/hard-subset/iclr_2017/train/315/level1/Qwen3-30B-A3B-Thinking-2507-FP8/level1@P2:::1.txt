

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions
This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding the generalization gap observed in large-batch deep learning training. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity to small perturbations in the weight space) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers, with the latter exhibiting superior generalization performance. The paper effectively connects this observation to existing literature on flat vs. sharp minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) and provides a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify this phenomenon. The authors also explore several promising strategies to mitigate the generalization gap in large-batch training, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization techniques. The work is well-motivated by practical concerns in deep learning scalability, with clear implications for future research on distributed training.

## 2. Decision
**Reject**

The key reasons for this decision are:
1. The paper lacks theoretical justification for the observed correlation between batch size, sharpness, and generalization performance. The authors present strong empirical evidence but do not establish causality or provide a theoretical framework explaining why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima.
2. The paper's sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is heuristic and not rigorously connected to the Hessian eigenvalues, which are the standard measure of sharpness in optimization. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not provide sufficient justification for why their metric reliably captures the phenomenon they're investigating.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is the empirical demonstration of the correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization performance. The parametric plots (Figure 3) and sharpness metric results (Tables 3-4) are well-executed and convincing, showing a clear pattern across multiple network architectures and datasets. The authors effectively rule out several alternative explanations for the generalization gap (overfitting, saddle points, etc.), strengthening their core hypothesis.

However, the lack of theoretical grounding is a significant limitation. The paper repeatedly states that "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" but provides no explanation for why this occurs. Without a theoretical basis, the observed correlation could be coincidental or specific to the experimental setup. The authors note that "we only provided some numerical evidence" (Section 4), which is an understatement of the paper's main limitation.

The sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) is another critical issue. While the authors acknowledge it's an approximation, they don't rigorously establish its validity. The connection to the Hessian eigenvalues is tenuous ("Note that Metric 2.1 is closely related to the spectrum of ∇²f(x)"), but the paper doesn't demonstrate that their metric reliably correlates with actual Hessian-based sharpness measurements. Without this validation, the main empirical finding rests on a shaky foundation.

The paper also lacks a clear explanation for why the noise in small-batch methods leads to flatter minima. The authors state "noise in the gradient pushes the iterates out of the basin of attraction of sharp minimizers" (Section 3) but don't provide any theoretical or empirical evidence for this mechanism. This is a critical gap in the reasoning.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a theoretical analysis explaining why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima. Even a simple heuristic argument would be valuable.
2. Rigorously validate their sharpness metric against Hessian-based measures on a subset of their experiments.
3. Conduct ablation studies to determine whether the sharpness phenomenon is specific to certain network architectures, optimizers, or data types.
4. Clarify the relationship between their sharpness metric and the theoretical concept of "sharp minima" in optimization literature (noting that the term has a different meaning in convex optimization per Ferris, 1988).
5. Discuss the computational cost of their sharpness metric in comparison to alternative measures of sharpness.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. Can you provide evidence that the sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) reliably correlates with the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian in the regions where your experiments show sharp minima? Without this validation, it's difficult to accept that your metric measures the phenomenon you're claiming.

2. The paper states that "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" but doesn't explain why. What theoretical or heuristic arguments support this claim? How does the noise in small-batch methods specifically cause the iterates to escape sharp basins?

3. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) shows that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when warm-started with small-batch iterations. How does this relate to the sharpness of the minimizers? Is the improvement in generalization due to the sharpness reduction, or is there another mechanism at play?

4. The paper notes that "the sharp minimizers identified in our experiments do not resemble a cone, i.e., the function does not increase rapidly along all (or even most) directions." How does this observation align with the standard understanding of sharp minima in the literature, which typically refers to high curvature in all directions?
