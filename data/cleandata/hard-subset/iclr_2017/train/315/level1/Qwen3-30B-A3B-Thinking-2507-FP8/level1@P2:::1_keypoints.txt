- Empirical claim: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers with worse generalization, while small-batch methods find flatter minimizers with better generalization.
- Contribution: proposes a computationally feasible sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) to quantify minimizer sharpness.
- Contribution: presents parametric plots and tables showing the sharpness/generalization pattern across multiple architectures and datasets.
- Contribution: investigates mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) to reduce the large-batch generalization gap.
- Practical motivation: addresses scalability and distributed training implications.

- Decision: Reject.

- Key reason 1: No theoretical justification or causality shown between batch size, sharpness, and generalization.
- Key reason 2: The proposed sharpness metric is heuristic and not validated against Hessian eigenvalues, so its reliability is questionable.

- Supporting: Empirical results (figures/tables) are convincing and several alternative explanations are reasonably ruled out.
- Supporting: The claim that noise in small-batch methods pushes iterates out of sharp basins is asserted but not theoretically or empirically substantiated.
- Supporting: The connection between Metric 2.1 and Hessian-based sharpness is asserted but not demonstrated, leaving the main finding on shaky ground.
- Supporting: Observed sharp minimizers do not increase loss rapidly in most directions, which complicates alignment with standard definitions of sharpness.

- Improvement 1: Provide theoretical analysis or heuristic explanation for why large-batch methods converge to sharp minima.
- Improvement 2: Rigorously validate Metric 2.1 against Hessian-based measures on some experiments.
- Improvement 3: Add ablation studies to test dependency on architectures, optimizers, or data types.
- Improvement 4: Clarify relationship between their metric and the optimization literature’s notion of sharp minima (including convex differences).
- Improvement 5: Discuss the computational cost of their sharpness metric versus alternatives.

- Question 1: Can you show Metric 2.1 reliably correlates with the largest Hessian eigenvalue where sharp minima are claimed?
- Question 2: What theoretical or heuristic arguments explain why large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers and how small-batch noise enables escape?
- Question 3: In warm-starting experiments, is improved generalization due to reduced sharpness or another mechanism?
- Question 4: How does the observation that sharp minimizers are not cone-like align with standard definitions of sharpness in the literature?
