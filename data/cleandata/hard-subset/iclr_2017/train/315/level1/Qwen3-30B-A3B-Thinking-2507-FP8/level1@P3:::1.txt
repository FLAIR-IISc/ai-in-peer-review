

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

This paper addresses a critical practical issue in deep learning: the observed generalization gap between large-batch and small-batch training methods. The authors present compelling empirical evidence that large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers (with high curvature in the loss landscape), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers. They propose a novel sharpness metric to quantify this phenomenon and demonstrate that this difference in minimizer quality explains the generalization gap. The paper makes a valuable contribution by connecting a common practical observation with a theoretical concept (sharp vs. flat minima), and it provides several promising directions for improving large-batch training, including data augmentation, conservative training, and adversarial training.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**. While the paper presents interesting observations, the methodology for measuring sharpness lacks theoretical grounding, and the paper fails to adequately address alternative explanations for the generalization gap. The core claim that sharp minima cause the generalization gap is not sufficiently supported by the evidence presented.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's primary weakness lies in its sharpness metric (Equation 4). The authors acknowledge this metric is "imperfect" and computationally feasible, but they fail to establish its theoretical connection to the true curvature of the loss function. The metric measures "sensitivity" rather than true sharpness, which is defined in terms of the Hessian eigenvalues. The authors note that their metric relates to the largest eigenvalue when A = I_n, but they don't provide evidence that this relationship holds in practice for deep neural networks. Without this theoretical link, the sharpness metric cannot be used to make definitive claims about the curvature of minima.

Furthermore, the paper fails to rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap. The authors consider four possible explanations but dismiss the first two (overfitting and saddle points) without sufficient evidence. However, the paper does not adequately address the possibility that the generalization gap might be due to differences in the optimization dynamics themselves (e.g., different effective learning rates, or different convergence rates to the same minima), rather than differences in the quality of the minima.

The experimental design also has significant limitations. The paper uses a fixed batch size for large-batch training (10% of training data), but doesn't explore how the generalization gap changes as batch size increases from small to large. The threshold batch size where the generalization gap appears (Figure 4) suggests that the problem is not simply about "large" vs. "small" batches but about a specific transition point. The paper doesn't investigate why this threshold exists or how it varies across architectures.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a theoretical analysis connecting their sharpness metric to the true Hessian curvature. They could compare their metric to the true largest eigenvalue of the Hessian for small networks where this computation is feasible.

2. Conduct experiments that directly compare the generalization performance of models trained to the same loss value but with different batch sizes. The paper's current experiments don't control for the final loss value, making it impossible to determine whether the generalization gap is due to different minima or simply different convergence points.

3. Explore alternative explanations for the generalization gap more thoroughly. For instance, they could investigate whether the generalization gap persists when using the same number of effective gradient evaluations (i.e., same total data processed) rather than the same number of iterations.

4. Include more thorough analysis of the relationship between batch size, learning rate, and the observed generalization gap. The paper mentions that "the noise in the stochastic gradient" causes the difference, but doesn't quantify how the noise level changes with batch size.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The authors claim that "the generalization gap is not due to over-fitting or over-training," but they don't provide evidence that the training and testing curves don't follow the typical overfitting pattern. How did they verify that the observed difference in performance isn't simply a case of overfitting to the training data?

2. The paper states that "the solutions obtained by a large-batch method defines points of larger sensitivity of the training function." However, the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is normalized by f(x). Given that f(x) differs between SB and LB methods (even if slightly), how can the authors be sure the metric isn't being influenced by differences in the loss value rather than differences in curvature?

3. In Table 3 and 4, the authors report a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness between SB and LB methods. However, the sharpness values for LB methods are still relatively small (e.g., 0.02-0.1% in Table 3). How do these values compare to the sharpness of "sharp" minima in other contexts, and why would such small differences in sharpness lead to a 5% drop in generalization performance?

4. The paper claims that "the loss function landscape of deep neural networks is such that large-batch methods are attracted to regions with sharp minimizers." Can the authors provide evidence that this is a property of the loss landscape itself, rather than an artifact of their specific optimization procedure (ADAM with fixed hyperparameters)?
