- Paper explains the large-batch vs. small-batch generalization gap, showing large-batch converges to sharp minima and small-batch to flat minima.  
- Presents parametric loss plots and a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify sharp vs. flat minima.  
- Empirical results are shown across multiple architectures (AlexNet, VGG variants, fully-connected nets) and datasets, demonstrating consistent sharpness differences.  
- Explores practical strategies to improve large-batch training, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization.  
- Decision: Accept — the paper offers a valuable, empirically strong contribution to understanding a key deep-learning issue.  
- Well-motivated by prior literature on sharp/flat minima and loss surfaces, and appropriately framed as numerical evidence rather than theory.  
- Experimental design rules out alternative explanations (overfitting, saddle attraction) and shows 1–2 orders of magnitude difference in sharpness metrics.  
- Warm-starting experiments compellingly show small-batch steps can steer optimization toward flatter minima that large-batch alone cannot reach.  
- Improvement suggestion: provide theoretical justification for the ad-hoc sharpness metric and relate it to the Hessian spectrum and top eigenvalues.  
- Improvement suggestion: discuss the computational cost and scalability of the proposed sharpness metric for large networks.  
- Improvement suggestion: expand discussion of warm-starting implications for practical distributed training strategies.  
- Improvement suggestion: acknowledge and address counter-evidence (e.g., Soudry & Carmon) that challenges the prevalence of sharp minima in deep nets.  
- Question: How does the sharpness metric behave as problem dimensionality increases; does it reliably indicate Hessian properties?  
- Question: Have you validated the metric against principled measures (top Hessian eigenvalues) on smaller networks?  
- Question: Why does conservative training improve generalization without eliminating sharpness, and is remaining sharpness sufficient to explain residual gap?  
- Question: What is the minimal number of small-batch steps needed in warm-starting to enable good large-batch generalization, and how does this scale with network size?
