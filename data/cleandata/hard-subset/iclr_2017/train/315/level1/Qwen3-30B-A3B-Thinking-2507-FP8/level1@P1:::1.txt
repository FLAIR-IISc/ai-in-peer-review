

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper makes a significant contribution to understanding a well-known practical issue in deep learning: the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods compared to small-batch methods. The authors present compelling numerical evidence supporting the claim that large-batch methods converge to "sharp minimizers" (characterized by many large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to "flat minimizers" (with many small eigenvalues), and that sharp minima lead to poorer generalization. The paper effectively uses parametric plots of the loss function along paths between minimizers and introduces a computationally feasible sharpness metric to quantify this difference across multiple network architectures (AlexNet, VGGNet variants, and fully-connected networks). The authors also explore several strategies to improve large-batch training, including data augmentation, conservative training, and robust optimization, providing valuable insights for practitioners seeking to scale deep learning training.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding a critical practical issue in deep learning, and the empirical evidence supporting the main claim is strong and comprehensive.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a well-documented phenomenon in deep learning practice with clear relevance to the community. The main claim—that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers leading to worse generalization—is well-motivated by the literature on sharp vs. flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016) and recent theoretical work on loss surfaces (Choromanska et al., 2015; Lee et al., 2016). The authors effectively rule out alternative explanations for the generalization gap, such as overfitting or saddle point attraction, through careful experimental design and analysis.

The empirical evidence is robust and comprehensive. The authors use multiple network architectures (F1, F2, C1, C2, C3, C4) across different data sets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants), and they provide both parametric plots and their proposed sharpness metric (equation 4) to demonstrate the difference between small-batch and large-batch minimizers. The results consistently show a 1-2 order of magnitude difference in sharpness metrics between the two regimes, which strongly supports their main claim. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) provide particularly compelling evidence that small-batch methods explore the loss landscape to find flatter minima that large-batch methods cannot reach on their own.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, citing relevant work on stochastic optimization, generalization, and loss surface properties. The authors correctly frame their contribution as providing numerical evidence rather than theoretical proof, which is appropriate for the scope of the paper.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The sharpness metric (equation 4) is ad-hoc and lacks a thorough theoretical justification. The authors should discuss how this metric relates to the actual Hessian spectrum, particularly the top eigenvalues that define sharpness, and address how it scales with problem dimensionality.

2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of their sharpness metric. For large networks, even their approximate metric might be expensive, which would limit its practical applicability.

3. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) suggest that large-batch methods can achieve good generalization when properly initialized by small-batch methods. The authors should expand on this insight, discussing its implications for practical training strategies in distributed systems.

4. The paper mentions that "large-batch methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers" but doesn't address the counter-evidence from recent work (e.g., Soudry & Carmon, 2016) suggesting that deep networks may not have many sharp minima. This tension should be acknowledged and addressed.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does your sharpness metric (equation 4) behave when the dimensionality of the problem increases? Does it remain a reliable indicator of Hessian spectrum properties?

2. Have you considered using a more principled measure of sharpness (e.g., top few eigenvalues of the Hessian) for smaller networks, to validate your metric against a theoretically grounded measure?

3. Your conservative training approach (section E.2) improves generalization but doesn't eliminate sharpness. Can you provide more insight into why this happens, and whether the remaining sharpness is sufficient to explain the remaining generalization gap?

4. The warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) suggest that a small number of small-batch steps are sufficient to enable good generalization with large-batch training. What is the minimal number of small-batch steps required for this effect, and how does this scale with network size?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to understanding a critical practical issue in deep learning. The empirical evidence is compelling, and the findings have important implications for practitioners seeking to scale deep learning training to larger distributed systems. With the suggested improvements, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR 2017.
