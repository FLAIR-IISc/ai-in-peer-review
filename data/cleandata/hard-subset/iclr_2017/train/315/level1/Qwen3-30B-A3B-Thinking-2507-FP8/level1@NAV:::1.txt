

This paper makes a valuable contribution to understanding the well-known generalization gap between small-batch and large-batch training in deep learning. The authors present compelling numerical evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (characterized by high sensitivity in the loss landscape), while small-batch methods converge to flatter minimizers, and this difference explains the observed generalization performance gap. Their sharpness metric (Equation 4) provides a computationally feasible way to quantify this difference, and the results consistently show a 1-2 order-of-magnitude difference in sharpness across multiple network architectures (F1-F4, C1-C4) and datasets (MNIST, TIMIT, ImageNet variants).

Decision: Accept

Reasons: 1) The paper addresses a significant practical problem in deep learning with well-motivated hypotheses grounded in existing literature on sharp vs. flat minima. 2) The empirical evidence is robust, consistent across multiple experiments, and supported by both parametric plots and the proposed sharpness metric.

The paper provides a clear and valuable characterization of the phenomenon, with additional attempts to mitigate the problem (data augmentation, conservative training) that help contextualize the issue. The authors' warm-starting experiments (Figure 5) particularly strengthen the argument by showing how small-batch exploration enables large-batch methods to reach flatter minima.

Additional feedback: Consider providing more detail on why the sharpness metric (Equation 4) correlates with the Hessian eigenvalues, and perhaps include a brief discussion of how this relates to the theoretical work by Choromanska et al. (2015) on loss surface properties. The warm-starting approach shows promise as a practical solution and deserves more emphasis.

Questions for authors: 1) How do the authors explain the observed relationship between batch size and the threshold at which generalization begins to degrade (Figure 4)? 2) Do the authors believe their sharpness metric would correlate with generalization performance across different optimization algorithms (e.g., SGD vs. ADAM) or is this phenomenon specific to the optimizer used in the experiments?
