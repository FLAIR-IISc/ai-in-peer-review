- Paper claim: large-batch training converges to sharp minima while small-batch training finds flat minima; authors propose a sharpness metric and suggest ways to improve large-batch training.

- Decision: Reject — metric lacks theoretical grounding and the evidence does not adequately support the core claim that sharp minima cause the generalization gap.

- Main methodological weakness: the sharpness metric (Eq. 4) measures "sensitivity" not true curvature and its connection to Hessian eigenvalues is not established for deep nets.

- Authors acknowledge metric is imperfect but do not provide empirical or theoretical validation that it reflects true curvature in practice.

- Paper fails to rule out alternative explanations (e.g., optimization dynamics, effective learning rates, convergence behavior) and dismisses some possibilities without sufficient evidence.

- Experimental design limits: only a fixed large-batch size (10% of data) is studied, no systematic sweep of batch sizes or analysis of the observed threshold where the gap appears.

- Lack of control for final loss: experiments do not compare models trained to the same training loss across batch sizes, so differences may reflect convergence points rather than minima quality.

- Missing analysis of how batch size, learning rate, and gradient noise quantitatively interact to produce the generalization gap.

- Suggested improvements: theoretically link the sharpness metric to Hessian curvature (e.g., compare eigenvalues on small networks).

- Suggested experiments: compare models with the same final loss but different batch sizes; test equal effective gradient evaluations; analyze batch-size vs learning-rate vs noise relationships.

- Key questions for authors: how was overfitting ruled out; could the normalized sharpness metric be biased by differing loss values; why do small measured sharpness differences produce large generalization gaps; is the effect due to the loss landscape or the specific optimizer/hyperparameters?
