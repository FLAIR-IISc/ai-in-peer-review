- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch, claiming large-batch finds sharp minimizers and proposing a practical sharpness metric; provides empirical results across architectures and mitigation strategies.

- Decision: Reject.

- Observation is important but not novel; similar claims exist in prior work.

- Empirical evidence is insufficient to establish causation or exclude alternative explanations for the generalization gap.

- The proposed sharpness metric is only an approximation of Hessian-based sharpness and lacks quantitative validation against true Hessian eigenvalues.

- The authors' dismissal of overfitting is weak; the generalization gap could stem from underfitting or other causes not ruled out.

- Warm-start experiments suggest the optimization path, not batch size per se, may explain results, which contradicts the paper's main claim.

- Recommend a rigorous theoretical link between the metric and Hessian and comparisons with direct Hessian calculations on small networks.

- Recommend providing training-accuracy evidence to rule out underfitting as the cause of the gap.

- Emphasize that sharp minima are directional (not cone-shaped); this nuance should be highlighted.

- Clarify whether the generalization gap persists with optimizers other than ADAM.

- Question: How do the findings reconcile with theory that many local minima have similar loss values—why would sharp minima not have similar losses?

- Question: Can you provide quantitative correlation between the sharpness metric and the Hessian's largest eigenvalue (e.g., via direct Hessian comparisons)?

- Question: How will you distinguish overfitting from the sharpness hypothesis beyond absence of test-accuracy peak-and-decay?

- Question: Does warm-starting imply the issue is the optimization path rather than batch size, and how does that align with your claim that large-batch methods inherently converge to sharp minimizers?
