- Paper studies the generalization gap: large-batch (LB) training yields poorer test performance because LB converges to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) noise finds flatter minima.  
- Authors provide compelling empirical evidence linking batch size to solution sharpness and generalization.  
- Introduces a quantitative sharpness metric and visualization techniques to measure and illustrate sharpness across architectures.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Strength: strong, convincing set of empirical results supporting the central hypothesis.  
- Strength: rigorous, well-executed experimental design directly comparing LB and SB on identical tasks.  
- Notable experiments: warm-starting LB from SB solutions and identification of a batch-size "threshold" for generalization collapse.  
- Paper explores potential remedies (data augmentation, robust training), though they were not fully successful.  
- Suggestion: analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to hyperparameters (ε, p, matrix A, number of L-BFGS-B steps).  
- Suggestion: further quantify the low-dimensional subspace phenomenon, e.g., via Hessian top-eigenvector analysis on smaller models.  
- Questions for authors: how does optimizer choice (ADAM vs. SGD with momentum) affect findings, and can dynamic batch-size schedules (small→large) mimic SB exploration to close the gap?
