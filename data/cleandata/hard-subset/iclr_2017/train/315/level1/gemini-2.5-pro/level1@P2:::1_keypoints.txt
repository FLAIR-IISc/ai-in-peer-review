- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training degrades generalization and hypothesizes LB finds "sharp" minima while small-batch (SB) methods find "flat" minima due to gradient noise.  
- Provides broad empirical evidence across architectures/datasets: 1D loss visualizations, a novel sharpness metric, and diagnostic experiments.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Reason 1 for acceptance: Tackles an important, poorly understood problem with a clear, testable hypothesis linking sharp minima to poor generalization.  
- Reason 2 for acceptance: Strong, multi-faceted empirical support (visual, quantitative, diagnostic) compellingly backs the central claim.  
- Problem statement: Explains why LB methods generalize worse despite similar training loss.  
- Approach and context: Well-motivated, placed in historical literature, and convincingly rules out simple overfitting explanations.  
- Parametric plots: 1D visualizations clearly show SB solutions lie in wide/flat basins while LB solutions are narrow/sharp.  
- Sharpness metric: Introduces a practical neighborhood-based heuristic (avoiding Hessian computation) showing 1–2 orders of magnitude sharper LB solutions.  
- Diagnostic experiments: Show a threshold batch size with phase-transition-like generalization drop; warm-starting shows SB explores basins SB finds and LB can exploit them; SB solutions are farther from initialization.  
- Feedback — metric robustness: Sharpness metric depends on epsilon and inner optimization steps; sensitivity analysis is needed.  
- Feedback — explain the "why": Paper should more deeply connect gradient-noise intuition to SGD theory (noise covariance, Hessian interaction) to explain mechanism.  
- Feedback — Appendix E: Results on improving LB (e.g., data augmentation helps but doesn't fix sharpness) merit a brief summary in the main text.  
- Question 1 for authors: Could a practical strategy be to start with small batches then progressively increase batch size to find flat basins then accelerate convergence?  
- Question 2 for authors: Did you consider anisotropic sharpness measures (e.g., top Hessian eigenvalues via Lanczos) to capture directional sharpness?  
- Question 3 for authors: Why might adversarial training fail to improve LB generalization despite its link to robust/flat solutions?
