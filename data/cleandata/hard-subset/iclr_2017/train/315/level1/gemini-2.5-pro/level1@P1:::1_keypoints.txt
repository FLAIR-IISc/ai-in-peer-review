- Large-batch (LB) training degrades generalization because LB methods converge to "sharp" minima, while small-batch (SB) methods find "flat" minima due to gradient noise.
- Authors provide 1D parametric visualizations showing LB minima are much sharper than SB minima.
- A novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric is introduced; LB solutions score one to two orders of magnitude higher in sharpness.
- SB methods explore a larger parameter-region before settling, explaining their tendency to find flat minima.
- Mitigation attempts (e.g., data augmentation, conservative training) yield partial but incomplete improvements for LB training.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for acceptance: the problem is important/timely and the paper supplies strong, thorough empirical evidence.
- The paper reframes the issue via loss-surface geometry, strengthening the flat-minima–generalization connection.
- Experiments are robust: multiple architectures, datasets, and random initializations validate the phenomenon is not setup-specific.
- The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a practical contribution since Hessian computation is infeasible for large models.
- Warm-starting experiments show SB pre-training enables LB to succeed, supporting the exploration hypothesis for SB noise.
- Appendix analyses of potential remedies are informative and highlight the non-triviality of resolving the LB gap.
- Suggestion: discuss sensitivity of the sharpness metric to hyperparameters (e.g., random subspace dimension p).
- Suggestion: emphasize that sharpness is concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace and report variance across random projections.
- Suggestion: summarize appendix findings (e.g., partial success of data augmentation) in the main conclusion.
- Question: clarify ADAM learning-rate choices and whether rates were tuned per batch size to rule out LR effects.
- Question: could inexact maximization (fixed L-BFGS-B steps) bias sharpness estimates between SB and LB solutions?
- Question: compare final test accuracy of warm-started LB to pure SB trained for the same total number of epochs to assess the role of LB vs SB phases.
