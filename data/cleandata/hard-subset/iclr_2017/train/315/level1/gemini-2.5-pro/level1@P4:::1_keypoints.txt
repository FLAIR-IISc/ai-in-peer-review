- Decision: Accept.

- Central claim: Large-batch training converges to sharp minima while small-batch training finds flatter minima due to gradient-noise-driven exploration, explaining the generalization gap.

- Empirical scope: Strong, comprehensive experiments across six architectures and multiple datasets (MNIST, CIFAR-10/100, TIMIT).

- Qualitative evidence: 1D parametric loss visualizations clearly show sharper minima for LB solutions vs flatter SB solutions.

- Quantitative evidence: A novel sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) shows 1–2 orders-of-magnitude higher sharpness for LB solutions.

- Robustness of results: Experiments report mean and standard deviation over five runs and include diverse model families (fully-connected, AlexNet/VGG-style convnets).

- Warm-start finding: LB methods warm-started from SB iterates can achieve good generalization, supporting the exploration hypothesis.

- Optimization trajectories: Plots demonstrate distinct paths for LB vs SB methods with respect to solution sharpness.

- Clarity: Paper is well-written, figures/tables are clear, and the central argument is consistently supported by experiments.

- Suggested theoretical grounding: Expand discussion connecting the sharpness metric to Hessian spectrum (e.g., largest eigenvalues, random subspace approximation).

- Geometry nuance: The observation that LB sharpness rises steeply only in a low-dimensional subspace should be quantified (e.g., via principal component analysis).

- Data-augmentation nuance: Appendix E shows LB generalization can improve with augmentation while remaining sharp, indicating a more complex sharpness–generalization relationship that merits comment.

- Question 1: How sensitive are sharpness comparisons to the neighborhood size ε in Equation (4) across a wider ε range?

- Question 2: Have you tried curriculum or progressive batch-size schedules (start small → increase) to close the generalization gap while preserving LB efficiency?

- Question 3: How sensitive are sharpness values to the number of inner L-BFGS-B iterations used to approximate the maximization in the sharpness computation?
