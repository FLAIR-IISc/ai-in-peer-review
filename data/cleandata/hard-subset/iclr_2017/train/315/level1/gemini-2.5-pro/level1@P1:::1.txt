### **Review of "On Large-Batch Training for Deep Learning: Generalization Gap and Sharp Minima"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper investigates a critical and widely observed phenomenon in deep learning: the degradation in generalization performance when training with large batch sizes. The authors posit that this "generalization gap" is not due to overfitting in the traditional sense, but rather stems from the geometry of the solutions found by different optimization regimes. The central claim is that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers of the loss function, which are characterized by high sensitivity to perturbations in the parameter space. In contrast, small-batch (SB) methods, benefiting from the inherent noise in their gradient estimates, are guided towards "flat" minimizers, which exhibit better generalization properties.

To substantiate this claim, the paper presents a comprehensive set of numerical experiments across six different network architectures (including fully-connected, AlexNet-like, and VGG-like models) and four datasets. The evidence provided is twofold:
1.  **Qualitative Visualization:** Using 1D parametric plots, the authors visualize the loss landscape between the solutions found by SB and LB methods, clearly illustrating the pronounced sharpness of the LB minima compared to the flatness of the SB minima.
2.  **Quantitative Metric:** A novel and computationally feasible metric for "sharpness" is proposed. This metric measures the maximal increase in the loss function within a small neighborhood of a given solution. Experiments show that the sharpness values for LB solutions are consistently one to two orders of magnitude higher than for SB solutions.

Furthermore, the paper explores the dynamics of the optimization process, providing evidence that SB methods explore a larger region of the parameter space before settling into a flat minimum. Attempts to mitigate the LB generalization gap using techniques like data augmentation and conservative training are discussed, showing partial but incomplete success, thereby reinforcing the difficulty of the problem.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Important and Timely Problem:** The paper provides a clear and compelling explanation for a significant practical barrier to scaling up deep learning training. Understanding and overcoming the limitations of large-batch training is crucial for leveraging parallel hardware and reducing training times.
2.  **Strong and Thorough Empirical Evidence:** The central hypothesis is supported by a rigorous and convincing set of experiments. The combination of intuitive visualizations and a novel quantitative sharpness metric provides strong evidence for the connection between batch size, minima sharpness, and generalization.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

This work represents a significant contribution to our understanding of optimization in deep learning. Its strengths lie in its clear focus, strong empirical methodology, and the practical relevance of its findings.

First, the paper successfully frames the problem in the context of the geometry of the loss surface, moving the discussion beyond simple heuristics. The connection between flat minima and good generalization, while a long-standing idea, is here demonstrated with convincing clarity to be at the heart of the large-batch training problem in modern neural networks.

Second, the experimental design is thorough and robust. The authors validate their claims across a diverse set of models and standard datasets, demonstrating that the observed phenomenon is not an artifact of a specific setup. The repetition of experiments with different random initializations adds to the credibility of the results. The introduction of the sharpness metric (Equation 4) is a key contribution, providing a practical tool to quantify a concept that is otherwise difficult to measure, especially as computing the Hessian is infeasible for these models.

Finally, the paper provides valuable insights into the underlying dynamics. The "warm-starting" experiment (Figure 5), where an LB method succeeds only after an SB method has performed initial exploration, is particularly insightful. It supports the hypothesis that the noise in SB-SGD acts as an explorer, enabling it to escape the basins of sharp minimizers near the initialization point, a region where LB methods appear to get trapped. The analysis is well-rounded, even including an exploration of potential remedies in the appendix, which highlights the non-trivial nature of the problem.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   The proposed sharpness metric is a valuable contribution. A brief discussion on its sensitivity to the choice of hyperparameters, such as the dimension `p` of the random subspace `A`, would be beneficial. While consistency between the full-space and random-subspace results is shown, understanding this sensitivity would make the metric more robust for future use by other researchers.
*   The paper makes the fascinating observation that sharp minima are steep only along a small-dimensional subspace. This is a powerful statement that could be emphasized more, perhaps by showing the sharpness metric's variance across several different random projections for the same solution.
*   The findings from the appendix, particularly regarding the partial success of data augmentation, are quite interesting. A sentence or two summarizing these attempts in the main conclusion would provide a more complete picture for the reader without requiring them to delve into the appendix.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

Your response to these questions would help clarify some details of the experimental setup and strengthen my confidence in the assessment.

1.  Could you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate, for the ADAM optimizer in both the small-batch and large-batch regimes? It is common practice to adjust the learning rate based on the batch size. Were the learning rates kept the same, or were they tuned independently for each regime? This is important for ruling out the possibility that the poor performance of LB methods is simply due to a suboptimal learning rate.
2.  The maximization for the sharpness metric is solved inexactly using a fixed number of L-BFGS-B steps. Have you explored whether a more exact maximization (i.e., more iterations) would alter the quantitative results? Specifically, could this inexactness be systematically underestimating the sharpness of one type of minimum over the other?
3.  The warm-starting experiment in Figure 5 is very compelling. How does the final test accuracy of a warm-started LB model compare to a pure SB model trained for the same total number of epochs (e.g., 200 epochs of SB vs. 100 epochs of SB + 100 epochs of LB)? This would clarify whether the LB phase is merely converging faster within a good basin found by SB, or if there is some other synergistic effect.
