## Review of "The Drawbacks of Large-Batch Methods"

This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon of generalization performance degradation when using large batch sizes in Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) for training deep learning models. The authors posit that this is due to large-batch methods converging to *sharp* minimizers of the loss landscape, which are known to generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to *flat* minimizers due to the inherent noise in gradient estimation. The paper provides numerical evidence supporting this claim across several network configurations and datasets, using parametric plots and a sharpness metric to characterize the minima found by each approach.

I **reject** this paper. While the observations presented are intuitively plausible and align with existing understanding of generalization in deep learning, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance at a competitive AI conference. Specifically, the connection between sharpness and generalization is presented as observational rather than demonstrably causal, and the proposed sharpness metric, while computationally feasible, feels somewhat ad-hoc and lacks theoretical justification.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear presentation of the problem and its systematic exploration of the differences between large and small batch training. The experiments are well-designed, and the results consistently show a correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and generalization gap. However, correlation does not equal causation. The authors acknowledge this, but the paper doesn’t go far enough to establish a stronger link. The attempts to remedy the generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are briefly mentioned, but the results are inconclusive and don’t significantly strengthen the core argument. The paper essentially confirms a known empirical observation with further empirical evidence, without offering a substantial theoretical advance or a practical solution.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of related work, particularly recent theoretical analyses of the loss landscape in deep learning.
*   The sharpness metric (Equation 4) needs more justification. How does it relate to the Hessian spectrum? What are its limitations?
*   The authors mention the potential for dynamic batch size scheduling. Exploring this idea further, even with preliminary experiments, would strengthen the paper.
*   It would be helpful to see a more detailed analysis of the impact of different optimizers (beyond ADAM) on the sharpness of the minima.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the limitations of your sharpness metric and how it might be improved to better capture the characteristics of the loss landscape?
2.  Have you investigated the relationship between the learning rate and the sharpness of the minima obtained with large-batch methods? Does a smaller learning rate mitigate the issue?
3.  The paper states that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting. Could you provide more detailed analysis (e.g., learning curves, weight decay experiments) to definitively rule out overfitting as a contributing factor?
4.  What is the computational cost of calculating the sharpness metric for the networks used in your experiments? Is it scalable to even larger models?
