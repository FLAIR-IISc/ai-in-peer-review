- Paper studies how large-batch SGD degrades generalization, arguing large batches find sharper minima while small batches find flatter ones due to gradient noise.
- Authors provide numerical experiments showing a correlation between batch size, minimizer sharpness, and the generalization gap.
- Recommendation: reject the paper in its current form.
- Main criticism: lacks sufficient rigor and novelty; the core claim reiterates known intuition rather than offering a breakthrough.
- Experiments are descriptive rather than analytical and do not introduce novel experimental methodology or insights.
- Strength: clear presentation and useful initial exploration of the problem.
- Analysis is shallow: the chosen “sharpness” metric is not well-justified and its link to generalization is not thoroughly explored.
- Proposed remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are mentioned briefly with limited experimental detail and show no significant improvement.
- Discussion acknowledges open questions but fails to propose concrete avenues for future research.
- Concrete improvements requested: justify the sharpness metric, perform deeper loss-landscape analysis with additional techniques, explore alternative characterizations of minima, and provide detailed results/limitations for remedies.
- Encouragement to revise and resubmit if authors address these points and more compellingly analyze how loss-landscape geometry and architecture susceptibility vary with batch size.
