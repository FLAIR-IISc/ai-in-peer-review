This paper investigates the generalization gap observed when increasing batch size in deep learning, attributing it to large-batch methods converging to sharper minimizers. The authors provide numerical evidence supporting this claim, showing that large batches indeed lead to sharper minima compared to small batches, which are associated with better generalization. They also explore initial strategies to mitigate this issue.

**Reject.** While the paper identifies a potentially important phenomenon and provides supporting empirical evidence, the core argument – a direct causal link between sharpness of minima and generalization gap *solely* due to batch size – isn't sufficiently substantiated. The analysis lacks the necessary rigor to definitively claim this is *the* cause, rather than a contributing factor entangled with other complexities of large-batch training.

The core argument rests on the observation that large batches converge to sharper minima. The provided evidence – parametric plots and a “sharpness” metric – does visually and quantitatively demonstrate this. However, the parametric plots, while insightful, only explore a one-dimensional slice of the loss landscape and a curved path. This is insufficient to generalize about the overall landscape structure. The proposed sharpness metric, though practical, is acknowledged to be a heuristic and its correlation with generalization performance isn’t proven, only asserted. A more thorough analysis of the Hessian spectrum, even on a smaller scale, would strengthen this claim.

Furthermore, the paper doesn’t convincingly rule out other factors contributing to the generalization gap. The role of learning rate schedules, optimizer differences, and potential interactions between batch size and network architecture are only briefly touched upon. The discussion acknowledges other potential causes (overfitting, saddle points), but dismisses them based on observations of training curves – this dismissal seems premature. The attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are presented without strong evidence of efficacy, essentially confirming the initial observation without offering a solution.  The fact that they don’t *completely* remedy the problem isn’t particularly insightful.

The paper’s strength lies in its clear articulation of the problem and its accessible presentation. The connection to existing literature on flat vs. sharp minima is well established. The inclusion of an idealized performance model in the Appendix is a thoughtful addition, illustrating the potential benefits of scaling large-batch training.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Sharpness Metric:** Elaborate on the limitations of the sharpness metric and provide justification for its choice over other potential metrics. How sensitive are the results to the choice of ‘λ’ in Equation (4)?
* **Hessian Analysis:** Consider including a limited Hessian analysis (e.g., top eigenvalues) for a subset of experiments to provide a more direct measure of sharpness.
* **Learning Rate:** Investigate the influence of different learning rate schedules specifically designed for large-batch training (e.g., linear scaling rule) on both sharpness and generalization.
* **Optimizer Comparison:** A more comprehensive comparison of different optimizers (beyond ADAM) under both small and large batch conditions would be valuable.
* **Network Architectures:** Explore whether the observed phenomenon is consistent across a broader range of network architectures (e.g., transformers).
* **Statistical Significance:** Provide statistical significance testing for the observed differences in sharpness and generalization performance.
* **Clarify ‘Sharpness’:** Define more precisely what constitutes a "sharp" versus "flat" minimum in the context of the experiments. Is it based purely on eigenvalue magnitude, or are other properties considered?



I would like to ask the authors:

1.  How sensitive are the conclusions regarding the sharpness of minima to the choice of the parameter λ in Equation (4)? Have you performed a sensitivity analysis?
2.  The parametric plots are 1D slices of the loss landscape. Are there any guarantees that the observations made along these slices hold true for the higher-dimensional loss surface?
3.  Besides the remedies explored in Appendix E, have you considered techniques like Layer-wise Adaptive Rate Scaling (LARS) or other large-batch specific optimization techniques? If so, what were the results?
