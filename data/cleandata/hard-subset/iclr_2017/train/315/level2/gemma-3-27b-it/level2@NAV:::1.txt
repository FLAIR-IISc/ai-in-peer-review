## Review of “The stochastic gradient descent method and its variants”

This paper investigates the degradation in generalization performance observed when using large batch sizes in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for deep learning. The authors posit that large batches converge to sharper minimizers of the loss function, which are known to generalize poorly, while small batches converge to flatter minimizers due to the inherent noise in gradient estimation. They support this claim with numerical experiments demonstrating a correlation between batch size, sharpness of the minima found, and generalization gap.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper in its current form. While the observed correlation between batch size and minimizer sharpness is interesting and aligns with established theory regarding generalization, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance. The core idea – that large batches find sharp minima – isn’t groundbreaking and is largely a restatement of existing intuition within the field. The experiments, while performed on a variety of network configurations, are descriptive rather than analytical and do not offer a novel experimental methodology or insight beyond confirming existing beliefs. 

The paper’s strength lies in its clear presentation of the problem and its initial exploration. However, the analysis lacks depth. The “sharpness” metric, while computationally feasible, isn’t clearly justified as a comprehensive measure of minimizer quality, and its connection to generalization isn't thoroughly explored beyond referencing established theory. The attempts to remedy the problem (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are briefly mentioned with limited experimental detail and offer no significant improvement. The discussion section acknowledges open questions, which is positive, but doesn't propose concrete avenues for future research.

To improve this paper, the authors should: 1) Provide a more rigorous justification for the chosen sharpness metric and its relevance to generalization performance. 2) Conduct a more in-depth analysis of the loss landscape, potentially utilizing techniques beyond the presented parametric plots. 3) Explore alternative methods for characterizing the differences between minima found by large and small batches. 4) Offer more detailed experimental results for the attempted remedies and discuss their limitations. 

I would encourage the authors to revise and resubmit if they can address these points and provide a more compelling and novel contribution to the understanding of large-batch training. Specifically, how does the *geometry* of the loss landscape change with batch size, and can we predict this change? What specific network architectures are most susceptible to the generalization gap?
