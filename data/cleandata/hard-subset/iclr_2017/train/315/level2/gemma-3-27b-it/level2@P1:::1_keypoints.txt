- Paper studies the generalization gap with increasing batch size and provides experimental evidence that large-batch training finds sharper minima while small-batch training finds flatter minima across architectures and datasets.

- Decision: Reject — valuable empirical observation but insufficient methodological novelty or theoretical justification.

- Experiments show correlation between batch size and sharpness but do not establish causation beyond existing suspicions.

- Core finding reiterates a widely held belief; the work is more descriptive (visualizations, sharpness metrics) than explanatory.

- Attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are standard and yield only preliminary, limited improvements.

- Heavy reliance on empirical results without new theoretical analysis linking batch size to bias toward sharp minima.

- The proposed sharpness metric (Eq. 4) requires more justification; its sensitivity to the choice of matrix A and relation to the Hessian spectrum are unclear.

- The paper needs a deeper comparison to existing theoretical and empirical literature on loss surface properties (e.g., Marchenko-Pastur–related work).

- Alternative explanations for the generalization gap are not sufficiently explored or ruled out.

- Practical implications are underdeveloped; clearer guidance for practitioners on choosing batch sizes or modifying training is lacking.

- A convergence-rate analysis comparing large- and small-batch methods is missing and would strengthen the paper.

- Question for authors: how sensitive is the sharpness metric to the choice of matrix A and what are the implications of different A choices?

- Question for authors: are there theoretical results predicting the batch-size vs. sharpness relationship, and how do the empirical findings align with them?

- Question for authors: have other mitigation strategies (different optimizers, learning-rate schedules) been explored to counteract sharp minima?

- Question for authors: how does the proposed sharpness metric compare to other flatness measures such as the Hessian condition number?
