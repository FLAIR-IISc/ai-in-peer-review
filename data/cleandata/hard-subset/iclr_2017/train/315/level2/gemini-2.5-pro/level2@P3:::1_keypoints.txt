- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), hypothesizing LB finds "sharp" minima while SB finds "flat" minima due to gradient noise.
- Presents extensive empirical evidence across six architectures/datasets using 1D loss visualizations and a novel, computationally feasible sharpness metric.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Provides a clear, plausible connection between batch size, minima geometry (sharp vs. flat), and generalization.
- Key reason 2: Claims are supported by thorough, consistent empirical results (visuals + quantitative sharpness metric) across diverse models.
- Strength: Clear problem formulation, strong motivation, rigorous experiments, and relevance to practical parallelized training.
- Sharpness metric results: LB solutions are consistently one to two orders of magnitude sharper (Tables 3–4).
- Warm-starting experiment: Initializing LB with a trained SB model lets LB find flatter, better-generalizing minima, supporting the SB exploration hypothesis.
- Remedies tested (e.g., data augmentation, robust training) do not fully close the gap, suggesting the issue is fundamental to optimization dynamics.
- Feedback 1: The sharpness metric’s constraint set C scales with solution norm—provide justification for this unconventional choice.
- Feedback 2: Recommend a small-scale Hessian eigenvalue comparison to validate the sharpness metric as a curvature proxy.
- Feedback 3: Minor typo: Appendix E.2 references Figure 7 incorrectly; fix the figure reference.
- Question 1: Clarify why the search space depends on solution x and whether systematic norm differences between LB and SB affect sharpness comparisons.
- Question 2: Please provide sharpness versus training epoch for a typical SB run to illustrate the purported exploration phase.
- Question 3: Elaborate on adversarial training results—did adversarial training change LB solution sharpness even if test accuracy didn’t improve?
