**Summary**

This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large batch sizes results in a "generalization gap"—poorer performance on unseen data compared to models trained with small batch sizes, even when achieving similar training loss. The central contribution is to provide strong empirical evidence for the hypothesis that this gap is caused by the different types of minima found by the two training regimes. The work posits that large-batch (LB) methods tend to converge to "sharp" minimizers, which are characterized by large positive eigenvalues of the Hessian, while small-batch (SB) methods, aided by the inherent noise in their gradient estimates, find "flat" minimizers that generalize better. To support this claim, the paper presents an extensive set of experiments across six different network architectures (fully-connected, AlexNet-style, and VGG-style) and multiple datasets. The evidence is presented through qualitative 1D visualizations of the loss landscape and a novel, computationally feasible metric for quantifying the sharpness of a minimum. The paper further explores the dynamics of the optimization process, suggesting that SB methods perform an essential exploration phase, and tests several potential remedies for improving LB training, such as data augmentation and robust training.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: **Accept**

This paper addresses a problem of significant practical and theoretical importance in the optimization of deep neural networks. The work is recommended for acceptance for two primary reasons:
1.  It provides a clear, well-motivated, and plausible explanation for the generalization gap in large-batch training. The connection drawn between batch size, the geometry of the converged solution (sharp vs. flat minima), and generalization performance is compelling and provides a valuable new perspective on the issue.
2.  The claims are supported by a thorough and convincing empirical investigation. The combination of qualitative visualizations and a quantitative sharpness metric provides multifaceted evidence. The consistency of the findings across a diverse set of modern network architectures and standard datasets demonstrates the generality of the phenomenon.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths lie in its clear problem formulation, strong motivation, and rigorous experimental methodology. The practical need to understand the limitations of large-batch training is high, given its potential to accelerate training through parallelization. The work successfully connects this practical problem to more fundamental concepts of optimization landscapes and generalization, citing relevant literature on minimum description length and Bayesian perspectives to ground its hypothesis.

The experimental evidence is the core of the paper and is very well-executed. The 1D parametric plots (Figure 3) provide a striking and intuitive visualization of the difference between LB and SB minima. This is effectively complemented by the proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), which allows for a quantitative comparison. The observation that this metric is consistently one to two orders of magnitude higher for LB solutions across all experiments (Tables 3 and 4) is a powerful result.

Furthermore, the paper includes insightful secondary analyses that deepen the understanding of the optimization dynamics. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5), which shows that an LB method can find a good solution if initialized by a sufficiently trained SB model, strongly supports the idea that SB methods are crucial for *exploring* the loss surface and locating the basins of flat minima. The investigation into potential remedies in Appendix E, while not yielding a complete solution, is valuable as it demonstrates that common regularization techniques like data augmentation are insufficient to close the generalization gap entirely, reinforcing that the problem is a fundamental property of the optimization dynamics.

**Additional Feedback**

The following points are offered as constructive feedback to potentially improve the paper further. They are not critical for the decision to accept but could enhance the clarity and impact of the work.

1.  **On the Sharpness Metric:** The proposed sharpness metric is a key contribution. However, the definition of the constraint set `C` in Equation 3 causes the size of the neighborhood to depend on the norm of the solution `x`. This is an unconventional choice. A brief justification for this design, perhaps explaining how it aims for scale invariance or another desirable property compared to a simpler fixed-size neighborhood (e.g., an epsilon-ball), would strengthen the definition and interpretation of the results.
2.  **Connection to Hessian:** The paper correctly notes the connection between sharpness and the eigenvalues of the Hessian. While computationally expensive, a small-scale experiment (e.g., on a smaller network) directly comparing the proposed metric with the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian could serve as a valuable validation of the metric as a reliable proxy for curvature. This is a nice-to-have suggestion for future work if not feasible for the revision.
3.  **Typo Correction:** There appears to be a minor typo in Appendix E.2. The text refers to Figure 7 for results on conservative training, but Figure 7 in Appendix D contains curvilinear parametric plots. This reference should be corrected.

**Questions for Authors**

To help clarify some aspects of the work, it would be helpful if the authors could address the following:

1.  Regarding the sharpness metric, could the rationale for making the search space dependent on the solution `x` be elaborated upon? Is it possible that LB and SB solutions have systematically different norms, and if so, how might this dependency affect the sharpness comparison?
2.  The warm-starting experiment is very illuminating. Figure 6 shows the evolution of sharpness versus loss. Could a plot of sharpness versus training epoch for a typical SB run be provided? This might more directly illustrate the "exploration phase" where the optimizer moves through sharper regions before settling into a flat one.
3.  The finding in Appendix E that adversarial training did not improve generalization is interesting and somewhat counter-intuitive, as such methods are often motivated by finding solutions robust to perturbations. Could this result be discussed in more detail? For instance, did adversarial training have any effect on the measured sharpness of the LB solutions, even if the final test accuracy did not improve?
