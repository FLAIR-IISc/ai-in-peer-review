- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training degrades generalization, attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima while small-batch (SB) finds flatter minima.  
- Authors provide extensive empirical evidence across multiple network architectures and datasets.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Key reason: Addresses an important, practical problem in large-scale deep learning.  
- Key reason: Presents a clear hypothesis (sharp vs. flat minima) supported by comprehensive experiments.  
- Strong empirical methodology: rigorously demonstrates the generalization gap and rules out simple explanations like traditional overfitting.  
- Parametric 1D plots (line between SB and LB solutions) visually illustrate differences in sharpness between minima.  
- Proposes a practical, computationally feasible sharpness metric as a heuristic related to the Hessian.  
- Quantitative results show a consistent 1–2 order of magnitude difference in sharpness between SB and LB solutions.  
- Warm-starting experiments suggest SB performs exploration that enables LB to reach flatter regions if initialized appropriately.  
- Plots relating batch size, sharpness, and test accuracy reinforce the observed correlations.  
- Paper is well-written, experiments are clearly described, and conclusions are well-supported; appendix explores preliminary remedies.  
- Suggestion: Make the connection between the sharpness metric and the largest Hessian eigenvalue more explicit via a second-order Taylor discussion.  
- Suggestion: Analyze directions found by the L-BFGS-B maximizer to characterize the low-dimensional “sharp subspace.”  
- Suggestion: Comment on hybrid training strategies (start small batch then increase) and relation to dynamic sampling work.  
- Suggestion: Report number of updates (not just epochs) required for SB and LB to reach final training loss to clarify computational cost.  
- Suggestion: Discuss whether converging to a sharp minimum could be considered a form of overfitting to the loss geometry (semantic point).
