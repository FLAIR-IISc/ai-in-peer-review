This paper investigates the widely observed phenomenon that training deep neural networks with large batches leads to poorer generalization compared to small-batch training. The authors' central claim is that this "generalization gap" is caused by large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers of the loss function, whereas the noise inherent in small-batch stochastic gradient descent guides the optimization to flatter minimizers, which are known to generalize better. The paper presents a compelling suite of numerical experiments across multiple network architectures and datasets to support this hypothesis, using visualization techniques and a novel, computationally efficient metric for quantifying the sharpness of a minimum.

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The work addresses a timely and significant problem in deep learning optimization with practical implications for training speed and scalability. The primary reasons for my decision are the clarity of the central hypothesis and the strength of the supporting empirical evidence. The experiments are well-designed, and the results consistently show that large-batch solutions exhibit significantly higher sharpness, providing a convincing explanation for the generalization gap.

The supporting arguments for this work are robust. The use of 1D parametric plots effectively visualizes the difference in the loss landscape geometry around the solutions found by small-batch (SB) and large-batch (LB) methods. More importantly, the paper introduces a quantitative sharpness metric that moves beyond qualitative visualization, showing a 1-2 order of magnitude difference in sharpness between LB and SB solutions. The warm-starting experiment is particularly insightful, demonstrating that LB methods can find good solutions if initialized in the basin of a flat minimum discovered by an initial SB phase. This strengthens the claim that the noise in SB methods is crucial for exploration.

For improvement, while the proposed sharpness metric is a valuable contribution, the construction of the constraint set C in Equation (3) could be further motivated. A brief justification for the specific form `(|(A+x)i|+ 1)` would strengthen the metric's credibility. Furthermore, the observation that the sharpness of LB minima is concentrated in a low-dimensional subspace is fascinating and warrants more prominent discussion in the main text, perhaps with speculation on its cause.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1. Your experiments show a clear batch size threshold where generalization degrades. Have you investigated whether this threshold correlates with the signal-to-noise ratio of the stochastic gradients?
2. The warm-starting experiment suggests a transition from an "exploration" phase to a "convergence" phase. Can you characterize when this transition occurs, for instance, by monitoring the sharpness metric or the training loss trajectory during the initial small-batch training?
