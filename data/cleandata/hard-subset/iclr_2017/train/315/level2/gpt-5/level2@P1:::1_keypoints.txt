- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), claiming LB converges to sharper minima which correlate with poorer generalization.
- Contributions: 1D/curvilinear loss plots, a practical sharpness metric via worst-case local perturbations (L-BFGS-B), experiments across architectures/datasets, warm-start studies, and preliminary mitigation attempts.
- Decision: Reject.
- Primary rejection reason: Sharpness metric is not parameterization-invariant in ReLU/BN networks, so observed correlations may be artifacts of weight rescaling rather than intrinsic function properties.
- Secondary rejection reason: Experimental controls are incomplete—batch-size/learning-rate coupling, optimizer effects, and compute/updates are not rigorously controlled.
- The research question is well motivated and practically important.
- Literature placement is adequate but omits critical discussion of parameterization invariances that affect flatness/sharpness definitions.
- The proposed sharpness measure depends on parameter scaling and may be arbitrarily altered by layer-wise rescaling that preserves the function.
- The box-based normalization around parameters does not convincingly address re-scaling degrees of freedom in BN/positively homogeneous networks.
- 1D and curvilinear plots are visually compelling but probe negligible manifolds and don't characterize high-dimensional local geometry.
- Most experiments use ADAM with limited detail on SGD/results; optimizer and hyperparameter effects are insufficiently ablated.
- Learning rates, weight decay, and schedule choices across batch sizes are not systematically controlled or fully reported.
- It is unclear whether comparisons are matched for parameter updates, epochs, or gradient evaluations, complicating fairness across batch sizes.
- Mitigation experiments (data augmentation, conservative steps, adversarial/stability training) lack sufficient detail to draw strong conclusions.
- Overall: hypothesis plausible and some experiments are thought-provoking, but evidence is insufficient due to invariance issues and experimental confounds.
- Suggested improvement: adopt parameterization-invariant flatness measures (e.g., per-filter/BN normalization, function-space or PAC-Bayes based metrics) and test robustness of conclusions.
- Suggested improvement: report Hessian diagnostics (top eigenvalues/traces) using stochastic Lanczos/power iteration and control for scale.
- Suggested improvement: systematically tune and report training schedules per batch size (e.g., linear LR scaling with warmup), match compute budgets and number of updates.
- Suggested improvement: provide optimizer ablations (SGD+momentum vs ADAM) with tuned hyperparameters per batch size.
- Suggested improvement: test additional architectures (e.g., modern ResNets) and larger datasets or reference prior LB-closure results for context.
- Suggested improvement: detail adversarial training protocol (epsilon, norm, steps) and report robustness metrics; consider function-space penalties.
- Suggested improvement: empirically analyze gradient noise covariance and its evolution with batch size/learning rate to relate to escape from sharp basins.
- Key questions for authors: how were LRs, decay, weight decay, and BN params chosen per batch size, and does linear LR scaling with warmup close the gap?
- Key question: is the sharpness metric invariant under layer-wise rescaling in ReLU/BN networks; can you show metric stability under reparameterization?
- Key question: can you report top Hessian eigenvalues at SB and LB solutions and their sensitivity to reparameterization?
- Key question: are results reproduced with SGD+momentum and tuned schedules on standard benchmarks (e.g., CIFAR with ResNets)?
- Key question: in warm-start experiments, can you quantify the threshold (loss or sharpness) where LB succeeds to guide dynamic batching?
- Key question: what adversarial attack parameters were used and were attacks equally effective on SB and LB models?
