1. Summary and contributions: The paper tackles why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) methods. Its central claim is that LB converges to sharp minima—characterized by large positive curvature—while SB, aided by gradient noise, gravitates toward flatter minima that generalize better. The authors provide: (i) consistent empirical evidence across six architectures/datasets using parametric loss plots between SB/LB solutions and a computationally feasible sharpness metric; (ii) warm-start experiments suggesting that SB “exploration” helps LB reach flatter basins; (iii) negative/partial results from mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative proximal steps, adversarial training); and (iv) a simple performance model motivating the value of LB if the generalization gap can be closed.

2. Decision: Accept. Key reasons: (a) Clear, timely, and well-motivated empirical characterization linking batch size, noise, and landscape geometry; (b) Practical diagnostic tools and insights likely to influence practice and follow-on research.

3. Supporting arguments: The paper is well placed in the literature and uses standard architectures and losses, multiple runs, and optimizer variants. The parametric plots and sharpness scores consistently show LB sharper minima with similar training losses but worse test performance, addressing “does the paper support its claims?” with convergent evidence. The warm-start results compellingly connect gradient noise to flatness. While the sharpness metric is heuristic and the maximization is inexact, the authors discuss its relation to Hessian spectra and provide full-space and random-subspace variants. Limitations include lack of theory (acknowledged), potential sensitivity of the metric to scaling/parameterization, and incomplete control of LB hyperparameters (e.g., learning-rate scaling/warmup, weight decay), which could confound the gap. Nonetheless, the overall empirical case is strong and useful.

4. Suggestions to improve (constructive, not essential to decision): 
- Control for total number of optimizer updates; report schedules, warmup, momentum, and weight decay for SB/LB.
- Provide sensitivity analyses for the sharpness metric (epsilon, p, number of L-BFGS-B steps, number of random subspaces).
- Estimate top Hessian eigenvalues via power/Lanczos to triangulate sharpness.
- Compare to noise-injection baselines that match SB gradient noise (e.g., add calibrated Gaussian noise to LB).
- Run a simple dynamic batch-size schedule, given the warm-start findings, and quantify test sharpness.
- Discuss parameterization invariance and batch norm effects on sharpness.

Questions for authors:
- Did you equalize training by number of updates rather than epochs? How does this affect the gap?
- What LR scaling/warmup and weight decay were used for LB, and how sensitive are results to these?
- How many random subspaces A were averaged per run, and how stable is φx,f across them and epsilon?
- Can you report top-k Hessian eigenvalues at SB/LB solutions?
- Does the observed sharpness–generalization link hold when measuring sharpness on the test loss?
