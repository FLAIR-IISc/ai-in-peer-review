Summary of contributions
- The paper investigates why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse generalization than small-batch (SB) training in deep networks. It advances the hypothesis that LB tends to converge to sharp minima, while SB—due to gradient noise—finds flatter minima that generalize better.
- It provides several empirical probes across multiple architectures and datasets: (i) 1D and curvilinear parametric plots between SB and LB solutions showing much sharper landscapes near LB solutions; (ii) a computationally tractable “sharpness” measure based on local sensitivity (Eq. 4) computed both in full space and in random subspaces; (iii) batch-size threshold experiments showing a clear phase transition in generalization and sharpness; (iv) warm-start experiments where LB initialized from an SB trajectory recovers good generalization once the SB iterate reaches a flatter region; (v) attempts to mitigate LB’s generalization gap (data augmentation, conservative training, adversarial training), which help but do not eliminate sharp minima.
- The work relates its findings to MDL/Bayesian intuitions about flatness and to recent theory on the multiplicity of minima with similar training loss.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) The problem is important and timely for scaling deep learning, and the empirical evidence is thorough and compelling across models, optimizers, and seeds.
2) The connection drawn between batch-induced gradient noise, basin escape, and flatness is insightful and actionable (e.g., dynamic sampling/warm-start), even if the flatness metric is heuristic.

Supporting arguments
- Relevance and originality: Understanding the generalization gap with large batches is crucial for distributed/parallel training. The warm-start experiments, batch-size thresholds, and sharpness correlations offer novel, informative perspectives, beyond anecdotal observations.
- Empirical support: The consistency of the findings across six networks and multiple optimizers (ADAM, ADAGRAD, SGD, adaQN), five random initializations, and both full-space/random-subspace sharpness measurements strengthens the central claim. The observation that both SB and LB achieve similar training loss while differing sharply on test accuracy is carefully documented.
- Methodological clarity: The paper explains the sharpness proxy and why an eigenvalue-based analysis is impractical at scale. It validates sharpness differences using complementary tools (1D/curvilinear paths and sensitivity maximization).
- Practical implications: The dynamic sampling/warm-start result is concrete: as SB exits sharp basins, LB can then “lock in” and maintain good generalization, suggesting a principled hybrid recipe for scalable training.
- Relation to literature: The paper situates the flat vs. sharp minima discussion within MDL and Bayesian views and acknowledges recent landscape analyses in deep learning.

Weaknesses and limitations (not blocking)
- The sharpness metric depends on parameterization and the choice of ε, and the proposed normalization may not fully address scale invariances in ReLU networks (e.g., per-layer rescalings, effects of batch normalization). This makes absolute comparisons across runs and architectures delicate.
- Hyperparameter confounders for LB are not fully ruled out. In particular, batch-size-specific tuning (learning rate scaling/warmup, momentum, weight decay, BN/Dropout interactions) could reduce the gap; the paper uses ADAM by default and does not present a systematic LR/momentum sweep per batch size.
- The maximization in Eq. (4) is approximate (10 L-BFGS-B steps), and the results may be sensitive to ε and to the subspace dimension p. More ablations would strengthen robustness claims.
- The study matches “until the loss ceases to improve,” not a fixed compute budget or fixed number of epochs/steps; some comparisons might be affected by differing training dynamics.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Make sharpness more invariant: Complement Eq. (4) with parameterization-robust proxies (e.g., layer-wise normalized sharpness, spectral norm/path-norm normalization, Fisher-based measures, PAC-Bayesian local entropy) to strengthen the causal link between “flatness” and generalization and address scale invariances.
- Hyperparameter controls: Provide batch-size-aware tuning (linear LR scaling, warmup, momentum dampening, weight decay search) and report whether careful LB tuning reduces the gap. Include a grid or Bayesian search summary per batch size for key hyperparameters.
- Spectral validation: Use Hessian-vector products with Lanczos/Power iteration to estimate top eigenvalues around SB vs. LB solutions for at least a subset of models, corroborating the sensitivity metric.
- Sensitivity analyses: Report how sharpness and test accuracy vary with ε, p, and number of ascent iterations in Eq. (4). Include confidence intervals for sharpness and accuracy.
- Evaluation protocol: Provide results under matched budgets (same epochs, same total gradient evaluations, and same wall-clock budgets where feasible). This will rule out confounds tied to training duration.
- Stronger baselines: Evaluate gradient-noise injection or temperature-adjusted updates to emulate SB noise in LB (e.g., add Gaussian noise calibrated to batch variance). Also try label smoothing or mixup to assess interactions with flatness.
- Reporting: Release code and full hyperparameter details to facilitate replication; include more seeds where possible.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are your sharpness conclusions to ε, p, and the number of ascent iterations in Eq. (4)? Can you provide curves of sharpness vs. ε?
2) How do you address scale invariances introduced by ReLU and BN? Did you try layer-wise normalized sharpness or path-norm-normalized measures, and do your conclusions persist?
3) Did you systematically tune learning rate (including linear scaling and warmup), momentum, and weight decay for each batch size? If so, how much does the gap shrink with careful LB tuning?
4) Under a fixed compute budget (e.g., same number of parameter updates or same wall-clock), do the conclusions remain?
5) Can you report approximate top Hessian eigenvalues (via Lanczos) at SB and LB minima for at least one model to directly validate the curvature difference?
6) In the warm-start experiments, if you continue SB alongside LB from the same checkpoint with matched hyperparameters, do the trajectories diverge in sharpness in a predictable way?
7) Do your batch-size thresholds scale with dataset size, model capacity, or optimizer choice in a principled manner (e.g., threshold ∝ variance/curvature)? Any empirical law?

Overall, this is a well-executed and insightful empirical study on an important problem, with actionable observations (e.g., dynamic sampling) and a compelling narrative linking gradient noise, basin escape, and flat minima. With stronger invariance controls and hyperparameter tuning, the work would be even more convincing.
