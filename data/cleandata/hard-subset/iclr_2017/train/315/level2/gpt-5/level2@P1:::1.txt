Summary and contributions
The paper studies why large-batch (LB) training often yields worse test performance than small-batch (SB) training, despite achieving similar training loss. The central claim is that LB methods tend to converge to sharp minimizers, whereas SB methods converge to flatter ones, and that sharpness correlates with poorer generalization. The authors support this with: (i) parametric 1D and curvilinear plots of the loss between SB and LB solutions; (ii) a practical sharpness metric based on local worst-case perturbations computed with L-BFGS-B, both in the full space and on random low-dimensional subspaces; (iii) empirical evidence across several architectures and datasets; (iv) warm-start experiments suggesting that SB noise helps escape sharp basins and that LB can succeed when initialized near flat regions; and (v) preliminary attempts to mitigate the gap via data augmentation, “conservative” proximal steps, and adversarial/stability training. The paper is timely and tackles a question of clear practical importance, and provides a thoughtful, accessible empirical narrative.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The primary evidence hinges on a sharpness measure that is not parameterization-invariant in deep ReLU/BN networks, where simple re-scalings can arbitrarily change Hessian-based or local perturbation sharpness without changing the represented function. This makes the central correlation potentially confounded and the main claim insufficiently supported.
- Experimental controls are incomplete: batch-size–learning-rate coupling, optimizer effects, and matching of compute/updates are not rigorously controlled, making it difficult to attribute the generalization gap specifically to “sharp minima” rather than to differences in noise scale, training schedules, or optimization dynamics.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem posed: The paper asks whether the generalization gap for large batches arises because LB methods converge to different types of minima than SB methods. This is well motivated and important.

2) Placement in the literature: The authors connect their view to MDL/Bayesian intuitions and to empirical visualization work. However, there is no discussion of parameterization invariances ubiquitous in ReLU/BN networks that can make “flatness”/“sharpness” ill-defined in parameter space. This omission is critical given the paper’s focus.

3) Do results support the claims?
- Sharpness metric: The proposed metric (maximizing f within a box defined from A and A+ around x) depends on the parameterization and the scale of x. In networks with batch normalization or positively homogeneous activations, multiplying weights in one layer and dividing in another can leave the function unchanged while altering curvature/perturbation sensitivity arbitrarily. Although the authors aim for some normalization via the box definition, it is not clear this addresses these re-scaling degrees of freedom. Without parameterization-invariant diagnostics, the observed 1–2 order-of-magnitude differences could be artifacts of weight scaling choices during training rather than intrinsic properties of the function class minima.
- 1D and curvilinear plots: These are visually compelling but probe a tiny manifold between two points. Sharpness in an arbitrary slice does not characterize the local geometry in high dimensions, particularly under reparameterization.
- Optimizer and schedule confounds: Most results use ADAM, with brief mention that SGD/Adagrad behave similarly (details not shown). Learning rates and schedules, weight decay, and number of updates vs epochs are not fully specified or controlled across batch sizes. The noise scale depends jointly on batch size and learning rate; without a systematic schedule (e.g., linear scaling of learning rate with batch size and tuned decay), part of the gap could stem from suboptimal hyperparameters for LB.
- Compute matching: It is unclear whether comparisons are matched for total number of parameter updates, epochs, or gradient evaluations. LB methods often require different schedules to reach similar validation accuracy.
- Remedies: The negative/partial results on data augmentation, proximal “conservative” steps, and adversarial/stability training are interesting, but the setups (e.g., adversarial strength, optimization of inner problems) are not described in enough detail to draw strong conclusions.

Overall, the paper’s hypothesis is plausible and interesting, and several experiments (especially the warm-start and batch-size threshold curves) are thought-provoking. However, the lack of a parameterization-invariant analysis of sharpness, and incomplete controls for batch-size–dependent training dynamics, leave the main causal story insufficiently substantiated for acceptance.

Suggestions to improve the paper (constructive, not all part of my decision)
- Parameterization-invariant flatness: Adopt or design sharpness measures invariant to layer rescaling (e.g., normalize per-filter/BN scales; consider measures in function space, margin-based, or PAC-Bayesian flatness bounds). Report whether your conclusions hold under such invariants.
- Hessian diagnostics: Estimate top eigenvalues/traces via stochastic Lanczos/power iteration and Hutchinson’s method; report across seeds. Combine with scale normalization to mitigate BN/re-scaling effects.
- Control training schedules: For each batch size, tune learning rate via a principled rule (e.g., linear scaling with warmup), match weight decay and total number of updates, and report exact schedules. Compare SB vs LB under matched compute budgets and matched number of updates.
- Optimizer ablations: Provide full results for SGD with momentum (commonly used in vision) and ADAM, including tuned hyperparameters per batch size. Report whether the sharpness/generalization trend is optimizer-agnostic.
- Architectures and datasets: Include at least one modern residual architecture to test generality. If possible, add a larger-scale dataset or reference prior results that close the LB gap with specific schedules to contextualize your findings.
- Robust/adversarial training: Specify attack strength and protocol (epsilon, norm, steps) and report robustness metrics. Consider robust objectives that are parameterization-aware or function-space penalties.
- Noise scale analysis: Empirically measure gradient covariance and its evolution with batch size and learning rate; relate to escape from sharp basins.

Questions for the authors
- How were learning rates, decay schedules, weight decay, and BN parameters chosen per batch size? Did you try linear LR scaling with warmup for LB, and does the gap persist under that regimen?
- Is your sharpness metric invariant under layer-wise rescaling in networks with ReLU/BN? If not, can you provide experiments where you reparameterize a trained solution (preserving function) to show metric stability?
- Can you report top Hessian eigenvalues (and their sensitivity to reparameterization) at SB and LB solutions?
- Are results reproduced with SGD+momentum and tuned schedules on CIFAR-10/100 with ResNets?
- In the warm-start experiment, what threshold of training loss or sharpness indicates that LB will succeed? Can you quantify this to guide dynamic batch-size schedules?
- For adversarial training, what attack parameters were used, and did you verify that attacks were effective on both SB and LB models?

I appreciate the clarity of the empirical narrative and the practical importance of the question. With stronger, parameterization-robust evidence and tighter experimental controls, this could become a valuable contribution.
