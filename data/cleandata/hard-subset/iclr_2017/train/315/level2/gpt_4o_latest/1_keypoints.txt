- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) SGD generalizes worse than small-batch (SB) SGD, attributing the gap to convergence to sharp minimizers.  
- Authors introduce a sharpness metric to quantify minimizer sensitivity and provide numerical evidence that LB finds sharper minima while SB finds flatter ones.  
- Extensive experiments across multiple architectures and datasets support the central hypothesis using parametric plots and sharpness measurements.  
- The paper explores mitigation strategies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) but finds only limited improvement.  
- Recommendation: Accept.  
- Novelty/relevance: Addresses a critical, well-documented deep learning problem and offers new empirical insights into its root causes.  
- Scientific rigor: Empirical claims are well-supported, the methodology appears sound and reproducible.  
- Clear problem identification: Effectively frames the generalization drop in LB methods and links it to sharp minimizers.  
- Comprehensive experiments: Evaluations span six network configurations and multiple datasets, providing robust quantitative and visual evidence.  
- Practical implications: Findings are relevant for improving scalable training methods, though proposed remedies are preliminary.  
- Suggestion — sharpness metric: Clarify computational feasibility, approximations, and limitations for broader applicability.  
- Suggestion — dynamic sampling: Provide experimental results for dynamic sampling as a potential mitigation strategy.  
- Suggestion — theory: Add theoretical analysis linking sharpness to Hessian eigenvalues to deepen understanding.  
- Suggestion — broader applicability: Test generalization of results on modern architectures (e.g., transformers).  
- Questions for authors: How sensitive is the sharpness metric to hyperparameters; is there a quantified correlation between sharpness and test accuracy; can warm-starting LB methods be further explored?
