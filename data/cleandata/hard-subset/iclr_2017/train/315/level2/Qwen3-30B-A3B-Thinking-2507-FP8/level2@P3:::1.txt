

The paper investigates the generalization gap observed when using large-batch training methods for deep learning, presenting numerical evidence supporting the hypothesis that large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers of the training function, which generalize poorly, while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers with better generalization properties. The work makes a clear contribution to understanding a well-known empirical phenomenon in deep learning, with strong experimental evidence including parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric that quantitatively captures the difference between small-batch and large-batch solutions.

The paper should be accepted. The main reason is that it provides a well-motivated and thoroughly supported explanation for a significant empirical observation in deep learning. The second key reason is that the proposed sharpness metric and associated experiments offer a clear, quantifiable framework for measuring the phenomenon, making the findings more robust and reproducible.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, drawing appropriate connections to concepts of sharp and flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Chaudhari et al., 2016). It clearly distinguishes its work from previous explanations for the generalization gap (e.g., overfitting, saddle points), and the numerical evidence is compelling across multiple network architectures and experimental setups. The experimental design is thorough, with multiple trials to ensure robustness, and the paper thoughtfully explores potential remedies for the generalization gap, providing a comprehensive view of the problem.

To further improve the paper, the authors should clarify the rationale for their specific batch size choices (10% for large-batch and 256 for small-batch). While the paper mentions these values are typical in practice, a more detailed explanation of why these particular values were selected would strengthen the methodology. Additionally, a more thorough discussion of the limitations of the proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4) would enhance the paper, as this metric is not directly based on Hessian eigenvalues, which are the standard measure of sharpness in optimization literature.

The paper could also benefit from a more detailed exploration of why small-batch methods find flatter minima. While the paper notes that gradient noise may be the cause, additional evidence establishing a causal link would strengthen this claim. For instance, experiments measuring noise levels in small-batch versus large-batch methods and correlating these with the sharpness of found minimizers would provide stronger support.

The following questions would help clarify the paper:
1. The paper claims the generalization gap is not due to overfitting, but the evidence for this is limited. Could the authors provide more detailed analysis of training and testing curves to better distinguish between the generalization gap and overfitting?
2. The sharpness metric (Equation 4) approximates the largest eigenvalue of the Hessian. How sensitive are the key results to the choice of this metric? Would the findings hold with a more direct Hessian-based sharpness measure?
3. The warm-starting experiments show that large-batch methods benefit from small-batch initialization. What is the minimum number of small-batch iterations needed for effective warm-starting, and how does this scale with network complexity?
