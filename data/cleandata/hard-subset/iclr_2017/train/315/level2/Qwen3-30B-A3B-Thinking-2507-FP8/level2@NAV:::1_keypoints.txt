- Paper studies why large-batch training generalizes worse than small-batch training despite similar training accuracy.  
- Main claim: large-batch methods converge to sharp minimizers (large positive Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (many small eigenvalues), driving the generalization gap.  
- Provides strong empirical evidence across six network architectures using parametric plots and a sharpness metric (Eq. 4), showing a 1–2 order-of-magnitude sharpness difference.  
- Recommendation: accept with minor revisions.  
- Strengths: rigorous, systematic numerical experiments with multiple random seeds and consistent results across configurations; thoughtful discussion of limitations and remedies.  
- Revision request 1: add a brief theoretical discussion explaining why the sharpness metric (Eq. 4) correlates with Hessian eigenvalues.  
- Revision request 2: include convergence speed comparisons between large-batch and small-batch methods to address training-time trade-offs.  
- Revision request 3: clarify the exact relationship between the sharpness metric and the Hessian spectrum in the main text.  
- Question 1 for authors: provide more details on how Eq. 4 correlates with actual Hessian eigenvalues in experiments, especially for the largest networks.  
- Question 2 for authors: relate the findings to recent theoretical work on deep learning loss landscapes (e.g., Choromanska et al., 2015; Soudry & Carmon, 2016).
