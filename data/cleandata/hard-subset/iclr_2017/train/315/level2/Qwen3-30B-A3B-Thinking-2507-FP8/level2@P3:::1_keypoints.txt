- Paper studies the generalization gap in large-batch training, presenting evidence that large-batch methods converge to sharp minima while small-batch methods find flatter minima that generalize better.
- Provides strong experimental evidence (including parametric plots) and proposes a sharpness metric to quantify differences between small-batch and large-batch solutions.
- Recommendation: accept — well-motivated and thoroughly supported explanation for an important empirical phenomenon.
- The proposed sharpness metric and experiments give a clear, quantifiable, and reproducible framework for measuring the effect.
- Good placement in the literature, connecting to prior work on sharp/flat minima (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber; Chaudhari et al.) and distinguishing from other explanations (overfitting, saddle points).
- Experimental design is thorough, with multiple trials, architectures, and exploration of potential remedies for the generalization gap.
- Ask for clarification of the rationale behind specific batch-size choices (10% for large-batch, 256 for small-batch) beyond being “typical.”
- Recommend a more thorough discussion of limitations of the sharpness metric (Eq. 4), since it is not directly based on Hessian eigenvalues.
- Suggest further evidence to establish causality that gradient noise causes small-batch methods to find flatter minima (e.g., measure noise levels and correlate with sharpness).
- Question 1: Provide more detailed training and testing curve analyses to better distinguish the generalization gap from overfitting.
- Question 2: How sensitive are results to the choice of sharpness metric; would findings hold with a Hessian-based sharpness measure?
- Question 3: For warm-starting, what is the minimum number of small-batch iterations needed for effectiveness and how does this scale with network complexity?
