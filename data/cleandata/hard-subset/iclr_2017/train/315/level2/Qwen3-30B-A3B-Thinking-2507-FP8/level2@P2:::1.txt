

**Review**

This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon that large-batch SGD methods in deep learning often exhibit poorer generalization compared to small-batch methods. The authors claim that this generalization gap is due to large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers (characterized by large positive eigenvalues in the Hessian), while small-batch methods converge to flat minimizers (with small eigenvalues), and that sharp minimizers generalize worse. They support this claim with parametric plots and a proposed sharpness metric (Equation 4), and explore strategies to address the problem.

I recommend rejection for the following key reasons: (1) The paper fails to establish a causal relationship between sharpness and generalization performance; the evidence presented is correlational, not causal. (2) The proposed sharpness metric lacks theoretical justification and its connection to the actual Hessian spectrum is not rigorously established.

The paper's main contribution is the numerical evidence suggesting a correlation between large batch sizes, sharp minima, and poor generalization. While the parametric plots (Figure 3) visually support the claim that LB minimizers appear sharper, the paper does not demonstrate that this sharpness *causes* the generalization drop. The authors assume this connection based on prior work (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), but provide no direct evidence linking their sharpness metric to generalization performance. For instance, they don't show that for a fixed network, decreasing sharpness (through their metric) consistently improves generalization.

The sharpness metric (Equation 4) is particularly problematic. The authors acknowledge it's "imperfect" but fail to justify its use or demonstrate its robustness. They don't explore how sensitive their results are to the choice of ε (Equation 4), which controls the neighborhood size. More critically, they don't establish how their metric relates to the actual Hessian spectrum, despite claiming it "relates to the largest eigenvalue of ∇²f(x)". The connection between the metric and the theoretical definition of sharp minima is vague and unproven.

The paper also fails to adequately address why small-batch methods find flatter minima. The authors mention "inherent noise in gradient estimation" but don't provide evidence that this noise specifically enables escaping sharp minima. Their warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) suggests that SB methods find flat minima that LB methods can then converge to, but this doesn't explain why LB methods themselves can't find these minima.

The paper could have strengthened its contribution by comparing with related work. For instance, Chaudhari et al. (2016) introduced "Entropy-SGD" which explicitly aims to find wider minima, and their work directly relates to the paper's claims. The paper should have discussed how their sharpness metric relates to this prior work, rather than treating it as a novel concept.

Additionally, the paper uses ADAM for both small and large batch methods, which is problematic. ADAM is a first-order method that doesn't have the same noise properties as SGD, so the comparison between SB and LB methods is confounded by the optimizer choice. The authors should have used SGD for both cases to properly isolate the batch size effect.

The paper's claim that "the generalization gap is not due to overfitting" is not sufficiently supported. The authors note that the training-testing curves don't show the typical overfitting pattern (peaking then decaying), but this doesn't rule out the possibility that LB methods are overfitting to a different part of the loss landscape. They provide no evidence that the LB minima have higher test error due to sharpness rather than overfitting.

**Additional feedback:**
- The paper would benefit from a more rigorous theoretical justification for the sharpness metric, including analysis of how it relates to the Hessian spectrum and generalization performance.
- The authors should compare their results with Chaudhari et al. (2016) and other work on flat vs. sharp minima to better situate their contribution.
- The paper should use SGD (not ADAM) for both small and large batch experiments to isolate the batch size effect.
- The paper should provide more evidence that the sharpness metric is causally related to generalization, not just correlated.

**Questions for authors:**
1. How does your sharpness metric (Equation 4) relate to the actual eigenvalues of the Hessian? Can you provide a theoretical justification for why this metric would correlate with generalization performance?
2. Why did you choose to use ADAM for both small and large batch experiments? How would the results change if you used SGD for both cases?
3. Can you provide evidence that the sharp minima identified by your metric are actually worse for generalization, beyond the correlation observed in your experiments? For example, could you show that for a fixed network, reducing sharpness (through your metric) consistently improves generalization?

The paper addresses an important practical problem in deep learning, but the evidence provided does not sufficiently support the causal claim that sharp minima cause the generalization gap. Without establishing this causal link, the paper's main contribution is not substantiated.
