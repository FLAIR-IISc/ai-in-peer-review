- Problem: Large-batch training often generalizes worse than small-batch training despite similar training loss.
- Core claim: The generalization gap arises because large-batch methods converge to sharper minima (large Hessian eigenvalues) while small-batch methods find flatter minima.
- Contribution: Introduces a novel sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) as a computationally feasible proxy for curvature with MDL/Bayesian grounding.
- Evidence: Numerical results across six architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants) show consistent sharpness differences between batch regimes.
- Warm-starting: Figure 5 shows small-batch exploration enables subsequent large-batch methods to reach flatter minima.
- Recommendation: Reviewer recommends weak acceptance based on strong empirical support.
- Strength: Rigorous experimental design — multiple architectures, repeated trials, and careful separation from overfitting (Figure 2).
- Strength: Warm-starting experiment is particularly insightful about the role of exploration.
- Strength: Authors thoughtfully evaluate potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training) though they only partially mitigate the issue.
- Limitation: Mechanism of noise is unclear — paper doesn't explain how small-batch noise pushes iterates out of sharp basins (gradient variance vs. Hessian stochasticity).
- Limitation: Sharpness metric validity is not fully established; need stronger evidence correlating the metric with actual Hessian spectra.
- Limitation: Insufficient comparison to concurrent work (e.g., Smith et al. 2017) and how their perturbation-based sharpness relates to this metric.
- Suggestion: Report standard deviations for sharpness values in Table 3 to show consistency across trials.
- Suggestion: Clarify why ADAM was used for both batch regimes instead of SGD, and how that choice affects noise interpretation.
- Suggestion: Temper scalability/performance claims in Appendix C by noting communication overhead limits parallel efficiency in practice.
- Question: What is the minimum number of small-batch epochs required in warm-starting across different networks for large-batch success?
- Question: How is the dimensionality of the sharp subspace quantified (e.g., number of large eigenvalues) to support the "small subspace" claim?
- Question: If identical minimizers are reached via small- or large-batch training, does the sharpness metric differ — i.e., can batch size affect sharpness for the same minimizer?
