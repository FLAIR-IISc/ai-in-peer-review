- Paper studies why large-batch (LB) training generalizes worse than small-batch (SB), attributing it to LB converging to sharp minima and SB to flat minima.

- Evidence is correlational and the paper fails to establish a causal link between sharpness and worse generalization.

- The proposed sharpness metric (Eq. 4) lacks theoretical justification and its relation to the Hessian spectrum is not rigorously shown.

- The authors do not analyze sensitivity of the sharpness metric to the neighborhood size ε or other hyperparameters.

- No experiments demonstrate that decreasing the proposed sharpness metric for a fixed network consistently improves test performance.

- The paper does not adequately explain why SB methods find flatter minima — the role of gradient-noise in escaping sharp minima is asserted but not evidenced.

- Warm-start experiments show SB can find flat minima LB later reaches, but do not explain why LB cannot find those minima on its own.

- The work omits comparison to related methods (e.g., Chaudhari et al. 2016 Entropy-SGD) and fails to situate the sharpness metric relative to prior techniques for finding wide minima.

- Using ADAM for both SB and LB confounds the batch-size effect because ADAM has different noise properties than SGD; SGD should be used to isolate batch-size impact.

- The claim that the generalization gap is not due to overfitting is not convincingly supported; alternate overfitting explanations are not ruled out.

- The paper would benefit from theoretical analysis linking the sharpness metric to Hessian eigenvalues and to generalization bounds.

- Experimental fixes needed: use SGD for both batch regimes, test robustness to ε, and provide causal interventions showing sharpness reduction improves generalization.

- Key questions for authors: how does Eq.4 relate to Hessian eigenvalues and why expect it to predict generalization; why choose ADAM; can you causally show lowering sharpness improves test error?

- Recommendation: reject — main contribution is numerical correlation without sufficient causal evidence, theoretical grounding, or proper experimental controls.
