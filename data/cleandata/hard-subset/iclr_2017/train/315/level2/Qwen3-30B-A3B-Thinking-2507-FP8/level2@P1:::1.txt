

This paper addresses a critical empirical observation in deep learning: large-batch training methods often exhibit worse generalization than small-batch methods despite achieving similar training loss. The authors propose that this "generalization gap" stems from large-batch methods converging to sharp minimizers (characterized by large Hessian eigenvalues), while small-batch methods find flatter minima. They support this with numerical evidence using a novel sharpness metric (Metric 2.1) across six architectures (including AlexNet and VGG variants), showing consistent sharpness differences between batch regimes. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) further demonstrates that small-batch exploration enables large-batch methods to reach flatter minima.

I recommend **weak acceptance**. The core claim—that large-batch methods converge to sharper minima—is strongly supported by compelling numerical evidence across diverse networks. The sharpness metric provides a computationally feasible way to quantify a concept with theoretical grounding in MDL and Bayesian learning. The paper effectively contextualizes the problem within existing literature and offers actionable insights for practitioners.

The primary strength lies in the rigorous experimental design: multiple architectures, repeated trials, and careful distinction from overfitting (Figure 2). The warm-starting experiment (Section 3) is particularly insightful, revealing how small-batch exploration enables large-batch success. The authors also thoughtfully address potential remedies (data augmentation, conservative training), though these only partially mitigate the issue (Table 6, Figure 7), reinforcing the central hypothesis.

Key limitations to address for revision:
1. **Mechanism of noise**: The paper states small-batch noise "pushes iterates out of sharp basins" but doesn't clarify *how* (e.g., is it due to gradient variance, or stochasticity in Hessian estimation?). A brief theoretical discussion of the noise-minimizer interaction would strengthen the motivation (e.g., comparing to recent work on noise-induced saddle escape).
2. **Sharpness metric validity**: The metric (4) approximates Hessian eigenvalues but isn't proven equivalent. The authors note it correlates with the largest eigenvalue when A=Iₙ, but the claim that "sharp minimizers rise steeply only along a small subspace" (Section 2.2.2) needs stronger evidence. A small-scale experiment showing the metric correlates with actual Hessian spectra (e.g., for a 10D toy model) would be valuable.
3. **Comparison to concurrent work**: The paper cites Smith et al. (2017) but doesn't discuss how their "sharpness" measure (based on loss change after perturbations) relates to the authors' metric. Clarifying this connection would position the work more precisely in the literature.

Additional constructive suggestions:
- In Table 3, report standard deviations for sharpness values to indicate consistency across trials.
- Clarify why ADAM was used for both batch regimes (Section 2.2) when SGD is more commonly associated with small-batch training—does this affect the noise interpretation?
- The performance model in Appendix C is compelling but overstates scalability; note that communication overhead (not just batch size) limits parallel efficiency in practice (e.g., Das et al. 2016).

Questions for authors:
1. The warm-starting experiment (Figure 5) shows accuracy improves after ~20 epochs of SB exploration. What is the *minimum* number of SB epochs needed for LB success across different networks? Is this related to the "exploration phase" duration?
2. In Section 2.2.2, the authors state LB minima "do not resemble a cone." How do they quantify the dimensionality of the sharp subspace (e.g., via the number of large eigenvalues)? This would directly support the "small subspace" claim.
3. How does the sharpness metric behave for *identical* minimizers trained with different batch sizes? If a single minimizer could be reached via SB or LB, would its sharpness differ? This would isolate batch size effects from minimizer location.
