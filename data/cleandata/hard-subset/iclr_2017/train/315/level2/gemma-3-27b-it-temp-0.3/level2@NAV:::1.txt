## Review of "The Generalization Gap in Large-Batch Training"

This paper investigates the well-known phenomenon of reduced generalization performance when using large batch sizes in stochastic gradient descent (SGD) for deep learning. The authors posit that large batches converge to *sharp* minimizers of the loss function, which are known to generalize poorly, while small batches converge to *flat* minimizers due to inherent noise. They support this claim with numerical experiments across several network configurations and datasets, demonstrating a correlation between batch size, sharpness of the minima found, and generalization gap.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper in its current form. While the observed correlation between sharpness and generalization is intuitively plausible and aligns with existing theoretical understanding, the paper lacks sufficient rigor and novelty to warrant acceptance at a competitive AI conference. The core argument – that large batches lead to sharp minima – is largely descriptive and doesn’t offer substantial new insight beyond what is already known in the field. 

The supporting evidence, while presented clearly, is primarily empirical. The authors acknowledge the limitations of their sharpness metric and don’t provide a strong theoretical justification for *why* large batches gravitate towards sharp minima. The experiments, while comprehensive in terms of network architectures, don’t explore a wide range of hyperparameters or regularization techniques that could potentially mitigate the observed generalization gap. Furthermore, the attempted remedies (data augmentation, conservative training, robust optimization) are presented briefly and show limited success, lacking a detailed analysis of *why* they fail to fully address the problem.

However, the work is not without merit. The clear presentation of the problem and the empirical evidence could be valuable to practitioners. To improve the paper, the authors should: 1) Provide a more rigorous theoretical analysis linking batch size to the geometry of the loss landscape. 2) Conduct a more thorough hyperparameter search and explore more advanced regularization techniques. 3) Offer a more in-depth discussion of why the attempted remedies fail and suggest potential avenues for future research. 

Specifically, I would like to understand: What is the role of the learning rate schedule in this phenomenon? Does a carefully tuned learning rate schedule mitigate the generalization gap for large batches? Also, the authors mention a performance model in Appendix C – could they elaborate on the assumptions and limitations of this model? Finally, what specific characteristics of the datasets used contribute to the observed behavior? Would the results hold for more complex or high-dimensional datasets?
