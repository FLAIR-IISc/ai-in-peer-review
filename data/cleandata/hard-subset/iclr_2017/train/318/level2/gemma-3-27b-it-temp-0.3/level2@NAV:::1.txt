## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation and allows for graph manipulation. The core idea of learning to construct and modify graphs based on input, and then using those graphs for downstream tasks, is compelling and well-motivated. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex rules and representations.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. The approach is novel and the results, particularly the performance on the bAbI tasks and the successful modeling of Turing machines, are promising. The ability to learn graphical representations from textual input and utilize them for reasoning is a significant contribution.

The paper clearly articulates the problem of representing state and data structures using graphs and builds upon existing work in graph neural networks. The introduction of differentiable graph transformations is a key innovation, enabling end-to-end learning. The experimental results provide strong evidence that the GGT-NN can effectively learn and manipulate these graphical representations. The performance on the bAbI tasks is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the successful application to the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks demonstrates the model’s ability to generalize to more complex problems. 

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification and elaboration. The description of the graph transformations, while present, could be more intuitive with illustrative examples beyond those in the appendix. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the model, particularly as it scales with graph size, would be valuable. Additionally, while the authors acknowledge limitations, exploring the potential for sparsity in the graph connections to improve efficiency would be beneficial. 

I have a few questions for the authors: 
1. How sensitive is the model to the initial graph structure (if any) provided as input?
2. Could you elaborate on the choice of activation functions and network architectures within the graph transformations? Were other options explored?
3. What is the impact of the number of propagation steps (T) on performance and computational cost? Is there a clear optimal value?
