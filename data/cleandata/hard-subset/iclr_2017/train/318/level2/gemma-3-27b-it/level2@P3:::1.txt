This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that utilizes graph-structured data as an intermediate representation and allows for the construction and modification of graphs based on textual input. The model demonstrates success on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex rules and represent state transitions effectively.

Reject. While the work presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures, the paper lacks sufficient novelty to warrant acceptance. The core idea of combining graph transformations with recurrent models is intuitively appealing, but the presented results do not demonstrably outperform existing state-of-the-art approaches to a degree that justifies the complexity of the proposed model. Furthermore, the experiments lack a thorough comparative analysis, particularly regarding computational cost and scalability.

The primary concern is the limited demonstrable advantage over existing methods, especially considering the increased complexity of the GGT-NN. While the paper claims to achieve competitive results on the bAbI tasks, it largely reiterates performance levels already attained by models like MemNN and DMN+, and doesn't clearly establish a compelling benefit. The improvements on tasks 5, 8, 16, and 19, while noted, are not substantial enough to justify the increased architectural complexity. A more rigorous comparison, including statistical significance testing, is needed to validate these observations. The performance on task 17 is particularly concerning, suggesting a limitation in the model’s capacity to handle more complex relational reasoning.

The experiments on rule discovery (cellular automaton and Turing machines) are interesting but lack detailed comparison to alternative methods specifically designed for these tasks. Is the GGT-NN’s performance state-of-the-art on these problems? Are there simpler models that achieve comparable results with less computational overhead? Moreover, the scalability of the approach should be discussed. The quadratic scaling mentioned in the conclusion raises concerns about its applicability to larger, more realistic datasets. 

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Novelty:** More clearly articulate the novelty of the GGT-NN compared to existing graph neural networks and memory networks. How does the differentiable graph transformation component uniquely contribute to the state-of-the-art?
*   **Comparative Analysis:** Provide a more thorough comparison against relevant baselines, including statistical significance testing. Include a discussion of computational cost (training time, memory usage) for the GGT-NN and baseline models.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components of the GGT-NN (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation).
*   **Scalability Discussion:** Address the scalability concerns raised by the quadratic computational complexity. Explore potential optimizations to mitigate this issue.
*   **Task 17 Analysis:** Provide a deeper analysis of the GGT-NN’s performance on task 17. What specific aspects of this task pose challenges for the model?
*   **Clarity of Appendices:** While comprehensive, the appendices could benefit from a more concise presentation. Focus on essential details and avoid excessive repetition.
*   **Algorithm 1 Detail:** It isn’t immediately clear what `i(k)` and `D(k)` represent in Algorithm 1 without referring back to Section 4. A brief explanation within the algorithm would improve readability.
*   **Extended Model Justification:** Explain the rationale for the slightly inferior performance of the extended model using the sequence of graphs, and if there are potential approaches to address it.
