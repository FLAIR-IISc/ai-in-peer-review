This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation, allowing the model to both construct and modify graphs based on textual input and utilize these graphs for various output tasks. The authors demonstrate the model’s capabilities on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, achieving impressive results, particularly on tasks well-suited for graphical representation.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures and demonstrates strong performance on specific tasks, the novelty appears incremental and the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and comparative analysis to justify acceptance at a top-tier conference like ICLR. The experimental results, while promising, are not convincingly contextualized within the broader landscape of sequence modeling and reasoning.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited discussion of how the proposed GGT-NN compares to existing sequence-to-sequence models *without* explicitly enforcing a graph structure. The bAbI tasks, in particular, have been extensively studied with LSTMs and Transformers. The paper needs to convincingly demonstrate why encoding the information as a graph provides a significant advantage – beyond simply stating that some tasks are “naturally suited” to a graphical representation. A more thorough ablation study, comparing performance with and without the graph-structured intermediate representation, is crucial. The claim of state-of-the-art performance (or competitive performance) needs to be more rigorously established and placed in context with other approaches.

Secondly, the description of the graph transformations, while detailed in the appendix, is somewhat buried within the paper and could benefit from clearer visual explanations and a more intuitive narrative. The connection between the transformations and the underlying GRU mechanism isn’t always clear. The paper states that the choice of a GRU layer for *fadd* was effective, but provides no justification for this choice relative to other options. Additionally, the benefits of “direct reference” aren't fully elucidated—why does explicitly referencing node types improve performance so substantially?

Furthermore, the rule discovery tasks, while interesting, lack a strong comparative baseline. How does the GGT-NN’s ability to learn these rules compare to, say, a standard LSTM trained on the same sequential data? Without such comparisons, it's difficult to assess the true value of the proposed approach. The claim of being able to generalize to longer sequences is also stated without much supporting evidence, merely stating that the model performed well.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity:** The paper's organization could be improved. The jump between the background, the introduction of graph transformations, and the GGT-NN model feels somewhat disjointed.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies by removing certain transformations (e.g., edge updates) or disabling direct reference would help identify the contributions of each component.
*   **Visualization:** Providing visualizations of the learned graphs for the bAbI tasks and the cellular automaton would greatly enhance understanding.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning:** More details about the hyperparameter tuning process would be helpful, especially regarding the choice of learning rate, batch size, and the number of propagation steps.
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN, particularly concerning the quadratic scaling with graph size, would be valuable.
*   **Error Analysis:** An analysis of the types of errors made by the GGT-NN, especially on task 17, could provide insights for future improvements.
*   **Appendix:** The appendix is lengthy and contains crucial implementation details. Consider integrating more of this information into the main body of the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational overhead of using a graph-structured intermediate representation compared to a standard sequence-to-sequence model on the bAbI tasks?
2.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of the functions *f fwd y* and *f bwd y* within the propagation step? Were alternative implementations explored?
3.  Can you provide examples of the graphs learned by the GGT-NN for specific bAbI tasks, highlighting how the graph structure captures the relationships between entities and events?
4.  How does the performance of the extended model (Algorithm 2) compare to the original model when using the same amount of training data for the bAbI tasks?
5.  Why were GRU units specifically chosen for the node state updates and the *fadd* transformation, rather than other recurrent network architectures?
