## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The core idea – enabling the model to construct and modify graphs based on input and utilize these graphs for downstream tasks – is a promising approach to tackling problems involving relational data and state representation. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing its ability to learn both structural representations and transformation rules.

I recommend **rejecting** the paper in its current form, but with encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the core concept is interesting and the results on the bAbI tasks and rule discovery are promising, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and rigor to fully convince of the work’s significance and generalizability.

My primary concern is the lack of a clear comparative baseline. While the authors compare to several existing approaches on the bAbI tasks, many are significantly older or employ drastically different supervision strategies (strong vs. end-to-end). A more direct comparison against recent graph neural network architectures capable of dynamic graph construction would strengthen the contribution. Secondly, the ablation study – comparing models *with* and *without* direct reference – is somewhat superficial. The benefits of direct reference are stated, but the mechanism and impact aren’t thoroughly explored. Finally, the experimental setup, particularly for the Turing machine task, feels underexplained. What specifically constitutes a successful “learning” of the Turing machine rules? 

Several points could improve the paper. The authors should clarify the advantages of the GGT-NN’s graph transformations over existing techniques.  A more detailed explanation of the hyperparameter settings, training procedures, and computational resources used would increase reproducibility.  The implementation details in Appendix B are valuable but could be streamlined for better readability.  A discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the approach would be helpful, considering the quadratic scaling mentioned.

I would be interested to know: What are the limitations of the current graph transformation set? Are there specific types of relationships or graph structures that the GGT-NN struggles to represent? Could the model benefit from attention mechanisms *within* the graph transformations themselves? Finally, is there a detailed analysis of the learned graph structures – what patterns or features emerge that reveal insights into the model’s reasoning process?
