### **Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture for learning from sequential data by building and modifying an explicit, graph-structured internal state. The core contribution is a set of five differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update, edge update, information propagation, and graph-level aggregation. These transformations serve as modular building blocks that can be composed to create models that incrementally construct a graph from unstructured text and then reason over that graph to produce an answer.

The author demonstrates the model's capabilities on two distinct sets of tasks. First, on the 20 bAbI question-answering tasks, the GGT-NN achieves excellent performance, solving 19 of the 20 tasks with high accuracy. This shows its ability to parse natural language, update a knowledge graph, and perform reasoning. Second, the model is applied to rule discovery tasks, successfully learning the transition rules for a 1D cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines. This demonstrates the generality of the framework beyond natural language processing, highlighting its capacity for learning complex, algorithmic state transitions.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that introduces a novel and significant contribution to the field. The core idea of a differentiable, dynamic graph memory is compelling, and the proposed framework of composable transformations is elegant and powerful. The decision to accept is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novelty and Conceptual Strength:** The paper proposes a new class of models that bridges the gap between neural networks that operate on fixed graph structures (like GNNs) and those with unstructured, implicit memory (like DNCs). The GGT-NN's ability to dynamically build and modify an explicit, interpretable graph from unstructured input is a significant step forward. This explicit representation could be invaluable for tasks requiring interpretability, debuggability, or integration with symbolic systems.

2.  **Impressive Empirical Validation:** The model is thoroughly evaluated on a diverse and challenging set of benchmarks. It achieves near state-of-the-art results on the bAbI dataset, demonstrating its effectiveness for language understanding and reasoning. The experiments on the cellular automaton and Turing machines are particularly compelling, as they show that the model can learn fundamental, general-purpose algorithms, supporting the claim that the graph transformations are a powerful tool for modeling dynamic systems.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is well-written, clearly motivated, and positions its contributions effectively within the existing literature. The comparison with related work in Section 6 is particularly insightful, clearly articulating the advantages of an explicit graphical state (e.g., distributed computation for tasks like pathfinding) and acknowledging its disadvantages (e.g., quadratic complexity).

The central technical contribution—the set of differentiable graph transformations—is well-defined and intuitive. By breaking down the complex process of graph manipulation into modular operations, the author provides a flexible framework that could be adapted to many other problems. The use of soft, continuous representations for node existence (`s_v`) and edge connectivity (`C`) is a key element that enables end-to-end differentiability.

The experimental results are strong and provide convincing evidence for the model's capabilities. The high performance on bAbI task 19 (pathfinding) and task 16 (induction) nicely illustrates the benefits of a graphical representation for specific types of reasoning. The rule discovery experiments are a standout feature, demonstrating that the GGT-NN is not just a QA model but a more general framework for learning to manipulate structured data.

While the model's reliance on strong, step-by-step graph supervision is a significant limitation for its direct application to new problems, the author is transparent about this. Within the context of a research paper introducing a new architecture, this form of "teacher forcing" is an acceptable method for validating the model's capacity to learn the desired transformations. The paper correctly identifies the reduction of this supervision requirement as a critical direction for future work.

#### **4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Author**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   **Complexity Analysis:** The paper mentions the quadratic time and space complexity as a downside. It would strengthen the paper to provide some empirical numbers to contextualize this. For example, what were the maximum number of nodes and the approximate training times for the bAbI and rule discovery tasks? This would give readers a more practical sense of the model's scalability.
*   **Ablation Study:** The comparison of models with and without "direct reference" is a useful ablation. To further justify the complexity of the proposed framework, it might be beneficial to include a brief ablation study on the graph transformations themselves. For instance, are all five transformations essential for high performance on bAbI, or could a simpler model (e.g., one without dynamic edge updates) solve some of the tasks?
*   **Error Analysis:** The model fails to solve bAbI task 17. The paper hypothesizes this is due to the larger number of entities and shorter stories. A more detailed error analysis could provide valuable insights. By inspecting the graphs the model generates for this task, can you identify the primary failure mode? Is the model failing to create the correct nodes, failing to add the right edges, or failing at the final reasoning step?

**Clarifying Questions:**

1.  Regarding the supervision mechanism (Section 4.1), the paper states that the correct graph values (`C*`, `s*`, `x*`) are substituted into the graph for further processing. Does this mean that at training time, the model's own predicted graph structure is entirely discarded at each step, with only the node hidden states (`h_v`) being propagated based on the ground-truth graph? Could you clarify how gradients flow back to the transformation parameters that produced the (discarded) predicted graph?
2.  The node addition transformation (`T_add`) uses a GRU to recurrently generate new nodes. How is the maximum number of nodes that can be added in a single step determined? Is this a fixed hyperparameter, and if so, how was it chosen and how sensitive is the model to this choice?
3.  The generalization results on the rule discovery tasks are very promising. The model generalizes well to longer simulation times. Have you investigated whether it also generalizes to larger spatial configurations (e.g., a cellular automaton with a wider initial state than seen during training)?
