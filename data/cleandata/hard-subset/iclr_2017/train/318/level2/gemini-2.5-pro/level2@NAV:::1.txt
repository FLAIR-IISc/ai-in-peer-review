**Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture for learning dynamic, graph-structured state representations from unstructured input. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, edge/state updates, etc.) that allow the model to incrementally construct and modify an internal graph. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on two distinct fronts: first, by achieving state-of-the-art results on 19 of the 20 bAbI question-answering tasks, showcasing its ability to parse text into a relational structure for reasoning; and second, by successfully learning the underlying dynamics of a cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines, proving it can discover complex, rule-based state transitions.

**Decision: Accept**
I recommend accepting this paper. The proposed GGT-NN model is a significant and novel contribution that elegantly bridges the gap between neural networks that operate on fixed graphs and memory-augmented networks with implicit internal states. The claims are well-supported by strong and diverse empirical results that convincingly demonstrate the model's effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments**
The paper's primary strength lies in its novel formulation of differentiable graph transformations. This allows for an explicit, interpretable, and dynamically changing graphical state, which is a powerful concept with broad applicability. Unlike prior GNN work that assumes a fixed graph, or models like DNCs where the structure is implicit, the GGT-NN learns to build the graph itself.

The experimental validation is thorough. The performance on the bAbI tasks is impressive, particularly on tasks naturally suited to graph representations like pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16). Furthermore, the rule discovery experiments provide compelling evidence that the model can learn fundamental algorithmic procedures by manipulating its graph state, moving beyond simple question-answering. The paper is transparent about its reliance on strong supervision (providing ground-truth graphs during training), which is an important limitation to note but does not detract from the core contribution.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**
While the results are strong, the paper would be improved by further discussion on two key points. First, the reliance on strong supervision is the most significant limitation. While acknowledged as future work, a brief discussion on potential avenues to relax this requirement—perhaps using semi-supervised methods or reinforcement learning with only the final answer as a reward—would strengthen the paper. Second, the quadratic complexity of the edge update transformation could be a scalability bottleneck. Mentioning potential solutions, such as sparse or attention-based edge modifications, would add valuable perspective.

**Questions for the Authors**
1.  How robust is the model to imperfections in the supervision graphs? For instance, if some edges or nodes were missing from the supervision signal during training, how gracefully would performance degrade?
2.  The "direct reference" mechanism relies on word matching. Have you considered learning this reference mechanism, for example, through an attention model that maps input words to node types?
3.  Could you elaborate on the design of the five transformations? Was this set empirically found to be sufficient, or did you experiment with a larger/smaller set of primitive operations?
