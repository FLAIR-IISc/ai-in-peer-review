### Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that uses a graph as an explicit, differentiable intermediate state. The core contribution is a set of modular, differentiable graph transformations that allow the model to dynamically add nodes, update node states, and modify edges based on sequential, unstructured input like text. This extends prior work on Graph Neural Networks (which operate on fixed graphs) by allowing the graph structure itself to be learned and manipulated. The paper demonstrates the GGT-NN's capabilities on two distinct sets of tasks. First, it shows that the model can learn to solve 19 out of 20 of the bAbI question-answering tasks by constructing and querying an internal graphical representation of the story. Second, it demonstrates that the model can discover the underlying dynamics of complex systems by learning the transition rules for a 1D cellular automaton and for 4-state Turing machines, directly from observing their graphical state evolution.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This paper presents a significant and novel contribution by introducing a general framework for learning to manipulate dynamic, graph-structured representations. The two key reasons for my recommendation are:

1.  **Conceptual Novelty and Importance:** The idea of a differentiable, dynamic graph as an internal memory is a powerful concept that elegantly bridges the gap between models for static graphs (like GNNs) and models with unstructured memory (like DNCs or Memory Networks). It opens up a promising new direction for building models that can perform explicit, interpretable, relational reasoning.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling evidence that the proposed framework is effective. The strong performance on the bAbI suite, particularly on tasks well-suited to graphs like pathfinding and induction, validates the approach for language understanding. Furthermore, the success on the rule-discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) is a powerful demonstration of the model's ability to learn complex, algorithmic transformations of its internal state.

**3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its novel formulation of a dynamic, differentiable graphical memory. The proposed set of transformations (`T_add`, `T_h`, `T_C`, `T_prop`, `T_repr`) provides a flexible and powerful toolkit for building models that reason over explicit relational structures. This explicit representation is a major advantage over concurrent memory-augmented architectures, as it offers a degree of interpretability that is often missing. The visualizations of the learned cellular automaton rules, for example, provide a clear window into the model's learned behavior.

The experimental results are thorough and largely support the paper's claims. The model achieves state-of-the-art or competitive performance on the majority of bAbI tasks. The analysis highlighting its superior performance on tasks 16 (induction) and 19 (pathfinding) provides strong evidence that the graphical representation is indeed beneficial for problems with inherent relational structure. The rule-discovery experiments are particularly impressive, as they show the model can learn local, parallelizable update rules that define a complex dynamical system, a challenging task for standard sequential models.

However, the paper has two significant weaknesses that temper its contributions, though they are not fatal to its acceptance.

First, the model relies on **strong supervision** for the bAbI tasks, requiring the ground-truth graph at every step of the story during training. The authors are commendably transparent about this. However, it means the model is learning a supervised mapping from (text, previous_correct_graph) to next_correct_graph, rather than learning to form a latent graphical representation from text alone. This limits the practical applicability of the current method to domains where such detailed supervision is available, which are rare. While a crucial proof-of-concept, the claim of learning to "construct and modify graphs... based on textual input" should be interpreted with this heavy reliance on supervision in mind.

Second, the model's **computational complexity** is quadratic in the number of nodes due to the dense edge matrix representation and the edge update step. The authors acknowledge this as a limitation. This makes it difficult to scale the model to problems involving larger graphs, which is a key direction for future work in this area.

Despite these weaknesses, the novelty of the core idea and the strength of the proof-of-concept experiments make this a valuable contribution to the field. The paper clearly articulates its limitations and provides a solid foundation for future research on learning with dynamic, structured representations.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper.

*   **Supervision:** It would strengthen the paper to discuss potential pathways to reducing the strong supervision requirement. For instance, could the model be pre-trained with full supervision and then fine-tuned with only answer-level supervision? Or could techniques from reinforcement learning be used to reward plausible graph constructions that lead to correct final answers? Even a brief discussion of these possibilities would add value.
*   **Direct Reference Mechanism:** The "direct reference" mechanism, while effective, feels somewhat hard-coded (matching words to node type names). It would be interesting to see if this could be replaced with a more general, learned attention mechanism that allows the model to associate parts of the input text with specific nodes or node types based on their current states and annotations.
*   **Node Addition:** The node addition mechanism adds a fixed maximum number of nodes at each step, with the model learning to set the "strength" of unused nodes to zero. It would be interesting to hear the authors' thoughts on more dynamic mechanisms for controlling the graph's size.
*   **Ablation Studies:** While the comparison between direct reference and no direct reference is useful, further ablation studies could be beneficial. For instance, how crucial is each of the five transformation types to solving certain tasks? Understanding the contribution of each component would provide deeper insight into the model's workings.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:

*   Regarding the "soft" graph representation: during training and inference, do the node strengths (`s_v`) and edge strengths (`C`) tend to converge to binary {0, 1} values, or do they remain "fuzzy"? How does the model behave if these values are forced to be binary via a straight-through estimator or similar technique?
*   How was the maximum number of new nodes for the `T_add` transformation determined for each task? How sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter?
*   Have you considered experiments that test the model's robustness to partial supervision, for instance, by providing the ground-truth graph for only a subset of the sentences in a story during training? This could be an interesting first step towards weaning the model off the strong supervision requirement.
