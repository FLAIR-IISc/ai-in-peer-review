

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending GGS-NNs to allow graph-structured intermediate representations that can be constructed and modified based on textual input. The authors claim this model can learn to produce graph-based internal states from natural language, use these graphs to answer questions, and discover rules for graphical formulations of cellular automata and Turing machines. The key contributions include the novel graph transformation framework (node addition, state updates, edge modifications, propagation, and aggregation) and empirical demonstrations across multiple domains.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks by enabling models to dynamically construct and modify graph structures rather than working with fixed graph inputs. The empirical results are strong: the GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 bAbI tasks (with direct reference improving performance), and successfully learns the rules for Rule 30 cellular automata and 4-state Turing machines. The experiments are well-designed, with appropriate baselines and thorough analysis of results.

The paper effectively addresses the key question of how to extend neural networks to work with graph-structured internal states rather than just graph inputs or outputs. The authors place their work well within the literature, clearly differentiating GGT-NN from related approaches like Memory Networks and DNCs by emphasizing the explicit graph structure of the internal state. The model's ability to handle tasks naturally suited to graphical representations (like pathfinding in bAbI task 19) provides compelling evidence for the approach's value.

To improve the paper, I recommend:
1) Adding a more detailed discussion of when graph-structured intermediate representations are necessary versus when simpler approaches would suffice, to clarify the model's practical utility.
2) Including a more concrete analysis of the quadratic time/space complexity limitation, perhaps with specific examples of graph sizes where the model would become impractical.
3) Clarifying whether the graph transformations could be applied to other graph neural network architectures beyond the specific GGT-NN implementation.

The paper provides sufficient detail for reproducibility, and the authors appropriately acknowledge limitations regarding computational complexity and supervision requirements. The work represents a meaningful advancement in neural network architectures for structured data processing.
