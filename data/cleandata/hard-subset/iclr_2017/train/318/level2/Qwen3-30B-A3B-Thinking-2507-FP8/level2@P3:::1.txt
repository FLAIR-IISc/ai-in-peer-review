

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that uses graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The model learns to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, then uses these graphs to produce outputs. The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness on the bAbI tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks), a cellular automaton (Rule 30), and a family of Turing machines, showing it can learn complex update rules from unstructured input.

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by extending GGS-NNs to allow graph structures to be built and modified from unstructured input, rather than requiring fixed graph inputs. The empirical results are strong, particularly on the bAbI tasks where the model achieves state-of-the-art performance on several tasks, demonstrating the advantages of the graphical representation for certain reasoning problems.

The paper is well-motivated and properly placed in the literature. It clearly explains the limitations of existing graph-based approaches (which require fixed graph structures) and positions GGT-NN as a solution that can build and modify graphs based on textual input. The comparison with related work in Section 6 is thorough and highlights the key differences between GGT-NN and existing approaches, particularly regarding the explicit graph representation versus implicit memory structures.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The bAbI results include detailed analysis of performance with and without direct reference, and the model's success on tasks like pathfinding (Task 19) where the graphical representation is naturally suited is well-explained. The rule discovery tasks on cellular automata and Turing machines provide compelling evidence that the model can learn complex update rules from unstructured data. The ablation studies showing that direct reference significantly improves performance (95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks vs 9/20 without it) are particularly convincing.

Additional feedback to improve the paper:

1. The paper acknowledges the failure on Task 17 (Positional Reasoning) but could provide more detailed analysis of why the model struggles with this task. Given that Task 17 has more entities (20 unique entities) compared to other tasks, it would be valuable to understand whether this is due to the increased complexity of the entity space or the shorter stories. A more detailed analysis would strengthen the paper's claims about the model's capabilities.

2. The paper could provide more insight into how the graph structure is learned from textual input. While the direct reference mechanism is explained, the paper could include a concrete example showing how specific textual phrases translate into specific graph structures during training. This would help readers better understand the model's learning process.

3. The computational complexity (quadratic in graph size) is acknowledged as a limitation, but the paper could discuss potential approaches to address this. While the authors mention sparse connections as a future direction, a brief discussion of how this might be implemented (e.g., through attention mechanisms or graph sparsification) would be helpful for readers interested in practical applications.

4. The paper claims the model "successfully learns to solve almost all of the bAbI tasks" but doesn't provide a clear comparison of the required training data. It would be valuable to know how the training data requirements (e.g., 1,000 vs 10,000 examples) compare to other state-of-the-art approaches, particularly since the GGT-NN model uses strong supervision (full graph information) while other models are trained end-to-end.

Questions for clarification:

1. How does the model handle ambiguous references to node types in the input text? For example, if a sentence refers to "John" but multiple nodes of type "person" exist, how does the model determine which node to update?

2. In the cellular automaton task, how does the model handle the initial condition when the graph structure is being built from scratch? Specifically, how does the model learn to add the correct number of initial cells (13 in the training examples)?

3. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference performs better on most tasks, but the improvement on Task 19 (pathfinding) is particularly large (64% improvement over MemNN). What specific aspects of the direct reference mechanism contribute to this significant improvement, and how might this inform future work on similar tasks?
