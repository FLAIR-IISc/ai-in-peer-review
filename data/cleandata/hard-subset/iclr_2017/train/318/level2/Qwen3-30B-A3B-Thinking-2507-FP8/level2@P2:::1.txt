

**Review**

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that uses graph-structured intermediate representations. The authors claim the model can learn to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, and use these graphs to produce outputs, demonstrating success on bAbI tasks and rule discovery for cellular automata and Turing machines.

Decision: Reject

Key reasons: The paper fails to clearly articulate its novel contribution relative to prior work, particularly Li et al. (2016) on GGS-NNs. The claimed contributions are not properly substantiated with rigorous comparisons or analyses.

Supporting arguments:
1. The paper's core claim is that GGT-NN is an "extension" of GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations. However, the GGS-NN paper (Li et al., 2016) already handles graph-structured input, and the paper doesn't clearly explain how GGT-NN differs. The authors cite Li et al. (2016) as the basis for their work but don't properly distinguish their contribution. The paper states "GGS-NNs extend GG-NNs by performing a large number of propagation-output cycles," but doesn't clarify how GGT-NN extends this further.

2. The evaluation on bAbI tasks is problematic. The paper claims "the GGT-NN model was able to reach 95% accuracy in all but one of the tasks" without specifying whether this is with 1,000 or 10,000 training examples. The comparison with other methods is confusing - the paper states the GGT-NN was trained with only 1,000 examples, but then compares with results from 10,000 examples. It's unclear if the model's performance is comparable to other methods trained on the same number of examples.

3. The claim that the model "discovers the rules governing graphical formulations" for cellular automata and Turing machines is not properly substantiated. The paper shows high accuracy on these tasks, but doesn't demonstrate that the model has actually learned the underlying rules. For example, the model could be memorizing the specific examples rather than learning generalizable rules. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of the learned representations or the model's ability to generalize beyond the training examples.

4. The paper doesn't properly address the limitations of the approach. While it mentions quadratic complexity, it doesn't discuss how this limits practical applicability or provide evidence of how the model scales with larger graphs. The claim that "the model can learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways" is not supported by evidence of the model's actual behavior.

Additional feedback:
- Clarify the relationship between GGT-NN and GGS-NN. What specific limitations of GGS-NN does GGT-NN address? What new capabilities does it provide?
- Provide evidence that the model is learning the underlying rules for cellular automata and Turing machines, not just memorizing examples. Show examples of the model's behavior on unseen examples.
- Include a more rigorous comparison with prior work, ensuring all models are trained on the same number of examples.
- Discuss the model's limitations more thoroughly, including how the quadratic complexity affects practical applicability.
- Clarify the experimental setup for bAbI tasks - specify whether 95% accuracy claims are based on 1,000 or 10,000 examples.

Questions for authors:
1. How does GGT-NN differ from GGS-NN in terms of capabilities? What specific limitations of GGS-NN does GGT-NN address?
2. Can you provide evidence that the model is learning the underlying rules for cellular automata and Turing machines, rather than simply memorizing the training examples?
3. What are the actual training time and memory requirements for the model on bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks? How does this scale with graph size?
4. Please clarify which results are based on 1,000 training examples versus 10,000 examples.
