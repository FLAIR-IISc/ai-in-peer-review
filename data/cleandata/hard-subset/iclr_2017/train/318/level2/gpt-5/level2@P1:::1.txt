Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular extension of GGS-NNs that (i) maintains an explicit, differentiable, graph-structured latent state with soft node/edge existence and types, (ii) provides a set of trainable transformations to add nodes (Tadd), update node states (Th, with a direct-reference variant), update edges (TC), propagate messages (Tprop), and aggregate representations (Trepr), and (iii) composes these into end-to-end differentiable pipelines that can read text, build/modify graphs over time, and either output answers or a graph. The model is evaluated on bAbI tasks (text-to-graph-to-answer), and two rule discovery settings (graph-to-graph): a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines. With strong supervision (ground-truth graphs during training) and optional direct reference, the model achieves ≥95% accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks (except Task 17), performs strongly on pathfinding and induction, and learns to simulate the cellular automaton and Turing machines with apparent generalization to longer sequences. The approach is conceptually appealing: it yields interpretable graph states, supports graph-structured outputs, and demonstrates flexible reuse of the proposed transformations.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and dataset modifications for the main results, leading to limited evidence that the approach works in more realistic, weakly supervised settings; (2) Empirical evaluation lacks ablations and apples-to-apples comparisons to dynamic-memory baselines under identical supervision, and does not convincingly establish advantages beyond bAbI.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The goal of learning dynamic graph-structured latent states from text is timely and well-motivated, and the decomposition into differentiable graph transformations is clear and useful. The paper is well-situated relative to GNN/GGS-NN, Memory Networks, and DNCs. The emphasis on interpretable graphs is a strength.

- Novelty: Extending GGS-NN to include dynamic graph construction (soft nodes/edges, direct reference, and explicit add/update/propagate/aggregate operators) is a meaningful increment. However, many components resemble standard gated updates and attention; the novelty lies primarily in the modularization and training with soft graph variables.

- Empirical evidence:
  • bAbI: The strongest results use full supervision of graphs at every step, direct reference, and dataset alterations (e.g., exposing the generator’s “Knowledge” object; adding history nodes; collapsing multi-word entities), which are not available to typical baselines. The paper acknowledges that MemNN was also trained with strong supervision, but most other comparisons are end-to-end and therefore not directly comparable. It is difficult to attribute gains to the proposed modeling rather than the supervision signal. The non–direct-reference variant performs substantially worse in many tasks.
  • Rule discovery: The cellular automaton and Turing machine studies convincingly show that GGT-NNs can learn structured transition rules and generalize to longer rollouts in synthetic settings. However, the experiments are self-contained and lack baselines or ablations to understand which transformations are essential.
  • Missing analyses: No systematic ablations of the five transformations, the role of intermediate propagation, or supervision granularity. Limited investigation of robustness (e.g., vocabulary mismatch for direct reference), and scaling (|V|, |E|, number of edge types Y).

- Rigor and clarity: Mathematical descriptions are mostly clear (details in Appendix B), and code availability is a plus. The paper itself notes quadratic time/space complexity and the need for strong supervision, which are important limitations for practical deployment.

Additional feedback to help improve the paper (not all are decision-critical)
- Supervision and fairness:
  • Provide results on bAbI without graph-level supervision (or with partial/weak supervision) and without direct reference. Even if absolute accuracy drops, demonstrating that the model learns useful graphs end-to-end would substantially strengthen the claims.
  • Where strong supervision is used, ensure fair comparisons by training baselines with equivalent supervision or provide variants of GGT-NN trained under the same regime as end-to-end baselines.
  • Clarify precisely what information is exposed from the bAbI generator and which modifications were made (e.g., renaming entities, adding history nodes), and provide results on the unmodified dataset.

- Ablations and analysis:
  • Study the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr). For example, can tasks be solved with only TC+Tprop? What is the gain from Th,direct vs attention without hard node-type matches?
  • Analyze sensitivity to the number of node/edge types, maximum nodes per step, and the permutation-matching step for node supervision.
  • Report how continuous edge strengths are thresholded at test time and how results vary with thresholds.

- Scaling and efficiency:
  • Provide empirical scaling curves (runtime/memory vs |V|, Y) and explore sparse message passing (e.g., top-k neighbors, learned sparsity) to mitigate O(|V|^2) cost.
  • For pathfinding and larger graphs, compare to graph-neural baselines that accept text but operate over extracted/sparse graphs.

- Generalization and robustness:
  • Evaluate on additional datasets where graph states are not provided (e.g., real information extraction or knowledge base completion tasks) to showcase the method’s utility beyond synthetic settings.
  • Test robustness when direct reference is imperfect (e.g., synonyms/paraphrases), and whether learned attention can replace exact matching.

- Interpretability:
  • Visualize and qualitatively analyze learned graphs for several tasks, highlighting how edges/nodes evolve across sentences and how query-time propagation solves problems like pathfinding.

Questions for the authors
- How are the numbers of node types (N) and edge types (Y) chosen per task? Do they scale with vocabulary/entities, or are they fixed a priori?
- What is the computational cost of the node permutation matching during training? How does it scale with the maximum number of nodes added per step?
- For bAbI, how many nodes are added per sentence on average, and how sensitive are results to this cap?
- For the non–direct-reference setting, what mechanism (if any) substitutes for hard type matches? Could a learned text-to-node attention mitigate the gap?
- How are soft connectivity values C used at test time for discrete evaluation (argmax per edge type, thresholding)? Does calibration of C matter?
- Can you report ablations removing Tadd or TC for tasks that ostensibly only need edge updates or only need node additions?
- In the rule discovery tasks, how often do learned graphs deviate transiently yet recover correct rollouts? Any failure modes when generalizing to much longer horizons (e.g., 100 steps)?

Overall, the paper presents a promising and thoughtfully designed framework with clear potential. Strengthening the empirical section with weaker supervision, ablations, and fairer comparisons would make a compelling case for acceptance.
