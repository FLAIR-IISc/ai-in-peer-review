- Proposal: Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), extending GGS-NNs to learn differentiable graph-structured internal states from sequences/text.  
- Technical contribution: continuous graph formalism with soft node types, node existence strengths, dense typed connectivity, and a library of differentiable graph transformations (node add, node-state updates incl. direct-reference, edge updates, propagation, attention aggregation).  
- Training: permutation-invariant loss for unordered node addition and cross-entropy losses for edges; uses additional graph-level supervision.  
- Empirical claim: strong bAbI performance when trained with full graph supervision and direct reference, excelling on pathfinding and induction.  
- Additional demos: learns rule-based transitions for Rule 30 cellular automaton and families of small Turing machines, with some length generalization.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Heavy reliance on privileged/bespoke supervision (full ground-truth graphs and exact name matching) limits evidence that meaningful graphs/reasoning are learned end-to-end.  
- Main reason 2: Modeling is an incremental extension of existing graph/message-passing models; novelty and generality are not convincingly established via ablations or broader evaluation.  
- Motivation: Problem is compelling and well-motivated, contrasting fixed-structure graph models and implicit-memory approaches.  
- Technical quality: Formalism and transformation library are clearly specified and coherent, using GRU-style updates and attention; permutation-invariant loss is appropriate.  
- bAbI results caveat: With direct reference/full supervision ≥95% on 19/20 tasks, but without direct reference only ≥95% on 9/20 tasks, showing reliance on privileged signals; comparisons to end-to-end baselines are not apples-to-apples.  
- Rule-discovery caveat: Cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments show flexibility but are custom setups lacking baselines/ablations to isolate necessary components.  
- Missing analyses: No empirical scaling study, memory/time profiles, or sparse variants despite O(|V|^2) cost; limited ablations on transformations and continuous vs binary node/edge strengths.  
- Supervision and engineering issues: bAbI supervision uses internal generator objects and exact name matching (including entity modification), reducing external validity for noisy real-world text.  
- Essential feedback 1: Run experiments under weaker supervision — end-to-end without graph supervision, semi-supervised with few labeled graphs, and replace exact-name matching with learnable mention/entity linking.  
- Essential feedback 2: Provide ablations isolating each transformation’s contribution (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Th,direct, Trepr) and effects of continuous edge/node strengths.  
- Essential feedback 3: Include scaling study: runtime/memory vs node count and sparse update strategies (top-k neighbors or learned sparsity).  
- Nice-to-have 1: Broaden evaluation to datasets without generator-access graphs or a small real IE task with gold graphs.  
- Nice-to-have 2: Clarify decoding at test time (how most-likely graph is produced, thresholds, node count selection) and sensitivity to thresholds.  
- Nice-to-have 3: Report full training details, hyperparameters, stability across seeds, and whether model collapses to degenerate graphs without supervision.  
- Nice-to-have 4: Provide qualitative analyses and visualizations of learned graphs without supervision and error analyses (e.g., bAbI task 17).  
- Clarification questions: max nodes added per step and sensitivity; test-time graph selection/decoding procedure and constraints; whether learned token attention can substitute for direct reference; baselines tried for rule-discovery tasks; consistency across random seeds and node-order alignment during training.
