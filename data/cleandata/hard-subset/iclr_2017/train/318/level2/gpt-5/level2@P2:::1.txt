Summary
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that maintains an explicit, differentiable graph-structured latent state which can be constructed and modified from sequential (textual) input. The core contribution is a library of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation) which can be composed to build, update, and query a graph. The model is evaluated on bAbI with strong supervision (access to the generator’s knowledge graph at each step), achieving ≥95% on 19/20 tasks with direct reference; and on two synthetic rule-learning/simulation tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines), showing high accuracy and some generalization to longer rollouts. The paper argues that explicit graph states are interpretable and well-suited for tasks like pathfinding, and discusses relations to memory networks/DNCs.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific direct reference for the main results, which undermines the central claims about learning to construct and use graphs from text. (2) Empirical scope and comparisons are limited to synthetic benchmarks with non-comparable supervision; practical significance and scalability remain unclear.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement. The idea of differentiable graph construction/modification as an explicit latent state is interesting and timely. However, relative novelty beyond GGS-NN/GNN/message passing architectures is incremental: the key addition is continuous edge strengths, node strengths/types, and trainable transforms for add/update edges/nodes. The paper positions against memory/DNCs on interpretability, but does not convincingly show advantages under comparable supervision.
- Supervision and evaluation setup. The bAbI experiments use “strong supervision” by exposing the internal knowledge object of the generator as ground-truth graphs and training with graph-level losses at each step (including permutation alignment for newly added nodes). This circumvents the most challenging part—inducing structure from raw text—and conflates parsing with state tracking by relying on exact name matching (direct reference). Many baselines cited are trained end-to-end without such supervision; the comparisons are therefore not apples-to-apples. The MemNN baseline does use supporting facts supervision; still, the paper’s strongest bAbI results critically depend on both direct reference and graph supervision.
- Claims vs evidence. The paper claims the model “learns to construct and modify graphs ... based on textual input.” In practice, the supervision provides the exact graph to be produced at each step; without direct reference, performance drops substantially (95% on only 9/20 tasks). There is no demonstration that the model can learn useful graph structures with only answer supervision, even with regularization.
- Synthetic rule tasks. The cellular automaton experiment does demonstrate learning update rules from graph sequences. The Turing machine experiment is more of a learned simulator: the rules are provided as graph edges; the model executes them for a small fixed number of steps. Both experiments are compelling as capability demos but remain small-scale and synthetic.
- Technical clarity. The transformation definitions are mostly clear, and the modularity is a plus. However, some important details are missing or glossed over:
  - The “max over permutations” for node addition supervision: what algorithm is used (Hungarian/assignment)? What are the typical set sizes and computational cost?
  - Complexity: propagation and edge updates are O(|V|^2Y); no reporting of memory/runtime or limits on |V|.
  - Decoding to discrete graphs at test time: how are thresholds selected; how sensitive are results?
  - Ablations: limited analysis of the contribution of each transform (e.g., edge update vs propagation), intermediate propagation, and the impact of strong supervision components.
- Significance and generality. Beyond bAbI and synthetic simulations, there is no evaluation on real-world datasets (e.g., KB construction, AMR, program traces) where explicit graphs would shine. The reliance on specialized graph formats for bAbI (including injected history nodes) raises concerns about general applicability. The method’s scalability to larger graphs is acknowledged as a limitation but not empirically quantified.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide an end-to-end variant trained only with answer supervision on bAbI (even if weaker performance), and/or a semi-supervised setup mixing a small amount of graph supervision with many answer-only examples. This would better support the central claim.
- Report training/inference time, peak memory, typical graph sizes, and wall-clock costs; include an analysis of sparsity (and perhaps experiments with sparse edge tensors).
- Detail the permutation alignment procedure (objective, solver, complexity), and analyze robustness to inconsistent node orderings.
- Add ablations on each transformation block and on direct-reference; quantify where edge updates vs propagation provide gains.
- Evaluate on at least one non-synthetic graph-centric task (e.g., shortest-path on textual maps without exposing a generator’s graph; or a simple KG completion task with text-to-graph induction).
- Strengthen fairness of comparisons: either train GGT-NN without graph supervision or compare primarily to methods with comparable supervision; also include GGS-NN baselines where input is provided in graph form to isolate the benefit of text-to-graph construction.
- Clarify how entity types/names are obtained at test time, how unseen entities are handled, and how multi-word entities are treated without manual renaming.
- Improve reference formatting and discuss related message-passing frameworks (e.g., Interaction Networks) and set-based losses.

Questions for the authors
1. How is the permutation max in Lnode computed in practice? What are typical numbers of new nodes per step and the computational overhead?
2. What thresholds or procedures are used to convert continuous strengths to discrete nodes/edges at test time? How sensitive are results to these thresholds?
3. Can the model be trained on bAbI without graph supervision (only answer loss)? If so, what accuracy is achieved with/without direct reference?
4. How large can graphs get before training becomes infeasible (|V|, |E|)? Please report memory/time profiles and any sparsification attempts.
5. For the Turing machine task, do you ever hide or perturb the rule edges and ask the model to infer them from observed trajectories? If not, could the method discover rules rather than just execute provided ones?
6. Did you compare against a GGS-NN baseline that receives prebuilt graphs at train/test time on task 19 to isolate the benefit of text-to-graph construction?
7. How robust is the approach to noisy entity mentions (coreference, synonyms) if direct reference is imperfect or unavailable?
