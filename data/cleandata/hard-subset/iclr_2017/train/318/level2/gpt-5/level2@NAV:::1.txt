Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that maintains an explicit, differentiable graph as an internal state. It defines a set of composable graph transformations (node addition, node/state update with optional direct reference, edge update, propagation, aggregation) and a training scheme that includes set-matching losses for unordered node additions. The model is shown to (i) solve most bAbI tasks from text by constructing intermediate graphs (notably excelling on pathfinding and induction), and (ii) discover and apply transition rules in graph-formulated 1D cellular automata and 4-state Turing machines, with some generalization to longer horizons. The authors argue this yields interpretable, task-suited internal structure.

Decision: Reject (main reasons: reliance on strong supervision; limited empirical rigor and scope)
- The primary results depend on strong supervision with full ground-truth graphs and direct lexical references; it is unclear the approach works end-to-end when the graph is latent.
- The empirical evaluation lacks ablations, statistical rigor, and realistic datasets beyond synthetic/templated tasks, limiting evidence of practical impact.

Supporting arguments
- Question/problem: How to learn and use explicit, editable graph-structured state from text to support reasoning and algorithmic tasks. This is a timely and well-motivated problem, and the paper articulates the need for explicit, inspectable structure versus implicit memories.
- Motivation/literature placement: The paper engages with GNNs, GGS-NNs, memory networks, DNCs, and HAM. However, more recent literature on dynamic graph induction and neural relational inference is not discussed, and differences to prior dynamic-structure methods are not sharply quantified.
- Support for claims: bAbI scores are competitive under strong supervision; pathfinding improvements are plausible given graph bias. Yet, no variance across runs, no ablations for each transformation (e.g., Tadd, TC, Th,direct), and no compute/latency analysis despite acknowledged quadratic scaling. The rule-learning tasks are convincing sanity checks but remain synthetic with engineered graph encodings.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive, not decisive)
- Evaluate with weaker supervision: partial graph hints, distant supervision, or fully latent graphs with structural regularizers.
- Add ablations for each transformation and the set-matching loss; quantify the impact of direct reference and intermediate propagation.
- Report training/runtime/memory scaling; explore sparse edges or neighborhood sampling.
- Expand to less templated text (e.g., paraphrases) or pre-existing graph benchmarks; include error analysis and interpretability examples of learned graphs.

Questions for the authors
- Can GGT-NN learn useful graphs without any graph-level supervision? What training signals/regularizers are required?
- What is the effect size of removing Th,direct and of disabling Tadd/TC individually?
- How stable are results across random seeds, and what are the compute and memory footprints versus baselines?
- How sensitive is performance to node/edge type vocabularies and to noisy/multiword entity mentions?
- Can you show qualitative examples of learned graphs on bAbI and rule tasks, including failure cases?
