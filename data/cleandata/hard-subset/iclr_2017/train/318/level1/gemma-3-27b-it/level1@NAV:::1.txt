## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs) that allows for learning and manipulating graph-structured intermediate representations. The core idea – leveraging graphs as a flexible internal state for tasks involving relational data – is promising, and the authors demonstrate successful application to bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing the model’s capacity to learn both structural representations and transformation rules. The demonstration of solving almost all bAbI tasks and learning Turing machine rules is particularly noteworthy.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the work presents a compelling concept and achieves impressive results on the chosen tasks, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding implementation and analysis to fully convince me of the robustness and generalizability of the approach. 

The primary concern revolves around the limited comparative analysis. While comparisons to existing methods on the bAbI tasks are provided, the discussion feels cursory and doesn’t deeply analyze *why* the GGT-NN performs better (or worse) on specific tasks.  Specifically, the advantages of using a graph representation for tasks like pathfinding are stated but not thoroughly demonstrated with ablation studies. Secondly, the hyperparameter settings and training details for the more complex Turing machine and cellular automaton experiments are largely absent, making it difficult to assess the reproducibility of the results.  Without this, it’s hard to gauge if the success is due to the architecture itself or specific tuning for these tasks. 

The paper could be significantly strengthened by: 1) providing a more detailed ablation study on the bAbI tasks, specifically investigating the contribution of “direct reference”; 2) including more comprehensive training details (learning rate, batch size, network architecture specifics) for the Turing machine and cellular automaton experiments; 3) elaborating on the choice of graph representation for these tasks and exploring potential alternative representations; and 4) providing qualitative examples of the learned graphs, to better understand what the model is actually learning. 

I have several questions for the authors: 

*   For the Turing machine experiments, what is the size of the hidden states and the input/output dimensions of the graph transformations? How was the network initialized, and what optimization algorithm was used?
*   The paper mentions a modularity to the GGT-NN architecture. Could you elaborate on the design choices that enable this, and potentially provide examples of alternative configurations for different types of tasks?
*   What is the computational cost associated with the GGT-NN compared to the other methods used for evaluation? Is it scalable to larger graphs or datasets?
*   You mention that the model struggled with task 17 of the bAbI dataset. Was any specific effort made to address this, such as modifying the architecture or using a different training strategy?
