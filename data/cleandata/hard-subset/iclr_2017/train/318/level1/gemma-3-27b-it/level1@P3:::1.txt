This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending GG-NNs by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and transformations. The core idea – learning to construct and manipulate graphs from textual input for tasks like bAbI, cellular automata, and Turing machines – is compelling and addresses a crucial aspect of reasoning: representing relationships. The results, particularly the near-perfect performance on many bAbI tasks and successful learning of rule-based systems, are impressive.

**Reject**

While the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient detail regarding implementation and experimental setup to confidently assess the claims. The presentation feels more like a proof-of-concept demonstration than a rigorously evaluated scientific contribution. The most significant issue is the lack of comparative analysis. While the paper mentions comparison to other state-of-the-art methods, the comparison is often superficial and doesn't demonstrate a clear advantage of the GGT-NN, especially given its architectural complexity.  Furthermore, the relatively small scale of some experiments (e.g., training on only 1000 examples) raises concerns about generalizability.

Here's a breakdown of the supporting arguments:

* **Insufficient Implementation Details:** The paper mentions “a 2-layer fully connected network” for several components but lacks critical details like layer sizes, activation functions, and initialization strategies.  The description of the graph transformations is similarly high-level.  Reproducibility is a major concern.  The GitHub link cited in Appendix B is essential, but the paper itself should contain more specifics.
* **Weak Comparative Analysis:** The comparison with existing methods, while present, isn't convincing.  Simply stating that GGT-NN outperforms some methods on certain tasks isn't enough. A more detailed analysis of the strengths and weaknesses of each approach, as well as a statistical comparison of performance metrics, is necessary. The discussion of advantages (e.g., distributed processing) is valid but lacks quantitative support.
* **Limited Experiment Scale:** The experiments on the bAbI tasks, while showing high accuracy, rely on a limited number of training examples (1000 in some cases). This raises concerns about overfitting and generalizability, especially considering the complexity of the model.  Larger-scale experiments are needed to demonstrate the robustness of the approach.
* **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results with and without “direct reference,” but a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of different components of the GGT-NN (e.g., specific graph transformations, node update mechanisms) would be valuable. This would help identify the key factors driving performance.
* **Unclear Justification for Architectural Choices:** Several architectural decisions, such as the specific choice of GRU cells and the structure of the graph transformations, seem somewhat arbitrary. The paper doesn’t provide a strong justification for these choices, leaving the reader wondering if other architectures might perform better.



**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

* **Clarify the Notion of "Graph Structure"**: The paper refers to "graph-structured intermediate representations" but doesn’t fully articulate what this means in practice. Provide a more formal definition of the graph structure and how it relates to the input data and the tasks being solved.
* **Elaborate on the Loss Function**: The loss function used for training is briefly described. Providing a more detailed explanation, including the rationale for each component, would be helpful.
* **Provide Error Analysis**: Discuss specific examples where the GGT-NN fails and analyze the reasons for these failures. This would provide valuable insights into the limitations of the approach.
* **Address Computational Cost**: The paper acknowledges the quadratic scaling of computational cost. Discuss potential strategies for mitigating this issue, such as sparse graph representations or approximation techniques.
* **Expand Discussion of Related Work**:  The related work section is decent, but could be expanded to include more recent advances in graph neural networks and knowledge representation learning.
* **Explain the significance of the success on Rule Discovery tasks**: Why is it important to be able to learn simple cellular automaton and Turing Machines?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more detailed information about the hyperparameters used in the experiments, including layer sizes, learning rates, batch sizes, and regularization techniques?
2.  What is the computational cost of training and evaluating the GGT-NN on the bAbI tasks, compared to the baseline models?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the GGT-NN to the choice of graph transformation functions (e.g., the architecture of the neural networks used in fset and freset)?
4.  Have you performed any experiments with different graph structures or representations? If so, what were the results?
5.  What is the motivation for using GRU cells specifically, and have you considered other recurrent architectures, such as LSTMs?
