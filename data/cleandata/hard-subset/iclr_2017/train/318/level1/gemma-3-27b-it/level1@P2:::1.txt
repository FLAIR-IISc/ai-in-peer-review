This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension to existing Gated Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to learn to construct and modify graphs based on textual input and then use these graphs for downstream tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and Turing machine simulation. The paper demonstrates strong results on bAbI tasks and shows the model can learn complex update rules for the other tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea of using learnable graph transformations is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and clarity in its presentation to warrant acceptance. The improvements over existing methods, particularly on the bAbI tasks, are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed architecture. Moreover, the results on the rule discovery tasks, while positive, feel somewhat underexplored.

The main supporting argument for this decision is the limited novelty. The paper builds upon existing work (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) by introducing differentiable graph transformations. However, the concept of learning to manipulate data structures is not new, and similar ideas have been explored in other contexts (e.g., Neural Turing Machines, Differentiable Neural Computers). The specific contribution of learning *graph* transformations doesn't appear to offer a significant advantage over these existing approaches, and the paper doesn’t provide a strong enough argument why this is a superior representation for the chosen tasks.

Furthermore, the experimental validation is not entirely convincing. The bAbI task results are competitive with state-of-the-art, but the gains are marginal, and the reliance on strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep) raises concerns about the model's ability to learn useful representations without explicit guidance. The rule discovery tasks are interesting, but the paper lacks a thorough comparison with other approaches that could learn these rules without explicitly representing them as graph transformations. The discussion of the computational cost – quadratic scaling with graph size – is acknowledged, but not adequately addressed with potential mitigation strategies.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Novelty:** The paper would benefit from a clearer articulation of the unique contributions of the GGT-NN. How does this approach fundamentally differ from, and improve upon, existing methods for learning with structured data? A more detailed comparison with related work (NTMs, DNCs, LRNNs) is needed.
*   **Clarity:** The presentation of the graph transformations (Section 3) is dense and difficult to follow. More intuitive explanations and diagrams would be helpful. The mathematical notation could be simplified.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to analyze the contribution of different components of the model (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation). This would help to understand which parts of the architecture are most important for performance.
*   **Supervision:** Investigate the impact of reducing the level of supervision used during training. Can the model learn effectively with weaker signals, or without explicit graph-level supervision?
*   **Scaling:** The quadratic scaling issue needs to be addressed. Explore techniques for reducing the computational cost of the model, such as sparse edge connections or graph coarsening.
*   **Rule Discovery Tasks:** Provide a more comprehensive evaluation of the rule discovery tasks. Compare the performance of the GGT-NN with other methods that could learn these rules without explicitly representing them as graph transformations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the primary advantage of representing the intermediate state as a graph, compared to other data structures (e.g., vectors, matrices, trees)?
2.  How does the performance of the GGT-NN degrade as the graph size increases? Have you experimented with techniques to mitigate the quadratic scaling issue?
3.  How sensitive is the model to the choice of graph transformations? Would different sets of transformations lead to significantly different results?
4.  Can you provide more details on the implementation of the neural networks used within the graph transformations (e.g., number of layers, activation functions)?
5.  What is the impact of removing the direct reference mechanism? Does the model still learn effectively without it?
