- Paper summary: Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) to learn and modify graph-structured intermediate representations from text using differentiable graph transformations, evaluated on bAbI tasks, cellular automata, and Turing machines.

- Decision: Reject — interesting idea with promising results but presentation, comparisons, and rigor are insufficient for acceptance.

- Lack of strong baselines: Comparisons are unfair because GGT-NN receives full graph supervision while many baselines do not; need baselines (e.g., GG-NN/GGS-NN) trained with the same supervision.

- Limited ablation studies: Missing systematic removal/analysis of each graph transformation to show which components are essential.

- Insufficient detail in rule-discovery experiments: Task difficulty, complexity of learned rules, and visualizations of learned graphs are not provided; "perfect output" is not clearly defined.

- Clarity and organization: Descriptions of graph transformations are dense and need more intuitive explanations/illustrations; transitions between experiments feel disjointed.

- Computational cost: Paper notes quadratic scaling with graph size but does not explore mitigation strategies or scalability analysis.

- Role of node strength ('sv') unclear: Explanation of how sv affects propagation and information flow is incomplete.

- Implementation details missing: Framework, layer sizes, activation functions, and other implementation specifics are not sufficiently reported.

- Overfitting concerns: High accuracy with strong supervision raises overfitting concerns; need details on regularization and held-out test performance.

- Activation-function choice unexplained: No justification provided for using tanh in GRU cells and output layers.

- Appendix placement: Appendix C is detailed and should be incorporated into the main text to clarify inputs/outputs.

- Questions for the authors: Provide performance of GG-NN/GGS-NN with same graph supervision; results of per-transformation ablations; visualizations of learned graphs for CA and TMs; scalability of computational cost; regularization and hyperparameter selection; clarify meaning of "perfect output."
