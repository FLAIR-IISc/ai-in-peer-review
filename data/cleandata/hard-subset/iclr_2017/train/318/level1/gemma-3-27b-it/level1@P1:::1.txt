This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and transformations. The core idea is to learn to construct and modify graphs based on textual input and then use these graphs for various downstream tasks, demonstrated through bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines. The approach leverages differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) to manipulate the graph structure.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates some promising results, particularly on the bAbI tasks and rule discovery problems, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and rigor to warrant acceptance. The experimental results, while seemingly positive, are presented without adequate comparison to simpler baselines and lack detailed ablation studies to justify the complexity of the proposed architecture.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Strong Baselines:** The comparisons to existing approaches (Table 2) include a wide range of models with varying levels of supervision. The GGT-NN is often compared to models trained end-to-end *without* the strong supervision the GGT-NN receives (full graph information during training). This makes it difficult to fairly assess the model's true contribution.  A comparison against a simpler baseline that *also* receives the same level of graph supervision would be crucial. For example, a GG-NN or GGS-NN trained with the same provided graphs would establish a clearer performance improvement attributable to the GGT-NN's transformations.

2. **Limited Ablation Studies:** The paper mentions that direct reference improves performance (and shows results for models with and without it), but a more thorough ablation study is needed. What is the contribution of each individual graph transformation? Removing each transformation and observing the impact on performance would help justify its inclusion in the overall architecture and pinpoint the most critical components. For example, is the edge update transformation essential, or does node state update suffice?

3. **Insufficient Detail in Rule Discovery Experiments:** The rule discovery experiments with the cellular automaton and Turing machines are presented superficially. While the model achieves high accuracy, the paper lacks details about the complexity of the rules learned and the difficulty of the tasks.  Are these truly challenging tasks for a neural network, or are they relatively simple? Providing visualizations of the learned graphs for these tasks would also be beneficial.  Furthermore, the claim of "perfect output" needs to be clarified: Does "perfect output" mean the graph structure is identical to the expected graph, or merely that the next state is correctly predicted?

4. **Clarity and Organization:** The paper suffers from a lack of clarity in certain sections. The descriptions of the graph transformations (Section 3) are somewhat dense and could benefit from more intuitive explanations and illustrations. The organization feels disjointed in places, particularly the jump between the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Elaborate on the computational cost:** The paper mentions the quadratic scaling with graph size as a downside but doesn’t explore potential mitigation strategies.
*   **Clarify the role of node strength:** The explanation of the `sv` parameter feels incomplete. How does it impact the propagation mechanism beyond simply reducing information flow?
*   **Provide a more detailed discussion of the implementation:** What framework was used? What are the specific layer sizes and activation functions?
*   **Address the potential for overfitting:** The high accuracy on the bAbI tasks raises concerns about overfitting, especially given the strong supervision. Discuss regularization techniques used and provide results on a held-out test set.
*   **Explain the choice of activation functions:** Why was tanh chosen for the GRU cells and the output layers?
*   **Appendix C is very detailed, which is good, but it could be incorporated into the main paper to better explain the inputs/outputs.**

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the performance of a simpler GG-NN or GGS-NN model trained with the *same* graph-level supervision as the GGT-NN?
*   What were the results of ablation studies removing individual graph transformations from the GGT-NN architecture?
*   Can you provide visualizations of the learned graphs for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks?
*   How does the computational cost of the GGT-NN scale with the size of the graph and the complexity of the task?
*   What regularization techniques were used to prevent overfitting, and how were the hyperparameters chosen?
*   Can you clarify what "perfect output" means in the context of the rule discovery tasks? Is it a perfect structural match of the predicted graph versus the ground truth?
