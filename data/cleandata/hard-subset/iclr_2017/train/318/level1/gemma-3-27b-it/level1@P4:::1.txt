This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of previous work on Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs). The core idea is to allow the model to learn and manipulate graph-structured intermediate representations, enabling it to solve tasks involving relational reasoning and rule learning. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, achieving competitive results.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity regarding the implementation details, and the experimental results, while positive, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing state-of-the-art methods. The lack of ablation studies further weakens the claims regarding the contribution of specific components like direct reference.

The primary issue lies in the clarity and completeness of the technical description. The paper describes several “transformations” (Node Addition, State Update, Edge Update, Propagation, Aggregation) but the interplay between them, and the precise details of the neural network architectures used *within* each transformation, are often glossed over.  For example, the “functions” f fwd y and f bwd y within the Propagation transformation are simply stated to be neural networks without specifying their architecture. Similarly, the implementation of the node addition function fadd remains vague. This makes it difficult to assess the technical soundness of the proposed approach.  The paper relies heavily on references to previous work (Li et al., 2016) for core components like GRUs and GG-NNs, but doesn't fully explain how these are integrated into the GGT-NN framework.

Regarding novelty, the idea of using graphs as intermediate representations is not entirely new. The key contribution hinges on the differentiable graph transformations and the ability to learn these transformations directly from data. However, the performance gains on the bAbI tasks, while good, are not substantial enough to warrant acceptance, particularly given the complexity of the proposed model. The comparison to other models is also problematic, as many of them were trained without the same level of supervision (full graph information).  Furthermore, the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) are interesting but lack a thorough comparison to specialized algorithms designed for those tasks.

The empirical evaluation suffers from a lack of ablation studies. The authors state that direct reference improves performance, but do not provide a detailed analysis of *why* this is the case or how much each component contributes to the overall result. It's unclear whether the performance gains are solely due to direct reference, or if other aspects of the model are also crucial.  The experiments focus on a relatively limited set of tasks, and it would be beneficial to see the model evaluated on more diverse datasets.



**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide a more detailed description of the neural network architectures used within each graph transformation. Include information on layer sizes, activation functions, and optimization algorithms. Provide pseudocode for each transformation, elaborating on the key equations.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation) to the overall performance.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** Provide a more thorough comparison to state-of-the-art methods, preferably trained with a similar level of supervision.
*   **Explain the Choice of Hyperparameters:** Justify the choice of hyperparameters used in the experiments (e.g., learning rate, batch size, number of propagation steps).
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform an error analysis to identify the types of errors that the model makes and to gain insights into its limitations.
*   **Visualizations:** Include visualizations of the learned graphs to provide a better understanding of how the model represents and manipulates information.
*   **Address Scalability:** Discuss the computational complexity of the GGT-NN and potential strategies for scaling it to larger graphs and datasets.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide more details on the architecture of the neural networks used within each graph transformation (e.g., layer sizes, activation functions)?
2.  What is the computational complexity of the GGT-NN, and how does it scale with the size of the graph and the number of nodes?
3.  What specific design choices were made to ensure the differentiability of the graph transformations?
4.  How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of hyperparameters? Have you performed a hyperparameter search?
5.  Can you provide examples of learned graphs for the bAbI tasks or the cellular automaton, illustrating how the model represents and manipulates information?
6.  How does the performance compare if direct reference is applied only to a subset of the nodes? Does the model learn to prioritize certain nodes for direct reference?
