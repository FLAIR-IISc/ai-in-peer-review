### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture designed to learn dynamic transformations of graph-structured data. Building upon the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN), the key innovation is a modular framework of differentiable graph transformations. These transformations allow the model to incrementally construct and modify an explicit, interpretable graph representation from unstructured sequential input, such as natural language. The proposed transformations include operations for adding nodes, updating node states, modifying edges, propagating information between nodes, and aggregating a graph-level representation. The paper demonstrates the model's capabilities on two distinct sets of tasks. First, it shows that GGT-NN can learn to solve 19 out of 20 bAbI question-answering tasks by building an internal graphical knowledge base from stories and reasoning over it. Second, it demonstrates that the model can discover the underlying dynamics of complex rule-based systems by learning to simulate a 1D cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines from example traces.

### **Decision: Accept**

I recommend accepting this paper for two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Generalizable Framework:** The proposed set of differentiable graph transformations provides a powerful and general framework for modeling problems where an underlying graphical state evolves over time based on external inputs. This significantly extends the capability of graph neural networks beyond static, predefined graph structures.
2.  **Strong and Compelling Empirical Results:** The paper provides robust empirical evidence supporting its claims. The model achieves excellent performance on the challenging bAbI benchmark, and the experiments on learning the rules of cellular automata and Turing machines are a particularly convincing demonstration of the architecture's unique ability to learn to manipulate structured representations.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's core strength lies in its conceptual advance. Traditional Graph Neural Networks are powerful but are limited to operating on fixed graph structures. This work addresses that limitation by proposing a principled way for a neural network to *build and modify* its own graphical memory. The redefinition of a graph as a "soft" entity with differentiable node/edge strengths is an elegant solution that enables end-to-end training of these dynamic operations. This contribution is well-motivated and opens the door to applying graph-based reasoning to a much wider range of problems where the structure is not known a priori.

The claims are thoroughly supported by a well-designed set of experiments.

*   **bAbI Tasks:** The results on the bAbI dataset are strong. The model solves 19 of the 20 tasks, which is competitive with other state-of-the-art models. The paper is transparent about its use of strong supervision, and the comparison with the similarly supervised MemNN model is fair and insightful. The analysis of tasks where GGT-NN excels, such as pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16), convincingly highlights the advantages of an explicit graphical representation for problems with inherent relational structure. The ablation study comparing performance with and without direct reference further strengthens the results by showing the impact of this design choice.
*   **Rule Discovery Tasks:** The experiments on the cellular automaton and Turing machines are particularly compelling. These tasks go beyond simple QA and require the model to learn abstract, algorithmic update rules. Successfully learning these complex dynamics from graphical snapshots demonstrates a powerful capability for program induction and algorithmic reasoning that is not easily addressed by other architectures like Memory Networks or DNCs. The fact that the model generalizes to longer simulation times further validates that it has learned the underlying rules rather than just memorizing sequences.

Finally, the paper is well-written, clearly structured, and does an excellent job of placing its contributions within the context of related work. It effectively differentiates the GGT-NN's explicit, structured memory from the implicitly structured memory of models like DNCs and Memory Networks, while also acknowledging important trade-offs like computational complexity.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended as constructive feedback and are not critical to my decision to accept.

1.  **Reliance on Strong Supervision:** The model's heavy reliance on ground-truth graph states at every timestep is its most significant limitation for practical application. The paper acknowledges this in the conclusion. The work could be strengthened by discussing potential paths to relax this requirement in more detail. For example, could the model be trained with a loss that combines a sparse graph-supervision signal (e.g., only at the end) with the final task-level loss? Or perhaps a curriculum learning approach could be used, starting with full supervision and gradually fading it out.
2.  **Computational Complexity:** The quadratic complexity of the edge update and propagation steps is a practical concern for scaling to larger graphs. The conclusion briefly mentions using sparse edge connections as a potential optimization. It would be beneficial to elaborate on this. For instance, could an attention mechanism be used to propose updates for only a small, relevant subset of node pairs, rather than all pairs, thereby approximating a sparse update?
3.  **Analysis of Failure on Task 17:** The reasoning for the model's failure on Task 17 (Positional Reasoning) is plausible but brief. A more detailed error analysis would be insightful. Does the model fail to construct the correct graph from the short stories, or does it construct the correct graph but fail in the final reasoning step? Pinpointing the failure mode could provide valuable insights into the model's limitations.

### **Questions for the Author(s)**

1.  The model requires strong supervision in the form of the correct graph at each timestep. Have you experimented with or considered alternative supervision schemes, such as providing supervision only on the final graph, or using reinforcement learning to reward correct final answers without any intermediate graph supervision?
2.  The node addition mechanism (Tadd) uses a GRU to recurrently propose a fixed maximum number of new nodes. How was this maximum number chosen for the experiments, and how sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter?
3.  The "direct reference" mechanism seems to rely on an exact string match between a word in the input and a node type's name. Have you considered replacing this with a learned soft-attention mechanism that could identify which parts of the input refer to which node types, potentially improving generalization?
