## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The core contribution lies in enabling the model to learn to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, and subsequently utilize these graphs for various output tasks. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex rules and representations.

I **reject** this paper. While the idea of learning graph transformations is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient detail and justification for the design choices, and the results, while promising, are not convincingly demonstrated as significantly advancing the state-of-the-art. The primary reason for rejection is the lack of a clear comparative analysis demonstrating a substantial benefit over existing methods, particularly given the strong supervision used in training.

The paper provides a good overview of related work, but doesn't adequately position the GGT-NN within the landscape. The comparison to other models on the bAbI tasks (Table 2) shows competitive but not superior performance, and the authors acknowledge the strong supervision used. The success on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks is interesting, but these are relatively simple examples and the paper doesn’t demonstrate generalization to more complex rule-based systems. The detailed descriptions of the graph transformations (Appendix B) are helpful, but the rationale behind the specific architectural choices within each transformation (e.g., using GRUs) is not fully explained. Furthermore, the impact of the direct reference mechanism isn't thoroughly analyzed – is the improvement solely due to this feature, or are other aspects of the architecture equally important?

Additional feedback: The paper would benefit from ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the GGT-NN. A more rigorous comparison to unsupervised or weakly supervised methods would strengthen the claims. The code availability is a positive aspect. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  The paper mentions the quadratic scaling of the model with graph size. What strategies were considered to mitigate this, and what are the limitations of the current implementation in terms of graph size and complexity?
2.  Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the model to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly those related to the graph transformations?
3.  The direct reference mechanism seems crucial for performance. How does the model perform when this is removed, and what alternative methods could be used to achieve similar benefits?
4.  The results on the Turing machine task are presented as "learning the rules." Can you provide a more quantitative evaluation of how well the learned rules align with the ground truth rules of the Turing machine?
