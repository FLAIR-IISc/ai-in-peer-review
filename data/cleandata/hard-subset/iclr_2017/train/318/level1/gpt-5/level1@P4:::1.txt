Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of Gated Graph (Sequence) Neural Networks that can construct, modify, and use graph-structured intermediate representations. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update (including a direct-reference variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation. These are composed to incrementally build a graph from text and to query it.
- The model supports graph-structured outputs and learns to manipulate graphs based on input sequences. It demonstrates strong performance on most bAbI tasks (notably pathfinding and induction) and learns transition rules for a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and families of 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines, with evidence of generalization to longer rollouts.
- The paper introduces supervision strategies for unordered node creation and edge updates, including a permutation-invariant loss for node addition (via best matching over permutations), and reports that strong supervision with ground-truth graphs helps learning.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision with ground-truth graph states and name-based direct reference undermines the central claim of learning to construct and use graph-structured internal states from unstructured input; comparisons to predominantly end-to-end baselines are not apples-to-apples.
- Empirical validation is limited to synthetic/toy domains (bAbI, simple cellular automaton, and small Turing machines) without ablations quantifying the necessity of each component (especially direct reference and full graph supervision) or robustness; scalability and complexity are not evaluated.

Supporting arguments
1) Specific question/problem tackled
- The paper addresses how to learn and use explicit, differentiable graph-structured state transitions from unstructured inputs (text), enabling a model to build, modify, and query a graph as an internal state. This goes beyond prior GGS-NN work by allowing dynamic graph construction and explicit graph outputs.

2) Motivation and placement in literature
- The motivation is sound: many tasks are naturally graph-structured, and explicit graph states can be interpretable and support distributed computation (e.g., pathfinding). The work is positioned relative to GNNs/GGS-NNs (which assume fixed graphs), memory-augmented models (with implicit structure), and related methods (LRNNs, HAM). The distinction of explicit, learnable graph transformations is clear and relevant for 2016/2017.
- However, the “Transformer” nomenclature may be confusing (no relation to the Transformer architecture), and the related work omits discussion of fairness across supervision regimes (strong vs. end-to-end) in the bAbI comparisons.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- Clarity: The transformation suite and overall algorithm are described with reasonable detail, with equations and pseudocode; implementation details are deferred to appendices. The use of node strengths, soft edge strengths, and permutation-invariant supervision is explained. Some evaluation choices are under-specified (see questions).
- Technical soundness: The transformations are differentiable and compatible with backprop; the permutation-based loss for unordered node additions follows Vinyals et al. The propagation update is consistent with GG-NN-style GRU propagation, extended with learned message functions and soft strengths. Time/space complexity is correctly noted as quadratic in node count.
- Empirical evidence: Results on bAbI indicate strong performance when provided full graph supervision and direct reference; ablations show large drops without direct reference on many tasks. The CA/TM experiments indicate the system can learn deterministic update rules and generalize to longer sequences. However:
  - The bAbI evaluation uses strong supervision (ground-truth graphs, direct name references), while most baselines are end-to-end; only MemNN receives strong supervision of supporting facts. This limits conclusions about relative power.
  - It is unclear how performance changes with weaker supervision or without direct reference; the paper notes large dependence but lacks systematic ablations.
  - There is no reporting of variance across runs, sensitivity to hyperparameters, or runtime/memory usage. Thresholding procedures to convert soft graphs to discrete structures are not specified.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Provide a rigorous ablation study:
  - With/without graph-level supervision; partial supervision (e.g., edges only, nodes only).
  - With/without direct reference; replace exact name matches with learned mention detection to test robustness.
  - Vary the amount of supervised graph information to quantify data efficiency.
- Clarify evaluation protocols:
  - How are soft node strengths and edge probabilities binarized at test time? Fixed thresholds, learned calibrations, or decoding with structured constraints?
  - How is the maximum number of nodes per timestep selected? What prevents node duplication or graph explosion?
  - Report runtime, memory, and asymptotic costs vs. number of nodes/edges; discuss sparsity and batching.
  - Include multiple random seeds and standard deviations.
- Strengthen baselines and fairness:
  - Compare against GGS-NN variants that receive graph inputs at test time (to separate the text-to-graph step from graph reasoning).
  - Provide a “no graph supervision” setting to compare end-to-end learning with other models under the same data regime.
- Extend beyond toy tasks:
  - Evaluate on less synthetic text or noisy entity references to test the necessity of direct reference.
  - Include at least one real-world graph construction or reasoning benchmark to demonstrate applicability.
- Improve clarity:
  - Disambiguate the term “Transformer” from the later Transformer model family; consider renaming or clarifying early.
  - Detail the functions used for message passing (dimensions, activations), the number of propagation steps, and hyperparameters.
  - Describe how the permutation matching for node supervision is implemented efficiently.

Questions for the authors
- What exact procedure is used to convert continuous node strengths and edge probabilities into discrete graphs at evaluation? What thresholds are used and how were they selected?
- How many nodes can be added per timestep, and how sensitive are results to this cap? How do you prevent redundant node creation?
- What is the impact of removing graph-level supervision entirely (answers-only training)? Can partial supervision (e.g., only edges) suffice?
- How robust is the direct-reference mechanism to lexical variation or noise (e.g., synonyms, misspellings)? Did you evaluate with perturbed entity names?
- What are the computational costs (time, memory) as a function of number of nodes and edge types, and how large were the graphs in your experiments?
- How many seeds were run for each experiment, and what was the variance? Are the reported best, mean, or median accuracies?
- For the CA and Turing machine tasks, what constraints or inductive biases ensure the model learns local update rules rather than overfitting the training distribution? How is generalization measured (exact-match rate at longer horizons per instance)?
- Could you provide more details on the permutation-invariant node loss optimization: which matching algorithm is used (e.g., Hungarian) and its computational overhead during training?
