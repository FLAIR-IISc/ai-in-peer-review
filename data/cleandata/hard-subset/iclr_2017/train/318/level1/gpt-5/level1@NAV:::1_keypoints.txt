- Proposal: Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN) — modular differentiable graph transformations composed into a recurrent pipeline to build and reason over graph-structured internal states from text.  
- Empirical claims: Strong performance on many bAbI tasks (notably pathfinding and induction) and promising rule-discovery/generalization in cellular automata and Turing machines; interpretable explicit graphs.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main concern 1: Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific preprocessing for primary bAbI results (gold graphs, direct lexical signals, task tweaks).  
- Main concern 2: Insufficient end-to-end evidence and ablations to show advantages without graph-level supervision or direct-reference signals.  
- Comparability issue: Using gold “knowledge objects,” entity-name matching, and task modifications makes fair comparison to end-to-end baselines difficult.  
- Sensitivity to supervision: Performance degrades substantially in the no–direct-reference setting (only 9/20 tasks ≥95%), indicating reliance on supervision.  
- Rule-discovery evaluation: Promising but coarse — exact-match across timesteps without baselines, per-step/rollout robustness, or finer-grained metrics.  
- Ablation gaps: Limited analysis isolating transformation components (Tadd/TC/Th/Tprop/Trepr), supervision levels, and the role of intermediate propagation.  
- Scalability/efficiency gap: Acknowledged O(n^2) scaling but no empirical runtime/memory measurements, sparsity strategies, or stability analyses provided.  
- Recommendation: Provide end-to-end results without graph supervision or direct reference and include semi-supervised variants to support claims about reduced supervision.  
- Recommendation: Add ablations isolating each transformation and supervision component and report sample efficiency and learning curves.  
- Recommendation: Include fair baselines using the same supervision (e.g., GGS-NN with gold graphs) and report statistical significance.  
- Recommendation: Analyze learned graphs qualitatively and quantitatively (edge precision/recall, alignment consistency) and report compute/memory costs with/without sparsification.  
- Question: What is performance with no graph-level supervision and no direct-reference signals, and can few-shot graph supervision close the gap?  
- Question: Are edge types fixed or learned, and how sensitive are results to the edge-type inventory?  
- Question: What are time/memory costs versus input graph size, and do sparse updates or k-neighborhood propagation preserve accuracy?  
- Question: Can you provide per-timestep accuracy for rule-discovery tasks, robustness to longer/deeper rollouts and noisy inputs, and consistency of node alignments without teacher forcing?
