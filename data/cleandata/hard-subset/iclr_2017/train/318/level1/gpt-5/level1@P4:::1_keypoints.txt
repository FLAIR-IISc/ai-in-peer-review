- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) with differentiable graph transformations for node/edge updates, propagation, aggregation, and node addition.  
- Model can build, modify, and query graph-structured intermediate representations from text and produce graph-structured outputs.  
- Shows strong performance on most bAbI tasks and learns transition rules for Rule 30 CA and small Turing machines, with some generalization to longer rollouts.  
- Introduces supervision strategies for unordered node creation and edge updates, including a permutation-invariant loss via best matching; reports that strong supervision helps.  

Decision: Reject.  
- Heavy reliance on strong graph supervision and name-based direct reference undermines claim of learning graphs from unstructured input and makes comparisons to end-to-end baselines unfair.  
- Empirical validation is limited to synthetic/toy domains with no ablations quantifying component necessity, robustness, scalability, or complexity.  

Specific problem addressed:  
- Learning explicit, differentiable graph-structured state transitions from unstructured inputs to build, modify, and query internal graphs.  

Motivation and literature placement:  
- Motivation is sound: explicit graph states can be interpretable and suitable for graph-like tasks; extension beyond fixed-graph GGS-NN is relevant.  
- Terminology (“Transformer”) is potentially confusing; related work omits fairness discussion across supervision regimes in comparisons.  

Clarity and technical soundness:  
- Transformations, node strengths, soft edges, and permutation-invariant supervision are described with equations and pseudocode; implementation details are in appendices.  
- Transformations are differentiable and align with prior GRU-style propagation; complexity noted as quadratic in node count.  

Empirical evidence concerns:  
- bAbI performance relies on full graph supervision and direct reference; ablations show large drops without direct reference.  
- Lack of systematic ablations on supervision strength, direct reference, and component necessity.  
- No reporting of variance across runs, hyperparameter sensitivity, or runtime/memory usage.  
- Thresholding from soft graphs to discrete structures and decision procedures are unspecified.  

Suggested ablation experiments:  
- Evaluate with/without graph-level supervision and with partial supervision (edges-only, nodes-only).  
- Test with/without direct reference and replace name matching with learned mention detection.  
- Vary amount of supervised graph information to assess data efficiency.  

Evaluation and protocol clarifications requested:  
- Specify how soft node strengths and edge probabilities are binarized at test time and how thresholds are chosen.  
- State how maximum nodes per timestep is selected and what prevents node duplication or graph explosion.  
- Report runtime, memory, asymptotic costs vs. nodes/edges, and batching/sparsity strategies.  
- Include multiple random seeds and standard deviations for results.  

Baselines and fairness recommendations:  
- Compare against GGS-NN variants that receive graph inputs at test time to separate text-to-graph and graph-reasoning capabilities.  
- Provide an end-to-end “no graph supervision” baseline for fair comparison.  

Scope and applicability suggestions:  
- Evaluate on less synthetic or noisy text benchmarks to test robustness to indirect or noisy entity references.  
- Include at least one real-world graph construction/reasoning benchmark to demonstrate applicability beyond toy tasks.  

Clarity and presentation improvements:  
- Disambiguate the term “Transformer” or consider renaming; detail message-passing functions, propagation steps, and hyperparameters.  
- Describe efficient implementation of permutation matching for node supervision.  

Questions for the authors (concise):  
- How are continuous node strengths and edge probabilities converted to discrete graphs at evaluation and which thresholds are used?  
- How many nodes can be added per timestep; how sensitive are results to this cap and how is redundant node creation prevented?  
- What is the impact of removing graph-level supervision entirely or using partial supervision (e.g., only edges)?  
- How robust is direct-reference to lexical variation, synonyms, or noise; were perturbed-name experiments run?  
- What are the computational costs (time, memory) as a function of nodes and edge types, and what graph sizes were used?  
- How many random seeds were run, and are reported results best/mean/median with variance?  
- For CA and Turing machine tasks, what inductive biases prevent overfitting and how is generalization to longer horizons measured?  
- Which matching algorithm is used for permutation-invariant node loss (e.g., Hungarian) and what is its training overhead?
