Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GGS-NNs that makes the graph itself a differentiable, editable latent state. The core technical contributions are: (i) a differentiable graph formalism with soft node types, node “existence” strengths, and multi-type edge strengths; (ii) a suite of composable graph transformations—node addition (Tadd), node-state updates with optional direct-referencing (Th, Th,direct), edge updates (TC), message-passing propagation (Tprop), and graph-level aggregation (Trepr); and (iii) a training strategy that couples text encoders with these transformations to construct and manipulate graphs over time, plus a supervised loss over nodes/edges with permutation-invariant matching for unordered node sets. Empirically, the model achieves high accuracy on most bAbI tasks (notably excelling on pathfinding and induction) when trained with strong graph supervision, and learns the transition dynamics of a 1D cellular automaton and 4-state Turing machines with some generalization to longer horizons. The work is ambitious, clearly motivated, and shows that explicit, editable graph state can be useful for tasks that are naturally relational/graphical.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision (gold graphs at each timestep and direct entity matching) weakens the significance of the bAbI results and makes comparisons to end-to-end baselines difficult to interpret. (2) The empirical evaluation lacks ablations and controlled comparisons to convincingly support the central claim that editable graph-structured state is advantageous beyond task-specific supervision and engineering.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning to construct and update graph-structured latent states from sequences (text) and use them for reasoning and prediction. This is important and timely.
- Is the approach well-motivated and placed in the literature? Yes, the paper situates the work relative to GNNs/GGS-NNs, memory networks, DNCs, and HAM. The motivation for explicit, interpretable graph state and distributed computation over graphs is compelling, especially for tasks like pathfinding or induction. However, novelty over GGS-NN is incremental: the main step is to make the graph itself differentiable and editable (add edges/nodes) and to integrate text-to-graph construction. This is interesting but not sufficiently dissected experimentally.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - bAbI: The model attains strong performance on many tasks, particularly 16 and 19. But the training uses exposed “knowledge objects” converted to graphs and explicit name-based “direct reference” that maps tokens to node types, plus teacher-forcing of nodes/edges during training. These ingredients significantly reduce the challenge of grounding from raw text, and advantages over MemNN/MemN2N/DNC trained end-to-end are therefore not decisive. A fair comparison would include alternative models trained with equally strong supervision (e.g., providing relevant sentences or graph supervision), or a variant of GGT-NN trained without graph-level supervision. The paper notes the model “appeared to require additional supervision” but does not quantify the gap or show partially supervised regimes.
  - Rule discovery: The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments are promising and well aligned with the method’s strengths. Still, details are insufficient to assess rigor: no per-component ablation (e.g., what if TC is removed? what if Tprop alone with fixed nodes?), no error analysis, and limited reporting beyond exact-match accuracy over all timesteps. The generalization to 20/30 steps is encouraging, but the distributions of rule sets and initial tapes, and whether training/test overlap in difficulty, are not described.
- Methodological clarity and rigor:
  - The supervision scheme replaces predicted nodes/edges with gold values mid-forward pass, which creates a train/test mismatch; exposure-bias effects and their impact are not analyzed.
  - The “set-to-sequence” loss involves a hard max over permutations; while standard, it yields non-smooth assignments. Alternatives (e.g., Hungarian matching) or stability analysis would strengthen the case.
  - Complexity: Tprop is O(|V|^2Y); scalability is acknowledged but not empirically studied (no curves vs. graph size).
  - Reproducibility: Many architectural/hyperparameter details (dimensions, T, learning rates, regularization, max nodes per step) are not specified in the main text; code is referenced but not evaluated here.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
- Provide ablations isolating the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Th,direct, Tprop, Trepr). For bAbI 19, show that the gain arises from propagation across learned edges (e.g., turn off Tprop or enforce sparse/local edges).
- Evaluate weaker supervision regimes: (i) no graph supervision; (ii) partial supervision (edges only, nodes only, a subset of timesteps). Quantify the performance gap and sample efficiency.
- Control for supervision in baselines: train MemNN/MemN2N/DNC with similarly strong supervision (e.g., gold relevant facts, synthetic graph) to clarify where the advantage comes from.
- Replace direct string matching with a learned entity linker or noisy name resolution to test robustness beyond bAbI’s toy setting.
- Report compute and memory requirements as a function of |V| and sequence length; include timing/throughput.
- Expand on generalization in rule discovery: measure performance as a function of horizon and rule complexity; analyze failure modes.
- Clarify the mismatch introduced by substituting gold nodes/edges during training; consider scheduled sampling or fully predictive rollouts with auxiliary losses to reduce exposure bias.

Questions for the authors
1) How does GGT-NN perform on bAbI when trained without any graph supervision (only QA labels), and what training tricks/regularizers are needed to make this work? Can you report learning curves and final accuracies with and without Th,direct?
2) For fairness, can you train a baseline (e.g., MemN2N or DNC) with access to the same “knowledge object” supervision (or equivalent) and compare?
3) What is the sensitivity to the maximum number of nodes added per step and to the choice of edge types? Are edges/types hand-engineered per task?
4) In the rule discovery tasks, what is the distribution over Turing machine rules? Do you evaluate on held-out rule families? How does accuracy vary with the number of states/symbols and with longer horizons?
5) How large a graph can GGT-NN handle in practice? Please provide empirical scaling (time/memory) and accuracy vs. |V|, and discuss prospects for sparsity.
6) Can you provide ablations showing the necessity of Tprop vs. using only TC and Th (i.e., no message passing), especially on pathfinding and induction?
