- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs): an extension of GGNNs that maintains an explicit, differentiable latent graph.  
- Introduces five differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update (optional direct-reference), edge set/reset, message passing, and graph-level aggregation.  
- Processes text sequentially (GRU per sentence) to incrementally build/modify the latent graph and answers queries from final or aggregated graphs.  
- Training uses strong supervision: ground-truth intermediate graphs, permutation-invariant set loss for node additions, and teacher-forcing of graph components.  
- Empirical results: near-perfect accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks with 1k when using direct-reference; strong performance on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16).  
- Additional experiments on synthetic dynamics (Rule 30 CA and small Turing machines) show learned state transitions and some temporal generalization.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main criticism: Heavy reliance on full intermediate graph supervision and direct-reference undermines the claim of learning graph construction from text.  
- Comparisons are unfair: GGT-NN and MemNN are evaluated with strong supervision while most baselines are end-to-end, weakening claimed superiority.  
- Limited novelty and empirical rigor beyond GGNN/GGS-NN: insufficient ablations isolating each transformation’s contribution.  
- Missing analyses on scalability and efficiency; method scales quadratically with node count and experiments are small/synthetic.  
- Reproducibility concerns: key hyperparameters and training details (hidden sizes, propagation steps, optimizer, schedules, variance over seeds) are missing from the main text.  
- Reported ablations are insufficient—only direct-reference studied; need studies on teacher-forcing vs end-to-end, set-loss impact, and intermediate propagation necessity.  
- Recommend reporting graph reconstruction metrics (edge precision/recall, node-type accuracy) in addition to QA accuracy, since graphs are supervised.  
- Suggest adding baselines matched for supervision and evaluating end-to-end and partial-supervision settings to show robustness.  
- Recommend including at least one non-synthetic, real-world graph/text dataset to assess applicability beyond toy tasks.  
- Warn that direct-reference by string match is brittle; analyze sensitivity to noisy mentions and coreference.  
- Advise exploring sparsity/efficiency fixes (sparse adjacency, localized/top-k message passing, degree constraints) and reporting empirical scaling.  
- Improve clarity: clean up references and move essential implementation details from appendix into the main paper.  
- Key questions for authors: performance and learning curves without graph supervision or substitution; do meaningful graphs emerge without supervision (reconstruction metrics/qualitative examples)?  
- Provide exact hyperparameters and sensitivity analysis, typical node/edge counts, runtime/memory per step, and observed bottlenecks.  
- Could direct-reference be learned (neural mention typing/attention) instead of rule-based matching, and how would that affect performance?  
- Provide ablations quantifying each transformation’s contribution and the role of intermediate propagation.  
- Diagnose Task 17 failures: memory capacity, attention issues, or insufficient propagation depth; did increasing propagation steps or hidden size help?
