1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN), a modular architecture that learns to construct, modify, and use graph-structured internal states from textual input. It introduces a suite of differentiable graph transformations (node addition with strengths and types, node-state updates including “direct reference,” edge updates, propagation, and aggregation) and composes them into a recurrent pipeline for sequence processing and question answering. The approach aims to unify text-to-graph construction with graph-based reasoning and graph/text outputs. Empirically, the model attains strong performance on many bAbI tasks (notably excelling at pathfinding and induction) and learns transition rules in graph-structured simulations (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 4-state Turing machines), with encouraging generalization to longer rollouts. The method is interpretable (explicit graphs) and broadly applicable due to composability.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (a) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific preprocessing for the main bAbI results; (b) Insufficient end-to-end evidence and ablations to establish that the proposed architecture’s advantages persist without graph-level supervision or direct-reference signals.

3. Supporting arguments
- Comparability: The primary bAbI evaluations use gold “knowledge objects” exposed as graphs and direct lexical supervision (entity-name matching), with additional task modifications (e.g., history linked lists, single-word entities). While candidly disclosed, this makes comparisons to end-to-end baselines (MemN2N, DNC, etc.) difficult to interpret. The no–direct-reference setting performs substantially worse (9/20 ≥95%), highlighting sensitivity to supervision.
- Evidence for claims: The rule-discovery experiments are promising, but evaluation is coarse (exact-match across all timesteps) and lacks baselines and per-step/rollout robustness analyses. There is limited ablation of transformation components (e.g., Tadd/TC/Th/Tprop/Trepr), supervision levels, or the impact of intermediate propagation.
- Rigor and efficiency: The paper acknowledges O(n^2) scaling but does not provide empirical runtime/memory measurements, sparsity strategies, or stability analyses that would support practical applicability.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide end-to-end results without graph supervision and without direct reference; include semi-supervised variants to substantiate the claims about reduced supervision.
- Add ablations isolating each transformation and supervision component; report sample efficiency and learning curves.
- Include fair baselines using the same supervision (e.g., GGS-NN with gold graphs, sequence models with oracle sentence relevance) and report statistical significance.
- Analyze learned graphs qualitatively and quantitatively (edge precision/recall, alignment consistency), and report compute/memory costs with/without sparsification.

Questions for the authors
- What is the model’s performance with no graph-level supervision and no direct-reference signals? Can few-shot graph supervision close the gap?
- How are edge types determined—fixed schema or learned? How sensitive are results to the edge-type inventory?
- What are the time/memory costs versus input graph size, and do sparse updates (or k-neighborhood propagation) preserve accuracy?
- Can you provide per-timestep accuracy for the rule-discovery tasks and robustness to longer/deeper rollouts and noisy inputs?
- How consistent are node alignments across timesteps without teacher forcing during inference?
