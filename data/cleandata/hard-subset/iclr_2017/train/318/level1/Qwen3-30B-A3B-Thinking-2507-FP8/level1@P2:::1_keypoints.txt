- Paper introduces GGT-NN, extending GGS-NNs with differentiable graph transformations to build/modify graph-structured intermediate representations and reports strong results on bAbI, cellular automata, and Turing machines.  
- Decision: recommend rejection due to incremental novelty and unconvincing empirical justification for graphs.  
- Incremental novelty: approach largely builds on prior GGS-NN work and similar graph operations; paper does not argue why these specific transformations are novel or superior.  
- Lack of necessity for graph representation: high bAbI accuracy may be due to strong/full graph supervision rather than the graph model itself; no comparison to models that learn graphs from scratch.  
- Questionable practicality: relying on full graph supervision is unrealistic for real-world tasks and the paper offers no evidence the model works with reduced supervision.  
- Limited qualitative analysis: no visualizations or examples of generated graphs to demonstrate that learned structures are meaningful.  
- Ablation suggestion: compare GGT-NN to a standard seq2seq model on bAbI without graph supervision to test necessity of graph representations.  
- Qualitative suggestion: include visualizations/examples of graphs produced during training and inference (bAbI, cellular automaton).  
- Supervision comparison suggestion: quantify and clarify how supervision levels differ across models (e.g., MemNN vs GGT-NN).  
- Efficiency suggestion: provide concrete complexity analysis or empirical time/space measurements to support claims about inefficiency.  
- Questions for authors: how does GGT-NN perform without full graph supervision; can you show qualitative graph examples; what is the minimum supervision needed; clarify supervision comparisons across models; how do learned graphs correspond to rules in rule-discovery tasks?
