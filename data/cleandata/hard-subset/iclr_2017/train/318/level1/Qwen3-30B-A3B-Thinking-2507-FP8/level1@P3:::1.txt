

# Peer Review: LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations. The model dynamically constructs and modifies graphs based on textual input, using these graphs to produce outputs. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on most tasks, including the pathfinding task where it outperforms many other models), and show it can learn the rules governing a cellular automaton and Turing machines. The paper presents a compelling case for the utility of graph-structured intermediate representations in neural network architectures, particularly for tasks with inherent relational structure.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The key reasons are: (1) Insufficient theoretical justification for the proposed graph transformations, making it difficult to assess the novelty and validity of the approach; (2) Limited empirical evaluation without proper ablation studies to isolate the contribution of key components.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's theoretical foundation is underdeveloped. While the authors describe the graph transformations and their differentiability, they provide no rigorous proofs of differentiability for the key operations (particularly node addition and edge update). The claim that "the graph structure is fully differentiable" is asserted but not substantiated. The paper lacks comparison with existing differentiable graph operations in the literature (e.g., graph convolutional networks), making it difficult to assess the novelty of the proposed approach. The mathematical formulations in the paper are somewhat hand-wavy, particularly regarding the node addition transformation where the paper states "fadd can learn to output sv = 0 for some nodes to create fewer nodes" without justification.

The empirical evaluation is also limited in scope. The paper uses only 1,000 training examples for the bAbI tasks, while most state-of-the-art approaches use 10,000 examples. This makes the comparison potentially misleading, as the authors acknowledge that their model is "strongly supervised" (using full graph information) while other models are trained end-to-end. Crucially, the paper lacks ablation studies to determine how much each component (direct reference, node addition, intermediate propagation) contributes to the overall performance. Without these, it's impossible to determine whether the impressive results are due to the graph construction capability or simply the strong supervision.

The claim that the model "discovers the rules" for the cellular automaton and Turing machines is not substantiated. The paper evaluates performance by comparing the predicted graph with the correct graph at each timestep, but doesn't provide evidence that the learned graphs correspond to the true underlying rules. It's possible the model is simply memorizing patterns rather than learning the rules.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- Provide rigorous theoretical justification for the differentiability of the graph transformations, including proofs or detailed analysis.
- Conduct ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each key component (direct reference, node addition, etc.) to the overall performance.
- Compare the GGT-NN's performance with a baseline that uses the same graph structure as the training data (but without the graph construction and modification components).
- Discuss computational complexity more thoroughly and explore potential optimizations to address the quadratic scaling issue.
- Provide more evidence that the model is learning the underlying rules for the cellular automaton and Turing machines, rather than simply memorizing training examples.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. Can you provide a formal proof of differentiability for the node addition transformation, particularly regarding how the recurrent function fadd affects the differentiability of the overall model?

2. How does the GGT-NN perform when trained without the strong supervision (i.e., without the graph-level supervision described in Section 4.1)? Is the model still able to learn meaningful graph structures with only the final output supervision?

3. For the rule discovery tasks, can you provide examples of the graphs produced by the model during training, and show how they correspond to the true underlying rules (rather than simply matching the training examples)?
