

# Peer Review for "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that enables learning to construct and modify graph structures based on textual input. The model uses a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) to incrementally build and manipulate graphs as intermediate representations. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on three main domains: the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks), learning the rules of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and simulating arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper makes a significant contribution by explicitly modeling the internal state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges, enabling more interpretable and structured reasoning compared to previous approaches.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) The paper addresses a clear gap in the literature by enabling graph structure to be built and modified based on unstructured input, rather than requiring fixed graph structures; and (2) The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the model's capabilities across multiple domains, including natural language reasoning and rule discovery.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches (GNNs, GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, DNCs) that either require graph input or cannot modify graph structures. The GGT-NN model directly addresses this limitation by introducing differentiable graph transformations that allow for dynamic graph construction.

The empirical results are impressive and well-presented. On the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN achieves 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks (with direct reference), including 100% accuracy on 11 tasks, using only 1,000 training examples. The comparison to other state-of-the-art models shows the GGT-NN performs particularly well on tasks naturally suited to graphical representations (e.g., pathfinding in task 19). The rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) further demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex update rules from scratch.

The paper is scientifically rigorous in its methodology, with appropriate baselines, clear evaluation metrics, and thorough ablation studies (e.g., comparing models with and without direct reference). The authors provide a detailed analysis of why the model works well on certain tasks (e.g., pathfinding) but not others (e.g., task 17's complexity).

The only significant concern is the use of strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep during training), which might not reflect realistic scenarios. However, the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion and suggest future work to reduce supervision requirements.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these improvements would enhance its impact:

- Provide more detailed examples of the graphs produced by the model during training to illustrate the "graphical state transition" aspect. Visualizations would help readers understand how the model constructs and modifies graphs.
- Include a more thorough discussion of the model's limitations, particularly regarding the quadratic complexity. The paper mentions this as a downside but could suggest concrete strategies for addressing it (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing).
- Clarify the relationship between direct reference and the model's ability to handle multiple entity types. The paper states that direct reference greatly improves training but doesn't explain why this mechanism works so well for graph-based reasoning.
- The paper mentions that the model can be configured for different tasks by rearranging transformations, but it would be helpful to see a concrete example of a different configuration for a different task (e.g., for a task that doesn't require node addition).

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper uses strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep) during training. How would the model perform without this supervision (i.e., using only the final answer as supervision)? Would the model still learn meaningful graph structures from textual input alone?

2. The paper states that the GGT-NN with direct reference significantly outperforms the model without it. Could you provide more insight into why direct reference is so beneficial for graph-based reasoning, and how this mechanism might be integrated into other graph-based models?

3. The paper mentions that the model's quadratic complexity limits its applicability to large graphs. Are there specific types of graphs (e.g., sparse graphs, graphs with limited connectivity) where this complexity is less of an issue, and could the model be adapted to handle larger graphs through techniques like sparsification?
