

# Review for "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that enables neural networks to construct and modify graph-structured intermediate representations based on textual input. The model can learn to represent the world state as a graph, use that graph to answer questions, and even discover underlying rules in graphical formulations of cellular automata and Turing machines. The paper presents a comprehensive set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to form the GGT-NN architecture. The authors demonstrate strong empirical results on the bAbI tasks (achieving 95% accuracy on 19 out of 20 tasks with direct reference), and show the model's ability to learn rules governing a cellular automaton and Turing machines with high accuracy and good generalization.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a technically sound contribution that addresses a clear gap in the literature and demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple challenging tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's contribution is novel in that it extends GGS-NNs to allow for graph-structured intermediate representations rather than requiring fixed graph inputs. This is a significant advancement that enables the model to work with unstructured input and construct meaningful graph representations during processing, rather than relying on pre-defined graph structures. The paper is well-motivated by the limitations of existing graph neural network approaches, and correctly identifies the need for models that can dynamically construct graphs from textual input.

The technical soundness is strong, with well-defined graph transformations and clear explanations of the model architecture. The experimental evaluation is thorough, with appropriate baselines for the bAbI tasks (including comparison with a range of state-of-the-art models), and additional demonstrations on cellular automata and Turing machines to showcase the model's broader applicability. The results are scientifically rigorous, with detailed ablation studies (e.g., the impact of direct reference on performance) and clear presentation of accuracy metrics across multiple tasks. The paper also provides important insights into the limitations of the approach, such as the quadratic time/space complexity, and suggests promising directions for future work.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To further improve the paper, consider the following points:

- The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model uses quadratic time/space complexity, which limits scalability. It would be helpful to include a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the model and how it scales with graph size, including specific runtime measurements.

- While the paper demonstrates strong performance on bAbI tasks, it would be valuable to include a more detailed discussion of how the graph representations produced by the model correspond to the underlying task structure, particularly for tasks where the model performs well (e.g., pathfinding). Visualizations of the learned graph structures could provide additional insight.

- The paper states that the model was trained with "strong supervision" (full graph information), while other models were trained end-to-end. It would be helpful to clarify how the amount of supervision differs between the GGT-NN and other models, and to provide more details on the specific supervision provided for the graph structures.

- The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it, but it would be useful to have a more detailed analysis of why direct reference helps, including examples of cases where it significantly improves performance.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In the bAbI experiments, the paper states that the GGT-NN model was trained with full graph information. Could you clarify exactly what aspects of the graph structure were provided as supervision (e.g., node types, edge types, node strengths, etc.) and how this compares to the supervision provided to other models like MemNN?

2. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches this level on 9/20 tasks. Could you provide specific examples of tasks where direct reference makes the most significant difference, and why this might be the case?

3. For the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), the paper reports high accuracy on training tasks but also notes good generalization to longer sequences. Could you elaborate on how the model generalizes to longer sequences without additional training, and whether there are any specific patterns in the generalization performance across different sequence lengths?

4. The paper states that the GGT-NN model has quadratic time/space complexity. How do you envision addressing this limitation in future work, particularly for applications where the graph size might be large?
