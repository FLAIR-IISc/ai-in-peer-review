

# Review for "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations. The model uses differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) to process textual input and construct/modifiy graphs, which are then used to produce various outputs. The authors demonstrate the model on the bAbI tasks (achieving 95% accuracy on most tasks) and on learning the rules for a cellular automaton and Turing machines. The paper claims that this approach allows the model to learn complex relationships and transformations that would be difficult with other architectures.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network models, the novelty is incremental and the empirical results don't convincingly demonstrate the necessity of the graph representation.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Incremental novelty:** The paper builds on Li et al.'s GGS-NN work, but the core idea of allowing graph construction and modification is not substantially different from other recent work on graph-based models. The graph transformations described (node addition, edge update, etc.) are conceptually similar to operations in other graph-based architectures, and the paper doesn't clearly articulate why these specific transformations are necessary or superior to alternatives.

**Lack of clear necessity for graph representation:** The most significant issue is that the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate that the graph representation is essential for the observed performance. The GGT-NN achieves high accuracy on bAbI tasks, but this is primarily due to the strong supervision (full graph information) used during training. The paper acknowledges that "the GGT-NN model was trained with full graph information" and "the MemNN model was trained with information on which sentences were relevant to the query," but fails to compare against a model that learns the graph representation from scratch without this strong supervision. Without this comparison, it's impossible to determine whether the graph representation itself is driving the performance or whether the strong supervision is the key factor.

**Questionable practicality:** The reliance on full graph information as supervision is a major limitation. In real-world applications, such supervision would be unavailable, making the approach impractical. The paper acknowledges this limitation in the conclusion ("Another promising direction of future work is in reducing the level of supervision needed"), but doesn't provide any evidence that the model can learn meaningful graphs with reduced supervision.

**Limited qualitative analysis:** The paper provides quantitative results but lacks qualitative analysis of the graphs produced by the model. Without visualizations or examples of the graphs generated during training and inference, it's difficult to assess whether the model is actually learning meaningful graph structures that correspond to the problem domain.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- **Ablation study with no graph representation:** The paper would be significantly strengthened by comparing the GGT-NN to a standard sequence-to-sequence model on the bAbI tasks without the graph supervision. This would directly address whether the graph representation is necessary for performance.

- **Qualitative examples:** Including visualizations of the graphs produced by the model (e.g., for the bAbI tasks or the cellular automaton) would greatly enhance understanding of how the model is actually using the graph representation.

- **Supervision comparison:** The paper should clarify how the supervision levels compare across different models. The MemNN model is described as being trained with "information on which sentences were relevant," but the paper doesn't quantify how this compares to the "full graph information" used for the GGT-NN.

- **Efficiency analysis:** The paper mentions that "the distributed nature of the GGT-NN model means that it is less time and space efficient than these other networks," but doesn't provide concrete complexity analysis or empirical measurements of this inefficiency.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How does the performance of the GGT-NN compare to a standard sequence-to-sequence model on the bAbI tasks when the GGT-NN is trained without the full graph information (i.e., only with the textual input and final answers)?

2. Can you provide qualitative examples of the graphs produced by the model during training and inference, particularly for the bAbI tasks? How do these graphs correspond to the underlying problem structure?

3. What is the minimum amount of supervision required to achieve good performance on the bAbI tasks? Can the model learn to construct meaningful graphs with only partial supervision (e.g., only some nodes or edges provided)?

4. The paper mentions that the model was trained with "strong supervision" (full graph information) but doesn't discuss how this compares to the supervision used in other models. Can you clarify how the supervision levels compare across different models?

5. For the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton, Turing machines), how does the model's learned graph structure correspond to the actual rules of the system? Can you provide examples of the model's internal state during rule learning?
