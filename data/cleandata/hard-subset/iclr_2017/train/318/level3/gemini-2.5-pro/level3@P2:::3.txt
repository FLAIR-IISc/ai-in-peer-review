### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs). The key contribution is a model that learns to dynamically construct and modify an internal graph-structured representation from sequential, unstructured input like natural language. The authors propose a set of five differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation. These transformations allow the network to maintain an evolving, complex internal state that explicitly represents entities and their relationships. The model is fully differentiable and can be trained with gradient descent.

The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on two distinct sets of tasks. First, on the bAbI question-answering dataset, the model successfully solves 19 out of 20 tasks by learning to build a knowledge graph from the story and then querying it. Second, the model shows an impressive ability to discover the underlying dynamics of complex systems by learning the update rules for a 1D cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines directly from graphical representations of their states over time. The paper makes a compelling case for using explicit, manipulable graphs as an intermediate representation, contrasting this with the more implicit memory structures found in Memory Networks or Differentiable Neural Computers.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Heavy Reliance on Strong Supervision:** The model's primary weakness is its requirement for ground-truth graph states at every single timestep during training. This severely limits its applicability to a very small subset of problems where such detailed, step-by-step supervision is available. This reliance makes the comparisons to end-to-end models on bAbI less meaningful, as the GGT-NN is given access to a powerful learning signal that is unavailable to most other methods.
2.  **Paper Density and Readability:** The paper introduces a large and complex framework with many moving parts (five distinct transformations, each a neural network) in a very condensed format. This density makes it difficult to fully assess the individual contributions of each component and the overall model dynamics, hindering its accessibility and reproducibility for a conference audience.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of a differentiable model that learns to manipulate an explicit graph is highly innovative and promising. However, the current execution is hampered by the supervision requirement. The bAbI experiments, while showing strong results, depend on the data-generation process's "Knowledge object" being converted into a ground-truth graph for each sentence. This is an unrealistic assumption for almost any real-world QA task. While the authors are transparent about this, it frames the model less as a general QA system and more as a system for learning to mimic a known symbolic process. This makes it difficult to judge how the model would perform on tasks where the ideal graph structure is not known a priori. Without a clear path to reducing this supervision, the model remains a powerful but niche tool rather than a widely applicable architecture.

Furthermore, the paper's presentation is extremely dense. The introduction of five separate, parameterized transformations in Section 3, followed by their composition in Algorithm 1, presents a significant conceptual load. While the appendix provides details, the main text would benefit from a clearer, more gradual exposition. For a conference paper, this complexity can obscure the core insights and make it challenging for reviewers and readers to be confident in their understanding of the method's nuances. An ablation study examining the necessity of each of the five transformations would have been invaluable for justifying the model's complexity.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended to be constructive and are not the primary basis for my decision.

*   **Exploring Weaker Supervision:** The most impactful future direction would be to reduce the dependence on full, step-by-step graph supervision. The authors mention this in the conclusion, and I strongly encourage exploring it. Could the model be trained with only a final graph as supervision? Or perhaps with a combination of a few fully-supervised examples and many unsupervised ones? Another avenue could be reinforcement learning, where the model receives a reward for answering a question correctly, and the graph manipulations are treated as policy actions.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The paper would be much stronger with an ablation study. For example, how does performance change if you remove the `Edge Update (TC)` transformation and rely solely on `Propagation (Tprop)` to share information? Is the explicit `Node Addition (Tadd)` necessary, or could the model operate on a large, pre-allocated graph where node strengths are simply modulated from 0 to 1? This would help clarify the contribution of each component.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The quadratic complexity is mentioned as a downside in the conclusion but deserves a more prominent discussion. It would be helpful to report the typical graph sizes, number of parameters, and training times for the bAbI tasks to give readers a practical sense of the computational cost compared to the other models in Table 2.
*   **Presentation:** A running example throughout Sections 3 and 4, showing how a simple sentence like "Mary went to the kitchen" is processed through each transformation to update a simple graph, would significantly improve clarity and intuition.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  The requirement for ground-truth graphs at each step is the model's biggest limitation. Have you experimented with any methods to relax this strong supervision, for instance, by training end-to-end with only the final answer as the signal, perhaps with a regularization term to encourage graph-like structures to emerge?
2.  In Table 2, the GGT-NN is compared to many end-to-end models. Given that the GGT-NN and MemNN receive strong intermediate supervision (ground-truth graphs and supporting facts, respectively), how should we fairly interpret the performance comparison against models like EntNet or DNC that do not?
3.  Could you elaborate on the practical computational costs? For a typical bAbI task, what was the maximum number of nodes in the graph, and how did the training time per epoch compare to a baseline like the MemN2N model?
4.  The model's ability to learn the rules of a Turing machine is impressive. Does the learned graphical representation of the rules correspond to the actual rules? Have you tried visualizing the learned edge matrices (`C`) to see if the model correctly identifies the state transitions?
