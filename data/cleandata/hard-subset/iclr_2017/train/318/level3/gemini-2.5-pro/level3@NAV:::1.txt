This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that learns to perform complex reasoning tasks by manipulating an explicit, graph-structured internal state. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations that enable a model to dynamically construct and modify a graph from sequential input, such as text. This approach extends prior work on Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by allowing the graph to serve as a mutable intermediate representation. The authors demonstrate the model’s capabilities by achieving state-of-the-art results on 19 of the 20 bAbI question-answering tasks and by successfully learning the underlying dynamics of a cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines.

Decision: Accept.

This work is a strong contribution for two key reasons. First, it presents a novel and well-motivated framework for learning dynamic graph-to-graph transformations, a significant conceptual step beyond prior models that operate on static graphs. Second, the impressive empirical results, particularly on the complex bAbI benchmark, convincingly demonstrate the power of using explicit, structured representations for reasoning.

The proposed differentiable transformations allow for end-to-end training of models that reason by building and altering a knowledge graph. This provides a compelling and interpretable alternative to models with implicit memory structures like DNCs or Memory Networks. The GGT-NN's success on the bAbI tasks validates this approach, especially its outstanding performance on tasks with inherent graph structures like pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16). While not perfectly solved, the rule-discovery experiments on cellular automata further underscore the model's generality by showing it can infer complex transition rules from raw state changes.

For improvement, I would suggest the following:
*   The reliance on strong, step-by-step graph supervision is a major limitation and should be discussed more prominently as a caveat when comparing results with weakly-supervised state-of-the-art models.
*   An analysis of the failure cases on the Rule 30 task would be insightful. Why does performance fall short on this deterministic task, in contrast to the near-perfect performance on the Turing machine task?
*   While quadratic complexity is acknowledged, reporting the average graph sizes and timings for the bAbI experiments would help readers gauge the practical scalability of the approach.

I would appreciate clarification from the authors on these points:
*   Have you investigated weaker forms of supervision, such as providing only the final graph, to reduce the reliance on detailed ground-truth trajectories?
*   How sensitive is performance to the specific, hand-crafted schema used for representing the bAbI tasks as graphs?
