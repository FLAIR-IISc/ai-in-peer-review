### **Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel and ambitious architecture designed to learn dynamic manipulations of graph-structured data. The core contribution is a set of five differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation. These transformations allow the model to incrementally build and modify an internal graphical state based on a sequence of inputs, such as natural language sentences.

The work extends Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by treating the graph not just as a static input, but as a dynamic intermediate representation. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on two distinct classes of problems. First, using the graph as an internal knowledge base, the GGT-NN achieves excellent, often state-of-the-art, performance on the bAbI question-answering tasks. Second, the model is shown to be capable of discovering the underlying transition rules of complex systems, successfully learning to simulate a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and a family of Turing machines by observing their state evolution represented as graphs.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel Architecture for Dynamic Graphs:** The paper presents a well-motivated and significant conceptual advance over prior graph neural networks by introducing a framework for dynamically creating and modifying graph structures. The set of differentiable graph transformations is a powerful new tool for models that need to reason over structured, evolving data.
2.  **Strong Empirical Validation:** The claims are supported by strong results on the challenging bAbI benchmark. The model's superior performance on tasks that are intuitively graph-like (e.g., pathfinding and induction) provides compelling evidence that the explicit graphical representation is a powerful mechanism for complex reasoning.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its novel formulation of differentiable graph manipulation. Previous models like GNNs and GGS-NNs operate on fixed graph structures. The GGT-NN lifts this restriction, allowing the network itself to determine the structure of its internal state based on unstructured input. This is a crucial step towards building more flexible and powerful reasoning systems. The explicit, interpretable nature of the internal graph state is also a significant advantage over models with opaque memory stores, like DNCs or Memory Networks. The paper effectively demonstrates this with visualizations of the learned cellular automaton states.

The empirical results on the bAbI tasks are convincing. The GGT-NN solves 19 out of 20 tasks with high accuracy using only 1,000 training examples. Its performance on Task 19 (Pathfinding) is particularly noteworthy, as it dramatically outperforms other strongly supervised methods like MemNN and is competitive with the best end-to-end models. This result strongly supports the central hypothesis that an explicit graph representation is beneficial for tasks that require reasoning about entities and their relationships.

Furthermore, the successful application to rule-discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) demonstrates the model's versatility. By framing these problems as learning a graph-to-graph transition function, the paper shows that the GGT-NN can learn complex, algorithm-like procedures, which is an exciting direction for future research.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not central to my decision.

*   **Reliance on Strong Supervision:** The model's success currently depends on the availability of a ground-truth graph at every timestep. This is a significant limitation, as such detailed supervision is rare in real-world applications. The paper is honest about this, but it would be strengthened by discussing this limitation more prominently in the main body and speculating on potential paths to relax this requirement (e.g., semi-supervised learning, reinforcement learning, or better regularization).
*   **Complexity and Scalability:** The paper correctly identifies the quadratic complexity of the edge update and propagation steps as a downside in the conclusion. This is a major practical hurdle. It would be helpful to provide a brief analysis of the actual runtime and memory usage for the experiments conducted, giving readers a more concrete sense of the model's scalability limitations.
*   **Creation of Ground-Truth Graphs:** The process of converting bAbI knowledge states into graphs seems to involve non-trivial feature engineering (e.g., creating special edge types, adding history nodes). A more detailed discussion of the design principles and effort required for this conversion would make the work more easily reproducible and clarify the extent of domain knowledge needed.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

Answering these questions would help clarify my understanding and strengthen the final paper.

1.  The supervision method, where the correct graph state is substituted in at each step, seems crucial for stable training. Have you experimented with a weaker form of supervision where the model's own predicted graph is used for the next step, while still applying the intermediate loss? If so, how did the model perform? Does training become unstable?
2.  The 99.8% accuracy on the Rule 30 task is remarkable, especially given the strict metric of perfect graph identity over all timesteps. Could you elaborate on the complexity of the initial conditions used for training and testing? What types of errors does the model make in the few failure cases?
3.  The "direct reference" mechanism provides a substantial performance boost. This acts as a powerful attention mechanism guided by string matching. Have you considered learning this reference mechanism instead of hard-coding it, which could make the model more general and less reliant on entity names matching node type names?
