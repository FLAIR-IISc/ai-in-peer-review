Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN), extending Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NN) from producing sequences given a fixed input graph to constructing, transforming, and using a graph as an internal, differentiable state.
- It defines five composable, differentiable graph transformations—node addition, node state update (with optional direct-reference), edge update, message-passing propagation, and aggregation—enabling complex graph manipulation within a standard gradient-based training regime.
- The authors instantiate a model that incrementally builds an internal graph from text (sentences and queries), then reasons over it for QA. On bAbI, with strong supervision of the graph state at each timestep, the model achieves high accuracy on most tasks and excels on graph-suited problems like pathfinding and induction.
- Beyond QA, the model learns rules in synthetic domains (Rule 30 cellular automaton; 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines), including generalization to longer rollouts, evidencing learned state transitions.
- Strengths: maintains evolving, complex, interpretable graph states while remaining fully differentiable; effectively mixes continuous (soft node/edge beliefs) and symbolic (typed nodes/edges) representations; more general than fixed-shape memory architectures (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM/DNC) by enabling topology growth and modification.

Decision: Accept (weak)
Key reasons:
- Novel, general, and well-motivated framework for differentiable graph transformations with convincing demonstrations on QA and algorithmic tasks.
- Strong evidence that explicit, learnable graph states can outperform or complement fixed-memory models on graph-centric reasoning (e.g., bAbI pathfinding).

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning and using complex, differentiable graph-structured state transitions from sequential inputs, so that a model can both construct and reason over a latent graph.
- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. It builds directly on GG-NN/GGS-NN, situates itself relative to Memory Networks, NTM/DNC, HAM, graph convs, and prior state-machine extraction. The argument for explicit, manipulable graph states (vs implicit vector memories) is compelling and well articulated.
- Do the results support the claims?
  - bAbI: With graph supervision per timestep and a direct-reference mechanism, GGT-NN attains ≥95% on 19/20 tasks (100% on many), with notable gains on pathfinding and induction, and sample efficiency in several tasks. The paper acknowledges supervision differences versus end-to-end baselines and compares also to a strong-supervision MemNN. The method without direct reference underperforms, indicating the importance of structured input signals.
  - Rule discovery: High accuracy on learning Rule 30/Turing transitions and generalization to longer horizons is consistent with learning correct state-transition rules.
  - The main caveats are reliance on strong supervision for graph states and limited analysis of performance when that supervision is absent. Scalability concerns (quadratic in nodes) are acknowledged but not empirically explored.
- Additional strengths:
  - Clear, modular definition of transformations that cover the core operations needed for graph manipulation.
  - Fully differentiable formulation with soft node/edge existence/type enables training with gradient descent and potential interpretability of intermediate graphs.
  - The distributed message passing matches the inductive biases of graph-centric tasks and yields strong performance where topology matters.

- Main concerns:
  - Training requires ground-truth graph states at each timestep (both node additions and edge updates). This limits applicability to settings with rich supervision (e.g., synthetic generators) and leaves open the question of learning latent graphs from answer-only supervision.
  - Paper density/readability: the work is comprehensive and packed with details (many deferred to appendices). While thorough, it may be heavy for a conference format, potentially obscuring the core contributions for some readers.
  - Fairness of comparisons: many baselines are end-to-end; GGT-NN uses strong supervision. Although the paper is transparent about this, the relative merits under identical supervision regimes remain partially unresolved.
  - Scalability: time/space O(|V|^2) per step; no empirical scaling studies or sparse approximations shown.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper)
- Provide an ablation on supervision: train with only answer supervision, with noisy/partial graph supervision, or curriculum/semi-supervised settings. Even partial results would clarify the framework’s applicability beyond synthetic datasets.
- Add ablations per transformation (e.g., disabling Tadd/TC/Tprop) to quantify their contributions, and explore the role of direct-reference more systematically (e.g., learned mention resolvers instead of rule-based matching).
- Include complexity and scaling experiments: node/edge counts vs runtime/memory; preliminary sparse implementations or sampling strategies.
- Improve clarity by front-loading a concise “core model” section, pushing some implementation detail to the appendix; simplify notation where possible; add more intuitive diagrams for the five transformations.
- Strengthen evaluation: report variance over runs; include error analyses (e.g., failures on Task 17); visualize learned internal graphs during QA and rule discovery; consider at least one non-synthetic dataset with natural graph structure.
- Discuss limitations and future work on learning latent graphs (regularizers, structured priors, differentiable parsers, EM-style training).

Questions for the authors
- How robust is training to imperfect graph supervision? What happens if you inject noise or provide only partial edge/node labels at some timesteps?
- Can GGT-NN be trained from answer-only supervision on bAbI (even at reduced accuracy), perhaps with auxiliary regularizers that encourage discrete, sparse graphs?
- What are the typical numbers of nodes added per sentence and how is the max set? How sensitive are results to this cap and to the choice of Trepr feeding Tadd?
- How do edge updates (TC) and propagation (Tprop) interact empirically? Do you observe degenerate behaviors (e.g., overly dense graphs), and how do you mitigate them?
- What are the computational/memory requirements for typical bAbI and Turing setups (batch size, graph sizes, step counts)? Any profiling on how cost scales with |V| and Y?
- Could direct reference be learned (e.g., via an attention over tokens/types) rather than relying on string matches, and does this reduce the performance gap to the “no direct reference” model?
- For the rule discovery tasks, how often do learned graphs deviate from ground truth when the rollout length increases, and where do errors originate (node addition vs edge update vs propagation)?
