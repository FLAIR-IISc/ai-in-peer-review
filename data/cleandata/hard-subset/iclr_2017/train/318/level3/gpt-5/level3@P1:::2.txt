1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a neural architecture that treats an explicit, differentiable graph as a persistent working memory. The model composes a small set of trainable, differentiable graph transformations—node addition, node-state update (with an optional direct-reference mechanism), edge update, message passing/propagation, and aggregation—into a controller that can (a) build and modify graph-structured internal state from unstructured text and (b) either answer questions or emit graphs or sequences. This extends prior GNN/GGS-NN work from “fixed input graph → output” to “text → learned intermediate graph → output,” enabling on-the-fly graph construction during inference.

Empirically, the paper shows:
- On bAbI, with strong supervision on the intermediate graphs, GGT-NNs reach ≥95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference, comparable to top memory-network-style baselines and particularly strong on tasks naturally suited to graphs (e.g., pathfinding, induction).
- On algorithmic structure-evolution tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines), the model learns transition rules and generalizes to longer rollouts.

The approach appears to be the first end-to-end differentiable memory that is explicitly and persistently a graph (rather than implicit vector memories), and the paper provides a modular, reusable set of graph transformations. The revised draft is substantially clearer, technically sound as far as I can assess, and thought-provoking.

2. Decision: Accept (weak accept)
- Primary reason: Novelty and clarity of the “explicit differentiable graph as memory” idea and its modular transformation library, which is a meaningful step beyond fixed-graph GNNs and implicit-memory architectures (MemNN, DNC).
- Secondary reason: Solid, diverse demonstrations (bAbI, CA, TMs) showing the approach can learn both to construct graphs from text and to execute learned state transitions.

3. Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Learning to build and transform graph-structured state from unstructured input and to use that state for reasoning, thereby unifying structured prediction and algorithmic manipulation within one differentiable architecture.

Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The paper positions itself against GNN/GGS-NN (graph input only), graph convnets (fixed graphs), and memory-based architectures (implicit, vector-form memories). By making the memory an explicit graph that can be grown and edited, the model matches the structure of many relational/algorithmic domains. The related work section is broad and fair.

Do the results support the claims? Largely, with caveats:
- bAbI: Accuracy is comparable to strong memory-network systems and clearly strong on graph-suited tasks (notably task 19). However, many bAbI runs use strong supervision on intermediate graphs and a direct-reference mechanism; comparative baselines with matched supervision are limited, and there is no clear across-the-board gain over top end-to-end models. The underperformance on task 17 is acknowledged and plausibly explained.
- Rule discovery: The cellular automaton and Turing machine results compellingly show that the model can learn state transition rules and generalize to longer horizons, substantiating the “graph as algorithmic state” claim.
- Rigor: Losses and training regime are clearly described, including the set-matching loss for unordered node additions. Ablations demonstrate the impact of direct reference and intermediate propagation. Code availability is a plus.
- Limitations: Quadratic time/space scaling with nodes, reliance on strong supervision for best bAbI performance, and only preliminary generalization evidence beyond the presented tasks.

Overall, while the empirical gains on bAbI are not superior to the best alternatives, the conceptual and architectural contribution is significant and well supported.

4. Feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Fairness and supervision: Clearly separate results with strong graph supervision and direct reference from end-to-end settings; add baselines that match supervision level (e.g., MemNN with similar signals). Explore semi-supervised training where only a fraction of steps have graph supervision.
- Complexity and scalability: Provide empirical scaling curves (runtime/memory vs. number of nodes/edges) and discuss sparse adjacency and neighborhood sampling to mitigate quadratic costs. Report performance on larger graphs or more entities to stress test scalability (e.g., bAbI-17).
- Ablations and analysis: Quantify the contributions of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr), number of propagation steps, and the effect of direct reference. Include sensitivity to node/edge type vocab sizes and to errors in entity linking.
- Generalization: For CA/TM, provide length-generalization curves and failure modes; for bAbI, test robustness to paraphrases, OOV entities, and multi-word entities without rewriting.
- Interpretation: Visualize learned graphs during training and at test time; show how edges/strengths evolve, especially for pathfinding and induction tasks.
- Clarity/claims: Calibrate the “first” claim; cite closely related contemporary efforts on dynamic graph memories if any. Tighten notation and ensure all hyperparameters/datasets are fully specified for reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision: How much graph supervision is truly necessary? Can you quantify performance as a function of the proportion of supervised steps (e.g., 0%, 10%, 50%) and provide semi-supervised results?
- Inference details: At test time, how are continuous node/edge strengths turned into discrete structures (thresholding/argmax/beam)? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- Node growth and halting: How do you decide how many nodes to add per step? Is there a learned halting criterion or a fixed maximum? What happens when the number of entities exceeds this cap?
- Direct reference: How robust is the model to noisy or ambiguous mentions, coreference, and multi-word entities without pre-normalization? Can the model learn referencing purely from distributional cues?
- Scalability: Have you experimented with sparse message passing (e.g., k-NN neighborhoods, attention-based edge pruning) or batching tricks to reduce the O(|V|^2) cost?
- Generalization: For Turing machines, how well does the model extrapolate to more steps and larger tapes than seen in training, and what are the dominant failure cases?
- Comparisons: Could you include additional structured-reasoning baselines (e.g., DNC/EntNet variants) on the rule discovery tasks, and a GGS-NN baseline that receives precomputed graphs on bAbI to isolate the benefit of text-to-graph construction?

Overall, this is a constructive and timely contribution that advances differentiable reasoning with explicit graph memories.
