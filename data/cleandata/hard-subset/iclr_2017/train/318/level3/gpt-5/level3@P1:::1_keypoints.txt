- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) that learn differentiable graph transformations for end-to-end graph→graph learning and dynamic graph construction from text.
- Core technical contribution: set of differentiable transformations — node addition (Tadd), node state update (Th, Th,direct), edge update (TC), propagation (Tprop), and aggregation (Trepr) — enabling graph construction and manipulation within a neural architecture.
- Integrates a sentence encoder with a direct-reference mechanism to selectively update node types, incrementally builds latent knowledge graphs, and answers queries from the graph or via query-time propagation; can produce graph or textual outputs.
- Empirical results: strong performance on bAbI, especially on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16), often matching or outperforming prior work.
- Applies framework to Rule 30 cellular automaton framed as sequence-to-graph transformations, but results show limited long-horizon generalization and do not fully solve the task.
- Decision: Accept — key reasons are the novel differentiable graph-transformation formulation and strong empirical performance aligning with the inductive bias.
- Problem motivation and positioning: well-motivated for state-transition and relational reasoning tasks; appropriately builds on GG-NN/GGS-NN literature.
- Technical soundness: transformation set is simple yet powerful; supervision for unordered node sets and edge Bernoulli losses are reasonable and differentiable; objective is coherent.
- Empirical caveats: some bAbI gains rely on strong supervision (ground-truth graphs and direct reference); need to separate supervised vs. end-to-end settings.
- Suggestion: clearly distinguish and ablate results with/without ground-truth graph supervision and direct-reference signals, quantifying performance drops.
- Suggestion: analyze time/memory complexity and empirical scaling with node count and edge sparsity; consider sparse edge/neighborhood sampling variants.
- Suggestion: run ablations removing Tadd, TC, per-sentence propagation, Th,direct and report learned graph sparsity and node/edge type entropy to support interpretability claims.
- Suggestion: compare to GGS-NN fed pre-parsed graphs on text tasks to isolate gains from dynamic construction; stress-test pathfinding by varying graph size and degree.
- Suggestion for cellular automaton: provide quantitative metrics (exact-match per timestep, rollout horizons to failure, error localization) and probe whether the model learned the local update rule.
- Reproducibility: consolidate hyperparameters, training schedules, and cite a stable code commit if releasing code.
- Question: How much bAbI performance depends on ground-truth graphs and direct reference; can the model learn useful graphs with only QA supervision and what weak regularizers help?
- Question: Robustness to entity aliasing/synonyms and to previously unseen entities?
- Question: Sensitivity to node/edge capacity, number of propagation steps, and choice of Tprop (linear vs MLP)?
- Question: Effects of constraining edges to be sparse (top-k/thresholding) during training on scalability and accuracy?
- Question: Can you provide head-to-head comparison with GGS-NN that receives graph input, and how does pathfinding accuracy scale with graph size and path length relative to MemN2N/DNC?
- Question (Rule 30): Quantify performance across horizons (e.g., 10/20/50 steps) and whether the model captures the local 3-bit neighborhood rule when probed exhaustively.
- Question: Are failure modes due to limited receptive field, insufficient capacity, or compounding errors in Tadd/TC?
- Practical question: What are peak memory/time footprints as functions of |V| and |E|, and what implementation tricks (batching edges, fused ops) were used to make Tprop practical?
- Practical question: Have curriculum strategies or teacher-forcing schedules been tried to stabilize graph-construction training?
