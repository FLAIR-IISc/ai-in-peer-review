Summary of contributions
- The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), which construct and manipulate an explicit, differentiable graph as the model’s working memory, on-the-fly while reading text. The graph serves as an interpretable, structured state used for reasoning and answering questions.
- The model learns two things jointly: (i) long-term parameters for sentence encoding and a suite of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node/state updates, edge updates, propagation, aggregation), and (ii) per-example inference-time graph states built from text.
- The approach appears to be the first concrete instantiation of a differentiable memory explicitly represented as a graph (as opposed to vector memories with implicit structure), and it generalizes simpler memories such as stacks or lists.
- The paper reports results on bAbI (both QA and sequence variants), as well as two rule-discovery tasks (a 1D cellular automaton and 4-state Turing machines), demonstrating that the learned graph machinery can model nontrivial state transitions and sometimes generalize to longer horizons.
- The final version substantially improves clarity over prior drafts; the paper is technically accurate (to my reading) and raises interesting ideas about structured, interpretable memory.

Decision: Accept (borderline, for novelty and potential impact)
Key reasons:
- Novel and thought-provoking treatment of differentiable memory as an explicit graph, with on-the-fly construction and use during inference. This opens an important design space beyond memory networks/DNCs and provides interpretable internal states.
- Although empirical gains on bAbI are not stronger than top memory networks (and remain below traditional rule induction in some cases), the approach is constructive, broadly applicable, and worth disseminating.

Supporting arguments
Addressing the three review questions:
1) What problem is tackled?
- Learning to maintain and update a structured world state from unstructured text, represented explicitly as a graph that can be read/written differentiably and used for reasoning (QA). More broadly: learning graphical state transitions.

2) Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- Yes. The paper builds on GNN/GGS-NN ideas for message passing but contributes the crucial step of learning the graph structure itself as intermediate memory from text. It situates the work relative to Memory Networks, DNCs, HAM, and classic GNNs, making clear the contrast between implicit vector memories versus explicit graph memory. The motivation for graph memory (e.g., pathfinding, general relational structure) is compelling and arguably more powerful than stack/list memories.

3) Do results support the claims?
- Partially. On bAbI, performance is comparable to strong memory network baselines but not decisively better; rule-based induction still outperforms on some tasks. The pathfinding task highlights a natural advantage of graph memory. The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments show the machinery can learn nontrivial state transitions and sometimes generalize to longer sequences. However, bAbI uses strong supervision (full graphs), and the gains over prior methods are not significant; thus claims about superior learning/generalization should be tempered.

Strengths
- Conceptual novelty: a learned, explicit, differentiable graph as memory, with a modular library of graph operations.
- Interpretability: the internal state can be inspected and potentially reused by downstream graph-based systems.
- Breadth: demonstrations span QA, cellular automata, and Turing machines; the method is not tied to a single modality.
- Technical care: clear decomposition of transformations; training procedure for unordered node sets; discussion of complexity.

Weaknesses and limitations
- Complexity: substantially more complex than memory networks; quadratic scaling in nodes/edges and many moving parts.
- Supervision: bAbI results rely on strong graph supervision (and direct-reference signals). End-to-end QA training without graph labels is not yet convincingly demonstrated.
- Empirics: no significant performance gains over strong memory networks on bAbI; worse than traditional rule induction on some tasks; results feel preliminary.
- Generalization: while some extrapolation is shown for CA/TMs, QA generalization to longer contexts/more entities remains limited (e.g., task 17).
- Fairness of comparisons: training regimes differ (strong vs weak supervision); runtime and memory costs are not reported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (constructive, not all tied to the decision)
- Reduce supervision gap: include end-to-end QA results without graph supervision, perhaps with semi-supervised or regularized objectives; ablate the contribution of direct-reference.
- Report efficiency: wall-clock time, memory footprint, and scaling with number of nodes/edges; compare to MemN2N/DNC at matched accuracy.
- Ablations: quantify the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr), propagation depth, and the permutation-invariant node-matching loss.
- Robustness/generalization: include tests on longer stories, more entities, OOV names, and noisy lexical matches for direct reference.
- Broader baselines: add comparisons to GCN-style encoders given gold graphs at test time, neural stack/queue memories, and modern relational memory modules.
- Clarity: tighten notation across sections and appendices; earlier, concrete worked example of graph construction from a short story; summarize Algorithm 1 with a small running example.

Questions for the authors
- How well does the model perform on bAbI without graph supervision and without direct reference? Can you show learning curves and final accuracies?
- What is the exact training/inference-time complexity (time and space) as a function of nodes and edge types? Any sparsity tricks attempted?
- How sensitive is performance to the number of propagation steps and to the choice of fset/freset parameterization?
- How stable is the max-over-permutations node loss (for unordered node addition)? Did you try Sinkhorn or other differentiable matching relaxations?
- Can the method handle OOV entities and multi-word references robustly without manual preprocessing? How is entity grounding performed beyond string match?
- Did you attempt curriculum or semi-supervised setups mixing a small amount of graph supervision with many QA-only examples?
- On task 17, is the bottleneck representational (node typing) or data efficiency? Any evidence from ablations?
- Can you provide qualitative analyses of learned graphs for a few bAbI stories (and failure cases), to illustrate interpretability in practice?

Overall assessment
This is a technically sound, ambitious paper that opens a valuable direction—explicit differentiable graph memory—despite modest empirical gains on standard QA. I recommend acceptance for the novelty and potential impact, with the hope that future work will reduce supervision, improve efficiency, and push beyond bAbI.
