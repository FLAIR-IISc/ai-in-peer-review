1. Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GGS-NNs that treats a labeled, typed graph as a differentiable, explicitly structured memory. The model can construct nodes and edges on the fly from text, propagate information over the graph, and query the resulting state to produce answers or text.
- The core technical contribution is a toolkit of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node-state updates with optional direct reference, edge updates, message-passing/propagation, and graph-level aggregation), all trained end-to-end. This enables the network to learn both to build and to use a symbolic-looking graph state.
- On bAbI, the model achieves accuracy comparable to top memory-network-style approaches with strong supervision, with especially strong performance on tasks naturally aligned with graphs (e.g., pathfinding). It additionally demonstrates compelling results on rule discovery and simulation: learning the dynamics of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines from graph sequences, with some generalization to much longer rollouts.
- The work is technically careful, thought-provoking, and (to my knowledge) the first to explicitly position a differentiable, typed graph as the core memory that is constructed from text and then used to reason.

2. Decision
Accept (weak accept).
Key reasons: (i) Conceptual novelty and clear technical contribution: a learned, differentiable graph memory that is constructed from unstructured input; (ii) Solid empirical validation across qualitatively different domains (bAbI QA, cellular automaton, Turing machines), with especially strong results where graphs are the natural representation; (iii) The approach is likely to stimulate further research into explicit structured memories, even if current gains on bAbI over strong baselines are modest.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Learning to (a) build graph-structured state from text, (b) transform that state as new information arrives, and (c) read it out for answers—unifying representation construction and reasoning in a single differentiable model.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The model extends GG-NN/GGS-NN from fixed-input graphs to learned, dynamically modified graphs serving as memory. Compared to Memory Networks/DNC, it makes the structure explicit and interpretable rather than implicit via attention or pointers. Related graph learning methods (e.g., graph convs, GNNs) generally assume a graph is given; here, the graph is learned and updated from text—this is a meaningful step forward. The paper acknowledges computational costs (quadratic in nodes) and supervision needs, and contrasts performance across methods fairly.
- Do results support claims?
  - bAbI: With strong supervision (access to target graphs), the model reaches near-SOTA accuracy across most tasks, including 100% on several and strong gains on pathfinding and induction—tasks naturally mapped to graphs. While not uniformly superior to MemNN variants, performance is competitive and demonstrates that graph memory is viable. The benefit of direct reference is clearly evidenced via ablations.
  - Rule discovery: The model learns to simulate Rule 30 and Turing machines from graph sequences and generalizes to longer horizons for many cases, supporting the claim that the learned transformations can implement nontrivial state-transition rules on graphs.
  - Technical soundness: The transformation operators are well-defined and differentiable; supervision strategies for nodes/edges (including set loss over node orderings) are appropriate and carefully explained. The experimental setups appear correct, with the limitations (heavy supervision, computational cost) openly discussed.

Caveats:
- bAbI gains over strong baselines are modest; the approach is also more complex than memory-network-style models and requires strong graph supervision for best results.
- Computational/memory complexity scales quadratically with nodes; practical scalability is not yet demonstrated on larger problems.
- While the Turing/automaton results are compelling, the extent to which the model learns compact rules versus local pattern replication could be analyzed more deeply.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarity: The revised draft is much clearer. Consider a concise “design goals and trade-offs” section early on that states: why explicit graphs, when they help (e.g., relational reasoning, pathfinding), and what are the costs (quadratic scaling, supervision).
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Provide a fuller ablation table on bAbI: effect of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr), direct reference, intermediate propagation, and the supervision regime (full graph vs. edges-only vs. no graph).
  - Report variance over multiple seeds, hyperparameter sensitivity, and the effect of graph size caps.
- Fairness of comparisons: Emphasize that GGT-NN and MemNN both used strong supervision, while other baselines did not; consider including MemN2N/DNC variants with same supervision if feasible.
- Efficiency: Add wall-clock benchmarks, memory usage vs. number of nodes/edges, and any sparsity or batching strategies. Even simple sparsification (top-k edges per node) or neighborhood truncation would be informative.
- Interpretability: Include visualizations of the learned graphs over time for select bAbI tasks and the rule-discovery tasks; highlight where the model’s graph differs from ground truth to illustrate failure modes.
- Generalization tests: Extend Rule 30/TM rollouts beyond 30 steps, stress-test on unseen rules and longer tapes, and quantify degradation curves.
- Weaker supervision: Explore semi-supervised training (some examples with graphs, some without) or regularizers that encourage sparsity/consistency to move toward end-to-end training without full graph labels.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision:
  - How does performance degrade as you reduce graph supervision (e.g., only edge supervision, or only final-answer supervision)? Any preliminary results on semi-supervised setups?
  - Can the model learn useful graphs on bAbI without direct reference and without access to ground-truth graphs?
- Ablations:
  - Which transformations are most critical? What happens if you remove Tadd (fixed node set) or TC (fixed edges) on tasks where that might suffice?
  - How many propagation steps are needed per sentence and at query time? Any diminishing returns?
- Efficiency:
  - What are typical numbers of nodes/edges per story and the resulting compute/memory footprints? Any attempted sparsity mechanisms (e.g., top-k edges, sampling neighborhoods)?
- Comparisons:
  - For task 19, could you compare to a simple algorithmic baseline that runs BFS over an extracted graph from text to contextualize performance?
  - Did you attempt training with 10k bAbI examples for GGT-NN, and how does performance/overfitting behavior change?
- Rule discovery:
  - For Turing machines, how diverse were the rule sets during training/testing? Do failures correlate with particular transition structures (e.g., frequent “no move” or long-distance head motions)?
  - Can you show that the model learned the abstract transition structure (e.g., by probing its predicted rule edges) rather than memorizing short sequences?
- Robustness:
  - How sensitive is direct reference to entity surface forms (e.g., multiword names)? Could learned entity linking replace exact matches?

Overall, I find the paper constructive, technically sound, and likely to spur further work on explicit, differentiable graph memories, and I recommend acceptance.
