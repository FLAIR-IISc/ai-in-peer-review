- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGTNNs) for differentiable graph construction and transformation enabling end-to-end graph↔graph and text↔graph↔text tasks.  
- Core technical contribution: differentiable operations (node addition, node-state update with optional direct reference, edge update, message-passing propagation, graph-level aggregation) for building/updating/querying graph state.  
- Proof-of-concept experiments: modeling Rule 30 as a growing graph and applying GGTNNs to bAbI, claiming improvements via structured graph intermediates.  
- Decision: Reject — main reasons are heavy reliance on strong, task-specific supervision and insufficient evidence for realistic end-to-end effectiveness.  
- Empirical claims not consistently supported: Rule 30 remains far from solved; bAbI comparisons mix supervision regimes and lack careful ablations.  
- Problem addressed: learning to construct, maintain, and query an explicit graph-structured latent state from input sequences.  
- Motivation and positioning: well-motivated contrast between explicit graph states and implicit memory models, but needs clearer distinction from prior GGS-NN work and clarification of genuinely novel elements.  
- Cellular automaton results weaken the claim of learning complex graph→graph transitions, suggesting missing inductive bias or capacity issues.  
- bAbI concerns: apparent use of gold graphs and direct references during training makes comparisons to end-to-end baselines unfair; reported gains may be driven largely by supervision and direct-reference signals.  
- Ablation gaps: insufficient quantification of each component’s contribution (T_add, T_h, T_C, T_prop, T_repr), direct reference, and supervision signal.  
- Scalability concern: O(|V|^2) edge updates/propagation are expensive; no timing, memory, or sparse/sampled propagation results reported.  
- Claim that the model “generates the graph sequence” is only partially supported; strongest dynamics task (Rule 30) is not solved and broader rule-discovery evidence is thin.  
- Recommendation: clearly separate strong-supervision vs end-to-end results and provide baselines granted equivalent supervision to ensure fair comparisons.  
- Recommended ablations: remove/perturb direct reference, fix vs learned edges, vary propagation steps, and replace the GRU text encoder to test robustness.  
- Rule-learning improvements: analyze failure modes, try simpler automata/curriculum learning, add tasks with known ground-truth rules and horizon generalization studies.  
- Efficiency recommendations: include complexity analysis, peak memory, wall-clock timings, and experiments with sparse adjacency or sampled message passing.  
- Interpretability suggestions: visualize learned graphs over time, show edge confidences and node-type distributions, and include qualitative error analysis.  
- Reproducibility demands: release code, preprocessing, hyperparameters, and clarify efficient/stable implementation of permutation/set-loss for node creation.  
- Positioning vs GGS-NN: provide matched-supervision experiments where GGS-NN fails but GGTNN succeeds to justify novelty.  
- Questions on supervision: specify exactly what supervision bAbI training and test receive and whether gold graphs are used at test time.  
- Questions on direct reference: how is it constructed, does it rely on exact entity matching, and how robust is performance to noisy/partial linking?  
- Comparison questions: which baselines received equal supervision, and can you report truly end-to-end results with no graph supervision or direct reference?  
- Rule 30 questions: what metrics quantify failure, what are the observed failure modes, and were alternative inductive biases or curricula tried?  
- Scalability questions: typical node/edge counts, runtime/memory footprints, experiments with sparse propagation, and sensitivity to the max-nodes cap.  
- Training detail questions: how is permutation-invariant node-add loss optimized to avoid unstable matching, and how are node/edge strengths thresholded at inference (sensitivity)?  
- Overall verdict: idea is appealing and timely, but current empirical rigor and evidence are insufficient; a revision should address supervision fairness, ablations, scalability, interpretability, and reproducibility.
