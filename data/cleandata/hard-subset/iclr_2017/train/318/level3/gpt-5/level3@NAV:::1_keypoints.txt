- Introduces differentiable graph-to-graph transformations and the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) for end-to-end learning of graph state transitions.  
- Frames the task as learning cellular-automaton-like processes over growing graphs with neighbor pointers and special 0/1 nodes.  
- Uses graphs as explicit intermediate representations for text understanding/generation, combining ideas from GGS-NN to interface text and graphs.  
- Empirically, graph intermediates yield strong results on bAbI, outperforming many baselines, especially on structure-sensitive tasks.  
- Model can learn to generate graph sequences, but Rule 30 cellular automaton remains far from solved, showing current limitations.  
- Decision: Accept.  
- Main reasons: novel, clear formulation of differentiable graph-to-graph transformations and strong empirical gains on bAbI using graph intermediates.  
- Problem/motivation: Targets an important gap—learning dynamic structured graph states from unstructured input—well positioned relative to GNN/GGS-NN, Memory Networks, and DNCs.  
- Methodological soundness: Transformation set (node add/update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) is well-specified and differentiable; set-sequence matching for unordered node supervision is justified.  
- Empirical caveats: Reliance on strong intermediate graph supervision and partial failures on Rule 30 raise concerns about generality and scalability.  
- Suggest clarifying novelty vs. GGS-NN and providing ablations isolating each transformation’s contribution.  
- Request reporting of compute/memory costs and scalability (noting O(|V|^2) communication) and exploration of sparsity or neighborhood sampling.  
- Recommend providing end-to-end or semi-supervised results without graph supervision to assess real-world applicability.  
- Ask for deeper analysis of Rule 30 failure modes and why Turing machines perform better.  
- Questions to authors: sensitivity to direct reference vs. attention over spans; performance gap between strong graph supervision and end-to-end training on bAbI; how edge types are chosen and handled if mis-specified; typical graph sizes and optimizations tried (sparse ops, block message passing); dominant error types on Rule 30 and whether deeper propagation or curriculum helps.
