Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GGS-NNs that treats the internal state as a differentiable, evolving graph. It defines five learnable graph transformations—node addition, node state update (with an optional “direct reference” variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation—that can be composed to construct, modify, and query graphs from sequences of text.
- The key idea is to make graph structure itself differentiable: node types are probabilistic, nodes have existence strengths, and edges are continuous-valued per type. This allows learning complex, step-wise state transitions in graph space via gradient descent.
- The model is instantiated for text-to-graph-to-answer QA: a GRU encodes each sentence, transformations incrementally construct/modify a graph, and a query pass attends over the final graph to produce an answer. Supervision includes not only answer loss but also intermediate supervision of the graph (node/edge states) with a permutation-invariant node-addition loss.
- Empirically, GGT-NNs attain strong performance on bAbI (19/20 tasks ≥95% accuracy with direct reference), particularly excelling on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16). Beyond QA, the model learns state transition rules in two domains: 1D cellular automata (Rule 30) and 4-state Turing machines, with evidence of generalization to longer rollouts.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision (gold graph at each timestep) limits applicability and weakens the empirical claim that the model “learns” to construct and use internal graphs from text.
- The paper is dense and overlong for a conference format, with many moving parts and limited ablation/analysis to support necessity of each component.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation: The paper tackles learning explicit, manipulable graph-structured internal states from unstructured input, a timely and valuable goal. It is well-positioned relative to GNNs/GGS-NNs (fixed-graph input) and memory-based models (implicit structure), and the intuition that many tasks are naturally graph-like is compelling. The transformation set is clear and composable.

2) Novelty and relation to literature: Extending GGS-NNs to learn graph construction via differentiable node/edge variables and supervised graph transformations is a meaningful step beyond fixed-structure GNNs. The “direct reference” mechanism and permutation-invariant node supervision are sensible design choices. However, the degree of novelty over GGS-NNs is somewhat incremental: the core propagation/aggregation is inherited, while the main added capability—graph growth/editing—depends on extra supervision rather than end-to-end discovery. Positioning versus contemporary graph-convolutional methods and differentiable memory systems is fair, but stronger evidence that explicit graphs yield benefits without strong supervision is missing.

3) Empirical support and rigor:
- bAbI: Results are promising, especially on pathfinding and induction. Yet, fairness is a concern: GGT-NNs use strong per-timestep graph supervision (and entity-name-based direct reference), whereas most baselines are end-to-end with only answer supervision. The paper acknowledges this, but then draws conclusions about superiority on certain tasks that may conflate supervision advantages with modeling capacity. Moreover, some tasks required modifying the ground-truth representation (e.g., adding history lists), which could influence difficulty.
- Rule discovery: The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments are interesting demonstrations that the transformations can emulate program-like state transitions and generalize to longer horizons. Still, these are also fully supervised at the graph level, and it’s unclear whether the model learns the rules or memorizes local updates with teacher-forced structure. Quantitative analyses (e.g., robustness to noisy/partial supervision, parameter/data efficiency) are limited.
- Ablations and analysis: The paper lacks ablations for the five transformations (e.g., effect of edge update vs. node addition), the effect of intermediate propagation, the role of direct reference, or analyses of failure modes (Task 17). Complexity concerns (quadratic scaling in nodes/edges) are noted but not empirically characterized.

4) Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is thorough but dense. The number of components, transformations, and task-specific preprocessing makes it hard to parse what is essential. Some core training details (hyperparameters, tuning, compute costs, convergence behavior) are not sufficiently summarized in the main text. This limits accessibility for a broad conference audience.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide a “minimal working” GGT-NN variant and ablation study to isolate which transformations matter most on representative tasks.
- Include a weakly supervised or semi-supervised setting (e.g., partial graph labels, answer-only supervision with regularizers) to better support claims about learning graphical states from text.
- Report computational cost (time/memory), scaling with graph size, and the benefits from sparse connectivity or attention-based sparsification.
- Clarify and systematize the preprocessing required for bAbI (e.g., history nodes), and assess sensitivity to such design choices.
- Add controls to disentangle the benefit of direct reference from the transformation architecture (e.g., a baseline that injects entity embeddings without graph edits).
- Improve exposition by moving some technical details to appendices and adding a high-level “design map” of the transformations with a concrete running example.

Questions for the authors
- How far can GGT-NNs go without strong per-timestep graph supervision? Have you tried training with only answer supervision, or with partial/weak supervision plus consistency regularizers?
- What is the empirical impact of each transformation (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr)? Can you provide ablations and show where each is necessary?
- How sensitive is performance to the “direct reference” mechanism and to entity-name matching errors/noise?
- Can you quantify training/inference cost as a function of number of nodes/edges, and demonstrate effectiveness of sparsity (e.g., top-k edge updates) to mitigate quadratic complexity?
- For bAbI, how do results change if you do not modify the knowledge object (e.g., avoid adding history linked lists) and instead rely on the sequence-processing extension?
- In the rule-discovery tasks, do you observe true rule learning or pattern memorization? Can you test out-of-distribution generalization (e.g., altered local rules or unseen machine states) and visualize learned local update functions?
- How robust is the permutation-invariant node supervision to large numbers of new nodes per step, and what is the computational cost of the matching?
