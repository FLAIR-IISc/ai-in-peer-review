Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a framework that learns to construct, transform, and query graph-structured states end-to-end with gradient descent. The core technical contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that make graph→graph learning feasible within a neural architecture. This extends prior GG-NN/GGS-NN work beyond fixed-structure graph inference to dynamic graph construction and manipulation from text, enabling graphs to serve as an explicit, interpretable intermediate representation.
The model integrates a sentence encoder with a “direct reference” mechanism to selectively update nodes of referenced types, incrementally builds a latent knowledge graph from text, and then answers queries either from the final graph or via an extended query-time propagation. The approach is flexible enough to generate graph outputs and textual outputs.
Empirically, the paper reports very strong results on bAbI, outperforming or matching state-of-the-art on a number of tasks, with particularly compelling gains on pathfinding and induction where graph structure is a natural fit. The paper also frames learning state transitions as graph→graph prediction and studies a cellular automaton (Rule 30) using a growing graph with neighbor pointers and special 0/1 value nodes.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear and novel formulation of differentiable graph transformations enabling graph→graph learning and graph-as-intermediate reasoning from text; (2) Strong empirical performance and compelling qualitative alignment between task structure and the proposed inductive bias on bAbI.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets learning state transitions and reasoning over relational worlds by explicitly maintaining a graph state that can be updated from text and used for downstream inference. This is well motivated: many tasks (pathfinding, entity tracking, induction) are naturally graph-structured, and existing memory architectures (MemN2N, DNC, HAM) maintain implicit structure or fixed connectivity. Building on GG-NN/GGS-NN is appropriate and clearly positioned in the literature.
- Technical novelty and soundness: The transformation set is conceptually simple yet powerful: (i) Tadd learns to instantiate nodes with soft existence and type distributions; (ii) Th and an optional type-specific Th,direct update node states; (iii) TC updates typed, weighted edges; (iv) Tprop performs gated message passing over soft edges and node strengths; (v) Trepr provides attention-based pooling. Supervision schemes for unordered node sets (set matching à la Vinyals et al.) and edge Bernoulli losses are reasonable and differentiable; the training objective is coherent.
- Empirical support:
  - bAbI: The model achieves high accuracy across most tasks and especially shines on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16), where relational propagation is key, supporting the central claim that graph-structured intermediate states confer an advantage. The direct-reference mechanism appears important, and the paper is candid about it.
  - Cellular automaton: Framing Rule 30 as sequence-to-graph transformations is compelling. However, based on the reported behavior and qualitative evidence, the Rule 30 experiment appears far from solved; the learned dynamics and long-horizon generalization remain limited. This tempers the broader claim that the model “learns complex update rules” in fully general settings.
- Rigor and fairness: The approach is compared to strong baselines and related architectures. That said, some bAbI results rely on strong supervision (access to ground-truth graphs and direct reference), which should be clearly separated from fully end-to-end settings. The claimed advantages are most convincing where supervision is comparable; otherwise, ablations are essential.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarify supervision: Clearly distinguish results with ground-truth graph supervision and direct reference from fully end-to-end training. Provide ablations removing each supervision signal and quantify the drop.
- Complexity and scalability: Analyze time/memory complexity of each transformation and report empirical scaling with number of nodes and edge sparsity. Sparse edge and neighborhood sampling variants could be discussed or piloted.
- Ablations and diagnostics: Report performance without Tadd (fixed-node settings), without TC (edge-agnostic), without per-sentence propagation, and with/without Th,direct. Provide statistics on learned graph sparsity and node/edge type entropy to support interpretability claims.
- Evaluation breadth: Include comparisons to GGS-NN fed with pre-parsed graphs on text tasks to isolate the gain from dynamic construction. For pathfinding, vary graph size and degree to stress-test the model.
- Cellular automaton analysis: Provide quantitative metrics (exact-match graph accuracy per timestep, rollout horizons to failure, error localization), and show whether the model learns the local update law vs. memorizing short sequences.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters, training schedules, and code pointers should be consolidated. If code is released, cite a stable commit.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision and generalization:
  - How much of the bAbI performance depends on access to ground-truth graphs and direct reference? Can the model learn useful graphs with only QA supervision? What regularizers or weak supervision help?
  - Did you evaluate robustness to entity aliasing/synonyms and to previously unseen entities?
- Architectural choices:
  - How sensitive are results to the choice of node/edge capacity (hidden dimension), number of propagation steps, and the functions used in Tprop (linear vs. MLP)?
  - What happens if edges are constrained to be sparse via top-k or thresholding during training? Does this help scalability or accuracy?
- Comparisons and ablations:
  - Can you report a head-to-head comparison with GGS-NN that receives graph input, to isolate the contribution of graph construction vs. propagation?
  - For pathfinding, how does accuracy scale with graph size and path length, compared to MemN2N/DNC?
- Cellular automaton:
  - Please quantify Rule 30 performance across horizons (e.g., 10/20/50 steps). Does the learned model capture the local rule when probed with all 2^3 neighborhoods?
  - Could the failure modes be traced to insufficient local receptive field, insufficient capacity, or compounding errors in Tadd/TC?
- Practical considerations:
  - What are the peak memory and time footprints as a function of |V| and |E|? Any tricks (batching over edges, fused ops) used to make Tprop practical?
  - Have you tried curriculum strategies or teacher forcing schedules for graph construction to stabilize training?
