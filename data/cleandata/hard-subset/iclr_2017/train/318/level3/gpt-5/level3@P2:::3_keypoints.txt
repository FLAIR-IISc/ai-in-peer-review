- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) that learn differentiable graph transformations to build and modify latent graphs from sequential text.  
- Claims model constructs/modifies graphs for reasoning, trained by gradient descent, and excels with strong graph-state supervision on many bAbI tasks.  
- Decision: Reject, primarily due to heavy reliance on strong supervision and limited evidence of end-to-end graph induction; secondary concerns are incremental novelty and readability.  
- Strength: Clear motivation and positioning in literature; natural modular generalization of GGS-NNs and related models.  
- Strength: With graph-state supervision and direct reference, GGT-NNs achieve 95–100% on most bAbI tasks and perform well on pathfinding, induction, and rule-discovery tasks.  
- Strength: Rule-discovery experiments show learned local update rules can generalize to longer rollouts in many cases.  
- Main limitation: Central claim of learning to construct/modify graphs is demonstrated mainly under strong supervision (node/edge targets and direct-reference), which simplifies structure induction.  
- Empirical weakness: Performance degrades markedly without direct reference (95% on 9/20 tasks vs 19/20 with it), showing weak end-to-end learning evidence.  
- Fairness concern: Side-by-side comparisons mix models trained end-to-end with models receiving graph supervision, risking overclaiming.  
- Analysis gap: Limited ablations make it hard to assess necessity/contribution of each transformation, propagation steps, or set-matching loss.  
- Computational concern: O(|V|^2) complexity noted but not empirically profiled or mitigated with sparse/attention methods.  
- Readability issue: Paper is dense and many essential implementation/training details are relegated to appendices.  
- Suggested experiment: Report end-to-end results without graph supervision/direct reference and semi-supervised curves varying fraction of supervised timesteps.  
- Suggested ablations: Remove individual transformations, vary propagation steps, replace learned edge functions with simple maps, and compare to a GGS-NN variant on a fixed node set.  
- Suggested profiling: Report time/memory vs number of nodes/edges and demonstrate sparse implementations or attention pruning.  
- Data concern: Clarify bAbI “knowledge object to graph” mapping and whether modifications (e.g., history lists) leak task structure.  
- Baseline suggestions: Include a GGS-NN receiving the same supervised graphs and a text-only baseline with matched supervision to isolate benefits.  
- Presentation fixes: Streamline notation, move key training/loss details into main text, highlight differences from GG-NN/GGS-NN, and add qualitative visualizations of learned graphs.  
- Failure analysis: Discuss failure modes (e.g., Task 17) and representation limitations that hinder performance.  
- Question: Can GGT-NNs achieve competitive accuracy fully end-to-end with no graph-state supervision or direct-reference—please provide numbers and learning curves?  
- Question: How sensitive is performance to the amount/type of supervision (e.g., edges only or subset of timesteps)?  
- Question: What is the incremental gain of each transformation (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr)—please include ablations?  
- Question: How is direct reference implemented for multi-word names/synonyms and how robust is it to noisy mentions?  
- Question: What are exact computational costs (time/memory) as functions of |V|, |E|, and edge types, and how large a graph can you handle in practice?  
- Question: In rule-discovery tasks, do long-rollout failures arise from local update errors or graph growth/node-addition errors?  
- Question: How does GGT-NN compare to a GGS-NN given supervised graphs (no text) on bAbI-19/pathfinding to isolate transformation benefits?  
- Question: At test time without supervision, are learned graphs interpretable and stable across runs—can you show representative predicted graphs for bAbI stories and queries?
