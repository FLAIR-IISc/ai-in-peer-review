- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) that learn differentiable graph transformations as internal, evolving representations.  
- Introduces five transformation operators: node addition, node state update (with optional direct reference), edge update, propagation, and aggregation.  
- Trains operators in a supervised setting with ground-truth graph states provided at each timestep.  
- Presents a concrete instance that incrementally builds internal graphs from text for QA (bAbI) and learns rules in cellular automata and Turing machines.  
- Claims novelty and a clear advance over GGS-NNs by enabling learned differentiable graph transformations.  
- Strong and diverse empirical evidence across tasks, with competitive or superior results on graph-natural problems (e.g., pathfinding, bAbI task 19).  
- Rule-discovery experiments show the model can learn algorithmic graph updates and generalize to longer horizons.  
- Major limitation: reliance on per-timestep ground-truth graph states restricts applicability.  
- Scalability concern: computation scales at least quadratically with graph size.  
- Limited ablations and weak/unsupervised training results; per-operator contributions and unsupervised alternatives are underexplored.  
- Paper density hurts accessibility; needs clearer exposition and earlier intuitive examples.  
- Suggested improvements: add complexity/memory analysis and discuss sparsity/attention or neighborhood sampling for scalability.  
- Suggested ablations: remove/perturb each operator, vary direct-reference, and evaluate partial or no per-timestep supervision.  
- Recommend testing robustness to noisy/incomplete graph labels and adding qualitative visualizations of learned graph evolution.  
- Key author questions: can GGT-NNs be trained end-to-end without timestep supervision, and are there preliminary results?  
- Key author questions: how sensitive is training to noisy/partially observed graphs, and what are time/memory costs vs graph size (do sparse updates preserve accuracy)?  
- Key author questions: how many nodes are added per sentence and how critical is the permutation-invariant node addition loss?  
- Key author question: does the direct-reference mechanism risk leaking supervision, and can learned attention replace it without performance loss?
