Summary and contributions
- The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGTNNs), a framework for learning to construct and transform graphs differentiably, enabling graph-to-graph and text-to-graph-to-text tasks to be trained end-to-end with gradient descent.
- The core technical contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update with optional “direct reference,” edge update, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation). These operations allow the model to (i) build a graph state from unstructured input, (ii) update that state over time, and (iii) query it for downstream outputs.
- As a proof of concept, the authors model a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) as a growing graph with neighbor pointers and special 0/1 value nodes, and train the model to generate sequences of graphs. They also position GGTNNs as using graphs as explicit intermediate representations for NLP tasks (e.g., bAbI), claiming improvements over prior state-of-the-art by leveraging structured intermediates.

Decision
Reject. Main reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific scaffolding to obtain the claimed gains, with insufficient evidence that the approach works in realistic end-to-end settings.
- Key empirical claims are not consistently supported: the cellular automaton experiment is reported as far from solved, and comparisons on bAbI appear to mix supervision regimes and lack careful ablations.

Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- Learning graphical state transitions: constructing, maintaining, and querying an explicit graph-structured latent state from input sequences.

Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
- Motivation is clear: many domains are naturally graph-structured; existing GNN/GGS-NN methods operate on fixed graphs, while memory/DNC-style models have implicit structure. Providing differentiable graph construction is a meaningful extension.
- Related work is largely covered (GNNs/GGS-NNs, MemNN/EntNet/NTM/DNC/HAM, graph convolutions). The positioning—explicit graph states vs implicit memories—is compelling. However, the paper should more carefully separate contributions relative to GGS-NN (which already does message passing and sequence outputs) and clarify what is genuinely new beyond (i) differentiable edge/node existence/typing and (ii) node-add operations coupled with a text encoder.

Do the results support the claims?
- Cellular automaton: the review notes that Rule 30 remained far from solved. This weakens a central graph→graph claim (learning complex transition rules) and suggests the transformations alone may be insufficient without stronger inductive bias.
- bAbI: the paper claims outperformance of state-of-the-art via graph intermediates. However:
  - It appears the method uses strong supervision (exposing gold graphs and direct references), while many reported baselines are trained end-to-end without such supervision. Comparisons are thus not apples-to-apples.
  - There is limited ablation to quantify the contribution of each transformation (T_add, T_h, T_C, T_prop, T_repr), the “direct reference,” and the supervision signal. Reported deltas suggest direct reference and supervision drive most gains.
  - Scalability is a concern: O(|V|^2) edge updates and propagation are expensive. No timing/memory or sparse variants are reported.
- The claims that the model learns to “generate the graph sequence” are partially supported (the framework indeed produces graph predictions), but the most nontrivial dynamics task reported (Rule 30) is not solved, and the evidence on broader rule discovery is thin.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Supervision and fairness: Clearly separate results under strong supervision vs end-to-end. Provide baselines that also receive equivalent supervision (e.g., MemNN/EntNet with oracle sentence/entity supervision; GGS-NN given gold graphs), and end-to-end comparisons without any graph supervision. Report both.
- Ablations: Quantify the impact of each component:
  - Remove direct reference; vary its quality (noisy references).
  - Replace differentiable edge existence with fixed structure to isolate the benefit of graph construction.
  - Vary the number of propagation steps and the use of intermediate propagation.
  - Swap the GRU text encoder with alternatives to show robustness.
- Rule-learning tasks: Strengthen the evidence beyond Rule 30. If Rule 30 is far from solved, analyze failure modes (capacity vs inductive bias vs training instability) and try simpler automata or curriculum learning. Consider adding well-understood cellular automata or other graph rewriting tasks where ground-truth rules are known; include generalization to longer horizons.
- Efficiency: Provide complexity analysis, peak memory, wall-clock timings, and results with sparse adjacency or sampled message passing. Discuss practicality for larger graphs.
- Interpretability: Since a key selling point is explicit graph state, visualize learned graphs over time, show edge confidences and node-type distributions, and provide qualitative error analysis.
- Reproducibility: Release code, data preprocessing (especially graph supervision generation), and hyperparameters. Clarify how the set-loss/permutation matching for unordered node creation is computed efficiently and stably.
- Positioning vs GGS-NN: More explicit contrast showing tasks where GGS-NN fails without graph construction but GGTNN succeeds; ideally with matched supervision.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision:
  - On bAbI, what exact supervision is provided during training and at test time? Are gold graphs (nodes, edges, types) used only for training via losses, or is any oracle information used at test?
  - How is “direct reference” constructed—does it rely on exact entity-name matching? How would performance change with noisy/partial entity linking?
- Comparisons:
  - Which baselines are trained with the same level of supervision? Can you report end-to-end results (no graph supervision, no direct reference) and compare fairly?
  - How does GGTNN compare to GGS-NN when both are given gold graphs (i.e., no need for graph construction)?
- Rule 30:
  - You note the experiment remained far from solved. What metrics quantify this and what are the failure modes (incorrect value updates, graph growth, or propagation)?
  - Did you try alternative inductive biases (e.g., local convolutional message passing, weight sharing across positions, curriculum on horizon length)?
- Scalability:
  - What are the typical numbers of nodes/edges per instance in your experiments, and the runtime/memory footprint? Any results with sparse or sampled propagation?
  - How is the maximum number of nodes per step chosen, and how sensitive are results to this cap?
- Training details:
  - How is the permutation-invariant node-add loss optimized in practice? Any tricks to avoid unstable matching or degenerate solutions?
  - At inference, do you threshold node/edge strengths? How sensitive are answers to the threshold?

Overall, the core idea—learning explicit graph construction and transformation—is appealing and timely. However, the current empirical evidence and experimental rigor do not yet support the paper’s stronger claims, especially given reliance on strong supervision and the mixed results on rule discovery. I encourage a revised submission addressing the issues above.
