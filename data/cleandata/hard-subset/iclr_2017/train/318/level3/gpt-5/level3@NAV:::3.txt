1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), extending GGS-NNs with a set of differentiable graph transformations that enable learning complex, evolving graph states as internal representations. Five transformation operators (node addition, node state update with optional direct reference, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) are trained in a supervised setting with graph states provided at each timestep. A concrete instance incrementally builds an internal graph from text and uses it for QA (bAbI), and for learning rules in cellular automata and Turing machines. The work is ambitious and well-motivated: it mixes continuous and symbolic representations in a fully differentiable framework and is more general than fixed-shape memory models (Memory Networks, NTM/DNC).

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) Conceptual novelty and clear advance over GGS-NNs in learning differentiable graph transformations as internal state; (ii) Strong and diverse empirical evidence (bAbI, cellular automaton, Turing machines), including competitive or superior results on tasks naturally suited to graphs (e.g., pathfinding).

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles learning state transitions over graphs from unstructured inputs, a meaningful gap between GNNs that assume fixed graphs and memory models with implicit structure. It is well placed against GNN/GGS-NN and memory/DNC literature.
- Soundness and evidence: Claims are supported by detailed architecture, explicit supervision objectives for nodes/edges, and thorough experiments. On bAbI, the model attains high accuracy on most tasks (notably task 19) and demonstrates advantages where graphical reasoning is natural. Rule-discovery experiments convincingly show the system can learn algorithmic graph updates and generalize to longer horizons.
- Limitations: The reliance on ground-truth graph states at each timestep is a significant restriction that limits applicability. Computation scales at least quadratically with graph size. Some ablations (e.g., per-operator contributions) and weak/unsupervised training results are limited. The paper is dense, which hinders accessibility.

4. Feedback to improve (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarify and streamline presentation; move intuition and a small running example earlier. Summarize the five transformations and their roles with a compact figure/table.
- Provide complexity and memory analysis, and discuss sparsity/attention variants for scalability.
- Include ablations: remove/perturb each transformation, vary direct-reference, and measure performance without per-timestep graph supervision (e.g., partial supervision, consistency regularizers).
- Discuss robustness to noisy/incomplete graph labels and domain shift; add qualitative visualizations of learned graph evolution.

Questions for the authors
- Can GGT-NNs be trained end-to-end without timestep-level graph supervision (e.g., with latent graphs, weak constraints, or self-supervised objectives)? Any preliminary results?
- How sensitive is training to noisy or partially observed graphs?
- What are the time/memory costs vs graph size, and do sparse updates or neighborhood sampling maintain accuracy?
- How many nodes are typically added per sentence, and how critical is the permutation-invariant node addition loss?
- Does the direct-reference mechanism risk leaking supervision via entity names, and can learned attention fully replace it while retaining performance?
