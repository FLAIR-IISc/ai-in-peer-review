- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) with five learnable graph transformations to construct, modify, and query differentiable graphs from text.  
- Treats graph structure as differentiable via probabilistic node types, node existence strengths, and continuous-valued edges to enable gradient-based learning of stepwise graph transitions.  
- Instantiates model for text-to-graph-to-answer QA: sentence GRU encoder, incremental graph transformations, and a query-attention pass; training uses answer loss plus intermediate graph supervision with permutation-invariant node-addition loss.  
- Empirically strong on bAbI (19/20 tasks ≥95% with direct reference) and successful on rule-discovery tasks (1D cellular automata and 4-state Turing machines) with some generalization to longer rollouts.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary concern: Heavy reliance on strong per-timestep graph supervision limits applicability and undermines the claim that the model learns graphs end-to-end from text.  
- Primary concern: Paper is dense and overlong with many components and insufficient ablation/analysis to justify each design choice.  
- Problem & motivation: Addresses a timely goal of learning explicit, manipulable graph states from unstructured input and is well-positioned relative to GNNs and memory models.  
- Novelty: Extends GGS-NNs to allow graph growth/editing and introduces direct reference and permutation-invariant supervision, but overall novelty is somewhat incremental and dependent on extra supervision.  
- Empirical fairness: bAbI comparisons may conflate supervision advantages (per-step graph labels, direct reference) with model capacity; some tasks also modify ground-truth representations.  
- Rule-discovery limits: CA and Turing machine demos are interesting but equally rely on full graph supervision and lack analyses distinguishing true rule learning from memorization.  
- Missing analyses: Lacks experiments on robustness to noisy/partial supervision, parameter/data efficiency, and quantitative sensitivity studies.  
- Ablation gaps: No component-wise ablations for the five transformations, propagation steps, direct reference, or per-component necessity/failure modes (e.g., Task 17).  
- Computational scaling: Notes quadratic complexity in nodes/edges but does not empirically characterize runtime or memory scaling.  
- Clarity & reproducibility: Paper is thorough but dense; key training hyperparameters, compute costs, and convergence behavior are not sufficiently summarized in the main text.  
- Suggested fix: Provide a minimal working GGT-NN variant and systematic ablation studies to isolate essential transformations.  
- Suggested fix: Evaluate weakly or semi-supervised settings (partial graph labels or answer-only supervision with regularizers) to support learning claims.  
- Suggested fix: Report computation cost (time/memory) and assess sparsity/attention-based sparsification to mitigate quadratic scaling.  
- Suggested fix: Clarify and systematize bAbI preprocessing (e.g., history lists) and test sensitivity to those design choices.  
- Suggested fix: Add baselines/controls that disentangle direct reference benefits from the transformation architecture (e.g., inject entity embeddings without graph edits).  
- Suggested fix: Improve exposition by moving technical details to appendices and adding a high-level design map with a running example.  
- Key question: How well do GGT-NNs perform without per-timestep graph supervision or with partial/weak supervision plus consistency regularizers?  
- Key question: What is the empirical impact of each transformation (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr)—please provide ablations.  
- Key question: How sensitive is performance to the direct-reference mechanism and to entity-name matching errors/noise?  
- Key question: Can you quantify training/inference cost as a function of number of nodes/edges and demonstrate sparsity strategies (e.g., top-k edge updates)?  
- Key question: For bAbI, how do results change if you avoid modifying the knowledge object (no history linked lists) and rely on sequence processing?  
- Key question: In rule-discovery tasks, do you observe genuine rule learning or memorization—can you test out-of-distribution generalization and visualize learned local update functions?  
- Key question: How does permutation-invariant node supervision scale with many new nodes per step, and what is the computational cost of the matching?
