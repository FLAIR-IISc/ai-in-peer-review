Summary and contributions
The paper introduces differentiable graph transformations that enable learning graph-to-graph state transitions end-to-end with gradient descent. It frames the problem as learning a cellular automaton-like process over sequences of growing graphs with neighbor pointers and special 0/1 value nodes, and presents the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) that composes these transformations. The model uses graphs as explicit intermediate representations for text understanding and generation, combining ideas from GGS-NN to interface between text and graphs. Empirically, using graphs as intermediates yields strong results on bAbI, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines on many tasks. The architecture can learn to generate graph sequences, although the Rule 30 cellular automaton experiment remains far from solved, highlighting current limitations.

Decision: Accept
Reasons: (1) A clear, novel formulation of differentiable graph-to-graph transformations with an interpretable graph-state intermediate; (2) Strong empirical gains on bAbI when graphs are used as intermediates, demonstrating practical value.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets a timely and important gap—learning to construct and transform structured, dynamic graph states from unstructured input and using them for reasoning. Positioning relative to GNN/GGS-NN, Memory Networks, and DNCs is appropriate; the work’s key distinction is explicit, trainable graph dynamics rather than implicit memories.
- Methodological soundness: The transformation set (node add, node update with direct reference, edge update, propagation, aggregation) is well-specified and differentiable. The supervision strategy for unordered node sets is justified via set-sequence matching. The approach is coherent and extensible.
- Empirical support: On bAbI, the gains, especially on structure-sensitive tasks (e.g., pathfinding), substantiate the claim that explicit graph intermediates can be advantageous. However, reliance on strong supervision for intermediate graphs and partial failures on Rule 30 suggest the method’s current limits and raise questions about generality and scalability.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify the novelty delta vs. GGS-NN beyond using graphs as intermediates; an ablation isolating each transformation’s contribution would strengthen the case.
- Report compute/memory costs and scalability (O(|V|^2) communication) and explore sparsity or neighborhood sampling.
- Provide end-to-end results without graph supervision, or semi-supervised setups, to gauge applicability in realistic settings.
- Deepen analysis of failure modes on Rule 30 and why Turing machines fare better.

Questions to authors
- How sensitive are results to direct reference? Can attention over token spans replace name matching, and how does that affect performance?
- What is the performance gap between strong graph supervision vs. end-to-end training on bAbI?
- How are edge types chosen in practice, and how does the model handle mis-specified or overly coarse edge vocabularies?
- What are typical graph sizes at training/inference time, and what optimizations (sparse ops, block message passing) were attempted?
- For Rule 30, what concrete errors dominate (local update mispredictions vs. boundary growth), and does deeper propagation or curriculum help?
