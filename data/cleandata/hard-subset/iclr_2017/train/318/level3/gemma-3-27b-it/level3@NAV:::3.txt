## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper proposes a novel extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) called the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN). The core contribution lies in learning differentiable graph transformations as internal representations, allowing the model to manipulate and reason about graph-structured data. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex rules and maintain evolving graph states. A key strength of this work is its ability to seamlessly integrate continuous and symbolic representations, offering a more general approach than fixed-shape memory models.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper for this conference. While the idea is compelling and the technical implementation seems sound, the heavy reliance on complete graph-state supervision at each timestep is a significant limitation. This makes the approach currently impractical for a wide range of real-world applications where such supervision is unavailable. Additionally, the paper is quite dense and reads more like a journal submission than a conference paper, hindering its accessibility.

The authors convincingly demonstrate that the GGT-NN can learn to solve complex tasks *given* the ground truth graph states. However, the paper doesn’t adequately address how the model would perform, or how the training process would change, if this supervision wasn’t available. The claim of learning “graphical formulations” feels somewhat limited if the graphs are essentially being provided rather than discovered or constructed from less structured input. The experimental results, while impressive, are presented without sufficient comparison to unsupervised or weakly-supervised approaches for learning graph structures. The performance on the Turing machine task is also intriguing, but a more detailed analysis of *what* the network is learning would be beneficial. 

To improve this paper, I suggest the following: 
* **Address the supervision requirement:** Discuss potential avenues for reducing or removing the need for ground-truth graph states, even if only theoretically.
* **Improve readability:** Restructure the paper with a clearer narrative flow, perhaps expanding the introduction and providing more intuitive explanations of the transformations. Break up large paragraphs.
* **Clarify the role of direct reference:** Explain more clearly *why* direct reference improves performance and under what conditions it is most effective.
* **Provide more detailed visualizations:** Illustrate the learned graph transformations with more examples, to better understand how the model manipulates the internal graph representation.

I have the following questions for the authors:
* What is the computational cost of the graph transformations, and how does it scale with graph size?
* Could the GGT-NN architecture be adapted to handle graphs with varying node and edge types more flexibly?
* What strategies could be employed to initialize the graph transformations to accelerate learning?
* Is there an analysis of which graph transformations are most critical for performance on different tasks?
