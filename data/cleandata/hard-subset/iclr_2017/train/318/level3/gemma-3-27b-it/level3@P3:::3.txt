This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) to learn and manipulate graph-structured data as an internal representation. The authors demonstrate the model’s ability to learn complex graph transformations from textual input, successfully solving bAbI tasks and even discovering rules governing simple computational systems like cellular automata and Turing machines. The core contribution lies in maintaining evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable, enabling end-to-end training via gradient descent – a significant advantage over fixed-shape memory models. The combination of continuous and symbolic representations is also notable, providing a flexible framework for knowledge representation and reasoning.

**Reject.** While the approach is promising and technically sound, the significant reliance on ground-truth graph state supervision at each timestep severely limits the model's broader applicability. Furthermore, the paper's density and somewhat convoluted presentation hinder its accessibility and impact.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the strong supervision requirement. The authors acknowledge this limitation themselves, but it’s a critical one. The success of the model hinges on having access to the correct graph at *every* timestep during training. This is feasible for contrived tasks like the bAbI dataset where this information can be constructed, but severely restricts its use in real-world scenarios where such ground-truth graph states are unlikely to be available. Without a mechanism to learn graph structures from raw, unstructured data, the GGT-NN remains largely confined to problem domains where the supervision bottleneck can be overcome. The demonstrations with the cellular automaton and Turing machines, while impressive, also rely on a pre-defined graphical representation. The model learns *transformations* on this pre-defined structure, not the structure itself.

Secondly, the paper is exceptionally dense and challenging to follow. The combination of numerous transformations, algorithmic details, and experimental setups makes it difficult to grasp the core concepts quickly. The notation is heavy, and the explanations often lack intuitive grounding. While thoroughness is appreciated, a more streamlined presentation, potentially deferring some details to the appendix, would significantly improve readability and allow a wider audience to appreciate the contributions. The formatting of equations is inconsistent and hinders comprehension.

However, the technical work is solid. The differentiable graph transformations are well-defined and the GGT-NN architecture appears logically sound. The experimental results, particularly on the bAbI tasks, are encouraging and demonstrate the model's capability. The comparison to other memory-based architectures is fair and highlights the potential advantages of the graph-based approach. The supplementary material detailing the transformation implementations is helpful.

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Address the Supervision Bottleneck:** A crucial next step would be to explore methods for reducing the reliance on ground-truth graph states. This could involve semi-supervised learning, incorporating unsupervised graph learning techniques, or developing strategies for inferring graph structure from raw input.
*   **Simplify the Presentation:** Prioritize clarity and conciseness. Use more intuitive explanations, illustrative diagrams, and potentially a higher-level overview of the model before diving into the technical details. Restructure the paper to improve flow and readability.
*   **Expand the Discussion of Limitations:** More thoroughly discuss the computational cost and scalability limitations of the model, particularly regarding the quadratic scaling with graph size.
*   **Provide Ablation Studies:** Investigate the individual contributions of the different graph transformations and the direct reference mechanism through ablation studies. This would provide valuable insights into the model's inner workings.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Include visualizations of the learned graph structures to provide a more intuitive understanding of how the model represents knowledge.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What are the authors’ thoughts on exploring reinforcement learning or other unsupervised methods to learn the graph structure itself, rather than relying on ground-truth supervision?
2.  Could the authors elaborate on the computational complexity of the model, specifically the memory requirements and training time, as the graph size increases? Are there potential optimizations to mitigate these issues?
3.  In the bAbI experiments, how sensitive is the performance to variations in the graphical representation of the stories? Would alternative graph encodings significantly impact the results?
4.  The paper mentions the potential for applying the GGT-NN to other tasks. Can the authors provide more specific examples and discuss the challenges associated with adapting the model to those domains?
5.  Could you clarify the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the number of layers and hidden units, and provide some justification for these settings?
