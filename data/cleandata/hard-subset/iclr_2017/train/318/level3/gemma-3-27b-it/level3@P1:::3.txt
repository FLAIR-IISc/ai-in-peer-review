## Review of LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs). The core contribution is the incorporation of differentiable graph transformations – additions, updates, propagations, and aggregations – allowing the model to learn and manipulate graph-structured representations. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's ability to learn complex reasoning by successfully solving a large majority of the bAbI tasks, and by discovering the rules governing simple cellular automata and Turing machines. The key idea is to provide the model with supervised graph states at each time step, enabling it to learn how to construct, modify, and utilize graphs as internal representations for various tasks. 

**2. Decision & Key Reasons**

**Reject.** While the ideas presented are interesting and the results are promising, the reliance on full ground-truth graph supervision at each timestep significantly limits the practical applicability of the approach, and outweighs the novel technical contributions. Additionally, the paper’s density and length make it difficult to digest for a conference format.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The main limitation lies in the required supervision. The GGT-NN necessitates a precisely defined graph state at *every* timestep during training. This severely restricts its application to scenarios where such detailed ground truth is readily available. While the bAbI tasks and rule discovery problems can be formulated this way, many real-world applications would not have this level of supervision.  The authors acknowledge this, but do not offer a convincing pathway to relax this requirement. 

Furthermore, the paper is quite dense and mathematically involved. While rigor is appreciated, the level of detail presented, coupled with the length of the paper, makes it challenging to quickly grasp the core contributions and assess their significance. This makes it less suitable for a conference presentation and more aligned with a journal publication, where a deeper, more thorough examination is expected.

The empirical results, while positive, don’t overwhelmingly demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, especially considering the strong supervision used.  The comparison to other state-of-the-art models (Table 2) reveals competitive, but not groundbreaking, performance. The advantage on tasks 19 (pathfinding) is notable, but the model struggles with task 17 (positional reasoning). The explanation provided (larger number of entities) is reasonable, but it suggests limitations in scalability.

**4. Additional Feedback & Questions**

This research has potential, and the following suggestions could improve the work:

*   **Explore Weakly Supervised or Unsupervised Learning:** Investigate methods to reduce the reliance on full graph supervision. Could the model learn to construct graphs from textual input with only query-answer pairs, or even in a self-supervised manner?  
*   **Scalability Analysis:** The performance on task 17 suggests scalability issues. Conduct a more thorough analysis of how performance degrades as the complexity (number of nodes, edges, types) of the graph increases.
*   **Ablation Studies:**  Provide a more detailed ablation study to determine the contribution of each graph transformation (node addition, edge update, etc.). Which transformations are most crucial for performance?
*   **Simplification & Clarity:**  Streamline the presentation and reduce the mathematical density. Focus on clearly articulating the core ideas and their advantages without getting lost in implementation details.
*   **Consider a more detailed comparison to related work:** The comparison to existing graph neural network architectures could be expanded. 

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What is the computational cost (time & memory) of training and running the GGT-NN compared to other methods, particularly as the graph size increases?
*   Have you explored different methods for initializing the graph states or for learning the edge and node types?
*   What specific regularization techniques were used to prevent overfitting, given the strong supervision?
*   Could you elaborate on the potential for applying the GGT-NN to real-world problems where fully supervised graph data is unavailable, such as knowledge graph construction from text?
