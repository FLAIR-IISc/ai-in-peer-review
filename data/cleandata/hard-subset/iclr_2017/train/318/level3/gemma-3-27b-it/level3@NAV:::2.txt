## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper proposes a novel approach to representing dialogue states as graphs, utilizing these graphs as a differentiable memory within a Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN). The core idea of learning and manipulating graph structures during inference, particularly with long-term learning for graph transformation parameters, is promising. This represents a first implementation of a differentiable memory represented as a graph, which is a significant step. The authors demonstrate the approach on the bAbI tasks, and also show ability to learn rules governing cellular automata and Turing machines. 

I recommend **accepting** this paper, despite the current lack of substantial performance gains on bAbI. The significance lies in the conceptual novelty and the potential of this approach to modeling complex relationships. While the performance doesn’t currently surpass existing methods, the framework itself is a valuable contribution, and the initial results are encouraging, particularly given the increased complexity. The explicit graph representation appears inherently more powerful than simpler memory structures. 

The paper is technically sound and the revised version demonstrates a considerable improvement in clarity. The authors have addressed previous concerns regarding readability. The experimental setup appears rigorous, and the inclusion of results on rule-discovery tasks strengthens the demonstration of the method’s capabilities beyond simple question answering. The authors clearly position their work within the existing landscape of memory networks and graph neural networks, highlighting its unique contributions. 

However, the paper would benefit from further refinement. The complexity of the approach is a double-edged sword – while it offers flexibility, it also introduces challenges in interpretability and scalability. Additional analysis exploring the learned graph structures would be valuable. For instance, visualizing the graphs generated during bAbI tasks could provide insight into how the model represents and reasons about the information. Furthermore, a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of different components (e.g., direct reference, propagation steps) would strengthen the findings.

I have a few questions for the authors:

1.  **Scalability:** How does the computational cost of this approach scale with the size and complexity of the input graph? What strategies could be employed to mitigate the quadratic scaling issue mentioned in the conclusion?
2.  **Graph Structure Analysis:** Can you provide examples of the learned graph structures for the bAbI tasks, and discuss how these structures relate to the underlying semantics of the tasks? What features of the graph structure appear to be most important for performance?
3. **Generalization:** Although the rule-discovery tasks show impressive results, how well does the model generalize to *new* rules within the same domain (e.g., a different cellular automaton rule)? Are there any observed limitations in the model’s ability to learn and generalize new rules?
