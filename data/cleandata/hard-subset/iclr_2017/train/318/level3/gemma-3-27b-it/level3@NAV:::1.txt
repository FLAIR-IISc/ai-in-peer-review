## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

This paper presents a novel approach to graph-structured data processing, introducing differentiable graph transformations within a Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN). The core idea of learning a cellular automaton and representing data as evolving graphs is compelling, and framing this as a sequence of graphs with node/edge manipulations offers a flexible framework. The combination of these ideas with prior GGS-NN work to achieve strong performance on the bAbI tasks is a significant contribution.

I **reject** this paper, primarily due to concerns regarding the empirical validation and the significant gap between the promise of the approach and the demonstrated results on more complex tasks. While bAbI performance is impressive, the Rule 30 experiment, presented as a proof-of-concept for learning complex rules, yielded limited success, raising questions about the model’s ability to generalize to truly challenging graph manipulation tasks. 

The paper adequately motivates the approach, building upon established work in GNNs, GGS-NNs, and sequence modeling. The idea of using differentiable graph transformations to enable gradient-based learning of graph-to-graph mappings is a logical extension of existing techniques. The authors correctly identify the potential of this approach for tasks involving dynamic state representation. However, the support for claims regarding the ability to *learn* complex update rules feels weak. The Rule 30 experiment, which should represent a relatively simple rule learning task, falling far short of a solution diminishes confidence in the broader applicability of the proposed architecture. Furthermore, the improvement on bAbI tasks, while positive, doesn’t necessarily prove the effectiveness of the graph transformations themselves – it's possible the model is succeeding due to the strength of the sequence modeling component. The comparison to other models on bAbI is helpful, but the lack of comparable results on tasks explicitly designed to test graph transformation learning is a significant drawback.

Additional feedback: 

*   **Ablation studies:** It would be beneficial to include ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the different graph transformations and the direct reference mechanism. 
*   **Hyperparameter sensitivity:** The paper should discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameter settings, particularly those related to the graph transformations.
*   **Visualization of learned graphs:** Providing visualizations of the learned graph structures (particularly for Rule 30 and the Turing machine) would help to understand what the model is actually learning.
*   **Scalability:** The discussion of computational complexity is good, but a more detailed analysis of scalability, potentially with experiments on larger graphs, would be valuable.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors:

*   What specific metrics were used to evaluate performance on Rule 30, and what constitutes "far from solved" in quantitative terms?
*   Were any attempts made to increase the complexity of the Rule 30 experiment (e.g., larger initial states, longer simulation lengths) to assess the limitations of the model?
*   Can you elaborate on the choice of network architecture (number of layers, hidden units) and optimization parameters used for each experiment?
*   How does the performance of the GGT-NN compare to a standard sequence-to-sequence model when the input is already pre-processed into a graph representation for the bAbI tasks? This would help to isolate the benefits of learning the graph transformations directly from text.
