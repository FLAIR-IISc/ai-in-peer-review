This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that allows for learning and manipulating graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The core idea is to introduce differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – that can be combined to construct and modify graphs based on input, and subsequently use these graphs to produce outputs. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton (Rule 30), and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex relationships and rules.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures and demonstrates promising results on several tasks, the experimental validation is not sufficiently convincing, and the presentation lacks clarity in key areas, hindering a thorough assessment of the contributions.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The central innovation – differentiable graph transformations – is conceptually appealing. Allowing the model to *learn* the graph structure rather than relying on predefined or hand-engineered graphs opens up possibilities for applying graph-based reasoning to a wider range of problems. The reported performance on the bAbI tasks is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, although the paper acknowledges the strong supervision used. However, the results on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks are less compelling. The paper states that the model “successfully learned” these tasks, but provides limited quantitative results. The provided table only gives accuracy and fails to provide any insight into the quality of the learned rules. Did the model perfectly replicate the behavior of Rule 30, or did it approximate it with some error? Similarly, for the Turing machine, what types of machines could the model learn, and how complex were these machines? Without this information, it’s difficult to gauge the true capabilities of the model.

Furthermore, the paper lacks a detailed ablation study. While the authors compare results with and without “direct reference,” a more comprehensive analysis of the contribution of each graph transformation would be valuable. For example, what happens if the edge update transformation is removed? Does the model still learn effectively? This information would help to understand which components are crucial for the model’s performance.

The paper's presentation could be improved. The notation is dense, and the descriptions of the transformations are somewhat convoluted. Figures 1 and 2, intended to provide a high-level overview, are difficult to interpret. More intuitive visualizations and clearer explanations would significantly enhance the paper’s readability.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Rule 30 and Turing Machine Evaluation:** Please provide more detailed and quantitative evaluation of the performance on the Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks. Show examples of learned graphs, compare the model’s output to the expected output, and report metrics beyond simple accuracy.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study to assess the contribution of each graph transformation.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed model, particularly concerning the quadratic scaling with graph size mentioned in the conclusion. How does this limit the scalability of the approach?
*   **Comparison with Existing Work:** The comparison with related work is adequate but could be more nuanced. Specifically, elaborate on how the proposed approach differs from differentiable neural computers (DNCs) in terms of representational capacity and computational efficiency.
*    **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters. Were any particular parameters critical for achieving good performance?
*   **Visualization:** Include more illustrative visualizations of the learned graphs, especially for the Rule 30 and Turing machine experiments. This would help the reader to understand how the model is representing the problem internally.
*   **Algorithm 1:** The notation in Algorithm 1, particularly the use of `i(k)`, `D(k)`, and the query-related variables, is unclear. Explain these variables in more detail.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the limitations observed with Task 17 in the bAbI experiments? Is it a data issue, a model capacity issue, or something else?
2.  Regarding the Turing machine experiments, what is the maximum complexity of the Turing machines the model was able to learn successfully? What specific rulesets were used for evaluation?
3.  How does the performance of the GGT-NN compare to GGS-NNs when the input is *already* in graph form? Are there any scenarios where GGT-NN consistently outperforms GGS-NN even without the need to learn the graph structure?
4.  The paper mentions that the direct reference input significantly improves performance. What is the rationale behind this improvement? Is it simply providing more information, or is there a more fundamental reason related to the graph structure?
5.  Could you elaborate on the choice of GRU cells within the graph transformations? Were other recurrent network architectures (e.g., LSTMs) considered, and if so, what were the results?
