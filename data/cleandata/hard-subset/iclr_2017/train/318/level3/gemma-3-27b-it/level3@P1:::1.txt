## Review of LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of previous work on Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs). The key contribution is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – that allow the model to dynamically construct and modify graph-structured representations. This enables learning tasks that require manipulating graph structure, such as modeling cellular automata and Turing machines, as well as improving performance on tasks like bAbI question answering by leveraging graph-based internal states. The authors demonstrate that the GGT-NN successfully learns to solve almost all bAbI tasks and can learn the rules governing simple cellular automata and Turing machines.

**2. Decision**

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting idea and demonstrates some promising results, the claims of success, particularly regarding the Turing machine and cellular automaton tasks, are not adequately supported by the presented evidence. The results on bAbI are competitive, but the novelty in that context feels incremental given existing strong baselines.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary reason for rejection stems from a lack of convincing evidence for the more ambitious claims regarding learning complex dynamical systems. The paper states the model "successfully learns" these systems but provides minimal quantitative analysis. A visual inspection of the learned graph sequence (Figure 3) is insufficient to demonstrate successful learning of the underlying rules.  A quantitative measure of prediction accuracy for these tasks – for instance, the percentage of correctly predicted cell states in the cellular automaton, or correctly predicted tape configurations for the Turing machine – is crucial.  Without such metrics, it's difficult to assess whether the model is truly learning the rules or simply memorizing training examples. 

Furthermore, the ablation study and analysis of the graph transformation components are limited. The impact of each transformation, and their interactions, isn’t thoroughly explored. It’s unclear which transformations are most critical for performance and whether simpler models lacking certain transformations would be sufficient.  The authors mention that direct reference significantly improved performance, but more detailed analysis is needed to understand *why* this is the case.

The experimental setup also lacks detail. Information regarding hyperparameter tuning, network architecture specifics (e.g., hidden layer sizes, number of GRU units), and training procedures is not always comprehensive. This makes it difficult to reproduce the results and assess the robustness of the findings.

**4. Additional Feedback**

The idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is promising and could open up new avenues for research in areas like program synthesis and dynamic system modeling. Here are some suggestions to improve the paper:

*   **Quantitative Evaluation of Dynamical Systems:**  Provide quantitative metrics for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks. Report prediction accuracy, loss values, or other relevant metrics to demonstrate learning.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to assess the contribution of each graph transformation.  Systematically remove or modify transformations and measure the impact on performance.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameters.
*   **Visualization & Interpretability:** Provide more detailed visualizations of the learned graph structures, particularly for the Turing machine and cellular automaton tasks.  Attempt to interpret the learned rules.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:**  Include more relevant baselines for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks. Are there existing neural network approaches specifically designed for these problems?
*   **Clarify the Graph Structure:**  Provide a more formal definition of the graph structure used in the experiments.  What exactly do the nodes and edges represent?
*  **Algorithm 1 & 2:** Algorithm 1 seems to imply that all transformations occur at each timestep. Is this true? If the operations for each step are performed sequentially, it may be useful to clarify this.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide quantitative results (e.g., prediction accuracy) for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks?
*   What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to existing approaches for similar tasks?
*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the initial graph structure? Does it consistently converge to the correct graph representation, or is it susceptible to getting stuck in local optima?
*   What are the limitations of the proposed approach? Are there types of graph transformations or tasks for which it is likely to struggle?
*   What were the hyperparameters used for the training of the Turing Machine and Cellular Automaton?
