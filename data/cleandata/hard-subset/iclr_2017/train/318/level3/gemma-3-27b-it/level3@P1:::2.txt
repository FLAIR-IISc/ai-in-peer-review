## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

**1. Summary of Claims**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs). The key contribution is the ability to learn and manipulate graph-structured intermediate representations during processing. The model dynamically constructs and modifies graphs based on textual input and utilizes these graphs to produce outputs. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capability to solve bAbI tasks, a standard benchmark for question answering, as well as to learn the rules governing simple cellular automata and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex state transitions. The model’s core novelty lies in its differentiable graph manipulation, enabling end-to-end training of the entire system.

**2. Decision**

**Accept.** Despite not achieving state-of-the-art performance on the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN presents a novel and promising approach to modeling sequential data with dynamically constructed graph representations. The technical soundness of the approach and the potential for future development outweigh the current performance limitations. 

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper addresses a significant problem in AI: representing and reasoning about stateful information. Existing methods often rely on fixed or pre-defined memory structures. The GGT-NN distinguishes itself by *learning* a suitable graph representation from the input, offering greater flexibility and potentially more expressive power. The ability to learn graph transformations is particularly compelling, as it allows the model to adapt its internal state representation based on the input and the task at hand. 

The experimental results, while not exceeding the performance of established methods, demonstrate the GGT-NN’s ability to learn complex tasks such as simulating cellular automata and Turing machines. This indicates the model’s capacity for learning complex rules and internal state dynamics. The performance on bAbI tasks is competitive with strong baselines, and the comparison against memory networks highlights the potential benefits of the graph-based representation, particularly for tasks requiring relational reasoning. The ablation studies with and without direct reference provide valuable insights into the importance of different components of the model. 

The paper is well-written and technically sound. The authors provide a clear explanation of the model architecture and the graph transformations, along with detailed implementation details. The included appendices enhance reproducibility and provide further clarity.  

**4. Additional Feedback**

While the paper is strong, several areas could be improved:

*   **Performance Analysis:** A more thorough analysis of *why* the GGT-NN doesn’t outperform existing methods on bAbI would be beneficial. Is it due to the complexity of the model, the training procedure, or inherent limitations in the graph representation?
*   **Scalability:** The quadratic scaling of the model with graph size is a significant concern. Exploring techniques to mitigate this, such as sparse connections or graph summarization, would be valuable.
*   **Generalization:** The Turing machine experiments are encouraging, but the scope is limited. Evaluating the model on a wider range of rule-based tasks and datasets would provide further evidence of its generalization ability.
*   **Visualization:** Providing more visual examples of the learned graphs, especially during the bAbI tasks, would help to understand how the model is representing the information and making its decisions.
*   **Comparison to Knowledge Graphs:** Discussing the relationship between the learned graphs and established knowledge graph representations could be insightful.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   The paper mentions that the performance on task 17 (Positional Reasoning) was poor. Could you elaborate on the specific challenges this task presents to the GGT-NN architecture? Did you experiment with different graph structures or training parameters specifically for this task?
*   The implementation details mention that the direct reference input matrix `D(k)` is created by summing partial representation vectors. Is this the only method you experimented with to incorporate direct references? Could other methods, such as attention mechanisms directly applied to the relevant nodes, be more effective?
*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of the activation functions and hyperparameters within the GRU cells and graph transformations? Were extensive hyperparameter tuning experiments conducted?
*   The appendix mentions that the code for the model is available. Are pre-trained models also available for reproducibility and further research?
*   Given the computational cost, are there any planned investigations into parallelization or distributed training strategies for GGT-NNs?
