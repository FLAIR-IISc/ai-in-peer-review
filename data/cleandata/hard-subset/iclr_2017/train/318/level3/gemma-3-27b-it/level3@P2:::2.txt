This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel approach to representing and manipulating information using graph structures. The model extends prior work on Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by allowing for the dynamic construction and modification of graphs based on textual input, enabling the use of these graphs for both intermediate representation and final output. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex rules and solve reasoning problems.

I recommend **accepting** this paper. While the performance gains on the bAbI tasks are not dramatic compared to strong baselines like Memory Networks, the introduction of a differentiable, dynamically constructed graph-based memory representation is a significant contribution with the potential to unlock new capabilities in areas requiring structured reasoning and knowledge representation. The technical soundness of the approach and the thorough experimental evaluation, despite modest performance gains, justify acceptance.

The core strength of this work lies in its innovative approach to representing state. Existing methods, such as memory networks and differentiable neural computers, rely on less structured forms of memory. GGT-NN’s explicit graph representation allows for more intuitive reasoning about relationships between entities and offers a potential pathway towards interpretability. The ability to *learn* the graph structure itself from textual input, rather than relying on pre-defined knowledge graphs, is a particularly compelling aspect of this work. The demonstrated success in learning the rules governing a cellular automaton and Turing machines, where structural relationships are crucial, is a strong indicator of the model’s potential. 

The paper is well-written and presents a clear explanation of the GGT-NN architecture and its components, including differentiable graph transformations. The experimental setup is comprehensive, including comparisons with several established baselines on the bAbI tasks, and experiments demonstrating the model’s ability to learn complex dynamic systems. The inclusion of ablation studies (direct reference vs. no direct reference) provides valuable insight into the contribution of different components of the model. The detailed appendix provides necessary implementation details and clarifies complex components. 

However, the paper could benefit from further discussion of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN. The quadratic scaling with graph size is a potential limitation, and exploring methods to mitigate this issue (e.g., sparse graph representations) would strengthen the work. Additionally, while the bAbI tasks provide a good initial testbed, exploring more complex reasoning tasks and datasets would further demonstrate the GGT-NN's advantages. The reviewer notes the author’s acknowledgement of this limitation.

To further strengthen the paper, I have the following questions for the authors:

1. **Complexity Mitigation:** Beyond mentioning sparse graph representations, are there any specific techniques the authors are considering to address the quadratic computational complexity of the GGT-NN, especially as the graph size increases?
2. **Generalization to Larger Graphs:** The success on the Turing machine task is encouraging. Could the authors comment on the limitations of the model when applied to more complex Turing machines with larger state spaces and tapes? What modifications might be necessary to handle such scenarios?
3. **Interpretability:** While the graph structure offers a degree of interpretability, can the authors provide examples of how the learned graph structures align with human intuition regarding the underlying tasks (e.g., bAbI tasks, cellular automaton)? Visualizations of learned graphs would be highly valuable.
4. **Comparison to Rule Induction:** The reviewer notes that the performance on bAbI is worse than traditional rule induction methods. Could the authors elaborate on why this might be the case, and what modifications to the GGT-NN might be necessary to achieve comparable performance? Is the flexibility of learning the graph structure a trade-off against the efficiency of direct rule induction?
5. **Direct Reference Importance:** The ablation studies demonstrate the importance of direct reference. How sensitive is the model to errors or ambiguities in the direct reference mechanism? Would a more robust, but potentially less precise, reference mechanism impact performance?
