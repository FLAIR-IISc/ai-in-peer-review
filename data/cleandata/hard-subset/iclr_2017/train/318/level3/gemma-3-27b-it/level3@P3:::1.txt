This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by utilizing graphs as intermediate representations for learning graph-to-graph tasks. The core idea is to learn differentiable graph transformations – additions, updates, propagations, and aggregations – allowing the network to construct and modify graphs based on input, and subsequently generate outputs from these graphs. The authors demonstrate the model's capability on bAbI tasks, achieving state-of-the-art results, and on learning simple cellular automata and Turing machines, showcasing its potential for modeling complex rule-based systems.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a novel and compelling approach to graph-based learning. The idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is a significant contribution, offering a flexible framework for tasks requiring dynamic graph manipulation. The strong performance on the bAbI tasks, and the demonstration of learning basic computational rules (cellular automaton, Turing machines) provide solid evidence for the approach’s effectiveness.

**Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The introduction of differentiable graph transformations is a valuable addition to the field of graph neural networks. Existing methods typically operate on fixed graph structures. The GGT-NN allows for *learning* the graph structure itself, increasing its adaptability.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The model’s performance on the bAbI tasks is impressive, surpassing existing state-of-the-art methods. The consistent high accuracy across many tasks demonstrates the model's generalizability.
* **Demonstrated Learning Capability:** The ability of the GGT-NN to learn the rules of a cellular automaton and Turing machine (even if Rule 30 wasn't fully solved) is a strong indicator of the model’s ability to represent and manipulate complex relationships, going beyond simple pattern recognition.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the core concepts and methodology. The appendix provides detailed information about the transformations, enhancing reproducibility.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Rule 30 Performance Discussion:** The paper mentions that the Rule 30 experiment remained far from solved. Providing more detail about the observed behavior of the model during training and the potential reasons for this limitation would be valuable. What *did* the model learn? What specific difficulties did it encounter?
* **Scalability Concerns:** The paper acknowledges the quadratic scaling of computation with graph size. Further discussion of potential optimizations, beyond those mentioned in the conclusion (sparse connections, partial processing), would be beneficial. Could techniques like graph coarsening or attention mechanisms be employed to address this issue?
* **Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate the power of direct reference, more detailed ablation studies could help pinpoint its exact contribution. For example, how does performance degrade with varying degrees of noise in the direct reference signal?
* **Comparison to GGS-NN:** Although the paper builds on GGS-NNs, a more direct comparative analysis – perhaps using the same experimental setup on a common task – would highlight the specific advantages of the GGT-NN approach. What does the GGT-NN offer that the GGS-NN lacks?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the Rule 30 experiment: Can you describe the observed behavior of the model during training? Did it learn to approximate certain patterns or aspects of the rule, or did it struggle to converge even to a simple approximation? Providing visualizations of the learned graphs would be very insightful.
2.  The paper mentions the quadratic scaling of computation. Have you explored any methods for mitigating this scalability issue during experimentation? If so, what were the results? If not, are there specific architectural choices that you believe would be most promising for improving scalability?
3.  How sensitive is the performance to the initial conditions (e.g., the random initialization of weights) and hyperparameter settings? Were extensive hyperparameter searches performed for each task, and if so, can you provide some insights into the optimal settings?
4.  In the extended model with graph sequence input (Appendix D), you observed slightly inferior performance compared to the original model. Do you have any hypotheses about the reason for this, beyond the potential for overfitting? Perhaps the recurrent component used to combine the graph representations is a bottleneck?
5.  Could you elaborate on the choice of activation functions and network architectures within the individual graph transformations? Was this determined empirically, or are there theoretical reasons for preferring certain choices over others?
