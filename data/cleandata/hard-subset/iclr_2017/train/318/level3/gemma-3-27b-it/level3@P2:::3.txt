This paper proposes the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that utilizes graph-structured data as an intermediate, learnable representation. The core contribution lies in defining a set of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – allowing the model to construct and modify graphs based on input, and subsequently use these graphs for tasks like question answering. The authors demonstrate the model’s efficacy on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, achieving strong performance, particularly on tasks where a graphical representation is naturally suited.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents a technically interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures, the heavy reliance on complete graph-level supervision significantly limits the broader applicability and impact of the proposed method.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary reason for rejection is the strong supervisory signal required for training. The model necessitates ground-truth graphs at *each timestep* during training. This level of supervision is unrealistic for many real-world scenarios where such detailed graph structures are unavailable. The authors acknowledge this limitation but do not offer convincing arguments or explorations of alternative training methodologies, such as weakly supervised or unsupervised learning approaches, to mitigate it. While the results on the bAbI tasks are impressive, the performance gains are partially attributable to this strong supervision, making it difficult to assess the true generalization capability of the learned graph transformations.

Furthermore, the paper's density and extensive detail hinder readability. The numerous equations, transformation descriptions, and experimental setups require significant effort from the reviewer to fully grasp. While thoroughness is appreciated, a more concise presentation focusing on the core innovations and key results would improve accessibility. The organization, while logically structured, could benefit from more clearly delineated sections and a greater emphasis on intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical formulations. The extensive appendix material, while helpful for detail, suggests the content might be more appropriate for a journal publication.

The comparison to related work is comprehensive, correctly positioning GGT-NNs relative to existing graph neural networks, memory networks, and differentiable neural computers. However, it largely frames the advantages of GGT-NNs in terms of its explicit graph representation, which is somewhat diminished by the need for ground-truth graphs during training. The assertion that the distributed nature of the model is a disadvantage (in terms of computational cost) is a valid point, but could be further explored with potential optimization strategies.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Explore Weak Supervision:** Investigate techniques for training the model with weaker forms of supervision. Could the model be pre-trained on unlabeled data to learn initial graph structures, followed by fine-tuning with limited labeled examples? Or could contrastive learning be employed to encourage meaningful graph representations?
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to systematically evaluate the contribution of each graph transformation. This would help identify which transformations are most critical for performance and justify their inclusion in the model.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the model’s scalability. How does the computational cost scale with graph size and the number of nodes? Are there strategies to mitigate the quadratic complexity?
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Include a qualitative analysis of the learned graph structures. Visualizing the graphs generated by the model could provide valuable insights into how it represents and reasons about the input data. Examples of learned graphs from the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks would be particularly illuminating.
*   **Simplify Presentation:** Streamline the presentation by focusing on the core contributions and key results. Reduce the level of mathematical detail where possible, and prioritize intuitive explanations. Consider moving some of the more technical details to the appendix.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of training and inference for a graph of size *n*? What are the practical limitations on the size of graphs the model can handle?
*   Have you explored any alternative training strategies to reduce the reliance on complete graph-level supervision? If so, what were the results?
*   Could you provide examples of the learned graph structures for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks? This would help to understand how the model represents the underlying dynamics of these systems.
*   How sensitive is the model to the initial graph structure (if any) provided as input? Could the model learn meaningful representations starting from a completely empty graph?
