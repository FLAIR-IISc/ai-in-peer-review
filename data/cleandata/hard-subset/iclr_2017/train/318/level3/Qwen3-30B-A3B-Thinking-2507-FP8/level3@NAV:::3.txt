

# Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that learns complex, differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The authors propose five differentiable transformation types (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the model to iteratively construct and modify graphs based on textual input. The approach enables the model to maintain evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable by gradient descent, effectively mixing continuous and symbolic representations. The authors demonstrate promising results on bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks) and successfully train the model to discover rules for cellular automata and Turing machines.

I recommend rejection. The primary reason is the model's fundamental reliance on ground-truth graph states at each timestep for training, which severely limits its applicability to real-world tasks where such supervision is unavailable. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they do not adequately address how to overcome it, making the approach impractical for most realistic applications. Second, the paper is dense and technically complex, with many mathematical details that would be better suited for a journal publication rather than a conference, hindering readability for a broader audience.

The empirical results on the bAbI tasks are compelling, but the strong supervision requirement significantly undermines the model's practical contribution. The comparison with existing memory networks and neural Turing machines is well-placed, but the paper lacks a clear discussion of how the GGT-NN model would generalize to tasks without explicit graph supervision.

To improve the paper, the authors should: (1) provide a more thorough discussion of how to reduce supervision requirements, (2) simplify the presentation for better accessibility, and (3) include a more detailed analysis of computational complexity. The paper would benefit from a clearer statement of the specific problem the model solves that cannot be addressed by existing approaches.

I would like to ask the authors: How would you adapt this model to handle tasks where ground-truth graph states are unavailable, such as real-world question-answering on unstructured text? Additionally, could you provide a more concrete example of a practical application where the GGT-NN's ability to learn graph transformations would be essential compared to other approaches?
