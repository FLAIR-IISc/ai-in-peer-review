

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that allows for the construction and modification of graph-structured intermediate representations. The key contribution is a framework that enables neural networks to learn to build and manipulate graphs based on textual input, rather than requiring a fixed graph structure as input. The authors demonstrate this approach on the bAbI tasks (achieving 95% accuracy on most tasks) and on rule discovery tasks like cellular automata and Turing machines. The paper makes a compelling case for graph-structured memory as a useful representation for modeling relationships between entities, and provides a technically sound framework for differentiable graph transformations.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a novel approach to graph-based neural networks that extends previous work in meaningful ways, and the results demonstrate the viability of the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in neural networks for structured data: the ability to construct graph representations from unstructured input rather than relying on fixed graph structures. The GGT-NN framework is well-motivated by the observation that many data structures naturally form graphs (linked lists, binary trees, relationships between entities), and the authors provide a technically sound framework for learning to build and modify these graphs.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-presented. The model achieves 95% accuracy on most bAbI tasks, comparable to state-of-the-art memory networks (like MemNN and DMN+), and demonstrates the ability to learn complex rule sets for cellular automata and Turing machines. The authors provide careful ablation studies (comparing with and without direct reference) and thoroughly analyze the limitations of their approach.

The paper is a constructive contribution to the field, as it represents the first implementation of a differentiable graph memory that can be learned from unstructured input. While the performance on bAbI tasks is comparable to existing memory networks (not clearly superior), the paper makes a strong case for the value of graph representations in specific domains (like pathfinding tasks, where GGT-NN significantly outperforms other methods).

## 4. Additional Feedback

The paper would be strengthened by:
1. A more detailed comparison of computational complexity and training time with existing methods, as the quadratic complexity of the graph operations is acknowledged but not thoroughly analyzed in terms of practical implications.
2. A more comprehensive discussion of when graph representations are preferable to other structured representations (like stacks or trees), including concrete examples where the graph approach clearly outperforms alternatives.
3. Clarification on the exact nature of the "graph-level supervision" used in training - it's not entirely clear how much of the graph structure is provided versus learned by the model.
4. More detailed analysis of the failure case (task 17, Positional Reasoning) - why does the model struggle with this task when it works well on others?

## 5. Clarifying Questions

1. The paper states that "the GGT-NN model was trained with full graph information" for bAbI tasks. Could you clarify exactly what aspects of the graph structure were provided as supervision (e.g., node types, edge types, node strengths) versus what was learned by the model?

2. For the rule discovery tasks (cellular automata and Turing machines), the paper states that "no additional information was provided as textual input." Could you elaborate on how the model was able to learn the rules without any explicit textual description of the rules, and how this differs from standard rule induction approaches?

3. The paper mentions that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks." Could you provide more detail on the specific tasks where direct reference made the most significant difference, and why?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the field of neural networks for structured data. The paper is technically accurate, well-organized, and makes a compelling case for the value of graph-structured intermediate representations. The results are promising and open up new possibilities for neural network architectures that can learn to represent relationships between entities in a more flexible way.
