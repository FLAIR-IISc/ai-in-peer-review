

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending GGS-NNs to learn differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The authors propose five graph transformation types (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to iteratively construct and modify graph structures based on sequential input. The model demonstrates strong performance on the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks) and successfully learns rules for cellular automata and Turing machines. The key contribution is a framework that maintains evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable through gradient descent.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: First, the paper makes a significant methodological contribution by extending graph neural networks to learn dynamic graph structures rather than just processing fixed ones, addressing an important gap in the literature. Second, the empirical results are compelling, with the GGT-NN model outperforming strong baselines on several bAbI tasks, particularly those naturally suited to graphical representations (like pathfinding). Third, the authors provide thorough ablation studies showing the importance of direct reference mechanisms and intermediate propagation for specific tasks.

The paper supports its claims through rigorous experiments across multiple domains. The bAbI results are well-analyzed with appropriate baselines (including LSTM, Memory Networks, and Neural Turing Machines), and the authors demonstrate generalization to new task variants (e.g., longer cellular automaton sequences). The graph transformation details are well-specified with clear pseudocode and implementation details in the appendix. The comparison with related work is comprehensive, highlighting how GGT-NNs differ from existing approaches like Memory Networks and DNCs by explicitly modeling internal states as graphs rather than implicit vector representations.

However, the paper has two significant limitations. First, the model requires ground-truth graph states at each timestep for training, which limits applicability to tasks where such supervision is available. While the authors acknowledge this limitation, they don't sufficiently address how to overcome it in future work. Second, the paper is dense and technical, with complex graph transformations described in detail that may hinder readability for a conference audience. The content would be better suited for a journal publication where the technical depth could be more fully appreciated.

To improve the paper, I recommend: (1) Adding a more thorough discussion of how to reduce the supervision requirement, perhaps through semi-supervised learning approaches or regularization techniques; (2) Simplifying some of the technical descriptions to improve readability without sacrificing necessary detail; (3) Including visualizations of the graph representations learned by the model to help readers understand the internal representations.

I have two key questions for the authors: First, could the model learn the graph structure from scratch without ground-truth supervision at each timestep, or is this supervision truly necessary? If the latter, what would be a reasonable path toward reducing this requirement? Second, the paper mentions that the GGT-NN can process data in a distributed fashion, but this advantage comes with quadratic computational complexity. Could the authors comment on potential approaches to improve efficiency for larger graphs, such as using sparse connections or selective node processing?
