

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a significant extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that learns complex, differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The authors propose five differentiable transformation types (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be learned in a supervised setting with graph states provided at each timestep. This approach enables the model to iteratively construct and modify graphs based on textual input, which is then used to solve various tasks. The paper demonstrates promising results on the bAbI question-answering tasks, achieving 95% accuracy on most tasks (11 reaching 100%), and successfully models a cellular automaton and Turing machines. The key contribution is maintaining evolving graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable via gradient descent, effectively mixing continuous and symbolic representations in a more general framework than fixed-shape memory models.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of graph-based neural networks by introducing a novel framework for learning differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The approach is well-motivated, technically sound, and supported by compelling empirical results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in neural network architectures: extending GGS-NNs to learn complex graph transformations as internal representations rather than just using them as input. The approach is well-positioned within the literature, clearly distinguishing itself from related work such as Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines, and Differentiable Neural Computers by explicitly modeling internal states as graphs with labeled nodes and edges.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The GGT-NN achieves 95% accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks (11 at 100% accuracy), outperforming strong baselines on several tasks, particularly those suited to graph representations like pathfinding (Task 19). The comparison with other models is fair, noting that both GGT-NN and MemNN were trained with strong supervision (graph information for GGT-NN, relevant sentence information for MemNN), while others were trained end-to-end. The rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) further demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex graph transformations from unstructured input.

The technical contributions are solid, with the five differentiable transformations being novel and well-motivated. The framework's ability to mix continuous and symbolic representations while maintaining full differentiability is a significant advantage over fixed-structure memory models.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper makes a strong contribution, it would benefit from several improvements to enhance accessibility and impact:

1. The paper is dense and would be more suitable for a journal than a conference. Consider adding more intuitive explanations and visualizations to clarify the graph transformation concepts, particularly for readers unfamiliar with GGS-NNs.

2. The limitation of requiring ground-truth graph states at each timestep is acknowledged but could be more thoroughly discussed. The authors could explore potential solutions for reducing this supervision requirement, such as semi-supervised approaches or leveraging the model's ability to learn from partial graph information.

3. The computational complexity of the model (quadratic in graph size) is mentioned but could be better contextualized with comparisons to other architectures. A brief discussion of how the trade-offs between expressiveness and efficiency might affect practical applications would be valuable.

4. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is helpful but could be made more intuitive by adding a simple example of how the transformations interact during processing.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

1. The paper states that "the only task that the GGT-NN was unable to solve with 95% accuracy was task 17 (Positional Reasoning)." Could the authors elaborate on why this particular task was more challenging, and whether the graph representation could be modified to better handle the increased entity complexity?

2. The paper mentions that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks." Could the authors provide more insight into why direct reference was so impactful, and whether this could be achieved through alternative mechanisms without explicit supervision?

3. The paper states that "the GGT-NN model was trained using only 1,000 training examples" for bAbI tasks. How does the performance scale with more training data, and what is the point of diminishing returns for this model on these tasks?
