

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that enables neural networks to learn to construct and modify graphs as intermediate representations based on textual input. The key contribution is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to incrementally build and modify graph structures. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on two distinct problem domains: (1) the bAbI question-answering tasks, where the model uses a graph as an intermediate representation to answer questions about textual stories, and (2) rule discovery tasks involving cellular automata and Turing machines, where the model learns to predict graph transformations without textual input. The paper makes a compelling case for the utility of graph-structured intermediate representations, showing that the GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks, outperforming several state-of-the-art models on specific tasks where graphical representations are particularly advantageous.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural networks for structured data by enabling graph-structured intermediate representations rather than just graph-structured input. The empirical results on the bAbI tasks are strong and well-supported, demonstrating the practical value of the proposed approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a clear gap in the literature: most existing graph neural network approaches take a graph as input and produce an output, but cannot learn to construct or modify graphs based on unstructured input. The GGT-NN model directly addresses this limitation through its differentiable graph transformations, which are well-motivated and logically organized.

The empirical results on the bAbI tasks provide strong evidence for the model's effectiveness. The paper demonstrates that the GGT-NN model with direct reference achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks (compared to only 9/20 without direct reference), with 11 tasks reaching 100% accuracy. The analysis of task-specific performance (particularly on pathfinding and induction tasks) provides compelling evidence that graphical representations are well-suited for these particular types of reasoning. The comparison with existing state-of-the-art models further strengthens the argument, showing that the GGT-NN outperforms several models on tasks where graphical representations are naturally advantageous.

The model's ability to learn complex update rules for cellular automata and Turing machines also demonstrates the generality of the approach, though the Rule 30 results (which were "far from solved" according to the key points) could be more thoroughly discussed.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. Providing more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the model. The paper mentions that the model's time and space requirements increase quadratically with graph size, but it would be valuable to see concrete examples of how this affects performance on larger graphs or longer sequences.

2. Including a more detailed discussion of the Rule 30 results. The paper states that the model "successfully learned the assigned tasks," but the key points indicate the Rule 30 experiment "remained far from solved." Clarifying the exact accuracy achieved and providing insights into why this task might be more challenging than the bAbI tasks would strengthen the paper.

3. Adding a brief comparison of training time and resource requirements with other architectures (like DNCs or memory networks) to better contextualize the trade-offs between the model's flexibility and computational efficiency.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the GGT-NN model "successfully learned the assigned tasks" for both Rule 30 and Turing machines, but the key points indicate the Rule 30 experiment "remained far from solved." Could you clarify the exact accuracy achieved on Rule 30, and what specific challenges the model faced with this task compared to the bAbI tasks?

2. Given the quadratic complexity of the propagation step, how do you envision scaling this model to handle significantly larger graphs (e.g., with thousands of nodes) that might be needed for real-world applications? Are there specific techniques you're considering to address this scalability issue?

3. The paper mentions that "the model with direct reference greatly improves the training of the model." Could you provide more insight into why direct reference is so beneficial, and whether this approach could be applied to other graph-based architectures beyond GGT-NNs?
