

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends previous graph neural network approaches (GGS-NNs) by enabling the learning of graph-to-graph transformations through gradient descent. The key innovation is the introduction of differential graph transformations that allow the model to construct and modify graph structures based on textual input. The authors frame this as learning a cellular automaton represented by sequences of growing graphs with neighbor pointers and special 0/1 value nodes. The model can learn to generate the graph sequence from textual input and then use the graph to produce textual outputs. The paper demonstrates that this approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods on the bAbI question-answering tasks, achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks, and shows promising results on learning Rule 30 cellular automaton and Turing machine rules, though the Rule 30 task remains challenging.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** the paper.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature by enabling neural networks to learn to construct and modify graph structures rather than relying on fixed graph inputs. This is particularly valuable for tasks where the graph structure itself is part of the solution (like pathfinding in bAbI task 19).

The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The paper provides a thorough comparison with related work (GNNs, GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, etc.), clearly identifying how GGT-NNs differ and what advantages they offer. The authors make a strong case for the value of explicitly modeling the internal state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges, which allows for interpretability and potential use in downstream applications requiring graph-structured inputs.

The paper provides scientifically rigorous empirical validation. The bAbI task results are compelling, with the model outperforming strong baselines on multiple tasks, particularly those that benefit from graph representations (like pathfinding). The Rule 30 and Turing machine experiments demonstrate the model's capability to learn complex state transition rules, though the Rule 30 results show room for improvement. The authors carefully document their experimental setup, including the graph representations for each task, and provide appropriate baselines for comparison.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The Rule 30 experiment's limitations should be discussed more thoroughly. The paper notes it "remained far from solved," but doesn't analyze why this might be the case. A brief discussion of potential reasons (e.g., complexity of the transition rules, limitations in the graph representation) would strengthen the paper.

2. The computational complexity discussion (Section 6) is good, but the paper could provide more concrete examples of the time/space requirements for different task complexities to help readers understand the practical limitations better.

3. The direct reference mechanism is clearly important for performance, but the paper could include more examples of how this mechanism works in practice with specific bAbI examples to help readers understand its value.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The Rule 30 results show the model struggles with the task. Could you elaborate on whether the model learns partial patterns (e.g., correctly predicting some cells but not others) or fails more systematically? What specific aspects of the Rule 30 transition might be challenging for the model's current architecture?

2. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model was trained with strong supervision (full graph information), while other models were trained end-to-end. How much of the performance gain over baselines like MemNN can be attributed to this stronger supervision versus the graph representation itself?

3. The model uses a fixed maximum number of nodes in the graph. How does the model handle tasks where the required number of nodes exceeds this maximum, and what are the practical limitations of this approach for more complex tasks?
