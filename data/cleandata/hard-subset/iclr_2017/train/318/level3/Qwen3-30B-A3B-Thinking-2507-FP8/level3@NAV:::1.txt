

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

The paper introduces a novel architecture called Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) that enables learning graph-to-graph tasks using gradient descent. The key contribution is the introduction of differential graph transformations that allow the model to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input. The paper frames this as learning a cellular automaton represented by sequences of growing graphs with neighbor pointers and special 0/1 value nodes. The authors demonstrate that their approach successfully learns to solve most bAbI tasks (reaching 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks), outperforming state-of-the-art methods on several benchmarks, and shows promising results on Rule 30 cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper makes a significant contribution to the field by enabling gradient-based learning for graph-to-graph transformations, which is a substantial gap in current graph neural network literature, and (2) the experimental results on bAbI tasks are compelling, demonstrating clear performance improvements over existing methods on several tasks, particularly those with natural graphical structure like pathfinding.

The paper's approach is well-motivated, building effectively on prior GGS-NN work while addressing a clear limitation of existing graph neural networks. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous, with appropriate baselines, thorough analysis of results, and careful attention to the specific strengths of the graphical representation. The authors demonstrate both the effectiveness of their model on standard benchmarks and its ability to learn complex rule-based systems.

For improvement, the authors could more thoroughly analyze why the Rule 30 experiment remains far from solved, including potential limitations of the architecture for this specific task. Additionally, a more detailed comparison of computational complexity would help readers understand the trade-offs between the GGT-NN's expressive power and its computational demands.

I would like to ask the authors to clarify: How might the model be extended to handle graphs with significantly more nodes (beyond the 20 entities in Task 17), and what architectural modifications would be necessary to maintain performance? Also, could the authors provide more insight into why the direct reference mechanism substantially improves performance, beyond just noting its effectiveness?
