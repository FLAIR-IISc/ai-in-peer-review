

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), proposing a novel approach to represent dialog as a graph that serves as differentiable memory. The authors present a framework where graph structures are learned and modified during inference, with long-term learning for graph transformation parameters and sentence encodings. The work appears to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory represented as a graph, offering a more flexible approach than previous methods that use fixed-structure representations.

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach that addresses an important gap in the literature regarding structured memory representations, and (2) despite marginal performance gains on bAbI tasks compared to memory networks, the graph-based representation demonstrates clear advantages for certain tasks like pathfinding (task 19) where the graphical structure aligns naturally with the problem domain.

The paper's technical content is sound and accurately presented. The authors provide comprehensive empirical evaluation across multiple domains, including the bAbI tasks, cellular automaton, and Turing machines. While the performance on bAbI is comparable to top memory networks (with only marginal gains), the paper successfully demonstrates the graph-based approach's ability to learn complex state transitions that align with the problem structure. The clarity of the revised manuscript has significantly improved from earlier drafts, making the technical contributions accessible.

The paper's main limitation is that it does not yet demonstrate significantly superior learning or generalization capacity compared to existing methods. However, the approach is promising, particularly for tasks with natural graph structures, and the authors provide clear directions for future work to address current limitations.

For future work, I recommend the authors: (1) conduct a more thorough comparison with traditional rule-based approaches to better understand the trade-offs between neural and symbolic methods, (2) explore methods to reduce the need for graph-level supervision, and (3) investigate optimizations to improve computational efficiency. Additionally, the authors might clarify how the graph representation can be effectively applied to larger-scale real-world problems beyond the bAbI tasks.
