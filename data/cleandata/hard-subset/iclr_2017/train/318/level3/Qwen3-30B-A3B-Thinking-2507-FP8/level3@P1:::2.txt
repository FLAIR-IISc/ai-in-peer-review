

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends GGS-NNs by enabling graph-structured intermediate representations. The model learns to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, using differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state updates, edge modifications, propagation, and aggregation) to incrementally build a graph that serves as an internal state. The authors demonstrate the approach on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing that the model can successfully learn to represent relationships and rules in graphical form.

I recommend acceptance for this paper. The contribution is novel and well-motivated, representing the first implementation of a differentiable graph-based memory that can be constructed from unstructured input. The paper effectively positions itself within the literature, clearly differentiating from prior graph-based neural networks that require fixed graph structures as input, and from memory networks that use implicit internal states. The work is technically sound, with careful implementation details and thorough empirical validation across multiple domains.

The empirical results support the paper's claims. On the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN model achieves 95% accuracy on 19 out of 20 tasks (with direct reference), matching or exceeding state-of-the-art memory networks on several tasks, particularly those naturally suited to graphical representations like pathfinding (task 19). The model successfully learns the rules of a cellular automaton and Turing machines, demonstrating its ability to generalize beyond the training examples. The ablation study showing the benefit of direct reference (95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference vs. 9/20 without) provides strong evidence for the architectural choices.

The paper's technical contribution is significant as it establishes a framework for neural networks to manipulate graph structures during inference, rather than merely processing fixed graphs. This opens up new possibilities for modeling structured data with neural networks. The authors have also made a substantial effort to improve clarity from earlier drafts, resulting in a well-written and accessible paper.

To further strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Add more detailed analysis of the model's scalability, particularly how performance changes with graph size (number of nodes/edges).
2. Include visualizations of the learned graph structures for the bAbI tasks to provide better intuition about what the model is learning.
3. More explicitly discuss the trade-offs between the distributed nature of GGT-NNs (which enables complex graph operations) and their quadratic time/space complexity compared to memory networks.

The paper does acknowledge the quadratic complexity limitation in Section 7, but a more concrete discussion of practical constraints would be helpful for potential users of the approach.

Questions for the authors:
1. How does the model's performance scale with the size of the graph (e.g., number of nodes/edges) and what are the practical limits for real-world applications?
2. For which specific types of tasks does the graph-based representation provide a clear advantage over simpler memory structures, and why?
3. Could the model be extended to handle dynamic graphs where the number of nodes changes over time, such as in streaming data applications?

The GGT-NN represents an important step in making neural networks more flexible for modeling structured data. While the performance gains over memory networks on bAbI tasks are not massive (though they're significant for certain tasks), the approach opens up new possibilities for graph-based neural networks that could be valuable for future work. The paper's contribution is constructive, technically sound, and well-positioned within the field, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
