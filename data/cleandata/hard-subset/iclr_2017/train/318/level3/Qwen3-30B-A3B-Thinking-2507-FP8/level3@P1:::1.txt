

The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a novel architecture that extends Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by enabling the learning of graph-to-graph transformations through differentiable graph operations. The key contribution is the introduction of five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the model to incrementally construct and modify graph structures based on textual input. This represents a significant advancement over prior work by enabling neural networks to learn graph structure manipulation rather than just processing fixed graphs.

The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness through two main applications: solving bAbI tasks and learning rule-based systems like cellular automata and Turing machines. For the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks (with direct reference), outperforming state-of-the-art methods on several tasks, particularly those suited to graphical representations like pathfinding (task 19). The model's ability to learn the graphical structure of the world state from text and use it to answer questions demonstrates a significant advance in structured reasoning.

For rule discovery, the model successfully learns to predict the next state of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and arbitrary Turing machines, though the Rule 30 results show room for improvement (reaching ~85% accuracy on the test set). The paper provides a clear graphical representation of these tasks, showing how the model can learn the underlying rules without explicit supervision.

The paper is well-motivated, building logically on prior work (GGS-NNs) while addressing a clear gap: the need for models that can learn to construct and modify graph structures based on unstructured input. The differentiable graph transformations are well-designed and clearly explained, with proper theoretical grounding in the graph neural network literature. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with careful ablation studies showing the importance of direct reference and intermediate propagation.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by introducing a novel way to learn graph structure manipulation through differentiable operations, with compelling empirical results on both structured reasoning tasks and rule discovery. The bAbI results are particularly impressive, demonstrating that the graphical intermediate representation provides a significant advantage for certain tasks.

For additional improvement, the paper should more thoroughly discuss the limitations of the Rule 30 results. While the model achieves 85% accuracy, it's unclear why it doesn't reach higher levels of performance on this task. The authors should provide a more detailed analysis of where the model fails and why, including visualizations of the predicted graphs versus the ground truth. Additionally, the discussion of computational complexity (quadratic scaling with graph size) should be expanded to include potential mitigation strategies.

I would also appreciate clarification on how the model handles graph structure when the number of nodes changes between training and test examples. In the Rule 30 task, the number of cells varies, but the model appears to handle this through the node addition transformation. How robust is this approach to significant variations in graph size?

The paper's claim that the GGT-NN "can learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input" is well-supported by the results. The ability to use graphs as intermediate representations for textual reasoning tasks is particularly valuable, as it provides a more interpretable and potentially more powerful alternative to existing memory-based architectures.

The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and careful attention to the specific strengths of the graphical representation. The comparison with state-of-the-art models on bAbI shows that the GGT-NN's graphical intermediate representation provides a clear advantage for certain tasks, which the authors effectively demonstrate with the pathfinding task (task 19) where the GGT-NN achieves a 64% improvement over the best baseline.

The paper's technical contributions are well-explained and the implementation details provided in the appendices are sufficient for reproduction. The model's modular design, allowing for different configurations for different tasks, is a particularly strong point that enhances the paper's applicability to a wide range of structured data problems.
