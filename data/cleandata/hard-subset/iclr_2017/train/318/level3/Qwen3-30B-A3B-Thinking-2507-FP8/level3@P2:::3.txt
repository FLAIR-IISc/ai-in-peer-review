

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) to learn complex, differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformation types (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to construct and modify graphs incrementally based on textual input. The model demonstrates promising results on the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on most tasks) and successfully learns rules for graphical formulations of cellular automata and Turing machines. The work makes a significant contribution by enabling neural networks to work with evolving graph structures rather than just fixed graph inputs, providing a more flexible framework for handling structured data.

## 2. Decision
I recommend rejection. The key reasons are: (1) the model requires ground-truth graph states at each timestep for training, which severely limits its applicability to real-world tasks where such supervision is unavailable, and (2) the paper is too dense and complex for a conference format, hindering readability and impact.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The primary limitation of the work is its reliance on ground-truth graph states at every timestep for training. As the authors acknowledge in Section 7, "the GGT-NN model was trained using only 1,000 training examples" with "full graph information," but they provide no compelling evidence that this approach would work when such supervision is unavailable. This is a fundamental limitation that undermines the model's practical utility. While the results on bAbI are impressive, they are achieved through a form of strong supervision that is not typically available in real-world applications. The authors note this limitation but don't provide a convincing path forward for reducing the supervision requirement.

Additionally, the paper is overly dense and complex for a conference submission. The mathematical formalism, extensive appendix, and detailed explanations of graph transformations make it difficult to follow for most readers. The key contributions could be better presented in a more accessible manner, which is particularly important for a conference where clarity and conciseness are valued. The paper reads more like a journal submission than a conference paper, which is a significant disadvantage for ICLR.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

- Provide a clearer explanation of why the graph representation is superior to other approaches beyond the pathfinding task. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN "with direct reference attains a higher level of accuracy on the following tasks" but doesn't sufficiently explain why the graph structure enables this improvement.

- Discuss potential methods for reducing or eliminating the need for ground-truth graph states. The authors mention "reducing the level of supervision needed" in Section 7, but this remains vague without concrete suggestions or preliminary experiments.

- Include more qualitative examples of the graphs produced by the model on the bAbI tasks. Visualizations would help readers understand what the internal representations look like and why they're useful.

- Simplify the presentation to make it more accessible. The current paper requires significant effort to parse, which is a disadvantage for a conference submission.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How would you modify the training procedure to reduce or eliminate the need for ground-truth graph states at each timestep? Would unsupervised learning approaches (e.g., self-supervised learning) be applicable to this problem?

- Could you provide more detailed examples of the graphs produced by your model on the bAbI tasks? Specifically, what do the graphs look like for a representative question, and how do they evolve through the processing steps?

- In the case of the bAbI tasks, why does the model with direct reference consistently outperform the model without it? Is there a theoretical explanation for this, or is it purely empirical?

- How would your approach handle tasks where the graph structure is not known in advance, such as when processing raw text without explicit entity/relationship annotations?
