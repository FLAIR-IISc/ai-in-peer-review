## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

This paper proposes a novel extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) – the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) – capable of learning differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The core idea of learning to construct and modify graphs from sequential input, and then utilizing these graphs for downstream tasks like question answering, is compelling. The authors demonstrate this with promising results on the bAbI tasks, as well as on tasks involving rule discovery in cellular automata and Turing machines. A key strength of this work lies in its ability to maintain evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable via gradient descent, effectively bridging continuous and symbolic representations in a way that surpasses fixed-shape memory models like Memory Networks and NTMs.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper for this conference. While the technical contribution is significant, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity and accessibility that hinders its suitability for a conference setting. The core idea is strong, but the density of the presentation makes it difficult to quickly grasp the nuances of the approach and fully appreciate its contributions. 

My primary concern stems from the strong reliance on ground-truth graph states during training. While this allows for supervised learning of the transformations, it severely limits the applicability of the model to real-world scenarios where such supervision is unavailable. The paper acknowledges this limitation, but doesn’t adequately address potential avenues for mitigating it. A discussion of unsupervised or weakly supervised approaches, even if preliminary, would significantly strengthen the work. Additionally, the paper is quite dense and reads more like a journal article than a conference paper. The extensive background material and detailed descriptions of each transformation, while thorough, detract from the overall flow and make it challenging to identify the key innovations. 

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 
*   **Streamline the presentation:** Focus on the core contributions and simplify the explanations of the transformations. Consider moving some of the detailed implementation specifics to an appendix.
*   **Address the supervision issue:** Discuss potential strategies for reducing the reliance on ground-truth graph states.
*   **Provide more intuitive examples:** Illustrate the learned graph transformations with concrete examples to aid understanding.
*   **Clarify the novelty:** More explicitly highlight how the GGT-NN differs from existing approaches beyond simply stating it's more general.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors:
*   What is the computational cost of the graph transformations, particularly as the graph size increases?
*   How sensitive is the model to errors in the ground-truth graph states provided during training?
*   Could the five differentiable transformation types be learned automatically, rather than being pre-defined?
*   What are the limitations of the current implementation, and what future directions are being explored to address them?
