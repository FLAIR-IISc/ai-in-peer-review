## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper proposes a novel approach to representing dialogue state as a graph, serving as a differentiable memory, and demonstrates this on the bAbI tasks. The core contribution lies in learning both the graph structure *and* the transformations applied to it during inference, with long-term learning focused on these transformation parameters and sentence encodings. This is a conceptually interesting direction, and represents the first implementation of a differentiable memory represented as a graph, offering a potentially more expressive alternative to simpler memory structures like stacks. 

I **accept** this paper for publication, albeit with some caveats. The work is technically sound and presents a thought-provoking approach to state representation in dialogue systems. While the performance gains on bAbI tasks are not substantial compared to existing memory networks, the potential of the graph-based memory representation warrants further investigation. The improved clarity in the later version is also greatly appreciated.

The paper is well-motivated, building upon prior work in GNNs, GG-NNs, and memory networks. The authors clearly articulate the limitations of existing approaches and position their work as a step towards more flexible and expressive state representation. The technical details are presented accurately, and the experimental setup is clearly described. The results, while not groundbreaking in terms of absolute performance, demonstrate the feasibility of the approach and provide a baseline for future research. The comparison to other methods, including traditional rule induction, provides valuable context. 

However, the lack of significant performance improvement on bAbI tasks is a concern. While the authors acknowledge this, a more in-depth analysis of *why* the approach doesn't outperform existing methods would be beneficial. The complexity of the model is considerably higher than simpler alternatives, and the current results don't fully justify this added complexity. 

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** More detailed ablation studies are needed to understand the contribution of each component of the GGT-NN, particularly the direct reference mechanism and the different graph transformations.
*   **Scalability:** The computational cost of the approach is mentioned as a limitation. Exploring techniques to improve scalability, such as sparse graph representations or approximate inference methods, would be valuable.
*   **Generalization:** The paper demonstrates performance on bAbI tasks. Evaluating the model on more complex and realistic dialogue datasets would be crucial to assess its generalization capabilities.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific architectural choices or hyperparameters were found to be most sensitive to performance?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the GGT-NN makes on the bAbI tasks, and how these errors differ from those made by memory networks?
*   What are the computational costs associated with the graph transformations, and how do they scale with the size of the graph?
*   Do you foresee any challenges in applying this approach to continuous dialogue domains where the state space is much larger and more complex?
