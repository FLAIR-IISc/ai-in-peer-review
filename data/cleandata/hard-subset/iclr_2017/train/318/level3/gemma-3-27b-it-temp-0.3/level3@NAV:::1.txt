## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

This paper introduces a novel approach to graph-structured data processing by proposing Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs). The core idea – utilizing differentiable graph transformations to learn graph-to-graph tasks with gradient descent – is compelling. Framing this as learning a cellular automaton and leveraging graphs as intermediate representations for tasks like bAbI are particularly interesting contributions. The authors demonstrate the potential of this approach by achieving strong performance on bAbI tasks, outperforming state-of-the-art methods.

**Reject** – While the ideas presented are innovative and the bAbI results are promising, the limited success on the Rule 30 experiment and the lack of detailed analysis regarding the learned graph structures raise concerns about the robustness and interpretability of the approach. The performance gains on bAbI, while significant, are not fully convincing without a more thorough investigation of *why* the graph representation is beneficial in those specific cases.

The paper adequately motivates the approach by positioning it within the existing literature on GNNs and GGS-NNs. The authors clearly explain the limitations of prior work and how GGT-NNs address them by enabling dynamic graph construction and modification. The use of differentiable transformations is a key contribution, allowing for end-to-end learning. However, the claim that the model “discovers the rules governing” the cellular automaton is overstated given the reported performance. The results indicate learning is far from complete, and a more nuanced discussion of the limitations is needed. The empirical results on bAbI are well-presented and support the claim of outperforming existing methods, but a more detailed ablation study exploring the impact of different components (e.g., direct reference) would strengthen the findings.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 

*   **Expand the Rule 30 analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of *what* the network is learning in the Rule 30 experiment. Visualizations of the learned graph structures and comparisons to the correct rules would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study on the bAbI tasks to determine the contribution of each component of the GGT-NN architecture.
*   **Interpretability:** Discuss the interpretability of the learned graph representations. What kind of information is being encoded in the nodes and edges?
*   **Computational Cost:** Provide a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the GGT-NN model, particularly in comparison to other methods.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   What specific architectural choices were made to enable the differentiability of the graph transformations, and what were the challenges encountered?
*   How sensitive is the performance of the model to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate and the number of propagation steps?
*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of the current approach in terms of scalability to larger and more complex graphs?
*   What strategies could be employed to improve the performance of the model on tasks like Rule 30, where learning is incomplete?
