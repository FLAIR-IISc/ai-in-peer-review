**Summary**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that uses graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The GGT-NN model can learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input, and also to use the graphs to produce a variety of outputs. The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of the GGT-NN model on the bAbI tasks, a set of simple natural-language tasks, and on two additional tasks: learning the rules governing a simple 1D cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines.

I recommend accepting this paper, with one or two key reasons for this choice.

**Reasons**

First, the paper tackles a specific and well-defined problem in the field of natural language processing and graph neural networks. The authors introduce a novel architecture that extends existing work on GGS-NNs and demonstrate its effectiveness on a range of tasks. The paper is well-motivated, and the approach is well-placed in the literature.

Second, the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims. The authors present a thorough evaluation of the GGT-NN model on the bAbI tasks, including comparisons to state-of-the-art models, and demonstrate its ability to learn complex update rules on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks.

**Supporting arguments**

The paper's contributions are significant, as it introduces a new architecture that can learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input. The GGT-NN model is also shown to be effective on a range of tasks, including those that require reasoning about temporal sequences of events.

The authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of the GGT-NN model and its components, including the differentiable graph transformations and the supervision scheme. The paper also includes a detailed analysis of the results, including an evaluation of the model's performance on different tasks and a comparison to state-of-the-art models.

**Additional feedback**

One potential area for improvement is the scalability of the GGT-NN model. As the authors note, the time and space required to train the model increase quickly as the complexity of the task increases. Developing optimizations to reduce the computational requirements of the model would be beneficial.

Another potential area for improvement is the level of supervision required to train the model. While the authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the GGT-NN model with strong supervision, it would be interesting to explore whether the model can be trained with weaker supervision or even without graph-level information.

1. How do the authors plan to address the scalability issues of the GGT-NN model, and what potential optimizations could be explored?
