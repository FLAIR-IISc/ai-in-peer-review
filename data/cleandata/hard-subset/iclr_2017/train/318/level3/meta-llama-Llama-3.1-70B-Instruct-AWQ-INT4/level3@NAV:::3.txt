**Paper Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions**

**Summary**

This paper proposes an extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that enables learning complex, differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The authors introduce five differentiable transformation types, which can be combined to process graphs in various ways. They demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on question-answering tasks (bAbI) and rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines). The proposed model, called Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), maintains evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable by gradient descent.

**Decision**

I recommend accepting this paper, with the condition that the authors address some concerns regarding readability and applicability.

**Supporting Arguments**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear motivation for their work, highlighting the limitations of existing graph neural networks and the need for more flexible and differentiable graph transformations.
2. **Effective evaluation**: The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on various tasks, including question-answering and rule discovery. The results show that the GGT-NN model can learn complex graph transformations and achieve state-of-the-art performance on some tasks.
3. **Novel contribution**: The proposed GGT-NN model is a novel contribution to the field of graph neural networks, offering a more flexible and differentiable approach to graph transformations.

**Concerns and Suggestions**

1. **Readability**: The paper is dense and may be better suited to a journal than a conference. The authors could consider breaking up the text into smaller sections, using more headings, and providing more intuitive explanations of the technical concepts.
2. **Applicability**: The authors rely on ground-truth graph states at each timestep, which may limit the applicability of their approach to tasks where such supervision is available. They could consider exploring alternative supervision methods or evaluating their approach on tasks with limited supervision.
3. **Additional experiments**: The authors could consider conducting additional experiments to evaluate the robustness of their approach to different graph structures, node types, and edge types.

**Questions for the Authors**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of limited applicability due to the requirement of ground-truth graph states at each timestep?
2. Can the authors provide more insights into the choice of the five differentiable transformation types and how they were designed?
3. How do the authors plan to extend their approach to more complex tasks, such as those involving multiple graphs or dynamic graph structures?
