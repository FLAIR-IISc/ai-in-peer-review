This paper proposes a novel extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by introducing differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The model, called Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), is capable of learning complex graph transformations in a supervised setting, where graph states are provided at each timestep. The authors introduce five differentiable transformation types, including node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation. The model is applied to question-answering tasks, such as the bAbI tasks, with promising results.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. Firstly, the paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to extending GGS-NNs, which is well-placed in the literature. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of the model's architecture and its components, making it easy to follow and understand. Secondly, the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support the claims made, with results showing that the model can effectively learn complex graph transformations and achieve high accuracy on various tasks.

The paper's strength lies in its ability to maintain evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable by gradient descent. This allows the model to effectively mix continuous and symbolic representations, making it more general than fixed-shape memory models such as Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines. The authors also provide a thorough analysis of the model's performance, including an ablation study and a comparison with other state-of-the-art models.

However, there are some concerns that need to be addressed. One major concern is that the model requires ground-truth graph states at each timestep, which may limit its applicability to tasks where such supervision is not available. Additionally, the paper is dense and may be better suited to a journal than a conference, which may hinder readability.

To improve the paper, I would suggest the following additional feedback:

* Provide more details on the implementation of the model, including the specific architectures used for each transformation type.
* Discuss the potential applications of the model beyond question-answering tasks, such as graph generation or graph-based reasoning.
* Consider providing more visualizations of the model's performance, such as graph visualizations or attention heatmaps.
* Address the concern about the model's requirement for ground-truth graph states at each timestep, and discuss potential ways to relax this requirement.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the specific architectures used for each transformation type, and how they were chosen?
* How do you plan to address the concern about the model's requirement for ground-truth graph states at each timestep?
* Have you considered applying the model to other tasks beyond question-answering, such as graph generation or graph-based reasoning?
* Can you provide more visualizations of the model's performance, such as graph visualizations or attention heatmaps?
