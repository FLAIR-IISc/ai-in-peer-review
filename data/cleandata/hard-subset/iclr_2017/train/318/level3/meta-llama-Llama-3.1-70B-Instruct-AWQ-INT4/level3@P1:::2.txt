This paper proposes a novel approach to learning graph-structured state transitions, introducing the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) model. The GGT-NN model is an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN) model, allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The model can learn to construct and modify graphs based on textual input and use the graphs to produce a variety of outputs.

The paper clearly states its contributions, which include the introduction of the GGT-NN model, its application to the bAbI tasks, and its ability to learn complex update rules for a simple cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The approach is well-motivated, building upon existing work in graph neural networks and sequence-to-sequence models.

The paper supports its claims with experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of the GGT-NN model on the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks. The results show that the model can reach high levels of accuracy on most tasks, outperforming some existing state-of-the-art approaches. However, the performance gain compared to memory networks is not significant, and the results are still preliminary.

The paper is technically accurate and thought-provoking, providing a new perspective on learning graph-structured state transitions. The reviewer considers the work constructive and worth publishing.

**Decision:** Accept

**Reasons:**

1. The paper proposes a novel and well-motivated approach to learning graph-structured state transitions.
2. The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the GGT-NN model on the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks.
3. The paper is technically accurate and thought-provoking, providing a new perspective on learning graph-structured state transitions.

**Supporting arguments:**

1. The GGT-NN model is a significant extension of the GGS-NN model, allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs.
2. The model's ability to learn complex update rules for a simple cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines demonstrates its potential for modeling a wide variety of graph-based problems.
3. The experimental results show that the model can reach high levels of accuracy on most tasks, outperforming some existing state-of-the-art approaches.

**Additional feedback:**

1. While the paper provides a good introduction to the GGT-NN model, the clarity of the explanation could be improved, particularly for readers without a strong background in graph neural networks.
2. The paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the model's performance on the bAbI tasks, including a comparison with other state-of-the-art approaches.
3. The reviewer suggests that the authors consider providing more information on the computational resources required to train the model, as this could be a significant limitation for some researchers.

**Questions for the authors:**

1. How do the authors plan to address the issue of computational resources required to train the model, particularly for larger graphs?
2. Can the authors provide more information on the choice of hyperparameters for the GGT-NN model, and how they were tuned?
3. How do the authors plan to extend the GGT-NN model to more complex tasks, such as those involving multiple graphs or graph sequences?
