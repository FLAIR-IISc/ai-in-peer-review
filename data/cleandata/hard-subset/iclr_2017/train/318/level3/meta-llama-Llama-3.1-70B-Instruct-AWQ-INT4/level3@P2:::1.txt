This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The GGT-NN model uses a set of differentiable graph transformations to process and generate graphs, enabling the learning of graph-to-graph tasks using gradient descent.

I decide to accept this paper, with two key reasons for this choice. First, the paper tackles a specific and well-motivated question: how to extend GNNs and GGS-NNs to handle graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their approach, including a detailed description of the GGT-NN architecture and its components. Second, the paper provides strong empirical evidence to support the claims made about the GGT-NN model, including experiments on the bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks.

The paper provides a thorough explanation of the GGT-NN architecture, including the differentiable graph transformations used to process and generate graphs. The authors also provide a clear description of the training procedure, including the use of supervision to guide the learning process. The experiments on the bAbI tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the GGT-NN model in learning to generate graphs that capture the underlying structure of the data. The results on the rule discovery tasks also show the ability of the GGT-NN model to learn complex rules governing the evolution of graph structures over time.

One potential limitation of the paper is that the Rule 30 experiment remained far from solved, with the model struggling to learn the correct rules governing the evolution of the graph sequence. However, this limitation is acknowledged by the authors, and they provide a clear discussion of the challenges involved in learning complex graph-to-graph tasks.

To improve the paper, I would suggest providing more details on the implementation of the GGT-NN model, including the specific architectures used for the differentiable graph transformations and the training procedure. Additionally, it would be helpful to provide more analysis of the results on the bAbI tasks, including a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models.

Some questions I would like the authors to answer to clarify my understanding of the paper include:

* Can you provide more details on the implementation of the differentiable graph transformations used in the GGT-NN model?
* How did you choose the specific architectures used for the differentiable graph transformations, and what were the design considerations involved?
* Can you provide more analysis of the results on the bAbI tasks, including a more detailed comparison with state-of-the-art models?
* What are the potential applications of the GGT-NN model, and how do you envision it being used in real-world scenarios?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks. The GGT-NN model has the potential to be a powerful tool for learning complex graph-to-graph tasks, and the authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their approach.
