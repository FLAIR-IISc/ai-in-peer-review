This paper proposes an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) to learn complex, differentiable graph transformations as internal representations. The model, called Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), is designed to learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input, and also to use the graphs to produce a variety of outputs.

The GGT-NN model is well-motivated, building upon the success of GGS-NNs and addressing their limitations by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations. The approach is also well-placed in the literature, drawing on concepts from graph neural networks, recurrent neural networks, and differentiable neural computers.

The paper provides strong evidence to support its claims, including experimental results on the bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks. The results demonstrate the ability of the GGT-NN model to learn complex graph transformations and to apply them to question-answering tasks with high accuracy. The model's performance is also compared to several state-of-the-art approaches, and it is shown to outperform many of them on certain tasks.

One of the main strengths of the GGT-NN model is its ability to maintain evolving, complex graph states while remaining fully differentiable and trainable by gradient descent. This allows the model to effectively mix continuous and symbolic representations, making it more general than fixed-shape memory models such as Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines.

However, one of the main concerns with the GGT-NN model is that it requires ground-truth graph states at each timestep, which can limit its applicability to tasks where such supervision is available. Additionally, the paper is dense and may be better suited to a journal than a conference, which can make it difficult to read and understand.

To improve the paper, the authors could consider providing more intuitive explanations of the model and its components, as well as more visualizations to help illustrate the graph transformations and the model's performance. Additionally, the authors could explore ways to reduce the amount of supervision required to train the model, such as using fewer graph states or using alternative forms of supervision.

Overall, I would recommend accepting this paper, as it presents a novel and well-motivated approach to learning complex graph transformations, and provides strong evidence to support its claims.
