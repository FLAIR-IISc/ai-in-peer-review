

# Peer Review: "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends graph neural networks by enabling dynamic construction and modification of graph structures during processing. The key innovation is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the model to build and modify graph representations based on unstructured input. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks), cellular automata (Rule 30), and Turing machines. The paper makes a strong case for the utility of graph-structured state representations in tasks where relationships and connectivity are fundamental to the solution.

## 2. Decision

**Weak Accept** (with major revisions required)

The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graph neural networks by addressing a critical limitation of existing approaches: the inability to dynamically construct graph structures. The differentiable graph transformations are well-motivated and well-executed. However, the experimental setup and comparison with baselines require significant clarification to establish the true novelty and advantages of the approach.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's core contribution is well-motivated: existing graph neural networks (GNNs, GG-NNs, GGS-NNs) require fixed graph structures, limiting their applicability to tasks where the graph structure itself is part of the solution. The GGT-NN's ability to build and modify graph structures during processing is a significant step forward.

The empirical results are generally strong, particularly on the pathfinding task (task 19), where the GGT-NN with direct reference achieves 64% higher accuracy than the MemNN baseline. The model's ability to learn complex rule-based transformations (Rule 30, Turing machines) demonstrates its capacity for structured reasoning.

However, the paper's claims are undermined by two critical issues:

1. **Unfair comparison with baselines**: The authors acknowledge that their model uses "strong supervision" (full graph information at each timestep), while most baselines are trained end-to-end without this supervision. The comparison is therefore not fair, as the GGT-NN is essentially given more information than the competitors. The paper should either provide results on the standard bAbI benchmark without graph supervision or clearly acknowledge that their method's advantage comes from this additional supervision.

2. **Lack of computational complexity analysis**: The paper mentions that "the time and space required to train the model increase very quickly as the complexity of the task increases" due to the quadratic complexity, but doesn't explore potential solutions or provide analysis of how this limitation affects practical applications. The authors should discuss potential approaches to mitigate this (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing) and provide empirical evidence of how the model scales with graph size.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

**Critical revisions needed:**

1. **Fair comparison**: Provide results on the standard bAbI benchmark without graph supervision to make a fair comparison with other methods. This is essential for establishing the true contribution of the GGT-NN approach.

2. **Computational complexity**: Include a detailed analysis of how the model scales with graph size, with experiments showing performance and runtime on graphs of varying sizes. Discuss potential optimizations to address the quadratic complexity.

3. **Comparison with recent work**: The paper should cite and compare with recent approaches that also allow for dynamic graph construction (e.g., Dai et al. "Learning to Generate Graphs with Graph Neural Networks" - ICML 2018), which would better position their work in the current literature.

4. **Clarify direct reference mechanism**: The paper shows that direct reference improves performance but doesn't explain *why*. A deeper analysis of how direct reference aids learning would strengthen the paper.

**Other suggestions:**

- The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model can be configured for different tasks by reordering transformations, but doesn't provide examples of these alternative configurations. Including one such example would strengthen the argument for modularity.

- The failure on task 17 (Positional Reasoning) deserves more thorough analysis. Why did the model fail on this task when it succeeded on others with similar complexity? This analysis could provide valuable insights.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide results on the standard bAbI benchmark (without graph supervision) to make a fair comparison with other methods? This would be the most important addition to the paper.

2. How do you plan to address the quadratic computational complexity for larger graphs? Have you explored any approaches to make the model more scalable?

3. Could you clarify the relationship between GGT-NN and GGS-NN in terms of what each can and cannot do? The paper states that "GGS-NNs can only accept graphical data," but the GGS-NN paper (Li et al., 2016) actually discusses sequence-to-sequence tasks with a fixed graph structure.

4. Why did the model fail on task 17 (Positional Reasoning) when it succeeded on other tasks with similar complexity? What specific aspects of the task made it challenging?

The GGT-NN represents a meaningful advancement in the field, but to fully demonstrate its contribution, the authors need to address these critical issues. With these revisions, the paper would make a strong contribution to the literature on neural networks for structured data.
