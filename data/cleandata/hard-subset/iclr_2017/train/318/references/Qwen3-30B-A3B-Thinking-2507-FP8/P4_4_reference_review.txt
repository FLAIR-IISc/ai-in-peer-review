

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends previous graph neural network architectures (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) by allowing the graph structure to be built and modified based on unstructured input. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable the network to incrementally construct a graph from natural language input and use it for various tasks. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks, where it achieves 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks (11 tasks at 100% accuracy), and on Rule 30 cellular automaton and 4-state Turing machine tasks, where it successfully learns the underlying rules governing the graph evolution.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear contribution to the field of graph neural networks with a well-motivated approach that addresses a genuine limitation in existing architectures. The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the model's effectiveness across multiple domains.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision
The paper clearly identifies the limitation of existing graph neural networks (which require fixed graph structures as input) as the problem to solve. The GGT-NN architecture addresses this limitation by introducing differentiable graph transformations that allow the graph structure to be built and modified based on input. This is a well-motivated extension of prior work on GG-NNs and GGS-NNs.

The paper provides thorough empirical validation across multiple domains. For the bAbI tasks, the results show the GGT-NN model outperforms several strong baselines on specific tasks (particularly those with a natural graphical structure like pathfinding), and the ablation study demonstrates the importance of the direct reference mechanism. The experiments on the Rule 30 cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks provide compelling evidence of the model's ability to learn complex graph-based rules from scratch.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with a comprehensive comparison to related work in Section 6 that clearly articulates how GGT-NNs differ from existing approaches. The authors properly acknowledge the limitations of their approach (computational complexity) while demonstrating the model's advantages for tasks where graphical representations are beneficial.

## 4. Additional feedback for improvement
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN model, particularly regarding the quadratic scaling with graph size. While the paper mentions this limitation, providing specific runtime measurements or theoretical analysis of the complexity would strengthen the discussion.
- The comparison with baselines on the bAbI tasks could be enhanced with statistical significance testing to better support the claims of performance differences.
- The paper could provide more insight into the specific advantages of the graph structure for tasks like the bAbI tasks beyond the pathfinding example. A more detailed analysis of which specific task characteristics make a graphical representation advantageous would be valuable.
- The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it, but does not provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. A more in-depth discussion of the role of direct reference would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for the authors
1. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model was trained with strong supervision (full graph information) for the bAbI tasks, while most baselines were trained end-to-end without additional supervision. How would the performance of the GGT-NN model change if trained without this additional supervision, and how does this compare to other approaches that might use similar supervision?
2. In the Rule 30 cellular automaton task, the model was trained on 1000 examples with 7 timesteps each. How does the model's performance scale with the number of training examples, and what is the minimum number of training examples required to achieve good generalization?
3. The paper states that the GGT-NN model has quadratic time and space complexity with respect to graph size. What specific techniques could be used to reduce this complexity (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing) while maintaining performance, and have you explored these in your experiments?
