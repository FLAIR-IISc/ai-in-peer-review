

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to allow for graph-structured intermediate representations and graph-structured outputs. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to build and modify graphs from unstructured input. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI tasks (reaching 95%+ accuracy on most tasks), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and Turing machine simulation. The paper is well-organized and provides clear descriptions of the model's components and their implementations.

## Decision
Reject. The main reasons for rejection are: (1) insufficient theoretical justification for the differentiable graph transformations, and (2) the empirical results lack sufficient rigor and comparison to establish the novelty and effectiveness of the approach.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's primary claim is that GGT-NN enables "learning graphical state transitions" from unstructured input. However, the theoretical foundation for the differentiable graph transformations is weak. While the paper states that the transformations are differentiable, it provides no rigorous mathematical justification or proof. The transformations are described as "functions that take in an old graph and produce a new one," but the paper fails to demonstrate how the combination of these transformations maintains differentiability, which is essential for the model's training.

The empirical evaluation, while promising, suffers from several methodological issues. The paper reports accuracy percentages without standard deviations or statistical significance tests across multiple runs. The comparison to baselines is limited to a few older models (e.g., LSTM, MemNN, MemN2N), without including more recent state-of-the-art approaches for the bAbI tasks. The paper also lacks a thorough ablation study to determine which components of the model (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation) contribute most to performance. For instance, the paper states that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks," but doesn't provide a detailed analysis of why direct reference makes such a significant difference.

Additionally, the paper claims the model can "learn complex update rules" but provides limited evidence beyond the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks. The paper doesn't demonstrate how the model handles more complex or varied graph dynamics beyond these specific examples.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement
1. **Theoretical Rigor**: Provide a formal proof of differentiability for the graph transformations and their combinations. Discuss potential pitfalls in maintaining differentiability when chaining multiple transformations.

2. **Empirical Rigor**: Include multiple runs with standard deviations and statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) for all reported results. Conduct a thorough ablation study to identify which components of the model contribute most to performance.

3. **Comparative Analysis**: Expand the comparison to include more recent state-of-the-art models for the bAbI tasks. The paper mentions "a variety of existing state-of-the-art approaches" but only compares to older models, which makes it difficult to assess the true novelty and advancement.

4. **Task Analysis**: Provide more detailed analysis of why the model succeeds on some tasks (e.g., pathfinding) but struggles on others (e.g., task 17). This would help clarify the model's strengths and limitations.

5. **Computational Complexity**: Address the quadratic scaling issue more thoroughly. Discuss how the model's computational requirements might limit its applicability to larger graphs and suggest potential optimizations (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing).

## Questions for Authors
1. Could you provide a formal justification for why the combination of graph transformations maintains differentiability throughout the network, especially when multiple transformations are applied sequentially?

2. The paper claims a "64% improvement" on task 19 compared to MemNN. Could you provide statistical significance testing for this result across multiple runs?

3. How does the model handle cases where multiple valid graph structures could represent the same input? How does this affect training stability and convergence?

4. The paper mentions that "the model can be used without direct reference" but performs poorly without it. Could you elaborate on why direct reference is so crucial, and whether there are ways to reduce the model's dependence on this component?

Overall, while the GGT-NN concept has potential, the paper currently lacks the theoretical and empirical rigor required to establish it as a significant contribution to the field. With the suggested improvements, this work could make a valuable contribution to graph-based learning.
