

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## Summary of Contribution
This paper introduces a fundamentally novel approach to neural network architectures by proposing the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which enables neural networks to learn to construct and manipulate graph structures as their internal state from unstructured input. The key innovation is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to incrementally build and modify graph representations. Unlike existing graph neural networks (GNNs, GGS-NNs) that require fixed graph inputs, GGT-NN can learn to represent unstructured data (like natural language) as graph structures and solve tasks that are naturally suited to graphical representations. The paper demonstrates the approach on three distinct domains: bAbI question-answering tasks, cellular automata, and Turing machines, showing that the model can learn to construct meaningful graph representations from textual input and solve complex tasks that would be difficult with traditional sequence-based approaches.

## Decision
Accept

## Supporting Arguments
This paper presents a genuinely novel conceptual advance that fundamentally changes how neural networks can process structured data. The core idea of differentiable graph transformations that allow neural networks to build and modify graph structures as internal states is bold and significant, representing a major step beyond the current state of the art in graph neural networks. The paper's contribution is not incremental but rather a new paradigm for neural network architectures.

The empirical results are compelling: the model achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks (with direct reference), significantly outperforming previous approaches on tasks naturally suited to graphical representations (like pathfinding in task 19). The ability to learn the rules of cellular automata and Turing machines from raw textual descriptions is particularly impressive and demonstrates the model's versatility.

What makes this paper particularly strong for ICLR is that it prioritizes conceptual novelty over methodological perfection. The authors have taken a bold step beyond existing graph neural network frameworks, creating a new direction for research rather than making a small incremental improvement to existing methods. The paper's claim that "many types of data can be naturally expressed as a graph" and that the GGT-NN model can learn to represent this structure from unstructured input is well-supported by the experiments.

## Additional Feedback
1. While the paper compares well with existing graph neural networks, a more detailed comparison with recent graph transformer approaches (e.g., Graph Transformer Networks, GATs) would better position the contribution within the current literature.

2. The paper mentions quadratic computational complexity as a limitation but doesn't provide concrete analysis of how this impacts performance on larger-scale problems. A brief discussion of computational efficiency compared to alternative architectures (like Memory Networks or DNCs) would strengthen the paper.

3. The authors should provide ablation studies to quantify the contribution of each graph transformation component (e.g., how much does direct reference improve performance?).

## Questions for Authors
1. How does the GGT-NN model compare to more recent graph neural network approaches (e.g., Graph Transformer Networks, GATs) in terms of both performance and computational efficiency?

2. The paper notes that the model scales quadratically with input size. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of how this impacts performance on larger graphs or longer sequences, and what potential optimizations might mitigate this issue?

3. The model achieves impressive results on bAbI task 19 (pathfinding), which is naturally suited to graphical representations. How might the model be extended to handle more complex spatial reasoning tasks beyond the scope of bAbI?
