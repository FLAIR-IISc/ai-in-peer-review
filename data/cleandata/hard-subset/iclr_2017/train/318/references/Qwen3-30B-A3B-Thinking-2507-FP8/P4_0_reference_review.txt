

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by enabling dynamic construction and modification of graph-structured internal states. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to build graphs from unstructured input (such as natural language) and manipulate them to solve tasks. The authors demonstrate the approach on three domains: the bAbI question-answering tasks (with 20 different tasks), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and 4-state Turing machines. The model achieves high accuracy (95%+ on most bAbI tasks) while explicitly modeling graph-structured internal states, which provides interpretability and enables applications where graph structure is essential.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a clear technical contribution with well-supported empirical results. The key reasons for acceptance are the significant novelty in enabling dynamic graph construction through differentiable transformations and the strong empirical validation across multiple domains.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in technical soundness and novelty. The GGT-NN architecture directly extends prior work (Li et al., 2016) by addressing the key limitation of fixed graph structure in existing GNN approaches. The differentiable graph transformations are well-motivated and properly integrated into a coherent framework. The paper clearly demonstrates the advantages of this approach: the GGT-NN model with direct reference achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks, outperforming other models on tasks where graph structure is advantageous (e.g., pathfinding in task 19, which shows a 64% improvement over MemNN).

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically rigorous. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on both question-answering tasks and rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), with results showing high accuracy (95%+ on training examples) and strong generalization to longer sequences (20-30 timesteps). The comparison with existing approaches (including MemNN, MemN2N, DNC, etc.) is thorough, and the authors appropriately acknowledge the strong supervision used for GGT-NN and MemNN.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, clearly distinguishing its contribution from related work on GNNs, memory networks, and other structured data approaches. The discussion of how GGT-NN differs from existing approaches (particularly in its explicit graph-structured internal state and ability to modify graph structure) is compelling.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of computational complexity, especially regarding the quadratic scaling with graph size. While the paper mentions this limitation, a concrete analysis of training time and memory requirements would strengthen the discussion of practical applicability.
- The paper could provide more insight into why task 17 (Positional Reasoning) was particularly challenging (20 unique entities, short stories). A deeper analysis of this failure case would help understand the model's limitations.
- A more thorough comparison of training time and memory requirements between GGT-NN and other approaches would be valuable for understanding practical trade-offs.
- The paper mentions the need for graph-level supervision but could elaborate more on potential approaches to reduce this supervision requirement in future work.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- For the bAbI tasks, the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it significantly. Could you provide more insight into why direct reference is so beneficial? Is it primarily because it helps with node identification, or does it also aid in other aspects of graph construction?
- In the results, the model with direct reference required fewer training examples to reach 95% accuracy. Could you provide more details about the learning curves (e.g., accuracy at 100, 250, 500 examples) to better understand the training dynamics?
- For the Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks, the model achieved perfect accuracy on extended sequences (20-30 timesteps). Could you describe how the model generalizes to sequences longer than 30 timesteps, and whether there's a point where performance begins to degrade?
- The paper states that the graph transformations are differentiable, but does not detail the backpropagation through these operations. Could you provide more information about how gradients flow through node addition and edge update operations, particularly regarding the handling of node ordering?
