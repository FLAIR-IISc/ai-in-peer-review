

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) to enable the construction and manipulation of graph-structured internal states from unstructured input (e.g., natural language). The key innovation is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state updates, edge updates, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to incrementally build and modify graph representations. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on three distinct tasks: the bAbI question-answering dataset (where the model constructs a graph from textual input to answer questions), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and 4-state Turing machines. The paper makes a compelling case for the utility of graph-structured internal representations in certain domains, particularly where relationships between entities are central to the task.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's key contribution is promising but requires significant strengthening, particularly in experimental design and theoretical grounding to meet ICLR's standards.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's main weakness is its experimental evaluation, which does not fairly compare against baselines due to the strong supervision used for GGT-NN. The authors provide full graph-level supervision at each timestep (edge and node adjustments), while all comparison models (LSTM, MemNN, DNC, etc.) are trained end-to-end without this additional supervision. This creates an unfair advantage for GGT-NN, as the paper acknowledges ("the GGT-NN model was trained with full graph information"). The claim that GGT-NN "outperforms" other approaches on certain tasks (e.g., 64% improvement on task 19) is therefore misleading without a proper ablation showing the necessity of this strong supervision.

Second, the paper fails to adequately address the computational complexity of the model. The authors note that "the time and space required to train the model increase very quickly as the complexity of the task increases" (Section 7), but provide no evidence of scalability or practical limitations. The quadratic time complexity of the propagation step (Section 6) makes the model intractable for graphs with more than a few hundred nodes, yet the paper does not demonstrate this limitation or propose potential solutions (e.g., sparse graph techniques mentioned in Section 7). Without addressing this fundamental limitation, the practical utility of the approach is questionable.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a more rigorous experimental comparison: Train GGT-NN with minimal supervision (e.g., only final graph supervision) and compare fairly with other models. The paper should include ablation studies showing the necessity of different components (e.g., direct reference, node addition) and how much supervision is required for good performance.

2. Address computational complexity: Provide empirical evidence of scalability (e.g., runtime vs. graph size) and explore practical solutions for large graphs (e.g., sparse graph representations, approximate methods). The paper's claim about "distributed computation" (Section 6) should be supported with actual speed comparisons against alternatives.

3. Clarify theoretical foundations: The paper claims to make graphs "fully differentiable," but the mathematical formulation in Section 3 is somewhat informal. A more rigorous treatment of the differentiability of the transformations would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

4. Improve the writing: Some sections are repetitive (e.g., multiple descriptions of the same transformations), and the relationship between GGT-NN and GGS-NNs could be clarified more precisely.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How much of the GGT-NN's performance on bAbI tasks (particularly tasks 5, 7, 8, 17, 18, 19) is attributable to the strong supervision (full graph information at each timestep) versus the model's ability to learn graph structure? Could the model achieve similar results with only final graph supervision?

2. The paper states that GGT-NN "can learn how to transform its internal state based on the rules of each task" (Section 1). For the Turing machine task, how does the model learn the transition rules without explicit rule information? How does it generalize to Turing machines with rules it has never seen?

3. Given the quadratic complexity of the propagation step (Section 6), how would the model scale to graphs with thousands of nodes, which are common in real-world applications? What specific optimizations would you propose to address this limitation?

4. In the cellular automaton task (Section 5.2.1), the paper mentions "a linked list" representation. How does this differ from the standard GGS-NN approach used in Li et al. (2016), and why is this necessary for the GGT-NN to learn the task?
