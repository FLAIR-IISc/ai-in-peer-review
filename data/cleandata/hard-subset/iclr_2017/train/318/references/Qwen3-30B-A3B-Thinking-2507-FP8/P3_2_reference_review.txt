

# Peer Review: "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by enabling the network to learn to build and modify graph-structured internal states from unstructured input. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to incrementally construct graphs based on textual input and then use these graphs to answer questions or model complex graph-based processes. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on most tasks), 1D cellular automata (Rule 30), and 4-state Turing machines.

## Decision
**Accept with minor revisions**

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of graph neural networks by addressing a significant limitation of existing approaches: the inability to construct and modify graph structures from unstructured input. The technical innovations are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing methods on tasks with natural graphical structure.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Novel Technical Contribution**: The paper's most significant contribution is the set of differentiable graph transformations that make the graph structure itself learnable. This is a substantial extension beyond previous GNNs (GG-NNs/GGS-NNs), which treat the graph structure as fixed. The authors provide detailed mathematical formulations for each transformation, showing how they can be integrated into a differentiable framework.

2. **Strong Empirical Validation**: The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness across diverse tasks. The bAbI results are particularly compelling, with the GGT-NN model achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks (with direct reference), outperforming previous methods on several tasks where graphical structure is advantageous (e.g., pathfinding task 19, where it achieves 64% better accuracy than the best alternative). The model's success on Rule 30 cellular automata and Turing machines demonstrates its ability to learn complex graph-based rule systems without explicit supervision of the graph structure.

3. **Clear Comparison to Related Work**: The paper provides an excellent comparison to related work, clearly distinguishing GGT-NN from GNNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, and other approaches. The authors correctly identify that while these other models can work with structured data, they do not explicitly model the structure as a graph that can be modified during processing.

## Areas for Improvement

1. **Computational Complexity Discussion**: The paper mentions the quadratic complexity of the GGT-NN with graph size but doesn't thoroughly discuss when this approach would be preferable to alternatives. A more detailed analysis of the trade-offs between computational cost and performance would strengthen the paper. For example, how does the model scale with graph size, and what are the practical limits for real-world applications?

2. **Direct Reference Mechanism**: While the paper shows that direct reference significantly improves performance (95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference vs. 9/20 without), it doesn't deeply analyze *why* this mechanism works so well. More insight into how direct reference enables the model to better track entities would be valuable.

3. **Generalization Analysis**: The paper shows good generalization to longer sequences (20-30 timesteps) for the cellular automaton task, but doesn't provide a thorough analysis of *how* the model generalizes. It would be helpful to understand whether the model learns the underlying rules or simply memorizes patterns.

## Questions for Authors

1. In Table 2, the paper reports 100% accuracy on task 19 with direct reference, but the exact accuracy values for all tasks are not provided. Could you include a complete table of accuracy scores for all tasks (with and without direct reference) to better understand the model's performance across the full bAbI dataset?

2. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model was trained with "strong supervision" (full graph information), while other models were trained end-to-end. How much would performance decrease if the model were trained without this additional supervision? What would be the minimal amount of graph-level supervision needed for good performance?

3. For the Turing machine task, the paper reports high accuracy, but it's unclear whether the model learned the actual transition rules or simply memorized the input-output pairs. Could you provide more details on how the model's internal state evolves during training to learn the transition rules?

4. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model can be reconfigured for different tasks by changing the sequence of transformations. Could you provide an example of how the model would be configured for a different type of task, such as graph classification, rather than question-answering?

## Conclusion
This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks by introducing a framework that can learn to construct and modify graph structures from unstructured input. The technical innovations are sound, the experimental validation is thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over existing approaches on tasks with natural graphical structure. With minor revisions to address the computational complexity discussion and provide more insight into the direct reference mechanism, this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.

The paper's strength lies in its ability to bridge the gap between unstructured input and graph-structured processing, opening up new possibilities for applications where the internal state can be naturally represented as a graph. The authors have successfully demonstrated this with compelling empirical results across multiple domains.
