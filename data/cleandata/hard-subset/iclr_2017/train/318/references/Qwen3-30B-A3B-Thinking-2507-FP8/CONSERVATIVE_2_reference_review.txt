

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary of Claims

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends previous work on graph neural networks (GNNs) by allowing the model to dynamically construct and modify graph-structured internal states from unstructured input, such as natural language. The authors claim their model can:
1) Incrementally build a graph representation from textual input
2) Manipulate graph structures through differentiable transformations (node addition, state updates, edge modifications, propagation, and aggregation)
3) Achieve strong performance on the bAbI question-answering tasks and rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines)
4) Explicitly model the internal state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges, enabling visualization and downstream applications

The paper presents the GGT-NN as a more flexible alternative to existing graph neural network architectures that require fixed graph structures, and claims advantages over memory networks and neural Turing machines due to its explicit graph representation.

## Decision

Reject. The paper presents an interesting extension to graph neural networks, but the claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous validation against appropriate baselines, and the novelty is overstated.

## Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with this paper is the lack of rigorous validation against appropriate baselines. The authors claim strong performance on the bAbI tasks, but they train their model with "strong supervision" (full graph information at each timestep), while comparing against other models trained end-to-end without this additional supervision. This makes the comparison fundamentally unfair. For instance, the paper states that the GGT-NN model "was trained with strong supervision: the GGT-NN model was trained with full graph information, and the MemNN model was trained with information on which sentences were relevant to the query." This is a critical methodological flaw that undermines the claimed performance advantages.

Furthermore, the paper fails to provide sufficient ablation studies to demonstrate which components of the GGT-NN architecture actually contribute to the performance gains. The authors compare "with direct reference" versus "without direct reference," but don't isolate the specific contributions of the differentiable graph transformations, the node addition mechanism, or the edge update operations. Without this, it's impossible to determine whether the approach is truly novel or simply a more complex implementation of existing graph neural network concepts.

The paper also lacks a clear theoretical foundation for why the specific modifications to the graph representation (continuous edge weights, node strengths, etc.) are necessary. The authors claim these make the graph "differentiable," but don't provide evidence that this is essential for the performance gains they report, or that simpler approaches wouldn't work just as well.

The computational complexity of the approach is acknowledged as quadratic in the number of nodes, but the paper provides no evidence that this limitation is manageable for practical applications. The authors mention this as a "downside" but don't demonstrate that the performance gains outweigh this limitation.

## Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a fair comparison against a baseline that uses a graph representation but is trained with the same level of supervision as the GGT-NN (i.e., with full graph information provided at each timestep).

2. Provide a detailed ablation study that isolates the contributions of each component of the GGT-NN architecture.

3. Include a more thorough analysis of why the model succeeds on some bAbI tasks but fails on others (particularly task 17), to better understand the limitations of the approach.

4. Provide more concrete evidence that the "differentiable" graph representation is genuinely necessary for the performance gains, rather than simply being a more complex implementation of existing graph neural network concepts.

5. Include a more detailed discussion of the computational costs and scalability of the approach, with specific examples of realistic problem sizes.

## Clarification Questions

1. How exactly does the "strong supervision" provided to the GGT-NN differ from what would be required to train a standard graph neural network on the same tasks? Would a standard GNN be able to achieve similar performance with the same level of supervision?

2. Can you provide evidence that the continuous edge weights and node strengths are necessary for the performance gains, rather than simply being a more complex implementation of binary edge/node representations?

3. What specific advantages does the GGT-NN provide over a standard GGS-NN (as described in Li et al., 2016) that has already been shown to handle similar tasks? The paper claims the GGT-NN can handle textual input directly, but Li et al. (2016) also used textual input with a preprocessed graph representation.

4. How does the quadratic complexity of the GGT-NN affect its performance on larger graphs, and what specific techniques would be needed to scale the approach to more realistic problem sizes?
