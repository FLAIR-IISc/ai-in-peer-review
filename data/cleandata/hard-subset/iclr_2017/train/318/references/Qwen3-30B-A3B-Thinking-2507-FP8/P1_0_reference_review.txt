

# Peer Review: "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by enabling neural networks to construct and modify graph-structured internal states from unstructured input (like natural language). The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the model to incrementally build and manipulate graph representations. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GGT-NNs on three distinct domains: the bAbI question-answering tasks (where the model constructs a graph representing the story state), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper provides a thorough comparison with related work, including Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines, and other graph-based architectures, positioning the GGT-NN as a model that can work with unstructured input while explicitly modeling graph-structured internal states.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature by addressing a meaningful gap in graph neural network approaches—most prior work requires graph-structured input, while GGT-NN can construct and modify graphs based on unstructured input. The experimental results demonstrate strong performance on multiple tasks, particularly excelling on tasks naturally suited to graphical representations like pathfinding (bAbI task 19).

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly identify the limitation of existing graph neural networks (fixed graph structure) and position their work as a significant extension that allows for dynamic graph construction. The comparison with related work in Section 6 is thorough and highlights how GGT-NNs differ from Memory Networks, DNCs, and other approaches by explicitly modeling internal states as graphs.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The bAbI results are particularly compelling—GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks using only 1,000 training examples (with strong supervision), outperforming other models on tasks that benefit from graphical representations (e.g., 64% improvement on pathfinding task 19 compared to MemNN). The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex transformation rules, with high accuracy on both tasks and strong generalization to longer sequences.

The technical details are well-explained, with clear pseudocode (Algorithm 1) and thorough appendix documentation of the graph transformations. The ablation studies (direct reference vs. no direct reference) provide valuable insights into the model's behavior.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong, these suggestions could further improve it:

1. **Computational complexity discussion**: The paper mentions that "the time and space required to train the model increase very quickly as the complexity of the task increases" but doesn't quantify this. A brief analysis of how graph size affects training time and memory would be valuable for readers assessing practical applicability.

2. **Supervision discussion**: The paper acknowledges that the GGT-NN was trained with strong supervision (full graph information), unlike most other models. It would be helpful to discuss how this might limit real-world applicability and what approaches could be taken to reduce the need for such strong supervision.

3. **Direct reference explanation**: The paper shows that direct reference improves performance but doesn't deeply analyze why. A more detailed explanation of how the direct reference mechanism specifically helps the model learn relationships would strengthen the contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the model scales quadratically with graph size. How does this affect performance on larger graphs, and what practical limits does this impose on the model's applicability to real-world problems?

2. In the bAbI experiments, the GGT-NN with direct reference significantly outperforms the model without direct reference. Could you provide more specific examples of how direct reference improves the model's ability to learn relationships between entities?

3. The paper states that the GGT-NN was trained with "full graph information" as supervision, which differs from most other models. What would be your approach to training this model without such strong supervision, and what performance degradation might be expected?

## Conclusion

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks by introducing a model that can construct and manipulate graph-structured internal states from unstructured input. The results on multiple challenging tasks, combined with the thorough comparison to related work, make a compelling case for the value of this approach. The paper is well-written, technically sound, and demonstrates clear progress in the field. I recommend acceptance with the minor suggestions above for further strengthening the paper.
