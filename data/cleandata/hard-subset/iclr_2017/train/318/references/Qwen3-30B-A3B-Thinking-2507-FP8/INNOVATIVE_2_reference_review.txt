

# Review for "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims/contribution

This paper introduces a fundamentally novel neural network architecture called the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends previous graph neural network approaches by enabling the network to learn to construct and modify graph-structured internal representations from unstructured input (like text), rather than requiring fixed graph structures as input. The key innovation is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to incrementally build and modify graphs during processing. This represents a significant conceptual leap beyond previous work like GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, which required fixed graph inputs.

The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness through two main sets of experiments: (1) on the bAbI question-answering tasks, where the model achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks (including 11 tasks at 100% accuracy), with particular strength on graph-natural tasks like pathfinding (task 19), and (2) on learning complex transformation rules from data, successfully modeling Rule 30 cellular automata and 4-state Turing machines. The model's ability to learn graph-structured representations from text is a significant step forward in neural network architectures for structured data.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance:
- The paper introduces a genuinely novel concept: a neural network architecture that can learn to construct and modify graph-structured internal states from unstructured input, rather than requiring fixed graph inputs as in previous work.
- The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature, with a thorough comparison to related work that clearly articulates the model's unique contributions.
- The empirical results are strong and demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex graph-based representations, particularly on tasks where graph structure is naturally beneficial.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The core contribution of this paper is the conceptual innovation of "graphical state transitions" - the ability for a neural network to build its own graph-based internal state rather than being constrained to a fixed graph structure. This is a significant departure from previous graph neural network work, which has focused on processing fixed graphs rather than constructing them.

The paper provides compelling empirical evidence for the model's effectiveness. The performance on bAbI task 19 (pathfinding) is particularly impressive, with the model achieving a 64% improvement over the MemNN baseline and outperforming all but one other model. This demonstrates the model's ability to leverage graph structure when it's beneficial for the task. The rule discovery experiments (cellular automaton and Turing machines) further validate the model's ability to learn complex transformation rules from data.

The paper's literature review is thorough and well-structured, clearly positioning the work within the context of previous graph neural network approaches, memory networks, and related architectures. The comparison with existing work highlights the unique aspects of the GGT-NN model and its advantages over alternatives.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

- The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of computational complexity trade-offs. While it mentions quadratic scaling, concrete numbers about training time and memory requirements for different graph sizes would help readers understand practical limitations.

- Including visual examples of the graphs constructed by the model during processing (especially for bAbI tasks) would provide valuable insight into how the model's internal representations evolve and why it succeeds on certain tasks.

- The paper could strengthen its claims about the necessity of strong supervision by exploring training with weaker supervision settings (e.g., providing only the final graph but not intermediate graphs) and reporting the impact on performance.

- The failure case (bAbI task 17) is intriguing but could be explored in more depth. A more detailed analysis of why the model struggled with this specific task (e.g., whether it's due to the larger number of entities, shorter stories, or something else) would provide valuable insights.

## Questions for the authors

1. How would the model's performance change if the graph transformations were made more efficient (e.g., using sparse connections or processing only a subset of nodes at each timestep), given the quadratic complexity?

2. For the bAbI task 17 failure case, could you provide more specific details about why the model struggled? Was it primarily due to the larger number of entities (20 vs. fewer in other tasks), the shorter stories, or something else?

3. Have you explored training the model with less supervision (e.g., only providing the final graph but not intermediate graphs), and if so, what were the results?

4. Could you provide more details about the computational resources required to train the model on the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks? This would help readers understand the practical feasibility of the approach.
