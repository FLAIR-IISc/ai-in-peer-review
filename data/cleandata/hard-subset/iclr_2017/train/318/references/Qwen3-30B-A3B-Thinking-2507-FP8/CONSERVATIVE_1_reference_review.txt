

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends existing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by allowing the model to build and modify graph structures from unstructured input (e.g., natural language). The authors claim their model can learn to represent complex relationships as graphs and solve tasks that benefit from graphical representations, such as the bAbI reasoning tasks, cellular automata, and Turing machines. They propose five types of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to process graphs in flexible ways.

## Decision
Reject. The paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed approach is superior to established methods for the tasks it addresses, and the claimed advantages over existing methods are not rigorously validated.

## Supporting Arguments for Decision

1. **Unjustified Claim of Superiority**: The paper claims the GGT-NN model outperforms other methods on several bAbI tasks, but this comparison is fundamentally unfair. The GGT-NN model was trained with "strong supervision" (full graph information at each timestep), while the comparison models were trained end-to-end without additional supervision. This is a critical flaw that undermines the paper's main claim that the graphical representation is superior. The authors themselves acknowledge this in Table 2, noting that "the GGT-NN and MemNN models were trained with strong supervision" while others were not. Without this unfair advantage, the claimed performance improvements (up to 64% on task 19) become meaningless.

2. **Lack of Evidence for Graph Learning**: The paper's key claim is that the model learns to build meaningful graph representations from unstructured input. However, the model is trained with full graph supervision at each timestep, making it impossible to evaluate whether the model actually learns to construct meaningful graphs without this supervision. The authors provide no evidence of the model's performance when trained without this strong supervision, which is essential for establishing the value of the approach.

3. **Inadequate Baseline Comparison**: The paper does not compare against a standard GNN or GGS-NN model trained on the same graph-structured data. For example, for the bAbI tasks, they should have compared against a standard GGS-NN that is trained on the same graph representations (which they provide in the paper). Instead, they compare against models that were not designed for graph-structured input, making the comparison irrelevant to their core claim about graph representations.

4. **No Analysis of Learned Graphs**: The paper claims the model "explicitly models the internal state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges," but provides no analysis of what these learned graphs actually represent. There are no visualizations of the graphs, no analysis of how the graph structure relates to the input, and no evidence that the graph structure is meaningful for the task. This is a critical omission for a paper claiming the advantage of graph-structured representations.

5. **Theoretical and Practical Limitations**: The paper acknowledges the quadratic computational complexity of the model (time and space scaling with the square of input size), which severely limits its applicability. However, the paper makes no attempt to address this limitation or provide evidence that the approach can scale to larger problems. The authors acknowledge this as "one downside" but don't provide any justification for why this is acceptable.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

1. **Stronger Baselines**: The paper must compare against a standard GNN/GGS-NN model trained on the same graph-structured data, not just end-to-end models that weren't designed for graph inputs. This would provide a fair comparison to establish whether the graphical representation actually matters.

2. **Abilities Without Strong Supervision**: The authors should provide results for the model trained without full graph supervision at each timestep. This is essential to determine whether the model can actually learn to build meaningful graphs from unstructured input.

3. **Analysis of Learned Graphs**: Include visualizations or analyses of the graphs learned by the model to demonstrate that they capture meaningful relationships for the tasks.

4. **Address Computational Limitations**: Provide a more thorough discussion of how the quadratic complexity affects the model's applicability and potential solutions to this problem (e.g., sparse graph representations, selective node processing).

5. **Clarify the Novelty**: The paper should more clearly distinguish between what is truly new (the graph transformations) and what is a straightforward extension of existing GNN work. The "graph transformations" appear to be largely re-implementations of standard neural network operations (like GRUs) applied to graph structures.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide results for the GGT-NN model trained without the strong supervision (i.e., without providing the correct graph at each timestep during training)? How does performance change?

2. How would you compare the GGT-NN model against a standard GGS-NN model that is trained on the same graph representations (which you provide in the paper)? Would the GGT-NN model outperform a GGS-NN trained on the same graph data?

3. Can you provide visualizations or analysis of the graphs learned by the model for the bAbI tasks? How do these graphs correspond to the underlying relationships in the stories?

4. Given the quadratic computational complexity, what specific applications do you believe the GGT-NN model could realistically be applied to, beyond the small-scale examples shown in the paper?
