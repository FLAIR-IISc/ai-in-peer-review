

# Peer Review: "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces GGT-NN, a novel neural network architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by allowing the graph structure to be dynamically modified during processing. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be composed to build and modify graphs based on unstructured input. The model is demonstrated on three tasks: bAbI question answering, Rule 30 cellular automaton, and Turing machines. The authors claim that GGT-NN can learn to manipulate graph-structured internal states, which provides advantages for tasks with natural graph representations.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes a reasonable incremental contribution to the field of graph neural networks, but the claimed novelty is overstated, the evaluation methodology has significant flaws, and the computational limitations are inadequately addressed.

## Supporting Arguments

**1. Overstated Novelty**: The paper positions GGT-NN as a significant extension of GGS-NNs, but the core innovation is a straightforward extension of the existing framework rather than a fundamentally new approach. The "differentiable graph transformations" are largely variations of operations already present in GGS-NNs, with the main difference being the explicit handling of graph structure modification. The paper fails to clearly articulate how GGT-NN differs from GGS-NNs beyond the addition of these transformations, which are largely just more flexible implementations of existing mechanisms. The comparison with related work (Section 6) is superficial and doesn't adequately distinguish their approach from the existing literature.

**2. Flawed Evaluation Methodology**: The most serious issue is the evaluation of the bAbI tasks. The authors claim their model is "strongly supervised" by providing the correct graph at each timestep, while other models are trained end-to-end without this supervision. This creates an unfair advantage for GGT-NN, yet the paper presents results as if they were comparable. The authors acknowledge this in the text but fail to properly contextualize the results, making the comparison misleading. For instance, they claim their model outperforms others on certain tasks, but these other models were trained with significantly less supervision. A proper comparison would either provide the same level of supervision to all models or clearly demonstrate the impact of the additional supervision.

**3. Inadequate Discussion of Computational Limitations**: The paper acknowledges the quadratic complexity with respect to graph size ("the time and space required to run a GGT-NN step scales quadratically with the size of the input") but fails to provide any meaningful analysis of how this limitation impacts practical applications. The authors mention potential optimizations (sparse edge connections, processing subsets of nodes) but don't evaluate or discuss them. For a model that's supposed to be useful for real-world applications, this omission is significant. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of how the model scales with increasing graph size or complexity, which is crucial for assessing its practical utility.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify the Novelty**: The authors should more precisely articulate how GGT-NN differs from GGS-NNs. The paper should provide a clear comparison of the graph transformation capabilities and the architectural differences.

2. **Re-evaluate the Comparison**: The bAbI results should be re-evaluated with a fairer comparison. Either the authors should train other models with graph-level supervision, or they should provide a more nuanced analysis of how the supervision level affects performance across different models.

3. **Address Computational Complexity**: The paper should include an analysis of how the model scales with graph size, including empirical measurements of training time and memory requirements. The authors should also discuss potential approaches to mitigate the quadratic complexity, such as sparse graph representations or selective node processing.

4. **Provide More Analysis**: The paper mentions that GGT-NN with direct reference outperforms the model without it, but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of why. Visualizations of the learned graph structures or attention patterns would greatly enhance the paper's insights.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the GGT-NN model fundamentally differ from the GGS-NN model in terms of the graph transformations it can perform? The paper claims GGT-NN extends GGS-NNs, but the distinction isn't clearly articulated.

2. Can you provide a more detailed analysis of why GGT-NN performs better on task 19 (pathfinding) than other methods? What specific aspects of the graph representation enable this improvement?

3. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference significantly outperforms the model without it. Can you explain the mechanism behind this improvement and how it relates to the underlying graph structure?

4. Given the quadratic complexity of the model, what specific techniques do you plan to use to scale the model to larger graphs, and how would these techniques impact the model's performance?

The paper presents an interesting extension to graph neural networks, but the claims about its advantages are not sufficiently supported by the evidence provided. The evaluation methodology is flawed, and the computational limitations are inadequately addressed, making it difficult to assess the true potential of the approach.
