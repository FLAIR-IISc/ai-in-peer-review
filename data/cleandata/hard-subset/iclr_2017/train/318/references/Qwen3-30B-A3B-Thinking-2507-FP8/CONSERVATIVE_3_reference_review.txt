

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Claims

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs) by allowing the model to build and modify graph structures during processing, rather than being constrained to fixed graph inputs. The authors claim that this approach enables more flexible modeling of graph-structured internal states and can be applied to tasks requiring graph manipulation, such as question answering on the bAbI dataset, modeling cellular automata, and simulating Turing machines. The paper demonstrates that the GGT-NN achieves high accuracy on most bAbI tasks (95%+ on 19/20 tasks with direct reference), and successfully learns to model the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper's claims are not sufficiently supported by rigorous empirical validation against appropriate baselines, and the experimental design contains significant methodological flaws that undermine the reported results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with the paper is the lack of fair comparison with established graph-based methods, which is critical for a paper claiming to advance graph neural networks. The authors compare with sequence models (LSTM) and memory networks (MemNN, DNC, etc.), but not with current graph neural network approaches like Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs), Graph Attention Networks (GATs), or GraphSAGE, which are the standard for graph-based tasks. This is a fundamental omission for a paper that purports to extend graph-based methods.

More significantly, the paper uses strong supervision by providing the correct graph at each timestep during training, which is not comparable to the standard end-to-end training used for other models. The paper acknowledges this in section 5.1.1 but fails to properly contextualize the results. For instance, when claiming the GGT-NN with direct reference outperforms other models on task 19, they note that "the GGT-NN model was trained using only 1,000 training examples" and "the GGT-NN and MemNN models were trained with strong supervision," while all other models were trained end-to-end. This creates an unfair comparison that exaggerates the model's advantages.

The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical justification for the graph transformations. The introduction of "belief scores" for node existence and edge types is presented as a natural extension but isn't rigorously motivated or justified. The paper doesn't provide ablation studies to demonstrate which components of the graph transformation suite are essential or why the specific design choices were made.

Furthermore, the paper acknowledges the quadratic time complexity of the model (section 6) but provides no evidence that this limitation can be overcome for larger graphs, which is a major drawback for practical applications. The authors note this as a "downside" in the conclusion but don't address how this limitation might be mitigated.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend:

1. **Redefine the contribution:** The paper should clarify how GGT-NN differs from existing graph-based methods that already allow for graph structure modification (e.g., some recent GNN architectures with dynamic graph structures). The claim of "graph-structured intermediate representations" is not novel given the existence of GGS-NNs (mentioned in the paper) and other dynamic graph methods.

2. **Re-evaluate experimental design:** The authors should retrain the model without strong supervision (only using the final answer as the target) and compare with other models trained end-to-end. This would provide a fair comparison that reflects the model's true capability.

3. **Include ablation studies:** The paper should provide ablation studies to demonstrate which components of the graph transformation suite are most important, and why the specific design choices were made. This would help readers understand the contribution.

4. **Address scalability concerns:** The paper should discuss how the quadratic complexity might be mitigated for larger graphs, perhaps through sparse connections or sampling approaches, and provide preliminary evidence that this is feasible.

5. **Provide more detailed failure analysis:** The paper mentions task 17 was difficult, but doesn't analyze why. A failure case analysis would help readers understand the limitations of the approach.

## Questions for Authors

1. Why did the authors choose to compare with memory networks and sequence models rather than with current graph neural network methods (GCNs, GATs, GraphSAGE, etc.), which are the standard for graph-based tasks?

2. How would the results change if the model were trained end-to-end without the strong supervision (i.e., only using the final answer as the target)?

3. What is the theoretical justification for the specific graph transformations used, particularly the introduction of belief scores for node existence and edge types?

4. How do the authors plan to address the quadratic time complexity for larger graphs, and what evidence do they have that this can be overcome in practice?

5. Can the authors provide a more detailed analysis of why the model failed on task 17, and whether this failure is a fundamental limitation of the approach or simply due to the specific task structure?
