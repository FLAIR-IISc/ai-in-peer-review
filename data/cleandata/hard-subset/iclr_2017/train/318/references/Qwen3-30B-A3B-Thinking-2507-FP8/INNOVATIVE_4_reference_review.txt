

# Review for "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and graph-structured outputs. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable the network to incrementally construct and modify a graph based on unstructured input. The paper demonstrates that this approach is particularly effective for tasks where the natural representation of the problem is graphical, such as the bAbI question-answering tasks (especially pathfinding), cellular automata, and Turing machines. The authors show that GGT-NNs can learn to manipulate graph structures to solve complex reasoning tasks, with the key innovation being the ability to build the graph structure itself rather than relying on a fixed graph structure as in previous GNN approaches.

## 2. Decision: Accept

I recommend accepting this paper because it introduces a genuinely novel conceptual framework that opens new directions in neural network architectures for structured data. The paper successfully demonstrates that the GGT-NN approach can solve tasks where graphical representation is natural, with compelling results on the bAbI tasks (particularly task 19, pathfinding) and the rule discovery tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's key strength is its clear conceptual novelty. While building on existing work (Li et al., 2016), the authors make a bold conceptual leap by allowing the graph structure itself to be learned and modified, rather than requiring a fixed graph structure as input. This is a significant advancement over existing GNN approaches, which all require the graph structure to be fixed in advance.

The empirical results provide strong support for the claims. On the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN model with direct reference achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 out of 20 tasks, with particularly impressive results on task 19 (pathfinding), where it outperforms all but one of the other models. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to learn complex update rules for Rule 30 cellular automata and Turing machines, showing that it can generalize to unseen inputs. The ablation study showing that direct reference significantly improves performance (95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks vs. 9/20 without it) further supports the value of the approach.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough comparison to related work including GNNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, and other approaches. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from and improves upon existing methods, particularly by explicitly modeling the internal state as a graph rather than as an implicit set of vectors.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While I recommend acceptance, there are a few areas where the paper could be strengthened:

1. The paper notes that the model requires strong supervision (full graph information) during training, which is a significant limitation for real-world applications. It would be valuable to include a brief discussion of how this limitation might be addressed in future work, or to present preliminary experiments showing how the model might learn with less supervision.

2. The computational complexity of the approach (quadratic in graph size) is mentioned as a downside but not addressed in the experiments. It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to other approaches, especially for larger graphs.

3. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the specific advantages of the graphical representation for the tasks where GGT-NN outperformed other models. For example, why exactly does the graphical representation work better for pathfinding than a sequence-based approach?

4. The paper states that the GGT-NN can "learn complex update rules" but doesn't provide specific insights about what aspects of the graphical representation make it better at learning these rules. A brief analysis of the learned graph structures could provide valuable insight into the model's reasoning process.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it, but doesn't explain why direct reference is so beneficial. Could you provide more insight into what the direct reference mechanism is learning that makes it so effective?

2. For the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks, the paper states that the model "successfully learned the assigned tasks." Could you share more details about the specific patterns the model learned, or how the graph structure evolved during training to represent the underlying rules?

3. The paper mentions that the model with strong supervision (full graph information) was used, but this is not standard practice for most NLP tasks. How do you envision this approach being applied to real-world tasks where such supervision would not be available?
