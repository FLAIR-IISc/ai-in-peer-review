

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims

The paper proposes GGT-NN, a new neural network architecture that extends GNNs and GG-NNs by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and graph-structured outputs. The authors claim their model can incrementally construct a graph from natural language input, manipulate the graph structure using differentiable transformations, and produce either a final graph or natural language output. The paper demonstrates the model on the bAbI tasks (with strong supervision using graph representations), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and simple Turing machines. The authors argue that their approach is superior to existing methods for tasks that naturally lend themselves to graphical representations, such as pathfinding.

## 2. Decision

Reject

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper makes several claims about the advantages of GGT-NN, but the evidence provided is insufficient to justify the novelty and practical utility of the approach. Key issues include:

First, the comparison with existing methods is fundamentally unfair. The GGT-NN model is trained with strong supervision (full graph information at each timestep), while most comparison models (including MemNN, MemN2N, and others) are trained end-to-end without additional supervision. This makes the performance gains appear larger than they would be in a fair comparison. For example, the paper shows GGT-NN with direct reference outperforms MemNN on tasks 5, 7, 8, 17, 18, and 19, but MemNN was trained with relevance information while GGT-NN was trained with graph information. The paper does not present results with comparable supervision levels, which is critical for a fair assessment.

Second, the paper lacks adequate ablation studies to isolate the contribution of the key innovations. The model's performance is presented as a whole, but it's unclear which specific components (direct reference, graph transformations, etc.) are responsible for the gains. For instance, the paper states that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks," but it doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the direct reference mechanism itself is the primary driver of performance.

Third, the computational complexity of the model (quadratic in the number of nodes) is a significant drawback that isn't adequately addressed. The paper acknowledges this limitation but fails to demonstrate how it could be overcome for practical applications. The authors claim "the distributed nature of the GGT-NN model means that it is less time and space efficient than these other networks," but don't provide evidence that the benefits outweigh this cost for real-world applications.

Fourth, the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton, Turing machines) are too simplistic to demonstrate the model's capabilities on complex graph-based problems. The paper doesn't show results on more challenging graph-based tasks that would better demonstrate the model's advantages.

## 4. Additional feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a fair comparison with existing methods, training all models with the same level of supervision (only input text and final answer, without full graph supervision).

2. Provide detailed ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component (graph transformations, direct reference, etc.).

3. Address the computational complexity issue more thoroughly, including potential optimizations (e.g., sparse connections) and their impact on performance.

4. Demonstrate the model on more complex graph-based tasks beyond the simple rule discovery examples presented.

5. Provide a clearer justification for why the dynamic graph construction is necessary rather than simply using a fixed graph structure with a more sophisticated node representation.

## 5. Questions for the authors

- How would the model's performance change if trained with the same level of supervision as the comparison models (i.e., only using input text and final answer, without full graph supervision)?

- Can you provide more details about the implementation of the graph transformations and their computational complexity, particularly for larger graphs?

- What specific tasks would you say the GGT-NN model is uniquely suited for compared to existing approaches, and why wouldn't those tasks be solvable with existing methods?

- How do you plan to address the quadratic complexity issue for larger graphs, and what trade-offs would be involved?

- Can you provide more evidence of the model's ability to learn "complex" update rules beyond the simple cellular automaton and Turing machine examples?

The paper presents an interesting extension to graph neural networks, but the evidence provided is insufficient to justify the novelty and practical utility of the approach. The claims about performance advantages are based on an unfair comparison, and the paper fails to adequately address the significant computational limitations of the model. Without stronger evidence demonstrating clear advantages over existing methods that would justify the added complexity, the contribution is not sufficiently compelling for publication at ICLR.
