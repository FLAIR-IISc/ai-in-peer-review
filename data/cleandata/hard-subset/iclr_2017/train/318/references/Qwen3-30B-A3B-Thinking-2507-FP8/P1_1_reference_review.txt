

# Peer Review: "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs) by allowing the construction and modification of graph structures from unstructured input. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable the network to dynamically build and manipulate graph representations during processing. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on three main tasks: the bAbI question-answering benchmark (where it achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks), learning the rules of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and simulating arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper makes a compelling argument that graphical representations are particularly well-suited for certain structured reasoning tasks, and the GGT-NN model successfully bridges the gap between unstructured input and structured graph-based reasoning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons: The paper makes a clear, well-motivated contribution to the field of graph neural networks by addressing a significant limitation of existing approaches (fixed graph structures). The model is well-justified, thoroughly evaluated on multiple challenging tasks, and provides strong empirical evidence that graphical representations can improve performance on structured reasoning tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper excels in addressing a meaningful gap in the literature. Previous GNN approaches (Gori et al., 2005; Li et al., 2016) require a fixed graph structure as input, limiting their applicability to problems where the structure is not known in advance. The GGT-NN's ability to dynamically construct graphs from unstructured input (like natural language) represents a significant step forward. 

The experimental evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. For the bAbI tasks, the authors demonstrate that their model with direct reference outperforms previous approaches on several tasks, particularly those naturally suited to graphical representations (e.g., pathfinding task 19, where they achieve 64% improvement over the MemNN baseline). The ablation study showing that direct reference significantly improves performance (95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks vs. 9/20 without it) provides strong evidence for the value of their design choice.

The rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) provide compelling evidence of the model's ability to learn complex update rules without explicit supervision about the graph structure. The fact that the model can generalize to longer sequences (20-30 timesteps) demonstrates robust learning of the underlying rules.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough comparison to related work in Section 6. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from Memory Networks, DNCs, HAM, and other structured reasoning models, emphasizing that GGT-NN explicitly models the internal state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges, rather than implicitly storing structured information in vector form.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions for improvement:

1. **Scalability Analysis**: The authors mention that the quadratic time complexity of the model is a limitation but don't provide a thorough analysis of how this impacts performance on larger graphs. It would be valuable to include a small experiment showing how the model's performance and training time scale with graph size.

2. **Training Time Comparison**: The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model was trained with only 1,000 examples, which is impressive. However, it would be helpful to compare training time with the baselines, as the quadratic complexity might lead to significantly longer training times.

3. **Visualization**: The paper references Figure 3 showing the model at various stages of training, but doesn't provide the actual visualization. Including a representative visualization would help readers understand how the graph structure evolves during training.

4. **Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, adding a brief theoretical argument for why the graphical representation would be beneficial for certain tasks (beyond the pathfinding example) would strengthen the motivation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the bAbI task 17 (Positional Reasoning), the model achieves only 95% accuracy despite other tasks reaching 100%. The authors suggest this is due to the larger number of entities. However, would adding more training examples or modifying the graph representation (e.g., by grouping entities) help improve performance on this task?

2. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it, but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why this is the case. Could the authors provide more insight into how the direct reference mechanism specifically helps the model learn the graph structure?

3. The paper discusses the quadratic time complexity as a limitation. Have the authors explored any techniques to make the model more efficient, such as sparse graph representations or selective node processing, as mentioned in the conclusion?

4. For the rule discovery tasks, the model was trained on 1,000 examples for Rule 30 and 20,000 for Turing machines. How does the model's performance scale with more training examples for these tasks, and does it reach a plateau?

Overall, this is a strong contribution to the field of graph neural networks with clear motivation, thorough evaluation, and significant empirical results. The paper makes a compelling case that the ability to dynamically construct and modify graph structures from unstructured input is a valuable capability for neural network models. I believe this work will be of interest to the ICLR community and should be accepted for publication.
