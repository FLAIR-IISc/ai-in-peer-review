

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by enabling differentiable graph structure modifications. Unlike existing GNN approaches that require fixed graph structures as input, the GGT-NN can build and modify graph representations based on unstructured input (e.g., natural language sentences). The authors propose five types of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to process graphs in complex ways. The paper demonstrates the model's effectiveness on two main tasks: the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on most tasks) and rule discovery tasks (learning to model cellular automata and Turing machines). The key contribution is a framework that can learn to construct and manipulate graph-structured internal states from unstructured input, which enables applications that were previously difficult with existing graph neural network approaches.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

Key reasons: 
1) The paper identifies a clear gap in the literature (existing GNNs cannot build or modify graph structures) and provides a well-motivated solution.
2) The empirical results are strong and well-supported, demonstrating the model's ability to learn graph-structured representations for diverse tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the need for a model that can build and modify graph structures. The authors have carefully positioned their work within the literature, with a thorough comparison to related approaches (Section 6) that clearly demonstrates how GGT-NNs differ from memory networks, DNCs, HAM, and other structured data processing approaches.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. For the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN model achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 out of 20 tasks (with direct reference), outperforming many strong baselines on specific tasks (e.g., 64% improvement on task 19, pathfinding). The performance on pathfinding is particularly impressive, as it's a task naturally suited to graph representations, and the GGT-NN model can solve it directly from textual input without requiring preprocessed graph data—unlike previous GGS-NN models that required preprocessed graph input.

For rule discovery tasks, the model successfully learns to model Rule 30 cellular automata and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines, achieving high accuracy (95%+ for Rule 30, 98% for Turing machines) and demonstrating generalization to longer sequences. This shows the model can learn complex graph-based rules from scratch, which is a significant capability.

The paper also provides valuable insights through ablation studies (e.g., comparing models with and without direct reference), showing that direct reference significantly improves training efficiency and final performance. The authors have thoughtfully addressed the limitations of their approach (quadratic complexity, need for supervision) and suggested promising directions for future work.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance it further:

- The paper mentions the quadratic complexity of GGT-NN as a limitation (Section 6), but doesn't provide concrete numbers on how this impacts training time or memory requirements for the tasks they evaluated. Adding a brief analysis of computational complexity for the bAbI tasks would strengthen the discussion of limitations.

- The paper uses strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep), which might not be practical for all applications. While the authors acknowledge this as a limitation (Section 7), they don't explore how the model performs with less supervision. A small experiment showing the model's performance with reduced supervision would be valuable.

- The rule discovery tasks (Rule 30 and 4-state Turing machines) are relatively simple. Including a more complex rule discovery task would better demonstrate the model's ability to learn more complex graph transformations.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1) The paper states that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks." However, it doesn't explain why direct reference is so important. Could you elaborate on how direct reference helps the model learn the graph structure more effectively?

2) The paper uses 1,000 examples for Rule 30 and 20,000 examples for Turing machines. Could you explain why different numbers of examples were used for these tasks, and whether the model would perform similarly with the same number of examples?

3) The paper claims the GGT-NN model with direct reference "performs better than all but one of the other models (DMN+)" on task 19. However, it doesn't provide the exact accuracy numbers for DMN+ on this task. Could you share the DMN+ accuracy for task 19 to allow for a more precise comparison?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks by enabling the construction and modification of graph structures, which opens up new applications for neural networks working with structured data. The results are convincing, the approach is well-motivated, and the paper is clearly written. I believe it would be a valuable contribution to ICLR.
