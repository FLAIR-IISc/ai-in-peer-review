- Introduces GGT-NN: a neural architecture that dynamically modifies graph structure via five differentiable transformations and is tested on bAbI, Rule 30, and Turing machines.  
- Decision: Reject — contribution seen as incremental, novelty overstated, evaluation flawed, and computational limits inadequately addressed.  
- Overstated novelty: GGT-NN is largely a straightforward extension of GGS-NNs and the claimed differentiable transformations are variations of existing operations.  
- Superficial related-work comparison: The paper fails to clearly distinguish GGT-NN from prior models in Section 6.  
- Flawed evaluation: bAbI experiments are unfair because GGT-NN uses strong per-timestep graph supervision while baselines are trained end-to-end, making comparisons misleading.  
- Misleading claims: Authors acknowledge extra supervision but do not properly contextualize or control for it in comparisons.  
- Computational limitations ignored: Quadratic time/space complexity is noted but not empirically analyzed or evaluated for practical impact.  
- Missing scalability analysis: No measurements of training time/memory or experiments with proposed mitigations (sparse edges, subset processing).  
- Clarify novelty request: Authors should precisely articulate how GGT-NN differs from GGS-NNs and detail transformation/architecture differences.  
- Re-evaluate comparisons request: bAbI results should be re-run with fair supervision across models or include analysis of supervision effects.  
- Address complexity request: Include empirical scaling analysis and discuss/experiment with techniques to mitigate quadratic complexity.  
- Provide more analysis request: Explain why direct reference helps and include visualizations (learned graphs, attention) to justify claims.  
- Questions for authors: clarify fundamental differences from GGS-NN; explain superior performance on bAbI task 19; detail mechanism behind direct reference improvement; and specify concrete strategies to scale beyond quadratic complexity.
