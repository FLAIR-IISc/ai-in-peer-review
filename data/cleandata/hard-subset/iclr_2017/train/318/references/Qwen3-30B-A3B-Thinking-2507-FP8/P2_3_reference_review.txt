

# Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Contribution

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by enabling dynamic construction and modification of graph structures based on unstructured input. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to incrementally build and modify graph representations. This approach enables the model to process unstructured inputs (like natural language) and produce graph-structured outputs, which can then be used for tasks such as question answering, cellular automaton simulation, and Turing machine modeling.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of GGT-NN through extensive experiments on the bAbI tasks (where it achieves 95%+ accuracy on most tasks), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The authors show that the model's graph-structured internal state provides advantages for certain tasks (like pathfinding), and that direct reference mechanisms significantly improve performance.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

### Strong Theoretical Contribution
The paper makes a clear theoretical contribution by formalizing a set of differentiable graph transformations that extend previous work. The GGT-NN model addresses a genuine limitation in existing graph neural network approaches (fixed graph structures) and provides a well-motivated solution. The mathematical formulation of the graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) is rigorous and clearly explained, with detailed pseudocode and implementation details provided in the appendices.

### Comprehensive Empirical Validation
The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed, with multiple task domains demonstrating the model's versatility:
- On the bAbI tasks, the model achieves state-of-the-art results (95%+ accuracy on most tasks) while using only 1,000 training examples, significantly less than many competing models.
- The pathfinding task (Task 19) shows a dramatic 64% improvement over the MemNN model, which is compelling evidence of the advantages of graph-based representations for certain problems.
- The cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex rule-based transformations without explicit task-specific programming.

The ablation studies (comparing models with and without direct reference) provide strong evidence for the effectiveness of key design choices. The authors also provide thoughtful analysis of why certain tasks (like Task 17) were more challenging, showing good scientific rigor.

### Strong Positioning in the Literature
The paper provides an excellent literature review that carefully situates GGT-NN within the broader context of graph neural networks, memory networks, and related architectures. The comparison with related work is fair and comprehensive, highlighting both the similarities and key differences between GGT-NN and existing approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong overall, I have a few suggestions to make it even better:

1. **Clarify the "Strong Supervision" Advantage**: The paper notes that GGT-NN and MemNN both use strong supervision, but it's not entirely clear how the graph-level supervision differs from the sentence-level supervision used in MemNN. A more detailed comparison of the supervision requirements would strengthen the claims about why graph-level supervision is beneficial.

2. **Address Computational Complexity More Explicitly**: The paper mentions that the quadratic time/space complexity is a limitation but doesn't quantify it. For example, how does the model scale with graph size (number of nodes)? Providing some computational complexity analysis would help readers understand the practical limitations.

3. **Discuss Generalization Beyond the Evaluated Tasks**: While the paper shows good results on the specific tasks evaluated, it would be valuable to discuss how the model might generalize to other graph-based tasks not tested here (e.g., protein folding, social network analysis).

4. **Clarify the Relationship to Other Graph-Based Architectures**: The paper mentions that GGT-NN is different from graph convolutional networks (GCNs) but could more explicitly discuss how it relates to other recent graph-based architectures like Graph Attention Networks (GATs).

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper mentions that the model requires additional supervision (graph-level supervision at each timestep) to extract meaningful graph-structured data. Could you provide more details about the specific challenges in training without this supervision, and what alternative approaches you've considered to reduce this supervision requirement?

2. In the comparison with other memory-based architectures (DNC, MemNN, etc.), you note that GGT-NN's distributed nature makes it less time/space efficient. Could you elaborate on potential optimization strategies (e.g., sparsity, selective node processing) that could mitigate this issue, as mentioned in the conclusion?

3. The paper achieves 100% accuracy on 11 of the 20 bAbI tasks. For the tasks where the model doesn't reach 100% accuracy (particularly Task 17), could you provide more specific analysis about what specific aspects of the task are challenging for the model, and what changes might help improve performance?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graph neural networks. The proposed GGT-NN architecture is well-motivated, rigorously formulated, and demonstrated to be effective on multiple challenging tasks. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the ICLR community and will likely inspire further research in graph-based neural architectures.
