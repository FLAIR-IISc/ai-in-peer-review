

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends previous graph neural network approaches by allowing the model to dynamically construct and modify graph structures based on unstructured input (like natural language). Unlike existing graph neural networks that process fixed graph structures, the GGT-NN can learn to build graph representations of the world state from textual input and manipulate those structures through a set of differentiable graph transformations. The authors demonstrate this approach on three distinct domains: bAbI language reasoning tasks (where the model constructs a graph representation of the story state), 1D cellular automata (Rule 30), and Turing machine simulation. The paper makes a compelling case that graphical state representations are particularly beneficial for tasks involving relationships and spatial reasoning.

## Decision
Accept. The core contribution of the paper—enabling neural networks to dynamically construct and manipulate graph structures rather than just processing fixed graphs—is a significant conceptual advance that opens up new possibilities for neural network architectures. The novelty of the approach is substantial enough to outweigh minor methodological limitations.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper excels in presenting a bold, novel direction for graph neural networks that addresses a clear limitation in existing work. Previous approaches (GNNs, GGS-NNs, memory networks, DNCs) all require fixed graph structures or implicit representations, whereas the GGT-NN explicitly models the internal state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges. This is not just an incremental improvement but a fundamental shift in how neural networks can interact with structured data.

The paper's empirical evidence strongly supports its claims. The model achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks (with direct reference), outperforming other approaches on tasks naturally suited to graphical representations (like pathfinding in task 19). The ability to learn Rule 30 cellular automata and Turing machines from raw input is particularly impressive and demonstrates the model's generality. The authors make a convincing case that graphical representations are superior to linear sequence representations for certain reasoning tasks, as evidenced by the GGT-NN's performance on task 19 (pathfinding) where it achieves 64% higher accuracy than the MemNN baseline.

The paper is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly identify the limitations of previous approaches (Section 6) and position their work as a meaningful advancement. The comparison with related work is thorough and helps clarify the novelty of their approach. The authors also demonstrate the flexibility of their framework through various task-specific configurations, showing that the same core architecture can be adapted to different problem domains.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed analysis of why task 17 (Positional Reasoning) presents challenges for the model. The authors note it involves more entities (20 vs. fewer in other tasks) and shorter stories, but a more systematic analysis would help clarify the limitations of the approach.

2. The computational complexity (quadratic in graph size) is acknowledged but not thoroughly addressed. The authors should discuss potential optimizations (sparse representations, selective node processing) that could make the model more scalable for larger graphs.

3. Ablation studies showing the contribution of specific components (direct reference, propagation steps, etc.) would strengthen the claims about what makes the model effective.

4. The paper could more clearly articulate how the GGT-NN differs from the GGS-NN approach described in Li et al. (2016), which the authors claim to build upon. The distinction between "graph-structured intermediate representations" and previous approaches needs to be more explicit.

## Questions for Authors
1. How would you address the computational complexity issue for larger graphs? Are there plans to implement sparse graph representations or other optimization techniques to make the model more scalable?

2. Can you provide more insight into why task 17 (Positional Reasoning) is particularly challenging for the model, and whether this is a fundamental limitation of the approach or something that could be addressed with additional training data or architectural modifications?

3. How does the direct reference mechanism compare to other attention mechanisms used in previous work? Could the model be trained without direct reference, and if so, what would be the trade-offs in terms of performance and training data requirements?

This paper makes a genuinely novel contribution to the field of graph neural networks, opening up new possibilities for how neural networks can interact with structured data. The bold approach and clear demonstration of its value across multiple domains make it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
