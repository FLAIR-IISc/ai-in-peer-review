

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and graph-structured outputs. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) that enable the network to modify graph structure based on unstructured input. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks) and on learning complex rule-based tasks like cellular automata and Turing machines. The paper makes a strong case for the value of explicit graph representations in structured reasoning tasks.

## 2. Decision

I recommend rejection. While the paper presents an interesting extension to graph neural networks with promising empirical results, it lacks sufficient theoretical grounding, the experimental evaluation is not sufficiently rigorous, and the claimed advantages over existing methods are overstated due to unfair comparison conditions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main weakness is the unfair comparison with baseline models. The GGT-NN is trained with strong supervision (full graph information at each timestep), while most baselines (LSTM, MemNN, MemN2N, etc.) are trained end-to-end without additional supervision. This gives the GGT-NN an unfair advantage, as the baselines must learn to extract graph structures from raw text, while the GGT-NN is explicitly provided with the correct graph structure. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't sufficiently address how this affects the comparison's validity.

The paper also lacks theoretical analysis of the computational complexity and limitations of the approach. While it mentions the quadratic time/space complexity, it doesn't thoroughly analyze the trade-offs between this model and alternatives like Memory Networks or DNCs, especially for larger graphs. This is a significant gap given the paper's claim about the model's applicability.

Furthermore, the paper doesn't provide sufficient detail on hyperparameters, training procedures, or the exact nature of the "strong supervision" used. Without this information, the results are difficult to reproduce and interpret. The analysis of task 17's failure (with 20 unique entities) is superficial and doesn't explore whether the model would have succeeded with more training examples or architectural modifications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Conduct a fairer comparison by training the GGT-NN without graph-level supervision (or with minimal supervision) and compare it directly with end-to-end baselines. This would more accurately demonstrate the model's advantages.

2. Provide a more detailed theoretical analysis of the computational complexity, including how the quadratic scaling affects practical applications and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing).

3. Include ablation studies to identify which components of the GGT-NN architecture contribute most to performance, particularly the direct reference mechanism.

4. Provide more detailed analysis of failure cases, including visualization of the graph representations the model learns, to better understand its limitations.

5. Include a thorough discussion of the trade-offs between this approach and existing methods, particularly regarding the quadratic complexity versus the benefits of explicit graph representation.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How would you modify the training procedure to reduce the reliance on full graph supervision while maintaining performance on the bAbI tasks? Could you provide preliminary results for a version trained without graph-level supervision?

2. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it. Could you provide a detailed analysis of why direct reference is so beneficial, and what specific aspects of the tasks it helps with?

3. The paper states that the GGT-NN model shows perfect output on extended versions of the rule discovery tasks (20 and 30 timesteps). Could you clarify whether this means 100% accuracy on all 1000 examples, or whether the accuracy remains high across the extended tasks?

4. Given the quadratic complexity, what specific strategies would you recommend for scaling this model to larger graphs, and how would these affect the model's ability to learn complex graph structures?

The paper presents a promising direction for graph-structured reasoning, but to make a compelling case for its contribution, the authors need to address the issues of fair comparison, theoretical grounding, and practical limitations. With these improvements, the paper could make a significant contribution to the field of neural networks for structured data.
