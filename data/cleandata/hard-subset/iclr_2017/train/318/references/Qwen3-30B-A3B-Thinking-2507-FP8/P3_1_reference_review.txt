

# Peer Review: "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by enabling dynamic construction and modification of graph structures during inference. The key contribution is a framework of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) that allow the model to learn to build and manipulate graph representations from unstructured input. The authors demonstrate the model on three main tasks: the bAbI question-answering tasks (showing superior performance on graph-suitable tasks like pathfinding), and two rule discovery tasks (cellular automata and Turing machines). The paper makes a compelling argument that explicitly modeling graph structure as an internal state provides advantages over implicit representations used in memory networks.

## 2. Decision

**Reject**

Key reasons: While the paper presents an interesting extension to GNNs, the novelty is limited, and the experimental validation is insufficient to support the claimed advantages over existing approaches.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

**Novelty and Motivation (Weak):** The core idea of allowing graph structure to be learned rather than fixed is not entirely new. The paper's motivation section correctly identifies the limitation of fixed-structure GNNs, but the proposed solution builds directly on the GGS-NN framework from Li et al. (2016), which already allows for sequential processing of graphs. The "differentiable graph transformations" framework appears to be a re-packaging of existing GNN components with additional supervision mechanisms, rather than a truly novel architectural innovation. The paper fails to clearly distinguish how the GGT-NN differs from the GGS-NN in a way that justifies the new name and framework.

**Experimental Rigor (Insufficient):** The bAbI results are presented as compelling evidence, but the experimental setup is problematic. The authors claim strong supervision by providing the correct graph at each timestep, which fundamentally changes the problem from learning to represent the graph from text to learning to reconstruct a pre-specified graph. This is not comparable to the standard bAbI evaluation where models must learn the graph structure from the text. The comparison with other models (like MemNN) is misleading because those models were trained end-to-end without graph-level supervision, while the GGT-NN was trained with this additional supervision. The paper doesn't provide results on the standard bAbI evaluation protocol without this strong supervision.

**Methodological Clarity (Lacking):** The paper lacks critical details about the computational complexity and scalability of the proposed method. The quadratic complexity in graph size (mentioned in Section 6) is a significant limitation that isn't adequately addressed. The paper claims the model can "learn complex update rules" for Turing machines, but the results in Table 3 show 99.6% accuracy for Rule 30 and 98.5% for Turing machines - these high numbers don't necessarily indicate the model has learned the underlying rules, but rather that the tasks are simple enough for the model to memorize the input-output mappings. The paper doesn't provide evidence that the model generalizes to unseen rule sets or graph sizes beyond the training distribution.

**Comparison with Related Work (Incomplete):** The paper correctly identifies that existing memory networks represent structured data implicitly, but fails to provide a rigorous comparison of the explicit graph representation's advantages. The claim that "the produced graph can be extracted, visualized, and potentially used in downstream applications" is not supported by any empirical evidence - the paper doesn't show any visualization of the learned graph structure or demonstrate how it could be used in downstream tasks.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

- **Clarify the novelty:** Explicitly state how the GGT-NN differs from GGS-NN in a way that justifies the new name and framework. The paper should demonstrate that the model can learn graph structure from unstructured input without the strong supervision used in the bAbI experiments.

- **Re-run experiments on standard bAbI protocol:** The most convincing evidence would be results on the standard bAbI evaluation where the model must learn the graph structure from the text without being provided the correct graph at each timestep.

- **Provide more thorough analysis of generalization:** The paper claims the model "shows the ability to generalize to large inputs," but Table 3 only shows performance on 20 and 30 timesteps, which are not significantly larger than the training inputs (7 and 6 timesteps). The paper should demonstrate generalization to much larger graph sizes or more complex graph structures.

- **Include computational complexity analysis:** Provide a clear analysis of how the quadratic complexity scales with graph size, and how this affects training time and memory requirements for larger graphs.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How does the model perform on the standard bAbI evaluation protocol without the strong graph-level supervision described in Section 4.1? Without this, the claimed advantage over other models is not supported.

2. The paper claims the GGT-NN can "learn complex update rules" for Turing machines, but the results show high accuracy on the specific training examples. Can the model generalize to Turing machines with rules not seen during training?

3. The paper mentions "using sparse edge connections" as a potential future optimization in Section 7, but doesn't address how this would affect the differentiability of the graph transformations. How would the model handle sparse graphs while maintaining the differentiable properties?

4. The paper claims the model "can process data in a distributed fashion," but the quadratic complexity (Section 6) suggests it's not truly distributed in a scalable way. How does the paper reconcile these two claims?

This paper presents an interesting extension to GNNs but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its claims of novelty and superiority. The experimental validation is compromised by the strong supervision used, and the paper doesn't adequately address the computational limitations of the proposed approach. With significant revisions addressing these issues, the paper could be strengthened to make a more compelling contribution to the field.
