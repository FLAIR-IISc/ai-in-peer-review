

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes a new neural network architecture called GGT-NN (Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network) that extends existing Graph Neural Network models (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and graph-structured outputs. The authors claim that their model can incrementally construct a graph from natural language input, using a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation). They demonstrate the model on three tasks: bAbI question-answering tasks, a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper claims that the model achieves high accuracy on these tasks, particularly excelling in tasks that naturally lend themselves to graphical representation (like pathfinding in bAbI task 19), and that it provides a more interpretable internal state representation compared to other approaches like Memory Networks or DNCs.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to justify the claimed novelty and advantages of the proposed approach. The experimental evaluation is not rigorous, and the paper fails to demonstrate that the proposed method provides significant advantages over established baselines in a fair comparison.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary issue with this paper is the lack of rigorous validation against established baselines. While the authors claim their GGT-NN model achieves high accuracy on bAbI tasks (reaching 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks), they trained the model with "strong supervision" - meaning they provided the correct graph at each timestep. In contrast, all other baseline models (including MemNN, MemN2N, and DMN+) were trained end-to-end without any additional graph-level supervision. This creates an unfair comparison, as the GGT-NN model essentially has access to much more information about the internal state than the other models. The paper even acknowledges this by stating that "the GGT-NN and MemNN models were trained with strong supervision," yet still claims superiority on some tasks without demonstrating that the strong supervision is necessary or that the model can perform well with less supervision.

The paper also lacks statistical significance testing for the reported performance improvements. For example, when the authors claim that the GGT-NN model with direct reference outperforms the MemNN model on certain tasks by "0.4-64%," they don't provide any statistical evidence that these differences are meaningful. Without such tests, it's impossible to determine whether these improvements represent a genuine advantage of the method or simply random fluctuations.

Furthermore, the paper fails to provide a thorough ablation study to isolate which components of the GGT-NN architecture are actually contributing to the performance. The authors claim that "direct reference" greatly improves training, but they don't show how the model would perform without this component, or how it compares to other methods of providing similar information to the network.

The computational complexity of the model (quadratic in the number of nodes) is acknowledged but not properly addressed. The paper claims that the distributed nature of the model "allows complex computations to be distributed across the nodes of the graph," but it doesn't provide any evidence that this distributed computation actually leads to better performance on large-scale problems, or how it compares to other approaches in terms of scalability.

## 4. Additional Feedback

1. **Fair Comparison**: To make a convincing case for the advantages of GGT-NN, the authors should retrain all models on the bAbI tasks using the same supervision paradigm. Specifically, they should train both GGT-NN and baseline models end-to-end without graph-level supervision, and then compare performance. This would provide a fairer evaluation of the model's capabilities.

2. **Statistical Significance**: The paper should include statistical significance tests for all reported performance differences. Without these, it's impossible to determine whether the observed improvements are meaningful or due to chance.

3. **Ablation Studies**: The authors should provide ablation studies showing how each component of the GGT-NN architecture contributes to the overall performance. This would help clarify which aspects of the model are truly novel and beneficial.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper should provide a more thorough analysis of the computational costs of the GGT-NN model compared to other approaches. The quadratic complexity with respect to graph size is a significant limitation that needs to be addressed, particularly for practical applications.

5. **Generalization Analysis**: The paper claims the model shows "the ability to generalize to large inputs," but the evaluation only tests on 20 and 30 timesteps, which are still relatively small. A more comprehensive analysis of generalization to much larger inputs would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors

1. How would the performance of the GGT-NN model change if it were trained without the strong supervision (i.e., without providing the correct graph at each timestep)? Could it still achieve comparable performance to the baselines trained end-to-end?

2. The paper claims that the GGT-NN model is better suited for tasks with natural graphical representations (like pathfinding), but doesn't provide any theoretical justification for why the graph structure should lead to better performance for these tasks. Can the authors provide a more rigorous explanation for this claim?

3. The paper mentions that "the distributed nature of the GGT-NN model means that it is less time and space efficient than these other networks." How does the authors' claim that the model can solve tasks like pathfinding "in a distributed fashion" reconcile with this computational inefficiency? Is the distributed nature truly advantageous for these tasks, or is it simply a consequence of the quadratic complexity?

4. The paper doesn't provide any analysis of the graph structures learned by the model. How do these graphs compare to the ground truth structures used in training? Are they interpretable, and do they capture the relevant relationships for the task?
