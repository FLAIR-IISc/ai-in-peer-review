

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by enabling differentiable graph transformations. The key contribution is a framework of five graph transformation operations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to build and modify graph structures based on unstructured input. The authors demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex graphical state transitions on the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks) and on rule discovery tasks (learning Rule 30 cellular automata and Turing machines). The paper makes a compelling case for explicit graph representations as a powerful intermediate state for structured reasoning tasks.

## 2. Decision
Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses a meaningful gap in the literature: existing GNNs require fixed graph structures as input, while GGT-NN can dynamically construct and modify graphs based on unstructured input. The approach is well-motivated by a thorough review of related work, correctly positioning the contribution within the landscape of graph neural networks and structured reasoning models. The experimental results are scientifically rigorous with appropriate baselines (including simple LSTM, Memory Networks, and state-of-the-art approaches), ablation studies (with/without direct reference), and careful analysis of the model's strengths (particularly on graph-suited tasks like pathfinding) and limitations (task 17's complexity).

The paper provides strong evidence for its claims: the GGT-NN model with direct reference achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks, outperforming other models on tasks naturally suited to graph representations. The rule discovery experiments demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex update rules without explicit supervision beyond the graph structure, with high accuracy on both cellular automata and Turing machine tasks. The supplementary materials provide sufficient technical details for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback
The paper is well-written and clear overall. To further improve it:

- The computational complexity discussion (Section 6) could be strengthened with more specific details about the trade-offs between the distributed nature of GGT-NN and the efficiency of other approaches. A concrete example of how the quadratic complexity impacts practical applications would be valuable.
- The paper mentions "optimizations that would allow the model to train faster" but doesn't provide specific suggestions. A brief discussion of potential approaches (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing) would strengthen the future work section.
- The ablation study (Section 5.1.1) could be enhanced by providing more insight into why direct reference is so beneficial, beyond just showing it improves accuracy.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper states that the GGT-NN model's time and space complexity scales quadratically with graph size. Could you provide more specific examples of how this impacts training time and memory requirements for larger graphs (e.g., how long it would take to train on a graph with 100 nodes vs. 1000 nodes)?
- In the cellular automaton experiments, the model was trained on examples simulating 7 timesteps. How does the model's performance change when predicting longer sequences (e.g., 10, 20, or 30 timesteps), and what is the longest sequence it can reliably predict?
- For the edge update function (Section B.3), you mention using a "simple 2-layer fully connected network." Could you elaborate on why this architecture was chosen over alternatives (e.g., recurrent networks) and how the architecture choices might impact the model's ability to learn different types of graph transformations?
