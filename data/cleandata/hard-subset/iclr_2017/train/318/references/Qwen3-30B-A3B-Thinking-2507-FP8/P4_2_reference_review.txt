

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by enabling dynamic construction and modification of graph-structured intermediate representations from unstructured input (such as natural language). The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the network to build and manipulate graphs during processing. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on three task categories: bAbI question-answering tasks (where the model constructs a graph from textual input to answer questions), Rule 30 cellular automata (where the model learns to predict next states), and 4-state Turing machines (where the model learns to simulate the machine's operation). The paper claims that GGT-NNs provide a more flexible framework for graph-based learning than existing approaches by allowing the graph structure to be dynamically created rather than fixed.

## 2. Decision

Accept.

Key reasons: The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution to the field of graph neural networks by addressing a genuine limitation of existing approaches (fixed graph structures), and the empirical results on multiple challenging tasks demonstrate the model's effectiveness.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful gap in the literature: most graph-based neural networks require fixed graph structures as input, but many real-world problems require building or modifying graph structures based on input. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for their differentiable graph transformations, which are well-motivated extensions of existing GNN work. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous, with careful evaluation across multiple task domains (bAbI, cellular automata, Turing machines) and appropriate comparison to relevant baselines. The model achieves strong performance (95%+ accuracy on most bAbI tasks) and demonstrates clear advantages in tasks where graph representations are particularly beneficial (like pathfinding in bAbI task 19).

The paper's motivation is well-placed in the literature, with thorough comparison to related work including GNNs, GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, and others. The authors correctly identify that while other approaches can store information in structured forms, they lack the ability to dynamically construct and modify the graph structure itself, which is a key contribution of GGT-NN.

The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and detailed ablation studies (comparing models with and without direct reference). The results consistently demonstrate that the GGT-NN model outperforms baselines on tasks where graph-based representations are advantageous, and the authors provide reasonable explanations for why certain tasks (like bAbI task 17) are more challenging.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, the following points would improve clarity and impact:

1. The paper mentions computational complexity concerns (quadratic scaling) but doesn't provide concrete evidence of this limitation in practice. Adding a brief analysis of training time and memory requirements across different graph sizes would strengthen the discussion.

2. The comparison with other models could be made more equitable by training the GGT-NN with the same level of supervision as the baselines. The current comparison shows GGT-NN with strong supervision (graph-level information) versus baselines trained end-to-end. A more balanced comparison would be valuable.

3. The paper could benefit from a more detailed ablation study of the different graph transformations. It's unclear which transformations contribute most to the model's performance, which would help readers understand the key innovations.

4. The discussion of the "direct reference" mechanism is valuable, but the paper could clarify why this approach works better than alternatives (e.g., attention mechanisms) for the specific tasks studied.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that the GGT-NN model can generalize to larger inputs (e.g., 20-30 timesteps for the cellular automaton task). Could you provide more details about the generalization performance at these larger scales? Specifically, what was the accuracy at 20 timesteps versus 30 timesteps?

2. The paper mentions that the model requires "strong supervision" for training (graph-level information), which is different from most baselines. How much additional supervision (e.g., number of training examples with graph-level information) would be needed to achieve similar performance without this supervision?

3. In the cellular automaton task, the authors use a fixed graph structure with 13 initial cells. How would the model's performance change if the number of initial cells varied significantly (e.g., 5 vs. 20 cells)? Would the quadratic scaling of the propagation step become a limiting factor?

4. The paper claims that the GGT-NN model can "learn complex update rules" for Turing machines. Could you provide an example of a specific Turing machine rule that the model learned particularly well, and what aspects of the graph structure made this learning possible?
