

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends previous graph neural network approaches (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) by allowing for graph-structured intermediate representations and graph-structured outputs. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable the network to dynamically construct and modify graph structures based on unstructured input. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on three types of tasks: bAbI question-answering tasks (where the model constructs a graph from natural language input and answers questions based on this graph), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper claims that GGT-NNs outperform existing models on several tasks, particularly those where graph structure is naturally suited to the problem (like pathfinding), and that the model can generalize to larger inputs.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** the paper with minor revisions. The paper makes a valuable contribution to the graph neural network literature by addressing a clear limitation in existing approaches (fixed graph structures) and providing a well-motivated, flexible framework for learning graph transformations. The empirical results are generally strong and demonstrate the model's capabilities across multiple domains.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper excels in several key areas:

First, the motivation is clear and well-placed in the literature. The authors correctly identify that existing graph neural networks (GNNs, GG-NNs, GGS-NNs) require fixed graph structures, limiting their applicability to tasks where the graph structure must be dynamically determined from unstructured input. The paper effectively positions its contribution as a natural extension that enables neural networks to learn how to construct and modify graph representations based on input data.

Second, the technical contributions are significant. The introduction of differentiable graph transformations (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) provides a modular framework that can be configured for various tasks. The implementation details are well-described, and the mathematical formulations are clear and precise.

Third, the empirical results are compelling. The GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks, with particularly strong results on task 19 (pathfinding), where it outperforms all but one other model. The ability to generalize to larger inputs in the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks demonstrates the model's capacity to learn underlying rules from graph-structured data.

The main concern is the strong supervision used in the bAbI experiments (providing the correct graph at each timestep). While the paper acknowledges this as "additional supervision," it creates an uneven playing field compared to other models that are trained end-to-end. The authors note that "the GGT-NN and MemNN models were trained with strong supervision," but this is not adequately contextualized in the results. It would be valuable to see results with less supervision to better understand the model's true capabilities.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the supervision comparison**: Provide results with less supervision (e.g., only providing the final graph, or using a smaller subset of graph supervision) to better assess the model's ability to learn graph structures from unstructured input without full supervision.

2. **Expand the scalability analysis**: The paper mentions that "the time and space required to train the model increase very quickly as the complexity of the task increases," but doesn't provide concrete evidence. Add experiments showing how training time and memory consumption scale with graph size, and discuss potential optimizations (e.g., sparse connections).

3. **Improve the ablation study**: The paper shows that direct reference improves performance, but doesn't explore the impact of different transformation configurations. A more thorough ablation study would help understand which transformations are most critical for different tasks.

4. **Clarify the graph representation**: The paper mentions that "some of the graph representations had to be modified" for bAbI tasks, but doesn't detail these modifications. A clearer explanation of how the graph format was adapted for each task would help readers understand the model's flexibility.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. How would the performance of the GGT-NN change if the graph supervision was removed entirely (i.e., only providing the natural language input and the correct answer, with no intermediate graph supervision)?

2. The paper states that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks." Could you provide more specific examples of tasks where the model without direct reference performed poorly, and why direct reference was crucial for those tasks?

3. For the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), were there any specific challenges in representing the problem as a graph that required special adaptations to the GGT-NN architecture?

4. The paper mentions that "the distributed nature of the GGT-NN model means that it is less time and space efficient than these other networks." Could you provide a more concrete comparison of training time and memory usage for the GGT-NN versus other models on the same hardware?

The paper makes a strong contribution to the field of graph neural networks by addressing a meaningful limitation in existing approaches. With the suggested clarifications and additional analysis, it would be an excellent addition to the ICLR proceedings.
