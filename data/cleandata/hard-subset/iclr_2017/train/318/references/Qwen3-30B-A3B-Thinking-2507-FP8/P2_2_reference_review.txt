

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a significant extension of previous graph neural network architectures (GG-NN and GGS-NN) that enables the construction and modification of graph structures as part of the model's internal state. The key innovation is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations that allow the network to incrementally build a graph from textual input, rather than requiring a fixed graph structure as input. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on three distinct tasks: the bAbI question-answering tasks (where it achieves 95%+ accuracy on most tasks), 1D cellular automaton simulation (Rule 30), and arbitrary 4-state Turing machine simulation. The paper makes a strong case that modeling structured data as a graph with dynamic structure can lead to better performance on tasks that naturally involve relationships between entities.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **weak acceptance** of this paper.

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper makes a well-motivated contribution that addresses a clear gap in the literature regarding graph neural networks' ability to construct graph structures incrementally from unstructured input.
2. The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and demonstrate clear advantages of the approach on tasks that benefit from graph representations.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-motivated and properly situates itself within the literature. The authors correctly identify that existing graph neural networks (GNNs, GG-NNs, GGS-NNs) require a fixed graph structure as input, which limits their applicability to tasks where the structure itself needs to be learned. The introduction of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) provides a theoretically sound and practically implementable solution to this problem. The comparison with related work in Section 6 is thorough and highlights the key differences between GGT-NN and existing approaches.

The empirical evaluation is strong and well-designed. The bAbI results show that the GGT-NN model outperforms several baselines on tasks that naturally benefit from graph representations (particularly tasks 5, 7, 8, 17, 18, and 19). The ablation study (with/without direct reference) provides valuable insights into the model's behavior and confirms the importance of the direct reference mechanism. The cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks further demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex update rules, which is a significant contribution to the field.

The paper's limitations are acknowledged (computational complexity, need for supervision), and the authors suggest promising directions for future work. The writing is clear, the technical details are well-explained, and the paper is well-structured.

However, there are some concerns that prevent a stronger recommendation:
- The paper lacks sufficient detail on computational complexity and scalability. While the authors acknowledge this limitation in the conclusion, they don't provide experimental evidence of how the model's training time and memory usage scale with graph size.
- The claim that the model "learns complex update rules" for cellular automata and Turing machines is somewhat overstated. The results only show the model can simulate the specific rules it was trained on, not necessarily learn arbitrary rules beyond those seen during training.
- The paper doesn't include visualizations of the graphs produced by the model, which would help readers assess the quality of the representations.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To improve the paper, I recommend the following:

1. Add a section on computational complexity with experiments showing how training time and memory usage scale with graph size. This is crucial for understanding the model's practical applicability.

2. Include visualizations of the graphs produced by the model during training and inference. For example, show examples of the graph structure for a few bAbI tasks or cellular automaton steps. This would help readers understand the quality of the representations learned by the model.

3. Provide more details on the model's generalization capabilities. For the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks, how well does the model perform on rules it hasn't seen during training? For example, if trained on Rule 30, how well does it generalize to Rule 110?

4. Clarify the relationship between the GGT-NN and previous graph neural network work. While the paper does a good job of comparing with related work, it could better explain how the GGT-NN differs from and extends existing GNN architectures.

5. Add more details about the training procedure, particularly hyperparameters and how the model was tuned.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims the model can "learn complex update rules" for cellular automata and Turing machines. How well does the model generalize to rules it hasn't seen during training? For example, if trained on Rule 30, how well does it perform on Rule 110?

2. Can you provide more details about the computational complexity of the model? Specifically, how does training time and memory usage scale with the number of nodes in the graph?

3. The paper mentions that the model can be trained with "less supervision" in future work. What specific techniques are you considering for this, and how might they impact the model's performance?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graph neural networks. The GGT-NN architecture is well-conceived, the empirical results are convincing, and the paper is well-written. With some additional details on scalability and generalization, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
