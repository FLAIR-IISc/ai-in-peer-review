

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends GG-NNs and GGS-NNs to allow graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The authors propose a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to build a model capable of processing textual input and producing graph-structured outputs or answers to queries based on the internal graph state. The approach is evaluated on bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks (cellular automata and Turing machines), with the authors claiming strong performance relative to existing methods.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple technical inconsistencies and formatting errors that undermine confidence in the methodology and results.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper has numerous technical inconsistencies that would make it difficult to reproduce or understand the proposed method. These include:

1. **Critical notational inconsistencies**: The paper uses inconsistent notation throughout, particularly in equations. For example, in the GRU equations (2.1), the input vector is written as "Wx" instead of "Wx(t)", which is a critical error since the input to the GRU at time t is x(t), not x. This suggests a lack of attention to detail that undermines the technical credibility of the paper.

2. **Misuse of terminology**: The paper claims to introduce a "Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network" but never explains how the architecture relates to the Transformer architecture (which is based on attention mechanisms). The authors make no reference to attention mechanisms in the paper, making the "Transformer" name misleading and confusing.

3. **Graph representation inconsistencies**: The paper redefines graphs as having a connectivity matrix C ∈ R^{|V|×|V|×Y}, where Y is the number of edge types. This is unusual and not well-motivated. In standard graph theory, edge types are typically represented through labeled edges or a single adjacency matrix with edge labels, not a 3D tensor. The paper fails to justify why this representation is necessary or advantageous over standard approaches.

4. **Inconsistent notation**: The paper uses inconsistent notation for time steps (T vs t), node states (h̃(t) vs h~(t)), and variables (e.g., "x ∗ π(v) · ln(xv)" should be "x^*_π(v) · ln(x_v)"). The notation for the node adjustment loss (4.1) has a clear typo: "x ∗ π(v) · ln(xv)" should have a caret on the x (x^*), but it's missing.

5. **Technical errors in algorithms**: Algorithm 1 contains a clear typo: "G ← ∅ 2: for k from 1 to K do" should be "G ← ∅" followed by "for k from 1 to K do" without the "2:".

6. **Inconsistent references**: The paper states "Gates et al. (1992)" when it should be "Giles et al. (1992)" in the related work section, indicating a lack of careful proofreading.

7. **Missing critical information**: The paper claims "the GGT-NN model was able to reach 95% accuracy in all but one of the tasks" but fails to provide Table 2, which would be necessary to verify this claim. Similarly, the paper states "the GGT-NN model with direct reference performs better than all but one of the other models (DMN+)" but doesn't provide the actual accuracy numbers for comparison.

8. **Misleading claims**: The paper claims to "allow graph-structured intermediate representations, as well as graph-structured outputs" but the GGS-NN model (Li et al., 2016) already does this, making the claim of novelty questionable.

These issues collectively represent a substantial number of minor flaws that make it difficult to assess the paper's technical contributions with confidence. As a perfectionist reviewer, I cannot recommend acceptance of a paper with such pervasive technical inconsistencies.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Carefully proofread all equations and notation to ensure consistency throughout the paper. Pay particular attention to the GRU equations, which contain a critical error.

2. Justify the choice of graph representation (C ∈ R^{|V|×|V|×Y}) by comparing it to standard approaches and explaining the advantages of this representation.

3. Remove the misleading "Transformer" reference from the model name, or provide a clear explanation of how the architecture relates to the Transformer architecture.

4. Provide all tables and figures referenced in the text (such as Table 2) to allow readers to verify the claims about performance.

5. Correct the reference to "Giles et al." instead of "Gates et al." in the related work section.

6. Clarify how the graph structure is actually learned during training, as the paper claims to "learn complex update rules" but doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the graph structure is being learned rather than simply being a fixed representation.

## Questions for Authors
1. Can you clarify why the graph representation uses a 3D tensor C ∈ R^{|V|×|V|×Y} instead of a more standard representation (e.g., a single adjacency matrix with edge labels)? What advantages does this representation offer?

2. The paper claims to introduce a "Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network" but never explains the connection to the Transformer architecture. Could you clarify the relationship between the proposed architecture and the Transformer model?

3. In the node adjustment loss (4.1), the notation "x ∗ π(v) · ln(xv)" appears to be missing a caret on the x (should be x^*). Could you confirm this is a typo and provide the correct equation?

4. The paper states that "the GGT-NN model was able to reach 95% accuracy in all but one of the tasks" but fails to provide Table 2. Could you provide the accuracy numbers for verification?
