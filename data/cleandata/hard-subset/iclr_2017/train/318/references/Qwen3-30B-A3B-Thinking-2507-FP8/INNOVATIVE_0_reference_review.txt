

# Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

## Summary
This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) by enabling dynamic construction and manipulation of graph structures from unstructured input. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be composed to build a flexible framework for processing graph-structured data. The GGT-NN model demonstrates significant capabilities in learning to construct graph representations from natural language input and using these graphs to solve complex tasks, including the bAbI question-answering tasks, cellular automata, and Turing machines. The paper makes a compelling case for the importance of graph-structured internal representations in learning from unstructured data.

## Decision
Accept

## Key Reasons
1. The paper introduces a fundamentally novel concept: a neural network architecture that can dynamically construct and modify graph structures as part of its learning process, rather than just processing fixed graph inputs. This is a significant conceptual advance over previous graph neural network approaches.

2. The framework is general enough to be applied across multiple domains (natural language processing, rule discovery, cellular automata) and demonstrates strong empirical results across these diverse tasks, showing the versatility of the approach.

## Supporting Arguments
The GGT-NN framework represents a bold extension of graph neural networks that addresses a critical limitation of previous approaches. While existing GNNs require fixed graph structures as input (Li et al., 2016), GGT-NN allows the network to build and modify graph structures based on unstructured input. This is not just an incremental improvement but a new direction for neural network architectures that can work with structured data.

The paper's empirical results are compelling: the GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on most bAbI tasks (with 100% on 11 tasks), outperforms many strong baselines on specific tasks (particularly task 19, the pathfinding task, where it achieves 64% better accuracy than MemNN), and successfully learns complex rule-based systems like Rule 30 cellular automata and Turing machines. The demonstration of the model's ability to learn graph structure from natural language input is particularly impressive.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, clearly identifying the limitations of previous graph neural network approaches and positioning GGT-NN as a solution to these limitations. The comparison with related work (Memory Networks, DNCs, LRNNs) is thorough and effectively highlights the novelty of the approach.

While the paper uses strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep), this is justified by the authors as necessary for learning meaningful graph representations, and they compare against other strong-supervision methods (MemNN). The paper's main contribution is the framework itself, not necessarily the specific task performance.

## Additional Feedback
1. The paper could benefit from a clearer explanation of how the graph construction process is learned without the strong supervision. Specifically, how might the model generalize to cases where the correct graph is not provided during training?

2. The computational complexity (quadratic in graph size) is mentioned but not thoroughly discussed in terms of practical limitations. A brief discussion of potential optimizations (like sparse connections or selective node processing) would strengthen the paper.

3. While the paper demonstrates the ability to learn graph structures, it would be valuable to include some qualitative examples of the graphs constructed by the model, particularly for the bAbI tasks, to better illustrate the nature of the learned representations.

4. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN can be configured for different tasks through transformation composition, but a more detailed discussion of how the transformation configuration would change for different task types would be helpful.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it on most tasks. Could you elaborate on why direct reference is so beneficial, and whether this is a general property of the framework or specific to the bAbI tasks?

2. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference was trained with strong supervision (graph-level information), while other models (like MemNN) also used strong supervision. How would the performance change if the GGT-NN were trained with only the standard bAbI supervision (without graph-level information)?

3. The paper discusses the computational complexity as quadratic in graph size. For the bAbI tasks, what is the typical graph size, and how does the model scale with larger graphs (e.g., more entities or relationships)?

This paper makes a significant conceptual contribution to the field of graph neural networks by introducing a framework for dynamic graph construction. The novelty of the approach, combined with strong empirical results across multiple domains, makes it a compelling contribution to the field. The minor methodological concerns (strong supervision, computational complexity) do not detract from the fundamental novelty and potential impact of the work, and I believe this paper should be accepted.
