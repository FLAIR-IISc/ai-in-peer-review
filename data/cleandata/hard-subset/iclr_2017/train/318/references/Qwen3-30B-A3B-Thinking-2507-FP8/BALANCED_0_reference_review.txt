

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends previous graph neural network models by allowing the graph structure to be dynamically built and modified based on unstructured input (like text), rather than requiring a fixed graph structure as input. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to process graphs in complex ways. The model is evaluated on the bAbI question-answering tasks, where it demonstrates strong performance (reaching 95%+ accuracy on most tasks), and on two rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), where it successfully learns the underlying rules. The paper claims that this architecture enables more flexible and interpretable graph-based reasoning compared to existing approaches.

## 2. Decision

Reject. The paper makes an interesting contribution to graph-based neural networks, but the experimental results and analysis do not sufficiently demonstrate the advantages of the proposed approach over existing methods, particularly for the main bAbI tasks. The computational complexity limitations also aren't properly addressed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution—dynamically constructing graphs from unstructured input—is well-motivated and represents a meaningful extension of prior work. The five graph transformations are well-defined and seem to be a natural extension of existing graph neural network approaches.

However, the experimental evaluation has significant limitations. The paper claims that GGT-NN outperforms other models on several bAbI tasks, but the comparisons are not entirely fair. The GGT-NN is trained with "strong supervision" (full graph information at each timestep), while other models are trained end-to-end without additional supervision. This makes the comparison misleading, as the GGT-NN has significantly more information available during training. The paper acknowledges this but doesn't properly quantify how much this additional supervision contributes to the performance gains.

Furthermore, the paper claims the GGT-NN model "can be used as-is for tasks consisting of a sequence of input sentences and graphs," but doesn't provide clear evidence that the model learns meaningful graph representations without the strong supervision. The paper notes that "the model can be used without direct reference," but the performance without direct reference is significantly worse (9/20 tasks at 95% accuracy vs. 19/20 with direct reference), suggesting the graph representations may not be learned effectively without heavy supervision.

The computational complexity issue is also a major concern. The paper acknowledges that "the time and space required to train the model increase very quickly as the complexity of the task increases" but doesn't provide any experimental evidence to quantify this. For a model that's meant to be applied to real-world problems with potentially large graphs, this limitation needs to be properly addressed with concrete evidence.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough comparison with models trained under comparable supervision conditions. For example, if the GGT-NN uses full graph information, the comparison models should also be given some form of graph-level supervision to make the comparison fair.

2. Conduct ablation studies to quantify how much of the performance gain comes from the graph structure versus the additional supervision. For instance, how much would the model's performance drop if trained without the graph-level supervision?

3. Provide concrete evidence of the interpretability of the learned graph representations. What do the learned graphs look like for a specific bAbI task? Can humans understand the relationships being captured?

4. Address the computational complexity issue more thoroughly. The paper should include experiments showing how the model scales with graph size and complexity, and propose potential solutions (e.g., sparse graph representations) to mitigate these issues.

5. Expand the comparison to include more recent graph-based models beyond those mentioned in the related work section.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. How much of the performance gain on bAbI tasks is due to the graph structure itself versus the additional supervision (full graph information at each timestep)? Could you provide results for a version of the model trained without the graph-level supervision?

2. The paper mentions that "the graph transformations provided here may allow machine learning to interoperate more flexibly with other data sources and processes with structured inputs and outputs." Could you provide a concrete example of how the GGT-NN could be integrated with an external process that requires graph-structured input?

3. The paper states that the GGT-NN model "was trained using only 1,000 training examples" for bAbI tasks. How would the performance change with larger datasets (e.g., 10,000 or 100,000 examples)? Is there a point of diminishing returns for the model's performance with more training data?

4. The paper mentions that the model "can learn complex update rules" by training on cellular automata and Turing machines. Could you provide an example of a graph structure learned by the model for one of these tasks, and explain how it corresponds to the underlying rules?
