

# Peer Review: "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of existing Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) that allows for differentiable graph structure transformations. The key contribution is a set of graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable the network to incrementally construct and modify graph-structured internal states based on unstructured input, such as natural language. The authors demonstrate that this approach enables the network to learn complex update rules for tasks like question-answering on the bAbI dataset, simulating cellular automata (Rule 30), and modeling Turing machines. The paper makes a strong case for the utility of graph-structured internal states in modeling tasks with relational or spatial structure, showing that GGT-NNs outperform other models on specific tasks (particularly pathfinding) while maintaining flexibility through modular composition of graph transformations.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks by extending the paradigm to allow for dynamic graph structures, rather than being limited to fixed graph inputs. The approach is well-motivated, clearly articulated, and supported by thorough empirical evaluation across multiple challenging tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses a critical limitation in existing graph neural network approaches: the requirement for fixed graph structures. By introducing differentiable graph transformations, the authors enable neural networks to build and modify their own internal graph representations based on unstructured input, opening up new application domains.

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed. On the bAbI tasks, the GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks, with particularly strong performance on pathfinding (Task 19) where it outperforms all other models by a significant margin (64% improvement over MemNN). This demonstrates the clear advantage of graphical representations for certain tasks. The paper also convincingly shows the model's ability to generalize to new tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machine simulation) without task-specific architectural modifications.

The comparison with related work is thorough and fair. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from memory networks, DNCs, and other structured data models, emphasizing the explicit graph-structured internal state as a key differentiator. They also thoughtfully acknowledge limitations, particularly the quadratic complexity of graph operations, and suggest promising directions for future work.

The technical contributions are sound. The differentiable graph transformations are well-defined, with clear mathematical formulations and implementation details. The supervised training approach with additional graph-level supervision for node and edge learning is well-justified and effectively implemented.

## 4. Additional Feedback

While the paper is strong overall, I recommend the following improvements to enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the relationship between GGT-NN and GGS-NN**: The paper builds on GGS-NNs but doesn't sufficiently explain how GGT-NNs differ in architecture and capability. A clear figure comparing the two would help readers understand the evolution.

2. **Address computational complexity more thoroughly**: The quadratic complexity is acknowledged, but the paper could provide more concrete analysis of how this impacts real-world applications. How does the model scale to larger graphs (e.g., 100+ nodes)? What are the practical limits?

3. **Include ablation studies on transformation components**: While the paper compares direct-reference vs. non-direct-reference models, it would be valuable to see how each of the five graph transformations contributes to performance, especially for different tasks.

4. **Improve the presentation of results**: Some tables (like Table 1) would be clearer with more explicit comparisons between models and tasks. The paper could better highlight which specific tasks benefit most from the graph structure.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN with direct reference outperforms the model without it on most tasks, but the improvement on Task 17 (Positional Reasoning) is minimal (0.5%). Given that Task 17 has the most complex entity representation (20 unique entities), is there a particular reason why the direct reference mechanism doesn't help more on this task?

2. The authors state that the model generalizes well to larger inputs (e.g., 20-30 timesteps for the cellular automaton), but don't provide any quantitative analysis of this generalization. Could the authors provide more specific metrics on how performance changes as the input size increases?

3. The paper notes that the GGT-NN model has quadratic time/space complexity. Have the authors explored any techniques to mitigate this (e.g., sparse graph representations, sampling strategies, or approximation methods) that could make the model applicable to larger graphs?

4. For the bAbI tasks, the authors mention that they "exposed the knowledge object in graph format" but don't provide a detailed explanation of how this mapping was done. Could the authors elaborate on this transformation process, particularly for tasks requiring historical information (like Task 3)?

This paper makes a compelling contribution to the field of graph neural networks by enabling dynamic graph structure learning. The clear motivation, thorough evaluation, and well-executed experiments make a strong case for the value of graphical state transitions in neural network architectures. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the ICLR community.
