

# Peer Review: "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends previous graph neural network approaches (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) by enabling the construction and modification of graph structures from unstructured input. The key contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations that allow the network to dynamically build, modify, and reason with graph-structured internal states. The authors demonstrate that this approach can effectively solve tasks requiring graphical reasoning, including the bAbI question-answering tasks, cellular automata simulation, and Turing machine modeling. The paper makes a strong case that graph-structured internal representations provide advantages over sequence-based approaches for certain tasks, particularly those involving relationships, spatial reasoning, and temporal sequences.

## 2. Decision

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a well-motivated, novel approach that makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks. The technical innovation is solid, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages of the approach over existing methods for certain tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper successfully addresses a significant gap in the literature: most graph neural networks require fixed graph structures as input, making them unsuitable for tasks where the graph structure needs to be constructed from unstructured input. The authors provide a compelling argument for why a differentiable graph transformation framework is needed, and their GGT-NN model effectively fills this gap.

The experimental validation is strong and well-designed. The bAbI task results demonstrate that the GGT-NN model achieves state-of-the-art performance on several tasks (11 tasks at 100% accuracy, 19 tasks at 95%+ accuracy), with the most notable improvement (64% over MemNN) on the pathfinding task (task 19), which is naturally suited to graphical representation. The authors also provide insightful analysis of why certain tasks (like task 17) were more challenging, linking complexity to the number of entity types and story length.

The model's ability to generalize to larger inputs in the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks (perfect output on extended tasks with 20-30 timesteps) further demonstrates its robustness. The comparison with related work is thorough and well-placed, clearly distinguishing the GGT-NN's advantages over Memory Networks, DNCs, and other approaches.

The paper is well-written, with clear explanations of the technical innovations and sufficient detail in the appendices for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, I have a few suggestions to further strengthen it:

1. **Computational Complexity**: The paper acknowledges the quadratic time/space complexity of GGT-NNs but doesn't discuss potential solutions. I recommend adding a brief discussion on how this limitation could be addressed (e.g., sparse connections, selective node processing) and how these could be incorporated into the framework.

2. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The paper cites work up to 2016, but several relevant recent papers on graph neural networks have been published (e.g., GraphSAGE, GAT, Graph Isomorphism Networks). A brief comparison with these more recent approaches would strengthen the context.

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions "the code for each transformation, and for the GGT-NN model itself, is available at https://github.com/hexahedria/gated-graph-transformer-network" but doesn't include this in the main text. I recommend adding this link in the main paper for easier access.

4. **Task 17 Analysis**: The paper attributes the lower performance on task 17 to the larger number of entities, but it would be valuable to see a more detailed analysis of where the model fails (e.g., which specific questions it gets wrong) to better understand the limitations.

5. **Broader Impact Discussion**: The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of potential applications and ethical considerations of this technology, particularly regarding the interpretability of the graphical representations.

## 5. Clarifying Questions

1. The paper states that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks." Could the authors provide more details on which specific tasks were most affected by the absence of direct reference? This would help clarify the relative importance of this component.

2. In the cellular automaton experiments, the paper mentions "each of which simulated 7 timesteps of the automaton." For the Turing machine experiments, it states "each of which simulated 6 timesteps of the Turing machine." Why were these different numbers of timesteps used for the two tasks? Was there a specific reason for this difference, or was it arbitrary?

3. The paper mentions "the model with direct reference attains a higher level of accuracy on the following tasks, with an improvement of 0.4-64% depending on the task: task 5 (0.4%), task 7 (15%), task 8 (9%), task 17 (0.5%), task 18 (2.9%), and task 19 (64%)." The improvement for task 5 is only 0.4%, which seems very small. Could the authors elaborate on why this task showed such minimal improvement with direct reference?

4. The paper states that "the GGT-NN model was trained using only 1,000 training examples" for the bAbI tasks. For comparison, many other approaches use 10,000 examples. Could the authors provide more details on the data augmentation or training techniques that allowed the model to achieve high accuracy with such limited data?

These questions would help clarify some of the more nuanced aspects of the paper's results and methodology. Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
