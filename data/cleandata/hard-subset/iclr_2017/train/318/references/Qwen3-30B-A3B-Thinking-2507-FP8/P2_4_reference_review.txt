

# Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture that extends Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to allow for differentiable graph construction and modification. The key contributions are:

1. A redefinition of graph structures to be fully differentiable, where nodes have "strengths" (continuous values indicating existence) and edges have "belief" values (continuous values indicating connection likelihood)
2. A set of five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation)
3. A comprehensive framework that combines these transformations to build and manipulate graph structures from unstructured input (like natural language)
4. Demonstrations of the model's capabilities on the bAbI question-answering tasks, Rule 30 cellular automata, and Turing machines

The authors demonstrate that GGT-NNs can learn to construct meaningful graph representations from textual input, solve tasks where graph structures are naturally suited (like pathfinding), and learn complex rule-based systems without explicit rule coding.

## Decision

I recommend **acceptance** with minor revisions. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by addressing a clear limitation of current graph neural network approaches: the inability to construct and modify graph structures from unstructured input. The work is well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and presents a compelling case for the value of explicit graph representations.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several key areas:

1. **Problem Definition and Motivation**: The authors clearly articulate the limitation of existing GNNs (fixed graph structure) and the need for a model that can build graphs from unstructured input. The bAbI task example is particularly compelling as it demonstrates how natural language processing could benefit from graph-based internal representations.

2. **Technical Innovation**: The redefinition of graphs with continuous strengths and the set of differentiable transformations represent a substantial technical contribution. The paper provides sufficient mathematical detail for reproducibility (with implementation details in the appendix), and the transformations are well-motivated by the tasks they enable.

3. **Empirical Evaluation**: The evaluation is comprehensive and well-designed:
   - The bAbI results show the GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on most tasks, with the direct-reference variant outperforming other strong baselines on several tasks (particularly task 19, pathfinding, where it achieves 64% improvement over MemNN)
   - The rule discovery tasks demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex computational rules (Rule 30 and Turing machines) from scratch
   - The ablation study (comparing with and without direct reference) provides valuable insight into the model's behavior

4. **Comparison with Related Work**: The paper provides a thorough comparison with relevant literature (GNNs, GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, etc.), clearly articulating how GGT-NNs differ and why those differences matter.

## Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, the following suggestions would further improve it:

1. **Clarify Differentiability**: The paper mentions that graphs are redefined with continuous strengths to enable differentiability, but the exact mechanism for making transformations differentiable (particularly node addition) needs more detail. How does the model handle the discrete decision of adding nodes? The paper states "the recurrence should be computed as many times as the maximum number of nodes that might be produced," but doesn't fully explain how this is made differentiable.

2. **Fairer Comparison**: The paper acknowledges that GGT-NN is strongly supervised (with full graph information), while other models are trained end-to-end. This creates an unfair comparison. It would be valuable to include a comparison where GGT-NN is trained with only partial graph supervision (e.g., some graph information) to better understand the trade-offs between supervision level and performance.

3. **Complexity Discussion**: While the paper notes the quadratic complexity as a limitation, it doesn't explore potential optimizations (beyond sparse connections mentioned in the conclusion). A brief discussion of how this might scale to larger graphs or with approximation techniques would strengthen the paper.

4. **Readability**: The paper is quite dense. Some sections (particularly the algorithm descriptions) could be clarified with more intuitive explanations or diagrams to make the contributions more accessible to a broader audience.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does the model handle the differentiability of node addition, particularly when the number of nodes to add is variable? Is there a mechanism for the model to learn when to stop adding nodes?

2. The paper claims GGT-NNs are the first to allow neural networks to build and modify graph structures. Are there any related works in differentiable programming or neural program synthesis that achieve similar capabilities? How does GGT-NN differ from those approaches?

3. Could you provide more insight into why the GGT-NN with direct reference outperforms the model without it? Is it primarily due to better attention mechanisms, or are there deeper reasons related to the graph construction process?

4. Given the quadratic complexity, what specific optimizations would you recommend for scaling the model to larger graphs? Would sparse graph representations (with only non-zero edges) be sufficient, or would more sophisticated approaches be needed?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to the field of graph neural networks. The GGT-NN framework opens up new possibilities for neural networks to work with structured data in a more flexible way, and the empirical results provide compelling evidence of its value. With the minor clarifications suggested above, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
