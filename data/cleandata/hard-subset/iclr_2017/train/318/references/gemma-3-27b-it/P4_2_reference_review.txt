This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension to GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations to manipulate a graphical internal state, enabling the model to learn complex update rules and process graph-structured data. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN on the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to construct graphs from natural language and perform graph-based reasoning.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a conceptually interesting approach, the experimental validation lacks sufficient depth and comparison to convincingly demonstrate the claimed benefits. Furthermore, the presentation is somewhat verbose and lacks clarity in certain areas, hindering a thorough understanding of the method.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited novelty and impact demonstrated by the empirical results. While achieving high accuracy on the bAbI tasks is positive, the comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods is not compelling. The GGT-NN performs competitively with existing models, but doesn’t consistently outperform them, and the improvements are often marginal. Moreover, the successes on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks, while illustrating the model's capability to learn rules, do not represent a significant advancement in those domains. The ablation studies (direct reference vs. no direct reference) are informative, but don't address the core question of whether the GGT-NN offers substantial advantages over existing graph neural network architectures or sequence-to-sequence models.

Secondly, the paper suffers from a lack of clarity and detail in several areas. The descriptions of the graph transformations (Node Addition, State Update, etc.) are somewhat abstract. While Figure 2 provides a high-level overview, a more detailed explanation of the specific implementation choices (e.g., the neural network architectures used within each transformation) would be beneficial. The Appendix attempts to address this, but the level of detail could be increased. The justification for specific design choices, such as the choice of GRUs and the use of particular activation functions, is also lacking. Furthermore, the connection between the differentiable graph transformations and existing graph neural network paradigms is not fully explored. Is the GGT-NN a generalization of GNNs, or is it fundamentally different?

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Strengthen the empirical evaluation:** Conduct more extensive comparisons with relevant baselines, including more recent and powerful models. Include error analysis to identify the specific types of errors the GGT-NN makes. Investigate the impact of different hyperparameters and architectural choices.
*   **Clarify the technical details:** Provide more detailed descriptions of the graph transformations, including the specific neural network architectures used within each transformation. Explain the rationale behind the design choices.
*   **Improve the presentation:** The paper is quite verbose. Consider streamlining the text and focusing on the key contributions. Use more illustrative examples and visualizations to explain the concepts.
*   **Discuss limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the GGT-NN, such as its computational complexity and scalability. Discuss potential avenues for future research.
*   **Address the reviewer's questions:**
    *   How sensitive is the performance to the initial graph structure provided to the model (e.g., for the bAbI tasks)?
    *   What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to other graph neural network architectures, particularly as the graph size increases?
    *   What are the benefits of using a graph-structured internal state compared to other types of internal representations (e.g., vectors or matrices)?
    *   Could the model benefit from using different edge types to represent different relationships between nodes?
    *   How do the proposed graph transformations relate to existing graph editing operations used in other fields (e.g., graph databases)?
