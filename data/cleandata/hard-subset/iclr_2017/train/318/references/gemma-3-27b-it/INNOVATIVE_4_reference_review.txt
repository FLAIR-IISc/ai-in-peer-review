This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture capable of both constructing and reasoning over graph-structured data. The core idea – using differentiable graph transformations within a neural network – is a compelling step beyond existing methods that typically require pre-defined graph structures. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and perform reasoning tasks.

**Reject.** While the idea is highly innovative and addresses a crucial limitation of existing graph neural networks, the paper feels incomplete. The empirical validation, while promising, lacks depth. The performance gains on bAbI are not substantial enough to fully justify the architectural complexity, and the Rule 30/Turing machine experiments are presented without sufficient comparison to established methods for those specific tasks. The novelty is strong, but the supporting evidence isn’t quite strong enough for acceptance at this stage.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its novelty. The ability to *learn* graph structures, rather than relying on predefined ones, opens up exciting possibilities for applying neural networks to domains where graph representations are natural but not readily available. The modularity of the graph transformations is also a significant advantage, allowing for flexible adaptation to different tasks. The exploration of diverse tasks—question answering, cellular automata, Turing machines—is commendable, demonstrating the potential breadth of the approach.

However, the experiments feel somewhat superficial. The bAbI results, while competitive, don’t demonstrate a clear and significant advantage over existing state-of-the-art methods. A more thorough ablation study, exploring the impact of the different graph transformations and the direct reference mechanism, would be valuable. Furthermore, the lack of comparison to established methods on the Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks leaves the reader wondering how well the GGT-NN performs relative to alternatives specifically designed for those problems.  The choice to only report accuracy metrics and not, for example, learning curves or generalization performance, limits the ability to assess the robustness of the results. The implementation details, while present in the appendix, could benefit from greater clarity and conciseness. The leap from a successful demonstration to a convincingly superior method is not quite made.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:**  Conduct ablation studies to understand the contribution of each graph transformation (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) and the direct reference mechanism. This would help identify the key components driving performance.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:**  Provide a more thorough comparison to relevant baselines, particularly for the Rule 30 and Turing machine experiments. Are there existing methods tailored to these tasks that the GGT-NN outperforms?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  Discuss the sensitivity of the model to hyperparameters.  Were extensive hyperparameter searches performed?
*   **Scalability Analysis:**  Address the potential scalability issues mentioned in the conclusion.  What are the computational costs associated with larger graphs and more complex tasks?
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide examples of learned graphs for the bAbI tasks or the Turing machine to gain intuition for the network’s decision-making process.  Visualizing the learned graphs would be very helpful.
*   **Clearer Notation:** In section 3 and appendix B the notation is sometimes inconsistent or overly dense. Improving readability would be helpful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What steps were taken to prevent overfitting, particularly given the strong supervision used in the bAbI experiments?
2.  Why were specific choices made for the functions *fset*, *freset*, *f fwd y*, and *f bwd y* in the edge update and propagation transformations? Were other options explored?
3.  Could you provide more detail on the computational resources required to train the GGT-NN model on the different tasks? Specifically, how does the training time scale with the size of the graph?
4.   What is the role of node strength (sv) in practice? Did you experiment with different initializations or learning strategies for sv?
