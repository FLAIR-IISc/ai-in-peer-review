## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamically constructed and modified graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The key contribution lies in the introduction of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – enabling the network to learn to manipulate a graphical internal state based on input data (textual or graphical) and produce outputs (graphical or textual). The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI task dataset, a cellular automaton task, and Turing machine simulations, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and represent stateful information effectively.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to representing and manipulating stateful information using dynamically evolving graphs. The results on diverse tasks, particularly the Turing machine simulation, are impressive and demonstrate the model’s capability to learn complex relational reasoning.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** The GGT-NN architecture is a significant extension of existing GNN models, introducing differentiable graph transformations that allow for a more flexible and dynamic representation of information. This ability to *learn* graph structure is a substantial advancement.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:** The experiments on the bAbI tasks demonstrate competitive performance, with particularly good results on tasks requiring relational reasoning. The successful implementation of the 1D cellular automaton and Turing machine simulations is particularly compelling, as these tasks require the model to learn and apply complex state transition rules.  Achieving high accuracy on these tasks suggests the model is learning meaningful internal representations.
*   **Well-Motivated Approach:** The motivation for utilizing graph structures to represent state is well-articulated, given the prevalence of relational data and the inherent ability of graphs to capture such relationships.  The paper clearly places itself within the existing literature on GNNs and extends their capabilities in a meaningful way.
*   **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a relatively clear and detailed explanation of the GGT-NN architecture and the differentiable graph transformations. The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is helpful for understanding the model’s operation.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the authors acknowledge the potential for high computational cost, a more detailed discussion of the scalability of the approach would be beneficial.  Specifically, how does the time and space complexity scale with graph size and number of nodes? Are there any obvious optimizations that could be explored?
*   **Ablation Studies:** More comprehensive ablation studies could further strengthen the claims.  For example, a more granular analysis of the impact of each graph transformation (e.g., removing propagation or edge updates) would help to understand their individual contributions to performance.
*   **Visualizations:**  Visualizing the learned graph structures during the Turing machine simulations or for specific bAbI task examples would provide valuable insight into how the model represents and manipulates information.  While Figure 5 is helpful, expanding this visualization to other tasks would be impactful.
*   **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the paper provides comparisons to existing methods, focusing on those that *also* learn internal state representations (e.g., Memory Networks, DNCs) would be more direct. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between the GGT-NN and these alternatives would be valuable.
*   **Node/Edge Strength Interpretation:** The interpretation of node and edge strengths as "belief" is interesting, but could be further elaborated. Does this relate to confidence scores or uncertainty estimation in any way?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Scalability:** You mention the quadratic scaling with graph size. Have you explored any techniques for mitigating this, such as sparse graph representations or approximate nearest neighbor search for propagation?
*   **Transformation Parameter Sharing:** Do you observe benefits from sharing parameters between different graph transformations? (e.g., using the same weights for node state update and edge update).
*   **Node Type Initialization:** How sensitive is the model's performance to the initial assignment of node types?  Could the network benefit from learning node types directly from the input data, rather than relying on pre-defined types?
*   **Direct Reference Importance:** Why does direct reference appear to be so crucial for performance on the bAbI tasks? Is this related to the relatively small training dataset size?
*   **Generalization to Other Tasks:** Are there specific types of tasks or datasets where you anticipate the GGT-NN approach being particularly well-suited, beyond the examples presented in the paper?



In summary, this is a strong paper with a novel approach and compelling results. The minor revisions suggested above would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work.  I believe it represents a valuable contribution to the field of graph neural networks and has the potential to inspire further research in this area.
