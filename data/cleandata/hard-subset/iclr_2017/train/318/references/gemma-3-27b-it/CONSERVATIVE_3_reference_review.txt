This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs designed to manipulate graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The authors aim to leverage the flexibility of graph structures for tasks involving relational data, demonstrated through bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machine simulations. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – to build and modify graphs based on input, and then utilize these graphs for reasoning and output generation.

**Reject.** While the idea of utilizing differentiable graph transformations is interesting, the paper presents it as a rather monolithic and insufficiently validated approach. The core weakness lies in the limited novelty relative to existing graph neural network work, coupled with a lack of rigorous comparison to established baselines beyond the bAbI tasks. The reported performance, while good, doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advance over existing methods, especially considering the strong supervision used during training.

The paper’s primary argument rests on the claim that learning to manipulate graphs internally allows the model to reason more effectively. However, the reliance on explicitly providing the "correct" graph at each timestep during training (strong supervision) severely weakens this claim. It’s unclear how much of the performance is due to learning the graph manipulation itself versus simply learning to reproduce the provided target graphs. The modifications to the graph structure are presented with a high level of detail, yet their necessity isn’t adequately justified. Existing GNNs already handle relational data; the paper needs to demonstrate *why* this more complex transformation-based approach is superior. 

The results on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks are interesting, but lack sufficient comparison to specialized algorithms designed for these tasks. Showing that a GGT-NN *can* learn these tasks is not the same as demonstrating it learns them *efficiently* or *competitively*. The bAbI results are presented alongside a wide range of baselines, which is appreciated, but the gap in performance is not substantial enough to justify the added complexity of the proposed model. The analysis of task 17 is insightful, but doesn't address the broader issue of scalability and complexity. The concluding remarks about potential applications feel speculative without a more solid foundation of empirical evidence.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** A crucial addition would be ablation studies demonstrating the contribution of each graph transformation. Which transformations are most important for each task? Can the same performance be achieved with a simpler subset?
*   **Weakly Supervised/Unsupervised Learning:** Explore training paradigms with reduced supervision. How does the model perform when the "correct" graph is not provided at each timestep? Can the graph transformations be learned in an unsupervised manner?
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational complexity and scalability of the GGT-NN model. How does performance degrade as the graph size increases?
*   **Comparison to Existing GNNs:** Conduct a more direct comparison to state-of-the-art GNN architectures (e.g., Graph Convolutional Networks, Graph Attention Networks) on the bAbI tasks, using the same level of supervision.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Discuss the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameter settings.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide more qualitative examples of the learned graph structures. Visualizing the graphs generated by the model could provide valuable insights into its reasoning process.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of the differentiable graph transformations compared to standard GNN message passing?
*   How does the performance of the model change when the level of supervision is reduced (e.g., providing the correct graph only at the beginning and end of a sequence)?
*   Have you explored alternative methods for learning the graph transformations, such as reinforcement learning or generative adversarial networks?
*   What design choices were made regarding the functions f fwd y and f bwd y in the propagation step, and how sensitive is the performance to these choices?
*   Why was a GRU chosen over other recurrent network architectures (e.g., LSTMs) for the node state update and sequence processing components?
