This paper proposes the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, designed to manipulate graph-structured intermediate representations and produce graph-structured outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations to build and modify a graph based on natural language input, enabling tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and simulating Turing machines. The authors demonstrate the model on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and 4-state Turing machines, showing promising results, particularly on tasks where a graphical representation is naturally suited.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning graph transformations is interesting, the paper presents a complex architecture with limited evidence of substantial improvement over existing, well-established methods. The extensive reliance on strong supervision, especially in the bAbI experiments, raises concerns about the generalizability and practical applicability of the proposed approach. The gains observed, while present, aren't compelling enough to justify the added complexity and specialized training regime.

The primary issue is a lack of rigorous comparison to strong baselines *without* strong supervision. The bAbI results are presented alongside several state-of-the-art methods, but the authors explicitly acknowledge that the GGT-NN and MemNN models benefit from strong supervision (full graph information or relevance annotations). The performance gains against models trained end-to-end are therefore difficult to interpret. The claim that the GGT-NN outperforms other approaches on some bAbI tasks relies on this uneven playing field. Furthermore, the limited exploration of hyperparameter sensitivity and architectural variations weakens the confidence in the reported results. While the rule discovery tasks are novel, the results aren’t contextualized with other approaches for symbolic reasoning or program induction. The authors mention limitations regarding the scaling of the model, which is a significant drawback.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

*   **Motivation:** The motivation for using graph transformations is reasonable, but not entirely novel. Many approaches implicitly learn representations that could be considered graph-like. The paper doesn’t convincingly demonstrate why explicitly manipulating graph structures is fundamentally superior.
*   **Methodology:** The proposed architecture is intricate, involving numerous transformations and components. The detailed description in Appendix B is appreciated, but the complexity hinders understanding and reproducibility. The choice of specific functions (e.g., GRUs, tanh layers) feels somewhat arbitrary and lacks justification.
*   **Experiments:** The bAbI experiments are limited by the strong supervision. The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments are interesting proof-of-concept demonstrations but lack comparisons to established techniques for learning these types of tasks. The generalization capabilities are reported but not thoroughly analyzed. The choice of tasks feels slightly convenient – selecting problems naturally amenable to a graph representation.
*   **Novelty:** The core novelty lies in the differentiable graph transformations and their integration within a neural network. However, the overall architecture builds heavily on existing GNN frameworks (GG-NNs, GGS-NNs).

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more thorough ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components, such as direct reference and intermediate propagation.
*   **Baseline Comparisons:** Compare the GGT-NN to strong baseline models *without* strong supervision on the bAbI tasks. This would provide a more realistic assessment of the model’s performance.
*   **Scaling Analysis:** Investigate the scalability of the GGT-NN to larger graphs and more complex tasks. Provide concrete results on the computational cost and memory requirements.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Analyze the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameters.
*   **Generalization:** Provide a more comprehensive analysis of the model’s generalization capabilities. What types of graphs or tasks does it struggle with?
*   **Explainability:** Explore methods for visualizing and interpreting the learned graph transformations. What are the network’s internal representations of the input data?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the computational cost of the GGT-NN scale with the number of nodes and edges in the graph?
*   What strategies could be employed to mitigate the scalability issues of the model?
*   Have you considered using sparse graph representations to reduce the computational cost?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the model makes on the bAbI tasks?
*   What is the rationale for choosing GRUs and tanh activations in the graph transformations? Were other options considered?
