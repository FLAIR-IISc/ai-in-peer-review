## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture extending GG-NNs and GGS-NNs by enabling graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The key innovation lies in the use of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – which allow the model to dynamically construct and manipulate a graph representing the “state of the world.” The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton task, and Turing machine simulation, highlighting its ability to learn complex update rules and achieve competitive performance. The work is well-motivated by the ubiquity of graph-structured data and the limitations of existing methods in handling dynamic graph representations.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and technically sound approach to learning with dynamic graphs. The experimental results on the bAbI tasks and the rule-discovery tasks are promising, demonstrating the GGT-NN's ability to learn and reason about graph-structured information. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is a significant contribution. It allows the model to learn *how* to represent and manipulate information graphically, rather than relying on pre-defined graph structures.
* **Technical Soundness:** The mathematical formulation of the graph transformations and the GGT-NN architecture appears well-defined and reasonable.  The use of GRUs and attention mechanisms are standard techniques but integrated effectively within the graph transformation framework.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments on bAbI demonstrate competitive performance compared to other state-of-the-art models. The success on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks is particularly impressive, demonstrating the model’s ability to learn and apply complex rules. The comparison to other models, even with different levels of supervision, provides valuable context.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and the individual components are described in detail. The inclusion of an appendix with implementation details is helpful. 

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares models with and without direct reference, more comprehensive ablation studies would be beneficial. For example, investigating the impact of removing specific graph transformations (e.g., node addition, propagation) on performance could offer further insight into their contribution.
* **Scalability Discussion:** The authors acknowledge scalability as a limitation. It would be helpful to briefly discuss potential strategies for addressing this challenge (e.g., sparse graph representations, sampling techniques).
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A brief discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity would strengthen the empirical analysis.  How do the results change with different learning rates, hidden layer sizes, or numbers of propagation steps?
* **Visualization of Learned Graphs:**  Providing visualizations of the learned graphs (especially for the Turing machine and cellular automaton tasks) would enhance understanding and demonstrate the model’s learned representations.
* **Connection to Graph Databases:** Discussing the potential connections between the learned graph transformations and operations in graph databases (e.g., Cypher queries) could highlight the practical relevance of this work.  



**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the choice of edge and node strengths:** How sensitive is the performance to the constraints imposed on edge and node strengths (e.g., the requirement that node strengths sum to 1)?  Could relaxing these constraints potentially improve performance or allow for more flexible representations?
* **On the computational complexity:** You mention that the quadratic scaling of the GGT-NN is a limitation. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of each graph transformation?  Which transformations are the most computationally expensive?
* **Regarding generalization in the rule-discovery tasks:**  The results on the extended Turing machine and cellular automaton tasks are impressive.  Do you observe any systematic differences in the learned representations between different Turing machine rules or cellular automaton rules?  Could the model potentially generalize to unseen rules within the same class?
* **Regarding direct reference:** What are the negative impacts of using direct reference? Does it encourage the network to rely too heavily on surface-level features instead of learning more abstract relationships?




Overall, this is a well-executed and promising piece of work that has the potential to advance the field of graph neural networks. I am confident that addressing the suggested feedback will further strengthen the paper and solidify its contribution.
