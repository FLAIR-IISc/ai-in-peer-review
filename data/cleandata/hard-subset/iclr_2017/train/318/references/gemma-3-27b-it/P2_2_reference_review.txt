## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamically constructed and transformed graph-structured internal representations. The authors argue this flexibility enables the model to tackle tasks requiring manipulation of complex relationships, demonstrating its effectiveness on bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machine simulation. The core contribution lies in the differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – that enable this dynamic graph manipulation. The paper showcases strong performance on the bAbI tasks and the ability to learn complex update rules in the automaton and Turing machine experiments.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to graph-based reasoning and learning. The results are promising and demonstrate the potential of dynamically transforming graph structures for complex tasks. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The idea of learning and manipulating graph structures *within* a neural network is a significant contribution. Existing work often relies on pre-defined graphs or graph representations. The differentiable transformations are a clever way to achieve this.
* **Motivation:** The motivation is clear – many data types can be represented as graphs, and the ability to dynamically update and reason over these graphs is valuable. The paper connects well to existing work in GNNs and sequence modeling.
* **Empirical Validation:** The bAbI task results are strong, achieving high accuracy on most tasks and competitive results compared to existing state-of-the-art models.  Crucially, the results on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks provide compelling evidence that the model *learns* meaningful transformations. The demonstration of generalization to longer sequences in these tasks is particularly noteworthy.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper is well-written and provides a reasonably clear explanation of the GGT-NN architecture and the individual graph transformations. The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is helpful.
* **Ablation Study:** The comparison between models *with* and *without* direct reference provides a useful ablation study, highlighting the benefit of this feature.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Complexity Analysis:** While the paper touches on the computational cost, a more thorough analysis of the time and space complexity of GGT-NN compared to other approaches (like those mentioned in section 6) would be valuable. This is particularly important given the quadratic scaling issue.
* **Visualization:** While Figure 5, 6 and 7 provide examples of the graphical representation for each task, including visualizations of the *learned* graphs during training (especially for the automaton and Turing machine tasks) would significantly enhance understanding of what the model is learning.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper doesn’t discuss the sensitivity of the results to hyperparameter choices. Some discussion of the hyperparameters and a justification for their selection would be helpful.
* **Error Analysis:** Performing a detailed error analysis on the bAbI tasks could reveal insights into the limitations of the approach and areas for improvement.  Specifically, a closer look at task 17, where the model struggles, would be illuminating.
* **Code Release:** The mention of code availability is excellent! Ensuring the code is well-documented and easily reproducible will greatly benefit the research community.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Scalability:** Given the quadratic complexity, what strategies could be employed to scale the GGT-NN to larger and more complex graphs? Have you explored any approximation techniques (e.g., sparse graph connections, node sampling)?
2. **Interpretability:** Beyond visualizing the final graphs, are there any techniques you've explored to understand *how* the different graph transformations contribute to the overall performance? Can you identify which transformations are most crucial for specific tasks?
3. **Direct Reference:**  The direct reference mechanism appears to be highly beneficial. Is there a risk of overfitting to specific keyword associations? Have you explored alternative methods for identifying relevant nodes (e.g., attention mechanisms)?
4. **Task 17:** You mention task 17 as a challenge due to the larger number of entities. Is this solely due to the increased number of entities, or do you suspect other factors (e.g., the shorter story length) also contribute to the poor performance?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling contribution. The authors have convincingly demonstrated the potential of dynamically transforming graph structures for reasoning and learning. I believe addressing the points above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
