This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture extending GG-NNs to allow for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs, dynamically constructed from natural language input. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capability to learn complex update rules by successfully modeling a cellular automaton and Turing machines, alongside achieving strong results on the bAbI task dataset. The core idea – learning to manipulate a graph as an internal state – is a compelling and potentially impactful contribution.

**Reject.** While the idea is exciting and represents a significant departure from typical sequence-to-sequence models, the paper feels underdeveloped and lacks sufficient validation to justify acceptance at ICLR. The bAbI results, while strong, are presented without sufficient comparison to simpler baselines beyond those already established in the literature, and the rule-discovery tasks are not thoroughly analyzed. The claim of novelty is strong, but the evidence supporting the practical benefits of this approach feels limited.

The primary issue lies in the lack of convincing demonstration that the GGT-NN *needs* a graph structure to achieve its results. While the authors argue that certain tasks (pathfinding, induction) are “naturally suited” to graphs, they don’t provide a controlled experiment showing a performance benefit *specifically due to* the graph representation, compared to an equally complex non-graph model. The success on the bAbI tasks could potentially be attributed to the model’s capacity and the strong supervision, rather than the graph manipulation itself. The comparison to MemNN is helpful but not decisive, as both are strongly supervised.

Furthermore, the explanation of the graph transformations and their interplay feels somewhat hand-wavy. While each transformation is described, the rationale behind their combination in the specific configuration used for the experiments isn't fully explained. The ablation studies, particularly regarding the direct reference component, are good, but more thorough analysis of the contribution of each transformation would be valuable. The scaling limitations mentioned are also concerning and suggest the architecture might not be practical for larger, more complex datasets. The discussion of related work is thorough but doesn't sufficiently address the computational cost implications compared to alternatives.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Baselines:** Include comparisons to simpler, non-graph based models on the bAbI tasks. A standard LSTM or Transformer without graph manipulation would serve as a good benchmark.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct more targeted ablation studies, removing or simplifying individual graph transformations to assess their individual contributions to performance.
*   **Scaling Analysis:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost of the GGT-NN, including memory requirements and training time, as a function of graph size and complexity.
*   **Visualization:** Include more visualizations of the learned graphs, particularly for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks. This would help to understand what the model is actually learning.
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform an error analysis on the bAbI tasks to identify specific failure cases and gain insights into the model’s limitations.
* **Justification of Choices:** Provide more justification for the specific choices made in the architecture (e.g., the choice of GRU cells, the specific functions used for edge and node updates).

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of why a graph representation is essential for the tasks you’ve chosen, compared to alternative representations? Specifically, could you describe the limitations of a non-graph based approach on task 19 (pathfinding)?
2.  What are the computational bottlenecks of the GGT-NN, and what steps could be taken to address them?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of the GGT-NN to the initialization of the graph transformations?
4.  What is the effect of varying the number of propagation steps (T) on the performance of the GGT-NN? Is there a sweet spot, and how does it vary with task complexity?
5.  The code is available at a github link, but how will you ensure reproducibility for the reviewers (e.g. exact software versions, random seeds, training configurations)
