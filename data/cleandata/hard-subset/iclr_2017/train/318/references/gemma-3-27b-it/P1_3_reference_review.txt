## Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamically constructed and modified graph-structured internal states. The authors propose differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – which, when combined, enable the model to learn to manipulate graphs based on input data. The GGT-NN is demonstrated on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex state update rules and solve tasks requiring reasoning about structured data. The core contribution lies in the flexibility of representing and manipulating knowledge as a graph, learned directly from data.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to representing and processing sequential data with graph structures. The results are compelling, demonstrating the model’s ability to learn complex rules and perform well on a variety of tasks. The extension of GNNs to allow for dynamic graph construction is a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The ability to *learn* a graph structure and dynamically modify it based on input is a significant step beyond existing GNN approaches that typically operate on fixed graphs. The proposed differentiable graph transformations are a clever way to achieve this.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance on the bAbI tasks is competitive with, and in some cases exceeds, existing state-of-the-art methods. The success on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks demonstrates the model’s ability to learn complex, rule-based transformations. Specifically, the performance on task 19 (pathfinding) is impressive, given the task's natural alignment with graphical representations.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and clearly explains the GGT-NN architecture and the various graph transformations. The organization is logical, and the inclusion of an appendix detailing the transformation implementations is helpful.
* **Ablation Studies:** The comparison between models with and without direct reference provides valuable insight into the effectiveness of that component.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Scalability Discussion:** While the paper acknowledges the computational cost of the model, a more detailed discussion of scalability limitations and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., sparse connections, node sampling) would be beneficial.  This is especially important given the quadratic scaling of graph operations.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the model's performance to key hyperparameters.  How do different learning rates, hidden state dimensions, or transformation configurations affect the results?
* **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis on the bAbI tasks (particularly task 17, where performance is lower) could provide valuable insights into the model’s weaknesses and potential areas for improvement.  What types of questions does the model struggle with? What aspects of the graph representation are causing difficulty?
* **Visualizations:** Providing visualizations of the learned graphs during the cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments would greatly enhance understanding of how the model is representing and manipulating state information.
* **Comparison to Alternatives:** While the paper compares to a wide range of related works, a brief discussion of how GGT-NNs compare to graph autoencoders or graph variational autoencoders (which also aim to learn graph representations) would be valuable.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding scalability:**  What specific bottlenecks are observed during training and inference as the graph size increases?  Have you experimented with any techniques to mitigate these bottlenecks, even if preliminary?
* **Regarding task 17 (Positional Reasoning):**  You mention the complexity of this task as a reason for the lower performance. Could you elaborate on whether modifications to the graph representation or transformation rules might improve performance on this specific task? For instance, is a different node type structure potentially helpful?
* **Regarding the direct reference mechanism:** Does the performance improvement gained from direct reference significantly vary across different bAbI tasks? Are there any tasks where direct reference is detrimental to performance?
* **Regarding generalization in Rule Discovery:** In the rule discovery experiments, are the learned graphs interpretable in terms of the underlying rules of the cellular automaton and Turing machine?  Is there a way to assess the “correctness” of the learned graph representation beyond simply matching the output?
* **Regarding potential applications:** Beyond the tasks demonstrated in this paper, what other real-world applications do you envision for the GGT-NN architecture?



Overall, this is a well-executed and promising piece of work. Addressing the points raised above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact on the field. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
