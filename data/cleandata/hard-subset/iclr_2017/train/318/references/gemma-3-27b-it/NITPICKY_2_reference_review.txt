This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations to manipulate a graph representing the state of the world, enabling applications like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and Turing machine simulation. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN on the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and represent structured information.

**Reject.** While the core idea of using differentiable graph transformations within a neural network framework is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws and a lack of clarity in implementation details, ultimately hindering a confident assessment of the contribution's significance. The empirical results, while positive, are presented with insufficient detail to rule out alternative explanations or appreciate the true impact of the proposed method.

The primary issues are threefold: 1) **Insufficient methodological detail**: The descriptions of the graph transformations (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) are high-level and lack crucial implementation specifics. For example, the functions `fset` and `freset` in the edge update transformation are described only as "neural networks" without specifying their architecture (number of layers, activation functions, etc.). This makes it impossible to reproduce the experiments or understand the capacity of the transformations. 2) **Overly complex presentation**: The paper presents a large number of equations and algorithmic descriptions without sufficient explanation of their purpose or interrelation. This makes it difficult to follow the logic of the proposed method. Figures 1, 2, 4, 5, 6, and 7, while attempting to illustrate the concepts, are often overly simplistic and lack essential details. 3) **Lack of robust evaluation**: The bAbI task results are presented alongside a comparison to several baselines, but the experimental setup is not fully described (e.g., hyperparameters, optimization details, learning rate schedules). The claim of achieving high accuracy on many tasks is weakened by the single set of reported results. The rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machine) are interesting, but the evaluation metric (percent accuracy on identical graphs) is somewhat vague, and the generalization ability is only briefly discussed.

Additional feedback:

*   **Clarify the motivation**: The introduction could be strengthened by providing a more compelling motivation for using differentiable graph transformations. What specific limitations of existing approaches does this method address?
*   **Provide pseudocode with more detail**: The Algorithm 1 pseudocode is a good starting point, but it lacks detail regarding the inputs and outputs of each function call.
*   **Explain the choice of hyperparameters**: Justify the choices of hyperparameters used in the experiments.
*   **Address potential limitations**: Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed method and potential strategies for addressing scalability issues.
*   **Improve the figures**: Provide more detailed diagrams of the graph transformations and the overall GGT-NN architecture.
*   **Ablation studies**: Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the GGT-NN (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation).
*   **Error analysis**: Perform an error analysis to identify common failure cases and gain insights into the limitations of the method.

Questions for the authors:

*   Can you provide a more detailed description of the architecture of the neural networks used in the graph transformations (e.g., `fset`, `freset`, `fadd`)?
*   What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to existing graph neural network models?
*   How does the performance of the GGT-NN vary with the size and complexity of the graph?
*   What is the impact of the node strength parameter `sv` on the performance of the model?
*   Were any regularization techniques used during training to prevent overfitting? If so, which ones?
