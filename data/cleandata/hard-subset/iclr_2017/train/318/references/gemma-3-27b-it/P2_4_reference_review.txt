## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamic graph structures as intermediate representations.  The authors propose differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) to manipulate these graphs. They demonstrate the GGT-NN's ability to learn graph representations from natural language input (bAbI tasks) and model sequential state transitions (cellular automaton, Turing machines), showcasing its flexibility and potential for handling a wider range of tasks than existing graph neural network architectures.  The core contribution is a framework for learning and manipulating graph-structured internal states, enabling the network to reason about relationships and perform complex transformations.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and well-motivated approach to graph neural networks with a clear potential impact. The empirical results on bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks are promising and demonstrate the model's capabilities.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty:** The ability to *learn* and *modify* graph structures, rather than relying on pre-defined graphs, is a significant advancement.  This addresses a key limitation of many existing GNNs and opens up new possibilities for applying them to dynamic systems and tasks where the relationships between entities are not fixed.
* **Motivation:** The authors clearly articulate the motivation behind extending GG-NNs and the advantages of using dynamic graph structures. The connection to various data types (maps, data structures, etc.) is well-established.
* **Technical Soundness:** The proposed differentiable graph transformations are logically presented and provide a flexible building block for constructing more complex models.  The use of GRU cells for state updates and the attention mechanism for aggregation are reasonable choices.
* **Empirical Validation:** The experiments demonstrate the model's ability to perform well on a diverse set of tasks, including natural language understanding (bAbI) and rule learning (cellular automaton, Turing machine).  The results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, especially considering the relatively small training dataset size.  The fact that the model can learn the rules of a Turing machine is particularly impressive.
* **Clarity:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, although some sections (especially the detailed description of transformations in the appendix) are quite dense.  

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of the GGT-NN, more detailed ablation studies would be helpful to understand the contribution of each component (e.g., the impact of direct reference, the importance of different graph transformations).  How much does performance degrade if propagation is disabled?
* **Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the scalability limitations of the approach. Discussing potential strategies for addressing this, such as sparse graph representations or more efficient propagation algorithms, would strengthen the paper.
* **Visualization:** More visualizations of the learned graphs (especially for the Turing machine and cellular automaton tasks) would provide valuable insights into the model's internal representations and reasoning process.  It would be very helpful to see examples of the graphs learned by the model.
* **Comparison to Graph Construction Methods:**  Discuss how this work differs from or builds upon other approaches that aim to construct graphs from data (e.g., knowledge graph embedding techniques).
* **Appendix Organization:** The appendices are very detailed, which is good, but could be more streamlined.  Perhaps consolidating information and using clearer headings.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the scalability concerns:** What specific bottlenecks are preventing the GGT-NN from scaling to larger graphs or more complex tasks?  Are there any initial attempts or ideas for addressing these limitations?
* **Regarding the choice of graph transformations:** Was any effort made to systematically explore different combinations of graph transformations?  Are some transformations more crucial than others for specific tasks?
* **Regarding the loss function:** You mention the node adjustment loss.  How sensitive is the performance to the weighting of the edge loss vs. the node adjustment loss?
* **Regarding the direct reference mechanism:** Could the direct reference mechanism be replaced with a more general attention mechanism that learns to focus on relevant parts of the input without relying on explicit node type labels?
* **Regarding the Turing machine example:**  How was the initial state of the Turing Machine represented in the graph? More details would be helpful for understanding this task.



Overall, this is a strong paper that presents a novel and promising approach to graph neural networks. The authors have demonstrated the potential of GGT-NNs through a series of well-designed experiments. Addressing the feedback above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
