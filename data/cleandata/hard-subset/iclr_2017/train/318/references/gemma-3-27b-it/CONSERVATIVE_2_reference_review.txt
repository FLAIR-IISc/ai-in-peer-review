This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, capable of manipulating graph-structured intermediate representations and producing graph-structured outputs. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's ability to learn complex rules by applying it to the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines. The core idea is to define graphs with differentiable connectivity matrices and use a set of graph transformations to modify these graphs, allowing the network to learn and reason about relationships between entities.

**Reject.** While the idea of extending GNNs to learn and manipulate graph structures is intriguing, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous validation to warrant acceptance. The methodology feels incremental, building upon established GNN architectures, and the evidence presented doesn’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial improvement or unique capability over existing methods. The reliance on strong supervision in the bAbI tasks, and the limited comparison to existing state-of-the-art methods, weaken the claims made.

The paper’s primary weakness lies in its lack of comparative analysis. While results are presented alongside established methods on the bAbI tasks, the comparison isn’t comprehensive enough. The authors acknowledge that the GGT-NN and MemNN models are both strongly supervised, yet they don’t fully explore the implications of this shared characteristic for interpreting the performance differences. Simply stating that a graphical representation is “superior” for certain tasks based on a marginal performance gain is insufficient. Further, the claim that the GGT-NN solves the pathfinding task when given textual input is diminished by the fact that previous GGS-NN models could do the same, albeit with preprocessed graphical input. 

The rule discovery experiments with cellular automata and Turing machines, while interesting, lack detailed analysis. The reported high accuracy is encouraging, but there's no discussion of the complexity of the learned rules, the difficulty of the task for other approaches, or the sensitivity of the model to variations in the rules. The generalization ability assessment, while mentioned, is presented without sufficient detail to understand the extent of that generalization.

The extension to handle graph sequences (Appendix D) is a potential improvement, but its evaluation is limited, and the observed slight performance reduction raises concerns. The paper would benefit from a clearer discussion of the trade-offs between processing sequential graphs versus the fully integrated graph representation.

Here are suggestions for improvement:

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Compare against more recent and relevant graph neural network architectures, particularly those designed for dynamic or relational reasoning. Consider including unsupervised or weakly supervised baselines to demonstrate the benefits of the proposed approach without relying on full graph supervision.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the GGT-NN, such as direct reference, intermediate propagation, and specific transformation types. This will help identify which aspects of the architecture are most crucial for performance.
*   **Detailed Analysis of Learned Graphs:** Provide visualizations and qualitative analysis of the graphs learned by the GGT-NN. This would offer insights into the network’s reasoning process and help validate its interpretability.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** Discuss the computational complexity of the GGT-NN and explore potential optimizations to address the scalability concerns mentioned in the conclusion.
*   **Clarify the Notion of “Supervision”:** Be precise about the level of supervision used for each task. Quantify the information provided through supervision versus information the network learns on its own.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   The paper states that the choice of activation functions (tanh) is somewhat arbitrary. Have you experimented with other activations, and if so, did they affect performance?
*   In the Rule Discovery tasks, what is the range of complexity of the cellular automaton rules or Turing machines that the GGT-NN can successfully learn? Are there limits to the complexity it can handle?
*   Can you elaborate on the choice of hyperparameters for the graph transformations? Were these parameters tuned, and if so, what methods were used?
*   The introduction mentions the potential for the GGT-NN to extract graph-structured information from unstructured text. While the bAbI tasks provide a starting point, are there plans to evaluate the model on more realistic, less structured text data?
