This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – to manipulate a graph representing the state of the world, enabling the model to solve tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and simulating Turing machines. The authors demonstrate the model on the bAbI task dataset and show competitive results, particularly on tasks well-suited to a graphical representation.

**Reject.** While the idea of using differentiable graph transformations is intriguing, the paper lacks sufficient novelty and rigorous evaluation to warrant acceptance. The proposed GGT-NN builds upon existing work (GG-NNs, GGS-NNs) with incremental modifications, but the demonstrated benefits are not compelling enough, particularly given the complexity introduced. The results on the bAbI tasks, while respectable, are not significantly better than established baselines, and the success on the rule discovery tasks feels somewhat anecdotal without more extensive comparison.

The primary concern is the lack of comprehensive comparison to strong baselines. The paper compares to a large number of existing models, but primarily reports their published results. A direct, head-to-head comparison with a well-tuned, modern baseline (e.g., a Transformer-based model) on the bAbI tasks would be crucial. The claim that the GGT-NN outperforms existing models on specific tasks (e.g., pathfinding) relies on comparing to results from a table and doesn't demonstrate a statistically significant advantage from a controlled experiment. Furthermore, the superiority attributed to the graphical representation feels intuitive but isn't rigorously demonstrated. Why does a graph representation *specifically* lead to better performance on these tasks compared to, for example, a sequence-to-sequence model with attention? 

The methodology also raises concerns. The supervision strategy, particularly the use of the "correct graph" at each timestep, is strong supervision that makes the task significantly easier. While acknowledged, the impact of this supervision on the generalizability of the model is not adequately discussed. It's unclear how well the model would perform with weaker supervision or in a truly unsupervised setting. The ablation studies, showing the benefit of direct reference, are helpful, but more extensive ablation studies, exploring the contribution of each graph transformation, would strengthen the analysis.

Finally, the presentation of the results on the rule discovery tasks is weak. The claim of "successfully learned" is vague. What metric is used to evaluate the performance? How does the model's performance compare to other methods for learning cellular automata or simulating Turing machines? Visualizing the learned graph structure and comparing it to the known rules would be highly informative.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the novelty:** Explicitly state how the GGT-NN differs fundamentally from existing graph neural networks and sequence-to-sequence models with attention. What specific advantages does the proposed approach offer?
*   **Strengthen the experimental evaluation:** Conduct a more thorough comparison with strong baselines on the bAbI tasks, including a modern Transformer-based model. Provide detailed results on the rule discovery tasks, including quantitative metrics and visualizations.
*   **Investigate the supervision strategy:** Explore the impact of different levels of supervision on the model's performance. Consider experimenting with weaker supervision or unsupervised learning techniques.
*   **Provide more detailed analysis of the learned graphs:** Visualize the learned graph structures for the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks. This would help to understand what the model is learning and why it's succeeding or failing.
*   **Address the computational complexity:** The authors acknowledge the quadratic scaling of the model. Discuss potential optimizations to mitigate this issue.
*   **Explain the choice of functions within the transformations:** Why were GRUs and tanh layers chosen for the node state update and other transformations? Were other options explored?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a direct comparison of the GGT-NN with a Transformer-based model on the bAbI tasks?
*   What metric was used to evaluate the performance of the GGT-NN on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks?
*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the level of supervision? Have you experimented with weaker supervision strategies?
*   Can you provide visualizations of the learned graph structures for some example bAbI tasks and for the cellular automaton/Turing machine tasks?
*   How does the computational cost of the GGT-NN scale with the size of the graph and the complexity of the task?
