## Review for "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamic graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core contribution lies in defining differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – that can be combined to manipulate a graph representing the state of a system. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s ability to learn complex state transitions by applying it to the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The results suggest that GGT-NNs can effectively encode and manipulate information in a graph structure, offering a flexible approach to modeling dynamic systems.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.** The paper presents a novel and promising approach to incorporating dynamic graph structures into neural networks. The experimental results are compelling, demonstrating the GGT-NN's ability to tackle diverse tasks from natural language understanding to computational rule learning. While the presentation could be improved with clearer explanations of certain design choices and a more thorough comparison to related work, the overall quality of the work justifies acceptance.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** The idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is a significant contribution. It moves beyond fixed-graph architectures and allows the network to actively build and modify its internal representation.
*   **Experimental Validation:** The paper provides strong empirical evidence across three distinct tasks. Achieving high accuracy on bAbI tasks, and successfully modeling a cellular automaton and Turing machines, demonstrates the GGT-NN’s versatility and learning capability. The ablation studies on direct reference are particularly insightful.
*   **Theoretical Foundation:** The described graph transformations are well-defined and mathematically sound, allowing for end-to-end learning through backpropagation.
*   **Clear Motivation:** The paper effectively motivates the use of graph structures for representing state transitions, highlighting the limitations of traditional approaches.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

*   **Related Work:** The related work section could be more extensive. While the cited papers are relevant, a deeper discussion of alternative approaches for dynamic state representation learning (e.g., recurrent state space models, Kalman filters) would be beneficial.  A comparison with approaches that learn to *discover* graph structures from data (rather than defining transformations) would also strengthen the paper.
*   **Implementation Details:** While the paper outlines the transformations, some implementation details (e.g., the specific architecture of the neural networks used within each transformation, hyperparameter choices) could be elaborated on. Providing code (as stated in Appendix B) is helpful, but inline explanations would further improve reproducibility.
*   **Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the scalability limitations.  Further discussion on potential optimization strategies (e.g., sparse graph representations, efficient propagation algorithms) would be valuable.  Exploring the computational cost of the graph operations compared to alternatives (e.g., LSTMs) could also be useful.
*   **Visualization:** Including visualizations of the learned graphs for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks could provide valuable insights into the network's internal representation.
*   **Loss Function Details:** The explanation of the node adjustment loss function (Lnode) could be more detailed. Clarifying how the max operation over permutations is implemented and the potential impact of suboptimal orderings would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   **Choice of Graph Transformations:**  What motivated the specific set of graph transformations chosen? Were other transformations considered and, if so, why were they not included?
*   **Direct Reference Mechanism:** The direct reference mechanism appears crucial for performance.  How sensitive is the model to the accuracy of the direct reference mapping? Are there potential issues with noisy or ambiguous references?
*   **Scalability Trade-offs:**  What are the biggest bottlenecks in terms of scalability? Have you explored any techniques (e.g., graph sampling, approximate nearest neighbor search) to mitigate these bottlenecks?
*   **Generalization to Other Tasks:** Do you foresee any significant challenges in applying the GGT-NN architecture to tasks beyond those presented in the paper (e.g., robotics, game playing)?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate, the number of propagation steps, and the dimensionality of the hidden states?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a compelling contribution. Addressing the feedback and answering the questions above would further enhance the clarity and impact of the work.
