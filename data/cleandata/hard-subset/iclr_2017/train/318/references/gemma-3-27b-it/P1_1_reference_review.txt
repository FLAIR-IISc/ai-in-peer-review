## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel neural network architecture that extends existing Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs and GGS-NNs) by allowing for dynamically constructed and modified graph-structured internal states. The core contribution lies in the introduction of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – which enable the model to learn how to represent and manipulate knowledge as graphs. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on the bAbI task suite, achieving competitive results, and on rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), showcasing its ability to learn complex state transition rules.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and novel approach to representing and manipulating state using graph structures. The results on bAbI and the rule discovery tasks are promising, demonstrating the potential of the GGT-NN. The modularity of the transformations is a significant strength, opening the door for diverse applications.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** The concept of learning and manipulating graph structures *within* a neural network, rather than relying on pre-defined graphs, is a significant step forward.  The differentiable graph transformations are cleverly designed and enable end-to-end learning.
*   **Strong Empirical Results:**  The performance on the bAbI tasks is competitive with state-of-the-art methods, and the perfect scores on many tasks suggest a strong ability to learn relational reasoning. Demonstrating the GGT-NN’s ability to learn the rules of a cellular automaton and Turing machine is particularly impressive, highlighting its capacity for learning complex dynamics.
*   **Clear Explanation and Organization:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the key concepts and technical details. The inclusion of appendix material detailing the graph transformation implementations is valuable.
* **Modularity:** The decomposition into graph transformations is excellent. It promotes reusability and allows for easy adaptation to new tasks.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares performance with and without direct reference, more extensive ablation studies exploring the impact of individual graph transformations (e.g., removing propagation or edge updates) would provide deeper insights into their contributions.
*   **Scalability Discussion:** The paper acknowledges scalability concerns in the conclusion. A more detailed discussion of potential optimizations (e.g., sparse connections, approximate transformations) and computational complexity analysis would strengthen the work.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Reporting the hyperparameter search space and the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters would improve reproducibility and understanding of the model’s robustness.
*   **Visualization of Learned Graphs:** Including visualizations of the learned graphs for the bAbI tasks or the Turing machine examples would provide valuable qualitative insights into the model’s internal representations.
*   **Comparison to Graph Transformers:** Briefly discuss how GGT-NNs relate to recent work on Graph Transformers (e.g., those leveraging attention mechanisms directly on graphs).  Highlighting the differences in approach and potential tradeoffs could strengthen the positioning of the work.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   The paper mentions the "arbitrary orderings" selected during node adjustment based on Vinyals et al. (2016).  Could you elaborate on the potential impact of this arbitrary ordering on the learned representations, and whether you explored alternative ordering strategies?
*   In the cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments, the initial state and rules are provided as input.  How sensitive is the model to slight variations or noise in these inputs?  Would the model be able to learn from noisy or incomplete observations of these systems?
*   You mention the potential for combining supervised and unsupervised training. Could you elaborate on the specific regularization techniques you envision to encourage meaningful graph structure without full supervision?
*   Regarding scalability, what is the memory footprint of the GGT-NN for larger graphs, and how does it compare to the memory usage of other graph neural network approaches (e.g., GCNs)?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a novel approach and promising results. Addressing the suggested improvements would further enhance its contribution and impact.
