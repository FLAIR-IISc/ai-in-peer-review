## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamic graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) to manipulate a graph representing the "state of the world". The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and solve tasks requiring structured state representation. The key contribution lies in bridging the gap between unstructured input (like text) and graph-based reasoning, enabling the model to construct and reason about a graph internally.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting and potentially valuable architecture, the empirical results, particularly the only marginal gains over existing methods on the bAbI tasks, and the lack of a more thorough comparative analysis, do not sufficiently justify acceptance to ICLR. The claims of significant advancements, particularly concerning the ability to learn complex rules, aren’t convincingly demonstrated through the provided experiments.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Incremental Improvement, Not Breakthrough:** The bAbI results, while positive, show only modest improvements over strong baselines like MemNNs, and in some cases, are outperformed. The authors highlight specific tasks where GGT-NN excels, but the overall performance is not significantly beyond the state-of-the-art, especially considering the strong supervision used.
* **Supervision Reliance:** The model relies heavily on strong supervision – providing the correct graph at each timestep. While justified as a starting point, this significantly limits the generalizability and practical applicability.  The comparison to less supervised methods is hampered by this difference.
* **Limited Ablation/Analysis:** The ablation study is somewhat limited. While direct reference is shown to be important, a more granular analysis of the contribution of each graph transformation would be valuable. How crucial is node addition vs. edge update, for example?  The discussion around why Task 17 struggled feels somewhat cursory – a deeper dive into what limitations of the graph representation led to this failure is needed.
* **Complexity vs. Benefit:** The GGT-NN is a complex architecture. The paper doesn’t fully justify the added complexity compared to simpler approaches, particularly when considering the performance gains. The discussion of computational cost, though acknowledged, is brief and doesn't explore potential optimizations.
* **Rule Discovery Tasks – Limited Scope:** The experiments with the cellular automaton and Turing machines are positive, but feel more like proof-of-concept demonstrations than rigorous evaluations.  They don't provide sufficient evidence of the model's ability to generalize to more complex rule-learning scenarios. The simplicity of these tasks may not fully capture the challenges of real-world applications.

**4. Additional Feedback:**

* **More Thorough Baselines:**  A comparison against more recent and powerful graph neural network architectures (e.g., Graph Transformers) would strengthen the evaluation.
* **Weakly Supervised/Unsupervised Experiments:** Exploring scenarios with reduced supervision (e.g., training only on the input text and observing the graph) is crucial for demonstrating the model's true potential.
* **Explainability:** Visualizing the learned graph structures and transformations (e.g., showing which edges are being updated and why) would significantly enhance understanding and build confidence in the model's reasoning process.
* **Scalability Considerations:** A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and scalability of the GGT-NN, especially as the graph size increases, is needed.
* **Error Analysis:** A thorough error analysis on the bAbI tasks would help identify the specific types of reasoning failures and guide future improvements.
* **Clarify ‘Direct Reference’ impact:** The success of 'direct reference' is highlighted, but the reliance on perfectly named entities feels brittle. Discuss alternatives to handle more realistic, ambiguous language.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the choice of a GRU for transformations was somewhat arbitrary.  Have you explored other recurrent or transformer-based units for these transformations, and if so, how did their performance compare?
2.  Task 17 consistently shows the lowest performance. Can you provide more detailed insights into the learned graph representations and transformations for this task, and why the model struggles to learn the required reasoning?  What specifically makes this task more challenging for a graph-based approach?
3.  How sensitive is the model's performance to the initial graph structure provided? Does the performance change significantly if the initial graph is simpler or more complex?
4.  Regarding scalability: what is the memory footprint of the GGT-NN model when applied to larger graphs (e.g., graphs with thousands of nodes)? Are there any specific bottlenecks in the implementation that limit scalability?
5.  You mention potential optimizations for computational cost. Could you elaborate on these potential optimizations, and provide a rough estimate of their expected impact on training and inference time?



In summary, while the GGT-NN presents an intriguing approach to graph-based reasoning, the current paper doesn't provide enough compelling evidence to warrant acceptance to a highly competitive conference like ICLR. Addressing the concerns outlined above could significantly strengthen the work and potentially lead to a more impactful contribution.
