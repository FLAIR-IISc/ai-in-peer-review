## ICLR 2024 Review

**Paper:** Learning Graphical State Transitions

**Reviewer:** [Your Name/Anonymous Reviewer]

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to define graphs flexibly using connectivity matrices and apply differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – to manipulate these graphs. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s ability to learn and reason about state transitions on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing it can build and modify graphs based on input and perform tasks requiring complex state management.  The work is a compelling attempt to bridge the gap between symbolic reasoning (graphs) and neural network learning.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel approach to incorporating graphical reasoning into neural networks and demonstrates strong empirical results on a diverse set of tasks. The ability to learn to manipulate graph structures directly is a significant contribution.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** The GGT-NN architecture, with its differentiable graph transformations, is a genuinely novel contribution. Existing work often requires pre-defined graphs or focuses on learning *within* a fixed graph structure. This approach allows the network to *construct* and *modify* its internal representation.
*   **Strong Empirical Validation:** The experiments presented are well-designed and demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on tasks requiring varying degrees of complexity. Achieving competitive results on the bAbI tasks, and successfully modeling a cellular automaton and Turing machines, is significant. Specifically, the improved performance on tasks like pathfinding (Task 19) validates the benefit of the graphical representation.
*   **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation for using graphs as a state representation and provides a good overview of related work. The extension of GG-NNs feels natural and logical.
*   **Clear Technical Description:** The paper provides a detailed and mathematically sound description of the GGT-NN architecture and the graph transformations.  The pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is particularly helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

*   **Ablation Studies:** While the comparison of models *with* and *without* direct reference is helpful, a more thorough ablation study exploring the impact of each graph transformation (e.g., removing propagation or edge updates) would further clarify the contribution of each component.
*   **Scalability Discussion:**  The paper acknowledges the potential scalability issues. A more in-depth discussion of these limitations and potential mitigation strategies (e.g., sparse graph representations, hierarchical transformations) would be valuable.
*   **Visualization:**  While the paper mentions visualizing the learned graphs, providing more examples of these graphs (beyond those in the appendices) would greatly enhance understanding of what the network is actually learning. Visualizing the evolution of the graph during the Turing Machine simulation would be particularly insightful.
*   **Comparison to other graph methods:** While the paper does a good job relating to GNNs, a clearer discussion of how this work relates to *dynamic* graph neural networks (those that learn graph structure, such as relational inductive biases), would be beneficial.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*   Regarding scalability, have you explored any techniques for reducing the computational cost of the propagation step (e.g., limiting the number of hops, using attention mechanisms to focus on relevant nodes)?
*   The performance on Task 17 (Positional Reasoning) is significantly lower than other tasks. Could you elaborate on potential reasons for this, and whether adjustments to the architecture or training procedure could improve performance on this task?
*   In the Turing machine experiments, how was the initial tape configuration represented as a graph? Was there any manual engineering involved in this representation, or was it learned entirely by the network?
*   You mention potential benefits of the graph-structured internal state for downstream applications. Can you elaborate on specific examples of such applications beyond the tasks presented in the paper?



Overall, this is a well-written and innovative paper with significant potential. Addressing the minor suggestions above would further strengthen the contribution and make it even more impactful.
