## Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamic graph structures as internal representations.  The authors propose differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) and combine them with a recurrent input model to build and manipulate graphs based on natural language input. They demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex update rules on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its versatility in representing and processing structured data.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting approach with potential, the results presented don’t convincingly demonstrate a substantial advance over existing methods, particularly considering the strong supervision used. The experimental evaluation, while comprehensive, feels more like a proof-of-concept than a breakthrough.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Improvement over Strong Baselines:** The bAbI task results, while respectable, don't show a significant and consistent improvement over strong, pre-existing models (MemNN, DMN+). The authors acknowledge this, noting that GGT-NN and MemNN perform comparably on different tasks. The advantage is task-specific, and the gains aren’t substantial enough to justify the increased complexity of the GGT-NN.
*   **Heavy Supervision:** The reliance on providing the “correct graph at each timestep” as supervision is a major concern. This strong supervision heavily influences the model’s performance and makes it difficult to assess its true ability to *learn* graph structures from unstructured data. The claim of learning complex update rules is weakened by this reliance.  While the authors discuss this in the context of needing extra supervision to “extract meaningful graph-structured data”, it fundamentally limits the generalizability of the approach.
*   **Complexity and Scalability:** The quadratic scaling of the model with graph size, as acknowledged by the authors, is a significant practical limitation. While the authors mention future work to address this, it remains a critical issue.  Without addressing scalability, the method's applicability to real-world problems with large-scale graph structures is questionable.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The impact of each component (e.g., direct reference) isn’t fully explored through ablation studies. While the authors mention the benefits of direct reference, a more systematic analysis would strengthen the claims.
* **Presentation:** The paper is very long and contains a lot of details that don’t add to the overall message. It’s difficult to extract the key contributions of the work.



**4. Additional Feedback:**

*   **Explore weaker supervision:**  Investigate methods to reduce the reliance on full graph supervision. Could the model be trained with only edge-level or node-level supervision initially?  Exploring techniques like reinforcement learning for graph construction could be valuable.
*   **Address Scalability:** Provide a more detailed discussion of potential scalability solutions. Explore techniques like sparse graph representations or approximate propagation methods.
*   **More Detailed Analysis of Failure Cases:** The analysis of Task 17 is a good start.  However, a deeper dive into the specific challenges faced by the GGT-NN on difficult tasks would be helpful.
* **Simplify the presentation**: Trim down unnecessary detail, especially in the appendices. Focus on the core ideas and experimental results. A more concise presentation would make the paper more accessible and impactful.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How sensitive are the results to the specific initialization of the graph and node embeddings?
*   Could you provide more detail on the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to the baseline models, especially as graph size increases?
*   What is the rationale behind choosing GRUs for the node state updates and input processing? Have you explored other recurrent architectures?
*   In the cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments, how were the initial states and rules selected? Was there any hyperparameter tuning involved?
*   How would the approach need to be modified to handle graphs with continuous node or edge attributes?
