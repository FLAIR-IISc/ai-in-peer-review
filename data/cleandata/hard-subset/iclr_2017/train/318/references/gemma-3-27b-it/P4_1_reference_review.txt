This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs capable of handling graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core contribution lies in defining differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) and combining them with a recurrent input model to construct and manipulate graphs from natural language input. The authors demonstrate the model’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a novel extension to existing graph neural network architectures and demonstrates promising results, the limited novelty relative to the complexity and the lack of comprehensive comparative analysis with related work lead to a rejection. The core idea of applying differentiable transformations to graphs is interesting, but feels incremental given the substantial existing literature on graph neural networks and related memory augmented neural networks.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited demonstration of significant advantage over existing methods. The bAbI task results, while competitive, are presented alongside a wide array of baselines without a clear argument for *why* the GGT-NN succeeds where others struggle, or provides a unique benefit. The claim of a significant advantage on tasks 19 and 16 is interesting, but a deeper dive into the specific reasons for this improvement is needed. The experiments on the cellular automaton and Turing machines are valuable proof-of-concept, but lack extensive comparison to dedicated algorithms designed for those tasks.

A secondary reason is the presentation. The paper is very lengthy and repetitive. The description of the transformations is detailed but could be more concise. The notation is sometimes cumbersome, and the constant referencing of figures (which are not always essential) interrupts the flow. While the authors provide an implementation, the lack of ablation studies and detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity hinders a thorough understanding of the model’s behavior.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Novelty:** The paper needs to more clearly articulate the specific novelty of the proposed approach *beyond* simply applying differentiable transformations to graphs. How does this differ from existing work on differentiable structure learning or dynamic graph construction?
*   **Comparative Analysis:** A more rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods is crucial. This should include a clear discussion of the strengths and weaknesses of GGT-NN compared to alternatives, and ideally, a comparative analysis of computational cost.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Performing ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (e.g., direct reference, propagation, different transformation orders) would significantly strengthen the paper.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model’s performance to key hyperparameters.
*   **Clarity & Conciseness:** Streamline the presentation. Reduce redundancy and improve the flow of the paper.
*   **Task Selection:** While the chosen tasks demonstrate the model’s versatility, consider including tasks where the graph structure is inherently more complex or ambiguous to better showcase the GGT-NN’s capabilities.
*   **Generalization:** Assess the model's ability to generalize to different graph sizes or structures.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What specific limitations of existing graph neural networks or memory networks does the GGT-NN address, and how?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of why the GGT-NN outperforms other methods on tasks 19 and 16? Are there specific graph structures or relationships that the model excels at capturing?
3.  How does the computational cost of the GGT-NN compare to other methods, particularly for large graphs?
4.  What is the impact of the choice of activation functions (e.g., tanh) within the transformations on the model’s performance?
5.  In the cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments, how does the GGT-NN’s performance compare to dedicated algorithms designed specifically for these tasks?
6.  Why are “direct references” so crucial to the performance of the model on the bAbI tasks?
