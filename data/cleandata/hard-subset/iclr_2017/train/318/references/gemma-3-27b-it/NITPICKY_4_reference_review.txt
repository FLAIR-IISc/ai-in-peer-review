This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, capable of handling graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to utilize differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – to dynamically construct and manipulate graphs, enabling the model to process both textual and graph-structured data. The authors demonstrate the model’s capability on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its potential for tasks requiring complex state representation and manipulation.

**Reject.** While the core idea of combining graph transformations with neural networks is interesting, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor flaws in implementation details, presentation, and experimental rigor. These issues, cumulatively, undermine confidence in the reported results and the overall contribution.

The most significant issue is the lack of clarity and detail in the implementation of the graph transformations. For example, the functions `fset` and `freset` in the edge update are described vaguely as "neural networks" without specifying architectures, activation functions, or hyperparameter settings. This lack of specificity hinders reproducibility and makes it difficult to assess the complexity of the model. Similarly, the description of how direct reference is handled within the update mechanisms feels ad-hoc and lacks justification. Why sum the partial representations, and wouldn’t a simple concatenation or attention mechanism be more effective?

Furthermore, the experimental setup raises concerns. While the results on the bAbI tasks are promising, the comparisons to other state-of-the-art models are presented inconsistently. The authors mention the strong supervision provided to GGT-NN and MemNN, but don't fully explore the implications of this difference. The assertion that the GGT-NN "outperforms" the MemNN model on certain tasks feels misleading given the varying levels of supervision. Additionally, the rule discovery tasks, while intriguing, lack a detailed explanation of the data generation process and the evaluation metrics used. What is the size of the training and test sets? What constitutes a "correct" graph in these tasks?

The paper also contains several minor issues: typos ("sedges" should be "edges"), inconsistencies in notation, and a somewhat verbose writing style. The supplementary material (mentioned in passing) is crucial for understanding the implementation details, yet isn’t provided with this review, preventing a comprehensive assessment. The claims about the generalizability of the Turing machine model, while positive, need stronger evidence—simply stating “perfect output” isn't sufficient. A more rigorous analysis of generalization performance, perhaps with error bars or confidence intervals, is needed.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Code Release:** A complete and well-documented code release is *essential* for a paper of this nature.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of each graph transformation individually and in combination. Which transformations are most crucial for performance?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the model’s performance to different hyperparameters.
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Assess the computational complexity of the GGT-NN and discuss its scalability to larger graphs.
*   **Error Analysis:** Perform a detailed error analysis on the bAbI tasks to identify common failure modes of the model.
*   **Clarify Node Strength:** Elaborate on the role and impact of the node strength `sv`. How does it influence the propagation process and the overall performance? Does a different functional form of sv yield better results?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a more detailed description of the architectures and hyperparameters used for the neural networks within each graph transformation (e.g., `fset`, `freset`, `fadd`)?
2.  Why was the summation approach chosen for incorporating direct references? Have you experimented with other methods, such as concatenation or attention?
3.  What are the sizes of the training and test sets for the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machine)? What criteria were used to define a “correct” graph in these tasks?
4.  What is the computational cost associated with the propagation step for large graphs? Could you provide a complexity analysis of the model?
5.  The paper states graphs are used as the knowledge object for the bAbI task. Are the graphs manually constructed or automatically generated? If manually constructed how does that process work, and is the creation of these graphs scalable to other tasks?
