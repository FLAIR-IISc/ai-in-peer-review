## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs designed to learn and manipulate graph-structured internal states.  The key contribution is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) that allow the network to dynamically build and modify graphs from both textual and graphical inputs. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and reason about state transitions.  The results demonstrate strong performance on these tasks, often outperforming existing methods, particularly on tasks naturally suited to graphical representations.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and promising approach to incorporating dynamic graph structures into neural networks, demonstrating its efficacy through a range of tasks.  The demonstrated ability to learn complex state transitions and outperform established baselines is significant.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The concept of differentiable graph transformations is a significant contribution, enabling the network to learn not just *within* a graph but also to *construct and modify* the graph itself. This moves beyond existing GNNs that typically operate on fixed graph structures.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The experimental results on the bAbI tasks are compelling, demonstrating competitive or superior performance compared to several strong baselines.  The success on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks provides further evidence of the model's ability to learn complex procedural knowledge.  The ablation study comparing models with and without direct reference is insightful.
* **Clear Methodology:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the GGT-NN architecture, the graph transformation operations, and the training procedure. The inclusion of pseudocode (Algorithm 1) is helpful.
* **Well-Positioned in the Literature:** The paper provides a good overview of relevant work in graph neural networks, memory networks, and related areas. It clearly articulates how the GGT-NN builds upon and differentiates itself from existing approaches.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Computational Complexity:** The paper acknowledges the quadratic scaling of the GGT-NN with graph size.  A more detailed discussion of strategies for mitigating this issue (e.g., sparse graph representations, approximate transformations) would be beneficial. Even acknowledging limitations in scalability further would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper would benefit from a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.  Were the hyperparameters tuned extensively? A brief analysis of the impact of key hyperparameters on performance would strengthen the findings.
* **Visualizations:** While Figure 5, 6, and 7 show examples of the graphs, providing visualizations of the learned transformations in action (e.g., showing how edges are added or removed during training) could further illuminate the model's behavior.
* **Error Analysis:** A more in-depth error analysis of the bAbI tasks, specifically focusing on task 17 (Positional Reasoning), could provide insights into the model's limitations and potential avenues for improvement. Why does the model struggle with this particular task?
* **Connection to Knowledge Representation:**  Discuss the learned graph structures in terms of knowledge representation. What kind of “knowledge” is the GGT-NN learning and how does this knowledge contribute to its performance?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Scalability:**  What is the largest graph size (number of nodes and edges) that the current implementation can handle efficiently?  Are there any plans to explore techniques for scaling the model to larger graphs?
* **Transformation Selection:** How is the combination of different graph transformations determined (e.g., the order in which they are applied)? Is this fixed, learned, or manually designed?  Could a learned policy for transformation selection further improve performance?
* **Direct Reference Impact:** You note direct reference improves performance. Is there a quantifiable correlation between the quality of the direct references (e.g., the accuracy of entity recognition) and the overall model performance?
* **Generalization to Different Tasks:** Do you envision this architecture being readily adaptable to other domains beyond question answering and rule discovery? What modifications might be necessary?
* **Alternative Activation Functions:** You use tanh as the activation function.  Were other activation functions (e.g., ReLU) explored?  If so, what were the results?



Overall, this is a well-written and compelling paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks. The demonstrated ability to learn and manipulate graph structures opens up exciting possibilities for representing and reasoning about complex data. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions to address the points raised above.
