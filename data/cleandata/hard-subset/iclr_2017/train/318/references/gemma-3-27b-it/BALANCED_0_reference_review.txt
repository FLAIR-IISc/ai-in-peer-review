This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – to manipulate a graph representing the state of the world, enabling tasks like question answering and modeling cellular automata/Turing machines. The paper demonstrates the GGT-NN on the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents an interesting idea – leveraging differentiable graph transformations to build a more flexible and powerful neural network capable of reasoning about graph-structured data. However, the presentation feels underdeveloped, the experiments lack sufficient baselines, and the novelty of certain claims is unclear, ultimately making it difficult to assess the contribution with confidence.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Clear Novelty:** While the combination of GG-NNs with differentiable graph transformations is presented as novel, the paper doesn’t adequately differentiate itself from existing approaches. The discussion of related work is extensive but doesn't convincingly establish *how* GGT-NN meaningfully advances the state-of-the-art beyond existing graph neural networks or memory networks that can also represent and manipulate relationships.  The ability to *learn* the graph structure itself, while presented as a key advantage, is not unique; several prior works explore dynamic graph construction (though perhaps not with the same differentiable formulation).  A stronger argument for the uniqueness and benefit of the specific transformation set and their interplay is required.

2. **Insufficient Experimental Evaluation:** The results on the bAbI tasks are promising, but the comparison is not thorough enough. While comparisons to MemNNs are included, other modern and relevant baselines (e.g., Transformer-based approaches, more recent GNN architectures) are missing. Moreover, the benefits of *direct reference* aren’t fully justified. The improvement on certain tasks is notable, but the paper doesn't dissect *why* this works, or if this feature is essential to the model’s performance or simply an effective regularization technique. The experiments on the cellular automaton and Turing machine are intriguing demonstrations of capability, but lack comparative analysis.  How does the GGT-NN's performance compare to traditional methods for solving these problems? What is the computational cost relative to other approaches?

3. **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper suffers from several stylistic issues that hinder understanding. The notation is dense, and the detailed descriptions of the graph transformations in Appendix B are difficult to follow without significant effort.  Some explanations feel rushed, assuming a level of background knowledge that may not be reasonable for all reviewers. The flow between sections could be improved, and a more concise discussion of the motivations behind each design choice would be beneficial. The numerous references to figures throughout the text are also cumbersome.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify the Novelty:**  Explicitly state what specific aspects of GGT-NNs are truly novel compared to existing approaches. Focus on the advantages offered by the chosen graph transformations.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include more recent and competitive baselines for the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks. This will better contextualize the GGT-NN's performance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation, different transformation types). This will help understand which elements are most crucial to performance.
*   **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational complexity of the GGT-NN, especially regarding the quadratic scaling with graph size.  Are there strategies for mitigating this issue?
*   **Visualize Graphs:** Include more visualizations of the graphs learned by the GGT-NN, especially for the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks. This would provide valuable insights into the model’s internal representations.
*   **Simplify Presentation:**  Streamline the notation and descriptions of the graph transformations.  Consider providing pseudocode with clear comments.



**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the main advantage of using differentiable graph transformations compared to other methods for learning graph structures (e.g., reinforcement learning, evolutionary algorithms)?
*   Why were the specific graph transformations (node addition, state update, etc.) chosen?  Were other transformations considered?
*   How does the performance of the GGT-NN on the bAbI tasks scale with the size and complexity of the graphs?
*   Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of rules the GGT-NN is able to learn for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks?  What limitations exist?
*   Could you provide a discussion about the effect of hyperparameter tuning on model performance and generalization ability?
