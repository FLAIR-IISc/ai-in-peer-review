This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations to manipulate a graph representing the state of the world, enabling the model to perform tasks like question answering, modeling cellular automata, and simulating Turing machines. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on the bAbI task dataset and on rule discovery tasks, achieving competitive results.

**Reject.** While the idea of using differentiable graph transformations is interesting, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and rigor in its presentation to convincingly demonstrate its contributions. The experimental results, though positive, are not presented with enough detail or comparative analysis to establish a clear advantage over existing methods.

The main issues stem from a lack of clarity in the technical details and presentation. The description of the graph transformations (Node Addition, State Update, Edge Update, Propagation, Aggregation) is verbose and difficult to follow. The mathematical notation is often unclear, and the connection between the equations and the described transformations isn't always explicit. Specifically, the roles of various components within the transformations (e.g., the ‘f’ functions in edge update) aren't fully explained. It is unclear how the hyperparameters of the different transformations were chosen.

Furthermore, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. While the authors compare their results to several baselines on the bAbI tasks, the comparison is not comprehensive. The hyperparameters used for the GGT-NN and the baselines aren’t fully specified, making it difficult to assess the fairness of the comparison. The claim of achieving "100% accuracy" on some tasks is also questionable without a discussion of confidence intervals or statistical significance. The results on the Rule Discovery tasks are less compelling, as the problem setup and metrics aren't clearly defined. The use of a pre-defined graph structure for representing the cellular automaton and Turing machine, while clever, reduces the novelty of learning the graph structure.

Finally, the paper’s organization could be improved. The background section is concise but assumes a strong familiarity with GG-NNs and GGS-NNs. The appendix provides more detail, but this information should be integrated into the main body of the paper for better readability. The conclusions reiterate the benefits, but don't acknowledge the limitations of the approach.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity of Transformations:** Provide a more concise and mathematically precise description of each graph transformation. Consider using diagrams to visually illustrate the flow of information.
*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Thoroughly document the hyperparameters used for the GGT-NN and all baseline models. Include a discussion of how these hyperparameters were chosen.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report confidence intervals or statistical significance tests for all experimental results.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of different components of the GGT-NN (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation).
*   **Scalability Analysis:** Discuss the computational complexity of the GGT-NN and its scalability to larger graphs. The quadratic scaling mentioned in the conclusion is a significant limitation and requires further discussion.
*   **Generalization to Different Tasks:** Evaluate the GGT-NN on a wider range of tasks to demonstrate its generalizability.
*   **Clarify the advantage over existing methods**: What specific benefits does learning graph structure provide in these cases, over predefining it, or using other methods?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the role of the functions 'fset' and 'freset' in the Edge Update transformation? What activation functions are used within these functions, and how are their weights initialized?
2.  The paper mentions that the GGT-NN can be reconfigured for different tasks. Could you provide a more concrete example of how the transformations would be rearranged for a task other than question answering?
3.  What is the impact of the choice of the maximum number of nodes produced by the Node Addition transformation? How does this parameter affect the performance of the model?
4.  The computational complexity of the model is quadratic. Have you explored any techniques to mitigate this, such as using sparse connections or attention mechanisms to reduce the number of nodes considered during propagation?
5.  Regarding the bAbI task results, could you provide the standard deviation for the accuracy scores? Is the reported 100% accuracy statistically significant?
