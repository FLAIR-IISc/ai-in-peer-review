## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension to GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for dynamic graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The key contribution is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – which are combined with a recurrent input model to build and manipulate graphs. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of GGT-NNs on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton task, and Turing machine simulations, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and represent state information effectively.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling extension to existing graph neural network architectures, offering increased flexibility and expressiveness through the introduction of differentiable graph transformations. The experimental results on diverse tasks demonstrate the potential of the GGT-NN framework. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The ability to *learn* graph structure alongside content is a significant advance. Existing graph neural networks typically operate on pre-defined graph structures. GGT-NN's ability to dynamically construct and modify graphs opens up new possibilities for representing and reasoning about data with inherent relational structures.  The introduction of differentiable graph transformations is a well-defined and useful concept.
* **Strong Empirical Validation:** The paper provides compelling evidence of GGT-NN's effectiveness on a variety of tasks.  Achieving high accuracy on the bAbI tasks, particularly task 19 (pathfinding) where it outperforms several baselines, is a strong indication of its capabilities.  The success on rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machine) further demonstrates its ability to learn and generalize complex dynamics.  The ablation study comparing models with and without direct reference is well-conducted and informative.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is well-written and the concepts are explained clearly. The inclusion of Algorithm 1 provides a concise overview of the GGT-NN's operation.  The detailed description of the graph transformations in Appendix B is particularly helpful.
* **Comparison to Related Work:** The authors provide a thorough and insightful comparison to relevant work, accurately positioning GGT-NN within the broader landscape of graph neural networks and memory-augmented networks.  The limitations of existing approaches are clearly highlighted.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Computational Complexity Discussion:** While the paper acknowledges the quadratic scaling with graph size, a more detailed discussion of the computational bottlenecks and potential mitigation strategies would be valuable.  Are there any techniques (e.g., sparse graph representations, approximation methods) that could improve scalability?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper could benefit from a brief analysis of the sensitivity of the model to key hyperparameters (e.g., learning rate, hidden layer size, number of propagation steps).
* **Visualization of Learned Graphs:** While the results demonstrate strong performance, providing visualizations of the graphs learned by the GGT-NN on representative examples (e.g., from the bAbI tasks) would greatly enhance understanding and intuition.  Seeing *how* the network represents the state is crucial.
* **Code Availability:** The mention of code availability is great; ensuring it's readily accessible and well-documented will facilitate reproducibility and further research.
* **Clarify Graph Construction in Cellular Automaton/Turing Machine:** The description of how the cellular automaton and Turing machine are transformed into graphs (Appendix C) could be more visually clear with diagrams. Specifically, explaining the meaning of different edge types would be helpful.


**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  **Scalability:** What are the primary computational bottlenecks encountered when scaling GGT-NN to larger graphs? What strategies have you considered to address these limitations?
2. **Graph Visualization:**  Are you able to visualize the learned graphs for the bAbI tasks, and if so, what key insights have you gained about the network's internal representation?  Could you provide example visualizations?
3. **Choice of Transformations:**  Was the selection of the five specific graph transformations (addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) driven by theoretical considerations or empirical experimentation? Were other transformations explored?
4. **Direct Reference:** Can you elaborate on the rationale for using the sum of partial representations for direct reference? Were other approaches (e.g., attention mechanisms) considered?
5. **Error Analysis:** For task 17 (Positional Reasoning), where performance is lower, can you provide an error analysis to identify the specific types of reasoning failures the model exhibits?



Overall, this is a well-executed and promising work that makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks.  The proposed GGT-NN framework is a valuable addition to the toolkit for representing and reasoning about structured data, and I recommend its acceptance to ICLR 2024.
