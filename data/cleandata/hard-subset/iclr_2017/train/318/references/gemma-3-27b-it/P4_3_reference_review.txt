This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs capable of handling graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to employ differentiable graph transformations to manipulate a graph representing the state of a system, allowing the network to learn to update and reason about this internal state. The approach is demonstrated on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines.

**Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of sufficient novelty and impact compared to existing literature. While the combination of graph transformations and recurrent networks is a reasonable extension, the paper does not clearly demonstrate a significant advantage over established methods, particularly considering the strong supervision used during training. The improvements reported on bAbI tasks are modest and often overshadowed by similar performance from other, more established models (MemNN, DMN+). Furthermore, the presentation of the rule discovery tasks, though interesting, feels somewhat cursory and lacks a detailed comparative analysis. 

The paper’s claims of advancing beyond existing approaches are not convincingly supported by the experimental results. The improvements observed on certain bAbI tasks are not substantial enough to justify the complexity of the proposed architecture. While the ability to learn cellular automata and Turing machines is a positive result, it doesn’t represent a breakthrough given prior work in learning algorithmic tasks with neural networks. The authors acknowledge this somewhat in the discussion section, but don't sufficiently contextualize the contribution within the broader landscape of research.

Supporting arguments:

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core concepts – combining graph neural networks with differentiable transformations – build upon existing work and don't introduce fundamentally new mechanisms. While the specific transformations are well-defined, their impact is incremental.
2. **Strong Supervision:** The superior performance on bAbI tasks relies on providing the "correct graph" during training. This strong supervision significantly simplifies the learning problem and makes it difficult to assess the network's ability to learn meaningful graph representations from raw input. The comparison with other methods that achieve comparable results without such strong supervision highlights this limitation.
3. **Insufficient Comparative Analysis:** The paper lacks a rigorous comparison with state-of-the-art methods on the rule discovery tasks. Simply demonstrating that the GGT-NN *can* learn these tasks isn’t sufficient; the performance needs to be benchmarked against other sequence learning algorithms.
4. **Complexity vs. Performance:** The GGT-NN is a relatively complex architecture. The paper does not adequately justify this complexity given the modest improvements in performance. The authors acknowledge the computational cost, but don't propose solutions or analyze the trade-offs.



Additional Feedback:

* **Clarity of Graph Transformations:** While the transformations are described, a more intuitive explanation with visual examples would enhance understanding. The notation can be dense and difficult to follow.
* **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation) would be valuable.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Exploring the sensitivity of the results to different hyperparameters would strengthen the analysis.
* **Scalability Analysis:** A discussion of the scalability of the approach to larger graphs and more complex tasks would be beneficial. The authors mention quadratic scaling, but don't explore potential mitigation strategies.
* **Error Analysis:** A more detailed error analysis of the bAbI tasks could reveal insights into the limitations of the approach.
* **Algorithm 1 Details:** The 'i(k)' and 'D(k)' inputs within Algorithm 1 are not explicitly defined within the algorithm itself. Clarify their roles and data types.
* **Connection to Knowledge Representation:** The paper frames the internal state as a "graph representing the state of the world." A more thorough discussion of the connection to knowledge representation and reasoning would be helpful.

Questions for the Authors:

1.  How does the performance of the GGT-NN compare to other graph neural network architectures (e.g., GCNs, GraphSAGE) when applied to the bAbI tasks *without* providing the correct graph during training?
2.  What strategies could be employed to mitigate the quadratic scaling issue of the GGT-NN, enabling it to handle larger graphs?
3.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the types of errors the GGT-NN makes on the bAbI tasks, and how these errors relate to the graph representation?
4.  The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments feel isolated. Was there any attempt to transfer learning between these tasks and the bAbI tasks? If so, what were the results?
5.   Can you elaborate on the choice of activation functions and network architectures used within the graph transformations? What experiments were conducted to evaluate different options?
