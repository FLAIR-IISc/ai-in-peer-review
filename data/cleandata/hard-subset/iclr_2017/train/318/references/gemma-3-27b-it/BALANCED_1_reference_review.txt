This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to utilize differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) to manipulate a graph based on input, and then use this graph for tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and Turing machine simulation. The authors demonstrate the approach on the bAbI task dataset and show competitive performance, and also apply it successfully to rule discovery tasks.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of sufficient novelty and substantial improvement over existing methods, coupled with a somewhat convoluted presentation. While the idea of learnable graph transformations is interesting, the paper doesn't convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over established approaches like Memory Networks or GGS-NNs, especially considering the strong supervision required during training. A second reason is a lack of clarity in some of the implementation details, making reproducibility difficult.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Novelty:** The core contribution – learning graph transformations – feels incremental. Existing work (e.g., Differentiable Neural Computers, Memory Networks) already allows for learned manipulation of internal states, albeit not explicitly as graphs. The paper doesn’t fully articulate *why* representing the state as a graph is fundamentally superior for the tasks considered. The claim that "the produced graph can be extracted, visualized, and potentially used in downstream applications" is a potential benefit, but not fully realized or demonstrated within the paper.

2. **Performance Not Convincing:** The bAbI task results are competitive with other methods, but not significantly better, and crucially, rely on *strong supervision* (providing the correct graph at each timestep). This strong supervision undermines the claim of learning to construct a meaningful graphical internal state from scratch. The comparison to other methods in Table 2 is also somewhat uneven; several models were trained without the same level of supervision. The improvement on tasks 5, 8, 16, 18 & 19 is mentioned, but the magnitude of the improvement isn’t dramatic enough to justify the added complexity of the GGT-NN.

3. **Implementation Details & Clarity:** The paper mentions numerous transformations and parameters, but the specifics of how these are implemented (e.g., the exact network architectures for `fset`, `freset`, `i`, `j` functions) are glossed over. The Appendices provide more detail, but the overall presentation feels dense and difficult to follow.  For example, the “direct reference update” section (Section 4.2.1) feels somewhat ad-hoc.  Why is it implemented by summing partial representation vectors? What's the justification for this particular attention mechanism?  Without a clearer rationale, it reads as a heuristic.

4. **Scalability Concerns:** The discussion in the conclusion regarding the quadratic scaling of the GGT-NN is a significant drawback. While acknowledged, the paper doesn't offer concrete ideas for mitigating this issue beyond vague suggestions.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the contribution of each component of the GGT-NN (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation, specific graph transformations).  This would help identify which parts of the architecture are truly essential.
* **Weakly Supervised/Unsupervised Experiments:**  To truly demonstrate the power of learning graphical representations, explore training scenarios with less supervision. Can the GGT-NN learn meaningful graphs from raw text without being provided the correct graph at each step?
* **More Thorough Comparison:**  Provide a more direct comparison to existing methods under similar training conditions (same level of supervision, same dataset splits).  Focus on statistical significance when reporting results.
* **Visualization & Interpretability:** Include more visualizations of the learned graphs. What types of relationships are being captured? Are the graphs interpretable? This could help demonstrate the benefits of using a graph-based representation.
* **Clarify Implementation:** Provide more detailed explanations of the implementation choices. Justify the architecture of the various functions (e.g., `fset`, `freset`) and explain why they were chosen over other alternatives.
* **Address Scalability:** Propose concrete strategies for addressing the quadratic scaling issue. Explore techniques like sparse graph representations or approximate inference.

**Questions for the Authors:**

* The "direct reference update" mechanism seems specific to the bAbI tasks. How generalizable is this approach to other datasets where direct entity references might not be available or clearly defined?
* What is the sensitivity of the model’s performance to the hyperparameters of the graph transformations (e.g., the learning rate, the size of the hidden states)?
* How does the performance of the GGT-NN compare to GGS-NNs *without* the strong graph supervision?  If GGS-NNs perform similarly or better, what justifies the added complexity of the GGT-NN?
* Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned graphs? What types of relationships are being captured, and are these relationships meaningful or interpretable?
* Can you elaborate on potential methods for improving the scalability of the GGT-NN?
