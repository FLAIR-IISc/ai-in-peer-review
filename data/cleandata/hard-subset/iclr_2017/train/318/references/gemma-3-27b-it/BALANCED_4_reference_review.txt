This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs designed to manipulate graph-structured intermediate representations and produce graph-structured outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations to incrementally construct and modify a graph, enabling the model to learn complex update rules and reason about relationships. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN on the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing promising results.

**Decision:** Reject. While the paper presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and experimental rigor to warrant acceptance. The improvements over established baselines, especially on the bAbI tasks, are not compelling enough given the complexity introduced.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Lack of Clarity in Transformation Details:** The description of the graph transformations, while conceptually sound, is often vague. The notation is heavy, and the implementation details (e.g., the specific neural network architectures used within each transformation) are often glossed over. This makes it difficult to fully understand the model’s mechanics and reproduce the results.  For instance, the functions `fset` and `freset` in the Edge Update transformation are only stated to be neural networks, without specifying their size or architecture. 

2. **Limited Baselines & Incremental Improvements:** The comparison to existing baselines, while present, isn't strong enough to justify the complexity of the GGT-NN. While the authors achieve competitive performance on some bAbI tasks, the improvements are often marginal and not consistently observed across all tasks. Furthermore, the comparison isn’t always fair – the GGT-NN and MemNN models are described as ‘strongly supervised’ while others are not. This makes it difficult to attribute performance differences solely to the architectural advantages of GGT-NN. The paper needs a more thorough comparison, including ablation studies to understand the contribution of each component (e.g., direct reference).

3. **Experimental Setup Concerns:** The experimental setup raises questions. The use of “full graph information” as supervision in the bAbI tasks is a strong inductive bias. It’s unclear how well the model generalizes to situations where such explicit graph information isn’t available. The rules used for the Turing machine are not fully specified. More details on the training procedure (learning rates, optimization algorithms, regularization techniques) are needed to ensure reproducibility and assess the robustness of the results.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify Notation and Provide Pseudocode:** Rewriting key equations with clearer notation and including more detailed pseudocode for the graph transformations would significantly improve readability.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the impact of each component of the GGT-NN (e.g., node addition, edge update, direct reference). This will help identify which components are most important for performance.
*   **More Detailed Baseline Comparisons:** Include more relevant and state-of-the-art baselines. Ensure that the comparison is fair by carefully controlling for the level of supervision used in each model. If possible, train baselines with the same amount of supervision as GGT-NN.
*   **Error Analysis:** Provide an error analysis of the GGT-NN’s performance on the bAbI tasks. This would help identify the types of errors the model is making and provide insights into potential areas for improvement.
*   **Generalization to Unstructured Data:**  Explore the model’s ability to handle unstructured input and learn graph structures from scratch, rather than relying on pre-defined graphs.
*   **Computational Complexity Discussion:** The paper briefly mentions the quadratic scaling of the model. A more detailed discussion of the computational complexity and potential optimizations would be valuable.
*   **Rule Details:** Include the precise transition rules for the Turing machine experiments in the main text or supplementary material.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What are the specific architectures (number of layers, hidden units, activation functions) used for the neural networks within each graph transformation (e.g., `fset`, `freset`)?
*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the level of supervision provided in the bAbI tasks? Could the model achieve similar results with less explicit graph information?
*   What steps were taken to prevent overfitting, especially given the strong supervision used in the bAbI tasks?
*   Can you provide more details about the training procedure, including the learning rate schedule, optimization algorithm, and regularization techniques used?
*   What is the computational cost of running the GGT-NN on graphs with a large number of nodes and edges?  Are there any techniques that can be used to mitigate the quadratic scaling?
