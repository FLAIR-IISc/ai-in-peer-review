This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, capable of manipulating graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations—node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation—to build and modify graphs based on input, allowing the network to perform tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and Turing machine simulation. The authors demonstrate the model's performance on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and a Turing machine, showing competitive or state-of-the-art results in some cases.

**Reject.** While the idea of combining graph neural networks with differentiable graph transformations is interesting, the paper suffers from a substantial number of minor flaws, inconsistencies, and a lack of rigorous evaluation that ultimately undermine its contribution. The presentation is also overly verbose and lacks crucial detail in specific implementation choices.

Here's a breakdown of supporting arguments:

1. **Lack of Ablation Studies & Insufficient Justification of Design Choices:** The paper introduces numerous transformations and configurations, but lacks comprehensive ablation studies to demonstrate the individual contribution of each component. For example, the direct reference update consistently improves performance, but there's insufficient discussion on *why* this is the case and what specific scenarios benefit the most. The choice of GRUs, tanh activations, and specific network architectures within the transformations feels arbitrary without justification.

2. **Inconsistent Reporting and Minor Errors:** Table 1 states that most task models reached 95% accuracy with 500 or fewer training examples, yet Table 2 reports accuracy after 1000 examples. This inconsistency is confusing. Furthermore, the statement that task 17 attains a “high accuracy” when it’s the only task not reaching 95% is misleading. Throughout the paper, minor typographical errors and awkward phrasing detract from the professionalism.

3. **Overly Verbose and Redundant:** The paper is excessively long, with repetitive explanations and detailed background information that could be condensed. The comparison with related work is extensive but lacks critical assessment – it mostly *lists* related works rather than rigorously comparing and contrasting the GGT-NN with them. Appendix A, in particular, feels like a regurgitation of the original GG-NN paper.

4. **Insufficient Detail in Appendices:** The appendices, meant to clarify implementation details, are themselves lacking in precision. For example, the description of the graph format for the Turing machine task (Appendix C) is vague and requires significant interpretation. The function fadd (Appendix B) is described vaguely.

5. **Questionable Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the quadratic scaling issue, but don’t offer substantial discussion on potential mitigation strategies beyond mentioning sparse edge connections. This is a critical limitation, and the lack of deeper exploration weakens the practical relevance of the work.

6. **Lack of Statistical Significance:** The results presented in the tables lack any indication of statistical significance. Were the improvements observed actually meaningful, or could they be due to random chance?

**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Simplify the Presentation:** Condense the introduction, related work, and background sections. Focus on the novel contributions and avoid excessive detail on well-established concepts.
*   **Rigorous Evaluation:** Conduct thorough ablation studies to isolate the impact of each graph transformation. Provide statistical significance tests for all reported results.
*   **Clarify Implementation Details:** Provide precise specifications for all network architectures, activation functions, and optimization procedures.
*   **Address Scalability:** Explore and discuss potential solutions to the quadratic scaling issue in more detail.
*   **Improve Writing Quality:** Proofread carefully to eliminate grammatical errors and awkward phrasing.
*   **Appendices:** Provide code snippets or more detailed pseudocode in the appendices to clarify implementation choices.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific criteria were used to determine the maximum number of nodes added during the node addition transformation? How sensitive are the results to this parameter?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of the neural network architectures used within the fset, freset, and f functions? What were the specific layer sizes, activation functions, and initialization schemes used?
*   How does the performance of the GGT-NN compare to other graph neural network architectures (e.g., Graph Convolutional Networks) when applied to the same bAbI tasks, using the same level of supervision?
*   What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to other models on the bAbI tasks, specifically concerning memory usage and training time?
*   Given the quadratic scaling issue, are there any plans to explore alternative graph representations or approximation techniques to improve scalability?
