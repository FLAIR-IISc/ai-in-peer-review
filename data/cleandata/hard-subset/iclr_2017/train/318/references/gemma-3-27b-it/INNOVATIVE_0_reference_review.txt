This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs, enabling manipulation of a graphical internal state. The core idea is to combine graph transformations with a recurrent input model to incrementally construct a graph from natural language input, and subsequently use this graph for downstream tasks like question answering or modeling dynamical systems (cellular automata, Turing machines). The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a 1D cellular automaton, and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning and manipulating a graph-structured state is intriguing and represents a potentially significant shift, the paper feels incomplete and lacks sufficient validation to warrant acceptance at this stage. The results, while promising, aren't overwhelmingly convincing, and the novelty isn't fully leveraged to push the boundaries of performance on the chosen tasks beyond existing methods in a substantial way.

The primary reason for rejection stems from the limited scope of experimental validation. The bAbI results show competitive performance, but are often comparable to existing strongly supervised methods, and the improvements aren’t consistently significant across all tasks. Crucially, the performance on task 17 remains a concern, and the analysis doesn’t sufficiently address *why* the GGT-NN struggles with this specific problem. The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments are interesting demonstrations of principle, but feel more like proof-of-concept than rigorous evaluations. They lack comparison to existing methods designed for those specific tasks, making it difficult to assess the true value of the GGT-NN approach. Furthermore, the extended graph sequence input experiment shows inferior performance than the original configuration, raising questions on its utility.

The presentation also suffers from some issues. The extensive background material, while appreciated, feels slightly disconnected from the core contribution. The detailed descriptions of the graph transformations (Appendix B) are comprehensive, but somewhat difficult to follow without clear, concise diagrams illustrating the flow of information. The numerous equations also contribute to this issue. While the code is available, the explanations of the transformations could be made more accessible.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Ablation Studies:** It would be valuable to include ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components of the GGT-NN, such as the direct reference mechanism, intermediate propagation, and specific graph transformations. This would provide insights into which aspects of the model are most important for performance.
*   **Scalability:** The paper acknowledges the potential scalability issues. A discussion of potential solutions (e.g., sparse connections, node sampling) would be beneficial.
*   **Comparison with Graph Neural Networks:** A more thorough comparison with existing Graph Neural Network (GNN) architectures, beyond simply mentioning their limitations, would be helpful. How does the GGT-NN differ in its ability to *learn* graph structure versus simply operating on a pre-defined graph?
*   **Theoretical Analysis:** While not essential, some theoretical analysis of the GGT-NN's expressiveness or learning properties could strengthen the paper.
* **Task 17 Analysis:** The paper needs to provide a more detailed explanation for the poor performance on Task 17. Is it the increased number of entities? The shorter stories? A limitation of the graph representation?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the computational complexity of the GGT-NN, and how does it scale with the size of the graph and the sequence length?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the GGT-NN to the choice of hyperparameters, and what strategies were used for hyperparameter tuning?
3.  Why does the GGT-NN struggle with task 17, and what modifications could be made to improve performance on this task?
4.  What are the potential applications of the GGT-NN beyond the tasks presented in the paper? Can you provide some examples?
5.  You mention the possibility of combining supervised and unsupervised graph learning. Could you elaborate on how this might be achieved, and what benefits it could offer?
