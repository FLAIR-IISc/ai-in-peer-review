This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs capable of manipulating graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to learn graph transformations – addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – and combine them with a recurrent input model to construct and reason about graphs from natural language input. The authors demonstrate the model on the bAbI task dataset, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing promising results, particularly on tasks requiring graph-based reasoning.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning graph transformations and dynamically constructing graphs is exciting and represents a significant conceptual leap, the paper feels incomplete and lacks sufficient validation to warrant acceptance at this stage. The results, while positive, aren't dramatically beyond existing methods, and the experiments feel somewhat limited in scope considering the complexity of the proposed model.

The novelty of the approach is undeniable. The ability to learn how to *modify* graphs, rather than simply operating on pre-defined graph structures, is a potentially powerful paradigm shift. The modularity of the graph transformations is also a strong point, allowing for flexible application to different tasks. The demonstration on Turing machines and cellular automata, while simplified, suggests that the model can learn complex update rules. However, the experimental validation feels insufficient to support the broad claims made. 

Specifically:

*   **Limited Baselines:** The comparison to existing methods is somewhat weak. While several baselines are listed, the performance gains aren't always substantial, especially considering the added complexity of GGT-NN. A more thorough comparison with state-of-the-art graph neural networks or sequence-to-sequence models specifically tailored to the bAbI tasks would be beneficial.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper presents results with and without “direct reference,” but a more comprehensive ablation study exploring the impact of each graph transformation (node addition, edge update, etc.) would be valuable. Which transformations are most crucial for performance? Are there redundant transformations?
*   **Task 17 Performance:** The poor performance on task 17 (Positional Reasoning) is concerning. The authors acknowledge the complexity of the task but don’t offer a clear explanation for why GGT-NN struggles. Is it a limitation of the graph representation, the model architecture, or the training process?
*   **Supervision Strategy:** The reliance on providing the “correct graph” during training is a significant point. While justified as aiding learning, it raises questions about the model’s ability to generalize to scenarios where a ground-truth graph is unavailable. The degree to which the performance relies on this strong supervision needs to be more clearly articulated.
*   **Scalability:** The authors acknowledge the potential scalability issues. A discussion of strategies for mitigating these issues (e.g., sparse graphs, attention mechanisms) would be valuable.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   The figures are generally helpful, but Figure 1 could be clearer in illustrating the relationship between node/edge attributes (xv, sv, Cv) and the visual representation of the graph.
*   The appendix provides valuable details on the graph transformations, but it could be more concise and focused on the key implementation choices.
*   Consider exploring the potential of using the learned graph transformations for other tasks beyond question answering and rule discovery.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to other GNNs like GGNN or GCN on tasks of increasing graph size?
*   How sensitive is the model's performance to the choice of the initial hidden state for node addition?
*   Could you elaborate on the limitations of the graph representation for task 17 and potential modifications to address these limitations?
*   How does the performance of the model change when the supervision signal (providing the correct graph) is gradually reduced during training?
