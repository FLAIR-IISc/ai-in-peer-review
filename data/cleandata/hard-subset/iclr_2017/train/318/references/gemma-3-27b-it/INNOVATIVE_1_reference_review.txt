This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs capable of manipulating graph-structured internal states and producing graph-structured outputs. The core idea is to learn graph transformations—node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation—to process information and solve tasks. The authors demonstrate the model’s ability to learn from textual input, construct internal graphs, and answer questions (bAbI tasks), as well as model simple computational primitives like cellular automata and Turing machines.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning graph transformations is compelling and potentially impactful, the paper feels significantly underdeveloped and lacks sufficient validation to warrant acceptance at this time. The reported results, while positive, are presented without crucial comparisons and sufficient ablation studies to convincingly demonstrate the benefits of the proposed architecture. The novelty is high, but the experimental grounding is too weak.

The primary weakness lies in the experimental evaluation. The bAbI task results are presented alongside a comprehensive list of baselines, but the discussion focuses more on highlighting successes *relative* to other strongly supervised methods (MemNN) rather than demonstrating a clear advantage over unsupervised or weakly supervised alternatives. The claim of strong performance on tasks 19 (pathfinding) and 16 (induction) is compelling, but lacks detailed analysis. Why does the GGT-NN outperform the others specifically on these tasks? More importantly, the leap to demonstrating the model can learn cellular automata and Turing machines feels incomplete. While impressive in concept, the experimental setup is underspecified. How complex were the Turing machines? What is the computational cost of training these models? A table comparing performance with established models for these tasks is absent. The section on "Other Transformation Configurations" feels like a list of possibilities without concrete exploration. The limited analysis of the direct reference component (Table 2) is also a concern; the benefit isn't thoroughly dissected. Furthermore, the discussion of scaling issues and future directions (in the conclusion) implicitly acknowledges the current limitations, reinforcing the need for more substantial validation.

The paper’s strength is its novelty. The idea of learning graph transformations as a general framework for reasoning and computation is genuinely interesting. The modular design allows for potential applications beyond those explored here. The differentiation of graph structures opens up possibilities for end-to-end learning that are not available in more rigid graph neural network architectures.

Here are some suggestions for improvement:

*   **Expanded Experimental Evaluation:** Include a more thorough comparison with relevant baselines on the rule discovery tasks (cellular automata and Turing machines). Report training times and computational costs.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Systematically investigate the contribution of each graph transformation. What happens when specific transformations are removed? How does the direct reference component affect performance?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A discussion of the sensitivity to hyperparameter settings would strengthen the results.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Provide visualizations of the learned graphs for the bAbI tasks and the rule discovery tasks. This would offer insights into *how* the model is representing knowledge.
*   **Clarify Supervision:** Be precise about the degree of supervision used. The mention of "full graph information" is vague.
*   **Address Scaling Concerns:** Elaborate on potential strategies to mitigate the scalability issues identified in the conclusion.

I have the following questions for the authors:

1.  For the Turing machine experiments, can you provide more details on the complexity of the machines tested (number of states, symbols, rule length)?
2.  The paper mentions that Task 17 (Positional Reasoning) was challenging. What specific aspects of this task made it difficult for the model to learn? Did modifying the graph representation or training procedure improve performance on this task?
3.  Could you elaborate on the specific benefits of using differentiable graph transformations compared to traditional, non-differentiable graph manipulation techniques in the context of end-to-end learning? What are the key advantages in terms of optimization and gradient flow?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of the GGT-NN to the choice of activation function within the graph transformations (e.g., tanh, ReLU)? Did you experiment with different activation functions, and if so, what were the results?
