This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending GG-NNs and GGS-NNs to handle graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to utilize differentiable graph transformations—node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation—to dynamically construct and manipulate graphs from textual input, enabling tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and Turing machine simulation. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN on the bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks, achieving competitive results.

**Reject.** While the paper presents an interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures, the novelty feels incremental, and the experimental validation, while thorough, doesn’t demonstrate a significant breakthrough. The performance gains on the bAbI tasks are often marginal compared to existing strong baselines, and the rule discovery tasks, while interesting, lack a clear comparison to other methods suited for those tasks. The paper feels somewhat lengthy and verbose, leading to difficulty in quickly grasping the core contributions.

Here's a more detailed breakdown:

**Strengths:**

*   **Well-motivated extension:** The idea of allowing dynamic graph structures and transformations is a logical step forward from GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, opening up possibilities for representing and processing more complex data.
*   **Comprehensive framework:** The proposed GGT-NN architecture, with its various graph transformations, offers a flexible and potentially powerful framework for graph-based reasoning.
*   **Thorough experimentation:** The authors evaluate the GGT-NN on a diverse set of tasks, including the bAbI dataset and rule discovery tasks, providing some evidence of its capabilities.
*   **Clear description of transformations:** The individual graph transformations are clearly defined and explained, which aids in understanding the overall architecture.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Incremental novelty:** The core contribution—differentiable graph transformations—feels more like an engineering extension rather than a fundamental breakthrough. The authors don't convincingly demonstrate a substantial advantage over existing methods that can also represent relational data.
*   **Limited performance gains:** While the GGT-NN performs well on the bAbI tasks, the gains over strong baselines (MemNN, DMN+) are often small and not always statistically significant. The reported 64% improvement on Task 19 seems unusually high and warrants further scrutiny.
*   **Lack of comparison on rule discovery tasks:** The rule discovery experiments are interesting, but the paper lacks a comparison to other algorithms specifically designed for learning cellular automata or Turing machine rules. It’s unclear how the GGT-NN compares to dedicated methods in these areas.
*   **Verbosity & Redundancy:** The paper is quite lengthy, and some sections, particularly the background and related work, contain repetitive information. This makes it harder to focus on the key contributions.
*  **Supervision requirement**: The reliance on providing the "correct graph" during training as supervision is a significant limitation, particularly for tasks where obtaining such ground truth data is expensive or impossible.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Sharpen the novelty:** Clearly articulate what makes the GGT-NN fundamentally different and more powerful than existing graph neural networks or relational reasoning methods. Focus on the unique benefits of dynamically transforming the graph structure.
*   **Strengthen the empirical evaluation:**
    *   Conduct a more rigorous statistical analysis of the bAbI task results. Report confidence intervals and p-values to assess the significance of the observed improvements.
    *   Compare the GGT-NN to more relevant baselines on the rule discovery tasks. Consider algorithms specifically designed for learning cellular automata or Turing machine rules.
    *   Investigate the impact of different graph transformation configurations. Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of each transformation type.
*   **Address the supervision issue:** Explore methods for reducing the reliance on strong supervision. Consider semi-supervised learning approaches or techniques for learning graph structures from unstructured data.
*   **Streamline the presentation:** Reduce the length of the paper by removing redundant information and focusing on the core contributions. Improve the clarity and conciseness of the writing.
*  **Clarify the benefit of direct reference**: Provide a more detailed explanation of *why* direct reference improves performance. Is it simply making it easier to locate relevant entities, or is there a more fundamental reason?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Regarding the 64% improvement on bAbI task 19: Could you provide more details about this result, including the specific configurations used, statistical significance tests, and a comparison to other methods on this task?
2.  How does the computational complexity of the GGT-NN scale with the size of the graph and the number of transformations? Is there a strategy to mitigate the quadratic scaling?
3.  What strategies could be employed to reduce the reliance on providing the "correct graph" during training, especially for tasks where obtaining such supervision is difficult?
4.  Why was the decision made to implement the graph transformation functions (fset, freset, f fwd y, f bwd y) as fully connected neural networks? Were other options considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
5.  Can you elaborate on the types of tasks for which you believe the GGT-NN architecture would be particularly well-suited, beyond those presented in the paper?
