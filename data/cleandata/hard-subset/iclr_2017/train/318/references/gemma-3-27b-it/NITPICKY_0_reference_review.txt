This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs designed to manipulate graph-structured intermediate representations and produce graph-structured outputs. The core idea is to use differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – to build and modify graphs based on input data, such as natural language. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN on the bAbI task dataset, a 1D cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex update rules and internal state representations.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is intriguing and potentially powerful, the paper suffers from a multitude of minor, yet cumulatively significant, issues in presentation, implementation details, and experimental rigor. These flaws undermine confidence in the reported results and the overall validity of the approach.

The primary reason for rejection stems from a pervasive lack of clarity and detail in the methodological descriptions. The graph transformation functions (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) are introduced but their implementation specifics – particularly the neural network architectures used within them – are vague. For instance, the size and type of layers within the functions *fadd*, *fset*, *freset*, *f fwd y*, *f bwd y*, *i*, and *j* are not specified. This makes reproducing the results impossible and evaluating the design choices difficult. Further, the claim of using a GRU layer for *fadd* without specifying its hidden size or activation functions leaves the implementation open to interpretation.

Secondly, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail. The bAbI experiments report accuracy, but do not provide standard deviations or confidence intervals. The ablation study comparing models *with* and *without* direct reference is promising, but the statistical significance of the observed differences is not assessed. The “direct reference” mechanism itself feels ad-hoc. The specific criteria for identifying “direct references” from the input text aren’t fully clear. It’s stated that it’s based on node type names, but the treatment of multi-word entities (mentioned in Appendix C) raises questions about consistency.

The rule discovery tasks, while interesting, are also insufficiently detailed. The graphical format mapping for the cellular automaton and Turing machine is described, but lacks illustrations to aid understanding.  Specifically, how edge types represent transition rules in the Turing machine isn’t immediately obvious and a diagram would significantly improve clarity.  Furthermore, the claim of "perfect output" on extended tasks (20/30 timesteps) feels suspicious without a more rigorous statistical analysis of performance across a larger test set. Table 3 just states "perfect", which isn't a useful metric.

The comparison to related work is adequate but lacks nuance. While the authors acknowledge relevant approaches, the discussion doesn’t delve into the specific advantages and disadvantages of GGT-NNs in relation to those methods, beyond the general statements. The mention of limitations regarding computational cost is good, but doesn’t offer potential solutions.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Detailed Implementations:** Provide explicit details of all neural network architectures used within the graph transformations, including layer sizes, activation functions, and initialization schemes.
*   **Statistical Analysis:** Report standard deviations and confidence intervals for all experimental results. Perform statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to assess the importance of observed differences.
*   **Clarify Direct Reference:** Provide a clear and unambiguous definition of the “direct reference” mechanism, including how it handles multi-word entities and edge cases.
*   **Illustrations:** Include diagrams illustrating the graphical representation of the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks.
*   **Ablation Study:** Perform a more comprehensive ablation study to investigate the impact of individual graph transformation components and hyperparameters.
*   **Code Release:** Release the source code to facilitate reproducibility and further research.
*   **Address Scaling:** More fully address the scalability limitations of the approach and discuss potential optimization strategies.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What are the specific criteria used to determine the size and architecture of the neural networks within each graph transformation (e.g., *fadd*, *fset*, etc.)? What hyperparameters were tuned, and how?
2.  Could you provide more details on how “direct references” are identified in the input text, and how the model handles ambiguous or partial references?
3.  What is the computational complexity of the GGT-NN model with respect to the number of nodes and edges in the graph? How does this complexity scale with the size of the input?
4.  Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the "perfect output" results for the extended cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks? What is the sample size, and what is the confidence level?
5.  What specific advantages does the graphical representation offer over other methods (e.g. LSTMs, transformers) for the bAbI tasks? Can you explain how these advantages translate to measurable gains in performance?
