This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs that allows for graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to employ differentiable graph transformations to manipulate a graph representing the state of the world, enabling the model to solve tasks like question answering, cellular automaton modeling, and Turing machine simulation. The paper demonstrates the model’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks and rule discovery tasks, achieving strong results.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a technically sound and potentially interesting extension to existing graph neural network architectures, the novelty appears incremental, and the presentation lacks sufficient clarity regarding the implementation details and comparative advantage over existing approaches. The experimental validation, while demonstrating good performance, doesn’t convincingly establish a substantial improvement over state-of-the-art methods, especially considering the strong supervision employed.

The primary reason for rejection is the limited clarity and justification of novelty. The paper describes a combination of existing techniques (GG-NNs, GRUs, attention mechanisms) with a set of graph transformations. While the combination is presented as a novel contribution, the paper doesn't sufficiently articulate *why* this specific combination is advantageous compared to alternative approaches. The potential benefits of explicitly representing and manipulating a graph are mentioned, but not rigorously demonstrated. For instance, the comparison to MemNNs acknowledges overlapping performance on some tasks, without fully explaining the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. The improvements observed on tasks 5, 8, 16, and 19 are noted, but a deeper analysis of *why* the GGT-NN excels in these specific cases is missing.

Secondly, while the implementation is described in detail in the appendices, the main body of the paper lacks a high-level overview of the architecture and the interaction between different components. The extensive use of transformations (Node addition, state update, edge update etc.) makes the overall system complex, and the paper does not clearly explain how these components collaborate to achieve the desired outcome. Furthermore, the hyperparameter settings and architectural choices are not thoroughly justified. The choice of using GRUs, the specific network architectures for the transformations (e.g., number of layers, hidden dimensions), and the optimization process are presented without clear rationale.

Here’s additional feedback to improve the paper:

*   **Clarify the novelty:** Explicitly state the core contribution of the paper. Is it the specific set of differentiable graph transformations, the architecture for combining these transformations, or the application to tasks requiring dynamic graph manipulation?
*   **Provide a clearer architecture overview:** Include a diagram illustrating the overall architecture of the GGT-NN, highlighting the flow of information between different components.
*   **Justify architectural choices:** Explain the rationale behind the key design decisions, such as the choice of GRUs and the specific network architectures used in the transformations.
*   **Enhance comparative analysis:** Provide a more in-depth comparison with existing methods (e.g., MemNNs, DNCs, LRNNs), highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of each approach. Include ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual components of the GGT-NN.
*   **Discuss limitations:** Acknowledge the limitations of the current approach, such as the computational cost and scalability issues.
*   **Provide more details on supervision:** Clarify the level of supervision used in each experiment. The strong supervision with full graph information in the bAbI tasks should be clearly acknowledged as a potential factor contributing to the performance.
*   **Address the generalization on Rule Discovery tasks:** The claim of generalization to longer sequences (20 and 30 timesteps) in the Rule Discovery tasks needs further justification. What metric is used to assess "perfect output"? Showing a learning curve or examples of generated sequences would strengthen this claim.

Here are some questions for the authors:

*   What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to other graph neural network architectures like GG-NNs or GCNs?
*   How does the performance of the GGT-NN change when the level of supervision is reduced?
*   What are the specific hyperparameters used for each task and how were they tuned?
*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of the benefits of using differentiable graph transformations compared to other methods for manipulating graph structures?
*   How sensitive is the model to the choice of the activation function (tanh) in the GRU cells and the transformation layers?
