## Review of "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

**1. Summary:**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, capable of learning and manipulating graph-structured internal states. The key contribution lies in allowing both graph-structured *intermediate representations* and graph-structured *outputs*, facilitated by differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation). The authors demonstrate the model's versatility on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex state update rules and solve tasks requiring reasoning about relationships and sequences.  The paper argues that representing state as graphs offers advantages for tasks with underlying relational structure.

**2. Decision:**

**Reject**. While the paper presents an interesting extension to GNNs and demonstrates promising results, the experimental validation lacks sufficient depth and comparative analysis, and the presentation is somewhat verbose, obscuring key contributions.  The claims of substantial improvements over existing methods are not fully convincing given the limitations in the experimental setup.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Limited Baselines & Comparison:** The comparison to existing methods, while present, isn't comprehensive enough to justify the claims of improvement. Many of the compared models (MemNN, DMN+) are significantly more complex. The advantage of the GGT-NN appears to be largely due to strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep), and a fair comparison would require similar supervision for other models. The authors acknowledge this but downplay the impact.
* **Strong Supervision Reliance:** The model heavily relies on providing the "correct graph" at each timestep during training. This strong supervision significantly simplifies the learning problem and raises concerns about the model’s ability to learn useful representations in more realistic, unsupervised or weakly supervised settings.  The benefits of graph representation may be overshadowed by the supervisory signal.
* **Complexity vs. Benefit:**  The introduction of numerous graph transformations and the associated parameters add significant complexity to the model. The paper doesn't always clearly articulate *why* each transformation is necessary or how it contributes to performance gains beyond simpler alternatives.  The added complexity feels unjustified given the relatively modest gains on some tasks.
* **Presentation & Clarity:** The paper is quite lengthy and includes significant background material. While helpful, it sometimes obscures the core novelty of the GGT-NN. The descriptions of the graph transformations, while detailed in the appendix, could be more concise and integrated into the main text.  Figures are numerous, but sometimes lack clear annotation or are overly complex.



**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conduct ablation studies to assess the importance of each graph transformation. Specifically, evaluate the performance of the model without node addition, edge updates, etc., to understand their individual contributions.
* **Reduced Supervision Experiments:** Explore training the model with reduced supervision. Can the model learn meaningful representations with only partial graph information, or by using reinforcement learning to reward correct graph construction?
* **More Robust Baselines:** Compare the GGT-NN to more recent and competitive GNN architectures (e.g., Graph Attention Networks, GraphSAGE) using a standardized experimental setup. Consider pre-training techniques to level the playing field.
* **Analysis of Learned Graphs:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the graphs learned by the model. Visualize the graph structures and discuss what the learned connections represent. This would provide valuable insights into the model's internal reasoning process.
* **Clarify Motivation:**  More explicitly state the scenarios where a GGT-NN would be preferable to existing approaches.  What types of tasks *specifically* benefit from the flexibility of dynamically constructing and manipulating graph states?
* **Conciseness:**  Streamline the presentation by removing redundant information and focusing on the key contributions of the work.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Supervision Sensitivity:** How sensitive is the model’s performance to the level of supervision provided?  What is the performance drop when reducing the amount of graph-level supervision?
* **Scalability:** The paper mentions the quadratic scaling of the model. How does this limit its applicability to larger graphs or more complex tasks? Are there potential optimizations to mitigate this scalability issue?
* **Transformation Selection:** How does the choice of graph transformations influence the model’s performance? Is there a principled way to select the optimal combination of transformations for a given task?
* **Interpretability:** Beyond visualization, what techniques can be used to interpret the learned parameters of the graph transformations and understand their function?
* **Generalization to Unseen Tasks:** Have you tested the model’s ability to generalize to new tasks *without* retraining, leveraging the learned graph transformations?



In conclusion, while the GGT-NN presents a potentially valuable extension to GNNs, the lack of rigorous comparative analysis and the reliance on strong supervision prevent a positive recommendation at this time. Addressing the points raised above would significantly strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
