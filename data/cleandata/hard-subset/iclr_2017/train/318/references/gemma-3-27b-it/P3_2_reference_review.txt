## ICLR 2024 Review: Learning Graphical State Transitions

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture extending GG-NNs by enabling graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs. The core idea is to learn and manipulate a graphical internal state, built incrementally from natural language input, for tasks requiring reasoning about relationships and states.  The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks, showcasing strong performance, and on rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), demonstrating its ability to learn complex update rules. The paper proposes a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) as the building blocks of the GGT-NN. The supervision strategy, including graph-level training at each timestep, is also a valuable contribution.

**2. Decision**

**Accept with Minor Revisions.**  The paper presents a compelling and well-executed approach to incorporating graphical representations into sequential reasoning tasks.  The results are promising, particularly the success on the rule discovery tasks, showcasing the model's ability to learn complex dynamics.  However, the presentation could be streamlined and certain experimental details require further clarification.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

* **Novelty and Significance:** The GGT-NN architecture is a significant extension of existing GNN-based sequential models. Allowing the graph *structure itself* to be learned and transformed, rather than being fixed, is a key contribution. This opens up possibilities for more flexible and powerful representations.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The bAbI task results are competitive with state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of the GGT-NN. More importantly, the results on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks are very impressive, demonstrating the model's capability to learn and execute complex rules operating on graph structures.  These tasks are particularly convincing as they require genuine understanding of state transitions.
* **Well-Defined Methodology:** The paper provides a clear description of the GGT-NN architecture and the differentiable graph transformations. The explanation of the supervision strategy, including the handling of node order, is thoughtful and addresses a potential challenge.
* **Thorough Related Work:** The authors demonstrate a good understanding of relevant literature in GNNs, sequence modeling, and memory networks.

**However, a few points require strengthening:**

* **Ablation Studies:** While the paper compares models with and without direct reference, further ablation studies would be beneficial.  For example, investigating the impact of removing specific graph transformations, or varying the number of propagation steps, would help to understand the contribution of each component.
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:**  The paper lacks a discussion of hyperparameter sensitivity.  How do the performance and stability of the model change with different learning rates, hidden state dimensions, or regularization strengths?
* **Computational Cost:** The authors acknowledge the quadratic scaling of the model with graph size. A more detailed discussion of the computational resources required for training and inference, along with potential strategies for mitigation (e.g., sparse connections), would be valuable.



**4. Additional Feedback (for Improvement)**

* **Clarify “direct reference”:** The explanation of direct reference is a bit terse.  Could you provide a more detailed example of how this works in practice, perhaps with a snippet of a bAbI sentence and the corresponding attention weights?
* **Figure 1 & 2:** These figures are a bit abstract and could benefit from concrete examples.  Illustrating the graph transformations with simple, visual examples would greatly enhance understanding.
* **Appendix B:** The detailed descriptions of the graph transformations in the appendix are very valuable. However, consider including pseudocode snippets to further clarify the implementation.
* **Table 1 & 2 Formatting:**  Table 2 could be improved by clearly highlighting the best results for each task.
* **Code Availability:** While the authors mention code availability, ensuring a publicly accessible repository with clear instructions for replication would significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of the work.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

* **Scaling to Larger Graphs:**  Given the quadratic scaling, what are the practical limits of the GGT-NN's applicability? Have you explored any techniques to address this, such as graph partitioning or sparse connectivity?
* **Generalization Beyond bAbI:** Do you have any preliminary results on other datasets or tasks that require reasoning about relationships and states?  This would help to assess the broader applicability of the GGT-NN.
* **Choice of Graph Representation:**  How sensitive is the performance of the GGT-NN to the initial graph representation (e.g., the choice of node types, edge types, and initial connections)?  Have you explored different representation strategies?
* **Comparison to Symbolic Approaches:** The Turing machine experiments are compelling. How does the GGT-NN's performance compare to traditional symbolic approaches for implementing Turing machines (e.g., using a dedicated interpreter)?  What are the trade-offs between the two approaches?



Overall, this is a strong paper with a promising approach to combining graphical representations with sequential reasoning. Addressing the minor revisions outlined above would further strengthen the paper and increase its impact.
