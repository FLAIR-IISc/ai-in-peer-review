This paper proposes the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs and GGS-NNs, capable of manipulating graph-structured intermediate representations and producing graph-structured outputs. The authors demonstrate the model’s ability to encode states as graphs for question answering (bAbI tasks) and to learn update rules for simulating a 1D cellular automaton and Turing machines. The core contribution lies in the differentiable graph transformations allowing the network to learn to construct and modify graphs based on input.

**Reject.** While the idea of learning to manipulate graph structures is potentially interesting, the paper presents a substantial amount of novelty without sufficient empirical validation against established baselines, especially considering the complexity introduced. The results on bAbI, while competitive, do not convincingly demonstrate a significant advantage over existing strongly supervised methods and the reported gains on specific tasks seem marginal. The demonstrations on the cellular automaton and Turing machines, though intriguing, lack thorough comparison with algorithms specifically designed for those tasks.

The paper suffers from several key weaknesses. First, the extensive modifications to GG-NNs and the introduction of numerous transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) add significant complexity. This complexity isn’t justified by a commensurate improvement in performance over existing methods, particularly given the strong supervision provided during training. The extensive ablation study in Section 5.1.1, while attempting to justify design choices, doesn't definitively demonstrate the necessity of each component. Second, the experiments heavily rely on strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep). While acknowledged, this limits the generalizability of the approach. The claim of learning complex update rules for the Turing machine seems overstated without a comparison to a standard Turing machine implementation. Third, the paper’s presentation is dense and lacks clarity in certain sections, particularly regarding the intricacies of the graph transformations. The provided figures are helpful, but the accompanying explanations could be more concise and intuitive. Finally, the discussion of related work is somewhat superficial; a deeper engagement with relevant literature on both graph neural networks and reinforcement learning (given the task of learning update rules) is needed.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Baselines:** The inclusion of more standard, less-supervised baselines on the bAbI tasks is crucial.  Comparisons to models *without* strong graph supervision would better demonstrate the value of the proposed approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** More rigorous ablation studies are needed to assess the contribution of each graph transformation and the impact of direct reference.  How does performance degrade when specific transformations are removed?
*   **Turing Machine Evaluation:** Compare the learned Turing machine with a manually coded equivalent. What is the efficiency (steps to solution) and accuracy compared to a traditional implementation?  What is the generalization capability to new rules?
*   **Complexity Analysis:** Provide a more thorough discussion of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN, especially concerning the quadratic scaling with graph size. Are there strategies to mitigate this?
*   **Visualization:** Provide more insightful visualizations of the learned graph structures, especially for the Turing machine task. What types of representations does the network learn?
*   **Clarity:** Rewrite sections 2.3, 3, and 4 with a focus on clarity and conciseness.  Define all variables and parameters clearly.
*   **Address the choice of GRU:** Why GRU instead of LSTM or other recurrent network architectures? Was this empirically tested?



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The strong supervision used in training seems crucial for the observed performance. How would the model perform with reduced or no graph supervision?  Have you investigated techniques for learning graphs from unstructured input alone?
2.  What is the computational cost of the GGT-NN compared to standard GG-NNs or GGS-NNs, particularly as the graph size increases? Are there any optimizations planned to address the quadratic scaling?
3.  In the Turing machine experiment, how does the efficiency (number of steps) of the learned machine compare to a standard, hand-coded implementation of the same rules?
4.  Can you provide more examples of the learned graph structures, particularly for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks? What patterns or features emerge in these learned representations?
5.  The transformations feel very hand-engineered. What is the sensitivity of the performance to the choice of the specific network architectures (e.g., number of layers, activation functions) used within each transformation?
