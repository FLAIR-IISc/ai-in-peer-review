Summary and contributions
- This paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular architecture that learns to build and transform an explicit, differentiable graph-structured state from sequential input (e.g., text), and to use that state to answer queries or to output a graph. 
- The paper introduces a differentiable graph representation with soft node types, node strengths, and soft edge-type connectivity, along with five composable transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional direct-reference variant), edge update, propagation (message passing), and aggregation. A permutation-invariant training objective for node addition leverages the “set as sequence with optimal matching” trick.
- Experiments: (1) bAbI tasks, where strong supervision is provided in the form of ground-truth graphs derived from the simulator’s “Knowledge” object; the model achieves high accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference and shows strong performance on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16). (2) Rule discovery tasks: learning state transitions for Rule 30 cellular automaton and for 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines, including some generalization to longer rollouts.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision (full ground-truth graphs at every step and direct string-matched entity references) limits the scientific contribution and makes comparisons to end-to-end baselines unclear/unfair. 
- Empirical evaluation lacks rigor: key ablations, scalability analysis, and baseline comparisons on the rule-discovery tasks are missing; bAbI preprocessing involves non-trivial modifications that confound comparability.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The paper is well motivated and positioned as an extension of GG-NN/GGS-NN to dynamic graph construction. The modular transformations are clearly described and conceptually useful. The idea of an explicit, interpretable graph state is attractive when contrasted with implicit memories (MemN2N/DNC).
- Methodological soundness: The transformation definitions are reasonable, differentiable, and compatible with message passing. The permutation-invariant node addition loss is appropriate. However:
  - The approach’s success hinges on strong supervision: the model is trained with gold graph supervision at each timestep and a direct-reference mechanism keyed by exact entity names. The authors state that without this supervision “the model appeared to require additional supervision,” but do not quantify how performance degrades when graph supervision is reduced or absent. This makes it difficult to assess whether the method truly learns to induce structure from text versus learning to imitate a provided structure.
  - The bAbI conversion and task modifications (e.g., adding linked-list history nodes; changing multi-word entities to single words) depart from standard setups and could either simplify or alter the underlying tasks. The paper should report results on the canonical bAbI textual form without graph-level supervision to establish end-to-end capability, or else clearly delimit the scope as a strongly supervised semantic parsing-plus-reasoning system.
  - Fairness of comparisons: Many listed baselines are trained end-to-end without extra supervision; MemNN is noted as strongly supervised (relevant sentences), but GGT-NN supervision is much stronger (exact graphs and direct references). Reporting higher accuracy on selected tasks under these conditions is not a compelling demonstration of superiority.
- Empirical rigor:
  - Missing ablations: No systematic study of the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, Th vs Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr), the effect of intermediate propagation, or the necessity of the direct-reference channel (beyond a coarse “w/ vs w/o direct reference” that still uses strong graph supervision). 
  - Scalability: The paper acknowledges quadratic time/space, but provides no empirical runtime/memory profiling, nor experiments on larger graphs to demonstrate practical limits. 
  - Robustness: No variance across runs or confidence intervals; no sensitivity to hyperparameters or architecture sizes; no error analysis on failed tasks (notably Task 17).
  - Rule-discovery tasks: Results are encouraging but lack baselines (e.g., GGS-NN or standard GNNs operating on the current graph to predict the next graph), making it hard to attribute gains to the proposed transformations. Also absent are analyses of failure modes during long rollouts and the learned local update rules.
- Clarity and reproducibility: The paper is generally clear and provides technical details (appendices) and a code link. However, key implementation choices need more specificity (e.g., enforcing annotation simplex constraint; precise activations for fset/freset; dimensionalities; optimization hyperparameters; early stopping). The supervision pipeline (deriving and modifying bAbI graphs) should be fully documented to enable reproduction.

Constructive feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Calibrate supervision: Provide experiments with reduced or no graph-level supervision (e.g., weaker node/edge sparsity regularizers, partial supervision on a small subset, or consistency losses), and quantify the performance gap. This is crucial if the goal is to learn from unstructured text.
- Fair comparisons: On bAbI, include an end-to-end setting (no graph supervision, no direct string-matching) and compare to current end-to-end baselines; or, alternatively, compare to baselines that receive an equivalent level of supervision (e.g., strong supervision of a symbolic intermediate).
- Ablations: Systematically ablate each transformation and architectural option; report the impact of direct reference, intermediate propagation, the set loss for node addition, and the edge update module.
- Scalability: Provide complexity analysis with empirical runtime/memory vs number of nodes/edges; explore sparse connectivity or neighborhood sampling; show performance on larger graphs (e.g., larger pathfinding maps).
- Dataset fidelity: Report results on unmodified bAbI where possible and clearly separate results with modified graph augmentations; justify modifications and analyze their impact.
- Rule tasks baselines: Compare to GGS-NN/GNN predictors and simple local cellular-automaton predictors, and report generalization to much longer horizons with error compounding analysis.
- Analysis: Include qualitative visualizations of learned graphs and update patterns; conduct error analysis on Task 17 and other failures.

Questions for the authors
- How does GGT-NN perform without graph-level supervision and without direct-reference signals (i.e., end-to-end from raw text)? Can you quantify how much each supervision component contributes?
- What exact mechanisms enforce xv to lie on the simplex and edge strengths in [0,1]? Are these losses or hard projections (softmax/sigmoid)?
- What are the dimensionalities (D, N, Y), number of propagation steps, optimizer and learning rates, and training schedules? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- How large are the graphs during bAbI training/inference and what are the wall-clock training times and peak memory usage?
- For the rule-discovery tasks, how does GGT-NN compare to a GNN that predicts next-step edges/labels without node addition? How does performance degrade over 50–100-step rollouts?
- Can you release the exact bAbI-to-graph conversion code and document the task modifications so others can reproduce the pipeline?

Overall, the paper proposes an interesting and potentially useful modular framework for learning and manipulating graph-structured states, but the reliance on strong supervision and the limited empirical rigor currently prevent a confident acceptance.
