1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a framework that extends GG-NNs/GGS-NNs with a differentiable graph representation and a set of trainable graph transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional direct-reference variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation. A specific configuration is instantiated to build and update a graph from a sentence stream and answer queries. The model is evaluated on bAbI (with strong supervision using the full knowledge graph at each step), and on rule-discovery tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton; 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines). The paper claims the approach: (i) can learn to build/manipulate graph-structured state from text, (ii) can outperform some baselines on several bAbI tasks (notably pathfinding), and (iii) can learn complex state transitions.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision and teacher-forcing with ground-truth graph replacement undermines the central claim of “learning to construct and use” a graph from text; end-to-end performance without graph supervision is not convincingly demonstrated.
- Limited novelty over GG-NN/GGS-NN; the proposed “transformer” is not a Transformer in the modern sense, and the methodological deltas (continuous edge strengths, add/update ops) are incremental and insufficiently justified.
- Experimental methodology and presentation have multiple deficiencies (unfair comparisons, task modifications, missing training details/ablations) and numerous technical/formatting errors.

3) Supporting arguments
- Strong supervision/teacher forcing: Section 4.1 explicitly replaces new nodes and edges with ground-truth after computing loss. This effectively gives the model the correct intermediate graph at every step, making downstream query answering and further updates far easier. The paper acknowledges the model “appeared to require additional supervision” but does not provide compelling results without it, nor a careful analysis of failure modes. As a result, the main claim (learning to construct graphs from text) is not substantiated in an end-to-end setting.
- Direct reference and dataset modifications: Many bAbI results rely on “direct reference” that requires exact matching between words and node-type names. Appendix C indicates entity names were altered to single words in some tasks. This injects privileged signals beyond what typical systems have and makes comparisons with end-to-end baselines questionable. No results on the unmodified original text, nor ablations removing direct reference, beyond a brief table-level statement, are thoroughly analyzed.
- Novelty/positioning: The core propagation is GG-NN; the proposed “transformations” are largely parametrized ways to (i) add nodes; (ii) update edges; (iii) aggregate. Similar ideas (differentiable edges/adjacency, edge-type-specific message functions, attention-based readouts) are common within the GGNN/GNN family. The paper does not convincingly argue what is theoretically new or why these choices are essential vs. assembling known components. The term “Transformer” is confusing; there is no self-attention/Transformer block as typically understood.
- Comparisons and fairness: The bAbI comparison pits strongly supervised GGT-NN vs mostly weakly supervised baselines, and the paper admits this. Nonetheless, the paper repeatedly highlights superior performance on some tasks. A fair comparison would use matched supervision across baselines or emphasize end-to-end results without graph labels. Moreover, modifications to the bAbI graph schemas (e.g., adding history nodes; rewording entities) shift the task definition.
- Experimental rigor and missing details: Crucial hyperparameters are missing (hidden sizes, number of propagation steps, optimizer, learning rates, regularization, batch sizes, early stopping, seeds, compute budget). No statistical variation or multiple runs are reported. For the rule-discovery tasks, there are no baselines or ablations, and “generalization” is measured only by running more steps on the same rules, without evaluating robustness to longer inputs or noisier initializations. The Turing machine claim uses 2-symbol, 4-state machines; calling this “arbitrary” is misleading.
- Algorithmic clarity and notation errors: Algorithm 1 contains naming inconsistencies (e.g., Tqueryh vs Th), and unclear variable uses. Several equations have typographical issues (e.g., GRU update missing x(t) in h̃(t); elementwise product symbol referenced but not shown). Appendix B edge toggling rule (both set and reset at 1 “toggles”) is unusual; no justification or stability discussion is provided. Complexity discussion admits O(|V|^2) scaling but provides no empirical profiling or sparsity tricks.
- Writing and references: Numerous formatting errors, broken references, and missing years (e.g., “Andrychowicz and Kurach.,? Q2016 E”), inconsistent citation style, and missing figure/table references in the main text reduce readability and professionalism.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- End-to-end training: Provide results without graph-level supervision or with weak/partial supervision (e.g., a few annotated graphs + many unannotated) to support the central claim. Analyze failure cases and what supervision is minimally necessary.
- Fair baselines: Compare against GG-NN/GGS-NN variants augmented with similar edge/node addition modules and trained with the same supervision. Consider memory-based models given identical privileged signals (sentence supervision) for a fair assessment.
- Ablations: Systematically ablate each transformation (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr), number of propagation steps, and the teacher-forcing substitution. Show sensitivity to node/edge-type counts and removal of direct reference.
- Clarity: Fix algorithm pseudocode, notation, and GRU equations; define all dimensions; state training details comprehensively; report compute/memory use; include variance across seeds.
- Naming: Consider renaming GGT-NN to avoid confusion with Transformer architectures.
- Task design: Report performance on unmodified bAbI and clarify all preprocessing changes. For Turing machines, clearly state the class considered and avoid “arbitrary” unless truly supported.
- Code/data: Ensure the code link is functional, with scripts to reproduce all experiments and preprocessing.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact performance of the model when you do not replace nodes/edges with ground truth at training time? Can you report training curves and final accuracies in this purely end-to-end setting?
- How is D(k) computed without oracle entity-type names? Have you tried learned mention detection or string matching with noise/OOV entities?
- Please provide full hyperparameters, training schedules, number of propagation steps per layer, and seeds for all experiments.
- For bAbI, which tasks were modified (list all), and what are the exact textual changes? What are the results on the original tasks without renaming?
- How many node/edge types are used per task, and how is this chosen? What happens when the number of entities grows (e.g., task 17) and can you scale via sparse adjacency?
- In TC, why allow “toggle” behavior when both set/reset≈1? Did this cause instability? Any constraints or regularization applied?
- Can you show runtime/memory scaling with |V| and edge density, and any sparse or batched implementations?
- For the rule tasks, can you report accuracy vs. number of steps, noise robustness, and comparisons to simple programmatic baselines or learned simulators?
