Summary
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that can construct and transform an explicit, differentiable graph state from sequential input. The model includes five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) and a recurrent textual encoder, enabling it to build a graph incrementally and to answer questions via graph-level representations. Experiments include: (i) bAbI QA tasks, where the generator’s “knowledge object” is exposed in graph form and used for strong supervision of node/edge states at each step (plus an oracle “direct reference” mechanism), and (ii) two synthetic rule-discovery tasks (Rule 30 cellular automata and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines). The paper claims strong bAbI accuracy (19/20 tasks at ≥95% with direct reference) and the ability to learn graph transition rules and generalize to longer rollouts in the synthetic tasks.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision (full ground-truth graph states and oracle direct references) undermines the central claim that the model can learn to induce and manipulate structure from text; comparisons to prior work are not apples-to-apples. (2) Incremental novelty and insufficient empirical rigor: missing ablations, key baselines (e.g., GG-NN/GGS-NN on the same supervised graphs), runtime/memory analyses, and statistical robustness. The paper largely demonstrates that with near-oracle supervision, a GGNN-style model can do well on bAbI, which is unsurprising.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement. The proposed transformations are reasonable extensions of GG-NN/GGS-NN with soft node/edge strengths and learned edge addition/removal. However, the conceptual novelty is incremental: the components (GRU-style node updates, attention-based aggregation, edge functions) are standard, and the architecture is principally an engineered composition. Related works with differentiable external memory (MemNN, DNC/NTM) and graph models (GG-NN/GGS-NN) are cited, but no clear theoretical or empirical delineation of when/why GGT-NN is superior under comparable supervision is provided.

- Methodological concerns.
  • The training pipeline injects very strong supervision: at each step, node/edge losses are computed against ground-truth graphs, and the correct graph is then substituted to continue processing. This “teacher-forcing” of structure means the model is not truly evaluated on learning transitions end-to-end. There is no report of graph induction accuracy at test time or performance without intermediate graph substitution.
  • “Direct reference” is implemented via exact string matches of entity names to node types—an oracle NER/linking mechanism. This greatly simplifies bAbI and is unrealistic for natural text. The large gains with direct reference conflate the benefits of the model with those of the oracle signal.
  • The node-set loss maximization over permutations (Vinyals et al.) is mentioned, but the implementation is unspecified (exact/Hungarian vs heuristic) and the computational cost is unclear. This is important for scalability and reproducibility.
  • No complexity analysis beyond a qualitative statement. Given edge updates over all pairs and dense propagation, the method scales as O(|V|^2), yet no runtime/memory or limits on graph size are reported.

- Empirical evaluation.
  • bAbI comparisons are unfair: GGT-NN and MemNN use strong supervision while most baselines are end-to-end. The paper acknowledges this but still draws conclusions about superiority on certain tasks. A fair comparison would include: (a) GG-NN/GGS-NN with the same ground-truth graphs (no text), (b) text-to-graph parser + GG-NN, and (c) GGT-NN with weaker supervision (answer-only) and without oracle direct reference.
  • The pathfinding gains are expected given that the model is given graph structure and the task is inherently graphical. It’s unclear how much comes from the architecture versus the supervision.
  • Synthetic tasks (Rule 30, Turing machines) are interesting but highly controlled. Reported generalization to longer rollouts is promising, but details are sparse: exact metrics, error trajectories per step, sensitivity to rule diversity, and robustness to noise are not provided. Without such details, it is unclear whether the model truly learned transition rules versus overfitting to short horizons.
  • Missing ablations: effect of each transformation (Tadd/TC/Tprop/Trepr), with/without intermediate propagation, with/without graph substitution, with noisy or missing direct references, and hidden size sensitivity. No variance across runs.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide experiments with weaker supervision: (i) answer-only training, (ii) partial graph supervision, and (iii) no direct-reference oracle (replace with a learned NER/linker or noisy supervision). Report node/edge F1 at test time and end-to-end QA accuracy to substantiate the claim of learning graphical state transitions from text.
- Include fair baselines: (a) GGS-NN operating on the same supervised graphs, (b) GG-NN with static graphs (no additions) to isolate the benefit of dynamic transformations, and (c) a text-to-graph pipeline feeding a standard GG-NN.
- Report computational complexity with wall-clock time, memory usage, and max graph sizes handled. Discuss sparsity tricks or neighborhood sampling to curb O(|V|^2) costs.
- Clarify implementation details: dimensions, number of propagation steps, max nodes per Tadd, how the permutation matching is solved, whether scheduled sampling or curriculum was used, and training stability measures.
- Improve result reporting: include full tables with standard deviations over multiple seeds, per-task curves vs number of examples, and qualitative visualizations of learned graphs and transitions.
- Evaluate robustness: lexical variation, coreference, and paraphrases that break exact string matching; noise in input; and out-of-distribution entity counts (e.g., bAbI task 17 stress tests).

Questions for the authors
1) At test time on bAbI, do you still substitute ground-truth graphs between sentences, or is the model fully autonomous? Please report test-time node/edge accuracy and its correlation with answer accuracy.
2) How is the permutation-invariant node loss maximization implemented, and what is its computational cost per step?
3) Can you show results without the direct-reference oracle, or with a learned/noisy referencer? How much does QA accuracy drop?
4) How does GGT-NN compare to a GG-NN/GGS-NN fed with the same supervised graphs (i.e., no text), isolating the value of your transformation stack?
5) What are the typical graph sizes, runtimes, and memory footprints across tasks? How does performance degrade with larger graphs?
6) For the synthetic tasks, can you provide per-step error over long rollouts, and robustness to perturbations (e.g., flipping a cell, changing a rule mid-run)?
