Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that can build and manipulate a differentiable, labeled graph state while processing sequences. The core technical contribution is a composable set of differentiable graph transformations: node addition (with soft existence and type), node state updates (including a “direct reference” variant), edge updates via pairwise MLPs, propagation (message passing) extended to soft edge/node strengths, and graph-level aggregation with attention. These operations are composed to incrementally construct and update a graph from text via a GRU encoder, and to answer queries by further graph processing. Training uses strong supervision on intermediate graphs, including a permutation-invariant loss for unordered node additions. The method is demonstrated on bAbI (text to graph + QA), a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines (graph-to-graph next-step prediction), with some generalization to longer rollouts. The conceptual promise is an explicit, interpretable, graph-structured internal state that can be both constructed from unstructured input and used for reasoning.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision (gold intermediate graphs and direct referencing by exact string match) undermines the central claim of learning to induce structured state from unstructured text and limits applicability beyond toy settings.
- Limited novelty and insufficient empirical evidence of broader impact: the architecture largely extends GG-NN/GGS-NN with straightforward differentiable node/edge creation and relies on strong supervision to work; experiments do not convincingly show effectiveness without such supervision or scalability to larger graphs.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and relation to literature: The paper is well-positioned relative to GG-NNs/GGS-NNs and memory-based architectures (MemNN, DNC). The idea of dynamic, differentiable graph construction is timely and valuable. However, the proposed “transformations” are mostly standard neural building blocks (GRU updates, MLP-based pairwise edge predictors, attention-based aggregation) stitched together; the main novelty is the particular composition and a training protocol with strong supervision and set-to-sequence alignment for unordered nodes. The choice of “Transformer” in the name is potentially confusing given the unrelated Transformer literature; here it denotes graph transformations, not self-attention blocks.

- Empirical rigor: 
  - bAbI results depend critically on strong supervision and direct reference. With direct references, the model reaches 95% on 19/20 tasks; without, only 9/20. This suggests the method has not learned to discover entity references or structure from text end-to-end. Comparisons to MemNN and others are complicated: GGT-NN and MemNN both use supervision (graphs vs. supporting facts), whereas other baselines are end-to-end; the paper notes this, but then compares accuracies without a controlled supervision regime. The model also modifies the bAbI graph format and some entity names (single-token), which affects comparability. There are no reported variances across runs, no ablations of the five transformations (beyond toggling direct reference/propagation), and no analysis of graph accuracy on bAbI (only task accuracy), despite training with graph-level supervision.
  - Rule 30 and Turing machines: The mapping to graphs is well specified and the tasks are suitable for graph reasoning. The models achieve high exact-match accuracy and show some length generalization. However, the TM setting is narrow (2 symbols, 4 states, 6-step training) and the distribution of machines is not characterized; it is unclear how difficult the sampled machines are, or whether the model generalizes to out-of-distribution rules. Evaluation still uses full structural supervision during training and exact graph match at test; no robustness or noise studies, and no baselines (e.g., GG-NN on fixed known graphs, or explicit algorithmic baselines).

- Claims vs. evidence: The central claim—learning to construct and exploit a graph-structured state from unstructured input—is only partially supported. The requirement of gold graphs and exact lexical matching at training time is strong; the method struggles without direct reference. There is no demonstration that the approach can learn the structure with weak or no graph supervision, which would be key for real-world text. The admitted quadratic time/space complexity is a practical concern; no runtime or scaling experiments are provided.

- Clarity and reproducibility: The transformation definitions are clear, and implementation details are relegated to an appendix (good). However, important training details are missing (hyperparameters, optimizer, number of propagation steps, regularization), and the extent of dataset modifications is only briefly described. The paper claims code availability, which helps, but more quantitative detail is needed.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not necessarily part of the decision)
- Evaluate under weaker supervision: e.g., (i) supporting facts only, (ii) partial graph supervision, or (iii) no graph supervision with consistency/regularization losses. Show whether GGT-NN can induce structure and still answer correctly.
- Provide graph-level metrics on bAbI (precision/recall of edges/nodes) alongside QA accuracy to validate that the internal graphs are correct and useful.
- Add ablations: remove node addition, edge update, direct reference, and measure the contribution of each; vary propagation depth.
- Report training/inference time, memory footprint, and scaling with |V| and |E|; explore sparse edge updates and attention over candidate neighborhoods.
- Strengthen baselines: compare to text-to-graph parsers or neural semantic parsers where appropriate; for CA/TM, include a GG-NN with fixed graph, and simple algorithmic baselines.
- Clarify fairness in bAbI comparisons and provide 10k-example results for GGT-NN for parity with published baselines (even if strongly supervised).
- Improve clarity on permutation matching for node supervision (algorithm used, complexity) and how maximum new nodes per step are set.
- Consider replacing “direct reference” with a learned pointer/mention mechanism to remove reliance on exact string matching.

Questions for the authors
1) At test time on bAbI, do you still perform any teacher forcing on graphs, or is graph construction fully predicted? What is the graph reconstruction accuracy at test time?
2) How is the permutation-invariant node matching implemented (Hungarian algorithm?) and what is its computational cost during training?
3) What are the typical |V|, |E|, and number of edge types Y per task? What are training/inference runtimes and memory usage? How does performance degrade with larger graphs?
4) Can you report results with partial or no graph supervision to assess whether GGT-NN can induce structure end-to-end?
5) How sensitive is performance to noisy or ambiguous direct references (e.g., synonyms, multi-word entities)? Can a learned mention detector replace the hard lexicon?
6) For the Turing machine experiments, what is the distribution over rules? Do you generalize to unseen rule sets? How do results change with more states/symbols or longer rollouts?
7) Could you provide standard deviations over multiple seeds and more ablations of the transformation components?

Overall, the paper introduces a neat, modular toolkit for differentiable graph construction and shows promising results on synthetic tasks, but falls short on demonstrating end-to-end structure induction, empirical robustness, and scalability necessary for ICLR acceptance.
