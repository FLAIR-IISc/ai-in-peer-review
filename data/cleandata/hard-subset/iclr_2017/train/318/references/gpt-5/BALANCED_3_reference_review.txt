1) Summary of contributions
The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a framework that makes graph structure itself a differentiable, learnable state. It defines five differentiable graph transformations—node addition, node state update (with an optional “direct reference” pathway), edge update, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation—and composes them to build and update a graph from a sequence of sentences and then answer a query. The model extends GG-NN/GGS-NN by allowing dynamic graph construction and modification (including adding nodes/edges) rather than operating on a fixed graph. The authors evaluate on bAbI (with strong supervision over intermediate graphs), and on two rule-discovery tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 4-state 2-symbol Turing machines), showing that the architecture can learn to transform its internal graph state and generalize to longer rollouts. The paper is clearly written, includes nontrivial engineering (ordering-invariant node addition loss), and provides code.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision (gold intermediate graphs and direct entity references) on synthetic datasets limits the strength and generality of the empirical claims; key results are not end-to-end and are not comparable to most baselines.
- Novelty and positioning relative to existing graph neural network and memory architectures are not sufficiently established through rigorous ablations or modern comparisons; scalability and practical applicability remain unclear.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The goal—learning and manipulating a graphical internal state from language—is compelling and timely. The proposed set of differentiable graph transformations is a clean, modular abstraction that generalizes GG-NN/GGS-NN toward dynamic graphs. This is a clear conceptual contribution.
- Placement in literature: The paper discusses GNNs, GGS-NNs, memory/DNC/HAM models. However, the empirical comparisons are primarily on bAbI and do not include more recent or stronger baselines that also support dynamic or relational reasoning (e.g., modern transformer baselines, relational inductive biases, neural graph generation). The case that GGT-NN is superior in practice remains weak beyond pathfinding and induction tasks.
- Evidence supporting claims:
  - bAbI: The model achieves high accuracy on many tasks, particularly with “direct reference” and when trained with full graph supervision per timestep. While this demonstrates the machinery can be trained, it weakens the key claim of learning to construct useful graphs from unstructured text: direct reference provides entity linking and the knowledge object provides the ground-truth structure. The comparison to MemNN is not apples-to-apples across supervision regimes.
  - Rule discovery tasks: The model learns to simulate Rule 30 and Turing machines from graph inputs and generalizes to longer sequences. This is interesting, but the setups encode the rules explicitly as edges (for TMs) and/or use small, synthetic graphs. Stronger evidence (e.g., broader families, robustness to noise, or harder dynamics) would help.
  - Missing evaluations: There is no direct evaluation of graph reconstruction quality at test time on bAbI (precision/recall of nodes/edges), no ablation on the necessity of node addition vs. only edge updates, and limited analysis of the cost/benefit of intermediate propagation.
- Practicality and scalability: The method scales quadratically in nodes due to dense pairwise edge updates/propagation. There is no analysis of wall-clock performance, memory profile, or strategies for sparsity. It is unclear whether the approach scales beyond very small graphs (as in bAbI).
- Clarity/reproducibility: The method is fairly well specified, and the appendices help. The supervision scheme (teacher-forcing of structure, ordering-invariant node addition) is a useful engineering contribution. That said, key hyperparameters, training stability, and details on decoding (“most likely graph”) could be more precise.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Evaluate end-to-end: Remove graph supervision and direct reference (or reduce them substantially) and show how well the model learns structure from text. If some supervision is needed, try semi-supervised or weakly supervised regimes and regularizers (e.g., sparsity, consistency losses).
- Measure graph quality: Report edge/node F1, structural accuracy, and alignment quality at test time, not just QA accuracy. Provide qualitative visualizations of predicted graphs and error analysis.
- Stronger baselines: Include modern transformer baselines and graph-reasoning models that do not rely on strong supervision. For the generative graph side, relate to neural graph generation methods and Neural Relational Inference.
- Ablations: Systematically disable Tadd, TC, Th,direct, intermediate propagation, and vary the maximum nodes added per step. Show how each component contributes. Compare to a fixed-graph GGNN where entities are pre-instantiated.
- Efficiency: Introduce and evaluate sparse edge updates/attention or k-NN neighborhoods to make complexity sub-quadratic. Report memory/time per step and sensitivity to graph size.
- Generalization: In rule tasks, test on significantly larger horizons, altered rules (out-of-distribution), and noisy inputs. For TMs, ensure rules are not trivially linearly separable by local cues and report breakdowns by rule families.
- Linguistic realism: Beyond bAbI, test on a lightweight real-world IE task (e.g., small knowledge graph construction or AMR-style parsing) to demonstrate potential practical impact.
- Clarify design choices: Discuss how N (number of node types) is set; how multi-word entities are handled without pre-processing; how many nodes can be added per sentence; and how sensitive performance is to these choices.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision: At test time on bAbI, do you rely on any direct reference or graph supervision, or is the graph fully predicted from text? Can you report test-time graph reconstruction metrics?
- Direct reference: How is D(k) constructed when entities have multi-word names or coreference? What happens if entity names are unseen or paraphrased?
- Node types and capacity: How is the number of node types N chosen for bAbI, cellular automata, and TMs? Can the model handle a variable or unbounded set of entity types across stories?
- Scalability: What are the typical numbers of nodes/edges during bAbI runs? What are training/inference times and memory usage? Have you tried sparse connectivity or attention masks?
- Ablations: What is the effect of removing Tadd (pre-allocating nodes), of using edge-only updates, or of disabling teacher forcing of the gold graph during training?
- Generalization: For Turing machines, do you evaluate on unseen rule tables and longer tapes/horizons than trained? Please provide quantitative breakdowns for 20/30 steps cited in Table 3.
- Robustness: How sensitive are results to tokenization, to noise in entity mentions, or to missing/extra sentences?
- Code and reproducibility: Is the provided code sufficient to reproduce all tables? Please detail hyperparameters (hidden sizes, T, learning rates), early stopping, and random seeds.
