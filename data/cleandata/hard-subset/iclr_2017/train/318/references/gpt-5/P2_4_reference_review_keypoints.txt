- Proposal: Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) with five composable graph transformations and soft/differentiable node strengths and edge beliefs.  
- Demonstrations: evaluated on bAbI QA (with per-step graph supervision), Rule 30 cellular automaton, and small Turing machines, showing interpretability and some rollout generalization.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main rejection reason 1: Heavy reliance on strong, per-timestep graph supervision and dataset modifications (direct reference, history nodes, entity collapsing) undermines empirical significance and fairness of comparisons.  
- Main rejection reason 2: Positioning and novelty are weak relative to post-2016 dynamic graph/graph-generation literature; the “Transformer” name is potentially misleading.  
- Related work gap: Omits or underplays Graph Networks, dynamic/temporal GNNs, GraphRNN/JT-VAE, neural state machines, and other modern graph-generation work.  
- Empirical fairness: Comparisons to baselines (e.g., MemNN) are not apples-to-apples due to different supervision/data preprocessing and training regimes.  
- Ablations and rigor: Missing key experimental details (model sizes, optimization, node/edge counts, runtime/memory, stability) and no ablations isolating each transformation or soft vs hard edges.  
- Claims vs experiments: Assertions about distributed computation/pathfinding lack controlled experiments varying graph size, sparsity, and propagation steps.  
- Benchmark scope: Evaluation limited to toy/synthetic tasks; lacks realistic text-to-graph, dynamic KG, or program induction benchmarks and stronger quantitative baselines.  
- Scalability: Method is O(|V|^2) and there is no empirical study of scalability or sparse-update variants.  
- Actionable improvements: Strengthen positioning vs modern dynamic GNN and graph-generation literature; clarify terminology (avoid confusing “Transformer”); provide fair baseline training and 10k-example comparisons.  
- Suggested experiments: Reduce supervision (end-to-end/answer-only), ablate direct reference and set-matching loss, quantify each transformation’s contribution, test sensitivity to propagation steps and sparsity, and visualize induced graphs without supervision.  
- Benchmark and reproducibility suggestions: Add realistic tasks (AMR/OpenIE/KG construction), report exact generalization rates and failure modes, and release modified bAbI annotations and preprocessing.  
- Reporting needs: Provide training details (hyperparameters, seeds, compute/memory footprint) and runtime scaling vs graph size.  
- Questions for authors: How well does the model do without per-step graph supervision; what are exact compute/memory scalings; sensitivity to direct reference and ambiguous/multiword mentions; comparisons to modern dynamic/generative baselines; justification for the term “Transformer”; and whether modified bAbI annotations/scripts will be released.
