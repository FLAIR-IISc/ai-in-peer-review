Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a differentiable framework for graph construction and editing: nodes have type distributions and existence strengths; edges have continuous strengths per type. On top of this, it defines a modular set of graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) that can be composed.
- It introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), which read sequences (e.g., text) and incrementally build/modify an explicit graph state, then use it to answer queries. A “direct reference” mechanism routes token-level information to node types.
- A supervision strategy is provided to train graph construction: permutation-invariant node addition loss (via max over assignments) and edge Bernoulli losses, with teacher-forced substitution of ground-truth nodes/edges during training.
- Empirically, the model: (i) solves most bAbI tasks with strong supervision of the graph; (ii) excels on pathfinding and induction; (iii) learns and generalizes transition rules for a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines; (iv) shows some generalization to longer rollouts.

Decision: Weak Accept
Key reasons:
- Novel and bold idea: a differentiable, trainable set of graph-edit operations coupled with a controller that constructs an explicit, interpretable graph from text. This is a meaningful extension beyond GG-NN/GGS-NN (which assume fixed graphs) and beyond memory/DNC-style models (which use implicit structures).
- Convincing demos across qualitatively different domains (QA, pathfinding, algorithmic simulation) suggest the approach opens a new direction for neural models that write structured states.

Supporting arguments
- Positioning and motivation: The gap between graph encoders (fixed structure) and models that can construct structured states is well articulated. The paper’s explicit internal graph neatly complements implicit memory models (MemN2N, DNC), offering interpretability and direct interoperability with graph-centric tasks.
- Technical novelty: 
  - Continuous node/edge existence and type distributions with differentiable edits is a clean formulation.
  - Modular transformations make the architecture reusable across tasks and configurable.
  - The permutation-invariant supervision for node addition is a practical solution for unordered set outputs.
- Empirical evidence:
  - On bAbI, the model reaches high accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference, and is particularly strong on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16), both naturally graph-reasoning heavy.
  - The rule-learning studies (Rule 30, Turing machines) demonstrate the system can learn nontrivial state-transition rules and can generalize to longer horizons in many cases, supporting the claim that the architecture can model “graphical state transitions.”
- Clarity: The paper provides detailed descriptions of transformations and training losses, plus appendices mapping tasks to graphs.

Caveats (not blocking given novelty)
- Strong supervision and direct reference: Most bAbI experiments require access to a ground-truth “knowledge graph object” at each step, with teacher-forced nodes/edges and direct reference derived from entity names. This reduces the end-to-end challenge of extracting structure from text, and comparisons to end-to-end models are not apples-to-apples.
- Scaling and efficiency: The approach is quadratic in the number of nodes per step, and the paper acknowledges this. There is little empirical analysis of time/memory or performance on larger graphs.
- Ablations and sensitivity: Limited ablations on components (e.g., impact of each transformation, direct reference, intermediate propagation), and limited reporting of training stability/hyperparameters.
- External validity: bAbI and synthetic automata/Turing tasks are toy. No experiments on real-world information extraction or larger graph domains.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily part of the decision)
- Provide a clearer ablation study: with/without each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop), with/without direct reference, and with weaker/partial supervision. Quantify how much each contributes to bAbI and to rule-learning.
- Fairer comparisons: For bAbI, separate tables for strong vs weak supervision; consider adding a GGT-NN variant trained without graph supervision to indicate end-to-end potential, even if accuracy drops.
- Scaling: Report wall-clock, memory usage, and complexity as a function of |V| and |E|. Explore sparse edge updates and neighborhood-limited propagation; consider learned sparsifiers or top-k edge selection.
- Generalization: Test Turing machines and Rule 30 for much longer horizons (e.g., >100 steps), and report failure modes. Discuss how errors compound when supervision does not overwrite graph states during training.
- Clarify implementation details: hidden sizes, number of propagation steps, edge function architectures, thresholds used at inference to discretize strengths, optimization settings, and code availability (ensure anonymization if required).
- Writing polish: fix citation formatting; improve figure readability; explicitly detail how T_query modules differ from their training counterparts.

Questions for the authors
1. Training supervision: You substitute ground-truth nodes/edges after computing losses. How much learning signal remains for learning to update from predicted graphs? Did you test training without this substitution or with scheduled sampling?
2. Direct reference: How essential is it? Can the model learn to resolve entities from text spans without explicit matching, perhaps via an attention/linking mechanism? Any experiments on noisy/missing direct references?
3. Inference discretization: At test time, how do you convert node/edge strengths into a discrete graph for evaluation or downstream use? What thresholds are used, and how sensitive are results to them?
4. Scalability: What are the largest graphs you trained/inferred on? Can you report runtime/memory and any batching strategies? Have you tried sparse or local edge updates?
5. Generalization: For the Turing machine setting, how far can the model roll out accurately beyond training horizons? Does performance degrade gracefully or catastrophically?
6. Robustness: How does the model handle inconsistent or partially incorrect supervision of the intermediate graphs? 
7. Query modules: T_query have separate parameters—what differences emerged empirically? Could a shared set suffice?
8. Comparison with GGS-NN: Can you quantify the benefit of learnable graph construction (Tadd/TC) over preprocessing inputs into graphs and running GGS-NN?

Overall, despite limitations in supervision and scaling analysis, the work introduces a compelling and original paradigm—differentiable graph state construction and transformation—that I believe merits presentation and will stimulate further research.
