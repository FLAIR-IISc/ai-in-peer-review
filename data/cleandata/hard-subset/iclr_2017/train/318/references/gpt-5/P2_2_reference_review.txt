1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that supports differentiable graph construction and modification during processing. The core idea is to represent nodes/edges with strengths (soft existence) and to compose a small set of differentiable graph transformations: node addition (Tadd), node state updates (Th, including a “direct reference” variant), edge updates (TC), propagation (Tprop), and aggregation (Trepr). A text encoder (GRU) drives these transformations to incrementally build an internal graph from sentences and then answer queries. The paper reports:
- Strongly supervised results on bAbI (graph supervision at every step, plus direct reference using entity-name matches), achieving ≥95% on 19/20 tasks with direct reference and good data efficiency on many tasks.
- “Rule discovery” experiments: learning next-step transitions of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and arbitrary 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines from graph sequences, with generalization to longer rollouts.

Overall, the paper aims to demonstrate that explicit, dynamic, graph-structured internal state can be learned and used for reasoning, bridging unstructured inputs and structured computation.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific heuristics (gold graphs at each step, direct lexical matches, dataset modifications) weakens the central claim that the model learns to build and use graphs from text.
- Incremental novelty over GG-NN/GGS-NN is not convincingly supported by a rigorous, fair empirical evaluation. Comparisons to baselines are not controlled for supervision, and ablations are limited.

3) Supporting arguments
- On motivation and placement: The paper is well-motivated—explicit dynamic graphs are attractive, and the transformation set is clear. It relates to GG-NN/GGS-NN, Memory Networks, DNCs, and LRNNs. However, the positioning omits several relevant lines of work on learning latent relational structure and dynamic/learned graphs (e.g., neural relational inference, modern graph generation models, graph networks for reasoning). The claimed advantages over memory-based models are partly offset by the very strong supervision used here.
- On technical novelty: The main novelty is making graph structure differentiable (soft node/edge existence) and composing trainable transformations, including edge rewiring. This is a reasonable extension of GG-NN/GGS-NN and useful engineering, but conceptually incremental. The “direct reference” mechanism is simple and task-specific (name matching) rather than a learned entity grounding mechanism.
- On empirical rigor:
  - bAbI: The model is trained with gold graphs at each step and direct reference keyed to gold entity names. The authors also modify the supervision graphs (e.g., add history linked lists) and change multiword entities to single tokens, which undermines comparability. The comparison table includes models trained without such supervision. While the paper notes this, the headline result (high accuracy on 19/20 tasks) leans on supervision and custom preprocessing that other baselines do not use. There’s little analysis of performance without graph supervision (only “with/without direct reference”; both still use gold graph supervision).
  - Rule discovery: These tasks are clean and the results are promising (generalization to longer rollouts), but they are toy domains with no baselines, and details on diversity of Turing machine rules, error modes, and scalability are limited.
  - Ablations: No systematic analysis of which transformations matter, how sensitive training is to supervision strength, or the effect of removing direct reference. There is no runtime/memory profiling despite quadratic scaling being highlighted as a limitation.
- On claims: The paper convincingly shows that, with strong supervision, the system can track a graph state and use it for simple QA and for simulating small graph-based dynamical systems. It does not convincingly show that GGT-NNs can learn to induce useful internal graphs from raw text without heavy supervision or task-specific cues. Hence, the broader claims about extracting and using graphs from unstructured input remain under-supported.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the work)
- Provide controlled comparisons where all methods have access to the same supervision (e.g., give baselines the same gold knowledge object or, conversely, remove graph supervision and evaluate fully end-to-end).
- Clarify and isolate the impact of each supervision component:
  - Gold intermediate graphs (node/edge targets).
  - Direct reference (lexical matching).
  - Graph-format modifications (e.g., adding history nodes; converting multiword entities).
  Report results with/without each, include ablations on the degree of supervision (e.g., partial supervision, semi-supervised).
- Include qualitative analysis: visualize learned graphs vs gold, show error cases, and analyze attention/propagation patterns for tasks like pathfinding and induction.
- Position against more recent literature on learning latent relational structure and dynamic graph reasoning/generation; discuss when explicit dynamic graphs are preferable to latent structures.
- Provide compute and scaling analysis: time/memory vs number of nodes/edges; any sparsity tricks; batch sizes used.
- Strengthen the rule-discovery section: describe the distribution over Turing machine rules, provide baselines (e.g., GG-NN with fixed topology, DNC), show generalization curves, and error breakdowns.
- Improve writing and references (formatting inconsistencies).

Questions for the authors
- How does GGT-NN perform on bAbI without any gold graph supervision (no node/edge targets) and without direct reference? Can you provide end-to-end results and analysis?
- To what extent do the dataset modifications (single-word entities, added history nodes) affect difficulty and comparability? Can you evaluate on the original unmodified tasks?
- How robust is the “direct reference” mechanism to noisy mentions, coreference, and paraphrases? Can it be replaced with a learned grounding module?
- Can you report ablations isolating the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Th, Tprop) and the impact of soft strengths vs hard structures?
- What is the computational footprint (time/memory) as graphs grow, and can sparsity or neighborhood sampling be used without degrading performance?
- For the Turing machine experiments, how are rules sampled, and how diverse are they? Do failure cases correlate with particular rule patterns or tape growth?
