Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN), a modular architecture that maintains an explicit, fully differentiable graph-structured state and learns to transform it over time. Core, novel components are differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update (with a “direct reference” mechanism), edge update, propagation (GGNN-style), and aggregation. These can be composed to incrementally construct and modify a latent graph from unstructured inputs (text) and then use that graph for downstream prediction.
- The model is trained with strong supervision on graph structure (including a permutation-invariant loss for new nodes), and with teacher-forcing-like substitution of gold nodes/edges during training. It is evaluated on bAbI QA (text-to-graph-to-answer), and on two rule discovery tasks (1D cellular automaton, 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines), showing that the architecture can learn nontrivial state transition rules and generalize to longer rollouts.
- Empirically, the approach attains high accuracy on most bAbI tasks (especially pathfinding and induction) and demonstrates strong sequence generalization on the automaton and TM tasks.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Conceptual novelty and ambition: an explicit, differentiable, dynamically editable graph state with learned node/edge operations is a bold and valuable direction that goes beyond fixed-structure GNNs and implicit memory architectures. (2) Promising empirical signals: wins on graph-suited tasks (e.g., pathfinding) and rule-learning/generalization experiments suggest the approach captures genuinely useful computation. I am willing to overlook the reliance on strong supervision and limited benchmarks given the novelty and potential impact.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and positioning: While GGNNs/GGSNNs and memory/DNC models exist, this work uniquely focuses on learned graph construction and transformation from unstructured input, with explicit node existence probabilities, typed nodes/edges, and end-to-end differentiability. The modular transformation library (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) is a clear, reusable abstraction.
- Motivation: Many problems are inherently graph-structured; the ability to induce a graph from text and then reason via distributed propagation is compelling and well argued. The direct-reference mechanism is a pragmatic bridge from tokens to entities.
- Empirical evidence: On bAbI, the model with direct reference achieves high accuracy on 19/20 tasks and clearly excels on pathfinding and induction—precisely where graphs should help. On the automaton and TM tasks, the model learns correct transition rules and generalizes to much longer horizons, indicating it learned algorithmic structure rather than memorizing outputs.
- Technical clarity: The paper provides detailed formulations for each transformation, training losses (including set-like supervision for unordered nodes), and ablations (with/without direct reference, and an alternative sequence-of-graphs variant).

Caveats (not decision-blocking given novelty)
- Strong supervision: Training with full gold graphs and mid-episode substitution of gold structure is a heavy crutch; most baselines are end-to-end. It raises exposure-bias questions and obscures how well graph induction works without graph labels.
- Benchmarks: bAbI is toy, and textual entity grounding relies on exact name matching. More realistic datasets (e.g., OpenIE-derived graphs, knowledge probing, or code/dataflow tasks) would better establish external validity.
- Efficiency/scaling: Edge update and propagation are O(|V|^2). There is no empirical analysis of runtime/memory or mitigation (sparsity, locality).
- Missing ablations: Limited analysis of which transformations are essential, and limited error analysis on hard tasks (e.g., task 17).

Feedback to improve the paper
- Reduce supervision: Show results with weaker supervision (e.g., partial graph labels, edge-only supervision, or consistency/regularization terms) and evaluate purely end-to-end text-to-graph-to-answer training. Clarify test-time: is the graph entirely predicted without gold substitution?
- Efficiency: Provide complexity measurements and practical tricks (sparse C, top-k neighbor selection, local edge updates, or sampled message passing). Even a simple sparsification ablation would be informative.
- Broaden datasets: Add at least one non-synthetic benchmark where graphs are beneficial (e.g., multi-hop QA over knowledge bases, program analysis, scene graphs), and evaluate robustness of direct reference (synonyms, coreference).
- Deeper analysis: Include qualitative visualizations of induced graphs, failure analyses, and per-component ablations (removing Tadd/TC/Tprop, replacing direct reference with learned attention/entity linker).
- Fairer comparisons: Include baselines with similar supervision levels (e.g., models trained with gold supporting facts or gold graphs when available) and contrast with a GGNN that receives pre-parsed graphs at train/test.

Questions for the authors
1) Training vs. inference: During training you replace predicted nodes/edges with gold ones after the supervision step. At test time, do you run the full pipeline purely with predicted graphs? Any gap between train/test dynamics?
2) Weak supervision: Have you tried training without full graph labels (e.g., only answer supervision), and how does performance degrade? Could regularizers (e.g., sparsity, structural priors) help?
3) Direct reference: How sensitive is performance to exact string matching? Can you replace direct reference with a learned entity linker/attention over mention spans, and how does that affect results?
4) Efficiency: What are typical |V|, |E|, and wall-clock/memory usage for the bAbI and TM settings? Any experiments with sparse C or local neighborhoods?
5) Ablations: Which transformations are most critical? E.g., can pathfinding be solved without TC (edge update) once the graph is built? What happens if Tprop uses simple linear messages vs. your learned fwd/bwd functions?
6) Generalization: For the TM experiments, how far beyond 30 steps does performance hold, and does it degrade smoothly? Can the model handle larger tapes or more states/symbols than seen in training?
7) Code/data: Will you release code and the graph supervision used for bAbI to facilitate comparison?
