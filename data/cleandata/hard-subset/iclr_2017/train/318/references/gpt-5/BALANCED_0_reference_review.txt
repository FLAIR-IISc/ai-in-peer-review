1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that can construct and modify a differentiable, typed, graph-structured latent state while processing sequences (e.g., text). The main technical contributions are: (i) a set of differentiable graph transformations—node addition, node state update (with a “direct reference” variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation—that operate over a soft graph with node/edge strengths; (ii) a training regime that supervises the intermediate graphs, including a permutation-invariant loss for newly added nodes; (iii) a configuration that incrementally builds a knowledge graph from text and uses it to answer queries. Empirically, the paper reports strong results on bAbI tasks (often ≥95% accuracy, especially with “direct reference”), and demonstrates that the model can learn state transition rules for a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines, including some generalization to longer horizons. The work argues for interpretability (explicit graphs) and distributed computation over graphs, and provides code.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The core claims are supported primarily under strong supervision of full intermediate graphs and hand-specified “direct references,” with limited evidence that the method works end-to-end from text without such supervision.
- Relative to GG-NN/GGS-NN and related memory architectures, the conceptual novelty and empirical rigor are not yet at the level expected for ICLR: the proposed transformations are natural extensions, but the evaluation lacks thorough comparisons, ablations, and analysis to isolate what is truly new and necessary.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? Learning to build and use graph-structured latent states from sequences, and learning graph state transitions. This is important and timely.

Is the approach well-motivated and placed? The motivation—explicit, interpretable graph states that can be edited—is sound. The paper situates itself among GG-NN/GGS-NN, memory networks, DNC, and graph convolution methods. However, the pitch that GGT-NN learns to extract graphs from unstructured text is weakened by the heavy reliance on graph-level supervision and direct references derived from entity names. Much of the power demonstrated on bAbI seems to come from the supervision and direct mapping from words to node types.

Do the claims hold?
- bAbI: The model achieves strong performance on many tasks with direct reference and full graph supervision at every step, but accuracy drops substantially without direct reference (only 9/20 at ≥95%). This indicates that the learned transformations alone are insufficient to robustly induce graphs from text. The comparison set is uneven: MemNN is also strongly supervised, but other baselines are not, making it hard to assess the true value of the proposed approach in comparable regimes. There is no report of variance, hyperparameter sensitivity, or training stability, and no ablations to show the necessity of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Th,direct).
- Rule discovery (CA, Turing): These are compelling illustrations that the architecture can learn local update rules on graph-structured inputs. However, there are no competitive baselines (e.g., DNC/NTM, GG-NN with fixed graphs), and details are sparse (exact accuracies for extended horizons, distribution of failures, sensitivity to graph size). The tasks are synthetic and, here, do not require text-to-graph conversion.
- Training methodology: The procedure substitutes ground-truth nodes/edges into the graph during training (teacher forcing for structure). This reduces exposure to compounding errors and makes test-time performance less indicative of end-to-end learning. The paper acknowledges the need for additional supervision but does not provide strategies that scale to realistic settings where gold graphs are unavailable.
- Complexity: The paper correctly notes quadratic time/space scaling with node count; experiments do not probe scaling behavior.

Clarity and correctness: The transformation definitions are clear and plausible. The permutation-invariant loss for node addition is appropriate. Some implementation specifics and experimental details (model sizes, training schedules, exact baselines and settings) are missing or deferred, hindering reproducibility assessment despite the code link. References are inconsistently formatted.

4) Suggestions for improvement (constructive, not all part of decision)
- End-to-end evidence: Demonstrate training without graph supervision and without direct references. For example, pretrain with partial graph supervision and fine-tune end-to-end, or use self-supervised/consistency losses that encourage sparse, predictive structure. Evaluate robustness when the “knowledge object” is noisy or unavailable.
- Ablations: Quantify the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Th vs Th,direct), the effect of teacher forcing of graphs, and the number/types of edges. Include alternatives where edges are predicted sparsely (top-k) to address scaling.
- Fair comparisons: For bAbI, compare under matched supervision regimes (all with/without strong supervision). Include GG-NN/GGS-NN baselines fed gold graphs at train/test to separate the contribution of editable structure from message passing alone.
- Analysis: Provide error analyses (e.g., where pathfinding succeeds/fails, typical failure modes in positional reasoning), variance over seeds, and training stability. Report runtime/memory and scaling with graph size.
- Broader datasets: Add experiments on real information extraction or procedural text-to-graph benchmarks where gold graphs are not provided, to validate practical applicability.
- Rule discovery: Compare with DNC/NTM and GG-NN variants on CA/Turing tasks; report exact long-horizon generalization rates and how they degrade with horizon; explore curriculum and locality biases.
- Presentation: Fix reference formatting; consolidate key architectural and training details in the main text; clarify how node types, edge vocabularies, and direct references are defined for each task.

Questions for the authors
- Can GGT-NN be trained end-to-end on bAbI (or another dataset) without any graph supervision and without direct reference? What is the best accuracy you obtain, and what inductive biases were necessary?
- During training you substitute ground-truth nodes/edges into the graph. What happens if you do not substitute and only backpropagate through predicted graphs? How does performance change?
- How many edge types and node types are used per bAbI task? Are these fixed a priori, and how sensitive is performance to these choices?
- How sparse are the learned graphs at test time? Did you try explicit sparsification (e.g., top-k edges) to improve efficiency?
- For the rule discovery tasks, how do results compare to a strong GG-NN baseline operating on fixed known graphs, and to DNC/NTM-style models?
- What is the variance over training runs? Please report mean±std across seeds for key tasks.
- How does performance and memory/time scale as the number of nodes grows (e.g., larger maps in pathfinding)?
