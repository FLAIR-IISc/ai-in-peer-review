- Paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs): adds soft node types, node existence strengths, multi-type soft edges, and modular graph transforms; trained with strong supervision and evaluated on bAbI and rule-discovery tasks with high reported accuracy.  
- Decision: Reject — primary reasons are reliance on heavy oracle supervision/direct reference, non-standard dataset modifications, insufficient ablations/optimization details, and incremental novelty without evidence in weakly supervised settings.  
- Heavy supervision/direct reference: bAbI experiments use gold intermediate graphs and exact token→node matching (oracle entity linking), driving the strong results and undermining claims of learned graph construction.  
- Dataset interventions: authors alter bAbI (multi-word entities→single tokens, added history nodes), which changes task difficulty and breaks fair comparison to baselines.  
- Limited novelty: contributions (continuous node/edge strengths, modular transforms, direct reference) are incremental extensions of GG-NN/GGS-NN without a clear principled advance for learning dynamic graph structure from text.  
- Missing ablations/details: paper lacks ablations of each transform (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Trepr), propagation steps, effect of set-matching loss, sensitivity to hidden sizes and edge-type count, and runtime/memory scaling analysis.  
- Permutation-invariant loss ambiguity: unclear how Lnode’s max-over-permutations is computed (Hungarian?), its cost function, and computational overhead.  
- Exposure bias and training pipeline: training replaces predicted nodes/edges with gold after loss computation; implications for exposure bias and test-time drift are not analyzed.  
- Interpretability claims under-supported: no quantitative measures of alignment between learned and gold graphs or demonstrated utility of extracted graphs in downstream algorithms.  
- Rule-discovery reporting vague: “majority” generalization claims lack exact accuracies per horizon and detailed error breakdowns for Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks.  
- Reproducibility gaps: essential hyperparameters (state sizes, propagation steps, dimensions, optimizer, LR, regularization) and precise preprocessing for modified bAbI are missing.  
- Suggestion — reduce supervision: demonstrate learning without gold intermediate graphs and without exact-name direct reference (weak or partial supervision, QA-only) to support central claims.  
- Suggestion — fair comparisons: evaluate baselines with matched supervision and training size, and report results on original unmodified bAbI or isolate each modification’s effect.  
- Suggestion — targeted ablations/analyses: remove each transform, vary propagation steps, compare direct reference vs learned attention, and test robustness to noisy mentions/paraphrases.  
- Suggestion — complexity & scalability: specify assignment solver, report time/memory vs |V| and Y, explore sparse updates or neighborhood sampling to avoid O(|V|^2Y) costs.  
- Suggestion — mitigate exposure bias: test schedules that anneal gold replacement (e.g., scheduled sampling) and report test-time performance without gold injection.  
- Suggestion — quantify interpretability & downstream utility: measure learned-vs-gold graph alignment when supervision is withheld and apply learned graphs to classic graph algorithms (e.g., shortest path).  
- Suggestion — rule-discovery rigor: report exact generalization accuracies at 20/30 steps, failure modes, and comparisons to symbolic/program-induction baselines.  
- Questions for authors: how is permutation max computed and its complexity; exact hyperparameters and propagation steps; max nodes Tadd proposes per sentence and sensitivity; bAbI results without direct reference/gold graphs; results on original bAbI; TC parametrization and regularization against toggling; detailed rule-task accuracies; inference-time complexity and scalability remedies.  
- Overall: promising modular framework for dynamic graph states, but current evidence depends on oracle supervision and dataset tweaks; needs stronger empirical validation under realistic supervision and clearer technical details to be compelling.
