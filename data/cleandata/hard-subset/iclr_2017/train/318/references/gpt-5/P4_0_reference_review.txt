Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that supports differentiable graph construction and modification over time. The model introduces a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition with soft existence/typing, node state updates including a “direct reference” mechanism, edge updates, propagation, and aggregation).
- A specific configuration is instantiated for text-to-graph modeling: sentences are encoded with a GRU, used to update and expand a latent graph, and a query phase aggregates the state to produce an answer. Training includes strong intermediate supervision via losses on node existence/types and edges, with permutation-invariant matching for new nodes.
- Empirical demonstrations include: (i) bAbI tasks with strong graph-level supervision, where GGT-NN attains high accuracy (≥95% on 19/20 tasks with direct reference; especially strong on pathfinding and induction), and (ii) rule-learning settings (1D cellular automaton Rule 30 and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines) where the model learns transition rules and generalizes to longer rollouts.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision (full target graphs at each time step and direct-reference signals) undermines the central claim of learning to construct and leverage graphical state from unstructured input; limited evidence that the approach works in end-to-end settings without intermediate graph labels.
- Novelty appears incremental relative to GG-NN/GGS-NN: the proposed transformations (add/update nodes/edges, GGNN-style propagation, attention-based aggregation) are natural extensions, but the paper lacks ablations or broader comparisons to establish clear conceptual or empirical advantage under comparable supervision.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem definition and motivation
- The paper targets learning state transitions over graph-structured latent representations from sequential inputs, a relevant and important problem. The “graph transformations” abstraction and the claim of composability are well-motivated.
- Placement in the literature covers GG-NN/GGS-NN, memory-based models, and related neural-symbolic systems. However, the paper’s case for why its formulation offers unique benefits beyond dynamic GGNN variants is not fully established; the core mechanisms (soft edges, node addition, attention aggregation) largely mirror established components.

2) Technical soundness and clarity
- The transformations are described with reasonable clarity and plausible implementations (GRU-style updates, MLPs for edge set/reset, propagation akin to GG-NN with soft connections). The node-set permutation matching for supervision follows Vinyals et al.
- Some aspects would benefit from more specification: training setup and hyperparameters per task, stability or regularization when learning both graph structure and propagation, and details of the direct-reference construction beyond summation of token-level representations.
- The computational complexity is acknowledged as quadratic; no empirical profiling or sparsity strategies are evaluated, leaving practical scalability unaddressed.

3) Empirical support and rigor
- bAbI experiments show strong results, but crucially depend on strong supervision: the model is trained with ground-truth graphs at each step and relies heavily on direct reference; without direct reference, performance drops substantially. This setup does not demonstrate that the model can infer graph structure purely from QA supervision or raw text. The MemNN comparison is not apples-to-apples across all baselines; several SOTA systems are trained end-to-end.
- The rule-learning tasks are illustrative, and the generalization to longer horizons is encouraging. However, baselines are absent, and the tasks are constructed with explicit graph encodings, leaving open whether the same benefits hold in less curated settings.
- Ablations are limited: aside from toggling direct reference and per-sentence propagation, the contribution of each transformation (e.g., edge-update vs node-add vs propagation depth) is not disentangled.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Provide a weakly supervised or end-to-end variant on bAbI (no intermediate graph labels; avoid direct reference), and quantify the gap. Intermediate supervision is valuable as an oracle but does not validate the central end-to-end claim.
- Add ablations isolating each transformation’s contribution, propagation depth, and the impact of node/edge softness. Report variance over random seeds.
- Include computational profiling: time/memory vs graph size; explore sparsity (top-k neighbors, sampled edges) or local neighborhoods to improve scalability.
- Expand baselines: compare with dynamic-graph models that learn edges (e.g., learned adjacency in GGNN-style frameworks) and with memory-based models under strong supervision where applicable. For rule tasks, include simple program-induction or differentiable simulator baselines.
- Clarify data preprocessing for bAbI (entity renaming, added history nodes) and release code/data to facilitate reproducibility. Report hyperparameters and training schedules per task.
- Analyze errors, especially for Task 17; discuss failure modes (entity vocabulary size, short contexts).

Questions for the authors
- Can GGT-NN be trained end-to-end on bAbI without intermediate graph supervision and without direct reference? What is the performance gap?
- How sensitive is the model to the number of node/edge types? What happens when the number of entities increases substantially (e.g., Task 17)?
- What ablations isolate the roles of Tadd, TC, Tprop, and Trepr? Does graph construction (add/update) alone suffice for some tasks?
- What are the runtime and memory costs as a function of |V| and |E| in practice? Any attempts at sparse propagation or attention-pruned edge updates?
- For the rule-learning tasks, how does performance compare to a baseline that hand-codes the transition function in a differentiable module or trains a local rule predictor on neighborhoods?
- Could you report training stability and convergence behavior (e.g., with/without node/edge supervision), and provide learning curves?
- How robust is the permutation-invariant node matching during supervision to noisy or inconsistent node orders?

Overall, the paper presents a clear and interesting formulation for dynamic graph state learning, but the reliance on strong supervision and limited empirical breadth make it hard to assess its impact under realistic end-to-end settings.
