Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular framework of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update with optional direct reference, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enables neural models to construct, modify, and exploit explicit graph-structured latent states from unstructured inputs. Compared to GG-NNs/GGS-NNs, which assume a fixed graph, GGT-NNs can dynamically add nodes/edges and update connections, and can be configured for different tasks (text-to-graph construction, graph-to-text, question answering over evolving graphs). The paper introduces a training strategy with strong supervision over the intermediate graph (including a permutation-invariant loss for unordered node additions) and demonstrates:
- Textual bAbI tasks: competitive to strong baselines on 1k training examples; especially strong on pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16), and 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks with “direct reference.”
- Rule discovery: learning to simulate Rule 30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines from graphical inputs, with generalization to longer rollouts.
Key claimed advantages are the interpretability of the internal state, the ability to distribute computation over graphs, and flexibility to handle graph construction and reasoning jointly.

Decision: Reject
Primary reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision (access to full ground-truth graph states at each step and direct lexical alignment to node types) limits the generality and significance of the results; (2) Insufficient empirical rigor and ablation to isolate what drives gains, plus limited evaluation beyond synthetic tasks; (3) Incremental novelty over GG-NN/GGS-NN and memory-based models without compelling evidence that the proposed transformations yield clear advantages under realistic settings.

Supporting arguments
- Supervision and fairness of comparison: The bAbI results depend on strong supervision of intermediate graph states and a direct-reference mechanism that assumes string matching between words and node types. This is not typically available and sidesteps core challenges in text-to-graph grounding (e.g., entity linking, co-reference). Comparisons highlight that MemNN also uses supervision, but most competing models (MemN2N, EntNet, DNC, DMN+) are trained end-to-end; thus the superiority claims are hard to interpret. It remains unclear how the method performs without graph-level supervision beyond the limited “no direct reference” variant (which solves 9/20 tasks), and how much each supervision component contributes.
- Novelty and positioning: The core propagation and aggregation follow GG-NN/GGS-NN; the main additions are (i) soft adjacency with create/remove edge functions, (ii) node addition with strengths and a permutation-invariant training objective, and (iii) a controller that sequences these modules from text. While interesting, these ideas are conceptually close to existing differentiable controllers over external memories (e.g., DNC) and graph-structured networks. The paper would benefit from clearer theoretical/empirical justification that these specific transformations (e.g., fset/freset vs. attention-based edge construction) are necessary and superior.
- Empirical scope and rigor: 
  - Limited ablations. There is no systematic study of the impact of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop), node/edge strengths, direct reference, intermediate propagation, or the permutation-invariant node loss. 
  - Missing training details (hyperparameters, sizes, propagation steps T, K, learning rates, regularization) hinder reproducibility despite a code link.
  - No computational analysis (time/memory) beyond noting quadratic scaling; no evidence of sparsity or efficiency strategies.
  - The rule discovery tasks are synthetic and relatively simple. While generalization to longer rollouts is promising, the evaluation does not stress out-of-distribution generalization (e.g., larger tapes, more states/symbols, different topologies) or compare against baselines known to learn such systems.
- Data assumptions and potential leakage: For bAbI, the training injects gold graph structures and even substitutes ground-truth edges/nodes during training. This makes the text-to-graph learning problem much easier and could conflate the difficulty of parsing with the learned reasoning. It is unclear how robust inference is when the model must infer all structure from text end-to-end.
- Clarity and presentation: The overall idea is clear, but some technical choices are underspecified (e.g., maximum nodes per step; thresholds on strengths; stability of fset/freset; how edge types Y are chosen). Reference formatting is inconsistent.

Constructive feedback to improve the paper
- Reduce supervision and strengthen empirical evidence:
  - Evaluate with weaker supervision (no access to full graph states; only answer supervision), and/or semi-supervised setups mixing small graph-annotated data with larger unannotated sets.
  - Include realistic datasets where direct string matching is insufficient (co-reference, paraphrase, unseen entities), or tasks requiring text-to-graph extraction beyond bAbI (e.g., knowledge base population, narrative world modeling).
- Provide ablations and diagnostics:
  - Remove/replace each transformation in turn; compare soft vs. discrete edges; analyze the contribution of node strengths; study the effect of intermediate propagation; quantify the benefit of the permutation-invariant node-add loss.
  - Compare edge-construction via fset/freset to alternatives (e.g., attention-based link prediction, bilinear decoders, Gumbel-sigmoid for discrete edges).
- Report training details and efficiency:
  - Hyperparameters, model sizes, training times, memory footprint, and scaling with number of nodes/edges. Explore sparse adjacency or neighborhood sampling to mitigate quadratic costs.
- Strengthen comparisons:
  - Include GGS-NN with text preprocessor baselines, pointer-network/seq2graph baselines, and modern graph attention mechanisms. Align supervision levels where possible.
- Improve clarity:
  - Formalize the controller architecture, step counts, and stopping criteria for node addition. Clarify how the model decides the number of nodes. Clean up references and provide preprocessing code for bAbI graph conversions.

Questions for the authors
1. How sensitive is performance to the availability of graph-level supervision? Can you report results with only answer supervision, and with partial supervision (e.g., edges only, nodes only)?
2. What are the exact hyperparameters (hidden sizes, number of propagation steps T, number of sentences K processed, learning schedules) for the main experiments?
3. How is the maximum number of nodes per Tadd step chosen, and how does the model decide when to stop adding nodes? What thresholding/rounding is applied to node strengths at inference?
4. For edge updates, why use separate fset/freset instead of a single parametric function predicting edge probabilities? Did you try discrete relaxation (e.g., Gumbel-Softmax) to stabilize edge construction?
5. Can you provide runtime/memory benchmarks vs. GG-NN/GGS-NN, MemN2N, and DNC on Tasks 16 and 19? Any evidence that sparsifying C or limiting neighborhoods preserves accuracy while improving efficiency?
6. In the bAbI pipeline, does the model ever need to resolve co-reference or entity aliasing beyond exact string match? How would your direct reference mechanism generalize beyond bAbI?
7. For the Turing machine task, how does performance scale to more states/symbols or longer rollouts without retraining? Any failure modes when rules induce longer-range dependencies?

Overall, the paper introduces a neat and interpretable framework for dynamic graph construction with encouraging synthetic results. However, the reliance on strong supervision, limited ablations, and lack of evaluation on more realistic settings make it premature for acceptance.
