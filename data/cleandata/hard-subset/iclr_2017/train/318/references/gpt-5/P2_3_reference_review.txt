1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular framework that maintains an explicitly graph-structured, differentiable state and updates it via a set of trainable transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional “direct reference” variant), edge update, propagation (GGNN-like message passing extended to soft node/edge strengths), and aggregation. The model can ingest text via a GRU encoder and incrementally build/modify a graph, then answer a query using the learned graph. The paper also introduces supervision mechanisms for node/edge construction (including permutation-invariant node addition loss) and uses strong graph supervision derived from bAbI’s generator. Experiments show: (i) high accuracy on most bAbI tasks (especially pathfinding and induction) with strong supervision and direct reference; (ii) learned dynamics on synthetic “rule discovery” tasks (Rule 30 and 4-state 2-symbol Turing machines), including some generalization to longer rollouts. The paper argues that explicit graph states are interpretable and naturally suited for tasks like pathfinding.

2) Decision: Reject
Primary reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision and engineered “direct reference,” with limited evidence the approach works end-to-end from raw text without gold graphs. The strongest results require access to internal generator knowledge and tailored preprocessing.
- Limited empirical scope and rigor: evaluation is almost entirely on synthetic tasks (bAbI, cellular automaton, small Turing machines), with non-comparable supervision against many baselines, and without ablations that isolate which components matter.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The need for explicit, learnable graph-structured internal states is well motivated, particularly for tasks like pathfinding or relational reasoning. The proposed set of differentiable transformations is a coherent design building on GGNNs, and the interpretability angle is appealing.

- Novelty and positioning: While the combination of (i) soft node/edge existence, (ii) typed nodes/edges, (iii) trainable add/update/propagate/aggregate modules and (iv) text-conditioned construction is nicely packaged, many ingredients are incremental extensions of GGNNs and related memory architectures. The paper discusses related work but does not convincingly separate itself from prior graph-structure learning approaches or stateful memory models beyond the explicitness of the latent graph.

- Empirical validation:
  • bAbI: The model uses strong supervision by injecting the gold graph at each step and “direct reference” that maps token matches directly to node-type-specific inputs. This setup substantially simplifies the learning problem and is not comparable to reported end-to-end baselines without such supervision. The authors acknowledge this but still contrast to end-to-end models. The non–direct reference variant still uses graph-level supervision, so it does not establish end-to-end learning from text to answers.
  • Preprocessing changes: For some tasks, the authors modify bAbI representations (e.g., adding history linked lists, altering multiword entities). This raises comparability concerns and may bias results toward the proposed architecture.
  • Rule discovery: The CA and TM simulations demonstrate the framework can learn graph dynamics, with encouraging generalization to longer horizons. However, they are limited-scale, synthetic settings and do not probe robustness, scalability, or sparsity pressures.
  • Missing ablations: No systematic study of the necessity/impact of each transformation, the effect of strong supervision vs. weak/none, or the role of direct reference. No reporting of graph quality (precision/recall of nodes/edges) beyond task accuracy. No scaling experiments on larger graphs or more complex real data.
  • Complexity: The design applies pairwise edge updates and message passing over fully connected potentials (with soft C), leading to O(|V|^2) time/memory. This limitation is noted but not addressed experimentally; no sparsity, pruning, or efficiency strategies are evaluated.

- Clarity and correctness: The transformations and algorithm are clearly described; the permutation-invariant loss for node addition is appropriate in principle. However, practical details are missing (e.g., how the permutation is found—Hungarian or brute force?—and the computational burden). The training approach teacher-forces gold nodes/edges back into the graph at each step, which can introduce exposure bias; its impact is not studied.

- Significance: The framework is conceptually neat and interpretable, but the current evidence on synthetic tasks under strong supervision falls short of ICLR standards for significance and generality.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- End-to-end learning: Demonstrate performance without graph-level supervision or direct reference on text-to-answer tasks, and quantify learned graph quality. Even partial success would strengthen the paper’s claim that the architecture can infer graphs from unstructured text.
- Fair comparisons: When using strong supervision, compare against baselines that receive equivalent supervision; when not, compare against end-to-end baselines. Avoid mixing regimes.
- Ablations: Quantify the contributions of Tadd, TC, Tprop, Trepr, direct reference, and intermediate propagation. Report sensitivity to number of propagation steps, hidden sizes, and edge type schemes.
- Efficiency: Explore and evaluate sparsity (top-k edges, sparse attention over neighbors), local neighborhoods, or learned pruning to address quadratic scaling. Report wall-clock and memory usage.
- Graph metrics and interpretability: Report precision/recall/F1 for nodes/edges vs. gold graphs, visualizations, and case studies linking graph errors to QA errors.
- Dataset breadth: Include at least one real-world graph extraction or relational reasoning dataset, or a synthetic benchmark with larger graphs and longer temporal dependencies.
- Training details: Clarify the algorithm for permutation matching, its complexity, and how you stabilized training. Discuss exposure bias from substituting gold graphs and whether scheduled sampling or partial teacher forcing helps.

Questions for the authors
- How is the max over node permutations computed in practice? Hungarian matching on a per-step cost? What is its runtime and does it constrain the number of added nodes?
- Can the model be trained and evaluated without injecting gold graphs (no strong supervision)? If so, what are accuracy and learned-graph quality?
- How many edge types are used per task, and are f_fwd/f_bwd shared across types? How sensitive is performance to the number of types and to type misspecification?
- What are the typical graph sizes at train/test, and the runtime/memory footprint per step?
- Does performance degrade when removing teacher forcing (i.e., using the model’s own predicted graphs throughout training/testing)?
- How robust is “direct reference” to paraphrases, coreference, or multiword expressions without the simplifying preprocessing?
- On the CA/TM tasks, how does the model perform when scaling to larger tapes/cell lists and longer horizons, and does it preserve sparsity?
