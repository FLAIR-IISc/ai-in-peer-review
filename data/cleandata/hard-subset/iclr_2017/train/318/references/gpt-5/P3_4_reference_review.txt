1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NN/GGS-NN that supports dynamic, differentiable graph states: soft node types, node “existence” strengths, and multi-type, soft edges. It defines a modular set of transformations (node addition, node-state update including “direct reference,” edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be composed to build/update a graph from sequential natural language input. The model is trained with strong supervision on intermediate graphs, using a permutation-invariant node addition loss and per-edge cross-entropy. The approach is evaluated on bAbI (text-to-graph-to-answer), and on rule discovery tasks (1D Rule-30 cellular automaton; 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines). The paper reports high accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks with “direct reference,” strong results on pathfinding and induction, and generalization of the learned transition rules to longer horizons in the rule discovery tasks. The authors argue that explicit, interpretable graph-state representations are beneficial compared to memory architectures with implicit state.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (a) strong reliance on heavy, non-realistic supervision and “direct reference” that leverages oracle entity names; (b) non-trivial modifications to the data and supervision pipeline (e.g., changes to bAbI’s textual surface forms, injection of history nodes) confound fairness and limit external validity; (c) insufficient empirical ablations and clarity around optimization details and complexity; and (d) incremental novelty relative to GG-NN/GGS-NN with dynamic edges and node additions, without sufficiently compelling evidence that the approach works in more realistic, weakly supervised settings.

3) Supporting arguments
- Heavy supervision and direct reference: The bAbI experiments rely on exposing the generator’s internal “knowledge” object as ground-truth graphs at each step and using those to supervise node/edge creation. The “direct reference” mechanism maps word tokens to node types via exact name matching. This is effectively oracle entity linking and supervision, not available in real-world text. The main empirical claims (19/20 tasks) depend on this setup; without direct reference performance drops dramatically (9/20 tasks to 95%+). This undermines claims that the model “learns to construct” graphical states from unstructured input.
- Dataset interventions: The paper alters bAbI in ways that impact difficulty and comparability. Examples include changing multi-word entities to single tokens and adding linked-list “history” structures for certain tasks. These changes are not standard and make comparisons with published baselines problematic. Some baselines are end-to-end with 10k training examples and no strong supervision; GGT-NN is trained with 1k but with strong supervision and oracle references. The fairness of the comparison needs to be addressed more rigorously.
- Limited novelty beyond GG-NN/GGS-NN: The core technical additions are (i) continuous node/edge strengths with multiple edge types, (ii) a modular set of differentiable graph transforms including node add and edge update, and (iii) a “direct reference” channel. While useful engineering, these are relatively incremental extensions of GGS-NN and related dynamic graph propagation. The paper would be stronger with a clear formalization of what is fundamentally new (e.g., a principled treatment of dynamic graph structure learning from text without oracle supervision).
- Missing ablations and details: Important questions are not addressed: the contribution of each transformation (node add vs edge update vs propagation), the number of propagation steps, the effect of the set-matching loss for node addition, sensitivity to hidden sizes and Y (edge types), runtime/memory scaling (edge updates are O(|V|^2 Y)). The “max over permutations” loss for set outputs suggests solving an assignment problem; the paper does not specify whether the Hungarian algorithm is used, the cost function details, and the computational overhead at scale. The training pipeline replaces predicted nodes/edges by gold after computing loss; exposure bias and test-time drift are not analyzed. For the rule discovery tasks, the reported generalization “majority” is vague; exact numbers per horizon are critical.
- Claims on interpretability and distributed computation: The interpretability of learned graphs is plausible but not demonstrated quantitatively (e.g., alignment between learned and gold edges without supervision, or utility of the extracted graphs downstream). Likewise, the claim that distributed updates help tasks like pathfinding is intuitive, but there is no controlled study (e.g., comparing to DNC/MemN2N with matched supervision levels and training sizes).
- Reproducibility: While a code link is provided, the paper lacks essential hyperparameters (state sizes, edge functions dimensionality, number of propagation steps, optimizer, learning rates, regularization, early stopping) and the precise preprocessing steps for the modified bAbI graphs.

4) Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not all required for acceptance)
- Reduce supervision: Demonstrate learning without access to gold intermediate graphs and without exact-name “direct reference.” Explore weak supervision (question-answer only) or partial supervision (only final graph or sparse constraints). Show that the model can learn entity grounding and relation extraction from text, even partially, to support the central claim.
- Fair comparisons: Provide baselines with comparable supervision levels (e.g., MemNN/DNC with oracle support facts or schema supervision) and with the same training size (1k vs 10k). Report results on unmodified bAbI text, or clearly isolate the effect of each modification.
- Ablations and analysis: 
  - Remove each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Trepr) to quantify their contributions.
  - Analyze number of propagation steps and the cost-benefit tradeoff.
  - Compare “direct reference” vs learned attention over tokens/entities.
  - Evaluate robustness to noisy entity mentions and paraphrases.
- Training details and complexity: Specify the assignment solver for the permutation-invariant loss and its complexity. Report time/memory usage vs number of nodes/edge types. Explore sparse edge updates or neighborhood sampling to reduce O(|V|^2) costs.
- Exposure bias: Since gold nodes/edges are injected during training, test a schedule that anneals this replacement, and report test-time degradation without supervision. Compare to pure teacher forcing vs scheduled sampling variants.
- Quantify interpretability: Measure how close learned graphs are to gold structures (when supervision is withheld), and show use of extracted graphs in downstream graph algorithms (e.g., classical shortest-path).
- Rule discovery: Report exact accuracies for 20/30-step generalization, with distribution of failures. Compare to symbolic baselines or program induction models. Discuss failure modes (e.g., head movement at tape boundaries).

Questions for the authors
- How is the max over permutations computed for Lnode? Is this a linear assignment with a specific cost? What is the complexity per step and its impact on training?
- What are the exact hyperparameters (hidden sizes, number of propagation steps T, dimensions of f_fwd/f_bwd outputs, optimizer, LR schedule, regularization)?
- How many new nodes can Tadd propose per sentence, and how is this upper bound chosen? How sensitive are results to it?
- Can you report bAbI results without direct reference and without injecting gold intermediate graphs (i.e., QA-only supervision)? What is the gap?
- For tasks where you modified the text or added history nodes, can you provide results on the original bAbI format? How do the conclusions change?
- In edge update TC, are fset and freset independent? Did you consider a single delta parametrization? Any regularization to avoid toggling instability?
- For Rule 30 and Turing machines, please provide exact generalization accuracies to 20/30 steps and error breakdowns (e.g., which transitions fail).
- What inference-time complexity do you observe on larger graphs, and can sparse updates or locality constraints preserve performance while improving scalability?

Overall, the paper introduces a promising modular framework for dynamic graph state learning, but the current evidence relies heavily on oracle supervision and task modifications, limiting its significance and external validity. Strengthening the empirical story under weaker supervision and clarifying technical details would make the contribution much more compelling.
