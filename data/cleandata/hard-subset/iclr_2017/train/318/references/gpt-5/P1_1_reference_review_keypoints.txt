- Proposal: Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) extend GG-NNs/GGS-NNs with soft node/edge existence, typed nodes/edges, and trainable graph transformations (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr).
- Architecture: Modules compose to process sentence sequences, incrementally construct/modify a graph, and answer queries; uses permutation-invariant loss for unordered node additions.
- Empirical claims: Strong bAbI performance under strong supervision (large gains on pathfinding) and ability to learn/generalize state transitions for Rule 30 and small Turing machines.
- Claimed advantages: Explicit, manipulable graph internal state is interpretable and argued preferable to implicit memories; distributed computation benefits noted alongside quadratic complexity caveat.
- Decision: Reject — main reasons are heavy reliance on strong supervision/direct string-reference and limited, synthetic evaluations with insufficient ablations.
- Strength: Clear, modular formulation of differentiable graph transformations, including practical set/reset edge updates and permutation-aware node-addition loss.
- Strength: Explicit graph state is interpretable and potentially interoperable with external graph systems.
- Strength: Pathfinding and induction tasks plausibly show benefits of explicit structure; algorithmic experiments demonstrate learning of transition rules and horizon generalization.
- Strength: Substantial implementation detail provided, with pseudocode and code availability.
- Weakness: Uses full graph targets per timestep and dictionary string matches on bAbI—strong, task-specific supervision that undermines fair comparison to end-to-end baselines.
- Weakness: Little evidence the model can learn graph structure end-to-end; performance drops sharply without graph supervision or direct reference.
- Weakness: Novelty/positioning relative to prior dynamic memory and graph models is not fully quantified; the term “Transformer” is potentially confusing.
- Weakness: Missing ablations on individual transformations, propagation depth, sensitivity to graph size, and no diagnosis for bAbI Task 17 failure.
- Weakness: Scalability concerns due to quadratic time/space with dense connectivity; no experiments or mitigation strategies for sparsity/efficiency.
- Suggestion: Provide end-to-end and semi-supervised experiments without graph supervision or direct reference to validate claim of graph induction.
- Suggestion: Add thorough ablations (remove/replace transformations, vary propagation steps, analyze node-addition loss and supervision granularity).
- Suggestion: Include efficiency benchmarks (time/peak memory) and experiments with sparse edge updates or neighborhood-restricted propagation.
- Suggestion: Evaluate on at least one non-synthetic relational/text-to-graph task to demonstrate external validity.
- Suggestion: Clarify naming to avoid confusion with Transformer architectures and emphasize “graph transformations.”
- Suggestion: Improve baseline comparisons by matching supervision levels and report hyperparameters, training details, and variance across runs.
- Question: Can the model learn useful graphs end-to-end or with semi-supervision, and how large is the performance gap?
- Question: How critical is “direct reference”; can learned attention handle paraphrases or OOV entities instead of string matching?
- Question: What are computational costs (time/peak memory) as functions of node/edge counts and sequence length; were sparse tensors or sampling tried?
- Question: How sensitive are results to number of propagation steps and is there evidence of over-smoothing?
- Question: What caused the bAbI Task 17 failure; would type tying or compositional representations help?
- Question: How does performance degrade with noise or larger state spaces in algorithmic tasks, and can extrapolation scale to 100+ steps?
- Question: Can learned graphs and attention weights be visualized to validate that intended structures are constructed during inference?
