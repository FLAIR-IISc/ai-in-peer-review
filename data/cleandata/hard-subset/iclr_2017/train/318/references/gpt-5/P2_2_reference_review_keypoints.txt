- Proposal: Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) that learn differentiable, dynamic graph structures via soft node/edge strengths and composable transformations (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) driven by a GRU text encoder.  
- Empirical claims: strong supervised bAbI results (≥95% on 19/20 tasks with direct reference) and successful rule-discovery on Rule 30 and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines with generalization to longer rollouts.  
- Overall aim: demonstrate learnable explicit dynamic graphs for reasoning from unstructured text to structured computation.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main rejection reason 1: Heavy reliance on strong, stepwise gold graph supervision and task-specific heuristics (direct lexical matches and dataset modifications) that undermine claims of learning graphs from raw text.  
- Main rejection reason 2: Incremental novelty over GG-NN/GGS-NN; conceptual contribution limited to making structure differentiable and adding transform composition.  
- Main rejection reason 3: Empirical evaluation lacks fair, controlled baselines and sufficient ablations.  
- Motivation/positioning: Paper is well-motivated but omits relevant literature on latent relational structure learning and dynamic graph generation.  
- Technical novelty: Differentiable soft existence and edge rewiring are sensible engineering extensions but not a major conceptual leap; direct reference is task-specific rather than learned grounding.  
- bAbI critique 1: Training uses gold intermediate graphs at every step and direct reference keyed to gold entity names, plus dataset format changes, making comparisons to baselines unfair.  
- bAbI critique 2: Little analysis of fully end-to-end performance without intermediate graph supervision; only limited “with/without direct reference” reported.  
- Rule-discovery critique: Results promising but in toy domains with no baselines, limited details on rule diversity, error modes, or scalability.  
- Ablation critique: Missing systematic analysis of which transformations matter, sensitivity to supervision strength, effect of removing direct reference, and no runtime/memory profiling despite scaling concerns.  
- Claim assessment: Paper shows capability when heavily supervised, but does not convincingly show induction of useful internal graphs from raw text without strong supervision.  
- Suggested comparison: Run controlled experiments giving all methods equal supervision or removing graph supervision to assess end-to-end learning.  
- Suggested ablations: Isolate effects of gold intermediate graphs, direct reference, and dataset modifications; evaluate partial/semi-supervised setups.  
- Suggested analyses: Visualize learned vs. gold graphs, show error cases, and analyze propagation/attention patterns for interpretability.  
- Positioning request: Discuss and compare to recent work on latent relational learning and dynamic graph reasoning; clarify when explicit dynamic graphs are preferable.  
- Compute/scaling request: Provide time/memory scaling analysis, sparsity tricks, and batch-size/runtime profiling.  
- Rule-discovery improvements: Describe Turing-rule sampling, add baselines (e.g., GG-NN, DNC), show generalization curves and error breakdowns.  
- Writing/formatting: Improve presentation, references, and consistency.  
- Key author questions: Provide end-to-end bAbI results without gold graphs/direct reference; measure impact of dataset modifications; test robustness of direct reference to noise/coreference; ablate individual transforms and soft vs. hard structures; report computational footprint as graphs grow; detail rule sampling and failure correlations in Turing experiments.
