Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a general framework for learning on dynamic, explicit, differentiable graph states. Unlike prior GNN/GGS-NN work that assumes fixed graphs, GGT-NNs learn to grow and modify a soft graph: adding nodes with types/strengths, updating node states, adding/removing typed edges, propagating messages, and aggregating. These “graph transformations” are modular, differentiable, and can be composed.
- The model integrates text processing (GRUs) with graph construction via a “direct reference” mechanism that routes sentence representations to referenced node types. Training includes a permutation-invariant set loss for node addition and a cross-entropy loss for edges, with strong supervision from ground-truth graphs.
- Empirically, GGT-NNs achieve high accuracy on many bAbI tasks (notably pathfinding and induction), when trained with full graph supervision; and learn state transition rules for Rule 30 cellular automata and 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines, with promising generalization to longer rollouts.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
1) Novelty and scope: A bold, general-purpose, differentiable framework for learning graph-structured state transitions, including structure growth (node/edge creation/deletion), is a significant conceptual advance over fixed-structure GNNs.
2) Evidence of capability: Demonstrations on text-to-graph reasoning (bAbI, including multi-step pathfinding) and on program-like dynamics (cellular automata and Turing machines) indicate the approach can learn non-trivial, interpretable, graph-based state updates.

Supporting arguments
- Conceptual significance: The work moves beyond reading from fixed graphs to learning to build, update, and exploit an explicit graph memory with typed nodes/edges and soft confidences. This is an attractive alternative to implicit vector memories (e.g., DNC, MemN2N), with potential for interpretability and downstream reuse of the graph state.
- Technical ingredients: The five transformations (Tadd, Th/Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr) are clear, composable, and fully differentiable. The permutation-invariant training for set-valued node additions is apt. The propagation extends GG-NN to soft node/edge strengths and direction-specific learned message functions.
- Empirical signals: On bAbI, the model (with direct reference) reaches ≥95% on 19/20 tasks, and particularly shines on pathfinding and induction—tasks naturally suited to graph reasoning. The rule discovery experiments convincingly show the model learning deterministic transition systems and generalizing to longer horizons.
- Positioning: The paper situates itself well among GNNs/GGS-NNs and memory architectures, accurately highlighting the novelty in dynamic graph construction from unstructured inputs.

Caveats (not decision-blocking)
- Strong supervision: bAbI experiments rely on ground-truth graphs derived from the generator’s knowledge object and direct string-based references. This is a substantial assumption; end-to-end learning without graph supervision is not demonstrated. Comparisons to models trained without such supervision are therefore imperfect.
- Task modifications: Some preprocessing (e.g., history linked lists, single-word entity names) potentially favors the proposed approach. The fairness and impact of these changes should be discussed more explicitly.
- Limited analysis/ablations: The paper would benefit from ablations (w/ and w/o direct reference, varying degree of graph supervision, number of propagation steps, sparsity) and from an efficiency profile. Current complexity is quadratic in nodes, which may constrain scalability.
- Reproducibility details: While appendices specify transformations, key training details (hyperparameters, max nodes per step, early stopping, seeds, variance) are sparse.

Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors)
- Reduce supervision: Explore semi-supervised or weakly supervised training (e.g., partial graph hints, consistency/regularization on structure, contrastive objectives), and report results without ground-truth graphs and with noisy direct references.
- Fair comparisons: Include baselines that also receive graph supervision (e.g., GG-NN/GGS-NN on the same provided graphs), and clarify which baselines use strong vs. weak supervision.
- Ablations and diagnostics: Quantify the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Th,direct, Tprop) and the set-loss for node addition. Provide learned graph visualizations and error analyses (e.g., failure modes on task 17).
- Efficiency and sparsity: Investigate sparse edge updates/propagation and subgraph selection to improve scalability. Report time/memory vs. nodes/edges.
- Generalization: For Turing machines and Rule 30, probe longer horizons and out-of-distribution settings; discuss failure modes.
- Clarity: Specify how the model decides the number of nodes to add per step; how node strengths are used at inference (thresholding?); and the dimensions/architectures for fadd, fset/freset, f_fwd/f_bwd.

Questions for the authors
1) How does the model perform on bAbI without ground-truth graph supervision and/or without direct reference? Can you provide accuracy and qualitative graphs in those settings?
2) How is the maximum number of nodes per addition step chosen? Does the model reliably learn sv=0 to suppress superfluous nodes, and how sensitive is performance to this cap?
3) In TC, are edge types updated independently or with shared constraints (e.g., mutual exclusivity)? Did you observe instability from simultaneous set/reset predictions?
4) What is the computational profile (time/memory) vs. number of nodes/edges, and how large a graph can you handle in practice?
5) For the rule discovery tasks, how robust is generalization to longer sequences and to different initializations? Do you have per-step accuracy curves and error breakdowns?
6) Can you show examples of the learned graphs over time for bAbI and Turing machines to substantiate interpretability claims?
7) How sensitive are results to the exact direct-reference mechanism (e.g., imperfect name matches, multi-word entities) and to the task-specific graph modifications (e.g., history lists)?

Overall, despite reliance on strong supervision and limited scaling analysis, the paper presents a compelling, novel direction: learning to construct and transform explicit graph states from sequences. I recommend acceptance.
