Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), extending GG-NNs/GGS-NNs with a differentiable graph state and a suite of graph transformations: node addition with soft existence and type distributions, node state updates (including a “direct reference” variant), edge updates, propagation, and aggregation. These modules can be composed to build/modify a graph state from text and to produce text or graph outputs.
- A specific configuration is presented for sentence-by-sentence graph construction and question answering (Algorithm 1). Training uses strong supervision with known graphs (including teacher-forcing for new nodes/edges) alongside answer supervision.
- Experiments on bAbI show high accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference, and two rule-discovery tasks (Rule 30 automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines) demonstrate learning of state transitions and some length generalization.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and engineered direct-reference signals on synthetic datasets (bAbI), with unfair comparisons to end-to-end methods; limited evidence the approach works without privileged information. 2) Methodological and reporting shortcomings: missing or unclear details (hyperparameters, compute, variance, ablations, efficiency), ambiguous or inconsistent math, and extensive formatting/reference issues that impede reproducibility and clarity. 3) Significantly incomplete positioning relative to modern literature on dynamic graph learning, graph transformers, graph structure learning, and neural algorithmic reasoning; novelty is not compelling in the 2025 context.

Supporting arguments
- Fairness and rigor of evaluation
  - The main bAbI results use strong supervision with full per-timestep ground-truth graphs and direct reference derived from entity names, plus manual task-specific graph tweaks (e.g., adding history lists). Comparisons are made to MemNN (also supervised) and several end-to-end methods, but the supervision regimes differ. The non–direct-reference variant performs much worse, suggesting the approach relies on privileged signals. There are no experiments on realistic, noisy text-to-graph extraction tasks.
  - No statistical robustness: no multiple runs, error bars, seeds, or sensitivity analyses. No ablations to quantify the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) or of supervision components (node vs. edge vs. answer supervision).
  - Efficiency and scalability are not empirically supported. The paper admits O(|V|^2) time/space for dense edges but provides no runtime, memory, or scaling curves. Theano-based code and unspecified hardware further limit reproducibility.

- Methodological clarity and correctness
  - Training with “teacher forcing” that replaces predicted nodes/edges with ground truth after computing loss risks exposure bias; the paper does not clarify whether test-time uses predicted structures end-to-end, and whether train-test mismatch affects performance.
  - Loss definitions: Ledge is given elementwise without explicit summation; Lnode uses -max over permutations (equivalent to min NLL) but the matching procedure is unspecified (Hungarian or brute force? Complexity?). The x*·ln(xv) term assumes xv is a proper simplex; the enforcement mechanism for [0,1] constraints and sum-to-1 is not stated (sigmoid/softmax/clipping?).
  - Edge update rule with simultaneous set and reset gates (“toggles” when both 1) is ill-posed under soft probabilities; behavior and gradients are unclear. Potential oscillations are not analyzed.
  - Algorithm 1/Appendix B notational inconsistencies (e.g., T_query superscripts/subscripts, undefined α dimensions in propagation, occasional missing sums and biases). Several equations contain typographical errors or dimension ambiguities.
  - Direct reference relies on exact lexical matches to node-type names; the authors even modify multi-word entities to single tokens to enable this. This is an unrealistic assumption for natural text and undermines claims of broad applicability.

- Novelty and related work
  - The paper omits or undercites substantial, now-standard literature: Graph Network framework (Battaglia et al., 2018), graph transformers, neural relational inference and graph structure learning, neural algorithmic reasoning, dynamic and temporal GNNs, text-to-graph IE with neural architectures, pointer/structured attention approaches, and neural state machines. Much of the proposed functionality (learned edge existence, message passing, global readouts, composable modules) is subsumed by modern GN/MPNN formulations with learned relational structure.
  - Learning to simulate discrete systems and Turing machines has been widely explored; the current setup is limited (2 symbols, 4 states; 6–7 step training; small tapes) and lacks comparisons to contemporary baselines. The generalization tests are distribution-similar and do not probe out-of-distribution rules or much longer horizons.

- Presentation issues impeding review
  - Missing tables/figures in the submission; results are described but not shown. References are inconsistent and in places garbled (punctuation, years, author lists). Footnotes/links are malformed (code URL has a space; Theano is deprecated). Multiple first-person style and informal phrasing. Numerous minor typos.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not all decision-critical)
- Provide a rigorous end-to-end setting without graph-level supervision and without direct reference; alternatively, study semi-supervised regimes with limited graph supervision and assess whether the model can induce structure.
- Include thorough ablations for each transformation, teacher forcing, and supervision components. Report variance across seeds and provide training details (dims, layers, activations, learning rates, schedules, regularization).
- Evaluate on modern, realistic datasets for text-to-graph construction and reasoning (e.g., knowledge graph construction/extraction, scene graphs, algorithmic reasoning benchmarks with longer sequences).
- Compare against modern baselines: Graph Networks/MPNNs with learned relations, graph transformers, struct-attention models, neural algorithmic reasoning, neural state machines. Discuss relative computational trade-offs with empirical scaling.
- Clarify and correct mathematical details: constraint enforcement for [0,1] and simplex, permutation solver and complexity, edge set/reset parameterization, dimensions in propagation, and consistent notation. Consider a probabilistic edge update without ambiguous toggling semantics.
- Replace Theano; provide clean code, scripts, and configs. Add runtime/memory and scaling analyses.

Questions for the authors
1) At test time on bAbI, is the model run fully autonomously without graph teacher forcing? What is the drop if no ground-truth graphs are injected during training (end-to-end only)?
2) How is the permutation over new nodes computed (algorithm, complexity)? How many nodes per step and how does this scale?
3) How are [0,1] constraints and simplex constraints enforced in practice for C, s, and x? Sigmoid/softmax? Any clipping?
4) Can you ablate the direct-reference mechanism and the manual graph augmentations (e.g., history lists) while retaining performance?
5) Provide runtime/memory vs. number of nodes/edge types. Can you train with sparse matrices and achieve similar results?
6) For Turing machines, do you generalize to unseen rule sets or much longer horizons (e.g., 100+ steps)? What failure modes arise?
7) How sensitive are results to the choice and number of message-passing steps in Tprop and to the design of f_fwd/f_bwd?
