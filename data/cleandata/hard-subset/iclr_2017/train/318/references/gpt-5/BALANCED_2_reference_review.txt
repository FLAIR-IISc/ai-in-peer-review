1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN), an architecture that learns to construct and transform a graph-structured latent state from sequences (e.g., text) via a set of differentiable graph operations: node addition, node state update (with an optional “direct reference” variant), edge update, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation. The model integrates a recurrent sentence encoder and composes these operations to incrementally build a graph and answer queries. Training can be strongly supervised with graph-level targets; node-permutation invariance in supervision is handled via a set-to-sequence matching loss. Experiments show: (a) strong performance on bAbI tasks when provided graph supervision (19/20 tasks ≥95% with direct reference, often with ≤500 examples); (b) competitive performance on pathfinding and induction; and (c) the ability to learn and generalize update rules on two synthetic dynamical systems (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines), including extrapolation to longer horizons.

2) Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision and substantial task-specific graph engineering (e.g., modified knowledge objects, history lists, direct string matching), making comparisons to end-to-end baselines not apples-to-apples and limiting evidence that the method works in the standard weakly supervised setting.
- Incremental novelty relative to GG-NN/GGS-NN with limited evaluation on real-world datasets; scalability and reproducibility details are insufficient for an ICLR-level contribution.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Learning to build and manipulate a graph-structured latent state from sequential inputs and use it for reasoning and prediction, including learning state transition rules. This is a meaningful problem and the modular set of differentiable graph operations is an appealing abstraction.
- Motivation and placement: The paper positions GGT-NN as an extension of GG-NN/GGS-NN to allow graph construction and modification, contrasting with memory networks and DNCs where structure is implicit. This is reasonable, though the paper largely compares on synthetic tasks; related work that also builds graphs from text or learns dynamic graphs could be discussed more broadly.
- Claims vs. evidence:
  • bAbI results are strong but rely on strong supervision with injected ground-truth graphs at each step (including replacing edges and new nodes during training) and engineered graph schemas (context-specific edge types, added history linked lists, single-word entity changes). Many baselines reported are weakly supervised; even the “strongly supervised” MemNN setting is not strictly comparable to having full graph supervision. As such, it is hard to conclude that the approach would remain competitive without strong supervision and task-specific graph design.
  • The direct-reference mechanism further reduces the difficulty by mapping tokens to node types via exact name matching; the no-direct-reference condition performs substantially worse, underlining how important this supervision is.
  • The synthetic rule-learning tasks show nice generalization, but are relatively simple and constrained (1D CA; small TM class; short training horizons; rules given in structured form for TMs). They demonstrate feasibility rather than broad applicability.
  • Scalability is acknowledged as quadratic; no empirical analysis of memory/time vs. graph size is provided. The model composes several modules, yet ablations are limited (direct reference, intermediate propagation on two tasks). Missing are ablations on degree of supervision (e.g., partial supervision, no supervision), number of propagation steps, sparsity, or message function design.
  • Clarity/reproducibility: Core operations are described well (and code is mentioned), but key training details (hyperparameters, model sizes, propagation steps, runtimes), data preprocessing choices, and exact evaluation protocols are insufficiently documented for fair replication.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affected the decision)
- Evaluate under standard weakly supervised settings on bAbI (no graph-level targets, no direct reference, no graph schema modifications) to establish end-to-end viability. If some supervision is necessary, study the supervision curve (none → partial → full) and report how accuracy and learned graphs degrade.
- Provide ablations: number of propagation steps; effect of node/edge strengths vs. binary; alternative message functions; impact of replacing ground-truth nodes/edges during training (exposure bias), possibly with scheduled sampling.
- Benchmark efficiency: report graph sizes, memory, FLOPs, and wall-clock time; explore sparse connectivity (top-k edges, learned sparsification) to mitigate O(|V|^2).
- Comparisons: include baselines that also build or update graphs from text (e.g., GGS-NN with textual encoders, modern graph construction methods), and models for dynamic graphs. On pathfinding, contrast against text-to-graph parsers + graph planners to highlight advantages.
- Realistic datasets: demonstrate utility on a non-synthetic corpus where graph extraction is useful (e.g., KB construction, AMR-like parsing, narrative state tracking) to validate generality beyond bAbI.
- Clarify terminology: “Transformer” may be confused with the Transformer architecture; consider renaming to “Graph Transformation Network” or similar.
- Improve reproducibility: detail hyperparameters, architectures, training schedules, number of seeds and variance, and release the exact graph schemas and preprocessing.

Questions for the authors
- How many nodes are allowed to be added per sentence, and how is this limit chosen? How sensitive are results to this cap?
- During training you replace newly added nodes/edges with ground truth before subsequent steps. At test time this is not available. Did you observe exposure bias? Did you try scheduled sampling or partial replacement?
- How many propagation steps T are used per transformation, and how do results change with T?
- Do node/edge strengths saturate to 0/1 in practice? Is there any regularization that encourages discreteness?
- How robust is direct reference to tokenization, synonyms, or OOV entities? Can it be learned via attention rather than exact string matching?
- For bAbI tasks with added history nodes: do these additions advantage GGT-NN over baselines that do not receive such structure? Can you report results without modifying the knowledge object (or use the Appendix D variant) in the same supervision regime?
- What are typical graph sizes (|V|, |E|) during bAbI and rule-learning tasks, and what are the training/inference runtimes and memory footprints?
- For Turing machines: are the transition rules provided as text and then converted to the internal graph at training time? How diverse are the sampled machines? How often do rollouts diverge on the 20/30-step evaluations?
- Could GGS-NN with learned edge-update modules achieve similar performance if given the same supervision? What specific capability of GGT-NN is critical?
- Are there failure cases where the learned graph is systematically incorrect but still yields correct answers (or vice versa)? An error analysis would be insightful.
