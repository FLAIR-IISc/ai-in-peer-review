1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of Gated Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs/GGS-NNs) that supports differentiable graph construction and modification. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations—node addition (Tadd), node state updates with optional type-specific direct references (Th, Th,direct), edge updates (TC), propagation (Tprop), and aggregation (Trepr)—that allow building and updating a graph state from sequences of text and using this state to answer questions or produce further graph/text outputs. The model is trained with additional supervision over intermediate graph states via permutation-invariant node supervision and edge-level cross-entropy, enabling it to learn complex state-update rules.

Empirically, the paper demonstrates: (i) strong performance on most bAbI tasks when trained with full graph supervision (reaching ≥95% on 19/20 tasks with direct reference, and solid sample efficiency on many tasks), including competitive performance on pathfinding and induction tasks where graphs are naturally suited; and (ii) the ability to learn graph-based transition rules by simulating a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines, with generalization to longer rollouts. The work is clearly positioned against GNNs, Memory Networks, and DNCs, emphasizing explicit, interpretable graph-structured internal state.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision over full intermediate graphs and task-specific direct reference limits generality and comparability to end-to-end models; claims about learning to construct graphs from text are not substantiated under weak supervision.
- Experimental scope focuses on synthetic tasks with modified inputs and privileged information; scalability and robustness are not demonstrated on less curated benchmarks.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation/positioning: The need for dynamic graph construction is well argued, and the modular transformation design is clear and compatible with prior GG-NN/GGS-NN frameworks. The paper situates itself appropriately in the literature.
- Technical soundness: The transformations are differentiable, the permutation-invariant node loss is reasonable, and the propagation/aggregation extensions are consistent. However, training injects gold graph states after computing losses (“teacher forcing” on structure), which can understate exposure bias and does not evaluate robustness to compounding errors at test time.
- Empirical support: Results on bAbI are strong but rely on (a) full access to the generator’s “knowledge object” as graph supervision, (b) a dictionary-based direct reference mechanism that maps words to node types, and (c) dataset modifications (e.g., adding history nodes, altering multi-word entities). These choices make the setup less comparable to end-to-end baselines and raise questions about generalization to real-world text where such supervision is not available. The rule-discovery tasks convincingly show that the model can learn graph transitions, but they are small-scale and synthetic.
- Novelty: Allowing explicit, differentiable graph growth and edge updates controlled by text via a modular set of transformations is a meaningful extension of prior GG-NN/GGS-NN models. However, the significance is tempered by the reliance on privileged supervision and lack of results under weaker settings.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all part of the accept/reject decision)
- Include experiments without graph-level supervision (or with limited supervision) to demonstrate feasibility in realistic scenarios. For example, use distant supervision or regularizers that encourage discrete structure without gold graphs.
- Provide ablations: impact of (i) direct reference, (ii) teacher-forced graph substitution vs scheduled sampling, (iii) intermediate propagation, (iv) sparse vs dense edge updates.
- Clarify fairness of bAbI comparisons by reporting “strongly supervised” and “end-to-end” scenarios separately and ideally providing baselines that use the same level of privileged information.
- Report complete training details (hidden sizes, edge-type counts, optimization, runtime, memory) and parameter counts. Analyze error propagation at test time when no gold structure is injected.
- Discuss scalability: propose or evaluate sparse connectivity, neighborhood pruning, or subgraph routing to mitigate O(|V|^2) cost.
- Avoid modifying datasets where possible; if modifications are necessary, release the exact transformed data and justify the necessity.
- Consider evaluation on additional graph-centric NLP datasets (e.g., semantic graphs, AMR, knowledge base construction) to test generality beyond synthetic tasks.

5. Questions for the authors
- How does the model perform without graph supervision (only QA supervision)? Can limited graph supervision (e.g., small fraction labeled) suffice?
- What is the exact procedure for “teacher forcing” on graphs? After computing node/edge losses, are gold structures fully substituted before subsequent transformations? How does performance change without this substitution or with scheduled sampling?
- For bAbI, how many edge types are used per task and are relation labels pre-specified by the generator? Can the model discover relation types without predefining them?
- How is the maximum number of new nodes per step chosen, and how sensitive are results to this choice? What happens when the true number exceeds the maximum?
- What are the hidden/state dimensions, number of propagation steps, and training times per task? What is the typical graph size at test time?
- To what extent does direct reference rely on exact string matching to entity names? How would the approach handle paraphrases, OOV entities, or coreference?
- In the rule-discovery tasks, what are the initial tape/automaton sizes and distributions? How often do errors compound over long rollouts without gold interventions?
- Can the edge update functions be constrained to be sparse (e.g., via L1 or top-k masking) to improve scalability? Any preliminary results?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting and well-structured architecture for dynamic graph manipulation with promising results under strong supervision. Demonstrating effectiveness with weaker supervision and on less curated data would substantially strengthen the contribution.
