Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that maintains an explicit, differentiable graph-structured state and modifies it over time via a set of learnable, composable graph transformations: node addition (Tadd), node state updates (Th, with optional direct-reference Th,direct), edge updates (TC), propagation (Tprop), and aggregation (Trepr). Graph elements are soft (node types as distributions; edge strengths in [0,1]).
- A specific configuration parses a sequence of sentences with a GRU, incrementally constructs/updates a graph, and then answers a query from the final graph (or a sequence of graphs, Appendix D).
- Training uses strong structural supervision: gold graph edges and newly added nodes at each step, with a set-like loss for unordered node addition (max over permutations) and cross-entropy for edges; during training, gold structural components are substituted for the model’s predictions for subsequent steps.
- Empirically, on bAbI, the model achieves ≥95% on 19/20 tasks with direct references and shows particularly strong performance on path finding (task 19) and induction (task 16). It also learns to simulate a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines, with some generalization to longer horizons.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific direct referencing undermines the central claim of learning to construct and manipulate graphs from text; comparisons to mostly weakly supervised baselines are not apples-to-apples.
2) Limited novelty beyond prior GG-NN/GGS-NN work; the proposed “transformations” largely repackage known components without theoretical insights, and scalability/rigor are insufficiently addressed.

Supporting arguments
- Supervision and training protocol: The core training signal replaces predicted structure with ground-truth edges and nodes after computing losses. This breaks the end-to-end interaction between structure inference and downstream reasoning, risks exposure bias, and obscures whether the model can stably use its own predicted graphs at test time. The strong direct-reference mechanism (lexical mapping from words to node types) further simplifies entity grounding in bAbI. As a result, the main empirical claims (learning to build/update graphs from text) are not convincingly demonstrated in a weakly supervised setting.
- Fairness of comparisons: The model uses strong supervision (full graph at every step) while most baselines (MemN2N, EntNet, DNC, DMN+) are end-to-end with no gold structure; MemNN is the only “strongly supervised” baseline, but it is supervised with supporting sentences—not gold graph structures. The paper acknowledges this but still draws conclusions suggesting superiority of graphical state. This comparison should be qualified much more carefully or accompanied by weakly supervised GGT-NN results.
- Novelty and positioning: The “differentiable graph” definition (soft node/edge strengths) and modular transformations (add/update/propagate/aggregate) are sensible engineering choices but conceptually close to prior GG-NN/GGS-NN and related graph-state models. There is no theory, no new algorithmic insights (e.g., guarantees, learned algorithms analysis), and limited ablation to isolate which components matter. The direct-reference mechanism is essentially an attention mask by node type names.
- Experimental scope and rigor: bAbI is a useful sanity check, but the model uses the generator’s internal “knowledge object” to derive gold graphs, which can overstate practical utility. The Turing machine/cellular automata tasks are interesting but heavily engineered representations and limited in scale; generalization is reported to 20–30 steps without more detailed metrics (per-step performance, failure modes). There is little analysis of learned structures, sparsity, calibration of soft edges, or sensitivity to hyperparameters.
- Scalability: The paper itself notes O(|V|^2) compute/memory. There is no empirical characterization of runtime/space vs. baselines or attempts at sparsification, which is critical for real graphs. Missing details include sizes (D, number of node/edge types), parameter count, training budgets, and convergence behavior.

Constructive feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide weakly supervised and semi-supervised results (no gold graphs; partial supervision) and quantify the impact of structural supervision. Show end-to-end training without substituting gold structures.
- Add ablations: remove direct-reference; vary supervision strength; analyze the role of each transformation; quantify exposure bias (train-test mismatch without gold substitutions).
- Strengthen baselines: include GGS-NN with textual-to-graph preprocessing pipelines; compare to contemporary neural semantic parsers or AMR parsers on graph construction; for pathfinding, include ablations that use a DNC/HAM with graph-coded memory.
- Report compute/memory footprint vs. graph size, and experiments with sparsity (top-k edges, learned sparsifiers) to address scalability concerns.
- Improve clarity: detail hyperparameters, model sizes, training schedules; formalize the exact gradients when substituting gold structures (do gradients flow through substitution?); clarify how node-type vocabularies are managed across tasks.
- Evaluate on more realistic datasets where gold structure is not available (e.g., knowledge base population/AMR), even if performance is modest—this would better support the paper’s motivation.

Questions for the authors
1) How does training behave without substituting gold nodes/edges after computing losses? Can you report accuracy and stability in fully end-to-end training?
2) How are gradients handled when you replace predicted structures with gold ones—do you stop gradients through those components? What is the impact on learned dynamics at test time?
3) How many node/edge types are used per task? Are node types fixed in advance (entity-name tied), and how would the model handle open-vocabulary entities or coreference?
4) What is the computational and memory complexity in practice (D, Y, |V|)? Please include wall-clock, peak memory, and asymptotic scaling plots.
5) For the Turing machine experiments, how are rules sampled? Do models generalize to entirely unseen rule tables and longer horizons reliably? Provide per-step error rates and failure analyses.
6) Do the learned graphs become sparse/binary at convergence? Any regularization (e.g., entropy, L1) to encourage discrete structures?
7) Could you compare to GGS-NN on pathfinding when both receive equivalent supervision (text-to-graph preprocessing for GGS-NN), to isolate the effect of your dynamic graph construction?

Overall, the idea of modular, differentiable graph transformations is promising and the engineering is thoughtful, but stronger evidence under weaker supervision, clearer positioning, and more rigorous evaluation are needed for acceptance.
