Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a framework that makes graph structure itself a differentiable, trainable state. The core technical contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations—node addition, node-state update (with a direct-reference variant), edge update, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation—operating on a soft graph parameterization (continuous node “existence” strengths, node-type distributions, and multi-type edge probabilities). These modules are composed to incrementally build and manipulate an internal graph from sequences of sentences and then answer queries. The paper demonstrates the approach on (i) bAbI QA tasks by supervising the intermediate graphs at each timestep, including a direct-reference mechanism that targets node types named in text, and (ii) two rule-discovery tasks (1D cellular automaton Rule 30 and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines), showing the model can learn to update its internal graph according to task rules and generalize to longer rollouts. The work highlights interpretability (explicit graphs), modularity, and distributed computation over graphs.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- The main empirical results on bAbI rely on strong supervision with ground-truth intermediate graphs and handcrafted direct-reference signals, making comparisons to end-to-end baselines not apples-to-apples and weakening the central claim of extracting and using graph-structured state from text.
- The empirical evaluation and presentation lack rigor and clarity in several places (missing numbers/figures, substantial task-specific preprocessing, limited ablations, and scalability analyses), making it hard to verify the claims and assess generality.

Supporting arguments
- Dependence on strong supervision: The paper trains with full graph supervision at each timestep (node/edge structures substituted during training), and adds a direct-reference mechanism keyed by entity names. Because the model is guided to the correct latent graphs during training, it is unclear how much of the reported bAbI success comes from learning to construct graphs from text versus from using privileged supervision. The gap reported between “with direct reference” (95% on 19/20 tasks) and “without direct reference” (9/20) suggests the approach is fragile without this supervision channel.
- Comparability and preprocessing: For bAbI, the authors expose the generator’s “Knowledge” object as the supervision target, alter task graphs (e.g., add explicit history linked lists), and collapse multiword entities to single tokens for direct reference. These modifications likely make the problem easier and hamper fair comparison to prior end-to-end models. While the paper notes the strong supervision, the comparison tables conflate settings (1k vs. 10k training examples, supervised vs. not), which muddles conclusions about relative merits.
- Empirical completeness: Many crucial details are missing or difficult to parse (full per-task accuracy tables not shown here, figures referenced but not present, hyperparameters, model sizes, optimizer, training epochs, wall-clock time, memory footprint). There are limited ablations isolating the contribution of each transformation (e.g., how much do node addition vs. edge update vs. intermediate propagation matter?), limited sensitivity analyses (number of propagation steps, sparsity), and no variance reporting across runs.
- Scalability: The paper acknowledges quadratic time/space in the number of nodes but provides no empirical profiling or strategies beyond mentioning sparsity as future work. Given the architectural design, practicality on non-trivial graphs remains uncertain.
- Rule-discovery tasks: The cellular automaton and Turing machine results are interesting sanity checks, but they are highly synthetic and not compared against strong sequence/graph baselines. Claims of generalization to longer rollouts are not quantified here (“majority of extended tasks”), which weakens the evidence.
- Terminology: The term “Transformer” is potentially misleading since the model is not related to attention-based Transformer architectures; here “transformer” refers to graph transformations, which can confuse readers.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decisive for my recommendation)
- Clarify and quantify: Provide complete per-task tables, exact training regimes, hyperparameters, ablation studies per transformation, multiple random seeds, and error analyses (especially for the failure case on Task 17).
- Fair comparisons: Separate results by supervision regime; compare to baselines that also use privileged supervision (if any), and include end-to-end settings without graph supervision to show what performance is achievable in the intended use case.
- Reduce supervision: Explore semi-/weakly-supervised training (e.g., consistency regularizers, denoising, or joint learning with sparsity priors) to bridge to end-to-end settings where ground-truth intermediate graphs are unavailable.
- Robustness of direct reference: Replace lexicon-based direct reference with learned mention/entity linking or attention mechanisms; report performance with noisy or ambiguous references.
- Scalability: Implement sparse adjacency and batched message passing; profile runtime and memory vs. number of nodes/edges; demonstrate on larger graphs.
- Clarity and presentation: Include all figures, clean up references, and rename the model to avoid confusion with Transformer architectures.
- Broader evaluation: Add tasks where graph construction from text is essential but no ground-truth graphs are provided (e.g., Open Information Extraction, procedural text to dynamic KG, TextWorld/DynaBAbI variants), and tasks with real graphs as inputs where dynamic graph editing matters.

Questions for the authors
- What is the model’s performance on bAbI when no graph supervision is provided (end-to-end from text to answer), with and without direct reference? Can you provide full numbers and training curves?
- How sensitive are results to the number of propagation steps, the maximum number of added nodes per sentence, and the set-matching loss used for node addition?
- How is direct reference handled when multiple entities share names, or for coreference/pronouns? Can the model learn mention grounding without string matches?
- Can you provide full quantitative results for the generalization experiments (20/30-step rollouts) including accuracies and error types?
- What are the exact model sizes (D, N, Y), optimizer settings, compute resources, training time, and memory usage? How do these scale with graph size?
- Ablation: What happens if you remove Th,direct, remove intermediate Tprop, or train without substituting ground-truth nodes/edges during supervision?
- How robust is performance if you avoid modifying the bAbI graphs (no extra history nodes, no token collapsing), i.e., adhere strictly to the original text format?

Overall, the idea of differentiable graph-edit operations is appealing and potentially useful, and the modular design is elegant. However, the current evidence relies heavily on privileged supervision and lacks the rigor and clarity necessary for acceptance.
