- Paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) with soft node types/existence, soft typed edges, and modular graph transformations (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr).
- Evaluated on bAbI (with graph supervision) and synthetic rule-discovery tasks showing strong results when given ground-truth graphs and direct reference.
- Decision: Reject.
- Major concern: Central empirical claims rely heavily on strong supervision (ground-truth intermediate graphs) and oracle “direct reference,” limiting claims about learning from raw text.
- Novelty is incremental relative to GG-NNs/GGS-NNs, message-passing nets, dynamic graph models, and Graph Networks; not convincingly disentangled experimentally.
- Key methodological detail underspecified: the permutation-invariant node-addition loss (max over permutations) lacks algorithmic/complexity description.
- Training uses teacher-forcing/substitution of ground-truth nodes/edges, raising exposure-bias concerns not analyzed.
- Insufficient ablations: no clear isolation of contributions from Tadd, TC, Tprop, node strengths, or supervision regimes (answer-only vs graph-supervised).
- Missing fair baselines: no comparisons to text-to-graph parsers, dynamic edge inference methods, or models receiving the same graph supervision.
- Scalability/practicality issues: quadratic time/space in node count and multiplicative cost per edge type, with no empirical scaling curves or mitigation strategies.
- Empirical scope limited and small-scale: bAbI and synthetic tasks are tiny and often encode rules/graphs explicitly, making results closer to emulation than latent-structure learning.
- Lack of analysis/qualitative results: no visualizations of learned graphs, attention/propagation paths, or failure modes.
- Recommended experiments: clarify permutation matching, report runtime/memory scaling, ablations (including scheduled sampling), parameter-sharing for edge types, and baselines with comparable supervision.
- Questions for authors include details on permutation matching implementation/differentiability, exposure bias and scheduled sampling, parameter sharing for f_fwd/f_bwd, training with answer-only supervision, robustness to noisy direct reference, and comparisons isolating transition learning vs provided structure.
