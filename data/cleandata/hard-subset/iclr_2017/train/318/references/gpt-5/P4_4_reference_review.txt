1) Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes a differentiable framework for learning to build and transform graph-structured state, introducing (i) a soft graph representation with node strengths and probabilistic, typed edges; (ii) a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update with optional direct-reference, edge update, message-passing propagation, and attention-based aggregation); and (iii) a composed architecture, Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), that interleaves these transformations with a recurrent encoder of natural language.
- The approach extends GG-NN/GGS-NN by (a) allowing the graph structure itself to be created and modified (dynamic nodes/edges), and (b) enabling text-to-graph and graph-to-text processing within one model.
- Empirically, the model is evaluated on: (a) bAbI tasks, using strong supervision in the form of ground-truth “knowledge” graphs derived from the generator; and (b) synthetic “rule discovery” tasks: 1D Rule-30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines, where the model learns to roll out graph state transitions and reportedly generalizes to longer horizons. The authors also study a variant that reasons over sequences of intermediate graphs.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision and hand-engineered direct-reference and dataset modifications for bAbI substantially weakens the empirical claims about learning from unstructured text; the setup is not comparable to standard end-to-end settings and limits the significance.
- Limited empirical rigor and novelty relative to GG-NN/GGS-NN: the main technical advance (dynamic graph construction via differentiable transforms) is interesting but is validated only on toy datasets with strong supervision, lacks ablations, and raises scalability concerns (quadratic cost) not addressed experimentally.

Supporting arguments
- Problem tackled: Learning to maintain and transform an explicit, interpretable graph-structured state from text and to use that state for reasoning. This is a clear and relevant objective; the motivation is well articulated and the connection to tasks like pathfinding and induction is appropriate.
- Positioning in the literature: The paper correctly identifies the fixed-graph limitation of GG-NN/GGS-NN and differentiates against memory-augmented models (MemN2N, DNC) that use implicit vector memories. The conceptual step of making node/edge existence differentiable and learnable is natural and valuable. However, the paper does not compare to alternative text-to-graph methods or semantic parsing approaches, and the comparison baseline closest in supervision (MemNN with supporting facts) is discussed but not fully controlled for fairness.
- Do results support the claims?
  - bAbI: Reported accuracies are strong on many tasks, especially pathfinding and induction, and the paper is transparent about using strong supervision and direct-reference. However, the supervision is very strong: ground-truth graphs from the generator are provided at every timestep, and the model uses them during training (teacher forcing of new nodes/edges). Furthermore, entity names were altered to be single-word to enable direct-reference. These choices make it unclear whether the model learns robust text-to-graph construction beyond string matching and supervision. Comparisons to end-to-end baselines are thus not apples-to-apples, and ablations (e.g., removing direct-reference, training without graph supervision, or using unmodified bAbI text) are limited.
  - Rule discovery tasks: The results suggest the model can learn local update rules and rollouts, with some length generalization. This is a suitable stress test for the graph-transition mechanism. Yet, critical details are missing: whether test Turing machines use unseen rules; how accuracy degrades with horizon; and how sensitivity to graph size scales. The tasks are synthetic and do not address scalability or noise.
  - Technical soundness: The transformations are well specified and differentiable; the node-set matching loss (permutation-invariant supervision) is appropriate. The complexity is O(|V|^2|Y|) due to dense connectivity and per-pair edge updates, which the paper acknowledges; no empirical profiling or sparse variants are demonstrated.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (not all required for the decision)
- Strengthen end-to-end evaluation:
  - Train without graph-level supervision and without the direct-reference mechanism on bAbI (or with only partial supervision) to assess whether the model can learn text-to-graph construction from QA signals.
  - Use unmodified bAbI text (including multi-word entities) and report robustness to lexical variability.
  - Provide ablations isolating the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Th,direct).
- Fairness and clarity in comparisons:
  - Report results for baselines under the same supervision regime; e.g., GGS-NN taking graph inputs constructed from the same knowledge object; MemNN with/without supporting facts; and a GG-NN baseline that only propagates on the supplied graph (no dynamic structure).
  - Clarify training vs. inference: during training you replace edges/nodes by gold after computing the loss; at test time, does the model rely solely on its predictions across timesteps? Provide exact protocols.
- Scalability:
  - Report runtime and memory as a function of number of nodes/edges; include experiments with sparse edge updates or local neighborhood updates; quantify how performance degrades with graph size.
- Rule discovery details:
  - Explicitly state whether Turing machine rules at test time are unseen; report per-horizon accuracy and failure modes; include qualitative analyses of learned graph updates.
- Reproducibility:
  - Include all hyperparameters, training schedules, and the exact preprocessing required for “direct-reference.” Clarify which bAbI tasks were altered and how.

Questions for the authors
- At inference time on bAbI, do you ever inject ground-truth graph elements, or is the full graph built purely from predictions? Please detail the exact train vs. test discrepancy and any exposure bias mitigation.
- How many node types and edge types (Y) are used per task, and how does performance/memory scale with them?
- For the Turing machine experiments, are test rules disjoint from training rules? If not, please rerun with disjoint rule sets and report results. Also, how does accuracy change from 6 to 20/30 steps per machine?
- How sensitive are bAbI results to removing the direct-reference channel or to noisy/entity-ambiguous references? Can the model handle multi-word entities without modifying the dataset?
- Can you provide ablations showing the necessity of each transformation, especially Tadd and TC, versus a fixed-graph GG-NN/GGS-NN?
- What fraction of edges are actually used at convergence? Could sparsity or neighborhood-restricted updates recover accuracy while improving complexity?

Overall, the paper is clearly written and proposes an appealing, modular framework for dynamic graph-state learning, but the heavy supervision and engineered shortcuts in the main evaluation, together with limited analysis of scalability and ablations, prevent a confident acceptance at this time.
