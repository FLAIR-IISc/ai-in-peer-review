Summary and contributions
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular framework that extends GG-NNs/GGS-NNs by enabling differentiable graph-structured intermediate state and output, with five composable transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional “direct reference” variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation. Graphs are made soft/differentiable via node strengths and multi-type edge belief matrices in [0,1], and node addition is trained using a set-to-sequence matching loss to handle permutation. The model is demonstrated on: (1) bAbI QA tasks, where the internal “knowledge” state is supervised as a graph at each step; (2) rule discovery on a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines, showing generalization to longer rollouts. The work is conceptually clear, modular, and shows the promise of explicit, interpretable graph states constructed from text.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and dataset modifications undermines the significance of the empirical claims and the fairness of comparisons; (2) Positioning and novelty are insufficient relative to a substantial literature on dynamic graph neural networks and neural graph generation; empirical evaluation remains limited to toy/synthetic benchmarks without stronger end-to-end results.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement. The high-level motivation—explicit, differentiable graph construction and manipulation—is compelling. However, the related work omits or underplays much of the post-2016 literature (e.g., Graph Networks framework, dynamic/temporal GNNs, neural graph generation like GraphRNN, JT-VAE, as well as neural state machine approaches). As a result, the novelty claim is not well calibrated: representing node/edge existence with continuous scores and composing message passing with learned edge/node updates has close analogs in later graph generative and dynamic GNN work. The name “Transformer” is also potentially misleading given the modern connotation (self-attention architectures) versus the proposed notion of “graph transformations.”
- Empirical evidence and supervision. The central bAbI results use strong graph supervision at every timestep and a direct reference mechanism that aligns words to node types via name matching; the graphs are further modified (e.g., adding history nodes; collapsing multi-word entities to single tokens). These choices materially simplify the problem and weaken claims of solving language-to-graph parsing and reasoning. The paper shows that without direct reference performance degrades markedly, but does not demonstrate strong end-to-end results without graph supervision or with more realistic entity grounding. Comparisons to MemNN and others are therefore not apples-to-apples (most baselines are end-to-end with 10k examples), and the paper modifies the dataset in ways that could advantage the proposed approach.
- Rigor and ablations. While the transformation details are described and code is reportedly available, key experimental details are sparse: model sizes, optimization settings, number of nodes/edges per instance, runtime/memory, and training stability are not discussed. There are no ablations quantifying the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Trepr), the impact of soft vs hard edges, or the node-set supervision loss. Claims about distributed computation advantages (e.g., pathfinding) are plausible but would benefit from targeted controlled experiments (e.g., varying graph sizes, sparsity, and propagation steps). 
- Scope and benchmarks. The evaluation is restricted to bAbI (toy), Rule 30, and small Turing machines. These are useful for probing capabilities but are not sufficient in 2025 to demonstrate broad impact. For instance, realistic text-to-graph tasks (AMR parsing, knowledge graph construction), dynamic relational reasoning on real datasets, or program induction benchmarks would make the case stronger. The rule discovery tasks are interesting, but the paper does not give detailed quantitative results (e.g., exact generalization rates across longer horizons, failure modes), nor comparisons to strong sequence or graph baselines.
- Scalability. The method’s O(|V|^2) compute/memory is acknowledged as a limitation. There is no empirical study of scalability (e.g., performance vs graph size, effect of sparse edge updates/propagation), which is central to the practicality of the approach.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Strengthen positioning. Situate GGT-NN against modern dynamic graph and graph generation literature (Graph Networks, Dynamic GNNs, GraphRNN, GRAN, variational and autoregressive graph generators, neural state machines) and clarify specific technical advances vs prior art.
- Clarify terminology. Consider renaming to avoid confusion with “Transformer” architectures; emphasize “graph transformation” rather than attention-based transformers.
- Fair comparisons. Either train baselines under similar strong supervision or present a clearly separate section for strongly supervised results. Also report GGT-NN with 10k examples to align with reported numbers from other works.
- Reduce supervision. Show results with partial or no graph supervision (e.g., only final answer supervision, or limited graph annotation), and ablate direct reference. Evaluate robustness when entity names are ambiguous or multiword.
- Add ablations and diagnostics. Quantify the contribution of each transformation and of the set-matching loss; report sensitivity to propagation steps, sparsity, and graph size. Include qualitative visualizations of induced graphs on bAbI without supervision.
- Broaden benchmarks. Include a realistic text-to-graph task (e.g., AMR/SMATCH scores, OpenIE to KGs), dynamic KG reasoning, or algorithmic tasks where graphs are not pre-specified.
- Report training details. Parameters, optimizer, learning rates, stopping criteria, seeds, compute cost, and memory footprint.

Questions for the authors
- How far can the model go without per-timestep graph supervision? Can you achieve competitive bAbI performance end-to-end (answer-only supervision), and what inductive biases/regularizers help?
- What is the exact computational and memory footprint versus graph size, and how does accuracy scale with number of nodes/edges? Can you incorporate sparsity in edge updates/propagation?
- How sensitive is performance to the direct reference mechanism? What happens with ambiguous or multiword mentions without preprocessing?
- Can you compare to modern dynamic graph/generative baselines and Graph Networks on the rule discovery tasks and pathfinding, including generalization to larger graphs/horizons?
- Why use the term “Transformer” here? Is there any self-attention over edges/nodes beyond the Trepr attention?
- Are the modified bAbI graph annotations and preprocessing scripts released to ensure reproducibility?
