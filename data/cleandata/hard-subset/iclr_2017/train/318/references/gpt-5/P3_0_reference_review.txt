Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular framework that extends GG-NNs/GGS-NNs with differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node-level state updates (including a direct-reference variant), edge updates, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation. Unlike prior GG/GGS-NNs that assume fixed graph input, GGT-NNs aim to build and update a graph state from text and then reason over it.
- The model is trained with strong supervision on intermediate graph states (including permutation-invariant node supervision via best matching over additions) and optionally uses “direct reference” signals tying tokens to node types.
- Empirical demonstrations include: (i) bAbI QA tasks from raw text, where the model constructs and uses graphs; (ii) rule-learning settings where the model simulates a 1D cellular automaton and 4-state, 2-symbol Turing machines by learning state transition rules from graph sequences; and (iii) a variant that reasons over sequences of constructed graphs.
- Claims include improved performance on specific bAbI tasks (notably pathfinding and induction) versus strong-supervision MemNNs, and good generalization in the rule-learning tasks.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) The main empirical claims rely on very strong supervision and oracle “direct reference” that substantially simplify the learning problem; the paper does not convincingly demonstrate end-to-end learning of structure from text without such supervision, nor fair comparisons under equivalent supervision.
2) Methodological novelty is incremental: the transformations instantiate standard message passing and MLP updates around a known GG/GGS-NN core. The central advance—differentiable add/update operators—comes with significant scalability issues and limited analysis. The evaluation is narrow (bAbI, small synthetic automata/Turing setups) and lacks ablations that would isolate the contribution of each component.

Supporting arguments
- Supervision and fairness: The bAbI experiments expose the generator’s “knowledge object” as a full graph at each time step and use it to supervise node addition, edge updates, and even substitute the ground-truth graph into the state during training. This is much stronger than typical end-to-end setups. The direct-reference mechanism further provides a near-oracle mapping from words to node types. The model without direct reference performs dramatically worse on many tasks, indicating the approach currently depends on these strong signals. Comparisons to MemNN/EntNet/MemN2N/DNC that were trained without graph-level supervision are therefore not apples-to-apples; only the MemNN with supporting-facts supervision is arguably comparable, and even there the forms of supervision differ.
- Contribution relative to prior work: The proposed transformations are a sensible, modular packaging of (i) node GRU updates, (ii) MLP-based edge updates over all node pairs, (iii) GG-NN-style propagation with learned per-edge-type messages, and (iv) attention aggregation. The paper rightly argues this enables text-to-graph construction and graph evolution. However, the conceptual step beyond GGS-NN (which already updates annotations over time and produces sequences) is not clearly delineated beyond the node/edge “construction” operators and soft strengths. There is limited theoretical or empirical analysis showing that these specific choices are necessary or superior to alternative structured memory mechanisms.
- Methodological clarity: The node-set supervision uses a max over permutations to align predictions to targets; how this is implemented (Hungarian algorithm? complexity? differentiability) is not specified, and the subsequent substitution of ground-truth nodes/edges into the state implies substantial teacher forcing. This raises concerns about exposure bias and mismatch at test time; there is no analysis of error propagation when the model must rely on its own constructed graphs.
- Scalability: Edge updates consider all node pairs, making time/space O(|V|^2Y). The paper acknowledges quadratic cost but provides no runtime/memory measurements, no experiments on larger graphs, and no sparsity/approximation strategies beyond high-level suggestions. This limits relevance to realistic settings where graphs can be large.
- Experimental scope and rigor:
  - bAbI is known to be limited; strong-supervision results there are not particularly compelling in 2025. The paper claims advantages on tasks naturally expressible as graphs (pathfinding, induction), which is plausible, but a deeper breakdown (e.g., path-length/generalization stress tests, robustness to paraphrase, unseen entities) is missing.
  - For automaton/Turing experiments, the evaluation metric is exact graph match across timesteps, which is fine, but training includes full supervision and short horizons; generalization to 20–30 steps is encouraging but still over similar distributions, and the models remain small-scale. No comparison to simpler baselines (e.g., pure GGS-NN with fixed nodes; rule-based inference layers; program induction baselines) is provided.
  - Ablations are missing: impact of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Trepr), supervision stages, and the direct-reference mechanism; number of propagation steps; message function parameterizations; and the effect of substituting ground truth vs. using predicted graphs during training.
- Reproducibility: Hyperparameters, network sizes, learning schedules, early stopping, and implementation choices are not reported in sufficient detail in the main text. The code link is appreciated, but a minimal, self-contained experimental section is expected.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a thorough ablation study: remove direct reference; vary strength of supervision (full/partial/none); compare teacher forcing vs scheduled sampling; analyze error compounding.
- Report computational costs and scaling curves; consider sparse edge updates (kNN, attention-based edge selection, or learned sparsification) and show their effect on accuracy and runtime.
- Evaluate on more realistic text-to-graph tasks (e.g., open IE, AMR-like extractions, knowledge base construction) where graph structure is not provided; even weakly supervised or semi-supervised setups would strengthen the case for broad applicability.
- Clarify and formalize the permutation-matching procedure during node addition (algorithmic details, gradients, complexity).
- Add comparisons to related graph reasoning and structured memory works beyond those cited (e.g., modern graph networks, relational reasoning modules, graph transformers) and position the contribution accordingly.
- Consider renaming “Transformer” to avoid confusion with attention-based Transformers; the current architecture is not Transformer-based.
- Improve clarity on how D(k) is constructed from text (tokenization, multiword entities, OOV handling, coreference), and whether the approach can handle unseen entity types.

Questions for the authors
1) How is the max over permutations implemented for node supervision? Is it solved with Hungarian matching per step? Do gradients flow through the selection, or is it treated as a non-differentiable alignment?
2) During training, after substituting ground-truth nodes/edges, are subsequent transformations conditioned on predicted or gold states? At test time, how severe is error propagation when only predicted graphs are used? Please provide quantitative analysis.
3) Can you report performance without direct reference and without graph-state supervision on bAbI (and other datasets), and compare fairly to end-to-end baselines?
4) What are the time/memory costs as a function of number of nodes and edge types, and the largest graphs you successfully trained on? Any sparsity tricks attempted?
5) How sensitive is performance to the number of propagation steps and to the parameterization of fset/freset and message functions? Any signs of over-smoothing or vanishing gradients over long propagation?
6) Can the model handle unseen entity names/types at test time, or is the node-type inventory fixed? How would you adapt the method for open-world entity extraction?

Overall, the paper presents an interesting modularization of graph construction and reasoning, but requires stronger empirical validation under realistic supervision, clearer methodological details, and scalability evidence.
