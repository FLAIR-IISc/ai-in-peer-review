Summary of contributions
- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), which maintain an explicit, differentiable graph-structured state with node/edge “beliefs” and modular transformations: node addition, node state updates (with a direct-reference variant), edge updates, message-passing propagation (GG-NN style), and graph-level aggregation.
- Presents a generic pipeline to incrementally construct/modify a graph from text and answer queries, with algorithmic details and pseudocode.
- Introduces strong intermediate supervision for graph construction (node/edge losses with permutation-invariant matching for new nodes) and teacher forcing by substituting ground-truth graph elements during training.
- Empirical evaluation: (i) bAbI tasks (text to answer) with and without direct reference; (ii) synthetic rule-discovery tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton; 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines) predicting next graph state and some longer-horizon generalization.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) The main empirical claims rely on heavy, strong supervision (gold graphs at each timestep and direct-reference signals) and teacher forcing, making comparisons to standard end-to-end baselines not meaningful; it does not demonstrate the method can learn graph structure from text without privileged signals. (2) The technical novelty over GG-NN/GGS-NN and general message-passing GNNs appears incremental (a differentiable relaxation of graph structure and modular transforms), without rigorous ablations or compelling evidence on realistic datasets, scalability, or robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Methodological concerns:
  - Strong supervision and teacher forcing: The model is trained with full gold graphs at every step and even substitutes gold edges/nodes for further processing. This is a substantially easier setting than end-to-end QA, undermining claims about extracting structured knowledge from unstructured text. Most cited baselines are end-to-end; the only strongly supervised comparator is MemNN with supporting facts, which is not comparable to providing the complete graph at each step.
  - Direct reference: The mechanism maps tokens to node types (entity names) and supplies per-type inputs, effectively providing gold entity grounding. Reported gains with direct reference further highlight reliance on engineered supervision unlikely to be available in real scenarios.
  - Fairness of comparisons: Results on bAbI are presented alongside end-to-end models without controlling for supervision level. This conflates the effect of privileged information with model capability.
  - Limited novelty: The core is a GG-NN/GGS-NN style message passing with added modules for edge update and node addition plus differentiable relaxations (node/edge strengths). Such components are closely related to existing gated/message-passing architectures. The paper does not articulate a clear theoretical or empirical advantage that is robust under comparable training regimes.
  - Lack of ablations: No systematic study of which transformations are necessary/sufficient, effect sizes of edge-update vs propagation vs aggregation, or the contribution of teacher forcing vs weaker supervision. Only a coarse comparison of direct reference vs not is shown.
  - Scalability: The method is O(|V|^2) in time/memory per step due to dense pairwise edge updates and propagation. There is no empirical scaling study (e.g., graphs with hundreds/thousands of nodes, sparsity, or runtime/memory profiling). Claims of distributed computation are tempered by quadratic costs.
  - Evaluation breadth/rigor:
    - bAbI is synthetic and small. No experiments on realistic text-to-graph extraction or QA corpora where gold graphs are unavailable.
    - Rule 30 and Turing machine experiments are synthetic; although interesting, they do not demonstrate robustness to noise, larger alphabets/state spaces, or long horizons at scale. The generalization analyses are only briefly summarized; exact quantitative results, variance, and error modes are not fully detailed.
    - No multiple seeds or confidence intervals to assess stability.
  - Potential leakage: Using the generator’s “knowledge object” as supervision and substituting gold structures during training risks entangling evaluation with the simulator’s internal representation. It is unclear how much of the task is solved by privileged access.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide an end-to-end setting without graph-level supervision and without direct reference, with careful tuning and capacity, and compare fairly to strong recent baselines under the same supervision level. If strong supervision is the focus, compare to equally supervised baselines (e.g., models trained with gold support graphs/facts) and quantify the benefit of explicit graphs.
- Add thorough ablations: remove each transformation; vary the strength/availability of supervision (full, partial, none); study the impact of teacher forcing vs scheduled sampling; measure when the model learns meaningful graphs without substitution.
- Report computational metrics: wall-clock time, memory usage, and scaling with |V| and |E|; explore sparse edge updates and attention-based neighbor sampling to mitigate quadratic costs.
- Clarify and quantify hyperparameters (D, N, Y, T, K), propagation depths, and parameter counts; report training details (optimizer, LR, schedules) and multiple seeds with confidence intervals.
- Expand evaluation to realistic datasets where gold graph states are not available (e.g., event or relation extraction with distant supervision; AMR/knowledge-graph construction; real pathfinding on noisy text-derived maps). Demonstrating competitive end-to-end performance would substantiate claims about learning graphical state transitions from unstructured input.
- Provide more detailed results on the rule-discovery tasks: exact numbers for long-horizon generalization, per-edge/node accuracies, sensitivity to initial conditions, and diversity/complexity of sampled Turing machines.
- Improve positioning vs prior art beyond GG-NN/GGS-NN, including recent message-passing graph networks and graph transformers, and clarify distinctive contributions (e.g., learnable structure induction with minimal supervision).

Questions for the authors
- How well does GGT-NN perform on bAbI without any graph supervision and without direct reference? Can it learn to induce the structure purely from text? Please provide results and analysis.
- What proportion of the bAbI performance remains when you do not substitute ground-truth nodes/edges during training (i.e., no teacher forcing), or when you only supervise at the final answer?
- How are node types defined and fixed across tasks? In settings where entity names are multiword or ambiguous, how robust is direct reference? Can the model discover types/entities?
- What are typical graph sizes (|V|, |E|) per task, the number of edge types Y, propagation steps T, and the resulting training/inference time and memory? Any results with sparse updates?
- Can you provide ablations showing the necessity of TC (edge update) versus relying on message passing over a fixed evolving adjacency, and the necessity of Tadd (node addition) versus preallocating nodes?
- For the Turing machine experiments, how are rules sampled? Do you include degenerate/hard machines? What is the exact long-horizon accuracy (20/30 steps), with variance over seeds?
- Are results averaged over multiple runs? Please report mean ± std and learning curves to assess stability.
- Could you release datasets and scripts to reproduce the supervision signals (graphs), to ensure reproducibility?
