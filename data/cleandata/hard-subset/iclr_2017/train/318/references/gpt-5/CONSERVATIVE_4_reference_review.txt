Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of GG-NNs/GGS-NNs that maintains an explicit, differentiable graph-structured state and applies a suite of trainable graph transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional direct-reference variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation. This allows the model to incrementally build and modify a graph from text and then answer queries or output a graph.
- The model is demonstrated on: (i) bAbI tasks, where authors expose the generator’s “knowledge object” as a supervision signal to train the internal graph; and (ii) rule discovery tasks (1D cellular automaton Rule 30; and 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines), where the model learns to update graph states over time.
- The work emphasizes composability of transformations, differentiable graph construction, and claims good performance on many bAbI tasks, especially pathfinding and induction.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and dataset modifications substantially weakens the central claim (learning structured state from text). The bAbI evaluation leverages access to the generator’s knowledge graph, adds auxiliary history nodes, uses direct name matching for “direct reference,” and even modifies entity surface forms—none of which reflect the intended “from text” setting, and make comparisons to prior work unfair or inconclusive.
2) Insufficient empirical rigor relative to established baselines and ablations. There is no thorough comparison against strong graph baselines under matched supervision, minimal ablation of the proposed transformations, nor an unsupervised/weakly supervised variant. Rule discovery tasks lack baseline comparisons. Overall, the evidence does not convincingly support the broader claims.

Supporting arguments
- Supervision and fairness: The bAbI results depend on full graph-level supervision at each step (including node/edge labels and strengths) and a direct-reference mechanism keyed to exact string matches of entity type names. Moreover, the authors alter bAbI graphs to add linked-list history nodes and simplify multi-word entities to single tokens. These choices give the model privileged information and reshape the task; yet comparisons are made to MemNN, MemN2N, DNC, etc., typically trained end-to-end without such supervision. This confounds conclusions about the advantages of explicit graph state. The authors partially acknowledge this, but the empirical narrative still leans on headline accuracy.
- Lack of ablations: The architecture introduces multiple transformations (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr). There is no systematic ablation to quantify which components matter, how sensitive performance is to “intermediate propagation,” or the impact of replacing teacher-forced graph supervision with predicted graphs during training. The large performance gap with/without direct reference suggests the method is brittle without extra supervision; this isn’t analyzed.
- Rule discovery tasks: While interesting, they are set up with an explicit, clean graph encoding of the current state and rules. The model’s success is unsurprising and not benchmarked against simpler message-passing baselines (e.g., standard GG-NN/GNN variants) or classical programmatic simulators. Generalization tests (to 20/30 steps) are limited; no scaling to larger tapes/graphs is explored; and no robustness analysis (noise, partial observability) is provided.
- Claims vs. evidence: The paper claims the method “learns complex update rules” and can “incrementally construct a graph given natural language input.” However, on bAbI the learning is strongly teacher-forced to the ground-truth graph; on rule tasks the input is already graph-structured. There is no demonstration of learning graph structure without explicit graph supervision (e.g., weakly supervised or end-to-end from QA labels only), which would be necessary to substantiate the central promise.
- Methodological details and efficiency: The paper notes quadratic time/space complexity but provides no profiling, memory usage, or scaling study. Hyperparameters, training stability, and inference latency are not reported. Teacher-forcing decisions (replacing predicted nodes/edges with gold during training) raise exposure-bias concerns that are not addressed.
- Related work positioning: The paper builds on GG-NN/GGS-NN but does not compare against them on the graph-structured simulation tasks, nor against contemporaneous graph generation/scene graph models under matched conditions. Several references are poorly formatted; the literature discussion omits more recent graph generation methods that also construct graphs with neural components.

Suggestions to improve (not prerequisites for this decision)
- Provide a rigorous ablation: remove each transformation, analyze direct reference, intermediate propagation, and supervision strength (full graph vs. partial vs. none). Report performance, data efficiency, and stability.
- Evaluate under matched supervision. For bAbI, show results with only QA labels (no graph supervision, no direct reference), and optionally with weaker hints. Compare to MemN2N/EntNet/DNC under identical supervision regimes.
- For rule tasks, include baselines: standard message-passing GNNs on the same graph encodings, and a simple symbolic simulator as an upper bound. Probe scaling to larger tapes/longer horizons and robustness to noise/partial observation.
- Avoid dataset modifications or clearly segregate them; quantify their impact. If entity renaming/history nodes are necessary, justify why and analyze sensitivity.
- Report computational cost, memory footprint, and complexity vs. graph size. Consider sparsity or neighborhood sampling to mitigate O(|V|^2).
- Improve clarity and reproducibility: consolidated hyperparameters, training schedules, seeds, and verified code. Fix reference formatting.

Questions for the authors
1) Can you provide results on bAbI without graph-level supervision and without direct reference? What is the performance gap, and can you bridge it with weaker auxiliary losses or regularization?
2) How much of the gain on task 19 (pathfinding) remains if you remove dataset modifications and evaluate end-to-end from text, compared against GGS-NN with pre-parsed graphs and against DNC under matched supervision?
3) What is the exact computational/memory cost as a function of nodes and edge types, and how large a graph can you handle in practice? Any experiments with sparsity or subgraph sampling?
4) In training, you replace predicted nodes/edges with gold (teacher forcing). What happens if you train with scheduled sampling or fully on-model predictions? How does this affect test-time rollouts and error accumulation?
5) For the Turing machine setting, how well does the model extrapolate to longer tapes or more steps than seen in training? Can it handle different numbers of states/symbols without retraining?
6) Which components were most important per your internal experiments (e.g., Th,direct vs. TC vs. Tprop)? Please provide an ablation table and learning curves.

Overall, the paper has an interesting idea—explicit, trainable graph-state construction/manipulation—but the current empirical evidence relies heavily on strong supervision and task alterations, with limited ablations and unfair baseline comparisons. I encourage the authors to address these issues; with stronger methodology, the contribution could be impactful.
