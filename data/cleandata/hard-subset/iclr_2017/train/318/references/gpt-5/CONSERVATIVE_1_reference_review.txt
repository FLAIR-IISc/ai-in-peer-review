Summary
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular framework that extends GG-NN/GGS-NN by adding a set of differentiable graph transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional direct-reference mechanism), edge update, propagation, and aggregation. The model can build and update a graph-structured latent state from text, and then answer queries via additional graph processing. Experiments on bAbI (with strong supervision at the graph level) show high accuracy on most tasks, notably strong performance on pathfinding. Additional synthetic “rule discovery” tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 4-state Turing machines) show the model can learn graph-based transition rules and generalize to longer horizons.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision (oracle graph states and entity-type direct references) undermines the central claim of learning to construct and update graphs from unstructured text; comparisons to end-to-end baselines are not commensurate.
2) Incremental novelty over GG-NN/GGS-NN with limited methodological validation; empirical evaluation is confined to synthetic datasets (bAbI and toy automata), with insufficient ablations and rigor to substantiate broad claims.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The work is positioned as extending GGS-NN to allow graph-structured intermediate states and graph construction from text. However, the core components (message passing/propagation, attention-based aggregation, GRU-style node updates, edge parameterizations) are established. The main novelty is the composition of differentiable graph transformations, node/edge strengths in [0,1], and a training routine that uses graph-level supervision and permutation matching for unordered node sets. This is a reasonable engineering extension but conceptually close to prior GG-NN/GGS-NN and contemporary message-passing frameworks.

- Supervision and fairness of comparisons: For bAbI, the model is trained with the generator’s “knowledge object” converted to graphs, and the training repeatedly substitutes ground-truth nodes/edges (including a max-over-permutations node addition objective). The direct-reference mechanism also uses oracle string matches of entity types. By contrast, most baselines cited (MemN2N, EntNet, DNC, DMN+) are trained end-to-end without such supervision. The paper acknowledges this, but then draws conclusions about the superiority of graphical representations that are not well-supported given the uneven supervision. There is no comparison against methods that also receive equivalent oracle graph/slot supervision, nor against text-to-graph parsers on the same setting.

- Evidence and rigor: 
  - No ablations isolating the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr) or of design choices (direct reference vs. none, intermediate propagation, strength variables, differentiable vs. discrete edges). The only ablation is with/without direct reference; unsurprisingly, direct reference helps substantially.
  - No reporting of variance across random seeds, hyperparameter sensitivity, or model size. 
  - Limited analysis of computational complexity. The paper notes quadratic scaling but provides no empirical profiling, memory footprint, or sparsity strategies. Tasks used are small; scalability to realistic graphs is untested.
  - The “rule discovery” tasks are interesting sanity checks but remain toy problems. Generalization to 20–30 steps is limited evidence; there is no stress test on larger tapes/automata families or distribution shifts.

- Methodological questions:
  - The training procedure replaces predicted nodes/edges with ground truth after computing losses. This makes it unclear whether the model truly learns stable long-horizon transformations without teacher forcing. At inference, what is the degradation when supervision is absent at intermediate steps?
  - The permutation matching for node addition adopts the “order matters” trick; there is no study of its stability, convergence, or impact on downstream performance.
  - Direct reference effectively supplies a strong pointer/slot mechanism via exact string matches. Realistic text requires entity linking and coreference; the paper does not explore robustness when direct reference is noisy or absent.

- External validity: All experiments are on synthetic data. There is no demonstration on real-world text-to-graph construction (e.g., AMR/scene graphs/knowledge graphs) or on standard graph reasoning benchmarks where graph construction from text is necessary. Claims about interpretability and downstream use of the explicit graph are not substantiated with applications.

Constructive feedback
- Provide a fair comparison regime: either train baselines with the same strong supervision (e.g., give MemNN/EntNet access to oracle graphs or supervision on relevant edges/nodes) or train GGT-NN without oracle graphs/direct reference. Alternatively, include semi-supervised variants to bridge the gap (e.g., a consistency loss on predicted graphs, weak supervision from QA-only).
- Add thorough ablations:
  - Remove/alter each transformation and report performance.
  - Study the effect of node/edge strengths vs. binary edges.
  - Quantify the benefit of intermediate propagation and attention choices.
- Report training details and robustness: seeds, variance, hyperparameters, optimizer, model sizes, early stopping, and regularization. Include parameter counts and runtime/memory profiling; show scaling with number of nodes/edges. Explore sparse adjacency and neighborhood sampling.
- Evaluate on more realistic settings: a small real IE or semantic parsing task with graph outputs, or text-based navigation tasks where graphs must be constructed from language without oracle supervision.
- Analyze inference without teacher forcing: roll out the model without substituting ground-truth graphs and measure error accumulation.
- Clarify the direct-reference mechanism’s assumptions and evaluate with noisy or learned entity linking.

Questions for the authors
1) How does the model perform on bAbI when trained end-to-end from text and answers only, without graph-level supervision and without direct reference? Can you report accuracy and training stability?
2) Are your comparisons controlled for total supervision and parameter count? Can you provide baselines that receive equivalent oracle signals (e.g., sentence-level/edge-level supervision)?
3) What are the typical graph sizes in your experiments, and what are the runtime/memory requirements per step? Can you demonstrate sparse variants or neighborhood sampling to mitigate O(|V|^2) scaling?
4) During training you substitute ground-truth nodes/edges after computing losses. At test time, do you ever roll out without oracle substitutions? If so, how does performance change over long sequences?
5) How sensitive is performance to the node addition ordering and the max-over-permutations loss? Do you observe mode collapse to suboptimal orderings affecting downstream reasoning?
6) Can the direct-reference mechanism be learned (e.g., via attention over tokens) rather than relying on exact string matches? What is the drop if references are noisy?
7) Please provide variance across multiple runs and ablations on Tadd/Th/TC/Tprop/Trepr to clarify which components are essential.
