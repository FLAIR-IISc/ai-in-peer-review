Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a differentiable framework for constructing and transforming graph-structured internal states from unstructured inputs (e.g., text). The core novelty is a set of composable, trainable graph transformations: node addition with soft existence/typing, node state updates (incl. direct-reference updates), edge updates, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation.
- The model integrates a recurrent text encoder with these transformations to incrementally build a latent graph, then either output the graph or answer queries from it. Training can be strongly supervised by providing gold graphs (with a permutation-invariant loss for new nodes) or supervised only on answers.
- Experiments show strong results on bAbI (notably pathfinding and induction), and two “rule discovery” tasks: learning Rule 30 cellular automaton dynamics and simulating 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines from their graph encodings, with some generalization to longer rollouts.
- The work argues for interpretable, explicit graph states vs. implicit memory (MemN2N, DNC).

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) A bold, novel formulation of differentiable graph construction and transformation that goes beyond fixed-structure GNNs; (2) evidence that the framework is general (text-to-graph QA, automata, Turing machines), with clear interpretability benefits. I am willing to overlook limitations in supervision and scale given the conceptual advance.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem addressed
- Learning graph-structured state transitions from unstructured inputs, including adding/deleting nodes/edges with uncertainty, and using the evolving graph for downstream reasoning. This fills a gap between fixed-graph GNNs and memory architectures with implicit structure.

2) Motivation and placement
- Well motivated relative to GG-NN/GGS-NN: prior work assumes fixed graphs or graph-structured inputs; here the structure is learned and modified.
- Clear contrast to memory networks/DNC/HAM: those have implicit structure and limited parallel access; GGT-NN has explicit graph states and fully distributed propagation.
- Related work section is thorough for this line of work; the framing around interpretability and distributed computation is compelling.

3) Do the results support the claims?
- bAbI: With strong supervision and direct reference, the model reaches ≥95% on 19/20 tasks, with notable gains on pathfinding (task 19) and induction (task 16), where a graph is natural. Without direct reference, accuracy drops substantially—an informative ablation.
- Rule discovery: Accurately models Rule 30 and 4-state Turing machines and generalizes to longer sequences for many cases, indicating it does learn transition dynamics on graphs.
- Caveats: The strongest bAbI results use heavy supervision (full gold graphs and direct reference). Training uses “teacher forcing” at the graph level (replacing predicted structures with gold), which may create exposure bias. Still, given the novelty, the empirical evidence is sufficiently convincing.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not decisive)
- Clarify terminology: “Transformer” here means a composition of graph transformations, not the attention-based Transformer architecture. A brief disclaimer up front would avoid confusion.
- Supervision strength and exposure bias: Please quantify performance when training only on final answers (no gold graphs), or with partial graph supervision, and measure degradation vs. fully supervised training. Reporting test-time rollout stability without graph clamping would strengthen claims.
- Ablations: Show which transformations matter (e.g., Tadd/TC/Tprop/Th/Th,direct), the impact of node-strength and edge-strength modeling, and the role of Trepr during Tadd. Also ablate intermediate propagation.
- Scalability: Provide wall-clock, memory usage, and max graph sizes. Since the method is O(|V|^2) per step, experiments with sparse edges or neighbor sampling would be valuable; even a small-scale prototype would help.
- Data modeling clarity for bAbI: The paper states “each entity is assigned to a node type,” yet also creates “record” nodes. Please clarify the distinction between “node types” and “nodes,” and how entities/mentions/records map to each.
- Robustness of direct reference: Analyze sensitivity to noisy surface forms and ambiguous references; explore learned attention over tokens as a weaker, more realistic alternative.
- Generality: Consider at least one real IE/QA dataset where graph-structured outputs are meaningful (or release the produced graphs for bAbI to illustrate interpretability).
- Training details: Provide hyperparameters, hidden sizes, number of edge types, optimizer, learning rates, early stopping, and initialization. Some references need formatting cleanup.

Questions for the authors
1) Training without graph supervision: How well does GGT-NN train when only the final answer is supervised? Can partial supervision (e.g., only edges or only a subset of timesteps) suffice?
2) Exposure bias: Since you substitute gold graphs during training, how do errors compound at test time when the model must rely on its own predictions? Can you report answer accuracy vs. timestep depth without clamping?
3) Direct reference: How is D(k) constructed in practice when entity names are multi-word or ambiguous? Could learned token-level attention approximate direct reference without manual matching?
4) Node types vs. nodes: Do node types correspond to entity categories or entity identities in bAbI? How many node types are there, and how is the constraint sum_j x_v,j=1 enforced (softmax)?
5) Scaling: What is the largest |V| and |E| you handled, and what were the compute/memory footprints? Any preliminary results with sparse message passing?
6) Edge update semantics: fset/freset can toggle edges; do you observe oscillations? Did you try parameterizations that directly predict C via sigmoid with temporal smoothing?
7) Turing machines: Are rule edges given as input (fixed) or predicted? How far can the model generalize in rollouts (median/max steps) before failure modes appear?
8) Deletions/forgetting: Beyond toggling edges, do you have tasks that require removing nodes or decaying strengths, and how well does the model handle long-term consistency?

Overall, this is an ambitious and original direction—learning to build and transform graphs with explicit, interpretable states. Despite reliance on strong supervision and scalability concerns, the conceptual contribution and the breadth of demonstrations merit acceptance.
