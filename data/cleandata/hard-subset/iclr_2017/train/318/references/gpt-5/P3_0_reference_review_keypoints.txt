- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) to construct and update graph states from text using differentiable node/edge add/update, propagation, and aggregation operators.
- Trains the model with strong supervision on intermediate graph states and optionally uses a near-oracle “direct reference” tying tokens to node types.
- Empirically evaluated on bAbI QA from raw text, rule-learning (cellular automata and small Turing machines), and reasoning over constructed graph sequences.
- Claims improved performance on certain bAbI tasks (pathfinding, induction) and good generalization in rule-learning tasks.
- Decision: Reject.
- Main empirical concern: performance depends heavily on very strong supervision and oracle direct-reference signals, undermining claims of end-to-end structure learning from text.
- Methodological novelty judged incremental: components largely instantiate known GG/GGS-NN mechanisms; differentiable add/update operators lack clear necessity or advantage.
- Scalability issues: edge updates are quadratic in nodes (O(|V|^2Y)) with no runtime/memory measurements or sparsity strategies reported.
- Supervision/fairness concerns: bAbI training uses full graph supervision and substitutes ground-truth graphs during training, making comparisons to end-to-end baselines unfair.
- Permutation-matching for node supervision is underspecified (implementation, complexity, differentiability unclear), raising concerns about teacher forcing and exposure bias.
- Experimental scope limited: small synthetic domains, no stress tests (path-length, paraphrase robustness), and missing comparisons to simpler/other baselines.
- Missing ablations: effects of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop, Trepr), direct-reference, propagation steps, message parametrizations, and teacher forcing vs. predicted graphs are not analyzed.
- Reproducibility lacking: key hyperparameters, learning schedules, and implementation choices are not sufficiently detailed in the main text.
- Suggested improvements: add thorough ablations, report computational costs and scaling, try sparse edge updates, evaluate on realistic text-to-graph tasks, and clarify permutation-matching details.
- Additional suggested fixes: compare to more recent graph/network baselines, consider renaming “Transformer” to avoid confusion, and clarify token-to-node construction (tokenization, multiword, OOV, coreference).
- Key questions for authors request precise answers on permutation-matching implementation, teacher forcing vs. prediction during training, ablated performances without direct-reference/graph supervision, time/memory scaling, sensitivity to propagation and parameterization, and handling unseen entity types.
