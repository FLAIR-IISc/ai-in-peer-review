1) Summary of contributions (being positive)
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a modular framework that extends GG-NN/GGS-NN to support differentiable graph construction and modification from unstructured inputs. Key ideas include: (i) a differentiable graph representation with soft node types and strengths and soft, typed edges; (ii) five composable graph transformations (node addition, node-state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable learning graphical state transitions; (iii) a training scheme that supervises both node and edge formation with permutation-invariant losses for node sets; and (iv) a text-to-graph pipeline with “direct reference” inputs to update specific node types based on words. Empirically, the paper shows competitive or strong performance on a wide range of bAbI tasks, especially on pathfinding and induction, and demonstrates that the model can learn graph dynamics such as 1D cellular automata (Rule 30) and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines with some generalization to longer horizons. The work is clearly motivated, technically detailed (with appendices), and emphasizes interpretability via explicit graphs.

2) Decision
Reject. Main reasons:
- The core empirical claims rely on strong supervision with ground-truth graphs at every timestep and handcrafted “direct reference,” making comparisons with end-to-end baselines not apples-to-apples and limiting external validity.
- Insufficient ablations and analyses to establish that the modular transformations are necessary and effective without privileged supervision, or that the approach scales and generalizes beyond the curated settings.

3) Supporting arguments
- Use of strong supervision and teacher forcing on the graphical state: The method “requires additional supervision” and trains with the correct graph at each timestep, replacing predicted nodes/edges with ground truth during the forward pass. This dramatically simplifies the problem and can hide exposure-bias issues; it also diverges from real-world scenarios where such dense graph annotations are unavailable. The paper presents no end-to-end results without graph-level supervision (only with vs. without “direct reference”), so it is unclear whether the model can learn to construct graphs from raw text in practice.
- Comparisons are not like-for-like: Many reported baselines (MemN2N, EntNet, DNC, etc.) are trained end-to-end without strong supervision, but GGT-NN (and MemNN) use privileged signals. The paper acknowledges this but still draws conclusions about relative superiority on subsets of tasks. The comparisons on bAbI are thus difficult to interpret as evidence of methodological advantage rather than supervision advantage or task-specific design.
- Dataset modifications and tailored engineering: The bAbI knowledge graphs are obtained from the generator’s “Knowledge” object and further modified (e.g., multi-word entities collapsed, additional linked-list history nodes added). Direct reference relies on string matches between tokens and node-type names. These changes reduce ecological validity and may leak task structure into the model.
- Missing ablations/analyses: The contributions span several transformations (Tadd, Th, Th,direct, TC, Tprop, Trepr) plus supervision choices (edge vs. node losses, permutation matching). The paper lacks systematic ablation to quantify which components are necessary. There is also no study of scheduled sampling vs. full teacher forcing, or the effect of removing ground-truth graph replacement.
- Limited generality of rule-learning demos: The cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments are interesting and encouraging, but settings are constrained (Rule 30; 2-symbol 4-state TMs; short horizons) and do not compare to alternative dynamic-graph inference methods. Details on splits, distribution of machines, and robustness to longer horizons/rules are limited.
- Efficiency concerns: The method is O(|V|^2) due to dense message passing and pairwise edge updates. While acknowledged, there is no empirical scaling study (time/memory vs. graph size), limiting practical significance.
- Nomenclature and relation to broader literature: Calling the model a “Transformer” is confusing given the well-established Transformer architecture; the paper’s “transformations” are graph operations, not attention-based Transformers. Positioning vs. dynamic graph learning (e.g., Neural Relational Inference, dynamic GNNs) is limited.

4) Suggestions to improve (constructive; not all are required for acceptance)
- Provide end-to-end results without graph-level supervision on bAbI (and/or other text datasets), at least partial supervision regimes (e.g., edges only, nodes only, subset of steps) and regularization strategies that enable learning meaningful graphs from text. Include scheduled sampling to mitigate exposure bias from teacher forcing on graph states.
- Fair comparisons: Compare to baselines with access to similar supervision. Also, include a strong “gold graph + GG-NN” baseline to isolate the gains from dynamic graph construction vs. using prebuilt graphs.
- Ablations: Quantify the contribution of each transformation (Tadd, TC, Tprop), the direct-reference mechanism, and the permutation-invariant node-loss. Report performance when removing intermediate propagation and when using sparse edges.
- Scaling study: Report runtime/memory vs. number of nodes/edges; explore sparse message passing or neighborhood sampling to reduce O(|V|^2) overhead.
- Generalization and robustness: For Turing machines, vary number of states/symbols and demonstrate generalization to much longer rollouts with clear error modes. For the automaton, test broader rules (e.g., other elementary rules).
- Clarify dataset modifications: Precisely document all bAbI changes (entity renaming, added history nodes), and test the unmodified tasks to show robustness. Discuss how direct reference would work with OOV entities or ambiguous mentions.
- Naming and positioning: Consider renaming to avoid confusion with “Transformers.” Expand related work to include dynamic-graph inference methods (e.g., NRI) and recent graph construction from text.

Questions for the authors
- Test-time protocol: Do you rely on direct reference via exact string matching at test time? Is the set of node types fixed a priori? How do you handle unseen entities or synonyms?
- Supervision and train/test mismatch: During training, you replace predicted nodes/edges with ground truth after computing loss. At inference, there is no replacement. Did you observe exposure bias? Did you try scheduled sampling or partial replacement?
- End-to-end feasibility: Can the model learn on bAbI without any graph supervision? If not, what minimal supervision suffices (e.g., edge existence only, occasional steps)?
- Edge types and schema: How many edge types Y are used per task, and how were they chosen? To what extent are these choices handcrafted for bAbI?
- Baseline with gold graphs: If you feed the gold graphs to a GG-NN/GGS-NN, how does performance compare to GGT-NN? This would isolate the benefits of dynamic construction from text.
- Scaling: What are the typical numbers of nodes/edges and the compute/memory footprint? Any attempts with sparsity or neighborhood sampling?
- Rule-learning splits: For the Turing machine experiments, how are rules split across train/test? Do you hold out rules entirely, and how does performance change for 20–100 step rollouts?
- Reproducibility: Is the provided code sufficient to reproduce all results, including data transformations for bAbI and hyperparameters? Please enumerate seeds, hardware, and training times.
