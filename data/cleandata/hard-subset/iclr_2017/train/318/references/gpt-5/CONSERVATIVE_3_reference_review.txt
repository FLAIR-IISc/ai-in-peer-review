Summary
The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NN), a framework that augments GG-NNs/GGS-NNs with a differentiable set of graph transformations: node addition, node state update (with an optional “direct reference” variant), edge update, propagation, and aggregation. The model incrementally constructs and updates a graph from text and then answers queries using the learned graph state. The paper evaluates on (i) bAbI tasks, training with strong supervision by exposing the generator’s “knowledge graph,” and (ii) two rule-discovery settings (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol 4-state Turing machines), showing that the model can learn to update graphical states and generalize to longer rollouts. The work aims to provide a flexible, interpretable, graph-structured memory for reasoning over structured data from unstructured inputs.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Heavy reliance on strong supervision and task-specific graph engineering undermines the central claim that the model learns to build and reason over graphs from text. Much of the burden is shifted to the supervision (exposing the bAbI generator’s knowledge object, direct string-based entity matching, task-specific graph modifications).
2) Empirical validation is limited and not rigorously comparable to baselines: many reported improvements are under unequal supervision and with non-standard task preprocessing, and there is no thorough ablation or analysis to isolate where gains come from.

Supporting arguments
- Supervision and engineering: The bAbI experiments use strong supervision at each step with gold graphs, including node/edge labels and strengths, and the training procedure replaces predicted nodes/edges with gold (“teacher forcing” on the graph). Direct reference depends on string matching to predeclared node types. Several tasks are modified (single-word entities, added history linked-lists for temporal tasks). These choices make the problem substantially easier and deviate from standard bAbI evaluation, complicating fair comparison and reducing external validity.
- Comparisons not apples-to-apples: The paper compares GGT-NN (strongly supervised with gold graphs and direct references) to MemN2N/EntNet/NTM/DNC trained end-to-end without such supervision. Even the MemNN baseline has different supervision (relevant sentences vs full structured graphs). Reported wins on some tasks (e.g., pathfinding) are unsurprising given the design bias toward graphs and the injected structure. A careful control with equal supervision or a no-graph-supervision setting is missing.
- Limited scope of tasks: All datasets are synthetic/toy (bAbI, Rule 30, small Turing machines). There are no real-world text-to-graph or graph-reasoning benchmarks (e.g., AMR/IE, knowledge base completion, procedural text). Claims of broad applicability remain speculative.
- Lack of ablations and analysis: Although the paper toggles “direct reference” and “intermediate propagation,” there is no systematic ablation of each transformation (Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr), the supervision regime (with/without graph substitution, partial supervision), or the matching strategy for unordered nodes. It is unclear how much each component contributes, whether the model can learn without gold graphs, or how fragile it is to noise in entity linking.
- Methodological details needing clarity: The node-set matching loss maximizes over permutations but implementation details (e.g., Hungarian algorithm, computational cost, stability) are not described. Training hyperparameters, number of propagation steps, edge-type inventories per task, and sensitivity analyses are limited, affecting reproducibility.
- Efficiency concerns: The paper acknowledges quadratic time/space in the number of nodes, but provides no empirical profiling. Without sparsity or pruning, scalability to realistic graphs is doubtful.
- Generalization: The rule-discovery tasks show some length generalization, but settings are narrow (fixed 4-state TMs, 2 symbols). It is unclear how the method scales to broader rules, unseen state alphabets, or larger graphs.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Provide a genuinely end-to-end setting without access to the generator’s knowledge object. If strong supervision is central, position the paper as supervised graph induction and compare fairly to equally supervised baselines (e.g., GGNNs over gold graphs, seq2seq-to-graph parsers).
- Standardize bAbI evaluation. Avoid task modifications that change difficulty (or report both original and modified). If history nodes are added, justify and compare to models that encode the same information without structural hacks.
- Report comprehensive ablations: remove each transformation; train without substituting gold graphs; vary the amount of graph supervision; measure the impact of direct reference versus learned entity linking.
- Add real-world benchmarks (even small-scale) to demonstrate the utility of learning graphical state transitions from natural text without gold graphs (e.g., AMR parsing to graph + QA over the induced graph, or a knowledge extraction task).
- Provide efficiency metrics (runtime/memory vs. number of nodes/edges), and explore sparsity (learned or enforced) to mitigate quadratic costs.
- Improve methodological clarity: specify how permutation matching is implemented, whether gradients propagate through the max, details of fadd/fset/freset architectures, hyperparameters, and training schedules. Release code and scripts for graph conversion.

Questions for the authors
1) How is the permutation over new nodes computed in Lnode? Do you use Hungarian matching or brute force? How does this scale and how do you handle non-differentiability of the max?
2) During training, you replace predicted graph components with gold. At test time, the model must rely on its own predictions. Did you observe exposure bias? What is performance without graph substitution (“free-running”)?
3) How many and which edge types are used per bAbI task? Are they hand-crafted (e.g., disambiguating “is in” by context) and known to the model? How would the model fare if edge types were not pre-specified?
4) Direct reference relies on exact string matches to node-type names. How are OOV entities handled? Can the model learn entity linking without string matches?
5) What are the exact architecture sizes (hidden dimensions, Tprop steps), optimization details, and sensitivity to these choices?
6) For the Turing machine task, do you generalize to different numbers of states/symbols beyond the 4-state, 2-symbol setting? How does accuracy scale?
7) Can you report precision/recall/F1 on edge predictions (not only exact-graph match), and an error analysis on failed bAbI tasks (especially Task 17)?
8) What are training/inference runtimes and memory footprints as a function of graph size? Any attempts at sparsification or subgraph selection?
