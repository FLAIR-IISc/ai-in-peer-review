Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), extending GG-NNs/GGS-NNs with differentiable graph-state transformations that allow dynamic graph construction and modification. The core primitives are: node addition (with soft existence and type distributions), node state updates (including a direct-reference variant), edge updates, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation.
- A textual encoder (GRU) feeds sentence representations and optional direct references into a sequence of transformations that incrementally build a latent graph state; a query phase reads from the graph to produce an answer. Training includes strong supervision at the graph level with permutation-invariant node-addition loss and edge-level cross-entropy.
- Empirical demonstrations: (i) bAbI tasks, where the model reaches ≥95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference, including strong performance on pathfinding and induction; (ii) rule-discovery tasks (Rule 30 cellular automaton and 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines) where the model learns to update graph states over time and generalizes to longer rollouts.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Heavy reliance on non-standard strong supervision and task-specific preprocessing for bAbI undermines the empirical claims and comparability to prior work; (2) Important implementation details and ablations needed to establish technical soundness and novelty impact (e.g., permutation matching, scalability, sensitivity) are missing or insufficiently supported.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in literature: The motivation—to move from fixed-structure GNNs to models that can construct and update graph-structured internal states—is clear and relevant. The paper situates GGT-NNs among GG-NNs/GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, and DNCs, highlighting explicit graph-state advantages. This is well positioned, and the idea of differentiable graph-edit operations is interesting and potentially useful.
- Technical soundness:
  - The transformations are defined to be differentiable, and the use of soft node/edge strengths is coherent. However, key steps need clarification for soundness and feasibility: the permutation-invariant node-addition loss is written as a max over permutations, but the tractable algorithm (e.g., Hungarian matching, cost definition, complexity) is not specified. This is critical for reproducibility and to ensure the method scales beyond very small additions.
  - In bAbI training, the method uses teacher forcing at the graph level: after computing losses, ground-truth edges and newly added nodes are substituted into the state for subsequent steps. This can materially simplify learning and decouple errors across timesteps. While the paper acknowledges strong supervision, the implications for generalization and fair comparison are not fully analyzed.
  - Complexity: the paper notes quadratic scaling with graph size but provides no empirical analysis of runtime/memory or limits on graph sizes reached in experiments. Given the dynamic graph edits, this is a central practical concern.
- Empirical support:
  - bAbI results depend on exposing the generator’s internal “Knowledge object,” converting it to graphs, adding extra history nodes, and modifying entity names for direct reference. These departures from the standard setup, together with graph-level supervision and direct string matching to type names, make comparisons to end-to-end baselines less informative. The comparisons to MemNN are closer in supervision level, but different supervision signals (graphs vs. supporting facts) and dataset modifications still confound conclusions about relative strengths.
  - The rule-discovery tasks are a nice proof of concept, but they lack baselines, detailed quantitative results (the text references Table 3 but does not provide exact numbers here), and analysis of failure modes. It is unclear how performance scales with longer horizons, larger tapes, or rule diversity.
  - Ablations are limited. While a with/without direct-reference comparison is given, there is little analysis of the contribution of each transformation, the effect of intermediate propagation, or sensitivity to hyperparameters.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Clearly specify and analyze the permutation-invariant matching for node addition: matching algorithm, cost function, complexity, and its effect on training stability. Include ablations comparing exact matching vs. greedy vs. sinkhorn relaxations.
- Provide a thorough training and architecture section: hidden dimensions, number of propagation steps, edge-type counts, optimizer, learning rates, regularization, and early stopping criteria. Report variance over multiple runs.
- Strengthen empirical rigor on bAbI:
  - Report results on the standard dataset splits without using the generator’s “Knowledge object,” or clearly separate a “graph-supervised” setting with comparable baselines that also consume structured supervision.
  - Avoid dataset modifications where possible; if necessary, quantify their impact. For direct reference, consider learned entity linking rather than exact string matches to stress-test generality.
  - Add ablations isolating each transformation (e.g., no Tadd, no TC, fixed graph structure) to show necessity.
- For rule-discovery:
  - Provide exact quantitative metrics (per-timestep accuracy, exact-graph match rates), baselines (e.g., GG-NN with fixed topology, DNC, or transformer-based sequence models), and scaling analyses (longer horizons, larger tapes).
  - Analyze generalization limits and error propagation over long rollouts.
- Discuss and experiment with reduced supervision: e.g., partial graph supervision, consistency losses, or self-supervised structure learning. This will better support claims about learning graphical state transitions from unstructured input.

Questions for the authors
- How is the max over permutations in the node-addition loss computed in practice? What is the computational complexity, and how does it scale with the number of new nodes per step?
- What are the concrete model sizes (hidden dimensions, number of propagation steps), number of edge types, and training hyperparameters used for each task? How sensitive are results to these choices?
- In bAbI, what is the exact procedure for substituting ground-truth nodes/edges during training? Did you evaluate performance when not substituting ground truth (i.e., fully free-running)? How does accuracy degrade?
- Can you provide results on unmodified bAbI without access to the generator’s knowledge object, and with learned entity references rather than string matches?
- For the rule-discovery tasks, please report exact numbers for base and extended horizons, and compare to a fixed-topology GG-NN baseline to isolate the benefit of dynamic graph edits.
- What are the largest graphs you trained on? Please include wall-clock runtimes and memory usage, and discuss any optimizations (sparsity, batching) that enable scaling.
