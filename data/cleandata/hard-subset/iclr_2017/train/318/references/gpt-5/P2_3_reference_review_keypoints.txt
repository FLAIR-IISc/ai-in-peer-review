- Proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs): a modular framework that maintains an explicit differentiable graph state and updates it via trainable add/update/edge/propagate/aggregate modules, optionally ingesting text via a GRU.  
- Introduces supervision mechanisms for node/edge construction (including a permutation-invariant node-addition loss) and evaluates on bAbI, cellular automata, and small Turing machines.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Primary concern: Heavy reliance on strong supervision and engineered “direct reference”; limited evidence it works end-to-end from raw text without gold graphs.  
- Primary concern: Limited empirical scope — experiments are mostly synthetic and lack comparable supervision baselines and critical ablations.  
- Motivation and design are well argued: explicit learnable graph states are sensible for relational tasks and the modular transformations are coherent extensions of GGNNs.  
- Novelty is incremental: many components extend existing GGNN/memory ideas and the paper does not convincingly separate itself from prior graph-structure learning work.  
- bAbI results rely on injecting gold graphs and direct reference, making comparisons to end-to-end baselines unfair; non-direct variants still use graph-level supervision.  
- Preprocessing changes to bAbI (history linked lists, multiword entity handling) reduce comparability and may bias results.  
- Rule-discovery experiments (CA/TM) show learned dynamics and some generalization, but are limited-scale and don’t probe robustness, scalability, or sparsity.  
- Missing ablations: no systematic study of which transformations matter, the impact of supervision levels, or the role of direct reference; graph quality metrics (precision/recall) are not reported.  
- Efficiency/scaling concerns: pairwise edge updates and fully connected potentials imply O(|V|^2) time/memory; no sparsity/pruning or runtime experiments presented.  
- Clarity: transformations and permutation-invariant loss are clearly described, but practical details (how permutations are computed, computational cost) and exposure-bias effects from teacher forcing are missing.  
- Significance: conceptually neat and interpretable, but current synthetic, strongly supervised evidence falls short of the conference’s standards for generality and impact.  
- Recommendations: demonstrate end-to-end learning without graph supervision, evaluate learned graph quality, and compare fairly to baselines with matched supervision.  
- Recommendations: add ablations for Tadd/TC/Tprop/Trepr/direct reference, sensitivity analyses, and report wall-clock/memory usage and sparsity strategies.  
- Recommendations: report node/edge precision/recall, visualizations/case studies linking graph errors to QA errors, and include at least one real-world or larger-scale benchmark.  
- Training details to clarify: permutation-matching algorithm, its complexity, how training was stabilized, and effects of exposure bias (e.g., scheduled sampling).  
- Key questions for authors: How is the permutation max computed in practice and what is its runtime?  
- Key questions: Can the model be trained/evaluated without gold-graph injection and what are the resulting accuracies and graph qualities?  
- Key questions: How many edge types per task, are f_fwd/f_bwd shared, and how sensitive is performance to type specification?  
- Key questions: Typical graph sizes and runtime/memory per step, and whether performance degrades without teacher forcing?  
- Key questions: How robust is direct reference to paraphrase/coreference/multiword expressions, and can CA/TM tasks scale to larger tapes and longer horizons while preserving sparsity?
