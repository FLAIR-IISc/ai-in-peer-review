### Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture designed to learn and manipulate dynamic, graph-structured internal states. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that allow the model to incrementally construct and modify a graph based on sequential, unstructured input like natural language. This extends prior work on Graph Neural Networks (e.g., GG-NNs), which operate on fixed graph structures. The author demonstrates the model's capabilities on two classes of problems: first, on the bAbI question-answering dataset, where the GGT-NN learns to build a knowledge graph from a story and answer questions; and second, on rule-discovery tasks, where it learns the underlying dynamics of a 1D cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper claims that the explicit and interpretable graphical state is a key advantage over models with implicit memory structures like DNCs or Memory Networks.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Recommendation: Reject**

My recommendation for rejection is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Severe Presentation and Formatting Deficiencies:** The paper is incomplete and suffers from a significant number of formatting errors that impede a thorough evaluation. Crucially, all figures (1-7), tables (1-4), and algorithms (1-2) are referenced in the text but are not present in the manuscript. This makes it impossible to verify the claimed empirical results, understand the precise architecture used for experiments, or visualize the learned representations. The bibliography is also improperly formatted, suggesting a lack of care in the manuscript's preparation.
2.  **Over-reliance on Strong Supervision:** The model's success on the bAbI tasks is contingent upon full, step-by-step supervision of the ground-truth graph structure. At each timestep, the model is not only evaluated against the correct graph but also has the correct node and edge values "substituted into the graph for further processing." This is a form of teacher forcing on the latent reasoning structure itself. While the author is transparent about this, it represents a significant limitation on the method's applicability and weakens the comparison to end-to-end models that do not require such detailed supervision.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

Elaborating on the key reasons for rejection:

**Presentation Issues:** The absence of visual and tabular data is a critical flaw. For instance, Algorithm 1 is essential for understanding how the proposed graph transformations are sequenced to solve the bAbI tasks. Without it, the model's operation remains abstract. Similarly, Tables 1 and 2 are necessary to assess the central claim that the GGT-NN achieves competitive or superior performance on the bAbI tasks. The description of results in the text is no substitute for the actual data. The missing figures, particularly the visualizations of the graph formats (Figs. 5-7) and the training process (Fig. 3), hinder the reader's ability to gain intuition for the model's behavior and interpretability. Finally, the bibliography's formatting (e.g., `Cho et al\.,? \Q2014\E, Cho et al\.,`) is unprofessional and should have been corrected before submission.

**Methodological Limitations (Supervision):** The requirement for ground-truth intermediate graphs is a major constraint. For many real-world problems where one might wish to extract a knowledge graph from text, such detailed supervision is unavailable. The paper demonstrates that the model *can* learn if given this information, but it does not provide a clear path toward relaxing this assumption. The experiments, therefore, feel more like a proof of concept for the differentiable transformations rather than a demonstration of a practically applicable system. The performance comparison with models like DNC or EntNet is also somewhat misleading; while the author correctly notes the difference in supervision, the fact remains that those models solve the tasks end-to-end, which is a significantly harder and more general problem. The current work does not demonstrate that its internal graph structure emerges naturally from task-level supervision alone.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Clarity and Naming:** The name "Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network" is potentially confusing. The term "Transformer" is now strongly associated with the attention-based architecture of Vaswani et al. (2017), which this model does not appear to use. The author uses "Transformer" in the sense of "transforming a graph," but this name collision could be misleading. A more distinct name might be beneficial.
*   **Formatting and Style:** The use of the first-person singular ("I demonstrate," "I found") is somewhat informal for a conference paper of this nature. Adopting "we" or a more passive construction is standard. More importantly, a thorough proofreading is needed to fix all formatting issues, especially the bibliography and the inclusion of all referenced figures and tables.
*   **Minor Technical Inconsistencies:**
    *   In Section 2.1, the text states "Note that denotes elementwise multiplication," but the symbol is not used in the GRU equations presented.
    *   In the background on GG-NNs (Appendix A), equation (1) for `a(t)v` appears to be incomplete or oddly formatted with `A>v:[h (t−1)> 1 · · ·h (t−1)> |V| ] >`. This should be clarified.
*   **Future Work Exploration:** The paper would be strengthened by including preliminary experiments on reducing the supervision level. For example, how does the model perform with only final-graph supervision, or with supervision on only a fraction of the timesteps? This would provide valuable insight into the model's robustness and potential for broader application.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  The reliance on full, step-by-step graph supervision is my primary concern. Have you conducted any experiments to investigate how performance degrades as this supervision is relaxed or removed entirely? Is it feasible to train the GGT-NN with only answer-level supervision, perhaps with regularization to encourage meaningful graph formation?
2.  The paper notes the quadratic complexity with respect to the number of nodes for operations like the edge update. For the bAbI and Turing machine tasks, what were the typical and maximum number of nodes in the graphs, and what were the associated training times and memory requirements? This would provide a clearer picture of the model's practical scalability.
3.  Regarding the supervision for new nodes using the loss from Vinyals et al. (2016), you mention the network "arbitrarily chooses an ordering." Did you observe whether the model learned any canonical ordering for nodes of the same type, or was the ordering truly inconsistent across different training runs and examples?
