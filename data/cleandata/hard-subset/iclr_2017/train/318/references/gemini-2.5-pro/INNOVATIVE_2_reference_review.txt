### Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture designed to process sequential, unstructured input (like natural language) by dynamically constructing and manipulating an explicit, graph-structured internal state. The core of the contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations—node addition, edge updates, propagation, etc.—that serve as the fundamental building blocks of the model. These transformations allow the GGT-NN to incrementally build a knowledge graph from a story and then reason over this graph to answer questions. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on two distinct classes of problems: first, on 19 of the 20 bAbI question-answering tasks, where it achieves competitive or state-of-the-art results, and second, on rule-discovery tasks, where it successfully learns the transition rules for a 1D cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The paper positions this work as a step towards more interpretable and structured reasoning in neural networks, contrasting it with models that use less structured memory stores like Memory Networks or DNCs.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Advance:** The paper presents a bold and significant new direction for building reasoning systems. The idea of a neural network that learns to perform explicit, symbolic-like operations (add node, update edge) on a differentiable graph structure is a major conceptual leap beyond contemporary models that either operate on fixed graphs or use unstructured memory vectors. This work pioneers a framework for dynamic, learned manipulation of relational knowledge.
2.  **Strong and Convincing Empirical Support for the Core Idea:** The experiments effectively validate the central claim. The model’s strong performance on bAbI tasks that are inherently graph-like (e.g., pathfinding task 19, induction task 16) provides compelling evidence that an explicit graphical representation is a powerful inductive bias. Furthermore, successfully learning the complex, deterministic rules of cellular automata and Turing machines demonstrates the generality and power of the proposed graph transformations beyond the NLP domain.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this paper is its novelty. While GNNs and GG-NNs had already established the power of processing static graph inputs, this work addresses the far more challenging problem of *constructing* the graph itself from raw data. The proposed set of differentiable graph transformations is a powerful and compositional toolkit. This represents a significant step toward bridging the gap between connectionist and symbolic approaches, creating a model whose internal state is not an opaque high-dimensional vector but an interpretable, relational structure.

This explicit structure is a key advantage over related memory-augmented architectures like DNCs or MemN2Ns. While those models can store and retrieve information, the relationships between memories are often implicit (e.g., temporal links in a DNC). The GGT-NN, by contrast, learns to represent entities and their relationships explicitly as nodes and typed edges, which is a more natural fit for many reasoning problems. The excellent performance on the pathfinding task (bAbI 19), where GGT-NN significantly outperforms a strongly supervised MemNN and is competitive with SOTA, is a testament to the benefits of this approach. Pathfinding is trivial on a graph but difficult to learn from a flat list of memory slots; this result is the "smoking gun" that validates the paper's core premise.

The authors are forthright about the model's main limitation: the need for strong, step-by-step graph supervision during training. While this is a significant constraint for real-world application, it is an entirely reasonable simplification for a paper introducing such a fundamentally new and ambitious paradigm. The primary contribution is the framework itself and the demonstration that it *can* learn the correct transformations when guided. Proving this is a crucial first step. Reducing the reliance on supervision is a clear and exciting direction for future work, but its necessity here does not diminish the paper's core conceptual contribution. In line with valuing bold new ideas, the introduction of this powerful new modeling capability far outweighs the current imperfection of its training regime.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Scalability Concerns:** The paper rightly notes the quadratic complexity of the edge update and propagation steps with respect to the number of nodes. This seems to be a major obstacle for scaling to more complex problems. It would strengthen the paper to elaborate on this in the conclusion or future work section. Have the authors considered approximations, such as using sparse connectivity matrices or an attention mechanism to select a subset of node pairs to update at each step?
*   **On the "Transformer" in GGT-NN:** The term "Transformer" in the model's name might be a source of confusion for contemporary readers, as it does not seem to relate to the self-attention mechanism from Vaswani et al. (2017). The transformations are graph-modifying operations. While not a major issue, it's something to consider for clarity.
*   **The Role of "Direct Reference":** The performance gap between the models with and without direct reference is substantial. This suggests that grounding the model by linking specific text spans to node types is critical. Could the authors comment on whether they believe a more sophisticated attention mechanism could learn this grounding implicitly, potentially closing this gap and making the model more autonomous?
*   **Visualization:** The visualization in Figure 3 is very effective for understanding the rule-learning process. Given that a key selling point of the model is the interpretability of its internal state, I would encourage the authors to include a similar step-by-step visualization of the graph being constructed for one of the bAbI examples. This would provide powerful, intuitive evidence of the model's reasoning process.
*   **Ablation of Transformations:** The five proposed transformations seem intuitive, but were any others considered? For instance, was a "node merge" or "node deletion" operation explored? An ablation study showing the necessity of each of the five transformations would further strengthen the paper's claims about the proposed toolkit.
