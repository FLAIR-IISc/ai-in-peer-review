### **Review**

#### **1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture designed to learn complex tasks by incrementally building and manipulating an explicit, graph-structured internal state. The core of the contribution is a flexible, differentiable toolkit of graph transformations: node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation. These components can be composed to create models that process unstructured sequential input (like natural language), dynamically construct a meaningful internal graph representation, and use this graph to perform reasoning and generate an output.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness and versatility of the GGT-NN on three distinct problem domains. First, on the bAbI question-answering dataset, the model achieves competitive performance, solving 19 out of 20 tasks and notably outperforming many state-of-the-art models on tasks that are inherently graphical, such as pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16). Second, the paper shows that the GGT-NN can learn the underlying dynamics of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) from examples. Third, it successfully learns to simulate arbitrary 4-state Turing machines, showcasing its ability to master complex, symbolic, rule-based state transitions. The work positions itself as a bridge between Graph Neural Networks that operate on fixed graphs and memory-augmented networks (like DNCs and Memory Networks) that use less interpretable, implicit memory structures.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Novel and Powerful Framework:** The paper presents a novel and well-conceived framework for learning with dynamic, explicit graph structures. The set of differentiable graph transformations is a significant conceptual contribution that extends the capabilities of graph-based neural networks from static processing to dynamic construction and manipulation, opening up new modeling possibilities.
2.  **Strong and Diverse Empirical Validation:** The model's capabilities are convincingly demonstrated across a diverse range of challenging tasks. The strong performance on the bAbI benchmark, particularly on tasks where a graphical representation is intuitively advantageous, combined with the successful learning of complex algorithmic rules in the cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments, provides compelling evidence for the framework's power and generality.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this paper is the novelty and elegance of the proposed GGT-NN architecture. While GNNs have proven effective on tasks with pre-defined graphs, this work tackles the much harder problem of constructing the graph itself from raw input. The modular toolkit of differentiable transformations (T_add, T_C, T_prop, etc.) is a powerful abstraction that allows for building bespoke models for various tasks. This explicit, interpretable graphical state is a welcome alternative to the more opaque memory structures of models like DNCs or Memory Networks. The ability to visualize the learned internal state (as in Figure 3) is a significant step towards more transparent and understandable reasoning models.

The empirical results are thorough and provide strong support for the authors' claims. The bAbI results are impressive, not just in terms of overall accuracy but in the specific tasks where GGT-NN excels. Its superior performance on pathfinding and induction strongly suggests that the model is genuinely leveraging its graphical structure for reasoning, rather than just using it as a generic memory store. The success on the rule-discovery tasks (cellular automata and Turing machines) is particularly striking, as it demonstrates that the model can learn abstract, symbolic computations purely from observational data, a key challenge in machine learning. The authors are also commendably transparent about the model's limitations, such as its failure on bAbI Task 17 and the quadratic complexity of some operations.

The main weakness of the current work is its reliance on strong supervision during training, where the ground-truth graph is provided at every timestep. This limits the immediate practical applicability to domains where such detailed annotations are unavailable. In a sense, the model learns a mapping from text to pre-defined graph operations, rather than discovering the latent graph structure from scratch. However, for a foundational paper introducing a new modeling framework, this is a reasonable simplification to demonstrate the viability and potential of the core ideas. The contributions in defining and implementing the differentiable transformations are significant on their own, and provide a solid foundation for future work on reducing this supervision requirement.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and are not central to my decision to accept the paper.

*   **Exploring Reduced Supervision:** While noted as future work, it would strengthen the paper to discuss potential pathways to reducing supervision in more detail. Could the model be trained with reinforcement learning, where graph edits are actions and a reward is only given for the final correct answer? Or could a variational autoencoder-style objective be used to learn a latent graph structure with only answer-level supervision? Even a brief discussion of these possibilities would help frame the work's long-term potential.
*   **Complexity Analysis:** The quadratic complexity of edge updates is mentioned in the conclusion. It would be beneficial to include a more formal complexity analysis earlier in the paper, perhaps breaking down the time and space complexity of each of the five transformations with respect to the number of nodes and edges.
*   **Ablation of Graph Components:** The paper includes a valuable ablation on the "direct reference" mechanism. Further ablations could provide more insight. For example, on the bAbI tasks that use it (3 and 5), how crucial is the intermediate propagation step (`T_prop`)? What is the performance impact of removing it? Understanding the contribution of each transformation component would be very useful.
*   **bAbI Graph Construction:** The process of converting bAbI knowledge states into graphs involves some manual design choices (e.g., creating "record" nodes for history, defining specific edge types). It would be helpful to discuss the sensitivity of the model to these choices. This would clarify how much domain engineering is required to apply the model effectively.

#### **5. Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to ensure my understanding is correct:

1.  **Supervision during Propagation:** Section 4.1 states: "At this point the correct values C*, s*π(v) and x*π(v) are substituted into the graph for further processing." Does this mean that even during training, after the model makes a prediction for the graph structure, the ground-truth structure is used for subsequent steps within the same example (e.g., for the query processing phase)? If so, how do you expect this "teacher forcing" of the graph structure to affect the model's ability to recover from its own structural errors at test time, when the ground truth is not available?
2.  **Node Addition Hyperparameter:** The node addition transformation (`T_add`) generates up to a maximum number of new nodes per step. How was this maximum number determined for the experiments, particularly for the bAbI tasks where the number of new entities can vary? How sensitive is the model's performance to this hyperparameter?
3.  **Generalization on Rule-Based Tasks:** The generalization results for the CA and Turing machine tasks (to longer time sequences) are very impressive. Do you have any insights into *why* the model generalizes so well here? Does it appear to be learning the actual underlying update rules in a generalizable, symbolic-like manner?
