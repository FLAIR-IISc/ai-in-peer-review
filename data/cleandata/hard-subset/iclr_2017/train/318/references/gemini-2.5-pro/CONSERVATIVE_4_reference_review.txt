### Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture designed to learn tasks by incrementally constructing and manipulating an internal, graph-structured state representation. The core of the proposed model is a set of differentiable graph transformations that allow for the dynamic addition of nodes, updates to node states, and modification of edges based on unstructured textual input. The graph itself is defined in a "soft" manner, with continuous-valued strengths for nodes and edges to ensure differentiability. The GGT-NN is evaluated on the 20 bAbI question-answering tasks, where it is shown to solve 19 of the 20 tasks successfully. The paper also demonstrates the model's ability to learn complex update rules by training it to simulate a 1D cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The main claim is that this explicit graphical representation provides a powerful and interpretable alternative to the implicit memory structures used in other memory-augmented neural networks.

**2. Recommendation and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Extreme Reliance on Strong Supervision:** The model's success is heavily contingent on step-by-step, ground-truth graph supervision during training. This methodology fundamentally undermines the claim of *learning* to build a graph from raw input, as the model is effectively being taught a sequence of explicit graph edits.
2.  **Unfair Empirical Comparison:** The primary results on the bAbI benchmark are not comparable to state-of-the-art end-to-end models. By relying on privileged information (the ground-truth graph at each step), the GGT-NN solves a significantly simpler learning problem than models that must infer latent state from the final task objective alone.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The central premise of this work—enabling a neural network to reason by dynamically building a graph—is compelling. The proposed differentiable graph transformations are a technically interesting set of tools for this purpose. However, the experimental validation and methodological choices do not adequately support the paper's claims.

My primary concern is the nature of the supervision used. Section 4.1 describes a training regimen where, at each timestep, the model is provided with the "correct graph" and a loss is computed based on its deviation from this target. Critically, the paper states: "At this point the correct values C*, s*π(v) and x*π(v) are substituted into the graph for further processing." This is an extremely strong form of teacher forcing. Rather than learning the difficult task of inferring latent relational structure from sparse textual cues and a final answer, the model is essentially trained to perform a series of structured prediction tasks: mapping a sentence to a predefined graph modification. This severely limits the model's autonomy and makes it difficult to assess whether it has learned any generalizable reasoning skills or simply memorized a translation from text to graph operations. Such a supervision signal is unavailable in almost any realistic application, casting doubt on the practical utility of the proposed method.

This reliance on strong supervision directly impacts the interpretation of the experimental results. In Table 2, the GGT-NN is compared against a host of established models like End-to-End Memory Networks (MemN2N), Differentiable Neural Computers (DNC), and Recurrent Entity Networks (EntNet). While the paper commendably notes that the GGT-NN and the MemNN baseline use strong supervision while the others do not, this admission does not make the comparison valid. The GGT-NN is not performing the same task as the end-to-end models. Its strong performance is therefore expected and does not demonstrate a clear architectural advantage over methods that tackle the much harder problem of unsupervised structure discovery. A convincing demonstration would require showing strong performance with this supervision signal significantly weakened or removed entirely.

Furthermore, the model's success appears to depend on other forms of strong priors. The "direct reference" mechanism, which is shown to be critical for good performance, provides a hard-coded link between specific words in the input and node types in the graph. While effective, this is another feature that reduces the learning burden and relies on prior knowledge about the domain. Similarly, the successful application to the bAbI, cellular automaton, and Turing machine tasks required considerable, task-specific engineering to define the target graph schemas (as detailed in Appendix C). This suggests the framework is less of a general-purpose learning architecture and more of a differentiable programming framework that requires significant domain expertise to configure.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The modular design of the graph transformations is a positive aspect of the work. The paper would be much stronger if it focused on this contribution while being more transparent about the supervision requirements. For instance, framing the work as a method for "learning to execute programs on a dynamically changing graph" might be more accurate.
*   The experiments on the cellular automaton and Turing machines are interesting as a proof of concept for the propagation mechanism's expressiveness. They demonstrate that the model can simulate complex, rule-based dynamics on a given graph. This is a solid result in its own right, separate from the claims about learning from unstructured text.
*   The quadratic complexity in the number of nodes is a major scalability concern, which the authors acknowledge. To help readers gauge the practical limits, it would be useful to report statistics on the typical number of nodes per task in the bAbI experiments and the associated wall-clock training times.

**5. Questions for the Author(s)**

To better assess the contribution, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1.  The paper notes that initial experiments without strong supervision failed. Could you elaborate on the nature of this failure? Did the model's graph representations collapse to trivial solutions, or did the training process become unstable? Understanding this failure mode is key to appreciating why the strong supervision was necessary.
2.  What is the effect of removing the teacher-forcing step where the correct graph is substituted for further processing? If the model is instead forced to use its own predicted graph for subsequent steps (while still receiving the per-step graph loss), how does performance change? This would provide insight into the model's ability to recover from its own errors.
3.  Could you quantify the amount of human effort and task-specific design required to create the graph representations for the bAbI tasks? For example, the creation of a "linked list structure" for history in task 3 seems quite specific. How adaptable is this process to a new, unseen task?
