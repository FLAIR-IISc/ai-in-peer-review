### **Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"**

#### **1. Summary**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture designed to process sequential inputs, such as natural language, by building and manipulating an explicit, graph-structured internal state. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations that allow for modifying a "soft" graph representation where nodes and edges have continuous-valued strengths. These transformations include operations for adding nodes, updating node states and edges, propagating information through the graph, and aggregating information for output. The model is trained using strong, step-by-step supervision, where the ground-truth graph is provided at each stage of processing. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on two sets of tasks: the 20 bAbI question-answering tasks, and two rule-discovery tasks involving cellular automata and Turing machines. The results show that the model can solve most bAbI tasks to a high degree of accuracy and can successfully learn the underlying dynamics of the rule-based systems.

#### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**

1.  **Over-reliance on Strong Supervision:** The proposed method's primary weakness is its dependence on having a complete, ground-truth graph structure available at every single timestep during training. This is a very strong assumption that is not met in the vast majority of real-world applications. Consequently, the presented approach has limited practical applicability beyond settings where such detailed supervision can be synthesized. This also makes the comparison to state-of-the-art models that are trained end-to-end without this level of supervision fundamentally inequitable.

2.  **Significant Task-Specific Engineering:** The experiments on the bAbI dataset, while presented as building a graph from text, appear to rely on considerable manual engineering to define the target graph structures. As detailed in the appendix, the process involves creating bespoke graph schemas for different tasks, such as adding linked-list structures to represent history or renaming entities to fit the "direct reference" mechanism. This undermines the claim that the model learns to structure information from text, as it seems to be learning a mapping to a pre-specified, human-designed schema.

#### **3. Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The central premise of the GGT-NN is compelling: enabling a neural network to reason over an explicit, dynamic graph. However, the current work does not convincingly demonstrate that the model can learn to *form* this structure without being explicitly told what the structure should be at every step.

Regarding the strong supervision, while the authors are transparent about this requirement, it remains the most significant barrier to the method's adoption. Most contemporary research in memory-augmented networks (e.g., DNCs, EntNets) focuses on learning to structure and access memory with only final answer supervision. By requiring full intermediate state supervision, this work takes a step backward in terms of practicality. The paper notes that reducing supervision is a direction for future work, but in its present form, the model's high performance is inextricably linked to this unrealistic level of guidance. For this reason, citing performance on par with or exceeding end-to-end models is misleading, as the problem being solved is substantially different and easier.

Furthermore, the process of creating the supervision graphs for the bAbI tasks appears to be a form of feature engineering. For example, for task 3 ("Three Supporting Facts"), the authors state that a linked list structure was manually added to the knowledge object to represent history. The model then learns to generate this specific structure. This is not learning a temporal representation, but rather learning to mimic a specific data structure chosen by the experimenter. This pattern of intervention suggests the model's success is less about autonomous structure discovery and more about its ability as a function approximator to reproduce these heavily engineered targets.

Finally, the quadratic complexity in the number of nodes, which the authors acknowledge, is a serious practical limitation. While acceptable for the small-scale bAbI tasks, this prevents the model from being applied to more complex problems with larger state spaces, where explicit graph representations could otherwise be very powerful. Established methods like memory networks often scale more gracefully.

#### **4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While I cannot recommend acceptance at this time, the core ideas in the paper are interesting and could form the basis of a stronger contribution with further development.

*   **Exploring Weaker Supervision:** The most critical step would be to demonstrate a path toward reducing the supervision requirement. Could the model be pre-trained with full graph supervision and then fine-tuned on a larger dataset with only answer supervision? Or could a loss be formulated based only on the final graph state, rather than every intermediate one? Showing reasonable performance under a more realistic supervision setting would significantly strengthen the paper's claims.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The model is composed of five distinct transformation types. An ablation study analyzing the contribution of each transformation (e.g., removing `T_add` or `T_C`) on a representative task would provide valuable insight into the model's inner workings and justify the complexity of the proposed framework.
*   **The "Direct Reference" Mechanism:** This mechanism provides a substantial performance boost, suggesting the model struggles to link textual mentions to their corresponding node types without this explicit help. This could be interpreted as a weakness of the core architecture. It would be beneficial to analyze why this mechanism is so crucial and explore alternatives that are less heuristic.
*   **More Realistic Tasks:** The rule-discovery tasks are clean, synthetic problems. Applying the model to a more natural, albeit still structured, task like knowledge base population from text or semantic parsing into an abstract meaning representation would be a more compelling demonstration of its utility.

#### **5. Questions for the Author(s)**

1.  Could you elaborate on the extent of the task-specific modifications made to the bAbI knowledge objects to generate the supervision graphs? Was a unique graph generation pipeline required for each of the 20 tasks?
2.  Have you performed any experiments to test the model's robustness as supervision is relaxed? For instance, what happens to performance if ground-truth graphs are provided only for a fraction of the training steps or examples?
3.  The quadratic complexity is noted as a key limitation. In your experiments, what was the typical number of nodes per graph, and at what scale did the memory and computational requirements become prohibitive?
4.  Regarding the failure on bAbI task 17, your hypothesis is that it relates to the larger number of entities. Does the model's performance degrade gracefully with the number of node types, or is there a sharp drop-off? Could the issue be the compositional nature of the entities (e.g., "red square") rather than just the total number?
