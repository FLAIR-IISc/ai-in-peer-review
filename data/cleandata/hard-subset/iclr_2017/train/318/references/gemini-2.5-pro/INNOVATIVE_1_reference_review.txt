### Review of "LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel architecture capable of learning to dynamically construct and manipulate an explicit, graph-structured internal state from sequential, unstructured input like natural language. The core of the contribution is a toolkit of differentiable graph transformations—including node addition, edge updates, and information propagation—that allow the model to build and reason over its own internal world representation. The authors demonstrate the model's capabilities on two distinct classes of problems: first, on the bAbI question-answering dataset, where it learns to build a knowledge graph from a story and answer questions; and second, on "rule discovery" tasks, where it impressively learns the underlying dynamics of a cellular automaton and arbitrary Turing machines by observing their state transitions.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an ambitious and conceptually exciting paper that introduces a significant new direction for research. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two key strengths:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Advance:** The paper's primary contribution is not an incremental improvement on an existing method, but a bold new idea: a neural network that learns to operate on a dynamically evolving, explicit graph structure. This moves beyond the dominant paradigms of GNNs on static graphs and memory-augmented networks with unstructured, implicit memory stores. The proposed set of differentiable graph operations provides a powerful, modular framework for building models that can reason in a structured, interpretable way.
2.  **Compelling Proof-of-Concept on Algorithmic Tasks:** While the bAbI results are solid, the experiments on learning the rules of a cellular automaton and Turing machines are particularly strong. These tasks strip away the complexities of language and directly test the model's ability to learn complex, algorithmic transformations of its graphical state. The success on these tasks, including generalization to longer sequences, provides compelling evidence that the proposed graph transformations are a viable mechanism for learning symbolic-like computation.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The GGT-NN framework presents a compelling bridge between connectionist and symbolic approaches. Unlike models such as DNCs or Memory Networks, where the internal state is a set of vectors that are difficult to interpret, the GGT-NN maintains an explicit graph. This state is not only more interpretable—one can visualize the knowledge graph the model has built—but also holds the potential for direct integration with downstream symbolic reasoning systems. This is a highly valuable property that is often missing in end-to-end deep learning models.

The paper demonstrates the value of this explicit graphical representation on the bAbI tasks. The model's particularly strong performance on tasks with a natural graph analogue, such as pathfinding (Task 19) and induction (Task 16), supports the central thesis that a graphical internal state is beneficial for certain classes of reasoning problems.

I am willing to overlook some of the current limitations of the approach in light of its novelty. The reliance on strong, step-by-step graph supervision for the bAbI tasks is a significant one. However, for a foundational paper introducing a new modeling paradigm, this is an acceptable simplification. It effectively serves as a crucial proof-of-concept, demonstrating that the architecture *can* learn to perform these complex manipulations when guided. The challenge of reducing or removing this supervision is a natural and exciting direction for future work, rather than a fatal flaw in the current paper. Similarly, the acknowledged quadratic complexity is a practical engineering challenge that does not diminish the conceptual value of the work. The core idea is sound, and future research can address its scaling properties.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to my decision to accept.

*   **Reducing Supervision:** The reliance on full graph supervision is the paper's most significant practical limitation. I would be very interested to hear the author's thoughts on pathways to relax this requirement. Could the model be trained with only question-answer supervision, perhaps with regularization that encourages sparse, meaningful graph structures? Or could a hybrid approach be used, where a small amount of fully supervised data is used to bootstrap a model that is then fine-tuned on a larger, weakly supervised dataset?
*   **Discretization during Inference:** The paper describes node and edge strengths as continuous values in [0, 1]. The evaluation of the rule-discovery tasks mentions comparing the "most likely graph" generated by the model. How is this discretization performed during inference? Is a simple threshold (e.g., 0.5) used, or is there a more sophisticated procedure? Clarifying this would be helpful.
*   **Analysis of Failure Cases:** The analysis of the failure on bAbI Task 17 is brief. Deeper insight could be gained by inspecting the graphs the model generated for this task. Did the model fail to instantiate the correct nodes, or did it struggle to form the correct relational edges between them? A qualitative analysis of the erroneous graphs could strengthen the paper.
*   **Nomenclature:** The name "Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network" is a potential point of confusion, given that the "Transformer" architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) is now a canonical and distinct model class based on self-attention. While the model does *transform* a graph, the name might lead to incorrect assumptions about its mechanism. This is a minor point, but perhaps worth considering for future clarity.
