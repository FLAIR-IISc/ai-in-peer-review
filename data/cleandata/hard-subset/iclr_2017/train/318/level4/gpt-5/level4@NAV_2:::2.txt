1. Summary of claims and contributions (positive)
- The paper introduces a neural architecture that learns to construct and update a graph-structured internal state on the fly from text, using learned, differentiable graph transformations. The graph serves as the model’s memory and can be queried to produce answers.
- The approach extends GG-NN/GGS-NN by making the graph itself an evolving, differentiable representation manipulated during inference, not just a fixed input structure, and couples this with sentence encoders to drive the transformations.
- The work argues this is, to the best of my knowledge, the first implementation that treats memory explicitly as a differentiable graph rather than a stack or a flat memory array, and demonstrates the idea primarily on bAbI tasks.
- Although early, the paradigm is original, technically sound (as far as I could determine), and thought-provoking; it plausibly offers greater expressiveness than stack-based memory and standard memory networks.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons:
- Novelty and potential impact: an explicit, differentiable graph memory manipulated during inference is a substantive conceptual step beyond existing memory architectures.
- Despite preliminary results and higher complexity, the paper is intellectually stimulating and has improved substantially in clarity from the initial version.

3. Supporting arguments
- The method learns both the sentence encodings and the parameters of graph transformations, enabling the model to build and modify a relational state as it processes a dialogue/story—this goes beyond memory networks that store sentence vectors and attend over them.
- Empirically, performance on bAbI is broadly comparable to strong memory-network baselines; there is no clear, consistent performance gain across tasks yet. This supports the claim that the benefits are not primarily reflected in headline accuracy at this stage.
- The approach is significantly more complex than prior memory-network-style systems, both conceptually and computationally, which raises questions about efficiency versus benefit on current benchmarks.
- Nevertheless, the explicit graph memory is a compelling design that could ultimately prove more powerful, and the paper’s revised presentation is much clearer than the early draft while remaining technically careful.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarity and exposition:
  - Provide an intuitive walkthrough of each transformation with a small running example, before the full formalism. A figure mapping a short story to successive graph states would help.
  - Tighten the novelty claim: clearly delineate how this differs from GGS-NNs (graph as input vs. graph as evolving internal state) and from DNC/Memory Networks (implicit vs. explicit structure).
- Empirical evidence:
  - Add ablations (e.g., removing direct reference, edge update, or propagation) to isolate which transformations drive gains.
  - Report computational cost (time/memory) relative to memory networks on the same hardware to quantify the complexity trade-off.
  - Where possible, include comparisons to “traditional rule induction” baselines mentioned in the draft (the paper currently alludes to them); if unavailable, at least discuss expected gaps and why.
- Interpretation and analysis:
  - Visualize learned graphs on select bAbI tasks to illustrate how reasoning unfolds (e.g., pathfinding).
  - Discuss generalization: do graphs stabilize to task-meaningful structures, and how sensitive are they to lexical variation?
- Writing polish:
  - Consolidate notation early; minimize symbol overload and ensure all matrices/vectors are typed and dimensioned on first use.
  - Ensure references for claims like “first differentiable graph memory” are comprehensive and up to date.

Questions for the authors
- Novelty/positioning: Can you more precisely define the boundary of “differentiable memory as a graph” relative to GGS-NN (graph-in, sequence-out) and DNC/MemN2N (implicit memory with structural access)? What prior work, if any, uses an explicitly evolving graph as the memory during inference?
- Complexity: What is the asymptotic and empirical runtime/memory overhead versus a strong MemN2N/EntNet baseline on representative bAbI tasks? Any insights on scalability bottlenecks?
- Supervision: How dependent is performance on the strong supervision of intermediate graphs and direct references? With weaker or no graph supervision, how does accuracy degrade and what inductive biases help?
- Generalization capacity: Beyond bAbI, do you have evidence that the graph memory confers better systematic generalization (e.g., to longer stories or compositional variations) than memory networks?
- Analysis of negatives: On which bAbI tasks does the approach underperform rule-induction-style methods, and why? Are these due to search/credit assignment limits, or representation mismatch?
- Design choices: How critical are specific transformations (e.g., edge toggle vs. continuous update; per-sentence propagation) to the observed results? Any stability issues during training?
- Interpretability: Can you provide case studies where the extracted graphs give insight into the model’s reasoning that a memory-network attention map would not?
- Future efficiency: Do you foresee sparse connectivity or subgraph updates making the approach practical on larger real-world datasets, and how would that integrate with your current formulation?
