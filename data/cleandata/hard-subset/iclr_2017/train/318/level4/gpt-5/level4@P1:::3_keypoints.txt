- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a differentiable model that builds and edits graph-structured internal states via five learnable transformations.  
- Demonstrates an instantiation for QA where the model incrementally updates an internal graph and yields promising results on synthetic tasks (e.g., bAbI).  
- Claims novelty in mixing continuous and symbolic representations with a flexible, evolving graph state compared to fixed-shape differentiable memories.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason for rejection: training relies on strong per-timestep graph supervision, limiting applicability to realistic tasks.  
- Secondary reason: paper is overly dense and reads like a journal article, making it hard to digest in a conference format.  
- Strength: tackles an important problem bridging neural computation and symbolic structure, and is well positioned relative to prior work (extends GGS-NNs).  
- Weakness: empirical evidence depends on full intermediate graph supervision, so generality to latent-graph scenarios is unproven.  
- Readability concern: ambitious, technically dense presentation needs consolidation and clearer focus.  
- Request to clarify which components use full graph supervision versus answer-only supervision and provide ablation studies under reduced supervision.  
- Suggest including experiments where the graph is latent and learned end-to-end from input–output pairs to test practicality.  
- Recommend qualitative analyses of learned graphs across timesteps, failure cases, and contribution of specific transformations to support interpretability claims.  
- Ask for complexity and scalability analysis (time/space as nodes/edges grow), bottlenecks, optimizations, and empirical scaling curves.  
- Advise comparisons and ablations: baselines under identical supervision, ablate the five transformations, and test without “direct reference” to measure its impact.  
- Presentation fixes: foreground core transformation framework, move implementation details to appendix, and tighten related work.  
- Key questions for authors: how essential is per-timestep supervision and how does performance degrade when reduced or removed?  
- Key question: how would training graphs be obtained in realistic domains and could semi-/self-supervised strategies replace full supervision?  
- Key question: which of the five transformations are indispensable, and can simpler variants suffice for some tasks?  
- Key question: were there instabilities in node/edge creation, and what regularization helped?  
- Key question: what are the largest graphs trained, how do time/memory scale, and what optimizations are planned?  
- Key question: can models transfer across domains, and how robust are they to noisy or ambiguous inputs?
