This paper extends the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network by adding the ability to perform complex graph transformations. The core idea is to let the model build and modify a graph-structured internal representation while solving a task, here focusing on question answering. The authors propose five differentiable transformation types, learned from training data, typically in a supervised setting where the graph state is provided at each time step. They present a specific instantiation that takes a sequence as input, iteratively updates an internal graph state, and produces a final prediction. This instance is applied to QA tasks (e.g., bAbI) with promising results.

The approach is compelling: the model maintains a rich graph-based state while remaining fully differentiable and trainable via gradient descent. It can be viewed as a successful attempt to bridge continuous and symbolic representations. Moreover, it appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate “symbolic” structure into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), since the form of the state is not fixed and can evolve over time.

My main concern is the training paradigm. The model is trained by providing the complete graph state at each time step, which is feasible for particular datasets (such as bAbI) but unlikely to extend to more complex or realistic problems where intermediate graph states are not available or are prohibitively expensive to annotate. Without a strategy for weaker supervision or latent-state training, the applicability of the method may be limited.

A secondary concern is the paper’s presentation. The content and breadth seem more appropriate for a journal article than a conference paper. As written, the paper is dense and at times difficult to follow, which may hinder accessibility and reproducibility within the constraints of a conference format.
