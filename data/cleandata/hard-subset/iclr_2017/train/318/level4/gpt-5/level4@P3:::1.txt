Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a framework that extends GG-NN/GGS-NN by making the graph itself a differentiable, learnable intermediate state. The model supports a suite of differentiable graph transformations—node addition, node state updates (with an optional direct-reference mechanism), edge updates, propagation, and aggregation—and composes them to incrementally build and modify graphs from textual inputs and use those graphs to produce outputs (textual or graph-structured).
- The approach is motivated by the ubiquity of graph-structured representations for world state and data structures. It aims to learn both to construct the graph and to reason over it, within a single differentiable architecture.
- Empirically, the model attains strong performance on the bAbI tasks (19/20 tasks at ≥95% with direct reference under strong supervision) and demonstrates rule learning in two graph-to-graph prediction settings: a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30) and families of 2-symbol, 4-state Turing machines, with evidence of generalization to longer rollouts. The paper provides implementation details, training losses for nodes/edges, and discusses complexity and related work.

Decision
Accept (weak). Key reasons:
1) Clear and well-motivated architectural contribution: a principled, differentiable toolkit for constructing and transforming graphs that complements prior GNN/GGS-NN work and bridges to memory-based models.
2) Compelling empirical evidence that the approach is effective and broadly applicable (bAbI, cellular automata, Turing machines), with notable wins on tasks naturally suited to graphs (e.g., path finding).

Supporting arguments
- Problem and positioning: The paper addresses how to learn not only to propagate over graphs (as in GNNs) but also to construct and modify the graph structure as an internal state from unstructured inputs. This is well placed against GNNs/GGS-NNs (which assume a fixed input graph) and memory/DNC models (which have implicit, less interpretable structure).
- Technical approach: The proposed transformations are well specified and differentiable end-to-end. The supervision strategy for unordered node sets (via set loss/optimal matching) is appropriate; the edge and node-strength formulations are sensible; and the direct-reference mechanism is an interpretable way to inject entity mentions.
- Empirical support: 
  - bAbI results are competitive or superior on many tasks, with especially strong gains on pathfinding and induction—tasks that map naturally to graphs. The paper includes ablations with/without direct reference.
  - Rule-discovery experiments show the model can learn nontrivial transition rules and generalize to longer horizons in both CA and Turing machine settings.
- Rigor and clarity: The paper includes algorithmic pseudocode, training losses, and transformation details. Comparisons to a broad set of baselines are provided, including notes on supervision differences.

That said, the strongest results on bAbI rely on strong supervision (full intermediate graphs) and a direct-reference cue tied to entity names, which limits claims about end-to-end learning from raw text. Some graph representations are also engineered (e.g., adding history nodes) to encode temporal structure, which helps performance but complicates fairness and generality. Further, scalability remains a concern due to quadratic costs in dense graphs.

Feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not all decision-critical)
- Clarify supervision and fairness: Make explicit which comparisons use strong supervision (GGT-NN and MemNN) versus end-to-end baselines, and report additional results with reduced supervision (e.g., partial graph supervision, no direct reference). Even small-scale results would strengthen claims of generality.
- Detail graph construction in bAbI: The paper introduces task-specific graph augmentations (e.g., history linked lists). Provide a systematic description of all such modifications, with rationale and a discussion of how much performance depends on them. If possible, report the extended sequence-of-graphs variant (Appendix D) side-by-side with the original for temporal tasks.
- Ablations: 
  - Report the effect of each transformation (e.g., removing TC, disabling Tadd, varying propagation steps), and quantify the contribution of direct reference beyond the existing ablation.
  - Analyze sensitivity to the maximum number of nodes per sentence and the learned stopping behavior via node strengths.
- Scaling and efficiency: Include complexity and wall-clock/memory profiles versus graph size, and discuss sparse implementations or neighborhood sampling to reduce O(|V|^2) cost.
- Analysis of learned graphs: Provide qualitative examples of learned graphs for bAbI (with/without supervision), error breakdowns, and visualizations showing how reasoning unfolds (e.g., for path finding).
- Generalization: In the rule-discovery tasks, include per-timestep accuracy, failure modes on long rollouts, and whether the Turing machine rule sets are held out from training distributions.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision requirements: How well does the model learn without intermediate graph supervision? Can semi-supervised training (some examples with graph supervision, others without) maintain performance?
- Direct reference: To what extent does performance degrade if direct reference is removed or noisy? Could learned attention over tokens replace the explicit name match? How are multiword entities handled without modifying the tasks?
- Entity identity and coreference: How does Tadd avoid duplicating nodes for the same entity across sentences? Is there an explicit mechanism to merge or reference existing nodes?
- Node addition: What is the stopping criterion when adding nodes per sentence? Is it purely via node strengths up to a fixed maximum, and how sensitive are results to that maximum?
- Graph engineering in bAbI: Do the added history nodes leak information or bias the task? Can the sequence-of-graphs variant (Appendix D) match the main-model accuracy with more training or regularization?
- Generalization and diversity in rule discovery: For Turing machines, are rule sets and initial tapes sampled uniformly? Are train/test rule sets disjoint? How do results change with more states/symbols?
- Scalability: What are the largest graphs you successfully trained on? Any progress with sparse adjacency or neighborhood-restricted propagation?
- Reproducibility and anonymity: The appendix mentions a public repository. Please confirm an anonymized code link for the review process and include key hyperparameters and training settings (optimizer, learning rate, propagation steps, hidden sizes).

Overall, this paper offers a substantive and timely contribution by making graph construction and transformation differentiable, demonstrating clear benefits on graph-suited tasks, and opening avenues for interpretable intermediate representations. Addressing the supervision dependence and scaling concerns would further strengthen its impact.
