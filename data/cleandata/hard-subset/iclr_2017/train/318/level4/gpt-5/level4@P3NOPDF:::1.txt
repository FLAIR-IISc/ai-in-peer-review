Summary:
The paper introduces a differentiable framework for graph-to-graph transformations, enabling end-to-end learning of graph rewrite processes via gradient descent. A motivating application is learning cellular automata as sequences of graph states, where the graph grows and edges represent neighbor relations while special nodes encode binary values. Although the proposed architecture can, in principle, learn such iterative transformations, the experiment on Rule 30 remains far from solved. The authors also integrate their approach with GGS-NN to generate textual outputs from intermediate graph representations, achieving state-of-the-art results on the bAbI tasks.

Strengths:
- Conceptual novelty: Framing graph-to-graph prediction as a differentiable transformation is an appealing and timely idea, bridging structure learning and neural message passing.
- Generality: The framework appears flexible enough to model iterative processes (e.g., cellular automata), potentially broadening the scope of graph neural networks beyond node/edge classification to constructive tasks.
- Practical impact: Using learned intermediate graphs to mediate between input structure and textual outputs is compelling, and the empirical improvements on bAbI suggest the approach can provide concrete benefits on downstream tasks.
- Connection to prior work: Building on GGS-NN is sensible and shows how graph transformations can be layered with existing graph sequence models.

Weaknesses and concerns:
- Limited success on the core motivating example: The cellular automaton experiment (Rule 30) does not convincingly demonstrate that the method can learn nontrivial graph-to-graph dynamics. This undermines the central claim.
- Scope of evaluation: Aside from bAbI, which is not inherently graph-to-graph, there is a lack of targeted benchmarks designed to test graph construction accuracy (e.g., shortest-path tree extraction, BFS layer construction, programmatic graph rewrites). Without such tasks, it is difficult to assess the fidelity and robustness of the transformations.
- Clarity on mechanism: The paper needs a crisper and more formal description of how discrete graph growth (node/edge creation) is made differentiable. Are edges sampled or relaxed with continuous scores? How is sparsity enforced? Are there stability guarantees across multiple transformation steps?
- Supervision and training signals: It is unclear whether intermediate graphs are supervised, weakly supervised, or entirely latent. If latent, how is credit assignment handled across several graph transformation steps, and what prevents degenerate soft graphs?
- Computational complexity: The cost of maintaining and updating growing graphs could scale poorly (e.g., O(n^2) edge scores per step). The paper should quantify runtime/memory, especially on multi-step transformations, and compare to baselines.
- Comparisons and ablations: Improvements over prior methods on bAbI are promising, but it is hard to attribute gains without ablations isolating (1) graph-to-graph transformation vs. GGS-NN alone, (2) the effect of intermediate graph supervision (if any), and (3) architectural choices (edge parameterization, readout).
- Interpretability: Given the motivation, qualitative analyses of the learned intermediate graphs are essential. The paper would benefit from visualizations and metrics demonstrating that induced structures align with known reasoning chains or automaton states.

Questions for the authors:
- How exactly are new nodes and edges parameterized and instantiated during training? Do you use continuous relaxations (e.g., soft adjacency), straight-through estimators, or other techniques to bridge discrete decisions and gradients?
- What regularization encourages sparse, well-formed graphs and prevents dense, blurry intermediates?
- Are intermediate graphs supervised on any tasks, or learned end-to-end from the final loss? If unsupervised, how is training stabilized over long sequences?
- What is the asymptotic and empirical complexity per transformation step, and how does it scale with graph size and number of steps?
- On bAbI, which tasks see the largest gains, and do the learned graphs correspond to entities/relations expected from the stories?

Suggestions for improvement:
- Strengthen the core claim with targeted graph-to-graph benchmarks: e.g., learning BFS/DFS layerings, shortest-path trees, transitive closure, or simple algorithmic rewrites where ground truth is available. Report exact match on edges/nodes across multiple steps.
- Revisit the cellular automaton experiment with curriculum learning, scheduled discretization, or stronger inductive biases (e.g., local convolutional neighborhoods) and report progress across multiple rules, not just Rule 30.
- Provide a formalization of the transformation step: parameterization, relaxation, loss, and gradient flow. Include analysis of error accumulation over multiple steps and any techniques (e.g., teacher forcing for structure) that mitigate compounding errors.
- Add ablations and stronger baselines: compare against GGS-NN without graph growth, memory networks/Neural Turing Machines, and sequence models with attention. Isolate the contribution of the differentiable graph construction.
- Include qualitative and quantitative interpretability: visualize intermediate graphs on bAbI and synthetic tasks; measure structural metrics (sparsity, degree distributions, alignment with ground-truth relations).
- Report training stability and efficiency: convergence behavior, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and wall-clock/runtime comparisons.

Overall assessment:
The paper presents an intriguing and potentially impactful direction—differentiable graph-to-graph transformations—and demonstrates promising downstream gains on bAbI when combined with GGS-NN. However, the evidence that the method truly learns nontrivial graph construction dynamics is currently insufficient, given the limited success on the cellular automaton and the lack of focused graph-to-graph evaluations. With clearer methodological exposition and stronger, purpose-built experiments, this work could make a solid contribution.

Recommendation: Weak Accept, contingent on improved clarity and augmented experiments that directly validate graph-to-graph learning.

Reviewer confidence: Medium.
