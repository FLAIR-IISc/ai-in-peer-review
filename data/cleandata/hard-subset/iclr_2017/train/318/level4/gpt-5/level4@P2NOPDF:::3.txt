This paper extends the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network to support complex, learned graph transformations. The central idea is to allow the model to build and modify a graph-structured internal representation while solving a task, with a focus here on question answering. The authors introduce five differentiable transformation operations that can be learned from data, typically under supervision where the graph state is provided at each timestep. They present a concrete instantiation that consumes an input sequence, iteratively updates an internal graph, and produces a final prediction, demonstrating promising results on QA benchmarks such as bAbI.

The approach is compelling because it maintains a rich, dynamic graph representation while remaining fully differentiable and trainable via gradient-based optimization. It represents a thoughtful integration of continuous and symbolic computation. Compared with recent attempts to inject symbolic structure into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), this method appears more general: the shape of the internal state is not fixed and can evolve during processing.

My primary concern is the training paradigm. The model relies on supervision of the graph state at each timestep, which is feasible for synthetic or carefully designed datasets like bAbI but unlikely to scale to more complex, real-world problems where intermediate graph states are unavailable or ambiguous. It would be helpful to discuss alternative training strategies (e.g., weak supervision, latent structure learning, or reinforcement learning signals) and to provide evidence that the method can learn useful transformations without step-by-step graph annotations.

A secondary concern is presentation and scope. The paper is dense and packed with details, making it difficult to follow in a conference setting. The breadth of material—and the number of proposed transformations—may be better suited to a journal article, with more space for clear exposition, ablations, and a deeper analysis of when each transformation is necessary.

Overall, this is an innovative and potentially influential direction that advances the integration of dynamic, symbolic-like structures into neural models. The empirical results are encouraging, and the formulation is elegant. However, the reliance on fully supervised intermediate graph states limits practical applicability, and the exposition would benefit from streamlining, additional ablation studies, and experiments demonstrating learning with weaker supervision.
