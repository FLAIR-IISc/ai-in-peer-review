The paper extends the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network to support complex graph transformations. The core idea is to enable the model to build and modify a graph-structured internal representation to solve tasks, with a focus here on question answering. The authors propose five differentiable transformations that are learned from data, typically in a supervised setting where the graph state is provided at each timestep. They present a specific instantiation that takes a sequence as input, iteratively updates an internal graph state to produce a final prediction, and demonstrates interesting results on QA tasks (e.g., bAbI).

The approach is compelling because the model can maintain a complex graph representation of its current state while remaining fully differentiable and thus trainable with gradient-based methods. It can be viewed as a successful attempt to bridge continuous and symbolic representations. It also appears more general than recent efforts to add “symbolic” components to differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), since the state’s structure is not fixed and can evolve.

My main concern is the training procedure: providing the graph state at each timestep is feasible for specific tasks (e.g., bAbI) but is unlikely to be a viable solution for more complex problems. A secondary concern is that the paper’s scope and density seem better suited to a journal format than a conference, which makes it difficult to read.
