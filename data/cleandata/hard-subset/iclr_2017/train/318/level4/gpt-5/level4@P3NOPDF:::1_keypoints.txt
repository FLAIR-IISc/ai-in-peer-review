- Paper proposes a differentiable framework for graph-to-graph transformations enabling end-to-end learning of graph rewrite processes via gradient descent.
- Motivating application: learn cellular automata as sequences of growing graphs, with nodes/edges representing neighbors and special nodes encoding binary values.
- Integrates with GGS-NN to produce text from intermediate graphs and achieves improved results on bAbI tasks.
- Strength: conceptual novelty in framing graph-to-graph prediction as differentiable, bridging structure learning and message passing.
- Strength: framework appears general enough to model iterative constructive processes beyond standard node/edge classification.
- Strength: practical impact demonstrated by empirical improvements on bAbI when using learned intermediate graphs.
- Strength: sensible connection and building on prior work (GGS-NN) showing composability with graph sequence models.
- Weakness: cellular automaton experiment (Rule 30) shows limited success and does not convincingly prove learning of nontrivial graph dynamics.
- Weakness: evaluation scope is narrow—lacks targeted graph-construction benchmarks (e.g., BFS layers, shortest-path trees, transitive closure).
- Weakness: unclear mechanism for making discrete node/edge creation differentiable—need clarification on sampling vs. continuous relaxations and sparsity enforcement.
- Weakness: supervision and training signals for intermediate graphs are not clearly specified; credit assignment and prevention of degenerate soft graphs are unclear.
- Weakness: potential computational complexity and scalability issues (e.g., O(n^2) edge scores) are not quantified empirically or theoretically.
- Weakness: lack of comparisons and ablations to isolate contributions of graph-to-graph transformations vs. GGS-NN and other architectural choices.
- Weakness: interpretability is lacking—need qualitative visualizations and metrics showing learned graphs align with expected structures or reasoning chains.
- Questions: how are new nodes/edges parameterized and instantiated; are relaxations, straight-through estimators, or other techniques used for gradients?
- Questions: what regularization enforces sparse, well-formed graphs and prevents dense/blurry intermediates?
- Questions: are intermediate graphs supervised or latent; if latent, how is stability and credit assignment handled over long sequences?
- Suggestions: evaluate on targeted graph-to-graph benchmarks (BFS/DFS layering, shortest-path trees, transitive closure) and report exact match metrics across steps.
- Suggestions: improve cellular automaton experiments with curriculum learning, scheduled discretization, or stronger inductive biases and test multiple rules.
- Suggestions: provide formalization of transformation step (parameterization, relaxation, loss, gradient flow) and analyze error accumulation and mitigation techniques.
- Suggestions: add ablations and stronger baselines (GGS-NN without growth, memory networks, sequence models with attention) to isolate contributions.
- Suggestions: include qualitative/quantitative interpretability analyses (visualize intermediate graphs, measure sparsity, degree distributions, alignment with ground truth).
- Suggestions: report training stability, convergence behavior, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and wall-clock/runtime comparisons.
- Overall assessment: intriguing and potentially impactful direction, but current evidence for true graph-to-graph learning is insufficient; needs clearer methods and stronger targeted experiments.
- Recommendation: Weak Accept, contingent on improved clarity and augmented experiments directly validating graph-to-graph learning.
