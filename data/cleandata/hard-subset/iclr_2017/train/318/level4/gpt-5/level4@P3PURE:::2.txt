This paper proposes learning, on the fly, to represent a dialog as a graph that serves as memory, first demonstrated on the bAbI tasks. Graph learning is part of the inference process, while long-term representation learning is used to learn the graph-transformation parameters and the encoding of sentences that feed the graph. This seems to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory represented as a graph. Although the approach is substantially more complex than prior methods such as memory networks and does not yet deliver significant performance gains on bAbI, the work is still preliminary, and representing memory as a graph appears much more powerful than a stack. Clarity remains a major issue: the initial version was constructive but more easily parsed by a machine than by a human; however, the author has since provided a greatly improved revision. Overall, this original, technically accurate (within what I understood), and thought-provoking paper is worth publishing.

The preliminary results do not yet demonstrate whether the highly complex graph-based differentiable memory affords greater learning or generalization capacity than alternative approaches. Performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to the best memory networks, but still worse than more traditional rule induction approaches.
