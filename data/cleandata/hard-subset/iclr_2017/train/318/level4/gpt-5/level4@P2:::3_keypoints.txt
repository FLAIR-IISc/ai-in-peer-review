- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) that learns dynamic, differentiable graph-structured intermediate states via five trainable graph operations.  
- Instantiation uses a GRU sentence encoder to incrementally build graphs and answer queries, showing strong bAbI task performance and rule learning on cellular automata and Turing machines.  
- Decision: Reject.  
- Main reason 1: Primary results rely on strong supervision of full intermediate graphs and direct textual references, limiting generality and making comparisons unfair.  
- Main reason 2: Paper is dense and hard to follow; breadth of content needs a more focused presentation for conference format.  
- Motivation is solid and the problem is well positioned relative to GNNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, and HAM.  
- Conceptually neat to compose differentiable graph operators, but novelty over GGS-NN is incremental without stronger evidence.  
- Need ablations to show which of the five transformations are necessary for gains.  
- Fairness concern: strongest baselines are end-to-end while GGT-NN gets intermediate graph supervision and direct references.  
- Missing results: performance without any intermediate graph supervision.  
- Empirical strengths: impressive accuracies on many bAbI tasks (pathfinding, induction) and generalization in rule-learning tasks.  
- Empirical weaknesses: poor performance on task 17 and limited diagnostics of learned graphs and error modes.  
- Scalability concern: method is quadratic in node count due to dense message passing and edge updates; limited timing/memory analysis provided.  
- Suggest streamlining the paper to focus on core model and one application, moving engineering details to the appendix.  
- Suggest adding a concise figure tracing one example through the five transformations end-to-end.  
- Experimental suggestions: run ablations removing each transformation, remove intermediate graph supervision, and vary direct-reference strength.  
- Suggest including baselines given comparable supervision (e.g., MemNN/DNC with supporting sentences) for fair comparison.  
- Recommend reporting computational costs: nodes/edges per task, training time, memory, and scaling behavior.  
- Recommend testing on bAbI-10k and out-of-distribution entity counts to assess robustness and generalization.  
- Recommend evaluating tasks without curated graph supervision to demonstrate real-world feasibility.  
- Presentation suggestions: clarify node types/strengths and selection, and distinguish teacher-forced vs. predicted graph states in notation.  
- Key question: How does performance change with no or sparse intermediate graph supervision?  
- Key question: Can the model be trained on bAbI with only answer supervision and no direct references, and with what stability/accuracy?  
- Key question: How are numbers of node/edge types chosen and how sensitive are results to these choices?  
- Key question: What is the max number of nodes added per timestep and how is runaway growth prevented?  
- Key question: Which of the five transformations contribute most to pathfinding and induction (quantitative ablations)?  
- Key question: Can you provide baselines trained with comparable supervision and address task 17 with increased capacity/more data?  
- Key question: What are time/memory costs per example and have sparse adjacency or attentive neighbor schemes been tried to reduce quadratic costs?  
- Key question: Can you show example predicted graphs and typical errors to assess interpretability and task alignment?
