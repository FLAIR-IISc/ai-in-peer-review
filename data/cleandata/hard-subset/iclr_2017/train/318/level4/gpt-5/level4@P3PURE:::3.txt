This paper extends the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network with the ability to perform complex, differentiable graph transformations. The central idea is to learn how to build and modify an internal graph-structured representation to solve tasks, with a particular focus on question answering in this work. The authors propose five differentiable transformation operators that are learned from data, typically under supervised training where the full graph state is provided at each time step. They present a specific instantiation that takes a sequence as input and iteratively updates an internal graph state until producing a final prediction, demonstrating promising results on QA tasks such as bAbI.

Strengths:
- The approach is compelling: the model maintains a complex graph-structured state while remaining fully differentiable and trainable with gradient-based methods.
- It represents a successful integration of continuous and symbolic representations.
- Compared to recent models that incorporate symbolic elements into differentiable architectures (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), this framework appears more general because the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve over time.

Concerns:
- The training procedure relies on supervision of the full graph state at each time step. While feasible for specific benchmarks (e.g., bAbI), this assumption is unlikely to scale to more complex or realistic problems, limiting the practical applicability of the method as presented.
- The paper is very dense. The breadth and depth of material feel more suitable for a journal article than a conference paper, and the current presentation is difficult to follow due to its density.

Overall, this is an interesting and ambitious contribution that advances differentiable graph-structured reasoning and offers a potentially more general alternative to existing differentiable memory and symbolic-augmented models. However, the reliance on step-by-step graph-state supervision is a major limitation for broader applicability, and the paper would benefit from a clearer, less dense presentation better suited to a conference format.
