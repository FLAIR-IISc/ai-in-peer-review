This paper proposes learning, on the fly, to represent a dialog as a graph that serves as the model’s memory, with an initial demonstration on the bAbI tasks. Graph construction is part of the inference process, while there is longer-term representation learning to estimate the graph transformation parameters and to encode sentences as inputs to the graph. To my knowledge, this is the first implementation of a differentiable memory in the form of a graph. Although it is substantially more complex than prior approaches such as Memory Networks and does not yet yield significant performance gains on bAbI, the work is still preliminary, and representing memory as a graph appears more powerful than a stack.

Clarity is a major issue, though the authors have made substantial improvements over the initial version, which read more like a machine-generated description than a human-readable paper. The current version is much clearer. The paper is original, technically accurate to the best of my understanding, and thought-provoking. I believe it is worth publishing.

The preliminary results do not yet demonstrate that the highly complex, graph-based differentiable memory provides greater learning or generalization capacity than existing methods. Performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to strong Memory Network baselines, but still worse than more traditional rule-induction approaches.
