1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes a framework of differentiable graph transformations that enables learning graph-to-graph state transitions and outputs via gradient descent. Building on GGS-NNs, the authors introduce GGT-NNs that can construct and modify graphs as an explicit intermediate representation and produce either graph-structured or textual outputs. This formulation maps naturally to tasks where the world state is graph-structured and evolves over time (e.g., cellular automata), and to text-based question answering where a latent graph can support reasoning. The model is demonstrated on the bAbI tasks, where it reportedly outperforms prior approaches, and on Rule 30, where the graph grows by adding neighbor-linked nodes and uses special 0/1 nodes to encode values. The idea of using explicit differentiable graph edits is compelling and well-motivated by the literature on GNNs/GGS-NNs.

2. Decision
Accept (weak). The paper presents a novel and well-motivated extension of GGS-NNs with differentiable graph transformations, and shows strong empirical performance on the bAbI benchmark with graph-based intermediate representations.

Key reasons:
- Clear, novel contribution: a composable set of differentiable graph operations (add/update nodes, update edges, propagate, aggregate) that support graph-to-graph learning and text output.
- Empirical impact: reported state-of-the-art results on bAbI, showing the usefulness of explicit graph structure for reasoning.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning graphical state transitions and leveraging graphs as intermediate memory, a gap not handled by standard sequence models or fixed-structure GNNs. This is well grounded in prior work (GNNs, GGS-NNs).
- Technical approach: The transformations are differentiable and modular, enabling end-to-end training. The integration with sequence encoders and attention-like direct references is reasonable and aligns with recent memory-based models, but preserves explicit, interpretable structure.
- Evidence: On bAbI, the approach reportedly improves over strong baselines, suggesting the benefits of graph-structured reasoning. For the cellular automaton (Rule 30), the setup is natural (growing graph with neighbor links and 0/1 nodes), and the model can in principle learn the evolution as graph-to-graph transitions. However, the Rule 30 experiment appears far from solved in practice, indicating possible training or modeling limitations for more algorithmic dynamics.

4. Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarify supervision: Distinguish clearly between “strong” graph supervision vs. end-to-end training; quantify how much the bAbI gains rely on access to gold graphs and direct references.
- Ablations: Provide ablations for each transformation (e.g., without edge updates, without intermediate propagation, without direct reference) to isolate contributions.
- Rule 30 analysis: Diagnose why performance lags (capacity, training horizon, propagation depth, sparsity, curriculum). Include learning curves, failure modes, and generalization to longer horizons.
- Complexity: Discuss time/space costs versus memory networks/DNCs, and report empirical scaling with graph size; consider sparsification or localized updates.
- Robustness to node/edge permutations: Explain how the model handles unordered node creation and whether training enforces permutation invariance or relies on ordering heuristics.
- Interpretability: Provide qualitative visualizations of learned graphs on bAbI and automata, and link them to reasoning steps.
- Reproducibility: Include hyperparameters, training details, and, if possible, code.

Questions for the authors
- How essential is strong supervision on intermediate graphs for bAbI performance? What accuracy is achievable without graph-level supervision and without direct references?
- What is the propagation depth and how does it affect long-range reasoning (e.g., pathfinding)? Did you observe vanishing/exploding issues with deeper propagation?
- For Rule 30, what limits learning (model capacity, optimization, insufficient horizon, or representation bias)? Did curriculum learning or longer training help?
- How sensitive is performance to the maximum number of nodes added per step and to graph growth policies?
- What is the computational complexity per step in practice (as a function of node and edge counts), and how large can graphs be before training becomes impractical?
- How does the model behave under noisy or partially incorrect graph supervision, and can it self-correct?
- Did you compare against end-to-end models with external memory (e.g., DNC) on exactly the same supervision regime to isolate the benefit of explicit graphs?

Overall, the paper offers a promising and interpretable approach to learning with evolving graph states, with compelling bAbI results and a principled formulation. Strengthening the analysis of failure modes on algorithmic tasks and clarifying the role of supervision would further solidify the contribution.
