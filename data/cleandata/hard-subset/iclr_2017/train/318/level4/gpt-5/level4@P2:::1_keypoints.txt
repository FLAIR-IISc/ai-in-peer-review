- Proposes GGT-NNs: a differentiable graph-transformation framework that learns graph-to-graph state transitions and outputs via gradient descent.
- Extends GGS-NNs to construct and modify explicit intermediate graphs and produce graph-structured or textual outputs.
- Motivated for tasks with evolving graph-structured world states (e.g., cellular automata) and text QA with latent graph reasoning.
- Demonstrates strong empirical results on bAbI, reportedly outperforming prior approaches using graph-based intermediate representations.
- Applies model to Rule 30 cellular automaton, using growing neighbor-linked nodes and 0/1 nodes to encode values, but performance there is limited.
- Decision: weak accept — novel, well-motivated extension with promising empirical impact.
- Key novel contribution: composable differentiable graph operations (add/update nodes, update edges, propagate, aggregate) enabling graph-to-graph learning.
- Empirical impact: claimed state-of-the-art on bAbI indicates benefits of explicit graph structure for reasoning.
- Technical strength: modular, differentiable transformations integrate with sequence encoders and attention-like references while preserving interpretable structure.
- Evidence caveat: Rule 30 experiments suggest training or modeling limitations for algorithmic dynamics and longer horizons.
- Suggest clarifying the extent of strong vs. end-to-end supervision and quantifying reliance on gold graphs and direct references.
- Recommend ablation studies isolating each transformation’s contribution (edges, propagation, direct reference).
- Ask for deeper analysis of Rule 30 failures (capacity, horizon, propagation depth, sparsity, curriculum) with learning curves and failure modes.
- Request discussion and empirical measurement of time/space complexity and scaling with graph size, and possible sparsification/localized updates.
- Ask how the model handles node/edge permutation invariance and unordered node creation, and whether ordering heuristics are used.
- Suggest providing qualitative visualizations of learned graphs for interpretability and linking to reasoning steps.
- Request reproducibility materials: hyperparameters, training details, and code release.
- Specific questions for authors: necessity of graph supervision, propagation depth effects, limits on Rule 30 learning, sensitivity to node-add limits, practical per-step complexity, robustness to noisy supervision, and comparisons to external memory models like DNC.
