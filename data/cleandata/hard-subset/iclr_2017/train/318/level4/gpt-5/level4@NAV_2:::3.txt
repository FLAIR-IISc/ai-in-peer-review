The paper extends Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks by equipping them with learned, differentiable graph transformations. The central idea is to enable the model to construct and modify a graph-structured internal state for problem solving, with a focus here on question answering. The author introduces five differentiable transformation primitives that are trained on data—typically with strong supervision where the target graph is provided at each timestep. A specific instantiation is presented that ingests a sequence and iteratively updates an internal graph state to produce a final prediction, yielding promising results on QA benchmarks (e.g., bAbI).

The approach is compelling: the model maintains an explicit, richly structured graph state while remaining fully differentiable and trainable via gradient-based methods—an effective blend of continuous and symbolic representations. It also appears more general than recent efforts to inject “symbolic” components into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), since the internal structure is not fixed and can evolve. My primary concern is the training setup: providing full graph states at every timestep is feasible for curated tasks like bAbI but unlikely to scale to more complex, real-world problems. A secondary concern is scope and presentation—the paper is quite dense and might be better suited to a journal format, as the breadth of material makes it challenging to read within a conference constraint.
