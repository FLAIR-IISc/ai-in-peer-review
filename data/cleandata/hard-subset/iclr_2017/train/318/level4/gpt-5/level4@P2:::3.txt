1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that can construct, modify, and use graph-structured intermediate states via a set of differentiable graph transformations. The model defines five trainable operations—node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation—that together enable learning complex, dynamic graph states from sequences of text. A specific instantiation processes sentences with a GRU encoder, incrementally builds a graph, and then answers queries using the learned graph state. The paper reports strong results on many bAbI tasks (including pathfinding and induction), and demonstrates rule learning by modeling a 1D cellular automaton and families of 4-state Turing machines. The approach is appealing for its explicit, interpretable graph memory while remaining differentiable and trainable end-to-end.

2. Decision: Reject
Main reasons:
- The main experimental results rely heavily on strong supervision of the full intermediate graph at each timestep and on direct textual references. This substantially limits the generality and practical applicability of the method, and makes comparisons with unsupervised end-to-end baselines hard to interpret.
- The paper is dense and difficult to follow in its current form. The breadth of content (new model, five transformations, multiple task configurations, extensive appendices) would benefit from a more focused presentation and clearer exposition than is feasible in a conference format.

3. Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement in the literature: The problem—learning and using dynamic graph-structured internal states—is important and well motivated. The positioning relative to GNNs/GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, and HAM is appropriate. The key advantage claimed is an explicit, mutable graph memory that is differentiable.
- Novelty and technical clarity: Conceptually, composing a small set of differentiable graph operators is a neat idea. However, the novelty over GGS-NN is somewhat incremental without stronger evidence that the graph-transformer machinery is necessary (e.g., ablations showing which transformations drive gains).
- Supervision and fairness of comparisons: The strongest results use supervision on the entire graph state per timestep and direct-reference signals, whereas most competing methods are trained end-to-end without such supervision. This undermines claims about comparative performance. The no-direct-reference setting is informative but still supervised with intermediate graphs; results without any graph supervision are missing.
- Empirical support: The paper reports impressive accuracies on several bAbI tasks, notably pathfinding and induction, and demonstrates compelling rule learning on cellular automata and Turing machines with generalization to longer horizons. At the same time, performance on task 17 remains low, and it is unclear whether this reflects limits of capacity, supervision, or representation. More diagnostics (e.g., learned graphs, error modes) would help.
- Practicality and scalability: The approach scales quadratically in the number of nodes due to dense message passing and edge updates. The paper acknowledges this but provides limited empirical timing/memory analysis or sparse approximations. This raises concerns for real-world applicability beyond small synthetic domains.

4. Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Clarity and focus:
  - Streamline the main text to the core model and a single application (e.g., bAbI), and move more engineering details to the appendix.
  - Provide a concise figure tracing one example through the five transformations end-to-end.
- Experimental rigor:
  - Include ablations: remove each transformation (e.g., Tadd, TC, Tprop), remove intermediate graph supervision, vary the strength of direct reference, and report impacts.
  - Add baselines with comparable supervision (e.g., MemNN/DNC given supporting sentences or structured hints) to make fairness explicit.
  - Report computational cost: nodes/edges per task, training time, memory footprint, and scaling with graph size.
- Generalization and robustness:
  - Evaluate on bAbI-10k and/or out-of-distribution entity counts to assess robustness beyond the small-data regime.
  - Test the model on tasks without curated graph supervision to demonstrate feasibility in more realistic settings.
- Presentation:
  - Clarify the definition and role of node types, strengths, and how the number of node types is chosen.
  - Tighten notation and consistently distinguish between supervised “teacher-forced” graph states and the model’s predicted states used downstream.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision and training:
  - How does performance change if intermediate graph supervision is removed entirely, or only provided sparsely (e.g., for a subset of timesteps or edges)?
  - Can the model be trained successfully on bAbI with only answer supervision and no direct references? If so, what accuracy and training stability do you observe?
- Model capacity and design choices:
  - How are the numbers of node types and edge types selected per task? Are they fixed a priori or learned, and how sensitive are results to these choices?
  - What is the maximum number of nodes added per timestep (and overall)? How do you prevent runaway growth or degenerate graphs?
  - Which of the five transformations contribute most to performance on pathfinding and induction? Please provide quantitative ablations.
- Comparisons and fairness:
  - Could you provide baselines trained with comparable supervision (e.g., sentence relevance or partial graph hints) to contextualize the gains?
  - For task 17, have you tried increasing model capacity, using more training data (10k), or alternative graph encodings to address the larger entity space?
- Efficiency and scalability:
  - What are the time and memory costs per example across tasks, and how do they scale with node/edge counts? Have you tried sparse adjacency or attention over neighbors to reduce quadratic costs?
- Interpretability:
  - Can you show example predicted graphs for several bAbI tasks and the rule-learning settings, including typical errors? Are the learned graphs human-interpretable and task-aligned?

Overall, this is an exciting direction that blends differentiable learning with explicit, dynamic graph structure. With clearer exposition, stronger ablations, and results under weaker supervision, it could become a compelling contribution.
