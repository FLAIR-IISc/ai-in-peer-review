- Proposal: Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs) — a differentiable, learnable graph state with transformations (node add/update, edge update, propagation, aggregation) to build/modify graphs from text and produce textual or graph outputs.
- Motivation: Learn both graph construction and graph reasoning end-to-end, providing interpretable intermediate structure that bridges GNNs and memory-based models.
- Empirical results: Strong performance on bAbI (19/20 tasks ≥95% with direct reference under strong supervision) and successful rule learning/generalization on cellular automata and Turing machines.
- Technical contribution: Well-specified, end-to-end differentiable transformations, sensible supervision for unordered nodes (set loss/optimal matching), and an interpretable direct-reference mechanism.
- Rigor and clarity: Includes pseudocode, node/edge losses, complexity discussion, and comparisons to a broad set of baselines.
- Decision: Accept (weak) — key reasons are clear architectural novelty and compelling empirical evidence, especially on graph-suited tasks like path finding.
- Strength: Principled toolkit for constructing/transforming graphs that complements prior GNN/GGS-NN work and links to memory models.
- Strength: Ablations and comparisons included; notable gains on tasks that naturally map to graphs (pathfinding, induction).
- Main limitation: Best results depend on strong supervision (full intermediate graphs) and an explicit direct-reference cue tied to entity names, limiting claims about fully end-to-end learning from raw text.
- Engineering caveat: Some graph representations are task-engineered (e.g., history nodes), which may leak information and complicate fairness/generalization.
- Scalability concern: Quadratic costs in dense graphs raise questions about efficiency for large graphs.
- Suggested clarification: Explicitly state which comparisons use strong supervision and report reduced-supervision results (partial/no graph supervision, semi-supervised).
- Suggested detail: Systematically describe all bAbI graph augmentations (e.g., history linked lists) and show impact, plus sequence-of-graphs results side-by-side.
- Suggested ablations: Report effects of each transformation (e.g., remove TC, disable Tadd), vary propagation steps, and quantify direct-reference contribution and sensitivity to max nodes/node-strength stopping.
- Suggested efficiency analysis: Provide complexity and wall-clock/memory profiles versus graph size and discuss sparse implementations or neighborhood sampling strategies.
- Suggested analysis: Show qualitative examples of learned graphs (with/without supervision), error breakdowns, and visualizations of reasoning trajectories.
- Suggested rule-task analyses: Provide per-timestep accuracy, failure modes on long rollouts, and clarify train/test sampling and held-out rule sets.
- Question on supervision: How well does the model learn without intermediate graph supervision and can semi-supervised training preserve performance?
- Question on direct reference: How much does performance drop without direct reference or with noisy references, and can learned attention replace explicit name matching?
- Question on entity identity: How does Tadd avoid creating duplicate nodes for the same entity and handle coreference or multiword entities?
- Question on node addition: What is the stopping criterion for adding nodes per sentence, and how sensitive are results to the maximum node limit?
- Question on bAbI engineering: Do history nodes leak information or bias tasks, and can the sequence-of-graphs variant reach main-model accuracy with more training/regularization?
- Question on rule discovery: Are Turing-machine rule sets/initial tapes sampled uniformly and are train/test sets disjoint; how do results change with increased states/symbols?
- Question on scalability: What are the largest graphs trained successfully and any progress on sparse adjacency or neighborhood-restricted propagation?
- Reproducibility request: Provide an anonymized code link and include key hyperparameters and training settings (optimizer, learning rate, propagation steps, hidden sizes).
