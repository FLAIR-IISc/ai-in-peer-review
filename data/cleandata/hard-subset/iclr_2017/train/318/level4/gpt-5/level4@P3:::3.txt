Summary and contributions
The paper introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that can construct, maintain, and transform an explicit, differentiable graph-structured state while processing text or graphs. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph operations—node addition, node state update (including a direct-reference variant), edge update, message-passing propagation, and graph-level aggregation—that can be composed into end-to-end trainable architectures. A concrete configuration is presented for text-based question answering: the model incrementally builds an internal graph from sentences and uses that graph to answer queries. The paper also demonstrates the approach on rule-learning tasks (a 1D cellular automaton and Turing machines), showing that the model can learn to apply complex transition rules and generalize to longer sequences. On bAbI, GGT-NNs achieve strong results on most tasks (notably pathfinding), particularly when “direct reference” supervision is available.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Heavy reliance on strong supervision (full graph states and direct references at each timestep) limits the generality of the approach and makes comparisons to prior work (largely end-to-end) less informative.
- Clarity and scope: the paper is dense, with crucial technical details and design choices deferred to appendices; as written, it reads more like a journal article and is difficult to follow in a conference format.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation. The paper addresses how to learn state transitions over structured, relational world models represented as graphs, from unstructured inputs. This is a timely and important question. The motivation is well grounded in the literature: it builds directly on GG-NNs/GGS-NNs and relates to memory-augmented models (MemN2N, DNC) and graph neural networks. The case that explicit, manipulable graph states can benefit tasks like pathfinding and induction is compelling, and the bAbI and rule-discovery experiments are appropriate.

2) Approach and placement. The proposed differentiable graph transformations are clearly defined and, in principle, broadly applicable. Allowing soft node/edge existence, permutation-aware node addition, and direct-reference updates are thoughtful design choices. However, the novelty over GGS-NNs is incremental rather than transformative: the main step is to make the graph itself an evolving, differentiable latent with node/edge strengths and to add a suite of parameterized update operators. This is meaningful, but the paper would benefit from a more explicit positioning of what can and cannot be achieved with prior GGNN/GGSNN variants (e.g., could one simulate most of these operations by preconstructing graphs?).

3) Evidence and rigor. The empirical results are promising but hinge on strong supervision:
- For bAbI, the model is trained with full graph supervision at each step and an oracle-like “direct reference” that maps words to entity types, whereas most baselines are end-to-end. The authors acknowledge this but the comparison then mixes supervision regimes, reducing interpretability of the gains.
- The ablation on direct reference shows the model without it performs substantially worse on many tasks, underscoring that the core difficulty—grounding symbols to entities and relations from raw text—remains largely offloaded to supervision.
- For rule discovery, results are strong and include generalization to longer horizons. This is a clear positive, though more detail on data diversity, noise, and failure cases would strengthen the claims.
- Missing analyses: complexity/runtime/memory, scalability to larger graphs, robustness to supervision noise, sensitivity to hyperparameters, and a systematic ablation of the five transformations and the supervision components (node vs edge vs answer loss). These omissions make it hard to assess the method’s practicality and the necessity of each component.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify supervision regimes. Provide results with weaker supervision: (a) answer-only supervision; (b) partial graph supervision (e.g., edges only, or only at select timesteps); (c) noisy or learned direct reference (entity linking) rather than string matching. Quantify the minimal supervision needed to obtain meaningful graphs.
- Fair comparisons. Separate tables by supervision level and include a baseline that uses the same strong supervision (e.g., a GGNN or MemNN variant with oracle entity linking and relevant-sentence supervision). This will isolate the benefit of the proposed graph transformations.
- Ablations. Report the effect of removing each transformation; varying the number of propagation steps; and training without replacing predicted nodes/edges with ground truth (i.e., avoid teacher-forcing in the graph). Include the impact of the permutation-invariant node-addition loss.
- Scalability and complexity. Provide time/memory complexity as a function of |V|, Y, and propagation steps T, plus empirical runtimes and memory use. Explore sparse connectivity or sampled neighborhoods to mitigate the quadratic scaling.
- Clarity and organization. Move key algorithmic details (the five transformations and the QA configuration) from appendices into the main text. Tighten notation and fix citation formatting. A running toy example would improve readability. Provide more qualitative visualizations of learned graphs over time for bAbI, not only for the rule tasks.
- Generalization and robustness. Evaluate on more realistic text (entity aliases, multiword names, coreference) and report sensitivity to errors in direct reference. Consider tasks without pre-exposed knowledge graphs.
- Reproducibility. Consolidate hyperparameters, training schedules, loss weights (node/edge/answer), and early-stopping criteria. Ensure the code link is functional and includes bAbI graph-conversion scripts.

Questions for the authors
- What is the minimal level of supervision under which GGT-NNs still learn useful graphs and achieve competitive QA accuracy? Can you report answer-only training results, even if lower?
- How is the loss balanced between node addition, edge updates, and answer prediction? Does strong graph supervision cause exposure bias at test time when you don’t replace predicted structures with ground truth?
- How sensitive is performance to errors in direct reference? Could a learned entity linker replace the name-matching heuristic, and what accuracy drop should we expect?
- What is the computational footprint (time, memory) as |V| grows, and how large can graphs be in practice? Have you tried sparse edges or neighborhood sampling?
- How many nodes are typically added per step, and does the model ever over-generate nodes? Are there mechanisms to prune unused nodes?
- In the rule-discovery tasks, how diverse are the sampled Turing machine rules? Do failures correlate with particular transition structures (e.g., many “no move” rules)?
- Could you compare against a GGNN/GGSNN baseline that receives the same graph supervision but does not modify structure, to isolate the benefit of structural updates?

Overall, the paper presents an appealing direction—differentiable manipulation of explicit graphs—and shows promising results under strong supervision. With clearer exposition, stronger ablations, and weaker-supervision experiments, it could make a compelling future submission.
