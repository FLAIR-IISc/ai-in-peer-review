1. Summary of contributions
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that can construct and modify graph-structured intermediate representations while remaining fully differentiable and trainable with gradient-based methods. The model defines five learnable, differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) and composes them to maintain and evolve an internal graph state. A particular instantiation is presented for question answering: given a sequence of sentences, the model incrementally updates an internal graph and produces a final prediction. The approach is compelling in that it mixes continuous and symbolic representations: it maintains an explicit, interpretable graph that can grow and change, yet is trained end-to-end. Compared to prior differentiable architectures (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), this representation is more flexible because the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve with the input. The paper reports promising results on QA tasks (e.g., bAbI).

2. Decision
Reject. Primary reasons:
- The training setup relies on strong supervision by providing the full graph state at each timestep, which is feasible for specific synthetic tasks (e.g., bAbI) but does not appear viable for more complex or realistic problems.
- The paper is dense and reads more like a journal article than a conference paper, which makes it hard to digest within the conference format.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: The paper tackles learning to construct and transform graph-structured internal states to solve tasks such as QA. This is a well-motivated and important problem, bridging continuous neural computation and symbolic structure.
- Positioning and motivation: The paper is well placed in the literature and clearly extends GGS-NNs by introducing graph-building and graph-editing operations that are differentiable. The argument that this framework is more general than previous “differentiable memory” models is plausible because the topology and content of the graph can change over time.
- Evidence and rigor: The reported results on QA are interesting, suggesting the model can successfully maintain and use a structured internal state. However, the empirical evidence relies on strong supervision where the correct graph is provided at each timestep during training. While this is reasonable for demonstrating feasibility, it substantially limits generality. In many realistic scenarios, intermediate graph states are latent and cannot be supervised step by step. Without experiments showing that GGT-NN can be trained with weaker supervision (e.g., supervision only on final answers, or partial graph supervision), it is hard to conclude that the approach will scale beyond curated synthetic tasks. This training requirement is my main concern.
- Readability and scope: The paper is ambitious and technically dense. While the breadth is impressive, the current presentation is heavy and difficult to follow in a conference setting. Some consolidation, sharper focus on the core contributions, and clearer exposition would improve accessibility.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affected my decision)
- Clarify supervision regimes: Explicitly distinguish which components are trained with full graph supervision versus answer-only supervision. Provide ablations showing performance as supervision is reduced (e.g., no per-timestep node supervision, no edge supervision, delayed supervision).
- Reduce engineering overhead: Discuss the practicality of constructing ground-truth graphs for new domains. If possible, include a setting where the graph is entirely latent and learned end-to-end from input–output pairs.
- Model interpretability: One of the strengths is the explicit internal graph. Include qualitative analyses: examples of learned graphs across timesteps, failure cases, and how specific transformations contribute to solving a query.
- Complexity and scalability: Analyze time/space complexity as the number of nodes/edges grows. Discuss bottlenecks and potential optimizations (e.g., sparsity, localized updates), and provide empirical scaling curves.
- Comparisons and ablations: Where possible, compare against baselines trained under the same supervision level. Include ablations over the five transformations to show which are most critical. If “direct reference” is used, show results without it to quantify its impact.
- Presentation: Streamline the exposition by foregrounding the central transformation framework, moving some implementation detail to the appendix, and tightening related work to highlight the main distinctions.

Questions for the authors
- Supervision requirements: How essential is per-timestep graph supervision for successful training? Can the model learn competitive internal graphs when supervising only final answers? What performance drop occurs as graph supervision is reduced or removed?
- Generality: Beyond synthetic QA, how would you obtain training graphs in realistic settings? Do you envision semi-supervised or self-supervised strategies (e.g., consistency losses, structural priors) to replace full supervision?
- Role of each transformation: Which of the five transformations are indispensable in practice for QA? Do simpler variants (e.g., no node addition, only edge updates) suffice on some tasks?
- Stability and training dynamics: Did you observe instability or mode collapse in node/edge creation? What regularization or constraints were most important?
- Scalability: What are the largest graphs you trained on? How does training time and memory scale with graph size, and what optimizations would you prioritize?
- Transfer and robustness: Can a model trained on one domain adapt to another with minimal supervision? How sensitive is performance to noisy or ambiguous inputs?

Overall, the proposed framework is creative and promising, and I appreciate the attempt to blend symbolic structure with differentiable computation. Addressing the supervision dependency and improving the clarity and focus of the presentation would significantly strengthen the paper.
