- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Networks (GGT-NNs), a differentiable, explicit graph-structured memory built and transformed from text for reasoning and output generation.
- Model operations include node addition, node updates with optional direct reference, edge updates, message passing, and aggregation to answer queries or produce graphs.
- Demonstrated strong performance on bAbI tasks (notably pathfinding and induction) and successful rule discovery/generalization in cellular automata and 4-state Turing machines.
- Paper is original, technically sound, and the explicit graph memory is a compelling, broadly useful idea.
- Decision: Accept — motivated by novelty and promising empirical results on graph-suited tasks and rule learning.
- Motivation and positioning relative to GNNs, memory networks, and DNCs are clear; graph-structured memory offers interpretability and natural inductive bias.
- Empirical support: competitive bAbI results with strong supervision; excels on pathfinding (task 19) and induction (task 16), weaker on positional reasoning (task 17).
- Empirical support: learns update rules for Rule 30 and families of Turing machines, generalizing to longer rollouts.
- Major limitation: strongest results depend on heavy supervision (gold graphs and direct reference) while many baselines are end-to-end.
- Efficiency concern: model complexity and quadratic scaling with graph size raise runtime/memory questions.
- Ablation gap: need studies isolating each transformation, supervision levels, propagation steps, and model variance.
- Clarity: revised paper improved but remains dense; key protocol choices (supervision, direct reference, train/test graph access) should be more explicit in the main text.
- Suggestion: precisely clarify supervision protocol and report performance with weaker or no graph supervision.
- Suggestion: report parameter counts, average graph sizes, runtime/memory usage, and scaling behavior; discuss sparsity or batching tricks.
- Suggestion: expand ablations to isolate Tadd, Th, TC, Tprop, Trepr, propagation depth, hidden size, and direct reference effects.
- Suggestion: provide fair comparisons by matching supervised vs supervised and end-to-end vs end-to-end regimes (e.g., 1k vs 10k examples).
- Suggestion: include error analysis for failures (e.g., task 17) and strategies to mitigate entity explosion.
- Suggestion: improve interpretability with extracted graph examples and illustrations of message passing solving tasks like pathfinding.
- Suggestion: streamline notation, move essential appendix details into main text, and add a running example for Algorithm 1.
- Question: how much does accuracy degrade with reduced or removed graph supervision; can useful graphs be learned in weak/semi-supervised settings?
- Question: how are node types and multi-word entities resolved beyond bAbI; what is the quantitative gain from direct reference and can a learned mention detector replace string matching?
- Question: what are typical node/edge counts, time/memory footprint per step, and scaling; have sparse updates or attention been tried to reduce quadratic costs?
- Question: sensitivity to propagation depth and whether deeper propagation helps multi-hop reasoning tasks?
- Question: can learned transformations yield human-interpretable rules (e.g., via probing fset/freset or message functions), and how robust is long-horizon generalization beyond 30 steps?
- Question: can you report bAbI results with 10k examples under the same supervision and an end-to-end variant without graph supervision to clarify gaps to memory networks and DNCs?
- Question: ablation-specific: what happens if Tadd or TC are removed, or Trepr is replaced with simpler pooling — which components are most critical?
