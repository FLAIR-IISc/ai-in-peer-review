This paper proposes learning, on the fly, to represent a dialog as a graph that serves as memory, first demonstrated on the bAbI tasks. Graph construction is part of the inference process, while longer-term learning is used to estimate graph-transformation parameters and to encode sentences as inputs to the graph. To my knowledge, this is the first implementation of a differentiable, graph-structured memory. It is substantially more complex than earlier approaches such as Memory Networks, without delivering significant performance gains on the bAbI tasks; however, the work is still very preliminary, and a graph as a memory representation appears considerably more powerful than a stack. Clarity remains a major issue: the initial version was more suited to a machine than a human reader, but the authors have since provided a substantially improved revision. Overall, this original, technically accurate (to the extent I understood), and thought-provoking paper merits publication.

The preliminary results do not yet show whether this highly complex graph-based differentiable memory has greater learning or generalization capacity than alternative approaches.
