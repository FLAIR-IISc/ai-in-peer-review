This paper introduces on-the-fly learning to represent a dialogue as a graph serving as memory, with an initial demonstration on the bAbI tasks. Graph induction is integrated into the inference process, while longer-term representation learning is used to learn graph transformation parameters and to encode sentences as inputs to the graph. To my knowledge, this is the first implementation of a differentiable memory instantiated as a graph: it is substantially more complex than prior approaches such as memory networks, without clear performance gains on the bAbI tasks so far. Nonetheless, the work is very preliminary, and a graph-based memory representation appears considerably more expressive than a stack. Clarity is a significant concern, but from an initial version that was constructive yet more machine-readable than human-readable, the author produced a greatly improved later version. This original, seemingly technically correct (to the extent I could assess), and thought-provoking paper merits publication.
The preliminary results do not yet establish whether the highly complex graph-based differentiable memory offers greater learning or generalization capacity than other methods.
