- Proposes a neural architecture that builds and edits a graph-structured, differentiable memory on the fly from text with learned graph transformations and sentence encoders.
- Appears to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory explicitly represented as a graph; evaluated mainly on bAbI tasks.
- Idea is original, technically sound from reviewer’s checks, and the revised manuscript is substantially clearer.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reasons for accept: novel/impactful direction (explicit differentiable graph memory) and feasibility demonstrated by matching strong bAbI baselines.
- Problem targeted: learning to construct, update, and use graph-structured internal state for relational reasoning over text (entities, relations, paths).
- Literature placement: builds on GG-NNs/GGS-NNs, contrasts with memory networks and other differentiable memories; motivation for graphs is convincing despite added complexity.
- Results: partially supportive — matches baselines but no clear empirical advantage or improved generalization; complexity vs benefit not yet established.
- Strength: clear conceptual novelty of a fully differentiable, editable graph memory.
- Strength: integrates graph construction with inference rather than relying on pre-specified graphs.
- Strength: preliminary results show the model can learn to parse text into useful graphs and reason over them.
- Strength: revised manuscript improved clarity.
- Weakness: no demonstrated empirical gains over simpler memory mechanisms on bAbI despite higher complexity.
- Weakness: insufficient evidence of improved generalization; results remain preliminary.
- Weakness: model description is dense and hard to follow in parts.
- Weakness: comparisons to traditional rule-induction systems are not well documented with citations and numbers.
- Suggested improvements: streamline model description, add a step-by-step running example of graph updates, and separate training vs test-time behavior.
- Suggested improvements: include time/space complexity analysis and practical profiling (nodes/edges per instance, runtime).
- Suggested improvements: run ablations to isolate contributions of key design choices (direct reference, propagation, edge-update variants).
- Suggested improvements: clearly state supervision levels and include end-to-end baselines for fair comparison.
- Suggested improvements: provide explicit references and numbers if claiming inferior performance versus rule-based induction, or rephrase.
- Suggested improvements: add qualitative error analysis showing where graph memory helps and where it fails.
- Suggested improvements: include implementation details, hyperparameters, training setup, and ideally release code for reproducibility.
- Questions for authors: how much graph-level supervision is required and effect of reducing it?
- Questions for authors: how is the direct-reference mechanism implemented, how robust is it to noise, and what if disabled at test time?
- Questions for authors: typical graph sizes in bAbI, per-step computational/memory cost, and scaling bottlenecks?
- Questions for authors: which traditional rule-induction systems are referenced and what are exact comparative results on the same splits?
- Questions for authors: do models trained on shorter bAbI contexts generalize to longer contexts or more entities?
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of results to hyperparameters (propagation steps, hidden size, edge types)?
- Questions for authors: dominant failure modes—incorrect graph construction vs incorrect reasoning?
- Questions for authors: can the approach incorporate sparsity or local attention to mitigate quadratic scaling and are there preliminary results?
