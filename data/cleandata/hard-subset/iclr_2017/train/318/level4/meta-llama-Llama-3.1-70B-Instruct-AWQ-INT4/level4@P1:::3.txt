The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN) by incorporating the ability to produce complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to develop a method that can build or modify a graph structure as an internal representation for solving problems, particularly question-answering tasks. The author introduces five differentiable transformations that can be learned on a training set, typically in a supervised fashion where the state of the graph is given at each time step.

A specific instance of the model is presented, which takes a sequence as input and iteratively updates an internal graph state to produce a final prediction. This model can be applied to solve question-answering tasks, such as the bAbI tasks, with promising results.

The approach in this paper is interesting, as the proposed model can maintain a representation of its current state as a complex graph while remaining differentiable and easily learnable through gradient-descent techniques. This can be seen as a successful attempt to combine continuous and symbolic representations. Moreover, the model seems more general than recent attempts to incorporate symbolic elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines), as the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve.

However, my main concerns are about the training process and the overall content of the paper. The model is trained by providing the state of the graph at each time step, which may be feasible for specific tasks like bAbI but may not be a viable solution for more complex problems. Furthermore, the paper's content and density may be more suitable for a journal format rather than a conference format, making it challenging to read.

Decision: Accept (with minor revisions)

The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to combining neural networks with graphs, allowing the graph structure to be built and modified based on unstructured input. The model's ability to maintain a complex graph representation while remaining differentiable is a significant contribution. However, the training process and the paper's overall content need to be addressed to make the work more accessible and applicable to a broader range of problems.

Supporting arguments:

* The paper's core idea of extending GGS-NNs to produce complex graph transformations is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature.
* The proposed model is able to successfully solve question-answering tasks, such as the bAbI tasks, with promising results.
* The model's ability to maintain a complex graph representation while remaining differentiable is a significant contribution.

Additional feedback:

* To improve the paper's accessibility, consider breaking down the content into smaller, more focused sections, and providing more visual aids to illustrate the model's architecture and training process.
* Discuss the limitations of the training process and potential solutions for more complex problems.
* Consider providing more analysis on the model's performance, such as comparing it to other state-of-the-art models or evaluating its generalization capabilities.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details on the training process, particularly on how the state of the graph is given at each time step?
* How do you plan to address the limitations of the training process for more complex problems?
* Can you provide more analysis on the model's performance, such as comparing it to other state-of-the-art models or evaluating its generalization capabilities?
