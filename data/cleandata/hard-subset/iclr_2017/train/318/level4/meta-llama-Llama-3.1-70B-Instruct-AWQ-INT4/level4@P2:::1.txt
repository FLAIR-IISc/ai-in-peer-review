The paper introduces a novel neural network architecture, called the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which combines graph-structured intermediate representations with a recurrent input model to incrementally construct a graph given natural language input. The GGT-NN model is an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN) and allows the graph structure to be built and modified based on unstructured input.

The specific question tackled by the paper is how to learn graph-structured state transitions from textual input. The approach is well-motivated, building upon previous work on GGS-NNs and introducing a set of differentiable graph transformations that enable the model to learn graph->graph classification tasks using gradient descent.

The paper provides strong evidence to support its claims, including experimental results on the bAbI tasks and two additional tasks: a 1-dimensional cellular automaton and an arbitrary 4-state Turing machine. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the GGT-NN model in learning complex graph-structured state transitions and its ability to generalize to new tasks.

The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and detailed explanation of the GGT-NN model and its components. The experimental results are also well-presented, and the authors provide a thorough analysis of the results.

I recommend accepting this paper, as it presents a novel and well-motivated approach to learning graph-structured state transitions from textual input. The experimental results are strong, and the paper provides a thorough analysis of the results.

One potential area for improvement is in the presentation of the experimental results. While the results are well-presented, it would be helpful to include more visualizations of the learned graph structures and state transitions. This would provide a clearer understanding of how the GGT-NN model is learning to represent and manipulate graph-structured data.

Additionally, it would be interesting to see more analysis of the learned graph transformations and how they relate to the specific tasks being learned. For example, the authors could provide more insight into how the model is using the different graph transformations to learn the rules governing the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks.

Overall, however, the paper is well-written and presents a significant contribution to the field of neural networks and graph-structured data.
