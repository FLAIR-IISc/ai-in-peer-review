The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, enabling it to produce complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to develop a method that can build or modify a graph structure as an internal representation for solving problems, with a focus on question-answering tasks. The authors introduce five differentiable transformations that can be learned from a training set, typically in a supervised manner where the graph state is provided at each time step.

A specific instance of the model is presented, which takes a sequence as input and iteratively updates an internal graph state to produce a final prediction. This approach can be applied to question-answering tasks, such as the bAbI dataset, with promising results. The proposed model is notable for maintaining a complex graph representation of its current state while remaining differentiable, making it easily learnable through gradient-descent techniques.

This approach can be seen as a successful attempt to integrate continuous and symbolic representations. Furthermore, it appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate symbolic elements into differentiable models, such as Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines, as the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve.

However, my primary concern is the training method, which relies on providing the graph state at each time step. While this may be feasible for specific tasks like bAbI, it may not be a viable solution for more complex problems. Additionally, I question whether the paper's content is better suited for a journal format rather than a conference format. The article's density and complexity make it challenging to read, which may hinder its impact.

To improve clarity and readability, the authors could consider expanding on key concepts, providing more intuitive explanations, and adding visual aids to facilitate understanding. Overall, the paper presents an intriguing approach, but addressing these concerns could enhance its contribution to the field.
