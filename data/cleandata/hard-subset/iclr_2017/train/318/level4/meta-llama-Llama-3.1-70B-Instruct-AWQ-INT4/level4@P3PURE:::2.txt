This paper proposes a novel approach to representing a dialog as a graph, which serves as a differentiable memory, and demonstrates its application on the bAbI tasks. Notably, graph learning is an integral part of the inference process, supplemented by long-term representation learning to acquire graph transformation parameters and encode sentences as input to the graph. This work appears to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory in the form of a graph, offering a more complex and potentially powerful alternative to previous approaches, such as memory networks.

Although the proposed approach does not yield significant performance gains on the bAbI tasks compared to existing methods, its preliminary nature and the promise of graph-based representation make it an interesting and valuable contribution. The representation of memory as a graph seems more expressive than a stack, but it remains to be seen whether the increased complexity of this approach translates to improved learning or generalization capacity.

A significant issue with the paper is its clarity, which has been improved substantially in the revised version. The original submission was constructive but challenging to follow, whereas the later version is more readable and engaging. Overall, this original, technically accurate, and thought-provoking paper is worth publishing.

The preliminary results do not yet provide conclusive evidence on whether the highly complex graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capacity compared to other approaches. While the performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to that of the best memory networks, it still lags behind more traditional rule induction methods (see [citation]).
