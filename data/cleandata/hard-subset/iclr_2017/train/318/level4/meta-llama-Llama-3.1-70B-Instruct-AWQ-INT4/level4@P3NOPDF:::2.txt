This paper proposes a novel approach to representing dialog as a graph, which serves as a differentiable memory, and demonstrates its application on the bAbI tasks. The graph learning process is integrated into the inference stage, while also incorporating long-term representation learning to discover graph transformation parameters and sentence encoding as input to the graph. Notably, this work appears to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory as a graph.

Although this approach is significantly more complex than previous methods, such as memory networks, it does not yield substantial performance gains on the bAbI tasks. Nevertheless, the representation of memory as a graph seems more powerful than a stack-based representation. The paper's clarity has been a major issue, but the author has made considerable improvements in the revised version, making it more readable and accessible.

The preliminary results presented in this paper do not yet provide conclusive evidence on whether the proposed graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capabilities compared to other approaches. While the performance on the bAbI task is comparable to that of top-performing memory networks, it still falls short of more traditional rule induction methods (see [citation]). Further experimentation and evaluation are necessary to fully understand the potential benefits and limitations of this novel approach.

Despite some remaining clarity issues, this original, technically accurate, and thought-provoking paper is worth publishing. The authors have made a commendable effort to improve the manuscript's readability, and the work contributes a fresh perspective to the field. Overall, this paper has the potential to stimulate interesting discussions and inspire further research in the area of differentiable memory and graph-based representations.
