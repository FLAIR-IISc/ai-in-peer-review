This paper introduces a set of differential graph transformations, enabling the learning of graph-to-graph classification tasks using gradient descent. This concept naturally extends to learning cellular automata, represented as sequences of graphs, where the graph of nodes evolves at each iteration. Special nodes, denoted as 0/1, represent values, and nodes point to their neighbors. The proposed architecture facilitates the learning of these graph sequences. However, the experimental results for the Rule 30 task fell short of achieving a satisfactory solution.

The authors combine this idea with concepts from previous papers, such as Graph-Generated Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NN), to enable the model to produce textual output instead of graph output. By leveraging graphs as intermediate representations, the model achieves state-of-the-art performance on the BaBi tasks. This innovative approach showcases the potential of integrating graph transformations with sequence generation models, leading to improved performance on complex tasks.

One of the strengths of the paper is its ability to merge seemingly disparate concepts, such as graph transformations and sequence generation, into a cohesive framework. The authors demonstrate the flexibility of their approach by applying it to various tasks, including graph-to-graph classification and text generation.

However, there are some limitations to the current work. The experimental results for the Rule 30 task, while promising, do not fully demonstrate the capabilities of the proposed architecture. Furthermore, the relationship between the graph transformations and the sequence generation model could be explored in more depth. Providing additional insights into how these components interact and influence each other would strengthen the paper.

In terms of presentation, the paper is generally well-organized and easy to follow. The authors provide a clear explanation of their proposed architecture and its components. However, some sections could benefit from additional details and explanations. For instance, the connection between the graph transformations and the GGS-NN model could be elaborated upon to facilitate a deeper understanding of the underlying mechanisms.

Overall, the paper presents an intriguing idea and demonstrates its potential through promising experimental results. With some revisions to address the limitations and provide additional clarity, this work could make a significant contribution to the field.
