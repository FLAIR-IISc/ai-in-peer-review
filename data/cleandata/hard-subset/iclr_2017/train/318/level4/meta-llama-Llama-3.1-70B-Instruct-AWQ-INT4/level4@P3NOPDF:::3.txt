The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN) by incorporating the ability to produce complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to develop a method that can build or modify a graph structure as an internal representation to solve problems, particularly question-answering tasks. The authors introduce five differentiable transformations that can be learned from a training set, typically in a supervised fashion where the state of the graph is provided at each time step.

A specific instance of the model is presented, which takes a sequence as input and iteratively updates an internal graph state to produce a final prediction. This approach can be applied to question-answering tasks, such as the bAbI dataset, with promising results. The proposed model is interesting because it maintains a representation of its current state as a complex graph while preserving the property of being differentiable, making it easily learnable through gradient-descent techniques. This can be seen as a successful attempt to combine continuous and symbolic representations.

Compared to recent efforts to incorporate symbolic components into differentiable models, such as Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines, the proposed approach is more general, as the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve. However, my main concern lies in the training process, which relies on providing the state of the graph at each time step. This approach may be feasible for specific tasks like bAbI but may not be applicable to more complex problems.

Additionally, the paper's content and density may be more suitable for a journal format rather than a conference format. The article's complexity and technical details make it challenging to read and comprehend within the confines of a conference paper. The authors may consider expanding on the ideas and experiments in a more extensive journal version, which would allow for a more in-depth exploration of the proposed model and its applications.

Overall, the paper presents an innovative approach to graph transformations and question-answering tasks. While it shows promise, it requires further refinement and clarification to fully address the concerns mentioned above. With some revisions to address these issues, the paper has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field.
