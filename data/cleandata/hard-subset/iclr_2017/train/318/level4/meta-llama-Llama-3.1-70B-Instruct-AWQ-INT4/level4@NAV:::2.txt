This paper introduces an innovative approach to dialog representation by learning on the fly and utilizing a graph as a memory component, initially demonstrated through the bAbI tasks. Notably, graph learning is integrated into the inference process, complemented by long-term representation learning for acquiring graph transformation parameters and sentence encoding as input to the graph. This pioneering implementation of a differentiable memory as a graph appears more complex than preceding memory network approaches, without yielding substantial performance gains in the bAbI tasks. However, as a preliminary work, its potential remains untapped, and the graph-based memory representation exhibits greater power than a traditional stack. 

Although clarity was initially a significant concern, the author's revised version demonstrates substantial improvement, rendering the paper more accessible. The original submission, while technically sound and thought-provoking, has evolved into a valuable contribution worthy of publication.

The preliminary findings do not yet conclusively indicate whether the intricate graph-based differentiable memory surpasses alternative approaches in terms of learning or generalization capacity. While the performance on the bAbI task is comparable to top-performing memory networks, it still falls short of traditional rule induction methods.
