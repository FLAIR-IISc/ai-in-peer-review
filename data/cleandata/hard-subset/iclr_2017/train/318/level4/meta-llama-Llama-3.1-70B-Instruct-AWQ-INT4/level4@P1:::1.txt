The main contribution of this paper appears to be the introduction of a set of differentiable graph transformations, enabling the learning of graph-to-graph classification tasks using gradient descent. This concept naturally applies to tasks such as learning a cellular automaton represented as a sequence of graphs, where the graph of nodes grows at each iteration, with nodes pointing to neighbors and special nodes representing values. The proposed architecture allows for the learning of this sequence of graphs, although the experiment suggests that the task (Rule 30) was not fully solved.

This idea is combined with concepts from previous papers, such as GGS-NN, to enable the model to produce textual output rather than graph output and utilize graphs as intermediate representations. This approach allows the model to achieve state-of-the-art performance on the bAbI tasks.

I decide to accept this paper because it presents a novel and well-motivated approach to graph-to-graph classification tasks, and demonstrates its potential by achieving state-of-the-art performance on the bAbI tasks. The paper is well-written and provides a clear explanation of the proposed architecture and its components.

However, I have some concerns regarding the experiment on the Rule 30 task. The paper mentions that the task was "far from solved," which suggests that the model may not have fully learned the underlying rules of the cellular automaton. I would like the authors to provide more details on the experiment, such as the training data, model parameters, and evaluation metrics used. Additionally, it would be helpful to see a more in-depth analysis of the model's performance on this task, including any errors or limitations that were observed.

To improve the paper, I suggest that the authors provide more context and background information on graph-to-graph classification tasks and their applications. This would help readers who are not familiar with the topic to better understand the significance and potential impact of the proposed approach. Additionally, the authors may want to consider including more visualizations or illustrations of the graph transformations and the model's architecture to help readers understand the concepts more intuitively.

Finally, I would like to ask the authors to clarify the following points:

* Can the proposed architecture be applied to other types of graph-to-graph classification tasks, or is it specifically designed for tasks like learning a cellular automaton?
