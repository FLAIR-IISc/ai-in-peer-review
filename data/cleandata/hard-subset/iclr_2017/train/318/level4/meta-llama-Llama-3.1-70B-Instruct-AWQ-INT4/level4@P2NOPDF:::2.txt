This paper proposes a novel approach to dialog representation by learning a graph on the fly, which serves as a differentiable memory component. The authors demonstrate the efficacy of this method on the bAbI tasks, where graph learning is an integral part of the inference process. Notably, the approach involves long-term representation learning to acquire graph transformation parameters and sentence encoding as input to the graph.

The use of a graph as a differentiable memory is a unique contribution, offering a more complex and potentially powerful alternative to traditional memory networks. Although the results on the bAbI tasks do not show significant performance gains over existing methods, the preliminary nature of this work and the richness of graph-based representations suggest that further exploration is warranted.

One of the major challenges of this paper is clarity, which has been significantly improved in the revised version. The original submission was often difficult to follow, but the author's efforts to reorganize and rephrase the content have greatly enhanced its readability.

Despite the improved clarity, several questions remain unanswered. The preliminary results do not provide conclusive evidence on whether the proposed graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capabilities compared to other approaches. While the performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to that of top-performing memory networks, it still lags behind traditional rule induction methods.

Overall, this original and thought-provoking paper presents a technically accurate and innovative approach to dialog representation. Although the results are preliminary, the potential of graph-based differentiable memory warrants further exploration, making this paper a valuable contribution to the field.
