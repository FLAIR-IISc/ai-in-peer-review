This paper introduces a novel approach to representing dialog as a graph, which serves as a differentiable memory, and demonstrates its application on the bAbI tasks. Notably, graph learning is integrated into the inference process, supplemented by long-term representation learning to acquire graph transformation parameters and encode input sentences. This work appears to be the first implementation of a graph-based differentiable memory, offering a more complex yet potentially powerful alternative to previous methods like memory networks. Although the proposed approach does not yield significant performance gains on the bAbI tasks compared to existing methods, its preliminary nature and the promise of graph-based memory representation warrant further exploration.

A major concern is the paper's clarity, which has improved substantially from the initial version. The revised manuscript is more readable, although some technical aspects may still benefit from further clarification. The author's efforts to enhance the manuscript's accessibility are appreciated, making it more comprehensible to human readers.

This original, technically sound, and thought-provoking paper merits publication. However, the preliminary results do not yet provide conclusive evidence on whether the complex graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capabilities compared to other approaches. While the performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to that of top-performing memory networks, it still falls short of traditional rule induction methods (as seen in [citation]).
