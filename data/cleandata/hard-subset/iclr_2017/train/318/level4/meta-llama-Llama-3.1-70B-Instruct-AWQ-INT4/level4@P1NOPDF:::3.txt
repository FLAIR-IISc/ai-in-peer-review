The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, incorporating the ability to produce complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to develop a method that can build or modify a graph structure as an internal representation for solving problems, particularly question-answering problems. The authors propose five differentiable transformations that can be learned on a training set, typically in a supervised fashion where the state of the graph is provided at each time step. A specific instance of the model is presented, which takes a sequence as input and iteratively updates an internal graph state to produce a final prediction, yielding promising results when applied to question-answering tasks (e.g., BAbI).

The approach presented in this paper is intriguing, as the proposed model maintains a representation of its current state as a complex graph while preserving the property of being differentiable, making it easily learnable through gradient-descent techniques. This can be seen as a successful attempt to combine continuous and symbolic representations. Furthermore, this approach appears more general than recent attempts to incorporate symbolic elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines), as the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve.

However, my primary concern lies in the training methodology, which relies on providing the state of the graph at each time step. While this approach may be feasible for specific tasks (e.g., BAbI), it may not be a viable solution for more complex problems. Additionally, I question whether the paper's content is better suited for a journal format rather than a conference format, as the article's density makes it challenging to read.
