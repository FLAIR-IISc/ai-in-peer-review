The paper proposes an extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, enabling the model to produce complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to develop a method that can build or modify a graph structure as an internal representation for solving problems, particularly question-answering tasks. The authors introduce five differentiable transformations that can be learned from a training set, typically in a supervised manner where the graph state is provided at each time step. A specific instance of the model is presented, which takes a sequence as input, iteratively updates an internal graph state, and produces a final prediction, yielding promising results for question-answering tasks such as BaBi.

The approach presented in this paper is intriguing, as the proposed model maintains a complex graph representation of its current state while preserving differentiability, making it amenable to gradient-descent-based learning. This can be seen as a successful attempt to integrate continuous and symbolic representations. Furthermore, this approach appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate symbolic elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), as the shape of the state is not fixed and can evolve. However, my primary concerns lie in the training methodology, which relies on providing the graph state at each time step, a requirement that may only be feasible for specific tasks (e.g., Babi) and may not be scalable to more complex problems. Additionally, I question whether the paper's content, which may be better suited for a journal format, is too dense for a conference submission, making it challenging to read.
