This paper proposes a novel approach to representing dialog as a graph, which serves as a form of memory, and demonstrates its effectiveness on the bAbI tasks. Notably, graph learning is an integral part of the inference process, complemented by long-term representation learning to acquire graph transformation parameters and encode sentences as input to the graph. This work appears to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory as a graph, offering a more complex and potentially powerful alternative to previous approaches like memory networks. Although the performance gains on the bAbI tasks are not significant compared to existing methods, the representation of memory as a graph shows promise. A major limitation of the paper is its clarity, but the author has made substantial improvements in the revised version, making it more readable and accessible.

The preliminary results do not yet indicate whether the highly complex graph-based differentiable memory has greater learning or generalization capacity than other approaches. The performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to that of the best memory networks, but still falls short of more traditional rule induction methods (see [citation]). Further experimentation is necessary to fully assess the potential of this approach.

Despite the need for additional evaluation, this original, technically accurate, and thought-provoking paper is worthy of publication. The authors' innovative use of graph-based memory and their efforts to improve the clarity of the manuscript are notable strengths. With continued refinement and experimentation, this work has the potential to make a meaningful contribution to the field.
