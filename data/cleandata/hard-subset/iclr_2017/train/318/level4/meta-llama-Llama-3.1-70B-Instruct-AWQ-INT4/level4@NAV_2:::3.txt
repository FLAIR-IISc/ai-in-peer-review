The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network by including in this model the ability to produce complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to propose a method that will be able build/modify a graph-structure as an internal representation for solving a problem, and particularly for solving question-answering problems in this paper. The author proposes 5 different possible differentiable transformations that will be learned on a training set, typically in a supervised fashion where the state of the graph is given at each timestep. A particular occurrence of the model is presented that takes a sequence as an input and iteratively updates an internal graph state to a final prediction, and which can be applied for solving QA tasks (e.g., BaBi) with interesting results.

I decide to accept this paper with one key reason being the novelty and potential impact of the proposed approach. The paper presents a new and interesting method that combines the strengths of neural networks and graph-structured representations, allowing for the learning of complex graph transformations in a differentiable manner. This is a significant contribution to the field of neural networks and graph-based learning.

My main concerns are about the way the model is trained, i.e., by providing the state of the graph at each timestep, which can be done for particular tasks (e.g., BaBi) only and cannot be the solution for more complex problems. Additionally, the paper is dense and would perhaps best fit a journal format, making it difficult to read.

To support the claims made in the paper, I would like to see more experiments on different tasks and datasets, as well as a more detailed analysis of the model's performance and limitations. Furthermore, I would like to see a more detailed discussion on the potential applications and extensions of the proposed approach.

Additional feedback:

* The paper could benefit from a more detailed explanation of the differentiable graph transformations and how they are learned.
* The experimental results could be improved by including more baseline comparisons and a more detailed analysis of the model's performance on different tasks.
* The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion on the potential applications and extensions of the proposed approach, such as its potential use in other areas of AI research.

Questions for the authors:

* How do you plan to extend the proposed approach to more complex problems where the state of the graph is not provided at each timestep?
* Can you provide more details on the differentiable graph transformations and how they are learned?
* How do you plan to apply the proposed approach to other areas of AI research?
