This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel model leveraging graph-structured intermediate representations for learning and reasoning. The core idea is to dynamically construct and modify graphs based on textual input, utilizing these graphs for both internal state representation and output generation. This approach is demonstrated on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing the model’s ability to learn complex rules and relationships.

I recommend accepting this paper. The work presents a compelling and original approach to incorporating structured knowledge representation into a neural network architecture. The ability to learn graph transformations directly from data, rather than relying on pre-defined graph structures, is a significant contribution.

The paper is technically sound and well-motivated. The authors clearly position their work within the existing literature on graph neural networks, gated recurrent units, and memory networks. The experimental results, while not overwhelmingly superior to existing state-of-the-art methods on the bAbI tasks, are competitive and demonstrate the potential of the proposed approach. The successful application of the model to learn the rules governing a cellular automaton and Turing machines provides further evidence of its capacity for complex reasoning. The detailed descriptions of the graph transformations and their differentiability are particularly valuable.

Several points could further enhance the paper. While the ablation study on direct reference is insightful, a more comprehensive analysis of the impact of each graph transformation component would be beneficial. It would be helpful to explore the computational complexity of the GGT-NN and compare it more directly with other memory-based architectures. The discussion of related work could be expanded to include more recent advances in graph representation learning. Finally, providing visualizations of the learned graph structures for the bAbI tasks, cellular automaton, and Turing machine examples would significantly improve understanding and intuition.

I have a few clarifying questions for the authors: 
1. How does the performance of the GGT-NN scale with increasing graph size and complexity? Are there any strategies employed to mitigate potential computational bottlenecks?
2. The paper mentions the potential for overfitting. Were any regularization techniques (beyond those already implemented) explored to address this issue, particularly when extending the model to longer sequences?
3. Could the authors elaborate on the choice of activation functions and network architectures within each graph transformation? Was any hyperparameter optimization performed, and if so, what were the key findings?
4. Regarding the Turing machine experiments, how sensitive is the model’s performance to the specific choice of Turing machine rules and initial tape contents? Would the model generalize well to unseen Turing machines with different rule sets?
