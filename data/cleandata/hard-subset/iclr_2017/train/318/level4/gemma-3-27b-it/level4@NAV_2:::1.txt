The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The core innovation lies in a set of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – that enable the model to construct and modify graphs based on input, and subsequently utilize these graphs for various output tasks. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities by successfully learning to solve almost all bAbI tasks and by discovering the rules governing a simple cellular automaton and Turing machines.

Reject. While the idea of learning graph transformations is promising and the results on bAbI tasks are competitive, the experimental validation lacks sufficient depth and clarity, particularly concerning the more complex rule discovery tasks. The success on the cellular automaton (Rule 30) appears limited, and the description of the Turing machine experiment lacks crucial details regarding its complexity and the scale of rules learned. 

The paper’s primary weakness lies in the limited exploration of the proposed graph transformations’ full potential. The bAbI results, while positive, rely heavily on the strong supervision provided by the graph structure itself. The experiments on rule discovery, intended to showcase the model's ability to learn without explicit supervision, are not fully convincing. The description of the cellular automaton experiment does not clearly quantify the model’s ability to *predict* future states, only that it *learns* the rules. The performance on the Turing machine task needs further scrutiny – how complex were the learned Turing machines? What were the limitations encountered? The lack of a comparative analysis against other methods specifically designed for rule learning diminishes the impact of these results. Moreover, the ablation studies examining the influence of direct reference and intermediate propagation could be more comprehensive.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following: 1) Provide a more detailed quantitative evaluation of the cellular automaton and Turing machine experiments. Specifically, report prediction accuracy over multiple timesteps and analyze the complexity of the learned Turing machines. 2) Include a comparative analysis against existing rule learning algorithms. 3) Conduct a more thorough ablation study, systematically evaluating the impact of each graph transformation and the direct reference mechanism. 4) Clarify the role of the initial node strengths and the impact of the normalization constraint (∑xv,j = 1) on the learning process. 5) Discuss the computational complexity of the proposed approach and potential strategies for scaling it to larger graphs.

I have several clarifying questions for the authors: 1) For the Turing machine task, what is the maximum number of states and symbols the network can reliably learn? 2) How sensitive is the model to the initial node strengths? 3) Can you provide more details about the implementation of the `fset` and `freset` functions in the edge update transformation? 4) What is the impact of the normalization constraint on node annotations (∑xv,j = 1) – is it essential for stable learning? 5) Can you elaborate on the limitations of the current GGT-NN architecture and potential future directions for improvement?
