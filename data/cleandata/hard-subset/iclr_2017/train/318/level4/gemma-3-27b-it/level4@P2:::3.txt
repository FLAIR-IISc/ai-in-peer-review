This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that incorporates differentiable graph transformations. The core idea is to enable the model to learn and manipulate graph-structured data as an internal representation, particularly for tasks like question answering. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – and demonstrate their use in a GGT-NN architecture that iteratively constructs and modifies a graph based on textual input to arrive at a final prediction. The model achieves strong results on bAbI tasks and exhibits the ability to learn rules governing simple cellular automata and Turing machines.

**Reject.** While the approach is promising, the paper presents a substantial amount of work that lacks sufficient justification for conference presentation. The primary concern is the reliance on strong supervision (providing the correct graph state at each timestep) which significantly limits the model’s applicability to more realistic, less structured problems. Additionally, the paper's length and detail make it challenging to quickly grasp the key contributions, leaning more toward a journal-style presentation.

The paper tackles an important problem: bridging the gap between continuous neural network representations and symbolic graph structures. The motivation for learning graph transformations is well-placed within the literature of graph neural networks and memory-augmented neural networks. The approach of defining differentiable transformations allows for end-to-end learning, a significant advantage over methods requiring hand-engineered graph structures. The successful application of the GGT-NN to bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines demonstrates the potential of the approach. The results are presented clearly, and the comparison to existing methods is helpful.

However, the strong reliance on providing the ground truth graph at each timestep is a major limitation. While effective on the bAbI tasks, this level of supervision is unrealistic for many real-world applications where such explicit guidance is unavailable. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn’t offer a compelling path toward addressing it. The authors mention potential solutions like combining supervised and unsupervised learning, but these remain speculative. The sheer length and density of the paper, with extensive background information and detailed technical descriptions, hinder its accessibility and impact within a conference setting. While the thoroughness is appreciated, it makes it difficult to quickly identify the core innovations.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Address the supervision issue:** Investigate and discuss methods to reduce the reliance on ground-truth graph states. This could involve exploring alternative training objectives, regularization techniques, or curriculum learning strategies.
*   **Simplify the presentation:** Focus on the most essential contributions and streamline the technical details. Consider moving some of the background information and detailed transformation descriptions to the appendix.
*   **Provide more ablations:** Conduct ablation studies to better understand the contribution of each graph transformation and the impact of direct reference.
*   **Expand empirical evaluation:** Evaluate the model on more challenging tasks with less structured data and limited supervision.

I have several questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the accuracy of the initial graph structure provided in the bAbI tasks?
*   What strategies have you explored to mitigate the strong supervision requirement beyond the brief mention in the conclusion?
*   Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the computational cost associated with the graph transformations, particularly as the graph size increases?
*   What are the limitations of using GRU cells to determine the output of the graph transformations? Would exploring other alternatives prove beneficial?
