The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of a set of differentiable graph transformations, enabling the learning of graph-to-graph mapping tasks using gradient descent. This approach naturally lends itself to learning tasks like modeling a cellular automaton, which can be represented as a sequence of graphs where nodes connect to neighbors, and special nodes (0/1) represent cell values. The proposed architecture learns this sequence of graphs, although the experimental results on Rule 30 were not fully conclusive. 

This idea is combined with concepts from previous work (GGS-NN) to allow the model to produce textual output, using graphs as intermediate representations. This enables the model to achieve competitive performance on the bAbI tasks.

**Decision: Reject**

The primary reason for rejection is a lack of convincing empirical evidence demonstrating significant advantages over existing methods, particularly given the complexity of the proposed approach. While the results on bAbI tasks are competitive, they are not consistently superior to existing state-of-the-art methods, and the advantage is often marginal. More importantly, the results on the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines) are not strong enough to convincingly demonstrate the power of the approach.

Further, while the idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is appealing, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study to demonstrate the importance of each component. It is unclear, for instance, what the impact of the direct reference mechanism is, or whether all the proposed graph transformations are essential for achieving good performance.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Limited Improvement on bAbI:** The GGT-NN performs comparably to other strong baselines on the bAbI tasks, but doesn’t offer a substantial improvement, especially considering its increased complexity. The authors acknowledge that the MemNN model sometimes outperforms the GGT-NN.
*   **Weak Rule Discovery Results:** The experiments on the cellular automaton (Rule 30) and Turing machines, intended to showcase the model’s ability to learn complex update rules, don’t provide sufficiently compelling evidence. The paper states the model “successfully learned” these tasks, but the accuracy numbers are not presented in a way that clearly demonstrates complete or near-complete mastery of the rules.
*   **Lack of Ablation Studies:** The paper would benefit from an ablation study to assess the contribution of each component (e.g., node addition, edge update, direct reference). This would help to clarify which aspects of the architecture are most important for performance.
*   **Complexity:** The architecture is complex, with many moving parts and hyperparameters. This makes it difficult to understand the overall behavior of the model and to debug potential issues.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify Experimental Setup:** Provide more details on the experimental setup, including the specific hyperparameters used, the optimization algorithm, and the training procedure.
*   **Stronger Baselines:** Consider comparing the model against stronger, more recent baselines on the bAbI tasks.
*   **Detailed Analysis of Rule Discovery Results:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the results on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks. Show examples of the learned graphs and how they evolve over time. Visualize the internal state of the network.
*   **Quantitative Evaluation of Generalization:** Include more quantitative results on the generalization ability of the model to longer sequences in the rule discovery tasks.
*   **Discussion of Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost of the GGT-NN model and compare it to other approaches. Address the limitations of scaling the model to larger graphs or more complex tasks.
*   **Consider a simpler implementation:** Is it possible to remove some of the complexity of the graph transformation without significant loss of performance?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why the GGT-NN model did not achieve higher accuracy on task 17 of the bAbI dataset?
*   What is the sensitivity of the model’s performance to the choice of hyperparameters?
*   How does the computational cost of the GGT-NN model scale with the size of the graph and the complexity of the task?
*   Can you provide examples of the learned graphs for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks, illustrating how the model represents the underlying rules?
*   How do the different graph transformations contribute to the overall performance of the model? Is it possible to achieve comparable results by removing some of the transformations?
