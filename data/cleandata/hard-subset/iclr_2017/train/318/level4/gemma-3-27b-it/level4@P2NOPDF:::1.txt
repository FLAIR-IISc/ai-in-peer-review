This paper presents a novel approach to graph-to-graph learning based on differentiable graph transformations, enabling the application of gradient descent to tasks involving sequential graph modifications. A compelling framing of this work positions it as learning a cellular automaton, where the graph structure evolves iteratively with nodes representing states and edges denoting neighborhood relationships. The proposed architecture facilitates learning this sequence of graphs; however, the experimental results on the Rule 30 cellular automaton demonstrate that achieving full automation remains a significant challenge.

The authors effectively integrate their differentiable graph transformations with concepts from previous work, specifically GGS-NNs, to bridge graph-based computation with textual output. By utilizing graphs as an intermediate representation, the model achieves state-of-the-art performance on several bAbI reasoning tasks. This demonstrates a practical and valuable application of their core innovation.

The key strength of this work lies in its elegant formulation of graph manipulation as a differentiable process. This opens up new avenues for applying gradient-based learning to traditionally discrete graph problems. The connection to cellular automata provides a conceptually rich and intuitive understanding of the proposed approach, and the extension to tackle question answering tasks highlights its versatility. 

However, the Rule 30 experiment, while illustrating the framework's potential, reveals a substantial performance gap. A more detailed analysis of the challenges encountered during training and potential avenues for improvement within this specific domain would strengthen the paper. Further investigation into *why* the model struggles with Rule 30 – is it the expressiveness of the graph transformations, the optimization landscape, or limitations in the architecture itself? – would be beneficial. 

Regarding the bAbI results, while achieving state-of-the-art performance is commendable, the paper would benefit from a more thorough comparison to existing methods. A detailed ablation study examining the contribution of the differentiable graph transformations, versus simply leveraging the GGS-NN architecture, would provide a clearer understanding of the proposed method's unique advantages. How does the performance change when simplifying the graph representation or removing the differentiable transformation component? 

Furthermore, a discussion of the computational cost associated with these differentiable graph transformations would be valuable. Are the transformations computationally expensive, and how does this scale with the size of the graphs involved? This is particularly important given the potential applications in larger, more complex graph datasets.

Overall, this paper presents a promising and innovative approach to graph learning. The combination of differentiable graph transformations with established architectures like GGS-NNs yields strong results on the bAbI tasks. Addressing the limitations identified in the Rule 30 experiment and providing a more in-depth analysis of the method's performance, computational cost, and comparison to existing techniques would significantly strengthen the contribution. Despite these areas for improvement, the work represents a significant step forward in the field and merits acceptance.
