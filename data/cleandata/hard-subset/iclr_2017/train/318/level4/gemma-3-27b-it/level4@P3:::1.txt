## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The core contribution lies in the proposal of differentiable graph transformations – node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation – enabling the model to learn and modify graphs based on input and utilize these graphs for various output tasks. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, achieving competitive results, and showcase its ability to learn the rules governing a simple cellular automaton and Turing machines.

**Decision: Reject**

While the idea of learning differentiable graph transformations is interesting and potentially valuable, the paper lacks sufficient clarity and convincing empirical evidence to support its claims. Specifically, the performance on the Rule 30 cellular automaton task is underwhelming and casts doubt on the effectiveness of the proposed approach for complex graph manipulation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Empirical Validation:** The results on the bAbI tasks, while competitive, do not fully justify the complexity of the proposed GGT-NN. The paper acknowledges that the model is strongly supervised and compares favorably to other strongly supervised methods (MemNN), but doesn’t sufficiently demonstrate an advantage over simpler approaches. The results on the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks are presented without a clear baseline for comparison. The statement that the Rule 30 task was "far from solved" is a significant concern. A more detailed analysis of the failures, perhaps showing learned graphs or highlighting specific difficulties, would be crucial.

2. **Lack of Clarity in Implementation Details:**  The paper provides a high-level description of the graph transformations but lacks sufficient detail regarding the specific architectures and hyperparameters used within each transformation (e.g., network sizes, activation functions). This makes it difficult to assess the computational cost and potential bottlenecks of the approach.  The appendix attempts to address this, but is still quite dense and requires significant effort to fully understand. The extensive use of abbreviations (e.g., "Tadd", "TC") without consistent explanation also hinders readability. 

3. **Unclear Motivation for Design Choices:**  Several design choices, such as the constraint ∑xv,j = 1 and the use of node strengths, are not adequately motivated. The authors should clearly explain the rationale behind these choices and how they contribute to the model’s performance or differentiability.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

* **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies to assess the contribution of individual graph transformations (e.g., removing the node addition transformation) would provide valuable insights into their importance.
* **Baseline Comparisons:**  Include a comparison to simpler baselines for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks. For example, a standard RNN or LSTM trained on the sequential representation of the graph could serve as a useful benchmark.
* **Visualization of Learned Graphs:** Providing visualizations of the graphs learned by the GGT-NN, particularly for the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks, would help to understand how the model represents and manipulates information.
* **Discussion of Computational Cost:**  A discussion of the computational cost of the GGT-NN, especially in terms of memory usage and training time, is necessary.
* **Simplify Notation:** Reducing the reliance on abbreviations and using more descriptive variable names would improve readability.



**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the performance on the Rule 30 task? Specifically, what difficulties did the model encounter, and how did the learned graphs deviate from the expected patterns?
* What are the computational costs associated with training and running the GGT-NN, and how do they scale with the size of the graph?
*  Why was the constraint ∑xv,j = 1 imposed on the node annotations, and how does it affect the model's behavior?
* What are the specific network architectures and hyperparameters used within each of the graph transformations?
* Could you elaborate on why direct reference consistently improves performance across several tasks, and what specific benefits it provides to the model?
