## Review of “Learning to Communicate with Graphs”

This paper introduces a novel approach to graph-to-graph learning based on differentiable graph transformations, framed within the context of learning cellular automata. The central contribution lies in enabling gradient-based learning for tasks involving sequential graph manipulation, specifically by representing the evolution of a cellular automaton as a sequence of graphs. This approach leverages a learned transformation to evolve the graph structure at each iteration, effectively learning the rules governing the automaton’s behavior. Furthermore, the authors connect this graph-based learning with natural language processing by integrating ideas from Graph-to-Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs), allowing the model to generate textual output via graphs as an intermediate representation, achieving state-of-the-art results on the BaBi tasks.

The motivation for exploring differentiable graph transformations is well-articulated. Existing methods for graph manipulation often rely on discrete operations, hindering end-to-end learning. This work addresses this limitation by providing a continuously differentiable mechanism for modifying graph structure, opening up possibilities for applying gradient descent to problems previously intractable with traditional methods. The analogy to cellular automata is insightful; it provides a concrete and visually intuitive example of the model's capabilities and highlights the potential for learning complex dynamical systems. 

The experimental evaluation presents a mixed picture. While the model demonstrates an ability to *learn* the Rule 30 cellular automaton, the learned behavior falls significantly short of perfectly reproducing the rule’s dynamics. The visualizations provided in the paper illustrate this limitation clearly, showing approximations rather than exact reproductions of the intended pattern. This raises questions regarding the expressiveness of the proposed graph transformations and the model’s capacity to learn complex rules. However, the results on the BaBi tasks are compelling. The reported state-of-the-art performance suggests that the graph-based intermediate representation is effective for capturing the underlying logical structure of the questions and answers. The integration of GGS-NN ideas appears to be a crucial element of this success.

The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the core concepts. However, certain aspects could benefit from further elaboration. The specific implementation details of the differentiable graph transformations warrant more in-depth discussion. How are the edge and node attributes updated during the transformation process? What constraints are imposed on the transformations to ensure stable and meaningful graph evolution? A more detailed explanation would enhance reproducibility and understanding. Similarly, the hyperparameter settings for both the Rule 30 experiment and the BaBi tasks should be more thoroughly documented.

**Strengths:**

* **Novel Approach:** The idea of learning graph transformations with gradient descent is a significant contribution, addressing a key limitation of traditional graph manipulation techniques.
* **Strong Results on BaBi:** Achieving state-of-the-art performance on the BaBi tasks demonstrates the practical value of the proposed method for NLP applications.
* **Clear Explanation:** The paper provides a relatively accessible explanation of the complex concepts involved, with the cellular automaton analogy proving particularly helpful.
* **Interesting Connection:** The link between graph transformations and cellular automata provides a novel perspective on both areas.

**Weaknesses:**

* **Limited Success with Rule 30:** The imperfect reproduction of Rule 30 raises concerns about the expressiveness and learning capabilities of the graph transformation mechanism.
* **Lack of Implementation Detail:** Insufficient details regarding the specific implementation of the graph transformations hinder reproducibility and full comprehension.
* **Hyperparameter Documentation:** The paper would benefit from a more complete description of the hyperparameter settings used in the experiments.



**Overall Assessment:**

This paper presents a promising new direction for graph-to-graph learning. While the results on the Rule 30 experiment are not fully satisfactory, the strong performance on the BaBi tasks highlights the potential of this approach, particularly when combined with sequence-to-sequence models. The novelty of the idea and the encouraging empirical results warrant acceptance, contingent on the authors addressing the concerns regarding implementation details and providing a more thorough analysis of the limitations of the graph transformation mechanism.  I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.



**Questions for the Authors:**

* Could you provide more detail on the specific implementation of the differentiable graph transformations? For example, how are edge and node attributes updated?
* What is the computational complexity of the proposed graph transformation process, and how does it scale with graph size?
* What are your thoughts on the limitations of the current graph transformation mechanism, and what potential extensions or improvements could address these limitations?
* Could you provide a more comprehensive ablation study for the BaBi tasks to better understand the contribution of the graph-based intermediate representation?
