This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), extending previous work on Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs). The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations enabling the learning of graph-structured data and facilitating graph-to-graph mappings through gradient descent. This approach allows the model to utilize graphs as intermediate representations for tasks like question answering (on the bAbI tasks) and learning the rules governing dynamical systems (demonstrated with a cellular automaton and Turing machines).

**Reject.** While the proposed approach is interesting and builds upon relevant prior work, the experimental results, particularly on the cellular automaton task, are not compelling enough to warrant acceptance. The paper demonstrates strong performance on bAbI tasks, but this relies on relatively strong supervision (providing the correct graph at each timestep) and doesn’t fully demonstrate the transformative power of the proposed graph manipulation capabilities.

The paper clearly articulates the motivation for using graph-structured data as an intermediate representation, and the proposed graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) appear well-defined and flexible. The connection to existing work on GGS-NNs and related models (e.g., Memory Networks, DNCs) is appropriately discussed. The bAbI task results, while strong, are presented alongside comparisons to a variety of baselines, allowing for a reasonable assessment of the approach's effectiveness.

However, the results on the Rule 30 cellular automaton are less convincing. The paper states the task was "far from solved" which raises concerns about the practicality of learning complex dynamical systems with the proposed architecture. The description of the graphical representation of the Turing machine is brief and lacks sufficient detail to assess its effectiveness. Furthermore, the reliance on strong supervision for the bAbI tasks limits the generalizability of the results. The paper would benefit from a more thorough ablation study examining the contribution of different components, such as direct reference.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Cellular Automaton Experiment:** Provide more details on the experimental setup for the cellular automaton task. What were the specific performance metrics? Were different network architectures or hyperparameters explored? A more detailed analysis would help clarify the limitations of the approach in this context.
*   **Turing Machine Representation:** Elaborate on how Turing machines are encoded as graphs. Include a visual example to aid understanding.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct an ablation study on the bAbI tasks to assess the impact of different components (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation).
*   **Supervision:** Discuss the implications of strong supervision on the results. How would the performance change with less supervision, or with a different supervision strategy?
*   **Scalability:** Address the potential scalability concerns associated with the quadratic complexity of graph transformations.
*   **Clarify “fset” and “freset”:**  In Appendix B.3, explain the functions fset and freset in more detail. What activation functions are used? What is the network architecture?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   How does the performance on the Rule 30 cellular automaton change with different network architectures or hyperparameters?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how Turing machines are encoded as graphs?
*   What strategies could be used to reduce the computational complexity of the graph transformations, particularly for large graphs?
*   How sensitive is the performance on the bAbI tasks to the level of supervision? What results were obtained with less supervision?
