- Proposal: introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) that learns differentiable graph transformations as intermediate representations.  
- Core idea: dynamically construct and modify graphs from input (especially text) for downstream tasks.  
- Technical novelty: defines five differentiable transformations — node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, aggregation.  
- Empirical evaluation: tested on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines with promising results.  
- Verdict: Reject for the conference; paper is lengthy and complex and feels more suitable for a journal.  
- Major concern: extensive background and detailed transformation descriptions overshadow the core novelty for a conference audience.  
- Major limitation: training requires providing full correct graph state at each timestep, limiting real-world applicability and generalization.  
- Strength: innovative integration of graph manipulation into a differentiable neural framework.  
- Strength: strong performance on certain tasks (e.g., bAbI pathfinding) and flexibility shown on automata and Turing machine tasks.  
- Strength: modular transformation design allows configurable use across tasks.  
- Weakness: presentation density and steep learning curve reduce readability and obscure main contributions.  
- Weakness: paper acknowledges supervision limitation but does not offer sufficient alternative training strategies.  
- Weakness: experiments lack sufficient ablation studies to isolate component contributions.  
- Additional suggestion: streamline presentation by reducing background and moving details to appendix.  
- Additional suggestion: investigate alternative training (semi-supervised, reinforcement learning) to reduce reliance on full graph supervision.  
- Additional suggestion: add ablation studies to quantify each transformation’s impact and the role of direct reference.  
- Additional suggestion: expand discussion on limitations and generalization to less supervised, more complex settings.  
- Additional suggestion: improve in-paper code examples for readability despite available code.  
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of performance to direct-reference quality and effects of noisy/incomplete references.  
- Questions for authors: performance under partial/noisy graph supervision and mitigation experiments.  
- Questions for authors: scalability concerns due to quadratic graph-operation complexity and planned optimizations.  
- Questions for authors: rationale and experiments for activation/functions and architectures used in transformations.  
- Questions for authors: challenges and applicability to larger real-world problems (knowledge graphs, program synthesis).
