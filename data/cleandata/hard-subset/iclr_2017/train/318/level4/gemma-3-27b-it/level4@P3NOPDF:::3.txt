## Review of “Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks for Differentiable Graph Transformation”

This paper proposes an extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGSNNs) by incorporating differentiable graph transformation capabilities. The core idea is to enable the model to dynamically build and modify a graph structure as an internal representation, specifically targeting question answering (QA) tasks. The authors introduce five differentiable graph transformation operations, learned in a supervised manner, that allow the model to evolve its graph state over time. A specific instantiation of this framework iteratively updates an internal graph based on input sequences, achieving promising results on QA datasets like BaBi.

The approach presented is highly interesting. The model’s ability to maintain a complex, evolving graph representation while retaining differentiability – and therefore compatibility with gradient-based learning – represents a compelling blend of continuous and symbolic reasoning. This appears more general than recent attempts to integrate ‘symbolic’ elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines), as the state’s structure is not fixed *a priori* but dynamically adapts. The flexibility of the graph structure itself is a key strength.

However, I have several concerns. My primary concern revolves around the training methodology. The current approach relies on providing the complete graph state at each timestep, a viable strategy for specific tasks like the BaBi dataset where this information is readily available. However, this supervised state provision significantly limits the applicability of the method to more complex, real-world problems where such explicit state information is unlikely to be obtainable. How would this model be trained without complete state supervision? Addressing this limitation is crucial for demonstrating broader applicability.

Furthermore, the paper's overall presentation feels more suited to a journal submission than a conference paper. The density of the material and the level of detail make it challenging to grasp the key contributions quickly. While the technical depth is appreciated, streamlining the presentation and focusing on the core innovations would significantly improve clarity. Specifically, the motivation for each of the five proposed transformations could be more clearly articulated, and a more intuitive explanation of their interplay would be beneficial. A more visual explanation of how the graph evolves throughout a QA example would also aid understanding. 

I believe the work presents a worthwhile contribution to the field, but its impact is currently hampered by the limitations in training methodology and the density of the presentation. 

**Strengths:**

*   Novel approach to integrating graph transformation into differentiable neural networks.
*   Flexibility in graph structure compared to existing memory-augmented neural networks.
*   Promising results on QA tasks.
*   Potential for bridging the gap between continuous and symbolic reasoning.

**Weaknesses:**

*   Reliance on fully supervised graph state provision during training limits generalizability.
*   Paper is dense and difficult to read; better suited for a journal format.
*   Motivation for specific graph transformations could be clearer.




**Recommendation:**

I recommend a **weak accept** contingent upon the authors addressing the concerns regarding the training methodology and significantly improving the clarity and conciseness of the presentation. A detailed discussion of alternative training strategies, even if preliminary, would greatly strengthen the paper.
