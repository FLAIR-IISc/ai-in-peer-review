This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel approach to modeling sequential data and reasoning by representing information as a dynamically constructed and modified graph. The model extends previous work on Gated Graph Neural Networks (GG-NNs) by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations and outputs, learned through differentiable graph transformations. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN's capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a simple cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn both symbolic reasoning and procedural knowledge. While the initial clarity of the paper was a concern, significant improvements have been made, resulting in a technically sound and thought-provoking contribution.

**Reject, with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.** The core idea of learning and manipulating graph structures for knowledge representation is compelling. However, the empirical results, while demonstrating the model's functionality, do not currently justify the significant increase in complexity compared to existing memory network approaches. The performance on the bAbI tasks is competitive with, but not superior to, strong baselines, and a more convincing demonstration of the benefits of this graphical representation is needed.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its conceptual innovation. Representing a dynamic state as a graph offers a potentially powerful way to encode relationships and facilitate reasoning. The proposed differentiable graph transformations are a key contribution, enabling end-to-end learning of both the graph structure and the reasoning process. The successful application of the GGT-NN to tasks requiring procedural knowledge (cellular automaton, Turing machines) is particularly encouraging, suggesting the model’s potential to learn complex underlying rules.

However, several concerns prevent a positive recommendation at this time. First, the empirical evaluation, while thorough, lacks a clear demonstration of the GGT-NN’s advantages. The performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to existing memory networks, but the GGT-NN is considerably more complex. A more compelling evaluation would involve tasks where the graph representation is demonstrably superior, or a detailed ablation study highlighting the benefits of each component of the GGT-NN. Second, the paper lacks a discussion of the computational cost and scalability of the approach. The quadratic complexity of graph operations is acknowledged but not fully addressed, raising concerns about the applicability of the model to larger, more complex datasets. Finally, while the improved clarity of the revised manuscript is appreciated, the paper remains dense and challenging to follow in places, particularly for readers unfamiliar with graph neural networks.

**Additional Feedback (for revision):**

*   **Strengthen the empirical evaluation:** Consider including tasks where the graphical representation provides a clear advantage, or a more comprehensive ablation study comparing different components of the GGT-NN.
*   **Address scalability concerns:** Discuss the computational complexity of the approach and potential optimizations to improve scalability. Explore techniques like sparse graph representations or approximate inference.
*   **Improve clarity:** Simplify the technical descriptions and provide more intuitive explanations of the key concepts. Include more illustrative examples to aid understanding.
*   **Explore alternative graph construction methods:** The current approach relies on a predefined set of graph transformations. Investigate whether other methods, such as learning the graph structure directly from data, could improve performance.
*   **Compare to related work more thoroughly:** Provide a more detailed comparison to other relevant approaches, such as relational neural networks and differentiable neural computers, highlighting the specific advantages and disadvantages of the GGT-NN.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What is the computational cost (in terms of memory and time) of the GGT-NN compared to existing memory network models on the bAbI tasks?
*   Are there any tasks where the GGT-NN demonstrably outperforms other approaches, and if so, what characteristics of those tasks make the graph representation particularly beneficial?
*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to the choice of graph transformations and their hyperparameters?
*   What strategies have you considered for improving the scalability of the approach to larger graphs and datasets?
*   Could you provide a more intuitive explanation of why direct reference significantly improves performance? What information is the model gaining from this direct connection to node types?
