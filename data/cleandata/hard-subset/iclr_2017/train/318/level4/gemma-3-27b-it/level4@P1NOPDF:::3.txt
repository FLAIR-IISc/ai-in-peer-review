The paper proposes an extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, augmenting it with the capability to perform complex graph transformations. The core idea is to develop a method capable of constructing and modifying a graph structure as an internal representation for problem solving, with a particular focus in this work on question answering. The authors propose five differentiable transformations that can be learned from training data, typically in a supervised manner where the graph state is provided at each timestep. A specific instantiation of the model iteratively updates an internal graph state from a sequence input to arrive at a final prediction, and demonstrates promising results when applied to question answering tasks such as BaBi.

The approach presented in this paper is genuinely interesting, as the proposed model maintains a representation of its current state as a complex graph while remaining differentiable and therefore readily trainable via gradient-based techniques. This can be viewed as a successful attempt to combine continuous and symbolic representations. Furthermore, it appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate ‘symbolic’ elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTM), as the state’s structure is not fixed and can evolve dynamically. 

My main concern lies with the training methodology—specifically, providing the graph state at each timestep. While feasible for certain tasks like BaBi, this approach is unlikely to generalize to more complex problems. My other concern relates to the paper’s overall scope; it may be better suited for a journal publication than a conference format, as the density of the material currently makes it challenging to read.
