The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network by incorporating differentiable graph transformations. The core idea is to enable the model to construct and modify a graph structure as an internal representation for problem-solving, specifically focusing on question-answering tasks. The author introduces five differentiable transformations, learned in a supervised manner where the graph state is provided at each timestep. A specific model configuration is presented that iteratively updates an internal graph state from sequential input to produce a final prediction, achieving promising results on tasks like bAbI.

I recommend **rejecting** this paper. While the proposed approach is conceptually interesting, the reliance on full graph state supervision and the extensive presentation hinder its suitability for a conference format.

The strength of this work lies in its ability to represent and manipulate complex state information through a differentiable graph structure. This offers a potentially powerful and flexible approach compared to methods with fixed-size memory (e.g., Memory Networks, NTMs), as the graph’s structure can evolve dynamically. The proposed transformations represent a solid contribution to the field. However, the current training paradigm – requiring complete graph state information at each step – significantly limits the model’s applicability to tasks where such supervision is readily available (like bAbI). Extending this approach to scenarios with less explicit supervision is a crucial open question, and the paper doesn’t adequately address this limitation. Furthermore, the paper is exceptionally dense and detailed, resembling a journal article rather than a concise conference submission. The extensive background material and detailed descriptions, while thorough, detract from the core contributions and make the paper challenging to digest.

To improve the paper, I suggest the following:

*   **Address the supervision challenge:** Discuss potential strategies for training the model with partial or no graph supervision. Exploring techniques like reinforcement learning or unsupervised graph structure learning would significantly strengthen the work.
*   **Streamline the presentation:** Condense the background material and focus on the novel contributions of the GGT-NN. Consider moving some of the detailed explanations of the transformations to the appendix.
*   **Demonstrate generalization:** Evaluate the model on a more diverse set of tasks that require learning graph structures from unstructured input.
*   **Ablation studies:** Perform ablation studies to assess the contribution of each graph transformation and the impact of direct reference.

I have the following questions for the authors:

*   How sensitive is the model’s performance to errors in the provided graph state supervision during training?
*   What are the computational costs associated with the graph transformations, and how do they scale with graph size?
*   Could you elaborate on the specific choices made in the implementation of the transformation functions (e.g., the architecture of the neural networks used in fset and f fwd y)?
*   What are the potential benefits of using sparse edge connections to improve scalability?
