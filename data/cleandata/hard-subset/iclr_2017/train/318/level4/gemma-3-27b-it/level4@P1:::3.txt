The paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network by incorporating the ability to perform complex graph transformations. The core idea is to develop a method capable of building and modifying a graph structure as an internal representation for problem-solving, specifically question answering in this work. The author proposes five differentiable transformations learned during training, typically in a supervised manner where the graph's state is provided at each timestep. A specific model configuration is presented that iteratively updates an internal graph state from a sequence of inputs to produce a final prediction, demonstrating promising results on question-answering tasks (e.g., bAbI).

The approach is compelling because the proposed model can maintain a representation of its current state as a complex graph while remaining differentiable and thus easily trainable via gradient descent. It represents a successful attempt to integrate continuous and symbolic representations, and appears more general than recent approaches adding ‘symbolic’ elements to differentiable models (Memory Networks, NTMs, etc.) because the state’s shape is not fixed and can evolve. My primary concern lies with the training methodology – providing the graph’s state at each timestep – which is only feasible for specific tasks (e.g., bAbI) and may not generalize to more complex problems. Additionally, the paper’s density and length suggest it might be better suited for a journal publication than a conference format, making it challenging to read.

**Decision: Reject**

The paper presents a technically sound and interesting approach, but its reliance on strong supervision and the substantial length/density of the presentation make it unsuitable for a conference like ICLR. While the results are promising, the limited applicability of the training method raises concerns about its broader impact.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Supervised Training:** The requirement for providing the correct graph state at each timestep severely limits the model's applicability to scenarios where such supervision is readily available. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn't offer convincing strategies for overcoming it in more realistic settings. While providing a starting point for research, this makes it difficult to assess the long-term potential of the method.
2. **Paper Length and Density:** The paper is exceptionally long and detailed, resembling a journal article more than a conference paper. The extensive background information, detailed descriptions of transformations, and multiple experiments, while thorough, make it difficult to quickly grasp the key contributions and assess their significance. The high level of technical detail and mathematical notation demands significant effort from the reviewer.

**Additional Feedback (to help improve the paper):**

*   **Focus and Pruning:** Consider significantly streamlining the paper by focusing on the most impactful contributions. Perhaps emphasize the core GGT-NN architecture and results on the bAbI tasks, and relegate the details of the transformations and the Turing machine experiments to the appendix.
*   **Investigate Weakly Supervised Learning:** Explore alternative training strategies that reduce the reliance on full graph supervision. Could the model learn to construct graphs from textual input with minimal guidance, perhaps by using reinforcement learning or self-supervision?
*   **Address Scalability:** The paper mentions the quadratic scaling of the model. Discuss potential optimizations or approximations to address scalability issues and make it practical for larger graphs.
*   **Clarify Novelty:** More explicitly highlight the key differences between GGT-NNs and existing graph neural network approaches (GNNs, GG-NNs, GGS-NNs). While the paper touches on this, a clearer articulation of the novel contributions would strengthen the presentation.
*   **Consider Ablation Studies:** Include ablation studies to assess the importance of different components of the model, such as the direct reference mechanism and the various graph transformations.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What strategies have you considered, or plan to explore, for training the GGT-NN in scenarios where the complete graph state is not available at each timestep?
2.  The paper mentions the potential for quadratic scaling with graph size. Could you elaborate on the computational complexity of the different graph transformations and the overall model? Are there any planned optimizations to address this issue?
3.  Could you provide a more concise summary of the key differences between the GGT-NN and existing graph neural network architectures (e.g., GNNs, GG-NNs, GGS-NNs) in the abstract or introduction?
4.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters, particularly the learning rate, batch size, and the number of propagation steps? Have you performed a thorough hyperparameter search?
