The main contribution of this paper is the introduction of a set of differentiable graph transformations, enabling gradient-based learning for graph-to-graph classification tasks. This approach maps naturally to learning a cellular automaton represented as a sequence of graphs, where the graph evolves with each iteration – nodes expand to point to their neighbors, and designated nodes (0/1) represent cell values. The proposed architecture allows for learning this sequence of graphs, although the experimental results on Rule 30 demonstrate that the task remains challenging and was not fully solved.

This core idea is further combined with concepts from previous work (GGS-NN) to allow the model to generate textual output rather than graphs, utilizing graphs as an intermediate representation. This combination yields state-of-the-art performance on the BaBi tasks. 

The paper presents a novel and interesting approach to bridging discrete graph manipulation with continuous gradient-based optimization. The connection to cellular automata is particularly insightful and provides a compelling motivation for the proposed graph transformations. However, the Rule 30 experiment, while illustrative, reveals a significant gap between the model's current capability and full task completion. This suggests that either the chosen transformations are not sufficiently expressive, or the training process requires further refinement. More detailed analysis of the learned transformations and potential bottlenecks would be valuable.

The use of graphs as an intermediate representation for textual tasks is a clever extension, and the achieved results on the BaBi tasks are impressive. It would be helpful to understand how the performance scales with task complexity (e.g., increasing the number of hops in the BaBi questions). A comparison with other recent architectures specifically designed for question answering, beyond the ones mentioned in the paper, would strengthen the evaluation.

The paper is well-written and the proposed method is clearly explained. However, the notation could be slightly improved for readability. For example, consistently defining and using symbols for graph transformations would enhance clarity. Additionally, a discussion of the computational cost of the proposed transformations would be beneficial, especially considering their potential application to large graphs.

Overall, this paper presents a promising new direction for learning with graphs. While the Rule 30 experiment highlights current limitations, the strong performance on the BaBi tasks and the innovative use of differentiable graph transformations make this work a valuable contribution. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions addressing the points mentioned above regarding analysis of the Rule 30 experiment, scaling with task complexity, and clarifying notation.
