This paper proposes a novel approach to dialog representation learning, framing it as a graph that functions as a differentiable memory. The method is initially demonstrated on the bAbI tasks. Graph learning is integrated into the inference process, while long-term representation learning focuses on learning parameters for graph transformations and encoding sentences as input to the graph. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first implementation of a differentiable memory structured as a graph. While considerably more complex than previous approaches like memory networks, it doesn’t currently offer a significant performance gain on the bAbI tasks. However, this is still very preliminary work, and representing memory as a graph appears potentially more powerful than a stack-based approach. 

Clarity was a significant issue in earlier versions; the initial draft was more readily parsed by a machine than a human reader. However, the author has since proposed a substantially improved revision. Despite these initial challenges, this technically accurate (to the extent I could follow) and thought-provoking paper is worth publishing.

The preliminary results don’t yet demonstrate whether this highly complex, graph-based differentiable memory possesses greater learning or generalization capacity compared to other methods. While the current results are modest, the conceptual contribution and the demonstrated improvement in presentation justify acceptance.
