This paper proposes the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that incorporates differentiable graph transformations as an intermediate representation. The core idea is to enable the model to dynamically construct and modify graphs based on input data—particularly textual input—and leverage these graphs for various downstream tasks, including question answering. The authors define five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) and demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines.

**Reject.** While the approach is conceptually interesting and the results are promising, the paper feels more suited for a journal submission due to its complexity and length. The extensive background material and detailed descriptions of transformations, while thorough, overshadow the novelty and impact of the work for a conference setting. Furthermore, the reliance on providing the full correct graph state during training limits the broader applicability of the approach and raises concerns about its ability to generalize to scenarios without such strong supervision.

The primary strength of the paper lies in its innovative approach to integrating graph manipulation within a differentiable neural network framework. The ability to learn graph transformations offers a powerful way to represent and reason about structured data. The demonstrated success on the bAbI tasks, particularly achieving competitive results on tasks like pathfinding, highlights the potential of this approach. The extension to learning rules for cellular automata and Turing machines further demonstrates the model's flexibility and capacity for learning complex dynamics. The modularity of the transformations, enabling different configurations for various tasks, is also a positive aspect.

However, the paper’s length and density hinder readability. The detailed explanations of the transformations, while valuable, contribute to a steep learning curve for the reader. The core contribution gets somewhat lost in the extensive background material and technical details. More crucially, the training methodology – relying on providing the correct graph state at each timestep – raises significant concerns. While effective for the bAbI tasks, this level of supervision is unrealistic for many real-world applications where ground-truth graph states are unavailable. This reliance on strong supervision substantially limits the generalizability of the proposed method. The paper acknowledges this limitation but doesn’t sufficiently address potential solutions or alternative training strategies. Finally, the experiments, while comprehensive, could benefit from more extensive ablation studies to better understand the contribution of each component of the GGT-NN.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Streamline Presentation:** Consider significantly reducing the background material, focusing on the most relevant prior work. Condense the descriptions of the graph transformations, potentially moving some details to the appendix.
*   **Address Supervision:** Discuss alternative training strategies that reduce the reliance on full graph supervision. Explore semi-supervised learning or reinforcement learning approaches.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Include ablation studies to assess the individual contributions of each transformation and the impact of direct reference.
*   **Clarify Generalization:** Provide a more in-depth discussion of the limitations of the current approach and potential avenues for improving generalization to more complex and less supervised settings.
*   **Code Readability:** While the code is available, providing more concise examples in the paper itself would greatly aid understanding.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper highlights the importance of direct reference. How sensitive is the model’s performance to the quality of this direct reference mechanism? What happens if the direct references are noisy or incomplete?
2.  The training strategy relies on providing the correct graph state at each timestep. How would the model perform if trained with only partial or noisy graph supervision? Have you experimented with any techniques to mitigate the need for such strong supervision?
3.  The scalability of the model is a potential concern due to the quadratic complexity of graph operations. What optimizations have you considered, or are planning to explore, to address this issue for larger graphs and more complex tasks?
4.  Could you elaborate on the choice of activation functions and network architectures within the graph transformations? Were alternative choices explored, and if so, what were the results?
5.  The paper demonstrates success on relatively simple tasks. Could you discuss the challenges of applying GGT-NN to more complex real-world problems, such as knowledge graph reasoning or program synthesis?
