- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN): dynamic, differentiable graph-based representation and transformations extending GG-NNs.  
- Demonstrates GGT-NN on bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showing learning of symbolic reasoning and procedural knowledge.  
- Initial clarity was poor but substantially improved; manuscript is now technically sound and thought-provoking.  
- Recommendation: Reject, with strong encouragement to revise and resubmit.  
- Main weakness: empirical results do not justify the added complexity—performance on bAbI is competitive but not superior to strong baselines.  
- Need for clearer demonstrations where graph representation provides a decisive advantage or tasks tailored to its strengths.  
- Request for a detailed ablation study to isolate benefits of each GGT-NN component.  
- Scalability concerns: quadratic graph operation complexity is acknowledged but not addressed; computational cost and applicability to larger datasets are unclear.  
- Paper remains dense and difficult to follow for readers unfamiliar with graph neural networks; clarity can be improved.  
- Key strength: conceptual innovation of learning and manipulating dynamic graph structures for knowledge representation and reasoning.  
- Differentiable graph transformations are a major contribution enabling end-to-end learning of graph structure and reasoning.  
- Success on procedural tasks (cellular automata, Turing machines) is encouraging and suggests potential for learning complex rules.  
- Revision suggestions: strengthen empirical evaluation, address scalability (e.g., sparse graphs, approximations), simplify explanations, add illustrative examples.  
- Explore alternative graph-construction methods (e.g., learning graph structure directly) and provide more thorough comparisons to related work (relational nets, DNCs).  
- Questions for authors: computational cost vs memory networks; any tasks with clear GGT-NN superiority and why; sensitivity to transformation choices/hyperparameters; scalability strategies; intuitive explanation of why direct reference boosts performance.
