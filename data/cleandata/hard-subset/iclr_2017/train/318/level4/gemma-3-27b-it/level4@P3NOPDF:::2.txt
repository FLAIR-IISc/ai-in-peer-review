This paper introduces a novel approach to dialog representation learning, framing dialog history as a dynamically constructed graph that serves as a differentiable memory. The method is initially demonstrated on the bAbI tasks, and cleverly integrates graph learning into the inference process alongside long-term learning of graph transformation parameters and sentence encoding. To the best of my knowledge, this work represents the first implementation of a fully differentiable memory structure based on a graph, a departure from previous memory-augmented networks.

While conceptually promising, and significantly more complex than earlier memory network architectures, the performance gains on the bAbI tasks are not substantial. The results are competitive with state-of-the-art memory networks, but currently lag behind more traditional rule induction methods (as noted in the submission). However, recognizing the exploratory nature of this work, the potential advantages of a graph-based memory representation over simpler structures like stacks are considerable. A graph inherently allows for richer relational modeling, which could prove crucial in more complex dialog scenarios. 

The paper has undergone significant improvement since its initial submission. The original version, while technically sound, prioritized machine readability over human comprehension. The authors have responded constructively to feedback, producing a substantially clearer and more accessible manuscript. This responsiveness is commendable. 

Despite the clarity improvements, some aspects remain challenging to follow. The interplay between the learned graph transformations and the sentence encodings requires careful consideration, and a more intuitive explanation would be beneficial. Similarly, detailing the specific choices made in graph construction and traversal, and their impact on performance, would strengthen the presentation. 

**Strengths:**

*   **Novelty:** The use of a differentiable graph as a memory structure is a genuinely novel contribution.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The approach appears technically well-founded, and the authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the underlying concepts.
*   **Potential:** The graph representation offers a promising avenue for capturing the complex relational structure inherent in dialog.
*   **Author Responsiveness:** The substantial improvements made to the manuscript in response to initial feedback demonstrate a strong commitment to clarity and quality.

**Weaknesses:**

*   **Limited Performance Gains:** The performance on bAbI tasks, while competitive, does not yet justify the increased complexity of the proposed approach.
*   **Clarity:** While significantly improved, some aspects of the method remain difficult to grasp without careful study.
*   **Lack of Broader Evaluation:** The evaluation is currently limited to the bAbI tasks. Assessing the performance on more realistic and challenging dialog datasets is crucial.



**Overall Assessment:**

This is a technically accurate and thought-provoking paper that presents a novel and potentially powerful approach to dialog representation learning. While the current results do not demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods, the conceptual innovation and the potential for future development warrant publication. The authors have shown a willingness to address feedback, and I believe that further refinement and evaluation could lead to significant advances in the field. 

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions, specifically focusing on:

*   Providing a more intuitive explanation of the graph transformation process.
*   Elaborating on the design choices related to graph construction and traversal.
*   Discussing potential strategies for scaling the approach to larger and more complex dialog datasets.
*   Including a brief discussion of the computational cost of the method compared to alternative approaches.
