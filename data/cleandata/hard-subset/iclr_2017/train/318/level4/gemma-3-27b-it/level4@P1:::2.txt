This paper proposes a novel approach to representing and reasoning about dialog by learning to construct and manipulate a graph-structured memory. This memory is dynamically built during inference, and the model learns both the graph transformation parameters and how to encode sentences as input to the graph. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first implementation of a fully differentiable memory represented as a graph – a significantly more complex undertaking than previous approaches like memory networks. While the gains in performance on the bAbI tasks are not substantial, this is preliminary work, and the potential of a graph-based memory representation seems very promising. A major issue in earlier versions was clarity, but the author has significantly improved the presentation in subsequent iterations, making the paper much more accessible. This technically sound and thought-provoking paper is worthy of publication.

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:** The paper introduces a novel and potentially powerful approach to memory representation using graphs, and demonstrates promising initial results. The improvements in clarity demonstrate the author's responsiveness to feedback.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The core idea of learning to construct and modify a graph to represent the state of a dialog or task is compelling. Existing memory-based approaches, such as memory networks, represent information in a less structured manner. The ability to explicitly represent relationships between entities as edges in a graph could enable more sophisticated reasoning.

The author demonstrates the GGT-NN model on several tasks, including the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines. Achieving strong results on these diverse tasks suggests the model’s generalizability. While the performance on bAbI is comparable to existing memory networks, the novelty of the graph-based approach justifies publication, particularly given that this is early work.

The significant improvements in clarity between initial and final versions of the paper are noteworthy. This indicates the author’s commitment to clear communication and willingness to address feedback.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Complexity vs. Performance:** The paper acknowledges that the complexity of the graph-based approach does not yet translate into significant performance gains over simpler memory networks on the bAbI tasks. It would be beneficial to further investigate this trade-off. Are there specific bAbI tasks where the graph-based representation demonstrably outperforms other methods?
*   **Scalability:** The computational cost of manipulating graphs can be high, especially for large graphs. The paper briefly mentions this limitation. Further discussion of potential optimization techniques (e.g., sparse graph representations, efficient graph algorithms) would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Conducting ablation studies to evaluate the contribution of different components of the GGT-NN (e.g., direct reference, intermediate propagation) would provide further insights into the model’s behavior.
*   **Visualization:** Providing visualizations of the graphs learned by the model on example inputs (e.g., bAbI tasks, cellular automaton) would help readers understand how the model represents information.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the specific limitations of the current implementation that prevent it from outperforming existing memory networks on the bAbI tasks?
2.  What strategies are you considering to address the scalability issues associated with large graphs?
3.  Do you plan to conduct ablation studies to assess the importance of the different components of the GGT-NN model?
4.  Would it be possible to provide some example graphs learned by the model on the bAbI tasks or other datasets? This would aid in understanding the internal representations.
