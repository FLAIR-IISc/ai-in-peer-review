## Review of “LEARNING GRAPHICAL STATE TRANSITIONS”

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that leverages graph-structured data as an intermediate representation, allowing the model to dynamically construct and modify graphs based on input. The core idea – learning to represent and manipulate information using a differentiable graph structure – is compelling and offers a potentially powerful approach to reasoning and knowledge representation. The authors demonstrate the GGT-NN’s capabilities on the bAbI tasks, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, showcasing its ability to learn complex rules and solve tasks requiring structured reasoning.

**Decision:** Accept with minor revisions. The paper presents a novel and technically sound approach to incorporating graph structures into neural networks for dynamic reasoning. While the performance on the bAbI tasks is competitive, the potential for representing and manipulating knowledge in a graph-based manner is significant and warrants publication.

**Supporting Arguments:**

The primary strength of this work lies in its innovative approach to representing state and manipulating knowledge. Existing methods often rely on fixed memory structures (e.g., memory networks) or struggle to integrate structured information directly into the learning process. The GGT-NN, by learning to *construct* and *transform* graphs, offers a more flexible and potentially more powerful framework. The differentiable nature of the graph transformations is crucial, allowing for end-to-end training and adaptation. The successful application to both symbolic reasoning tasks (cellular automaton, Turing machines) and a natural language understanding benchmark (bAbI) demonstrates the model's versatility. The detailed description of the graph transformations, including node and edge updates, propagation, and aggregation, is well-presented and provides a solid foundation for future research. The authors also acknowledge limitations and suggest promising avenues for future work, indicating a thoughtful understanding of the challenges and opportunities.

**Additional Feedback:**

While the paper is significantly improved from earlier versions, some areas could benefit from further clarification. The notation is dense, and readers unfamiliar with graph neural networks might find some sections challenging. Providing more intuitive explanations alongside the mathematical formulations would enhance accessibility. A more comprehensive comparison to existing memory network architectures, specifically analyzing the trade-offs between the graph-based approach and traditional methods in terms of computational cost and scalability, would be valuable. The ablation studies (e.g., with and without direct reference) are helpful, but further analysis of the impact of different graph transformation configurations could provide deeper insights. For example, what are the benefits and drawbacks of enabling intermediate propagation? Is there a relationship between the size of the graph and model performance? A discussion of potential applications beyond the demonstrated tasks would also strengthen the paper. Finally, the description of the Turing Machine setup could be expanded slightly to allow a wider range of researchers to reproduce the results.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions the quadratic scaling of the GGT-NN with graph size. Are there potential strategies (e.g., sparse edge connections, graph pruning) to mitigate this scalability issue?
2.  How sensitive is the model to the initial graph structure (if any) provided as input? Does the performance vary significantly depending on whether the model starts with a completely empty graph or a pre-defined graph?
3.  In the bAbI experiments, you mention that Task 17 (Positional Reasoning) proved challenging. Could you elaborate on the specific difficulties encountered and whether exploring different graph representations or transformation strategies might improve performance on this task?
4.  Could you elaborate on how the choice of activation function (tanh) impacts the performance of the GRU units and graph transformations? Were other activation functions considered?
5.  The extended model using the sequence of graphs in Appendix D shows slightly inferior performance. Are there any hyperparameters that you tuned specifically for this architecture to try to improve performance?
