This paper presents an extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, introducing differentiable graph transformations as a mechanism for dynamically building and modifying graph structures. The core idea is compelling: to learn an internal graph representation that evolves to facilitate problem solving, specifically question answering. The authors propose five distinct, learned differentiable transformations that operate on the graph state at each timestep, typically trained in a supervised manner where the target graph state is provided. A concrete instantiation of this model iteratively updates an internal graph based on sequential input, achieving promising results on question answering tasks like BaBi.

The approach is highly interesting due to its ability to maintain a complex, graph-based representation of state while remaining fully differentiable, enabling end-to-end learning via gradient descent. This work represents a successful attempt to bridge continuous and symbolic representations, and appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate “symbolic” elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines). Unlike these prior works, the graph structure here is not fixed, allowing for a more flexible and evolving state representation.

However, I have two primary concerns. The first relates to the training methodology, which relies on providing the complete graph state at each timestep. While feasible for certain tasks like BaBi, this approach limits the model’s applicability to more complex problems where such intermediate supervision is unavailable or impractical. Scaling this technique to scenarios lacking explicit graph state supervision represents a significant challenge. 

My second concern is regarding the paper’s overall scope and presentation. The depth and detail of the work, while thorough, arguably align better with a journal publication than a conference format. The resulting density makes the paper somewhat challenging to follow and digest. Streamlining the presentation and focusing on the most crucial contributions could substantially improve its accessibility.
