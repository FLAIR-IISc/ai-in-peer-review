This paper proposes representing 'learning on the fly' as a graph (which acts as memory), and it has been demonstrated for the first time on bAbI tasks. Graph learning is part of the inference process, although it also involves long-term representation learning to encode graph transformation parameters and sentences as input to the graph. It seems to be the first implementation of a differential memory in the form of a graph: it is much more complex than previous approaches like memory networks without significant performance gains in bAbI tasks, but it is still very early work, and the representation of memory in the form of a graph seems much more powerful than a stack. Clarity is a major problem, but from an initial version that was constructive and read by a computer better than a human, the author later proposed a much better version. This original, technically accurate (as I understood it), and thought-provoking paper deserves publication.
