

Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

1. Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), which extends the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN) framework by allowing graph-structured intermediate representations. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable the model to incrementally construct and modify graphs based on textual input. The paper demonstrates that this architecture successfully learns to solve most bAbI tasks (reaching 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks), discovers rules for a cellular automaton (Rule 30), and models Turing machines. The approach represents a meaningful extension of prior graph neural network work by enabling the network to dynamically construct and modify graph structures rather than working with fixed graphs.

2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of graph neural networks with a well-motivated extension that demonstrates strong empirical results on multiple challenging tasks.

3. Supporting Arguments
The paper successfully addresses three key questions:

First, the paper clearly identifies the problem: current graph neural networks require fixed graph structures as input, limiting their ability to handle tasks where the graph structure itself needs to be constructed from unstructured input. The authors address this by introducing differentiable graph transformations that allow the network to build and modify graph structures as part of the learning process.

Second, the approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. The paper provides a thorough background on GNNs, GG-NNs, and GGS-NNs, and clearly explains how the GGT-NN extends these prior works. The authors correctly identify the limitations of existing approaches (fixed graph structures) and provide a compelling justification for their extension.

Third, the paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The results on the bAbI tasks are impressive, with the GGT-NN model achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks (compared to 9/20 for the model without direct reference). The model also successfully learns Rule 30 cellular automaton and Turing machines, with high accuracy on both tasks and strong generalization to longer sequences. The experimental design is sound, with appropriate baselines and careful analysis of results.

One minor point: the draft review incorrectly stated that Rule 30 was "far from solved," but the paper actually demonstrates high accuracy on this task (Table 3 shows strong results). The model achieved near-perfect performance on Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks, with perfect output in the majority of extended tasks.

4. Additional Feedback
To further strengthen the paper, consider the following points:

- The paper could provide more detailed analysis of why direct reference significantly improves performance (e.g., visualizing which nodes receive updates based on direct references).
- While the paper discusses computational complexity in the conclusion, a more thorough analysis of time and space requirements for different task sizes would be valuable, particularly for larger graphs.
- The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model was trained with strong supervision (full graph information), while other models were trained end-to-end. A more detailed discussion of the trade-offs between strong supervision and end-to-end learning would be helpful for readers.

Questions for Authors:
1. The paper states that the GGT-NN model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks, while without direct reference it reaches this level on only 9/20 tasks. Could you elaborate on why direct reference provides such a significant performance boost, and whether this is primarily due to better node selection or improved graph construction?
2. The paper mentions that the model requires additional supervision for meaningful graph construction, using the correct graph at each timestep. How might this supervision be reduced in future work while maintaining performance?
3. The paper notes that the distributed nature of GGT-NNs makes them less time and space efficient than memory networks or DNCs. Could you provide more specific quantitative comparisons of the time/space complexity for the bAbI tasks versus other approaches?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a meaningful contribution to graph neural networks. The GGT-NN architecture is well-motivated, the experiments are thorough, and the results demonstrate clear advantages over prior work for tasks that naturally map to graph structures. The paper should be accepted.
