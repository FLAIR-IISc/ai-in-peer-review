

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a novel extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that uses graph-structured data as an intermediate representation. The model learns to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, then uses these graphs to produce various outputs. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks (solving nearly all tasks with 95%+ accuracy), as well as on discovering rules for a simple cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines. The key innovation is the introduction of differentiable graph transformations that allow the model to incrementally build and modify graph structures during processing.

## 2. Decision

Accept (Weak Accept). The paper presents a technically sound and novel approach with promising results on multiple benchmark tasks. The main reason for acceptance is the clear demonstration of the model's ability to learn graph-based representations for complex reasoning tasks, particularly the pathfinding task (Task 19) where the graphical representation provides a clear advantage. The second key reason is the well-motivated extension of prior work on graph neural networks that properly situates the contribution within the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper successfully addresses three key questions:

1. The paper clearly identifies the problem: extending graph-based neural networks to allow for graph-structured intermediate representations rather than fixed graph inputs. This addresses a significant limitation in prior work where graphs were fixed inputs.

2. The approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. The authors carefully review related work on graph neural networks, memory networks, and other approaches to structured data processing, clearly demonstrating how GGT-NNs extend previous work while addressing its limitations. The motivation for using graph representations for tasks like pathfinding is particularly strong.

3. The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The model achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks, with particularly impressive results on Task 19 (pathfinding), where it significantly outperforms other approaches. The model also successfully learns the rules of a cellular automaton and Turing machines without explicit rule specification. The ablation study comparing direct reference versus non-direct reference models provides convincing evidence for the importance of the direct reference mechanism.

While the paper is technically sound, the claimed advantages over memory networks and other approaches could be more clearly articulated. The paper demonstrates comparable performance to state-of-the-art methods on most tasks, with clear advantages on specific tasks (like pathfinding) where the graphical representation is naturally suited. The authors effectively demonstrate that the GGT-NN model can learn to represent complex relationships that are difficult to encode in other architectures.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper further, I recommend the following improvements:

1. Clarify the relationship between GGT-NN and prior work more explicitly. The paper mentions that the GGT-NN model "can process data in a distributed fashion" while "memory networks, DNCs, and HAM modules, which are restricted to processing only a fixed number of locations in a given timestep." This advantage should be quantified with specific examples of tasks where this distributed processing provides clear benefits beyond what is demonstrated in the paper.

2. Address the computational complexity concern more thoroughly. The paper notes that "the time and space required to run a GGT-NN step scales quadratically with the size of the input," which could be a significant limitation for larger applications. Provide more discussion on potential optimizations (e.g., sparse edge connections) and their impact on performance.

3. Clarify the "direct reference" mechanism throughout the paper. While the paper demonstrates its importance, the implementation details and when it should be used could be better explained to help readers understand when this feature would be most beneficial.

4. Consider including a more detailed comparison of the model's performance with the GGS-NN model (discussed in Li et al., 2016), particularly since the paper mentions that the GGS-NN could not be directly evaluated on the textual form of bAbI tasks.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you provide more details on the computational efficiency of the GGT-NN model compared to other approaches, including training time and memory requirements for the tasks demonstrated?

2. The paper states that "the model with direct reference reaches 95% accuracy on 19/20 of the bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference reaches that level of accuracy on 9/20 of the tasks." Could you elaborate on which specific tasks benefit most from the direct reference mechanism, and why?

3. For the rule discovery tasks (cellular automaton and Turing machines), how does the model's performance change when the rules are more complex or when the number of states increases? This would help assess the scalability of the approach.

The paper presents a technically sound and innovative approach to using graph structures as intermediate representations in neural networks. The results are compelling, particularly for tasks where graphical representations are naturally suited. With the suggested clarifications, this work would be a strong contribution to the field of structured neural networks.
