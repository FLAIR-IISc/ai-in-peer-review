

The paper proposes an extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGNN) by incorporating the ability to generate complex graph transformations as part of its internal representation. The core idea is to develop a model that constructs or modifies graph structures during computation to solve problems, with a specific focus on question-answering tasks. The authors introduce five distinct differentiable graph transformations, learned in a supervised manner using provided graph states at each timestep. A specific instantiation of the model processes input sequences by iteratively updating an internal graph state to produce final predictions, demonstrating promising results on question-answering benchmarks like the Babi tasks.  

The approach is compelling, as it successfully maintains a complex graph as a differentiable internal representation—enabling gradient-based learning while blending continuous and symbolic computation. This represents a significant advancement over recent efforts to integrate symbolic elements into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTMs), as the model’s state shape is not fixed and can dynamically evolve during processing. However, a primary concern is the training methodology: requiring the graph state to be provided at every timestep is only feasible for specific tasks like Babi, rendering it impractical for more complex problems where such supervision is unavailable. Additionally, the paper’s depth and technical density suggest it may be better suited for a journal venue rather than a conference, as the current structure impedes readability and accessibility for a conference audience.
