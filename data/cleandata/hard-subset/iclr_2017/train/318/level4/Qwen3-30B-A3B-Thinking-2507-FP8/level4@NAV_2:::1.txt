

1. The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that enables learning to construct and modify graph-structured intermediate representations based on textual input. The model demonstrates the ability to use these graphs to produce various outputs, including textual answers for the bAbI tasks and learning rules for cellular automata and Turing machines. The authors present a comprehensive framework of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that enable this capability.

2. I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a significant contribution by providing a flexible framework for graph-based learning with strong empirical validation on multiple challenging tasks.

3. The GGT-NN model achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 out of 20 bAbI tasks, with particularly strong performance on task 19 (pathfinding), where it outperforms all but one state-of-the-art approach. The rule discovery tasks demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex update rules from graph-structured data, with high accuracy on both cellular automata and Turing machine tasks. The graph-structured intermediate representation provides a clear advantage for tasks with inherent graphical structure, as evidenced by the model's superior performance on pathfinding compared to models that don't use graph representations.

4. The authors should provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN model as the graph size increases. While the paper mentions quadratic scaling with input size, a more thorough discussion of practical limitations and potential optimizations for larger graphs would strengthen the paper.

5. Could you clarify how the model's performance changes when the graph structure becomes significantly more complex (e.g., with more node types or edge connections)? Additionally, how does the model handle cases where the graph structure requires dynamic changes beyond the transformations described in the paper?
