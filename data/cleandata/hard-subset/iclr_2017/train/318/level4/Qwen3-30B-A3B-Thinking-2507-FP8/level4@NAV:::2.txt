

This paper introduces a graph-based approach for on-the-fly representation of dialog as a memory structure, first evaluated on the bAbI tasks. The graph learning component operates during inference, while long-term representation learning optimizes graph transformation parameters and sentence encodings. This represents the first implementation of a differentiable memory structured as a graph, though it is substantially more complex than prior methods like memory networks without demonstrating significant performance gains on the bAbI tasks. The work remains preliminary, yet the graph-based memory representation holds greater expressive potential compared to stack-based alternatives. Clarity has been substantially improved from an initial machine-readable draft to a significantly revised version. Despite its preliminary nature, this original, technically sound (within the reviewer's comprehension), and thought-provoking contribution merits publication. The current results do not establish whether the complex graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capacity relative to other methods.
