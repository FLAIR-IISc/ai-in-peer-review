

This paper introduces a compelling extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGNN) that enables the model to dynamically construct and modify graph structures as its internal state during computation. The core innovation lies in learning differentiable graph transformations—specifically, five distinct operations (e.g., node/edge addition, modification, or deletion)—to iteratively refine the graph representation while solving structured tasks. The authors demonstrate this approach on question-answering (QA) tasks using the bAbI dataset, where the model processes input sequences, incrementally updates its graph state, and produces predictions. The results show competitive performance, highlighting the model’s ability to maintain complex, evolving representations.

The primary strength of this work is its elegant synthesis of symbolic reasoning and differentiable learning. Unlike prior approaches (e.g., Memory Networks, NTMs), which rely on fixed-size, rigid memory structures, this model’s graph state can grow, shrink, or reconfigure dynamically during inference. This flexibility allows the model to encode task-specific structural knowledge (e.g., relationships between entities in a QA context) without predefined constraints. Crucially, the graph transformations are fully differentiable, enabling end-to-end training via gradient descent—a significant advancement over non-differentiable symbolic systems. The authors effectively position this as a bridge between continuous optimization and symbolic manipulation, offering a promising pathway for integrating structured reasoning into neural architectures.

However, two concerns warrant careful consideration. First, the training methodology relies on supervised supervision of intermediate graph states (i.e., providing the target graph at each timestep). While feasible for benchmark tasks like bAbI (where the ground-truth graph evolution is known), this approach is impractical for real-world problems where such intermediate states are unavailable. The paper does not adequately address how the model would generalize to tasks lacking explicit graph supervision, potentially limiting its broader applicability. The authors should clarify whether the transformations could be learned via alternative means (e.g., reinforcement learning or self-supervision) or if the framework is inherently restricted to tasks with annotated graph dynamics.

Second, the paper’s depth and scope seem better suited for a journal venue than a conference like ICLR. The technical exposition is dense, with significant space devoted to defining transformations, theoretical properties, and ablation studies. While these elements are valuable, the current structure—particularly the lack of clear motivation for the five transformations, minimal comparison to related work beyond a brief mention of NTMs, and limited discussion of computational efficiency—makes the paper challenging to digest at a conference level. A more focused presentation (e.g., emphasizing one or two key transformations, streamlining theoretical derivations, and adding concrete failure cases) would strengthen its impact for a conference audience.

Overall, this work presents a novel and technically sound approach to dynamic graph-based reasoning. The ability to learn graph transformations while maintaining differentiability is a meaningful contribution to the field. The bAbI results are promising, though the reliance on intermediate state supervision requires deeper justification for real-world deployment. With revisions to address the training methodology’s limitations and streamline the presentation for conciseness, this paper would make a strong contribution. As currently written, it leans toward a journal submission due to its thoroughness, but the core idea is sufficiently innovative to merit acceptance at ICLR if the concerns above are resolved. I recommend *accept* pending minor revisions addressing the points above.
