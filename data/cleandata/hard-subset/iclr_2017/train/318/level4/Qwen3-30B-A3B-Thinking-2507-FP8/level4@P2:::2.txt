

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that enables graph-structured intermediate representations. The model learns to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, then uses these graphs to produce various outputs. The authors demonstrate the approach on three key domains: the bAbI question-answering tasks, a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines. The model achieves 95%+ accuracy on most bAbI tasks, with particularly strong results on pathfinding (task 19), where it outperforms all but one competing model. It also successfully learns the underlying rules for both the cellular automaton and Turing machine tasks without explicit rule specifications.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The paper makes a clear contribution to the literature on graph-structured neural networks, extending the GGS-NN framework in a meaningful way that allows for dynamic graph construction and modification. The technical approach is well-motivated, building appropriately on prior work with GNNs, GGS-NNs, and memory networks. The experimental results are thorough and demonstrate the model's versatility across different task types.

The key strengths of the paper include:
1. A novel extension to GGS-NNs that enables graph transformations rather than just graph processing
2. Strong empirical results on multiple domains, particularly the pathfinding task where the graph representation is clearly advantageous
3. Clear demonstration of the model's ability to learn rules from data without explicit supervision
4. Thoughtful comparison with relevant prior work, especially regarding the explicit graph structure versus implicit memory representations

The paper could be improved in several ways. First, the explanation of the direct reference mechanism could be clearer, as it appears to be a significant factor in performance improvements on most tasks. A more detailed description of how this mechanism works and why it's beneficial would strengthen the paper. Second, the discussion of computational complexity is somewhat brief; while the quadratic complexity is acknowledged as a limitation, more concrete discussion of potential optimization strategies would be valuable. Third, the paper could provide more comparative analysis of the trade-offs between the GGT-NN approach and existing memory network architectures.

The results on the bAbI tasks are competitive with state-of-the-art approaches, though the performance gains over memory networks are modest (0.4-64% improvement on specific tasks). The paper acknowledges this and rightly notes that the graph representation appears particularly advantageous for certain tasks like pathfinding, where the model's distributed processing capability is well-suited to the problem structure. The demonstration of rule learning in cellular automata and Turing machines is particularly compelling, as it shows the model's ability to learn complex transformation rules without explicit supervision.

The paper is well-structured and clearly written, though some technical details could be presented more accessibly. The authors have made significant improvements to the clarity from earlier versions, and the current version is much more readable than the initial draft.

To further strengthen the paper, I suggest:
1. Adding a more detailed explanation of the direct reference mechanism, including how it's implemented and why it improves performance
2. Expanding the discussion of computational complexity and potential optimization strategies
3. Including a more comprehensive comparison of the trade-offs between GGT-NN and other approaches
4. Clarifying the relationship between the GGT-NN architecture and the graph sequence input extension described in Appendix D

The authors should address these points to strengthen the paper's contribution and make the technical approach more accessible to readers.

Key questions for the authors:
1. How do you plan to address the quadratic complexity issue mentioned in the paper, particularly for larger graphs?
2. Could you provide more intuition about why direct reference improves performance across most tasks?
3. How would the model scale to significantly larger graphs with more complex structures, and what architectural modifications might be necessary?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances the field of graph-based neural networks. The approach is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrates clear benefits over existing methods for certain tasks. The paper provides a solid foundation for future work on graph-based memory and representation learning.
