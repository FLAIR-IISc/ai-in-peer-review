

The paper introduces a novel framework for graph-to-graph learning via differentiable graph transformations, enabling gradient-based optimization for tasks involving sequential graph structures. The core contribution lies in formalizing a set of differentiable operations that modify graph topology and node attributes, facilitating end-to-end training for graph sequence prediction. This approach is naturally applied to learning cellular automata (CA), where the graph evolves iteratively: nodes represent cells with binary states (0/1), edges define neighborhood relationships, and each time step generates a new graph state. The authors demonstrate this on Rule 30, a well-known chaotic CA, though the model achieves only partial success on this benchmark, suggesting significant room for improvement in handling complex dynamics. While the theoretical foundation is sound, the empirical results on Rule 30 lack depth—no ablation studies or error analysis are provided to explain the model’s limitations, which weakens the argument for the framework’s efficacy in highly non-linear systems.

The framework integrates insights from GGS-NN (Xu et al., 2018) to bridge graph representations with textual outputs, using graphs as intermediate representations for tasks requiring natural language generation. Specifically, the model processes input graphs to produce graph sequences, then maps these to textual answers via a decoder, achieving state-of-the-art results on the bAbI textual reasoning tasks. This integration is well-motivated: by leveraging graph-based intermediate representations, the model avoids the pitfalls of directly mapping graphs to text (e.g., loss of structural information), and the results on bAbI (particularly on tasks requiring relational reasoning) are compelling. The performance gains over prior graph-based methods (e.g., GAT, GIN) are significant and well-documented, with clear ablation showing the necessity of the graph-to-graph module.

However, the paper’s primary weakness lies in the imbalance between theoretical novelty and empirical validation. While the differentiable graph transformations are mathematically rigorous, the Rule 30 experiment serves more as a conceptual illustration than a robust evaluation. The authors do not compare against CA-specific baselines (e.g., rule-based simulators or neural CA models like those in [1,2]), nor do they quantify the model’s ability to generalize to other CAs (e.g., Rule 110). This omission leaves unanswered whether the framework is truly tailored for sequential graph dynamics or merely a flexible graph-processing module. Additionally, the Rule 30 results—where the model fails to reproduce the full CA evolution—should be explicitly framed as a limitation rather than a minor shortcoming, as the paper’s core claim hinges on the framework’s capacity for such tasks.

The bAbI results, while strong, lack contextual depth. The paper does not discuss why graph-based intermediate representations outperform direct text-to-text approaches (e.g., transformers) on relational tasks, nor does it analyze failure cases. For instance, it would be valuable to know if the model struggles with tasks involving long-range dependencies (e.g., bAbI task 20), which are common in relational reasoning. Without this, the superiority over baselines appears somewhat empirical rather than principled. Furthermore, the computational cost of the graph transformations (e.g., time complexity for edge additions/removals) is unaddressed, which is critical for scalability to large graphs.

The writing is generally clear but occasionally imprecise. For example, the phrase "the graph of nodes grows at each iteration" is misleading; it is the graph’s *structure* (e.g., node/edge sets) that evolves, not the node count (which remains constant in CA). Similarly, "special nodes 0/1" should read "nodes labeled 0/1" to avoid confusion with node identifiers. The connection between the CA task and the broader graph-to-graph learning objective is underdeveloped—why is CA a suitable testbed? A brief discussion of how this task’s properties (e.g., local update rules, state transitions) align with the framework’s design would strengthen the narrative.

Overall, the paper presents a technically interesting contribution with a clear path to applications like relational reasoning. The bAbI results validate the approach’s practical utility, but the Rule 30 experiment fails to substantiate the core theoretical claim. To merit acceptance, the authors should: (1) provide a more rigorous evaluation of the graph transformations on CA tasks (including comparisons to CA-specific baselines), (2) clarify the relationship between the framework’s design and the CA dynamics, and (3) deepen the analysis of the bAbI results to explain *why* graph intermediates improve performance. As-is, the work is promising but not yet sufficiently compelling for publication, as the empirical support for the central innovation remains thin. A revised version addressing these gaps would significantly strengthen the paper.
