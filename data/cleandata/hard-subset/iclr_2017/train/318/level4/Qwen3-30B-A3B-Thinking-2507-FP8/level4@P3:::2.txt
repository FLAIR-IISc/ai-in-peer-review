

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that enables learning to construct and modify graph-structured intermediate representations based on textual input. The model successfully learns to solve nearly all bAbI tasks (Weston et al., 2016), discovers rules for a simple cellular automaton, and models a family of Turing machines. The key innovation lies in introducing differentiable graph transformations that allow the network to incrementally build and modify graphs while maintaining full differentiability for end-to-end training. The paper makes a compelling case for graph-structured internal representations as a powerful paradigm for modeling relationships and state transitions in complex tasks.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating a novel approach to graph-based state representation that successfully handles a variety of tasks with graph-structured intermediate states. The core idea is well-motivated, and the experimental results demonstrate the model's capabilities on multiple challenging benchmarks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a clear problem: extending previous graph-based neural network approaches to allow for dynamic construction of graph structures rather than relying on fixed graph inputs. This is a significant step toward more flexible neural architectures that can manipulate structured representations.

The approach is well-placed in the literature, building on GGS-NNs (Li et al., 2016) while addressing their limitation of requiring fixed graph inputs. The paper provides a thorough comparison with related work, including memory networks, neural Turing machines, and graph convolutional networks, demonstrating how GGT-NNs differ in their ability to construct and modify graph structures.

The paper provides strong empirical support for its claims. The GGT-NN achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks (with direct reference), outperforming previous models on specific tasks like pathfinding (task 19) where graph representations are particularly advantageous. The model also successfully learns to model Rule 30 cellular automata and Turing machines without explicit rule specification, demonstrating the generalization capability of the approach. The authors provide sufficient detail about the model architecture, training procedures, and evaluation metrics to support their claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is well-executed, several improvements would strengthen it:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN model and potential optimizations to reduce the quadratic scaling of graph propagation. The current paper notes this limitation but doesn't explore practical solutions.

2. The comparison with other approaches could be strengthened by including more recent models (e.g., the 2016/2017 advances in memory networks and neural Turing machines) and by providing more detailed analysis of why GGT-NNs outperform other approaches on specific tasks.

3. The paper could provide more insight into when graph-based representations are superior to other approaches (e.g., by analyzing which bAbI tasks particularly benefit from the graph structure).

4. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the limitations of the current approach, particularly regarding the model's ability to scale to larger graphs and more complex tasks.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 bAbI tasks, while the model without direct reference achieves this on only 9/20 tasks. Could you provide more detailed analysis of which specific tasks benefit most from direct reference, and why?

2. For the pathfinding task (task 19), the paper notes that GGT-NN with direct reference outperforms all other models except DMN+. Could you elaborate on why the graph-based approach particularly benefits this task, and whether this advantage would persist with larger pathfinding problems?

3. The paper demonstrates the model's ability to learn rules for cellular automata and Turing machines without explicit supervision. How does the model's performance scale with the complexity of the rules (e.g., for more complex Turing machines with more states or symbols)?

4. The paper mentions that the model's time and space complexity scales quadratically with input size. Are there any specific strategies the authors have considered to mitigate this scaling issue for larger graphs, such as using sparse graph representations or selective node processing?
