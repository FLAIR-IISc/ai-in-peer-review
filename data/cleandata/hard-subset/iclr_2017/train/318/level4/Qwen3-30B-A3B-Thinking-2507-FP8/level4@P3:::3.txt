

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## Summary of Contributions
This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that enables the model to construct and modify graph-structured intermediate representations based on textual input. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to process graphs in complex ways. The model is demonstrated on two main tasks: the bAbI question-answering tasks, where it achieves 95% accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks, and rule discovery tasks involving cellular automata and Turing machines, where it successfully learns the underlying rules from graph-structured data. The key contribution is the ability to maintain and modify a graph-based internal state, providing a flexible framework for representing structured knowledge that can be learned end-to-end.

## Decision
**Reject**

## Supporting Arguments
The paper addresses an important problem in neural network architectures for structured data, and the GGT-NN framework is well-motivated by the limitations of existing graph-based models that require fixed graph structures. The approach is well-placed in the literature, building effectively on prior work in graph neural networks and sequence-to-sequence models.

However, the paper fails to provide sufficient evidence that the proposed model's key innovation—learning to construct and modify graphs from textual input without explicit supervision—actually works as claimed. The model's performance on bAbI tasks relies heavily on strong supervision where the correct graph is provided at each timestep, which is not a realistic scenario for most applications. As the authors acknowledge in Section 4.1, "the model appeared to require additional supervision to extract meaningful graph-structured data," and they provide the correct graph at each timestep as additional supervision. This undermines the main claim that the model can "learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input" without such supervision.

The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for the proposed graph transformations. The differentiable graph transformations are presented as ad hoc modifications to the GG-NN framework without justification for their specific formulations. The authors do not provide any analysis of why these particular transformations are necessary or sufficient for the tasks they address, nor do they discuss potential limitations or failure modes of the approach.

Furthermore, the empirical results are not sufficiently compared to the most relevant baselines. While the paper includes results on the bAbI tasks, it does not compare the GGT-NN against the GGS-NN model (which the authors claim to extend), making it difficult to assess whether the graph construction capability actually provides an advantage. The paper also fails to demonstrate the model's performance on tasks where the graph structure is not provided during training, which would be the most compelling evidence for the approach's value.

## Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide results on tasks where the graph structure is not provided during training, demonstrating that the model can learn to construct meaningful graphs from textual input without strong supervision.
2. Include a more detailed comparison with the GGS-NN model (the predecessor they claim to extend) to show the specific benefits of the graph construction capability.
3. Add theoretical justification for the proposed graph transformations, explaining why they are necessary and sufficient for the tasks they address.
4. Consider simplifying the presentation to focus on the core contribution and its empirical validation, rather than including extensive details about the graph format for specific tasks.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that the model "successfully learns to solve almost all of the bAbI tasks" with 95% accuracy, but the strong supervision using the correct graph at each timestep is critical to this success. Could you provide results on the bAbI tasks without this strong supervision, where the model must learn to construct the graph from scratch based only on the textual input and the final answer?
2. The paper claims that the GGT-NN model "learns to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways based on textual input," but the experiments only demonstrate this when the correct graph is provided during training. How would the model's performance change if the graph structure were not provided at all during training?
3. The paper states that the GGT-NN model "can learn complex update rules by training it to model a simple 1D cellular automaton and arbitrary 4-state Turing machines." However, the training examples for these tasks include the correct graph at each timestep. How would the model perform if it had to learn the graph structure from the sequence of states without explicit graph supervision?
4. The paper claims the model is more general than existing approaches like Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines because it can modify the graph structure. However, the model's performance on the bAbI tasks relies on the same type of strong supervision as these other models. How does the GGT-NN model's ability to modify the graph structure provide an advantage over these approaches in scenarios where graph structure is not provided during training?
