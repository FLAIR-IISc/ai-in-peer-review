

This paper introduces an approach for online learning to represent dialog as a graph (serving as the memory), initially evaluated on the bAbI tasks. Graph learning is integrated into the inference process, while long-term representation learning optimizes graph transformation parameters and sentence encodings for graph input. This constitutes the first implementation of a differentiable memory structured as a graph. Although the graph-based approach is significantly more complex than prior methods like memory networks, it does not demonstrate substantial performance gains on the bAbI tasks. The work remains preliminary, though the graph-based memory representation appears inherently more expressive than stack-based alternatives. The paper has substantially improved in clarity from its initial version, which was technically accurate but poorly written for human readers; the revised version is significantly more readable. Despite its preliminary nature and unresolved questions regarding learning capacity, this original, technically sound, and thought-provoking contribution is worthy of publication. The current results do not yet establish whether the complex graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capabilities compared to other methods. Performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to state-of-the-art memory networks but remains inferior to more traditional rule-based induction approaches (see [citation]).
