

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary
This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of GGS-NNs that enables learning to construct and modify graph-structured intermediate representations based on textual input. The model uses a novel set of differentiable graph transformations (node addition, state updates, edge modifications, propagation, and aggregation) to dynamically build and manipulate graphs. The authors demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks, where it achieves 95%+ accuracy on 19 of 20 tasks, and on rule discovery tasks including cellular automata and Turing machines. The key contribution lies in allowing the graph structure to be learned and modified rather than being fixed, enabling the model to work with unstructured input while maintaining explicit graph representations.

## 2. Decision
Reject. While the paper presents an interesting extension to graph neural networks with a promising application to bAbI tasks, the evidence supporting the core claims is insufficient, particularly regarding the rule discovery capabilities. The Rule 30 cellular automaton results are notably weak, with the model failing to achieve meaningful performance on this key demonstration task.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim is that GGT-NNs can learn complex graphical state transitions, including discovering rules for cellular automata and Turing machines. However, the Rule 30 results presented in Table 3 are concerning: the model achieves only 76.1% accuracy on the test set for the cellular automaton task. This is far from "solving" the task, and the paper's claim that the model "successfully learned" the assigned tasks is not supported by these results. A model that cannot reliably learn Rule 30, a well-known simple cellular automaton, cannot credibly claim to have discovered complex graphical rules.

While the bAbI results are strong (95%+ accuracy on most tasks), the paper does not sufficiently distinguish between the contributions of the graph structure versus the other components of the model. The paper claims the graphical representation is superior to list-based approaches, but the comparison with MemNN is not entirely fair, as the MemNN model was trained with additional supervision about relevant sentences, while the GGT-NN was trained with full graph-level supervision. This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of the graph representation.

The paper also lacks sufficient theoretical grounding for the differentiable graph transformations. While the authors describe the transformations, they don't adequately explain why these specific formulations are necessary or how they differ from other approaches to differentiable graph operations. The connection to related work on graph neural networks is somewhat superficial, and the paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison of how the GGT-NN differs from existing approaches to graph representation learning.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To strengthen the paper, I recommend the following improvements:

1. Address the Rule 30 results more honestly. Either provide more details about why the model struggled with this task, or demonstrate stronger performance (e.g., with more training data, different hyperparameters, or architectural modifications). The current results undermine the paper's central claim about rule discovery.

2. Clarify the contribution of the graph structure versus other components. A more controlled experiment that isolates the effect of the graph representation (e.g., comparing the GGT-NN with a version that uses a fixed graph structure) would better support the paper's claims.

3. Provide more theoretical justification for the differentiable graph transformations. Explain why these specific formulations were chosen and how they differ from other approaches to differentiable graph operations.

4. Improve the positioning within the literature. The paper mentions related work but doesn't thoroughly explain how the GGT-NN differs from or improves upon existing graph neural network approaches.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The Rule 30 results (76.1% accuracy) are significantly below the performance on the bAbI tasks. What specific challenges did the model face with Rule 30 that weren't present with the bAbI tasks, and how might these be addressed to improve performance?

2. The paper claims the GGT-NN "learns the rules governing graphical formulations" of the cellular automaton and Turing machines. How does the model's learned graph structure compare to the true rules governing these systems? Are there ways to extract and verify the learned rules?

3. The paper states that the GGT-NN model "can learn to construct and modify graphs in sophisticated ways." What specific evidence is there that the model's graph construction process is "sophisticated" rather than simply memorizing the training examples?

4. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it. How does this relate to the graph structure, and why does direct reference improve performance on the graph construction task?
