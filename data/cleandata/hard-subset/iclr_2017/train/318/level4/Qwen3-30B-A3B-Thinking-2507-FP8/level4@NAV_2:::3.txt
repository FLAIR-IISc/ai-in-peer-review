

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), a significant extension of GGS-NNs that enables the model to learn to construct and modify complex graph structures as internal representations based on textual input. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to process graphs in sophisticated ways. The model is demonstrated on multiple tasks including the bAbI question-answering dataset, a cellular automaton, and Turing machines, achieving strong results across these domains.

I recommend acceptance with minor revisions. The GGT-NN represents a meaningful advancement over existing graph neural network approaches by allowing dynamic construction and modification of graph structures rather than relying on fixed graph representations. The model's ability to learn graph-structured internal states while maintaining differentiability provides a compelling blend of symbolic and continuous representation that addresses limitations in existing approaches like memory networks and NTMs.

The paper's contributions are well-supported by empirical results, particularly on the bAbI tasks where the GGT-NN achieves 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks (with direct reference) and 100% accuracy on 11 tasks. The model's performance on the pathfinding task (task 19) demonstrates a clear advantage over other approaches when the task naturally lends itself to graphical representation. The authors provide thorough comparisons with existing methods and demonstrate how the GGT-NN's graph-based approach outperforms alternative representations on specific tasks.

The paper would benefit from more detailed discussion of the computational complexity of the model, particularly the quadratic scaling with graph size, and how this might limit scalability for larger graphs. Additionally, the authors should elaborate on potential applications beyond the demonstrated tasks and provide more concrete examples of how the model could be adapted for real-world problems requiring graph-structured reasoning.

1. How does the model handle graph structures that exceed the maximum number of nodes specified during training, and what are the implications for tasks requiring dynamic graph size?
2. What specific limitations exist in the current training approach that requires full graph supervision at each timestep, and how might these be addressed for more complex tasks where such supervision is unavailable?
3. Can you provide more details about the generalization performance of the model when applied to tasks that differ significantly from those demonstrated in the paper, such as more complex real-world knowledge graphs?
