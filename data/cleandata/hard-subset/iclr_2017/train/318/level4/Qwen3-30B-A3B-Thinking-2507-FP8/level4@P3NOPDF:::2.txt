

This paper introduces a novel approach to dialog modeling by representing memory as a differentiable graph constructed dynamically during inference. The authors present the first implementation of a graph-based differentiable memory, demonstrating its application on the bAbI tasks. While the graph structure offers a more expressive memory representation than simpler alternatives (e.g., stack-based memory), the current implementation is significantly more complex than standard memory networks without clear performance gains on bAbI. The model incorporates long-term learning of graph transformation parameters and sentence encodings as graph inputs, with graph construction integrated into the inference process.  

The revised paper exhibits substantial improvements in clarity over the initial draft, resolving readability issues that previously made the work feel machine-generated rather than human-written. The technical approach is sound within the scope of the presented experiments, and the core idea—using a graph as a dynamic, learnable memory structure—is thought-provoking and potentially impactful. However, the preliminary results do not yet establish whether the graph-based memory provides meaningful advantages in learning capacity or generalization over existing methods. Performance on bAbI tasks is comparable to state-of-the-art memory networks but remains inferior to rule-based baselines (e.g., the rule induction approach in Weston et al. 2015). This gap suggests the current graph framework may not yet outperform more interpretable, traditional methods.  

The paper’s primary contribution lies in its conceptual novelty: a differentiable graph memory that evolves during dialog processing, rather than relying on fixed structures like stacks or vectors. While the complexity of the graph construction and transformation mechanisms is justified by the expressive potential of graphs, the work remains preliminary. The authors acknowledge this by focusing on feasibility rather than empirical superiority, but future work should rigorously evaluate whether the added complexity translates to tangible benefits on larger, more realistic dialog datasets.  

The writing quality has improved notably in the revision, with clearer explanations of the graph construction process and model architecture. However, the paper could better contextualize its relationship to prior memory-based approaches. For instance, a more detailed comparison of the graph’s expressiveness versus standard memory networks (e.g., via theoretical analysis or ablation studies) would strengthen the argument for the proposed design. Additionally, the authors should clarify how the graph’s dynamic nature avoids the pitfalls of static memory structures (e.g., fixed-size buffers), which are common in existing work.  

Despite these limitations, the paper makes a valuable contribution to the field. It opens a promising direction for memory-augmented dialog systems by formalizing memory as a learnable graph, which could better capture relational dependencies in conversations. The technical execution is rigorous, and the work is sufficiently novel to warrant publication at ICLR. The authors should address the performance gap with rule-based methods in future iterations, but the current version provides a solid foundation for this line of research.  

In summary, this is a technically sound, innovative paper that advances the exploration of graph-based memory in dialog systems. While the empirical results are preliminary and do not yet justify the added complexity, the conceptual framework is compelling and aligns with growing interest in structured representations for neural models. The revision has significantly enhanced readability, making the contribution accessible to the broader community. Given its novelty and potential, the paper is recommended for publication with minor revisions to address the performance comparison and clarify the model’s theoretical advantages.
