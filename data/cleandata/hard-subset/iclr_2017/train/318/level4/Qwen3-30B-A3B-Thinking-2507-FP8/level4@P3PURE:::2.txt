

This paper introduces a novel approach to representing dialogues as graphs, which serve as a differentiable memory structure during inference. The method is first evaluated on the bAbI tasks, with graph learning integrated into the inference process. While long-term representation learning is employed to optimize graph transformation parameters and sentence encodings, the work represents the first implementation of a graph-based differentiable memory. The proposed graph representation offers greater expressive power than stack-based alternatives (e.g., traditional memory networks), though it is significantly more complex without demonstrating substantial performance gains on bAbI tasks—comparable to state-of-the-art memory networks but still inferior to traditional rule-based induction methods. The work remains preliminary, yet its conceptual novelty and technical accuracy (within the reviewer's understanding) make it thought-provoking. The authors have substantially improved clarity from an initial version that prioritized technical precision over readability, resulting in a much more accessible manuscript. Despite unresolved questions about whether the graph-based memory enhances learning capacity or generalization, the paper’s originality and technical rigor merit publication.
