

The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GGS-NN) that enables learning to construct and modify graph structures as intermediate representations for processing textual input. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be combined to build complex graph-based internal states. They demonstrate the model's effectiveness on the bAbI question-answering tasks, where it achieves 95% accuracy on 19 out of 20 tasks (with direct reference), and on rule discovery tasks including cellular automata and Turing machines, where the model successfully learns the underlying transformation rules without explicit textual input.

The approach is compelling as it successfully bridges continuous learning with symbolic representations, maintaining differentiability while allowing the graph structure to evolve dynamically. This represents a significant advancement over previous approaches like Memory Networks and Neural Turing Machines, which maintain fixed internal structures. The GGT-NN model's ability to represent problem state as a graph with labeled nodes and edges provides interpretability advantages, as the internal state can be visualized and potentially used in downstream applications.

Decision: Reject.

Key reasons for rejection: 
1) The model's training requires full graph supervision at each timestep, which is only feasible for specific tasks like bAbI but not generalizable to real-world problems where the correct graph structure is unknown.
2) The paper's content and depth would be better suited for a journal publication rather than a conference, making it difficult to fully evaluate within the standard conference format.

Supporting arguments:
The paper's most significant limitation is the reliance on full graph supervision during training. While the authors acknowledge this limitation and provide a reasonable explanation for why it's necessary for their tasks, this approach fundamentally restricts the model's applicability to domains where graph structures can be manually provided. For the bAbI tasks, the authors use the knowledge object (which is part of the task generation process) as the ground-truth graph, but this is not a realistic scenario for most practical applications. The model cannot learn to construct meaningful graphs from scratch without this supervision, which significantly limits its real-world utility.

The paper's density and technical depth would benefit from a more thorough journal-style presentation. The current conference format makes it challenging to follow the complex graph transformation mechanisms and their implementation details. The paper would be much stronger if it included a more detailed comparison with related work, especially regarding the trade-offs between interpretability and computational efficiency. The current discussion of related work is somewhat superficial and doesn't adequately address the limitations of the approach.

Additional feedback:
The paper would benefit from a more thorough discussion of the limitations and potential future directions for reducing the need for graph supervision. For instance, exploring semi-supervised learning approaches where the model learns from a small set of fully annotated examples alongside a larger set of unannotated examples could be valuable.

The model's quadratic time complexity with respect to graph size is a significant drawback that should be emphasized more prominently. While the authors mention this limitation in the conclusion, it's a critical factor for practical applications and should be addressed in more detail. The paper would be stronger if it included experiments showing the model's scalability with respect to graph size.

The description of the graph format for bAbI tasks (Appendix C) is valuable, but the paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the graph structure was derived from the task specifications. Some of the modifications to the original bAbI tasks (e.g., adding history nodes) are described but not fully justified, which makes it difficult to assess whether the model's success is due to the architecture or the task modifications.

Questions for authors:
1) How would you modify the training procedure to reduce reliance on full graph supervision? What experiments have you conducted with partial graph supervision?
2) Could you provide more detailed analysis of the computational complexity of the GGT-NN model, particularly regarding how the quadratic complexity scales with graph size in practice?
3) The paper demonstrates success on rule discovery tasks, but how would the model perform on more complex rule discovery tasks where the graph structure is not predefined? Could you provide an example of such a task and how the model would need to be adapted?
