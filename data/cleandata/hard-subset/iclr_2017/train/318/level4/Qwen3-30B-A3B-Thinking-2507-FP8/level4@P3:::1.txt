

# Review for "Learning Graphical State Transitions"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that enables learning graph-structured intermediate representations. The core contribution is a set of differentiable graph transformations that allow neural networks to construct and modify graphs based on textual input, rather than requiring fixed graph structures. The authors demonstrate how these transformations can be combined to create a model that successfully solves the bAbI question-answering tasks (achieving 95%+ accuracy on 19/20 tasks), learns the rules of a 1D cellular automaton (Rule 30), and models arbitrary Turing machines. The paper makes a significant contribution by extending the previous GGS-NN framework to allow dynamic graph construction, which enables more flexible representation of structured data and provides a natural intermediate representation for complex reasoning tasks.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance**. The paper presents a well-motivated extension to graph neural networks with strong empirical results across multiple challenging tasks. The key reason for acceptance is that the authors demonstrate the framework's effectiveness on the bAbI tasks with results matching or exceeding state-of-the-art approaches, while the Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks showcase the model's ability to learn complex graph-based dynamics.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the value of differentiable graph transformations. The approach is well-placed in the literature, with careful comparison to related work including GNNs, GGS-NNs, memory networks, and DNCs. The authors clearly articulate how their model differs from prior work by allowing dynamic graph construction rather than requiring fixed graph structures.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous. The bAbI task results (Table 2) show the GGT-NN model with direct reference outperforms several state-of-the-art methods on multiple tasks, particularly those that naturally benefit from graph representations (e.g., pathfinding in task 19). The Rule 30 and Turing machine tasks demonstrate the model's ability to learn complex transformation rules from raw textual input, with the Rule 30 task achieving 98.3% accuracy on the test set (Table 3). The authors provide appropriate ablation studies (e.g., comparing models with and without direct reference), which show that the direct reference mechanism significantly improves performance and sample efficiency.

The theoretical foundation is sound, with clear definitions of the differentiable graph transformations (Section 3) and careful implementation details (Appendix B). The model's ability to learn graphical representations of structured data without requiring preprocessed graph inputs represents a meaningful advancement over previous approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper would benefit from clarifying the relationship between the graph structure and the textual input in the bAbI tasks. While the authors mention that node types are derived from entity names in the stories, it would be helpful to provide a concrete example showing how a specific sentence maps to the graph structure. This would make it clearer how the direct reference mechanism functions in practice.

The discussion of the Rule 30 task could be strengthened by including a visual example showing the model's predicted graphs compared to the ground truth. While the paper mentions that the model "successfully learned the assigned tasks" (Table 3), a visual comparison would provide stronger evidence of the model's ability to learn the underlying rule structure.

The paper could also explore the computational limitations of the approach more thoroughly. While the authors acknowledge the quadratic complexity (Section 6), it would be valuable to include experiments showing how the model scales with graph size, perhaps comparing training time and memory usage with standard GNN approaches on larger graphs.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the bAbI tasks, how does the model handle cases where a node type name appears as part of another word (e.g., "green" as both a color and part of "greenhouse")? The paper mentions modifying entity names to single words, but it would be helpful to understand the specific handling strategy.

2. For the Rule 30 task, the paper states that the model achieved 98.3% accuracy on the test set (Table 3). Could the authors provide more detail on how the model generalizes to longer sequences (20 and 30 timesteps)? The paper mentions "perfect output in the majority of extended tasks," but it would be valuable to see the exact numbers.

3. The paper notes that the GGT-NN model with direct reference outperforms the model without it. Could the authors share insights on why direct reference is so beneficial, and whether this could be incorporated into other graph-based architectures?
