

**Review**

**Summary of Contributions**  
The paper introduces the Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN), an innovative extension of Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) that enables learning to construct and modify graph-structured internal representations. This model extends previous work by allowing the graph structure itself to be dynamically learned and transformed during processing, rather than relying on fixed graph inputs. The authors propose five differentiable graph transformations (node addition, node state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation) that can be composed to process graphs in complex ways. The GGT-NN is applied to two primary domains: solving the bAbI question-answering tasks and discovering rules for cellular automata and Turing machines. The results demonstrate that the model achieves strong performance on bAbI tasks (reaching 95% accuracy on 19/20 tasks with direct reference), outperforming many existing approaches on specific tasks like pathfinding (task 19), and successfully learning the underlying rules for graph-based tasks without explicit rule specification.

**Decision**  
Accept. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of neural networks for structured data by introducing a model that can dynamically construct and modify graph representations. The core idea is well-motivated, addressing a clear gap in the literature where previous graph neural networks required fixed graph structures as input.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper's key strength lies in its ability to maintain a graph-structured internal state that evolves as the model processes input, providing a more flexible and interpretable representation than previous approaches. This is particularly evident in the bAbI task results, where the model with direct reference achieves 100% accuracy on 11 tasks and 95%+ on 19/20 tasks, outperforming several strong baselines. The model's performance on task 19 (pathfinding) is especially compelling, as it demonstrates how a graph-structured representation naturally aligns with the problem structure, unlike previous approaches that required preprocessed graph inputs. The paper also provides convincing evidence for the model's ability to learn complex rules for cellular automata and Turing machines, showing both high accuracy and good generalization to longer sequences.

The paper is well-positioned in the literature, with thorough comparisons to related work (GNNs, GGS-NNs, Memory Networks, DNCs, etc.) that clearly articulate the model's advantages. The authors successfully demonstrate that the GGT-NN's explicit graph representation offers interpretability benefits over implicit representations used in other architectures, allowing the graph structure to be visualized and potentially used in downstream applications.

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper further, consider the following points:

1. The training procedure relies on full graph supervision at each timestep, which is a limitation for real-world applications where such supervision would be unavailable. While the paper acknowledges this limitation and suggests future work, it would be helpful to include a brief discussion of how the model might be adapted to work with less supervision (e.g., using self-supervised techniques or incorporating the model's own predictions to guide training).

2. The paper's density could be improved for a conference format. Consider moving some of the detailed mathematical descriptions to an appendix or providing more intuitive explanations of the graph transformations. A brief example showing how the graph evolves during processing would help readers understand the model's operation.

3. The connection between the graph transformations and the specific tasks could be made clearer. For instance, it would be valuable to see how the different transformations are utilized for specific tasks (e.g., how node addition is used in the bAbI tasks versus the cellular automaton).

**Clarifying Questions**  
1. The paper mentions that the model with direct reference outperforms the model without it on 14 tasks. Could the authors provide more insight into why direct reference is so beneficial? Specifically, how does it affect the model's ability to learn the relationships between entities in the bAbI tasks?

2. The paper states that the GGT-NN model can generalize to larger inputs (e.g., 20 and 30 timesteps for the cellular automaton). What specific architectural or training modifications were required to achieve this generalization, and how might these affect the model's performance on even larger graphs?

3. The paper mentions that the model's time and space complexity scales quadratically with input size. Are there any theoretical or practical bounds on the size of graphs the model can handle effectively, and what are the practical implications for real-world applications?
