This paper proposes a method for dynamically representing a dialogue as a graph, which serves as the model's memory. The approach is demonstrated on the bAbI dialogue tasks. While the graph structure is learned as part of the inference process, the model also learns long-term parameters for graph transformations and for encoding input sentences.

This appears to be the first implementation of a differentiable memory structured as a graph. The proposed model is significantly more complex than previous approaches, such as memory networks, yet it does not demonstrate a significant performance improvement on the bAbI tasks. However, the work is still in a preliminary stage, and the concept of representing memory as a graph seems significantly more powerful than simpler structures like a stack.

Clarity was a major issue in the initial submission, which was difficult to parse despite its formal precision. However, the authors have provided a substantially improved revision that is much more readable. Overall, this is an original, thought-provoking, and (to the best of my understanding) technically accurate paper that is worthy of publication.

The preliminary results do not yet conclusively show whether this highly complex, graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capacity compared to other approaches. Performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to that of the best memory networks, though it still falls short of results achieved by more traditional rule-induction methods (see [...]).
