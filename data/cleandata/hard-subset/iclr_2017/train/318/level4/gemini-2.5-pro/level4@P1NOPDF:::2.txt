This paper proposes a novel method for representing a dialog as a graph that is constructed dynamically during inference and serves as the system's memory. The approach is first demonstrated on the bAbI tasks. While the graph structure is determined at inference time, the parameters that guide graph transformations and encode input sentences are learned via a long-term training process.

To my knowledge, this paper presents the first implementation of a differentiable, graph-based memory. The proposed model is considerably more complex than existing approaches like memory networks and does not yet show a significant performance gain on the bAbI tasks. However, the work is still preliminary. The core idea of representing memory as a graph is compelling and seems potentially much more powerful than simpler structures, such as a stack.

While the clarity of the initial submission was a major issue, the authors have provided a vastly improved and much more readable revision. Overall, this is an original, technically sound, and thought-provoking paper that merits publication.

The preliminary results do not yet establish whether this highly complex, graph-based differentiable memory offers superior learning or generalization capacity compared to alternative approaches.
