This paper introduces a novel approach that dynamically learns a graph-based representation of a dialog, which serves as the model's memory. This method is first evaluated on the bAbI tasks. While the graph itself is constructed during inference, long-term representation learning is employed to acquire parameters for graph transformations and for encoding sentences. This work appears to be the first to implement a differentiable memory as a graph. Although the proposed architecture is significantly more complex than prior approaches, such as memory networks, without yielding substantial performance gains on the bAbI tasks, this is very early-stage research. The potential for representing memory as a graph seems considerably more powerful than using a stack-based structure. While the initial version of the paper suffered from major clarity issues, the author has provided a vastly improved revision. This is an original, technically sound (based on my comprehension), and thought-provoking paper that merits publication.

Based on the initial experiments, it remains an open question whether the increased complexity of this graph-based differentiable memory translates to superior learning or generalization capabilities compared to alternative methods.
