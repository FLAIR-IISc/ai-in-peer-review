This paper presents a novel extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, endowing the model with the capability to perform complex graph transformations. The core idea is to develop a method that can dynamically build and modify an internal graph structure as a means of solving a problem, applied here to question-answering tasks. The authors introduce five distinct, differentiable transformation operations that are learned from a training set, typically via a supervised approach where the complete graph state is provided at each timestep. A specific instance of the model is detailed, which takes a sequence as input and iteratively updates an internal graph to produce a final prediction, achieving noteworthy results on QA tasks such as bAbI.

The approach detailed in this work is compelling. Its primary strength lies in maintaining a complex graph as its internal state representation while remaining fully differentiable, thus enabling straightforward learning through gradient-descent optimization. It stands as a successful effort to merge continuous and symbolic representations. Moreover, the method seems more general than recent attempts to incorporate symbolic structures into differentiable models (such as Memory Networks or NTMs), as the state's structure is not fixed and can dynamically evolve.

My main reservations, however, relate to the training paradigm. Requiring the complete state of the graph at each timestep is a significant limitation, as this level of supervision is feasible only for particular tasks (like bAbI) and is not a scalable solution for more complex problems. My other concern is with the overall density and scope of the paper. The content might be better suited for a journal format rather than a conference, as its current density makes the article difficult to read and fully appreciate.
