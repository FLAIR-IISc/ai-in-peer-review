- Introduces Gated Graph Transformer Neural Network (GGT-NN) that constructs and updates an explicit graph state from sequential input using five differentiable graph transformations.  
- Core transformations: node addition, state update, edge update, propagation, and aggregation as modular building blocks.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical performance: solves 19/20 bAbI tasks and outperforms MemNN on several tasks, especially those suited to graph structure.  
- Shows generality beyond language by learning to simulate a 1D cellular automaton and a family of Turing machines.  
- Architec­tural novelty and ambition: learns dynamic, explicit graph manipulations, bridging symbolic reasoning and neural models.  
- Modularity is a strength: transformations can be composed differently for different tasks.  
- Experimental comparisons are fair and support claims, with clear statement about supervision levels across models.  
- Major weakness: requires strong, step-by-step graph supervision during training, limiting applicability to real-world tasks without such annotations.  
- Minor concern: paper is dense with many new concepts and equations, making the main text hard to parse on first read.  
- Suggestion: move a simplified example of a key transformation (e.g., edge update TC) into the main text to improve clarity.  
- Suggestion: expand discussion on reducing supervision (e.g., semi-supervised or RL approaches) in the main body.  
- Question: Have you tried pretraining with graph supervision then fine-tuning end-to-end with only answer supervision to relax supervision?  
- Question: Provide practical context for quadratic time/space scaling — typical node counts on bAbI and training runtime/hardware.  
- Question: How much of the bAbI graph-construction was handcrafted versus automated, and how sensitive is performance to graph-design choices?  
- Question: Is the direct reference mechanism simple string matching, and could it be replaced with a learned soft-attention mechanism for flexibility?
