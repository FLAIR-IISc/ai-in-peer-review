This paper proposes a novel method that learns to represent a dialog as a graph structure on the fly, with the graph itself serving as the system's memory. The approach is demonstrated on the bAbI dialog tasks. While the graph structure is learned dynamically as part of the inference process, the model also learns long-term parameters that govern graph transformations and the encoding of input sentences.

To my knowledge, this is the first implementation of a differentiable memory structured as a graph. The proposed model is considerably more complex than prior approaches, such as memory networks, yet it does not demonstrate a significant performance improvement on the bAbI tasks at this stage. However, the work is still preliminary, and the representation of memory as a graph is conceptually more powerful than simpler structures like a stack, suggesting significant potential. Clarity was a major issue in the initial submission, which was difficult for a human reader to parse. However, the author has since provided a vastly improved revision. Overall, this is an original, technically sound, and thought-provoking paper that is worthy of publication.

The preliminary results are not yet sufficient to determine whether this highly complex, graph-based differentiable memory offers greater learning or generalization capacity compared to existing approaches. Its performance on the bAbI tasks is comparable to that of state-of-the-art memory networks, but it does not yet surpass more traditional rule induction methods.
