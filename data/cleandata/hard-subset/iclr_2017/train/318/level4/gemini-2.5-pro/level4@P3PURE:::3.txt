This paper proposes an extension to the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network (GG-SNN), equipping the model with the ability to perform complex graph transformations. The underlying idea is to develop a method that can build and modify a graph structure as an internal representation for problem-solving. The authors apply this approach specifically to question-answering tasks, introducing five distinct, differentiable transformations that are learned from a training set. This learning is typically supervised, with the ground-truth graph state provided at each timestep. The paper presents a specific instantiation of the model that processes a sequence input and iteratively updates an internal graph to arrive at a final prediction, achieving interesting results on QA tasks such as bAbI.

The approach is compelling because the proposed model can maintain a representation of its current state as a complex graph while remaining fully differentiable and thus trainable with gradient-descent techniques. It represents a successful attempt to integrate continuous and symbolic representations. Furthermore, the method appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate symbolic-like components into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTMs), since the shape of the graph-based state is not fixed and can evolve dynamically.

Despite these strengths, I have two main concerns. First, the model's training requires providing the ground-truth state of the graph at each timestep. This level of supervision is feasible only for particular tasks (e.g., bAbI) and is unlikely to be a scalable solution for more complex problems where such detailed supervision is unavailable. My second concern is that the paper's content is very dense, which makes the article difficult to read. The scope and density might be better suited for a journal format than a conference paper.
