This paper proposes an extension of the Gated Graph Sequence Neural Network, endowing the model with the ability to perform complex graph transformations. The core idea is to develop a method that can build and modify a graph-structured internal representation to solve a given problem. The authors apply this approach specifically to question-answering tasks. They propose five distinct, differentiable graph transformations that are learned from a training set, typically using full supervision where the ground-truth graph state is provided at each timestep. A specific instantiation of this model is presented that takes a sequence as input, iteratively updates an internal graph state, and produces a final prediction. This implementation is applied to QA tasks (e.g., bAbI) and achieves interesting results.

The proposed approach is compelling because the model can maintain its state as a complex, dynamic graph while remaining fully differentiable, allowing it to be trained end-to-end using standard gradient-descent techniques. It represents a successful attempt to integrate continuous and symbolic representations. Moreover, the proposed framework appears more general than recent efforts to incorporate symbolic-like components into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, NTMs), as the structure of its internal state is not fixed and can evolve dynamically.

My primary concern regards the training methodology. The model requires full supervision of the graph state at each timestep, a form of supervision that is only available for certain tasks (like the bAbI dataset). This approach is unlikely to be a viable solution for more complex problems where such detailed, intermediate supervision is unavailable.

A secondary concern is the paper's presentation. The breadth and density of the content might be better suited for a journal publication than a conference paper. This density makes the paper challenging to read and fully digest.
