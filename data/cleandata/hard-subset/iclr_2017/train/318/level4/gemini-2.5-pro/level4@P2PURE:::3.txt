This paper proposes an extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GGS-NNs) by enabling the model to perform complex graph transformations. The core idea is to develop a method that can build and modify an internal graph structure as a means of solving a problem, with a specific focus on question-answering. The authors introduce five different differentiable graph transformations that are learned in a supervised fashion, requiring the ground-truth graph state to be provided at each timestep during training. An instance of this model is presented which takes a sequence as input, iteratively updates an internal graph state, and generates a final prediction, achieving interesting results on QA tasks like the bAbI dataset.

The approach presented in this paper is highly compelling. The model successfully maintains a complex graph as its internal state while remaining fully differentiable and thus amenable to training with gradient-descent techniques. It can be viewed as a successful integration of continuous and symbolic representations. Moreover, the method appears more general than recent models that incorporate symbolic-like structures (e.g., Memory Networks, NTMs), as the shape of the internal state is dynamic and can evolve, rather than being fixed.

My primary concern, however, lies with the training methodology. Requiring the complete graph state at each timestep is a strong form of supervision that is only available for specific tasks like bAbI and is not a scalable solution for more complex problems where such intermediate supervision is absent. My other concern relates to the paper's presentation; its density makes it difficult to read and suggests the content might be better suited for a journal format rather than a conference proceeding.
