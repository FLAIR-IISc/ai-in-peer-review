This paper introduces an extension to Gated Graph Sequence Neural Networks (GG-SNNs) by equipping the model with the ability to perform dynamic and complex graph transformations. The central idea is to learn a model that can build and modify a graph-structured internal state to solve complex problems, with a specific focus on question answering. The authors propose five distinct, differentiable graph transformations that can be learned from data, typically through a supervised approach where the ground-truth state of the graph is provided at each timestep. The paper demonstrates an instance of this model that iteratively updates its internal graph based on a sequence input to produce a final prediction. This approach is applied to Question Answering tasks, such as the bAbI dataset, where it achieves promising results.

The core contribution of this paper is highly interesting and significant. The model's ability to maintain a complex, dynamic graph as its internal state, while remaining fully differentiable and trainable via standard gradient-based methods, is a notable achievement. This work represents a successful step towards bridging the gap between continuous and symbolic representations in deep learning. Furthermore, the approach appears more general than other recent efforts to incorporate symbolic-like structures into differentiable models (e.g., Memory Networks, Neural Turing Machines). Unlike these models, which often rely on a fixed-size memory or state structure, the proposed method allows the state representation itself to evolve in shape and complexity, which is a powerful generalization.

Despite these strengths, I have two main concerns.

My primary concern lies with the training methodology. The model is trained with full supervision, requiring the ground-truth graph state to be provided at every timestep. While this is feasible for certain synthetic tasks like the bAbI dataset, it is a significant limitation. This reliance on dense, step-by-step supervision makes it difficult to see how the method could be applied to more complex, real-world problems where such intermediate ground-truth states are unavailable. The paper would be stronger if it discussed potential avenues for relaxing this strong supervision requirement, perhaps through reinforcement learning or other weaker supervision signals.

A secondary concern relates to the paper's presentation and density. The manuscript is packed with a substantial amount of conceptual and technical information. While comprehensive, this density makes the paper challenging to parse and fully digest within the typical constraints of a conference paper review period. The content might be better suited for a longer, journal-style publication where the ideas could be elaborated upon more gradually and with greater clarity for the reader.
