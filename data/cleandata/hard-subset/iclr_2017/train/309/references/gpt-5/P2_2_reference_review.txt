Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based RL agent augmented with auxiliary tasks designed to improve the learned representation and thereby accelerate and stabilize policy learning. Two classes of auxiliary tasks are introduced: (1) auxiliary control tasks trained off-policy via n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards, notably “pixel control” (maximizing local pixel-change in a spatial grid) and “feature control” (maximizing specific network unit activations); and (2) auxiliary reward-focused tasks, chiefly skewed immediate reward prediction from short observation stacks, plus value-function replay. These tasks are optimized from a small replay buffer, decoupled from on-policy actor-critic updates, with shared convolutional and recurrent encoders to encourage representation sharing. Empirically, on 13 Labyrinth levels, UNREAL more than doubles A3C’s mean human-normalized performance (54% to 87%), yields large data-efficiency gains (≈10× fewer environment steps to reach A3C’s best score), and is more robust to hyperparameter choices. On 57 Atari games, UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean/median human-normalized performance and exhibits improved robustness. Ablations show pixel control > input-change prediction > reconstruction, with feature control also beneficial.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, well-motivated idea of leveraging off-policy auxiliary control and reward prediction to shape shared representations without biasing policy/value targets, placed appropriately within the literature.
- Convincing empirical evidence across two challenging domains with thorough ablations and robustness analyses demonstrating substantial data-efficiency and performance gains.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses slow/unstable learning under sparse and complex visual rewards. Using auxiliary tasks to densify learning signals for representation learning is well motivated, with clear separation of concerns: skewed reward prediction is not used to directly shape policy/value targets, mitigating bias; control tasks are trained off-policy purely to enrich features.
- Relation to prior work: The work is carefully situated relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model/auxiliary prediction literature. The novelty lies in combining off-policy auxiliary control (pixel/feature control) and skewed immediate reward prediction with on-policy recurrent actor-critic, showing these can be trained jointly and scalably to benefit representation learning in rich 3D/Atari environments.
- Methodological clarity and rigor: The joint loss is well defined, the architectural choices (shared CNN+LSTM, deconvolutional Q-head using dueling parameterization) are documented, and replay is used consistently to drive off-policy updates for auxiliary tasks and value replay. Importantly, the authors discuss why skewed sampling is appropriate for reward prediction (since it does not directly update policy/value) to alleviate reward sparsity.
- Empirical support: Results span 13 varied 3D tasks and the 57-game Atari suite. The paper includes:
  - Substantial improvements in final scores and learning speed on Labyrinth; human-normalized performance and clear gains in hyperparameter robustness.
  - Cross-method comparisons on Atari with both best-of-top-k curves and robustness plots across 50 hyperparameter configurations per game.
  - Ablations isolating the benefits of pixel control vs. simpler prediction/reconstruction baselines, and preliminary evidence for feature control.
  - Implementation details sufficient to reproduce core choices (loss weights, replay size, task frequency, action repeats, network sizes).
These collectively support claims about data efficiency, robustness, and state-of-the-art performance relative to contemporaries.

Caveats and questions that temper enthusiasm (but do not outweigh strengths):
- Fairness of compute/sample comparison: UNREAL performs multiple auxiliary updates per environment step using replay, whereas A3C baseline does not. The speed/data-efficiency claims are reported in environment steps, but auxiliary gradients may confer extra compute per step. Clarifying wall-clock vs. sample efficiency and matching update budgets across baselines would strengthen the claims.
- Ablation breadth: While informative, ablations could more systematically quantify the marginal contribution of each component (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction, value replay) across the full Labyrinth/Atari sets, including combinations (e.g., feature control + reward prediction), and provide confidence intervals across seeds.
- Generality/sensitivity: Pixel control requires tuning λPC differently for Atari vs. Labyrinth due to pixel-scale differences, suggesting sensitivity to reward scaling. More analysis of sensitivity to grid size, replay buffer size, and task frequency would help.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Report both environment frames and wall-clock time for learning curves, and normalize auxiliary update counts across methods to definitively establish data vs. compute efficiency.
- Provide per-game ablation summaries on Atari (even as aggregates) to understand which tasks help where (e.g., sparse vs. dense reward games).
- Include confidence intervals (or interquartile ranges) for final performance for each method at its best hyperparameters across multiple seeds.
- Expand on feature control: show results when combined with pixel control and reward prediction; analyze what units are being controlled and whether this aligns with semantically meaningful features.
- Analyze interference/compatibility between on-policy actor-critic and off-policy auxiliary Q-learning: e.g., gradient cosine similarities, or ablate shared vs. separate LSTM to understand where sharing helps/hurts.
- Clarify evaluation protocol for human-normalized scores (e.g., number of evaluation episodes, treatment of stochasticity, life termination handling on Atari).

Questions for the authors
- How many auxiliary optimization steps per environment frame are performed, and how does this compare to the A3C baseline’s update count? Can you provide wall-clock comparisons?
- How sensitive is performance to λPC, λRP, λVR, replay buffer size (2k), and the pixel grid resolution? Any guidance for default settings across new domains?
- Does reward prediction help in dense-reward or visually simple games, or can it be detrimental? Do you adapt the class balance (P(r ≠ 0)=0.5) per domain?
- Have you evaluated an A3C baseline augmented with value replay only (without other auxiliaries) to isolate the effect of replay on value learning?
- For feature control, which layer/units were most effective to control? Did combining feature and pixel control yield additive gains in broader experiments?
- Do the auxiliary control policies ever induce harmful representation bias (e.g., overemphasis on motion) that impairs tasks requiring fine static cues? How is this mitigated?
