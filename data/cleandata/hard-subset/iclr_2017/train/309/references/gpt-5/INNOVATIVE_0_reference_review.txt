Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks designed to shape the representation and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: off-policy Q-learning heads trained to maximize pseudo-rewards defined on perceptual change (pixel control) and internal network features (feature control). A deconvolutional dueling head predicts per-cell action-values for pixel/feature changes.
- Auxiliary reward prediction: a feedforward head that predicts immediate reward from a short stack of recent observations, trained from a replay buffer rebalanced to oversample rare rewarding events, thereby reducing perceptual sparsity without directly biasing the policy/value losses.
- Replay for efficiency: use a small recent replay buffer to train auxiliary control (off-policy) and perform value function replay, while the primary policy is trained on-policy with A3C.
Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves final performance, learning speed, and hyperparameter robustness on DeepMind Lab (13 levels) and on 57 Atari games, with ablations showing that each auxiliary component helps.

Decision: Accept
Reason(s): The paper introduces a novel and bold formulation of control-based auxiliary tasks used purely for representation learning, and shows large, consistent gains in data efficiency and robustness across diverse domains. The approach is simple, broadly applicable, and points to a promising direction for unsupervised/auxiliary learning in RL; minor methodological imperfections do not detract from the conceptual contribution and strong empirical results.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem and motivation: The paper targets the data inefficiency and fragility of deep RL, especially under sparse rewards and partial observability. Rather than learning world models or pure reconstruction losses, the authors propose learning to control pseudo-rewards (pixel/feature change) as a way to induce rich, behaviorally-relevant representations. This is well motivated and well positioned relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and auxiliary prediction work (depth, internal features, etc.). The distinctive stance—using pseudo-reward policies not for behavior but solely to shape representation—feels fresh and practically appealing.

2) Novelty and design: Several novel elements reinforce each other:
- Pixel/feature control as auxiliary Q-learning heads, exploiting spatial structure via a deconv-dueling head to simultaneously predict per-cell Q-values. This turns a very large set of auxiliary tasks into a tractable shared computation.
- Reward prediction trained on a skewed replay distribution to counter reward sparsity, decoupled from policy/value losses to avoid biasing control.
- A principled combination of on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary Q-learning and value replay, with shared recurrent and convolutional features.
This is a clean, general template for auxiliary task design in RL.

3) Empirical support: Results on Labyrinth show >2x human-normalized improvement over A3C, large speedups in data efficiency (10x median fewer frames to reach A3C’s best), and improved hyperparameter robustness. Ablations indicate that each auxiliary component contributes; pixel control outperforms simple change prediction and reconstruction (which can hurt final scores), and feature control is competitive with pixel control on a tested level. On Atari, UNREAL (and a variant without pixel control) outperform A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN on mean/median human-normalized scores and are more robust over hyperparameter sweeps. While some reporting choices could be tighter, the breadth of evidence and cross-domain consistency support the claims.

4) Rigor: The methodology is sound, with careful architectural descriptions and training details, and ablations relevant to the central claims. Some statistical/reporting aspects (error bars, standardized budgets) could be improved, but the overall empirical case is strong.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify evaluation protocol comparability on Atari: total training frames per method, action repeat, evaluation setting (e.g., no-op starts, episode caps), and whether results are averaged over fixed budgets across agents.
- Provide statistical confidence (means ± SE) for key aggregate metrics and per-game Labyrinth/Atari summaries, not just top-3 or best hyperparameters.
- Expand ablations: (a) remove reward rebalancing to quantify its impact; (b) vary replay buffer size; (c) analyze λPC, λRP, λVR sensitivity; (d) compare pixel control grid sizes and normalization choices; (e) report performance on games with minimal pixel change to test limits.
- Stability/optimization: Discuss any training instabilities from mixing on-policy policy gradients and off-policy Q-learning (with RNN state); detail target network updates, gradient scaling/clipping, and any importance sampling corrections (if none, justify).
- Broaden auxiliary baselines if feasible: e.g., depth prediction, inverse dynamics, next-state feature prediction; discuss how UNREAL differs conceptually from curiosity/intrinsic-motivation lines (even if contemporary).
- Generalization/transfer: Since auxiliary tasks aim to shape reusable representations, a transfer experiment (e.g., new goal locations or new rewards with fixed features) would further substantiate the representation-learning claim.
- Minor: Clean up reference formatting and ensure consistency in notation (e.g., clearly define buffer size units: frames vs steps vs sequences).

Questions for the authors
- Off-policy details: Do you apply any off-policy corrections (IS ratios) for auxiliary Q-learning/value replay? What is the target network update cadence (θ−)? Any observed instability from mixing losses?
- Replay specifics: The buffer holds the most recent “2k observations” — is that 2k frames or 2k transitions? Why such a small buffer, and how sensitive are results to this size?
- Reward rebalancing: How critical is the 50/50 zero/non-zero sampling for reward prediction? What happens without rebalancing or with different ratios?
- Scaling and overhead: What is the wall-clock overhead of auxiliary heads and replay relative to A3C? How many auxiliary updates per environment step?
- Pixel control normalization: How do you normalize pixel-change rewards across games with different intensity dynamics? Would alternatives (e.g., optical flow, feature-space change) work as well or better?
- RNN state for off-policy: When replaying for Q-learning/value replay, how is recurrent state handled? Do you reconstruct hidden states from replayed sequences, and how long are those sequences?
- Negative cases: Are there environments where pixel/feature control hurts (e.g., visually noisy but reward-irrelevant dynamics)? Any mitigation strategies?
- Transfer: Can a UNREAL-trained representation accelerate learning on new reward functions within the same environment (UVFA-style transfer)? If attempted, please share results.
