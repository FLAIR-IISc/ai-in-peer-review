1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an architecture that augments an on-policy, recurrent A3C agent with off-policy auxiliary tasks optimized from recent experience replay to learn better representations and improve data efficiency. The auxiliary tasks include: (a) auxiliary control via Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel control, and preliminarily feature control) using a deconvolutional head; (b) reward prediction of immediate reward from short context with class-rebalancing to oversample rare rewards; and (c) value function replay to accelerate and stabilize value learning. The combined loss jointly trains shared encoders (CNN and LSTM) while keeping the main policy/value learning on-policy. Experiments on 13 Labyrinth 3D levels and 57 Atari games show large gains in final performance, learning speed, and robustness across hyperparameters. Ablations indicate each auxiliary component helps, with pixel control particularly strong on Labyrinth, and UNREAL outperforming A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN on Atari.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated approach that unifies auxiliary control and reward-focused auxiliary prediction with off-policy replay to shape representations for on-policy RL; (2) Strong and broad empirical evidence with ablations demonstrating substantial improvements in performance, data efficiency, and robustness across two challenging domains.

3) Supporting arguments
- What is the specific problem? The paper targets sample inefficiency and brittle representation learning in deep RL, especially under sparse rewards and partial observability. It asks how to use auxiliary tasks and recent replay to shape representations that accelerate and stabilize the primary policy/value learning.

- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper situates its contributions relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model-based prediction. Unlike options/UVFA work that exploits learned pseudo-reward policies directly, UNREAL uses pseudo-rewards solely to learn representations shared with the base task. Compared to earlier auxiliary prediction in 3D RL (e.g., predicting depth or emulator features), UNREAL focuses on control-based auxiliary tasks optimized off-policy and a reward-focused predictor with rebalanced replay. The relation to prioritized replay is noted, and the architectural choices (deconv heads, dueling parametrization) are grounded in prior value-based work.

- Do the results support the claims with rigor? Largely yes. On Labyrinth, UNREAL more than doubles A3C’s human-normalized mean (54%→87%) and achieves ~10× improvement in steps to reach A3C’s best score, with per-level breakdowns and ablations (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay). Additional comparisons show pixel control > predicting input change > pixel reconstruction, supporting the control-vs-prediction argument. On Atari-57, UNREAL (and UNREAL without pixel control) show strong mean/median human-normalized improvements over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN and improved robustness across hyperparameters. The use of many random hyperparameters/seeds and robustness plots partially addresses overfitting concerns.

That said, several aspects could be more rigorous:
- Statistical reporting is limited: confidence intervals and explicit significance tests are largely absent; the selection of “top-3” hyperparameter runs for some plots can bias comparisons.
- Atari evaluation protocol details (training frames, evaluation episodes, no-op starts, life-loss terminal, sticky actions) are not fully specified here; clarity is important for comparability with prior work.
- The computational overhead (extra heads, replay, deconv) and wall-clock training comparisons are not quantified.
- Feature control is only preliminarily evaluated; its broader contribution remains less clear than pixel control and reward prediction.

Technical soundness: The off-policy auxiliary Q-learning heads train from replay with no importance sampling; since they are auxiliary and do not directly control behavior, this is acceptable and empirically effective, though it can bias shared features. The reward prediction uses class-rebalanced replay; because the predictor is not used for returns/baselines, this avoids biasing policy gradients while shaping features—a sensible design. The architecture and loss composition are clearly described and reproducible, with key hyperparameters and replay buffer details provided.

Originality and significance: While auxiliary tasks and replay are not new, the specific combination—optimizing control-based pixel/feature pseudo-rewards off-policy alongside reward-focused prediction and value replay, integrated with recurrent A3C—feels novel and practically impactful. The improvements on Labyrinth and Atari-57 are substantial and likely to influence subsequent work on representation learning in RL.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify evaluation protocols for Atari and Labyrinth (training budget in frames, eval episodes, action repeat, preprocessing, life-loss terminal, no-op or human starts, sticky actions). Report median over seeds and include CIs for key aggregate metrics.
- Reduce reliance on “top-3” hyperparameter selection in headline plots; emphasize area-under-curve and full-distribution summaries across seeds/hyperparameters to avoid selection bias.
- Quantify computational overhead (parameters, FLOPs, wall-clock time) and report speedups both in samples and wall-clock.
- Provide a deeper ablation on Atari: contributions of each auxiliary task per game subset; analyze when pixel control helps/hurts.
- Explore generalization/transfer: does the learned representation help on new reward functions or modified visuals (beyond the Labyrinth variants)?
- Discuss limitations: environments with low pixel change but reward-relevant signals; potential conflicts between off-policy auxiliary optimization and on-policy policy gradients; sensitivity to λPC and replay buffer size.
- Consider comparisons to other representation-learning signals (e.g., CPC/contrastive, forward/inverse dynamics) and to successor features as auxiliary tasks.

Questions for the authors
- Atari protocol: What were the exact training frames per game, evaluation episode count, life-loss terminal handling, no-op starts, and whether sticky actions were used? How were human-normalized scores computed?
- Hyperparameters: How were λVR, λPC, λRP chosen per domain? Are they fixed across games/levels or tuned per-task? How sensitive are results to these weights?
- Robustness: Beyond the provided plots, can you report mean/median ± CI over seeds for the final scores and sample efficiency gains? Are improvements statistically significant across games?
- Compute: What is the wall-clock overhead of the auxiliary heads and replay relative to baseline A3C (per environment step and to reach target score)?
- Pixel vs feature control: On Atari, how often does pixel control help versus hurt? Can feature control replace pixel control without loss, and what layer choices matter?
- Replay buffer: Why 2k steps? Did larger buffers or prioritized replay (TD-error) change outcomes?
- Off-policy effects: Did you observe destabilization from off-policy auxiliary updates on shared encoders? Any mitigation (target networks, slower auxiliary learning rates) evaluated?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly presented, and empirically strong paper whose practical impact justifies acceptance.
