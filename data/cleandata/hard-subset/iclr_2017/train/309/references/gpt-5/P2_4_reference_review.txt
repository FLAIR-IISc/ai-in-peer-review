1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with off-policy auxiliary tasks to learn more effective representations and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key ideas are: (i) auxiliary control tasks that maximize pseudo-rewards defined over pixel-change or network-feature activations, optimized via n-step Q-learning from replay; (ii) an auxiliary reward prediction task trained on a replay buffer skewed toward rewarding events to densify reward-relevant representation learning without directly biasing the policy/value updates; and (iii) value function replay to further stabilize/improve value estimation. The authors provide a combined loss and a practical architecture leveraging deconvolutional heads for spatial control. Empirically, UNREAL yields large gains on 13 challenging 3D Labyrinth levels (2x human-normalized performance over A3C; ~10x fewer steps to match A3C’s best) and SOTA results across 57 Atari games, along with ablations (pixel control vs reconstruction vs change prediction; preliminary feature control).

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements in both performance and data efficiency across diverse domains, with meaningful ablations and robustness analyses.
- A well-motivated and well-placed contribution: using auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction to shape representations addresses a central bottleneck in deep RL (sparse rewards, partial observability) and is integrated cleanly into A3C with principled off-policy learning.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper tackles the challenge of learning rich, reward-sensitive representations in sparse-reward, visually complex, partially observed environments. The approach—to add auxiliary, efficiently trainable prediction/control tasks sharing representation—directly targets this bottleneck. The distinction from prior temporal abstraction work (options, Horde) and UVFA/SR is clear: auxiliary tasks are not used to directly control the environment nor to transfer policies, but to shape shared features. The relation to model-based/auxiliary prediction work (e.g., depth prediction) is made explicit; the novelty lies in control-based auxiliary tasks with off-policy Q-learning and reward-focused replay.
- Methodological soundness: The combined on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (Q-learning) training is described clearly, including the loss decomposition, replay usage, and architecture for spatial control (deconv head) and reward prediction (short stack of CNN features). The reward-skewed replay targets a clear desideratum (densify reward-related features) while keeping the policy/value learning on-policy to avoid bias. Using value-function replay to perform value iteration over recent experience is sensible and well integrated.
- Empirical support: Results on Labyrinth are compelling: large gains in both final performance and sample efficiency; robustness to hyperparameters; per-component ablations indicating that learning to control pixel changes outperforms predicting changes and reconstruction (and that reconstruction can even hurt final performance). Atari results also show substantial gains vs strong baselines (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN), with improved robustness. The paper reports multiple seeds/hyperparameter sweeps and presents performance aggregated across settings; this improves confidence in the findings.
- Limitations/concerns:
  - Evaluation reporting details could be tighter for fair cross-paper comparability on Atari (e.g., sticky actions, lives-as-episodes, eval protocol, frame budgets). Some plots focus on “top-3” agents; while the paper also shows robustness over all runs, explicit confidence intervals and fixed-sample-budget comparisons would strengthen claims of data efficiency.
  - The Labyrinth environment is in-house; although diverse and challenging, public availability or cross-benchmark comparisons (e.g., VizDoom) would aid reproducibility.
  - Feature control is only preliminarily evaluated; a fuller study would clarify when pixel vs feature control is preferable and whether they combine synergistically.
  - Off-policy Q-learning with RNN state requires care; more detail on how hidden state is reconstructed for replayed sequences would help assess stability/correctness.

Overall, despite these caveats, the methodological contribution and empirical gains are significant and well substantiated.

4. Suggestions for improvement (to help the authors)
- Evaluation details and fairness:
  - Specify exact training budgets (environment frames) for Atari/Labyrinth; report absolute frames-to-threshold to quantify the “10x” speedup.
  - Clarify Atari evaluation protocol (sticky actions, no-op starts, number of evaluation episodes, treatment of life loss as terminal, frame-skip/action repeat) to ensure comparability with prior work.
  - Report mean/median with confidence intervals over all seeds at fixed budgets; de-emphasize “top-k” curves to avoid selection bias.
- Methodological clarity:
  - Describe how LSTM hidden states are handled for replayed off-policy updates (sequence length, episode boundary handling, target networks, replay ratios).
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for λPC, λVR, λRP and replay buffer size; include per-domain recommended settings.
  - Expand feature control experiments (vary layer depth/width, combine with pixel control) and analyze when each auxiliary is most beneficial.
  - Analyze potential trade-offs from reward-skewed replay in dense-reward games; include an ablation with unskewed sampling.
- Analysis and ablations:
  - Visualize/control-policy behaviors learned by pixel/feature control heads; assess whether they aid exploration even if never executed.
  - Include an ablation without the deconvolutional spatial head to verify the importance of spatially grounded control learning.
- Reproducibility: Provide pseudo-code and, if possible, open-source code or training logs.

Questions for the authors
- How exactly do you reconstruct LSTM states for replayed off-policy Q-learning and value replay? Do you re-unroll over 20-step sequences, and how are episode boundaries treated?
- What are the exact training frame budgets (Atari and Labyrinth)? How many frames are required, on average, for UNREAL to reach A3C’s best performance?
- What is the Atari evaluation protocol (sticky actions, life termination, number of eval episodes, frame skip)? Are human-normalized scores directly comparable to prior reports you cite?
- How are λPC, λVR, λRP chosen per domain/game? How sensitive are results to these weights and to the replay buffer size?
- Does reward-skewed replay degrade performance in dense-reward tasks? Have you tried adaptive skewing?
- Did you observe any stability issues with the off-policy auxiliary heads (e.g., divergence)? Do you use target networks or double Q for auxiliary control?
- Can feature control match or exceed pixel control when applied to deeper layers or with different spatial resolutions? When would you recommend one over the other?
- What is the wall-clock overhead of the auxiliary heads, and net wall-clock speedup to a given performance threshold?
