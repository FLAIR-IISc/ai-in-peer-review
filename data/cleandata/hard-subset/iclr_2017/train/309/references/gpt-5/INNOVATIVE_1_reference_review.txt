Summary
The paper proposes UNREAL, an RL agent that augments a strong on-policy baseline (A3C with an LSTM) with off-policy auxiliary tasks to shape representations and accelerate learning. The key ideas are: (1) auxiliary control via Q-learning on pseudo-rewards, notably pixel control (maximize local pixel-change in a spatial grid) and feature control (maximize hidden-unit activations); (2) auxiliary reward prediction that rebalances replay to oversample rare reward events, thereby mitigating perceptual sparsity without biasing the policy/value loss; and (3) replayed value regression to further stabilize/improve value estimates. A deconvolutional head exploits spatial structure for pixel/feature control. Across 13 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, UNREAL substantially improves both final performance and data efficiency/robustness over A3C, surpassing contemporary state-of-the-art baselines. Ablations indicate pixel control > input-change prediction > reconstruction; preliminary feature-control results are promising.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: The paper introduces a bold and influential direction—using off-policy auxiliary control tasks from a single stream of experience to learn better representations—and demonstrates strong, broad empirical gains in complex visual RL. The concept is novel, well-motivated, and impactful even if some validations (e.g., feature-control breadth, statistical reporting) are incomplete.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and conceptual contribution: The framing of many off-policy auxiliary control tasks as representation-shaping objectives, trained jointly with an on-policy recurrent agent, is a clear step beyond prior work. Unlike Horde or UVFA/successor representations, the pseudo-reward policies are not deployed for control but for learning state representations; this unifies auxiliary control, reward-focused prediction, and replayed value updates in a single, practical agent.
- Methodological design: The pixel-control deconv head is a clever way to align spatial saliency with control; reward prediction with rebalanced replay directly addresses reward sparsity without biasing the policy/value update; and replayed value regression leverages fresh features to perform value iteration-like improvements. The off-policy n-step Q-learning heads allow efficient learning of many pseudo-rewards from the same experience stream.
- Breadth and strength of results: On Labyrinth, UNREAL more than doubles human-normalized mean performance vs A3C and achieves up to 10–18× data efficiency improvements. On 57 Atari games, it surpasses A3C and prioritized dueling DQN in mean/median normalized scores and is more robust across hyperparameters. The ablations are informative: control tasks beat pure prediction/reconstruction, aligning with the paper’s hypothesis about learning controllable structure.
- Placement in literature: The related work is comprehensive and positions the contribution relative to options, UVFAs, successor representations, model-based prediction, and recent auxiliary prediction in 3D RL.

Concerns and limitations (non-blocking)
- Evaluation protocol clarity: Atari/Labyrinth evaluation details (training frames, wall-clock, evaluation episodes, treatment of hyperparameter selection across runs) could be more standardized and directly comparable to prior work; reported “top-3” curves can inflate perceived gains.
- Statistical rigor: While robustness plots are provided, aggregate statistics (mean±std over fixed seeds/hyperparams) for final scores per domain would strengthen claims of stability and significance.
- Ablations breadth: Feature control is only preliminarily evaluated on one level; more systematic comparison to pixel control and combined effects would be valuable.
- Potential bias via skewed replay: Although the reward predictor does not directly enter the policy/value loss, shared convolutional features could be biased by the rebalanced data; a small analysis of whether this ever hurts tasks with minimal/reversed motion would help.
- Off-policy stability: Multiple off-policy heads trained from a small replay buffer (2k steps) co-adapt with an on-policy LSTM; reporting stability tricks/sensitivities (e.g., target networks, λPC schedules) would help reproducibility.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not decision-critical)
- Provide standardized evaluation metrics: training frames, evaluation protocol (episodes, no-ops, action repeat), and wall-clock time/compute overhead for auxiliary heads.
- Report mean±std over fixed seeds for a fixed hyperparameter setting, alongside robustness sweeps, to separate seed vs hyperparameter variability.
- Expand feature control experiments and analyze complementarity with pixel control.
- Conduct sensitivity to replay buffer size/composition and auxiliary loss weights (λPC, λRP, λVR); report failure modes.
- Add analyses of representation: e.g., probing tasks, saliency maps, or transfer to novel pseudo-rewards.
- Compare to additional auxiliary prediction baselines (e.g., depth, optical flow, forward/inverse dynamics), especially in 3D.
- Discuss possible negative transfer (e.g., tasks where minimizing motion is optimal) and show that auxiliary control does not degrade performance.

Questions for the authors
- Evaluation details: How many training frames were used on Labyrinth and Atari? What is the evaluation protocol (episodes, life-loss handling, no-ops, sticky actions if any)? How were human-normalized scores computed for Labyrinth (number of human testers, variance)?
- Hyperparameter selection: For Atari, results show top-3 per method; can you provide aggregate statistics over all seeds for a fixed hyperparam, and clarify whether hyperparams were tuned per game or globally?
- Compute and efficiency: What is the wall-clock overhead of auxiliary heads and replayed updates? How many auxiliary updates per environment step?
- Stability: What target network settings are used for the Q-heads? Any observed instabilities with off-policy updates sharing the LSTM? Sensitivity to λPC and replay buffer size (2k)?
- Pixel control reward: How is the normalization handled across games with different intensity dynamics? Any cases where pixel control hurt performance?
- Feature control: Which layer/units are used, and why? Do you see complementary gains when combining pixel and feature control?
- Future direction: Can the learned auxiliary policies be repurposed as options for planning/exploration? Any preliminary evidence of transfer to new reward functions or tasks?
