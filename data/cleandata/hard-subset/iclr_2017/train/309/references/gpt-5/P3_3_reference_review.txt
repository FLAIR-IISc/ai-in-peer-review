Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of an A3C agent with multiple auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The key components are: (i) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards, notably pixel control (maximize per-cell pixel change) and feature control (maximize hidden unit activations); (ii) reward prediction trained on rebalanced replay (equal mass on zero vs non-zero rewards) to reduce perceptual sparsity; and (iii) value function replay to further shape the critic. These are combined in a single loss, with a shared CNN-LSTM backbone for the base policy/value and deconvolutional heads for spatial auxiliary Q-values. Empirically, UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C on 13 Labyrinth levels (higher final scores, up to ~10x data-efficiency gains) and improves mean/median performance and hyperparameter robustness on 57 Atari games versus A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. The paper provides ablations (e.g., reconstruction vs change prediction vs control; with/without pixel control), implementation details, and robustness analyses across many hyperparameters and seeds.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons: (1) Simple, well-motivated auxiliary tasks that significantly improve sample efficiency and robustness in challenging 3D environments and Atari, with careful ablations; (2) A clear, scalable recipe that blends on-policy actor-critic with off-policy replayed auxiliary updates, likely to be impactful and broadly adopted.

Supporting arguments
Novelty and relation to prior work:
- The idea of auxiliary predictions/controls is well situated relative to options, UVFAs, successor representations, and Horde/GVFs. The distinctive aspects here are (a) using many off-policy control heads purely as auxiliary objectives for representation learning rather than for hierarchical control; (b) reward-prediction with rebalanced replay to break sparsity without biasing the policy/critic targets; (c) integrating these with a recurrent on-policy actor-critic in a unified, practical framework.
- Pixel control is a simple yet effective intrinsic control objective tailored to visual domains; the deconv, spatial dueling head is a smart architectural choice leveraging spatial structure.

Methodological strengths:
- The combined loss and training protocol are clearly described, with separation of on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (auxiliary) updates and a small replay buffer for recency.
- Ablations carefully probe alternatives: reconstruction vs change prediction vs control, with evidence that control is the key ingredient; the feature-control auxiliary is an interesting alternative that performs competitively.
- Robustness analyses across many hyperparameters and seeds, and replay-based prioritization only for reward prediction (reducing bias risk for the main policy/critic) are thoughtful design choices.

Empirical support:
- Labyrinth results show strong gains in both final performance and data efficiency across diverse tasks (navigation, procedurally generated mazes, laser tag), with per-level breakdowns and ablations.
- Atari results show improved mean/median human-normalized scores and robustness.
- The rebalanced reward prediction and value replay appear to materially accelerate learning in sparse-reward settings.

Concerns and suggestions
- Fairness and comparability on Atari: A3C is used with an LSTM and RGB inputs here, which differs from common feedforward grayscale setups in prior A3C/DQN reports. While you compare to your A3C baseline under the same architecture, comparisons to prior literature (e.g., Prioritized Dueling DQN) may not be apples-to-apples. Please clarify the evaluation protocol, whether numbers for baselines were reproduced under the same preprocessing/architecture, and include A3C without LSTM as an additional reference.
- Off-policy value replay: You perform value regression from replay without importance sampling, which in general evaluates V under a behavior policy different from the current policy. While this may work empirically and your buffer is short, some discussion of the potential bias introduced and why it did not destabilize training would help. An ablation toggling value replay (you include it in the final loss) is helpful; reporting its incremental benefit across tasks would be useful.
- Auxiliary non-stationarity: Feature-control defines rewards as functions of the agent’s own hidden activations, which change as learning progresses, making the pseudo-reward non-stationary. Please comment on stability and whether you used target networks or slower updates for those heads to mitigate drift; what schedule or θ− is used for auxiliary Q-targets?
- Pixel control design choices: The pseudo-reward is absolute pixel change over 4x4 cells. This can emphasize camera motion or texture flicker. While it’s only an auxiliary target, it could bias the representation. The ablation with “input change prediction” helps, but adding an analysis of learned features (e.g., sensitivity to ego-motion vs object motion) and performance in visually flickering environments would strengthen the claim. Also clarify any normalization per-game (you mention λPC ranges differ).
- Compute vs data-efficiency: UNREAL performs multiple auxiliary updates per environment step. It would help to report wall-clock gains and the ratio of gradient updates to environment steps, to separate data-efficiency from additional compute.
- Reproducibility: Labyrinth is described as an internal platform. To enable replication, please release environment configurations or a public equivalent, or provide seeds/checkpoints and code for the auxiliary heads. Also, for Atari, provide per-game scores with confidence intervals and the full hyperparameter grid tried.

Questions for the authors
1) Value replay details: How exactly are n-step returns computed on replay for V? Do you use the current V(st+n+1) as bootstrap, any target network, and do you ever apply importance sampling corrections?
2) Auxiliary Q-learning stability: What target network schedule do you use for the auxiliary Q-heads, and how sensitive are results to target update frequency and replay buffer size (2k)? Any divergence issues observed?
3) Pixel control reward: Did you experiment with optical flow or motion segmentation-based pseudo-rewards, or normalizing pixel-change by global motion (to discount camera pans)? How do results vary with grid size?
4) Reward prediction bias leakage: Although the reward predictor’s loss is decoupled from the policy loss, features are shared with the value/policy via the CNN. Did rebalancing induce any measurable bias in the value estimates? Any experiments with a separate encoder to isolate this?
5) Compute budget: What is the overhead (in wall-clock and FLOPs) relative to the A3C baseline per environment frame? Does UNREAL still dominate at equal compute?
6) Atari protocol: Did you use sticky actions, no-ops, or life-loss terminal handling? Are results averaged over multiple random starts? Please align with ALE best practices for comparability.
7) Transfer: Given the representational aim, did you test zero-shot or few-shot transfer to new reward functions or related tasks (e.g., different object types in Labyrinth) using the learned shared encoder?

Minor feedback to improve the paper
- Clarify the exact weighting schedule and update frequency for each auxiliary loss; consider a sensitivity plot over λVR, λPC, λRP.
- Report per-task contributions to gains (e.g., add ablation bars per Labyrinth level).
- Include confidence intervals (or interquartile ranges) for reported human-normalized scores and speedups.
- Tighten related work around GVFs/predictive knowledge (e.g., clarify differences from Horde’s use for representation learning) and cite contemporary intrinsic-motivation approaches where relevant.
- Some references have formatting issues; please fix.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, practical, and empirically strong contribution that advances deep RL training practice.
