1) Summary and claimed contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks that are trained off-policy from a short replay buffer to shape the representation and accelerate learning. The auxiliary tasks are: (i) auxiliary control via Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (notably “pixel control,” and also “feature control”), (ii) reward prediction with class-rebalanced sampling to handle sparse rewards, and (iii) value function replay. A single combined loss optimizes the on-policy A3C objective together with off-policy auxiliary losses. Architecturally, auxiliary control uses a deconvolutional head producing spatial Q-values. Empirically, the paper reports substantial gains in both sample efficiency and final performance on 13 diverse 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, and improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations show control > change-prediction > reconstruction, and that combining tasks yields further gains. Implementation details are provided.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence across many tasks that auxiliary control and reward-focused auxiliary prediction substantially improve data efficiency, robustness, and final performance.
- Method is simple, well-motivated, and clearly positioned with respect to prior work (Horde, UVFA, SR, model learning, auxiliary predictions), while introducing a practical and novel combination: off-policy pseudo-control purely for representation learning plus rebalanced immediate reward prediction.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses slow, brittle learning and poor representation shaping in sparse-reward, visually rich RL. Using auxiliary tasks to densify learning signals and exploit replay is well-motivated and distinct from using options or UVFAs to execute temporally abstract policies: here, auxiliary policies are not used at inference time and solely shape features.
- Placement in literature: Related work is fairly covered. The paper contrasts learning many pseudo-rewards (Horde) without shared representation to the proposed shared feature learning; differentiates from UVFAs and SR (transfer across goals) and from environment model learning; and situates recent auxiliary prediction work in 3D RL. The positioning that these auxiliary tasks are used for representation, not decision-time control, is clear and novel.
- Technical soundness: The combined loss is transparent; the off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control is appropriate; reward prediction uses deliberate class-rebalancing but does not bias the base policy/value because it trains only a shared encoder; value replay augments value estimation. The sampling, update cadence, and architectural details are specified.
- Empirical rigor:
  - Breadth: 13 Labyrinth levels spanning fruit collection, static/random goal navigation, procedurally generated mazes, and laser-tag; plus 57 Atari games. This breadth supports robustness claims.
  - Strength of results: Large gains in both data efficiency (up to ~10x) and final human-normalized scores (e.g., from ~54% to ~87% in Labyrinth; 880% mean and 250% median on Atari relative to baselines). Demonstrated hyperparameter robustness (learning rate, entropy cost) with many random configurations.
  - Ablations: Comparisons to reconstruction and change-prediction baselines support the claim that learning to control perceptual changes yields better representations than predicting or reconstructing inputs. Feature control shows promise. The reward prediction and value replay components are motivated and their roles discussed.
  - Reproducibility: Many implementation and training details are provided (network, losses, replay size/frequency, action repeats, task weights, sampling ranges).

Caveats (not decision-critical):
- While auxiliary control’s benefit is clear, the relative contributions of value function replay vs. control vs. reward prediction to final policy performance could be dissected more comprehensively across the full suite (some ablations are provided, but not all combinations on all tasks).
- Fairness of comparisons on Atari (training budget, evaluation protocol specifics) could be documented more explicitly.
- No formal analysis of off-policy bias or gradient interference; however, empirical stability is demonstrated.

4) Suggestions to improve (for authors; not all affect my decision)
- Provide a fuller ablation across Labyrinth and Atari isolating LVR (value replay) from LPC (control) and LRP (reward prediction), ideally with matched update budgets, to quantify each component’s contribution to both speed and asymptotic performance.
- Clarify the Atari evaluation protocol: training frames, reward clipping, life-loss termination, sticky actions, test-time evaluation episodes, and whether results are under identical budgets to baselines.
- Report compute/training-time overhead from auxiliary heads and replay, to contextualize data-efficiency gains with wall-clock performance.
- Analyze sensitivity to replay buffer size and auxiliary update ratios; include results for buffer sizes beyond 2k.
- Expand feature control results beyond a single level and examine whether combining pixel and feature control yields additive gains.
- Provide statistical confidence intervals or significance tests over seeds for key benchmarks.
- Consider measuring representational changes (e.g., linear probe accuracy for reward prediction, gradient cosine similarity between tasks) to support the representation-learning hypothesis.
- Discuss potential failure modes (e.g., low-visual-change environments) and how λPC is tuned; relate improvements to game-specific pixel-change statistics.

Questions for the authors
- Could you quantify the separate effects of LVR (value replay) vs. LPC (aux control) vs. LRP (reward prediction) on both speed and asymptotic performance across a broader subset of tasks (e.g., more Labyrinth levels and a representative Atari subset)?
- What exact Atari evaluation protocol was used (frames of training, reward clipping, life termination, sticky actions, test-time episodes)? Are training budgets matched to A3C and Dueling DQN?
- Do you apply any off-policy corrections (e.g., importance sampling) for value replay or auxiliary control? If not, did you observe bias or instability, and how was it mitigated?
- How sensitive are results to the replay buffer size (2k) and the frequency of auxiliary updates? Is there a trade-off between auxiliary update ratio and on-policy stability?
- How were λPC and other loss weights selected per domain? Are there heuristics or normalization strategies that make UNREAL “set-and-forget” across new environments?
- For feature control, which layers/units were most beneficial? Does controlling deeper vs. shallower features change outcomes?
- On games with minimal pixel change or where change is uninformative, does pixel control help or hurt? Can the agent learn to down-weight unhelpful auxiliary tasks automatically?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, clearly presented, and empirically strong paper. It makes a practical contribution likely to impact how we train deep RL agents for data efficiency and robustness. I recommend acceptance.
