Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of A3C that adds auxiliary tasks to shape the representation and improve learning speed and robustness. The key ideas are: (1) auxiliary control tasks trained off-policy via n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards defined either as pixel-change per grid cell (pixel control) or network feature activations (feature control); (2) a reward prediction task that predicts immediate reward with a deliberately rebalanced sampling strategy to over-represent rare rewards; and (3) value function replay to perform additional value regression from recent experience. All auxiliary tasks share the convolutional encoder (and LSTM for control), are trained from a small replay buffer, and are combined with on-policy A3C updates into a single loss.
- Empirically, UNREAL achieves large gains in data efficiency and final performance on 13 3D Labyrinth tasks and across 57 Atari games. Reported mean human-normalized performance on Labyrinth rises from 54% to 87% (median 45% to 92%), with up to 10–18× reduction in steps to match A3C’s best performance. On Atari, UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean and median human-normalized scores while being more robust to hyperparameters. Ablations show pixel control > input-change prediction > reconstruction; feature control is promising and close to pixel control on one level.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, well-motivated idea—using off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction to shape representations for on-policy RL—clearly placed in the literature and distinct from prior UVFA/SR/Horde/model-based approaches. (2) Compelling empirical evidence across diverse domains with careful ablations and robustness analyses demonstrating substantial gains in both sample efficiency and final performance.

Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? The paper addresses slow and brittle representation learning in deep RL, especially under sparse rewards and partial observability. It seeks to reduce perceptual sparsity and improve sample efficiency by adding auxiliary objectives that are related to environment dynamics and reward, without biasing the base policy updates.
- Motivation and placement in literature: The paper differentiates itself from options/pseudo-reward controllers by not using auxiliary policies at execution time; from Horde by sharing a representation for many pseudo-rewards; from UVFA/SR by not parametrizing a family of value functions per se but using auxiliary task learning to improve features for the primary task; and from model-based/representation-prediction work by showing efficacy in rich 3D visuals. This positioning is accurate and timely, and the design choices (off-policy Q-learning for many pseudo-rewards, small replay buffer, reward rebalancing only for the auxiliary predictor) are well motivated to avoid policy bias while still shaping features.
- Do results support claims?
  - Breadth: 13 Labyrinth tasks spanning fruit collection, static and random-goal navigation, procedurally generated mazes, and laser-tag; plus 57 Atari games. This breadth supports generality.
  - Strength of gains: Large improvements in both data efficiency and final score are consistently reported, including human-normalized gains and robustness to hyperparameters (plots across grids of learning rate and entropy cost). The 10–18× speedups on Labyrinth are substantial.
  - Ablations: The comparisons among pixel control, input-change prediction, and reconstruction clarify that control-based auxiliary tasks are more beneficial than pure prediction or reconstruction, aligning with the paper’s hypothesis about focusing on behaviorally relevant features.
  - Methodological care: The auxiliary reward predictor is trained with rebalanced sampling but is not used in the policy/value pipeline, avoiding bias in control. Value-function replay leverages recent experience without skew. Auxiliary Q-learning is naturally off-policy, making replay appropriate.
  - Reproducibility: Implementation details for architectures, buffer sizes, update schedules, and hyperparameter ranges are provided, which improves credibility.
- Caveats and remaining questions about rigor:
  - Atari evaluation protocol details (e.g., no-op starts, evaluation episodes, frame skip) are not fully specified here; cross-paper comparisons (e.g., to Prioritized Dueling DQN) rely on consistent settings. Clarifying this is important.
  - Statistical reporting: While many hyperparameter seeds are run and aggregate plots are shown, per-configuration variance or confidence intervals for headline numbers are not provided. Still, the robustness plots partially address this.
  - Human baselines for Labyrinth: Method for obtaining human scores (number of participants, instructions, training time) should be detailed to contextualize human-normalized metrics.
  - Computational cost: UNREAL uses multiple auxiliary heads and replay; wall-clock comparisons and resource usage vs baselines are not reported.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not central to decision)
- Provide a concise algorithm box with update schedules (on-policy A3C vs off-policy tasks), buffer operations, and how often each loss is applied. This will aid reproducibility.
- Report wall-clock time and compute resources to reach key milestones compared to A3C and Dueling DQN.
- Detail Atari evaluation protocol (no-op starts, life loss termination, episode length caps) to ensure apples-to-apples comparisons.
- Expand ablations on Atari to isolate contributions of pixel control, feature control, reward prediction, and value replay, not only on Labyrinth.
- Add sensitivity analyses: pixel grid size for pixel control; replay buffer size (2k vs larger); reward rebalancing ratio for the prediction task; λ weights.
- Provide more systematic analysis of learned representations (e.g., linear probes for reward/goal localization) beyond videos.
- Clean up references formatting and minor typos.

Questions for the authors
- Atari evaluation: What exact protocol was used (no-op starts, frame skip/repeat, terminal on life loss, number of evaluation episodes, action set treatment)? How does it match prior baselines?
- Compute and efficiency: What are the wall-clock speedups and GPU/CPU usage relative to A3C and Dueling DQN? How sensitive are results to number of asynchronous threads?
- Reward prediction rebalancing: How sensitive is performance to the 50/50 non-zero vs zero sampling choice? Does miscalibration of the predictor matter?
- Replay buffer size: Why 2k transitions? How do results change with larger/smaller buffers or prioritization beyond the simple split?
- Auxiliary control design: How does grid resolution and crop choice for pixel control affect results? How does feature control compare to pixel control across more tasks, and what are best practices for selecting which layer/features to control?
- Interference and stability: Did you observe negative interference between Q-learning updates and on-policy A3C? Any techniques (e.g., separate optimizers, gradient normalization) used to mitigate this?
- Generalization/transfer: Can a representation trained with auxiliary tasks on one task distribution transfer to new pseudo-rewards or environments (e.g., different Labyrinth levels) without fine-tuning?
