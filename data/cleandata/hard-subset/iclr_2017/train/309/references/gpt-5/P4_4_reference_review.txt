1) Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of an A3C agent with auxiliary tasks that shape the shared representation to improve data efficiency, stability, and final performance. The key components are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: off-policy Q-learning heads trained to maximize pseudo-rewards, notably “pixel control” (change in grid-cell pixel intensities) and “feature control” (activating internal feature units).
- Auxiliary reward tasks: immediate reward prediction trained from rebalanced replay to alleviate reward sparsity without biasing the main policy/value estimates.
- Value function replay: additional value regression from replay to exploit shaped features.
These are combined with on-policy A3C in a single loss, using a small replay buffer of recent experience and deconvolutional architectures for spatial control heads. Empirically, UNREAL significantly improves over A3C on 13 3D Labyrinth tasks (≈2× higher human-normalized score; up to 10–18× data-efficiency) and on 57 Atari games (substantially higher mean/median human-normalized scores and robustness to hyperparameters). Ablations indicate control > prediction > reconstruction, and show benefit of both pixel and feature control.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence with broad coverage, clear ablations, and robustness analyses demonstrating substantial gains in both data efficiency and final performance.
- Well-motivated, well-placed contribution that integrates on-policy learning with off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction in a technically sound way.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled?
- How to improve representation learning in deep RL to increase data efficiency and robustness in visually rich, partially observed environments with sparse reward.

Is the approach well motivated and situated in the literature?
- The paper positions itself against options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations: instead of using pseudo-reward policies for behavior, it uses them purely to shape shared representations. It contrasts model-based auxiliary predictions and prior auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., depth) by introducing control-based auxiliary tasks (pixel/feature control) and immediate reward prediction trained with rebalanced replay. This framing is appropriate and distinguishes the work from prior art.

Technical soundness:
- The combined objective is a multi-task loss: on-policy A3C plus off-policy Q-learning for multiple pseudo-rewards, plus replayed value regression, plus skewed immediate reward prediction. The use of separate heads with shared encoders is standard for multi-task RL; target networks (θ−) and small replay buffers are used for stability in the off-policy components. The deconvolutional Q-value heads exploit spatial structure appropriately. The approach is heuristic but coherent, and aligns with common deep RL practices.
- The paper is explicit about replay usage, sampling schemes, and task weights, and provides sufficient implementation detail (architectures, buffer sizes, update frequencies, hyperparameter ranges) for reproducibility.

Do results support the claims?
- Labyrinth: substantial improvements in final scores and dramatic gains in data efficiency; ablations demonstrate that control-based auxiliaries outperform prediction/reconstruction; robustness to hyperparameters is quantified across random sweeps.
- Atari: improvements over strong baselines (A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN) in mean/median human-normalized scores, plus robustness gains. The breadth (57 games) strengthens the empirical case.
- The paper provides multiple lines of evidence (final performance, learning speed, robustness, ablations), which supports the central claims.

Novelty:
- Using off-policy auxiliary control tasks (pixel/feature control) purely for representation shaping within an on-policy recurrent agent, coupled with skewed immediate reward prediction and replayed value regression, is a novel and effective combination. Pixel control as a spatial deconvolutional Q-head for change-maximizing pseudo-rewards is a distinctive idea.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Evaluation protocol clarity (Atari): Please specify evaluation details (no-op starts or sticky actions, number of evaluation episodes, training vs evaluation frames, frame-skip, life-loss termination, and whether scores are best-of or averaged across seeds). This will aid comparability with prior work.
- Compute accounting: Report wall-clock training time and total gradient updates to reach specified score thresholds, and compare compute budgets with baselines. The method adds auxiliary updates; quantifying overhead would be helpful.
- Replay design: Analyze sensitivity to replay buffer size (2k), recency, and the ratio of auxiliary updates to on-policy updates. Consider reporting stability metrics (e.g., variance across seeds) as buffer size varies.
- Ablations/extensions:
  - Pixel control grid resolution and reward normalization across games (especially Atari, where motion statistics differ). A brief sensitivity curve would strengthen the design choice.
  - Feature control: provide broader results across Labyrinth/Atari and combinations with pixel control.
  - Auxiliary loss weights: show sensitivity to λPC, λVR, λRP beyond the reported ranges.
  - Target networks and n-step returns: clarify the update cadence of θ− for auxiliary heads and any effect on stability.
- Fairness of hyperparameter tuning: Ensure identical hyperparameter search budgets across methods and clarify whether “top-3” curves are used consistently for all methods and how selection is performed to avoid bias.
- Theory/insight: A brief discussion connecting control-based auxiliaries to representation shaping (e.g., controllability, empowerment, or mutual-information arguments) would add intuition.

Questions for the authors
- How many auxiliary/replay updates are performed per environment step? Does each transition receive multiple uses (and how many on average)? Please quantify additional computational cost versus A3C.
- What is the exact Atari evaluation protocol (sticky actions/no-op, evaluation episodes, life-loss handling, test-time exploration/noise)? How do results change under sticky actions if not already used?
- How sensitive are gains to the replay buffer size (2k) and the reward rebalancing ratio (0.5 non-zero vs zero)? Did you compare to prioritized replay for all auxiliary tasks?
- For the auxiliary Q-heads, do you use a distinct target network θ− and what is the update interval? Any stability issues observed with recurrent shared encoders under off-policy updates?
- How robust is pixel control on games with low motion (e.g., static screens) versus high-motion games? How is pixel-change reward normalized across games to prevent domination or vanishing?
- Can you provide broader results for feature control and combined feature+pixel control, and report whether the benefits are complementary?
- Do the auxiliary tasks improve exploration (e.g., faster goal discovery) or primarily speed representation learning once reward is found? Any analysis of visitation or value approximation error over training?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, novel in its combination of auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction for representation shaping, and provides strong empirical support across diverse domains.
