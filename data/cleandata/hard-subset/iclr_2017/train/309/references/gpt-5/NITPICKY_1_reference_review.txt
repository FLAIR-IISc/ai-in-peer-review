1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with three auxiliary mechanisms intended to improve representation learning and data efficiency: (i) auxiliary control via off-policy Q-learning for “pixel control” (and optionally “feature control”), (ii) an auxiliary reward prediction task trained on rebalanced replay to denoise reward sparsity, and (iii) value function replay to stabilize and accelerate value learning. The design tightly integrates on-policy A3C with off-policy replayed updates and a deconvolutional spatial head for pixel/feature control. Empirically, the paper reports substantial gains in data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance on 13 Labyrinth levels and across 57 Atari games, including mean/median human-normalized scores that exceed strong baselines. Overall, the paper advances the idea that carefully chosen auxiliary tasks can significantly benefit RL in visually rich, partially observed 3D settings.

2. Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
- Insufficiently rigorous and ambiguously reported experimental methodology (fairness of comparisons, evaluation protocol, selection across hyperparameters/seeds, missing training budgets), limiting confidence in the central empirical claims.
- Numerous technical and presentation issues (typos, incorrect/mismatched citations, missing hyperparameters and algorithmic details, unclear loss/optimization choices), which collectively reduce clarity, reproducibility, and confidence in correctness.

3. Supporting arguments
- Evaluation protocol and fairness:
  - The Atari comparison selects the “top-3” hyperparameter settings per method and shows sorted human-normalized scores; it is unclear whether competing baselines receive comparable hyperparameter sweeps or identical computational budgets. This risks selection bias in favor of UNREAL.
  - Training budgets (number of frames/updates) for Atari and Labyrinth are unspecified; human-normalization requires consistent evaluation settings (e.g., no-op starts, sticky actions) which are not documented. Without this, the reported 880% mean / 250% median is hard to interpret or reproduce.
  - Labyrinth is an in-house environment; it is unclear whether it is publicly available, how human baselines are collected, and how variability across procedural maps is controlled, which limits external verification.
- Reporting and methodological gaps:
  - The paper blends on-policy A3C and off-policy replay (value replay and Q-learning) but omits critical details: discount factor, target network update scheme, gradient scaling/normalization across losses, and how loss magnitudes are balanced across tasks to avoid destabilizing the shared LSTM/CNN.
  - The auxiliary pixel-control head applies a ReLU to Q-values; while pixel-difference rewards are nonnegative, such clipping can interact with bootstrapping and terminal handling—this should be justified and ablated.
  - Reward prediction uses rebalanced replay (50% non-zero) but lacks analysis of potential representation bias and its downstream effect on policy/value. There is no ablation isolating the contribution of each auxiliary task on Atari (only “without pixel control” is shown).
  - Value function replay is off-policy; the paper does not specify whether importance sampling or other corrections are used (it appears not), nor quantify the bias–variance tradeoff or stability implications.
  - Buffer size is extremely small (2k steps), which may limit diversity and induce recency bias; no sensitivity analysis is provided.
- Presentation/accuracy issues:
  - Multiple citation errors and inconsistencies (e.g., policy gradient section cites Watkins 1989; Konidaris and Barto incorrectly cited as Konidaris and Barreto; several entries have malformed years/titles and punctuation; “and tree, … search” in Silver et al.), which undermines scholarship.
  - Numerous typographical/formatting errors (e.g., section “.4 UNREAL AGENT,” “accorded to a policy,” “doconvolutional,” inconsistent notation, unrendered symbols). Equations and notation are sometimes inconsistent (e.g., switching between s′ and st+n+1).
  - Figures/tables referenced but not included in the submission; many critical claims depend on unavailable plots.
- Missing or limited ablations:
  - Limited analysis of the individual contribution of reward prediction and value replay on Atari. The Labyrinth ablation is partial and does not quantify compute overhead vs. data efficiency.
  - No wall-clock comparison; the added heads and replay increase computation, so “10× data efficiency” in steps may not translate to wall-clock speedup.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the work; not all are decision-critical)
- Clearly specify all training budgets (frames, updates, threads), evaluation protocols (no-op starts, sticky actions, termination conditions), and random seeds. Report mean ± std over seeds for fixed hyperparameters; avoid top-k selection reporting for headline claims.
- Provide ablations for each auxiliary component on Atari; include sensitivity to λ weights, buffer size, and reward rebalancing ratio. Consider normalizing auxiliary losses (e.g., by running std) to stabilize multi-task optimization.
- Justify design choices: ReLU on Qaux; choice of n-step lengths; absence of off-policy corrections for value replay; target network details for auxiliary Q.
- Report wall-clock training time and compute resources; quantify overhead of auxiliary heads and replay.
- Improve clarity of the pixel/feature control learning target: how are per-cell TD targets aggregated? Are cells independent tasks summed in the loss? How are terminal states and episode boundaries handled?
- Fix all citation and typographical issues; ensure references are correct and complete. Provide missing hyperparameters (gamma, target update cadence, optimizer settings beyond “shared RMSprop”).
- If possible, release code, trained models, and the Labyrinth environment or a close public proxy.

Questions for the authors
- What are the precise training budgets (environment frames) for Labyrinth and Atari, per method? What are evaluation protocols (no-op starts, sticky actions, life-loss terminals)?
- How are hyperparameter sweeps allocated across baselines (A3C, Dueling DQN)? Do all methods receive identical compute and sweep sizes? Are headline numbers averaged over seeds without selection?
- What are the discount factor, target network update details (τ or hard update frequency), and handling of terminals for auxiliary Q-learning and value replay?
- How are gradients from LA3C, LVR, LPC, and LRP scaled/normalized before summation? Any gradient clipping? Did you observe interference or collapse without normalization?
- Why apply ReLU to Qaux? Did you try unrestricted Q-values or alternative reward scalings?
- Can you provide ablations on Atari for reward prediction and value replay individually? How much do they contribute beyond pixel control?
- How sensitive are results to the replay buffer size (2k) and to the 50/50 reward rebalancing ratio? Any evidence of representation bias affecting policy/value?
- Is Labyrinth publicly available, and how were human scores obtained (number of participants, instructions, variance)?
