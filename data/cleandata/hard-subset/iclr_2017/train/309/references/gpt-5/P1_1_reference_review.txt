1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with multiple auxiliary tasks aimed at improving representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness in deep RL, especially under sparse rewards and partial observability. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-reward functions derived from “pixel control” (maximize local pixel changes on a spatial grid) and “feature control” (maximize activations of internal units), sharing convolutional and recurrent representations with the base policy.
- Auxiliary reward prediction: a skewed-replay trained classifier that predicts immediate reward (positive/zero/negative) from a short stack of past encoded observations, explicitly oversampling rewarding events to break perceptual sparsity without biasing the policy/value updates.
- Value replay: additional off-policy value regression from a short replay buffer to accelerate and stabilize value estimation.
- A unified loss combining on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary losses.
Empirically, UNREAL substantially outperforms a strong A3C baseline on 13 DeepMind Lab (Labyrinth) levels in both final human-normalized performance (54% → 87% mean; 45% → 92% median) and data efficiency (≈10× fewer environment steps to reach A3C’s best performance). On 57 Atari games, UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean and median human-normalized scores and exhibits improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations show cumulative benefits of the auxiliary tasks, with pixel control outperforming pixel reconstruction and input-change prediction, and preliminary evidence that feature control is also effective.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (a) strong, consistent empirical gains in challenging 3D and Atari domains with clear sample-efficiency improvements; (b) a well-motivated, modular approach that cleanly integrates auxiliary control and reward-prediction tasks with off-policy replay into a standard actor-critic; (c) thorough ablations that support the representation-learning value of the auxiliary tasks.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem significance and formulation: The paper tackles a central practical challenge—learning effective representations under sparse rewards and partial observability—by leveraging auxiliary tasks that are naturally available during interaction. The objective and architectural design are clearly described, with a principled joint loss and practical training protocol (on-policy A3C + off-policy replayed auxiliary updates).
- Relation to literature: The work is well situated relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model-based/auxiliary prediction methods. The distinction—using pseudo-reward policies exclusively to shape representation instead of executing them—is clear and novel within this context.
- Empirical rigor: Experiments span diverse Labyrinth tasks (navigation, exploration, laser-tag) and a large Atari suite. The paper examines robustness via hyperparameter sweeps, and demonstrates consistent advantage in both final performance and learning speed. The ablations (pixel control vs change prediction vs reconstruction; removal of components) support the central claim that learning to control (not just predict) meaningful auxiliary signals yields better representations. The reward-prediction skew via replay is a sensible mechanism to mitigate reward sparsity without biasing policy/value estimates.
- Practicality: The auxiliary heads and replay machinery are straightforward to implement and generalize across domains. The approach is compatible with standard CNN-LSTM RL architectures.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Fairness of compute and wall-clock reporting: While the sample-efficiency gains are compelling, please report wall-clock time and total parameter-update counts relative to the A3C baseline. UNREAL performs additional replay-based updates every 20 steps; clarifying compute budgets would strengthen claims about efficiency.
- On-policy vs off-policy considerations: The method mixes on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning and replayed value regression. While this is common, discussing potential non-stationarities/interference and any mitigations (e.g., target networks, replay buffer sizing, update ratios) would improve methodological clarity. You mention θ− for control; provide the target update schedule for all replayed losses.
- Reward-balanced replay: Oversampling rewarding events for reward prediction helps representation, but could alter the shared convolutional features that also feed the on-policy actor-critic. While you argue this does not bias the policy, a short analysis or ablation showing no degradation to on-policy estimates (e.g., with/without skew, or varying the skew factor) would be reassuring.
- Architectural choices: The ReLU applied to Qaux outputs is unusual for Q-values; justify this choice given non-negativity of the pixel-change reward and discuss any observed training effects. Similarly, describe the role of the dueling parameterization in the auxiliary head and whether it was critical.
- Atari protocol comparability: Since your architecture and preprocessing (RGB 84×84, action repeat 4) differ from some published Atari baselines, include clarifications to ensure fair comparison (e.g., frame stacking, life-loss handling, episode termination, no-ops). If possible, provide raw scores and human-normalized scoring details consistently with prior work.
- Analysis and diagnostics: Add more interpretability/results on how auxiliary tasks shape the representation (e.g., t-SNE or linear probes for reward-related features; ablate removing auxiliary heads mid-training to test reliance; generalization to novel levels or reward remapping).
- Feature control: The preliminary feature-control results look promising; a more systematic study (e.g., which layers/features help most; interaction with pixel control) would further support the broader “control-as-representation” thesis.

Questions for the authors
1) Compute fairness: How many gradient updates per environment frame does UNREAL perform versus A3C, and what are the wall-clock training times to reach reported performance on Labyrinth and Atari?
2) Target networks and stability: What is the update schedule for θ− in auxiliary Q-learning and replayed value regression? Did you observe instability without it?
3) Sensitivity to replay buffer size and skew: How does performance vary with buffer size (2k) and the reward-oversampling ratio (e.g., 0.5 vs alternatives)? Any negative side effects on policy/value learning?
4) Human-normalized scores: How were human baselines obtained on Labyrinth (number of testers, training, instructions)? Are the results robust across raters?
5) Auxiliary head design: Why apply ReLU to Qaux? Did allowing negative values harm performance? Is the dueling architecture important for auxiliary pixel control?
6) Ablations on Atari: Can you provide component-wise ablations (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) on a subset of Atari games to mirror the Labyrinth analysis?
7) Off-policy correction: Did you try importance sampling or other corrections for the replayed updates, and if so, did they help/harm?
8) Generalization: Have you tested UNREAL’s representation for transfer (e.g., new goals/reward functions or new Labyrinth layouts) without fine-tuning all components?

Overall, the paper presents a compelling and practical approach that yields strong empirical gains; addressing the above clarifications would further solidify the contribution.
