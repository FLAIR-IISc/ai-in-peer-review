Summary and contributions
The paper proposes augmenting a standard A3C agent with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The main contributions are: (i) auxiliary control tasks defined via pseudo-rewards, specifically pixel-control (maximizing local pixel-change in a spatial grid) and (preliminarily) feature-control (maximizing hidden unit activations); (ii) a reward prediction auxiliary task trained on rebalanced replay to counter reward sparsity; (iii) value function replay; and (iv) a combined UNREAL loss that jointly optimizes on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning and supervised auxiliary heads from a small replay buffer. Empirically, the approach improves learning speed and final performance on 13 Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, with human-normalized gains and hyperparameter robustness.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Evaluation rigor and clarity are insufficient for strong claims (lack of standardized ALE protocol details, selection over hyperparameters, unclear training budgets, incomplete ablations), and (2) multiple methodological ambiguities and inconsistencies (off-policy stability, replay/value targets, reward balancing, architecture choices) combined with numerous formatting/reference issues undermine confidence.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: Learning better representations via auxiliary tasks in RL is well motivated and positioned relative to options/UVFA/successor features and recent auxiliary prediction work. However, the claimed lack of bias from skewed replay for reward prediction is overstated—representation shaping can indirectly bias policy/value via shared encoders.

- Methodological clarity and correctness:
  - Off-policy learning: The auxiliary Q-learning runs off shared LSTM features trained on-policy by A3C. No importance sampling or off-policy correction is used; stability is attributed to replay but not justified. θ− target usage is mentioned but not specified (update schedule, scope across heads). The blending of on-policy actor-critic and off-policy Q-learning sharing recurrent weights risks interference; no analysis or diagnostics are provided.
  - Replay/value targets: Two different “LVR” terms are introduced (A3C’s value regression and a replayed value loss), yet the notation and targets are conflated. It’s unclear whether replayed value updates use target networks, how n is chosen, and how these losses interact with LA3C to avoid overfitting to recent replay.
  - Pixel control reward: Using absolute pixel differences in dynamic-visual environments (e.g., Labyrinth’s animated textures) can emphasize visually salient but task-irrelevant changes. The paper asserts benefits but does not quantify how much pixel-control policies pursue spurious changes vs task-relevant ones.
  - Feature control: Introduced but only “preliminary” results on one level are provided; yet UNREAL is described as combining “all three” auxiliary tasks. The main UNREAL results appear to rely on pixel-control + reward prediction + value replay; this mismatch reduces clarity.
  - Reward prediction: Rebalanced sampling to P(rτ≠0)=0.5 improves learning signals, but the class imbalance within non-zero rewards (positive vs negative) is not addressed (despite using a 3-class classifier). No weighting or calibration details are provided.
  - Architectural details: The Qaux head applies a final ReLU to Q-values; while pixel/feature rewards are non-negative, this is a non-standard constraint for Q-values and should be justified (e.g., bounds on returns). The deconvolutional head and dueling parametrization are described tersely with some inconsistencies (“doconvolutional” typo, value/advantage tensor shapes and combination not fully specified).
  - State definition: The state space S for auxiliary rewards is said to include the agent’s hidden activations; this departs from the environment’s MDP and is conceptually odd unless carefully formalized.

- Empirical evaluation and rigor:
  - Atari protocol: Critical details are missing—training frames per game, evaluation procedure (no-op starts vs sticky actions, number of evaluation episodes, episode termination, reward clipping, frame preprocessing). Claims of surpassing state-of-the-art are not meaningful without protocol parity. The reported “mean 880% and median 250%” human-normalized scores are unusually high; without standardization, potential evaluation bias (e.g., no random starts) cannot be ruled out.
  - Hyperparameter selection: Results often report averages of the “top-k” hyperparameter settings out of 45–50 randomly sampled configurations. This introduces selection bias and is not directly comparable to baselines from the literature that report fixed hyperparameters. The “robustness” analysis averages across all sampled hyperparameters but still lacks statistical significance reporting (confidence intervals across seeds).
  - Training budget fairness: No total environment steps per method are reported for Atari; Prioritized Dueling DQN comparison may be unfair if training budgets differ. For Labyrinth, human-normalization is used, but the normalization formula and human evaluation protocol are not specified.
  - Ablations: While some Labyrinth ablations are shown (reconstruction vs change prediction vs pixel control; limited feature control), Atari ablations for each auxiliary component are lacking. The contribution of each component (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) remains unclear per domain.

- Related work and citations: The literature review is mostly adequate, but several references are malformed or incomplete, and some are not clearly connected to the text. Notably, Watkins (1989) is incorrectly cited alongside policy gradient. The UNREAL acronym is never defined.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Clarify and standardize the Atari evaluation protocol (sticky actions, no-op starts, episode life, reward clipping, frames, evaluation episodes) and report absolute scores and training frames. Avoid “top-k hyperparameter” selection or clearly separate tuning and test sets.
- Provide per-component ablations on Atari and Labyrinth, including sensitivity to λPC, λVR, λRP, and buffer size, and learning curves with confidence intervals.
- Detail off-policy stabilization: target network schedules, n-step choice, gradient scales, and any normalization/clipping. Consider reporting auxiliary-head TD errors and their effect on shared features.
- Justify architectural choices (ReLU on Qaux, dueling application, deconvolution shapes) and publish code-level specs.
- Quantify how pixel control correlates with task reward and whether it pursues spurious dynamics (e.g., via intervention or correlation analysis).
- Fix numerous typos, section numbering (e.g., “.4 UNREAL AGENT”), and reference formatting; define the UNREAL acronym.

Questions for the authors
- What exact Atari evaluation protocol did you use (sticky actions, no-op starts, number of episodes, episode termination rule, reward clipping, training frames)? How do training budgets compare to baselines?
- How frequently are θ− target networks updated for the auxiliary Q-learning and replayed value losses? Is θ− shared across heads?
- What is the n used for n-step Q-learning in auxiliary control? Is it aligned with the 20-step unroll?
- How are gradients from auxiliary heads scaled relative to the actor-critic losses to prevent interference? Any gradient norm clipping?
- For reward prediction, how are positive vs negative rewards balanced within the non-zero class? Did you observe miscalibration due to rebalancing?
- Does the ReLU on Qaux ever hurt learning when returns could be near-zero early on? Any empirical comparison to an unconstrained head?
- Can you provide Atari ablations isolating the effects of pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay?
- How is human-normalized scoring computed in Labyrinth, and what is the human evaluation protocol (number of episodes, expertise, instructions)?
