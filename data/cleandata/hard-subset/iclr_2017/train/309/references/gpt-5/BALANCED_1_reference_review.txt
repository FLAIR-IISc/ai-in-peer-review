Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of an on-policy A3C agent with off-policy auxiliary tasks designed to shape the representation and improve data efficiency and robustness. The auxiliary tasks include (i) auxiliary control via Q-learning on pseudo-rewards, with “pixel control” and “feature control” heads that learn policies to induce perceptual or hidden-unit changes; (ii) reward prediction using skewed replay to over-sample rare rewarding transitions; and (iii) value function replay. The combined, multi-task loss shares a convolutional encoder and LSTM, while auxiliary heads use deconvolutional architectures that exploit spatial structure. Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves final performance and data efficiency on DeepMind Lab (13 levels) and Atari (57 games), with careful ablations showing each auxiliary component contributes, improved robustness to hyperparameters, and significant speedups in steps-to-target performance.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Clear, well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction for representation learning in deep RL, with novelty in using off-policy pseudo-reward control purely as auxiliary objectives. (2) Strong, broad empirical evidence with ablations demonstrating large, consistent gains in both performance and data efficiency across diverse domains.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses a central challenge in deep RL—learning effective representations under sparse and delayed rewards—by leveraging auxiliary objectives that densify learning signals without biasing the main policy. This is well-motivated by prior work (options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, model learning, and recent auxiliary prediction in 3D environments) while clearly differentiating UNREAL: pseudo-reward maximization is used only to shape features, not as options for control, and is combined with replayed, off-policy updates compatible with an on-policy recurrent agent.
- Approach: The design is coherent: (a) pixel/feature control tasks encourage sensitivity to controllable aspects of the environment; (b) reward prediction with balanced replay focuses the encoder on reward-relevant features; (c) value replay exploits newly learned features. The architectural choices (shared CNN/LSTM, deconv heads, and multi-task weighting) are practical and scalable.
- Empirical support: On Labyrinth, UNREAL more than doubles human-normalized median/mean scores over A3C, with dramatic 10×+ speedups in steps to reach A3C’s final performance and improved robustness over hyperparameters. The ablations are informative: pixel control outperforms reconstruction and change prediction, and feature control is competitive. On Atari, UNREAL surpasses A3C and prioritized dueling DQN in mean and median performance and is more robust to hyperparameter settings. The paper also provides implementation details and additional robustness analyses (averaging over many hyperparameters/seeds).
- Rigor and clarity: The method and losses are precisely described, with sufficient training details and ablations to support the claims. The use of balanced replay for reward prediction is carefully justified to avoid biasing the policy/value learning.

Suggestions and limitations (for improvement, not blockers)
- Fair comparison and compute budget: Since UNREAL performs additional replayed updates, some gains might arise from more gradient updates rather than solely better representation. Please add a control that matches A3C’s total number of gradient updates or wall-clock compute (e.g., an A3C variant with proportional extra value-only updates) to isolate the effect of auxiliary objectives.
- Reporting and statistics: Results are sometimes presented for “top 3” hyperparameters. Although you include robustness analyses, please also report aggregate means ± standard errors across seeds at a fixed, reasonable hyperparameter for each method, and include the number of seeds per configuration for headline figures. For Labyrinth human-normalized scores, provide human evaluation protocol details (number of human subjects, runs per level, variance).
- Atari pixel control: You note different λPC magnitudes due to intensity heterogeneity, and also evaluate “UNREAL without pixel control.” Please clarify whether the reported Atari SOTA metrics are for the full UNREAL (with PC) or the ablation, and include per-game ablations to quantify the contribution of PC in Atari.
- Auxiliary-update scheduling and stability: More detail on the ratio of on-policy A3C updates to off-policy auxiliary updates, the use of target networks (θ−) and their update cadence, and any observed instability/divergences would strengthen the methodological transparency.
- Generalization and transfer: Given the link to UVFA/successor representations, it would be valuable to test whether the learned representations transfer to new reward functions or modified tasks (e.g., new goal locations or visual changes) without retraining auxiliary heads.

Questions for the authors
1) Compute and data efficiency: What is the wall-clock training overhead of UNREAL vs A3C (same hardware), and does UNREAL still reach A3C’s final performance faster in wall-clock time? How many additional gradient steps per environment step are performed on average?
2) Auxiliary scheduling: What is the exact ratio/frequency of auxiliary updates per 20-step A3C unroll? Is θ− for Q-learning synchronized with A3C updates, and what is the target update schedule?
3) Replay buffer design: How sensitive are results to buffer size (2k) and reward balancing (P(r≠0)=0.5)? Did you try balancing across three classes (neg/zero/pos) explicitly, and what was the effect?
4) Stability: Did you observe divergence or pathological runs due to off-policy updates through the shared LSTM? Are gradients from auxiliary heads truncated in time, and do they backprop through the LSTM state?
5) Pixel vs feature control: On Labyrinth, feature control is “only slightly worse” than pixel control. Do they combine additively? On Atari, does feature control offer a more stable alternative to pixel control?
6) Human-normalization: How were human scores collected (subjects, training time, instructions)? What variance do human baselines exhibit across runs and maps?
7) Ablation on update budget: If you hold total update count constant across methods, how much of the improvement remains?
8) Frozen auxiliaries: If you disable auxiliary losses after an initial training phase, do the gains persist or degrade, suggesting primarily representational vs continual shaping effects?

Overall, this is a timely and impactful contribution: technically sound, well-situated in the literature, and empirically convincing across challenging domains. I recommend acceptance.
