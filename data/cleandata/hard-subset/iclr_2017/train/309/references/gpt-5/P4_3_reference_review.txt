1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks intended to improve data efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep RL in visually rich, partially observed environments. The main ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: off-policy n-step Q-learning heads that optimize pseudo-rewards defined on perceptual changes (pixel control via a spatial deconvolutional Q head) and network feature activations (feature control). These tasks share the convolutional and recurrent representation with the main policy/value networks.
- Auxiliary reward prediction: a feedforward head trained to predict immediate reward (three-class: negative/zero/positive) from short encoded observation sequences. Sampling is deliberately skewed to balance rewarding and non-rewarding samples, shaping shared visual features without biasing the on-policy updates.
- Value function replay: additional regression to the (replayed) n-step returns to accelerate and stabilize value learning, beyond the on-policy A3C value loss.
- A unified objective that combines on-policy A3C losses and off-policy auxiliary losses trained from a small replay buffer.

Empirically, on 13 Labyrinth levels, UNREAL roughly doubles human-normalized median and mean performance over A3C and achieves large speedups in data efficiency (up to 10x+ fewer steps to reach A3C’s final score). On 57 Atari games, UNREAL (and a variant without pixel control) surpass A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean and median human-normalized scores and exhibits improved robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations show consistent gains from each auxiliary component and suggest control-based objectives (pixel/feature control) outperform purely predictive baselines (reconstruction, input-change prediction).

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (i) strong and consistent empirical improvements in performance, data efficiency, and hyperparameter robustness across diverse domains, supported by ablations; (ii) a technically sound and well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks with on-policy RL that advances representation learning for deep RL.

3. Supporting arguments
- What problem is tackled? Improving representation learning for deep RL to cope with sparse rewards and complex visual dynamics, aiming at faster and more robust learning while maintaining or improving final performance.
- Is the approach well motivated and well placed in the literature? Yes. The paper positions UNREAL relative to options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, predictive auxiliary tasks, and model-based approaches. The choice to use off-policy control signals over pseudo-rewards to shape shared features—without using those policies at test time—is a clear and novel twist. Reward prediction with deliberately skewed sampling is justified because it affects only auxiliary heads, avoiding bias to the policy gradient.
- Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The paper presents:
  - Cross-domain experiments (Labyrinth and Atari) with substantial gains over strong baselines (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN).
  - Ablations (turning off auxiliary components; comparisons to reconstruction and change-prediction) supporting the claim that control-oriented auxiliary tasks yield larger benefits.
  - Analyses of hyperparameter robustness and data efficiency; speedup metrics are compelling.
  - Implementation detail sufficient to understand architecture and training (task weights, buffer size, update frequencies).
Potential caveats: precise evaluation protocols on Atari (e.g., human-normalization definition, episode handling) are not fully specified here; statistical reporting (number of seeds per setting, confidence intervals) is limited; and fairness of comparisons w.r.t. training frames and compute budget could be clarified. Nonetheless, the breadth of empirical evidence and ablations substantively support the main claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily part of the accept decision)
- Evaluation protocol clarity: Please specify the exact human-normalized score definition (e.g., (agent – random)/(human – random)), number of evaluation episodes per checkpoint, whether sticky actions or no-op starts are used, and total training frames per game for all agents compared (including Prioritized Dueling DQN).
- Statistical reporting: Provide mean ± std (or confidence intervals) across multiple seeds for each setting (not only hyperparameter sweeps), and clarify how “top 3” hyperparameter results are selected to avoid selection bias. Reporting the distribution over seeds for a fixed, canonical hyperparameter set would help.
- Compute and wall-clock: Report training wall-clock time and compute resources for A3C vs. UNREAL, and the overhead from auxiliary heads and replay, to contextualize data-efficiency gains.
- Interference and stability: Discuss any observed interference between on-policy A3C updates and off-policy auxiliary updates. Did you observe cases where auxiliary control hurt the base task? A learning-curve analysis of loss interactions would be informative.
- Sensitivity analyses: Include sensitivity to λPC, λVR, λRP and to replay buffer size, reward rebalancing strategy, and pixel-control grid resolution.
- Pixel control in dynamic textures: Since Labyrinth has animated textures, discuss how the agent avoids learning to chase irrelevant visual flicker. Any normalization, movement compensation, or masking would be worth detailing.
- Feature control: Results are preliminary. Combining feature and pixel control (and possibly selecting which features to control) could be explored; reporting on joint usage would strengthen the narrative.
- Generality: Briefly discuss applicability to continuous-action domains and non-visual modalities; outline any architectural adjustments needed.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact training budget (environment frames) per Atari game and per Labyrinth level for UNREAL and baselines, and how is it matched against Prioritized Dueling DQN? Are sticky actions used?
- How many independent seeds per hyperparameter setting were run for the headline numbers? For the “top 3” curves, are these distinct seeds or distinct hyperparameters? How is model selection performed to avoid optimistic bias?
- How sensitive are results to the replay buffer size (2k) and to the reward rebalancing scheme for reward prediction? What happens if you remove the rebalancing?
- Does value function replay (off-policy) introduce bias into the policy gradient via the baseline? Any empirical evidence that off-policy-trained baselines do not harm policy optimization?
- For pixel control, do you normalize pixel-change rewards by global intensity statistics per game/episode? How do you prevent the agent from favoring actions that induce camera motion purely to increase auxiliary rewards?
- Are auxiliary heads used at inference time? If removed, does the learned policy’s performance change?
- Can you report wall-clock speedups (not just data efficiency) from auxiliary tasks?
- On Atari, why is λPC range much smaller than on Labyrinth? Is there a principled way to set it without a sweep?

Overall, the paper is clear, technically sound, empirically thorough, and offers a novel and effective approach to representation learning in deep RL.
