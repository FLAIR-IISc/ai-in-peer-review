1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: off-policy n-step Q-learning heads trained on pseudo-rewards defined from the agent’s perceptual stream (pixel-control over an n×n grid) and optionally internal network features (feature control).
- Auxiliary reward tasks: a reward prediction head trained on short sequences to predict immediate reward, using replay skewed to over-represent rare rewarding events, to combat reward sparsity without biasing the policy/value objectives.
- Value function replay: additional value regression on replayed sequences to exploit improved features.
- A unified loss combining on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary objectives, sharing the CNN/LSTM representation but using simple feedforward heads for auxiliary tasks.

Empirically, UNREAL substantially outperforms a strong A3C baseline on 13 Labyrinth tasks (mean human-normalized performance 87% vs 54%) with large gains in data efficiency (approx. 10× fewer steps to A3C’s best score) and improved robustness to hyperparameters. On 57 Atari games, UNREAL achieves higher mean/median human-normalized scores and improved robustness over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. Ablations indicate each auxiliary task helps, and that control-based auxiliaries outperform reconstruction or immediate-change prediction.

2. Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, consistent empirical gains in two challenging domains with thorough ablations and robustness analyses; (2) Technically sound and well-motivated integration of off-policy auxiliary control and reward prediction with on-policy actor-critic, clearly positioned relative to prior work.

3. Supporting arguments
Clarity
- The problem is clearly stated: accelerate and stabilize deep RL by shaping representations via auxiliary tasks derived from pseudo-rewards and immediate reward prediction.
- The method is described in sufficient detail to reproduce key components: joint loss, replay design, pixel-control reward definition, auxiliary architectures, and training schedules. Implementation details and hyperparameter sampling ranges are provided.
- The relationship to prior lines (options/Horde/UVFA/successor representation/modeling/aux predictions) is well articulated; the main difference is using pseudo-reward-driven control as auxiliary objectives solely for representation learning.

Technical soundness
- The combined objective separates on-policy policy optimization (A3C) from off-policy auxiliary learning (Q-learning for control, replayed value regression, and reward prediction), mitigating bias in the policy gradient while leveraging sample efficiency of replay for auxiliaries.
- Off-policy auxiliary updates use target networks and n-step Q-learning, a standard and appropriate choice; replay design (recent 2k steps) and task-update cadence are specified.
- Using reward prediction purely as an auxiliary avoids biasing the control objective, while rebalancing addresses reward sparsity; the argument is coherent.
- Value replay’s impact on the policy gradient is limited to the baseline; off-policy regression may change variance but not bias of the policy gradient itself.

Novelty and placement
- Prior work learned many predictions (Horde) and factored value functions (UVFA, successor representations), and more recent work used auxiliary predictions (e.g., depth). This paper’s novelty lies in: (i) defining and training many off-policy auxiliary control tasks (especially pixel-control) that exploit the spatial structure via deconvolution; (ii) combining such control auxiliaries with skewed immediate reward prediction and value replay into a single training regime; and (iii) demonstrating substantial gains across visually rich 3D environments and Atari with careful ablations and robustness analyses.
- While each ingredient connects to known ideas, the integrated framework and empirical validation are appreciably new and impactful.

Empirical support
- Labyrinth: clear and consistent improvements in both final performance and learning speed; human-normalized scores and level-by-level breakdowns; robustness across hyperparameters.
- Comparisons against reconstruction and change prediction baselines support the claim that learning to control pseudo-rewards is more effective than pure prediction/reconstruction.
- Atari: broad evaluation on 57 games with multiple random hyperparameters and comparison to strong baselines; robustness plots support claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Clarify human-normalization for Labyrinth: how human scores were collected (number of subjects, episodes, protocol, instructions), variance across humans, and exact normalization formula. This strengthens the interpretation of “>100% human.”
- Provide more detail on replay sampling for reward prediction: the precise ratio of zero to non-zero rewards, whether positives and negatives are balanced within the non-zero set, and sensitivity analyses.
- Analyze sensitivity to λPC, λRP, λVR and the update ratio between on-policy and each auxiliary task. A brief sweep or stability plot would help practitioners.
- Report computational overhead (wall-clock) relative to A3C for both Labyrinth and Atari to contextualize data-efficiency gains.
- Expand the feature-control results beyond a single level; a broader ablation would help quantify when pixel-control vs feature-control is preferable.
- Discuss potential adverse interactions of pixel-control in tasks requiring stationary behavior or stability. Are there games/levels where pixel-control degrades performance?
- For Atari comparisons, specify evaluation details: training frames, evaluation episodes, sticky actions or no-op starts, life termination handling, and whether frame stacking is used in addition to the LSTM. Ensure fairness vs DQN variants.
- Minor: there are some reference formatting artifacts; a pass to clean citations would improve readability.

Questions for the authors
- How exactly were human-normalized scores computed in Labyrinth (subjects, episodes, conditions, and variance)? What is the sensitivity of conclusions to the human baseline?
- What is the n used for n-step Q-learning in auxiliary control? Is it matched to the 20-step unroll? How sensitive are results to n?
- How does the 50/50 zero vs non-zero reward rebalancing for reward prediction handle positive vs negative rewards? Did you try different ratios, and how did that affect performance?
- What is the observed wall-clock speedup/overhead of UNREAL vs A3C for reaching target performance?
- Did you evaluate prioritized replay for value regression or auxiliary control (e.g., by TD error)? If tried, how did it compare to the simple recent-buffer scheme?
- In visually dynamic environments (animated textures), how do you avoid pixel-control rewarding exogenous changes unrelated to the agent’s actions? Is there normalization or masking, and does this vary by domain?
- Can you provide broader results on feature control, and any guidance on when to prefer feature vs pixel control, or to combine them?
- Does replayed value regression introduce measurable bias or instability in the actor-critic training, given the off-policy nature of those targets? Any diagnostics on baseline variance or policy gradient stability?
