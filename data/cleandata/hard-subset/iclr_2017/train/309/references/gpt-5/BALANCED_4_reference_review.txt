1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of A3C that learns auxiliary tasks in parallel to accelerate and stabilize representation learning. The key ideas are: (i) auxiliary control tasks defined via pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control) trained off-policy with n-step Q-learning on a replay buffer; (ii) an auxiliary reward prediction task trained on a class-rebalanced replay distribution to counteract reward sparsity; and (iii) value function replay. All tasks share the encoder and LSTM with the main A3C agent, and a combined loss jointly updates parameters. Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameters on 13 visually complex 3D Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games. Ablations show control-based objectives outperform prediction and reconstruction, and combining all auxiliaries yields the best results. The work is simple, broadly applicable, and supported by extensive experiments.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons: (1) Strong, general, and well-motivated approach to representation shaping via auxiliary control/prediction tasks, with a clear integration into A3C; (2) Compelling evidence of substantial gains in performance, data efficiency, and robustness across diverse domains, with careful ablations.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three questions:

- What problem is tackled?
The paper targets slow and brittle learning in deep RL due to sparse rewards and weak representations. It asks whether auxiliary tasks derived from environment dynamics and reward can improve representation learning and ultimately policy performance.

- Is the approach well motivated and placed in the literature?
Yes. The work connects to options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations, highlighting the distinct choice to use pseudo-rewards solely for auxiliary learning rather than for behavior. It relates to model-based auxiliary predictions and recent auxiliary tasks in 3D RL. The motivation to reduce perceptual reward sparsity via rebalanced reward prediction is clear. The architectural choice to combine on-policy A3C with off-policy replay/Q-learning for auxiliaries is pragmatic and well argued for efficiency.

- Do the results support the claims?
Overall, yes. The Labyrinth results show 2x improvements in human-normalized scores and up to 10–18x data-efficiency gains, with ablations isolating the impact of pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay. Atari results on 57 games surpass strong baselines (A3C, prioritized dueling DQN) in mean and median scores and show greater hyperparameter robustness. The unsupervised baselines (reconstruction vs. prediction vs. control) provide informative diagnostics that support the control-oriented auxiliary design.

Caveats:
- Theoretical guarantees for mixing on- and off-policy updates are not provided; stability is demonstrated empirically.
- Some comparison fairness questions remain: UNREAL adds replay (and extra updates) absent in vanilla A3C; a “A3C + replay-only” baseline would isolate the benefit of representation shaping vs. extra data reuse.
- Evaluation details (Atari protocol, number of frames, evaluation episodes/seeds, human-normalization specifics) could be more explicit to ease reproducibility and comparison.
- Labyrinth is internal; reproducibility depends on availability.

Despite these, the empirical breadth, consistency across domains, and ablations make a strong case for the main claims.

4. Additional feedback and questions (to help improve the paper; not all affect my decision)
Constructive suggestions:
- Baselines and ablations:
  - Include an A3C+experience replay baseline (no auxiliaries) to disentangle benefits of replay itself.
  - Report wall-clock time in addition to environment steps to quantify computational overhead of auxiliary heads and replay.
  - Provide sensitivity analyses for λPC, λRP, λVR beyond sampling ranges; show performance vs. task weights.

- Evaluation clarity and rigor:
  - Specify Atari evaluation protocol (training frames, evaluation frames, no-op/human starts, sticky actions, frame skip, life-loss handling, evaluation seeds/episodes).
  - Report mean ± std across seeds for key metrics; you mention 45 hyperparameter/seed runs, but per-game seed variability at fixed hyperparameters would be useful.

- Understanding the auxiliaries:
  - Analyze whether pixel control chases dynamic textures. Consider normalizing by background motion or evaluating on levels with strong visual flicker to test robustness.
  - Show reward prediction accuracy over training and its correlation with downstream performance.
  - Explore combining feature control with pixel control in the main results (you note promise in preliminary experiments).

- Generalization:
  - Evaluate on extremely sparse-reward games (e.g., Montezuma’s Revenge) or procedurally generated sparse tasks to test the claim about alleviating perceptual reward sparsity.
  - Consider transfer: does the learned representation help on novel tasks or goals within the same environment?

- Practicalities:
  - If possible, release code and the Labyrinth configurations or provide a substitute benchmark with similar properties.

Questions for the authors:
- Off-policy/on-policy mix: Did you observe instability from Q-learning heads sharing parameters with the on-policy actor-critic? Any tricks (target networks, learning-rate schedules) critical for stability?
- Replay-only baseline: How does A3C with value replay but without auxiliary heads perform relative to UNREAL? Similarly, what is the incremental gain from each component when added last?
- Data vs. wall-clock: What is the wall-clock speedup or slowdown relative to A3C for reaching A3C’s final score?
- Pixel control design: How sensitive is performance to the grid resolution and cropping? Does event-based pixel-change (e.g., thresholded changes) perform differently?
- Reward prediction rebalancing: Could rebalancing introduce representation bias that harms long-term credit assignment? Did you try importance-weighting to correct?
- Atari protocol: Please detail training frames per game, evaluation setup, and human-normalization references used.
- Robustness: You sweep learning rate and entropy. How sensitive is performance to λPC and λRP? The appendix hints at λPC effects; can you expand?
- Reproducibility: Are the Labyrinth levels and their assets available to the community?

Overall, this is a timely and influential contribution that advances practical deep RL through principled auxiliary learning, with strong empirical backing.
