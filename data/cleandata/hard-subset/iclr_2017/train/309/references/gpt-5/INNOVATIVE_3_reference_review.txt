Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an RL agent that augments on-policy A3C with a suite of off-policy auxiliary tasks designed to shape the representation and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: learn policies for pseudo-reward functions (pixel-control and feature-control) trained via off-policy, n-step Q-learning on replayed experience, while sharing representation (CNN and LSTM) with the main policy.
- Reward-focused auxiliary task: immediate reward prediction trained on a rebalanced replay buffer to oversample rare rewarding events, used purely to shape the shared representation (not to bias policy/value).
- Value function replay: additional value regression on replayed sequences to exploit newly learned features.
- A joint loss combining on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary objectives.

Empirically, the approach yields large gains on 13 Labyrinth 3D tasks (up to 2x human-normalized score over A3C, and ~10x data-efficiency speedups) and on 57 Atari games (state-of-the-art mean/median performance at the time, with improved hyperparameter robustness). Ablations show control > prediction > reconstruction for representation shaping, with preliminary evidence that feature control performs comparably to pixel control.

Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Novelty and conceptual boldness: using many off-policy auxiliary control tasks as pseudo-rewards for representation learning and unifying on-policy and off-policy learning within one architecture is a significant and influential idea.
- Strong empirical gains and robustness across diverse domains with ablations supporting the central claims.

Supporting arguments
- Problem: Sparse/delayed reward makes representation learning hard; on-policy learning is data-inefficient. The paper directly targets this by densifying training signals (control and reward prediction) without biasing the main objective.
- Motivation and placement: The work is well situated relative to options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and recent auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., depth). The distinctive contribution is to treat control over pseudo-rewards as auxiliary representation-shaping, trained off-policy and shared with a recurrent on-policy agent, rather than using those policies directly.
- Method: The design choices are coherent: replay enables efficient learning of multiple control tasks; rebalancing reward prediction addresses extreme sparsity; deconvolutional heads exploit spatial structure; joint optimization is straightforward.
- Evidence: Comprehensive evaluation on Labyrinth (diverse tasks including navigation and combat) and Atari with both top-k sweeps and aggregated-over-hyperparameters plots. The large and consistent improvements, the orderings from ablations (control > prediction > reconstruction), and the pronounced gains in sample efficiency and robustness support the paper’s claims.

Constructive feedback (to improve the paper; not central to the accept decision)
- Clarify compute fairness: Report total environment frames, wall-clock, and hardware/thread counts for all methods, including baselines (A3C, Dueling DDQN), to ensure fair comparisons.
- Expand ablations:
  - More systematic study of feature control vs pixel control (the current feature control results are preliminary and promising).
  - Sensitivity to auxiliary loss weights (λPC, λRP, λVR) and replay buffer size.
  - Effect of sharing LSTM vs sharing only CNN for auxiliary control (since auxiliary control currently shares both CNN and LSTM).
- Off-policy details: Discuss potential bias from off-policy Q-learning without importance sampling and why it’s acceptable here (e.g., robustness from value-based targets, short n-step). Consider reporting stability measures or divergence checks.
- Generalization and transfer: Since the framing emphasizes representation learning, it would be valuable to demonstrate transfer to new rewards/goals (e.g., UVFA-style new goals, or new reward functions on the same environments).
- Additional baselines: Compare against on-policy A3C with replay-only value regression (no control), prioritized replay variants, and other auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., depth/ego-motion in 3D) to further isolate the gains due to control.
- Analysis: Provide more insight into learned features/policies (e.g., which pixels/features are controlled in different games; how reward predictor activations correlate with value learning progress).

Questions for the authors
- Control task specifics:
  - What discount γ and loss (MSE vs Huber) are used for the auxiliary Q-learning heads? Any target network update schedule beyond θ− being prior parameters?
  - Was double Q-learning or any other overestimation mitigation considered?
  - How does sharing the LSTM with control heads affect credit assignment and stability? Did you try sharing only the CNN?
- Reward prediction:
  - How sensitive is performance to the 50/50 rebalancing of zero vs non-zero rewards? Did you try more granular reweighting by reward magnitude or recency?
  - Does oversampling immediate rewards risk overfitting to very local features at the expense of long-horizon cues? Any evidence supporting/contradicting this?
- Replay and off-policy:
  - How large is the effect of value-replay alone (A3C + LVR only) across both domains?
  - Any experiments with prioritized replay for control/value, or with varying replay buffer sizes?
- Evaluation protocol:
  - For Atari, what training budget (frames), evaluation (no-op starts or sticky actions), and action repeat are used for all baselines? Are gains retained under sticky actions (to mitigate overfitting to determinism)?
  - For the hyperparameter sweeps: do the “top-3” curves correspond to different random seeds or just different hyperparameters? Can you report mean±std over seeds for a fixed hyperparameter setting to separate seed variance from tuning?
- Feature control:
  - Which hidden layers work best as pseudo-rewards? Any guidance for selecting units/layers, or learned weighting over features?

Overall, this is a substantive and creative step toward self-supervised representation learning in deep RL, with strong empirical support. I recommend acceptance.
