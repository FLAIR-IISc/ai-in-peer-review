Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning: (i) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control), (ii) reward prediction from short histories with replay biased toward rare rewards, and (iii) value function replay. All losses are combined into a single objective and trained from a small replay buffer, while the primary policy/value is trained on-policy with A3C.
- Empirically, on 13 Labyrinth levels, UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency (claimed ~10x speedup to reach A3C’s best) and final human-normalized scores (mean from ~54% to ~87%). On 57 Atari games, UNREAL achieves higher mean/median human-normalized scores than A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN, and appears more robust to hyperparameter settings. Ablations suggest pixel control > change prediction > reconstruction; preliminary feature control looks promising.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons: (1) Insufficient experimental clarity and fairness (training budget, compute parity, evaluation protocol, and hyperparameter selection are not controlled or transparently reported), and (2) numerous methodological and presentation issues (missing details, inconsistent references/formatting, and several ad hoc design choices) that collectively undermine confidence in the rigor and reproducibility despite promising results.

Supporting arguments
- Experimental fairness and reporting
  - Training budgets are not clearly standardized across methods. For Atari, the number of frames, wall-clock compute, and number of optimization steps for baselines vs UNREAL are not specified. UNREAL performs additional off-policy updates every 20 steps (pixel control Q-learning, reward prediction, value replay). Without matching total updates/compute, the reported gains may conflate algorithmic benefit with extra optimization.
  - Human-normalized scoring: the precise normalization formula, human protocol (number of episodes, training/testing separation), and the handling of games with negative or near-zero human scores are not defined. This is critical for interpreting 880% mean vs 250% median, which suggests heavy outliers.
  - ALE evaluation details (action repeat, frame preprocessing, life-loss handling, reward clipping, sticky actions/no-ops, evaluation episodes, exploration at test time) are missing. This prevents comparison to existing baselines and can materially change reported numbers.
  - Hyperparameter selection: plots show “average of the top 3 agents” among 50 random settings. While the paper also shows distributions, the headline claims use a best-k aggregation that may unfairly favor methods with more tunable knobs (e.g., λPC, λVR, λRP ranges differ by domain). There is no control for total hyperparameter search budget vs baselines and no held-out seeds for model selection.
  - Labyrinth human scores and evaluation: source of human data is acknowledged but not characterized (participants, training, instruction, number of trials), and it is unclear whether levels with procedurally generated maps had the same distribution for human and agent evaluation.
- Methodological clarity/novelty
  - Many elements are known: auxiliary tasks (e.g., pseudo-rewards/Horde/UVFAs, forward modeling), replay, and multi-task training. The novelty is the particular combination (pixel/feature control + reward prediction + value replay) integrated with A3C. This is valuable but incremental; stronger claims need stronger ablations and controls.
  - Ablations are limited: feature control is only preliminarily evaluated on one level; reconstruction and change-prediction baselines are run on three levels; no ablation isolates the effect of value replay alone vs reward-prediction-only vs pixel-control-only across the full 13-level suite. The contribution of each component to Atari results is unclear.
  - Off-policy aspects: for auxiliary Q-learning, target network details, stability mechanisms, and update schedules are underspecified (θ− “previous parameters” is vague; no target update cadence). Off-policy corrections are not discussed for value replay; while V-learning is less sensitive, resampling can bias the value targets if bootstrapping from a moving policy/value.
  - Reward prediction uses intentionally skewed replay (P(r≠0)=0.5). While the paper argues no policy/value bias because the predictor is only auxiliary, shared convolutional layers do couple this bias into the representation used for policy/value, which deserves more analysis (e.g., behavior shifts, stability).
  - Pixel control defines rewards on a central 80×80 crop and uses a final ReLU on Qaux. Justification for cropping and the impact on learning are not given; ReLU on Q-values is unconventional (albeit pixel-change rewards are nonnegative), and can cause optimization issues or implicit clipping.
  - Replay buffer size is only 2k transitions, unusually small relative to environment heterogeneity; no sensitivity is reported (buffer size, sampling ratio, on/off-policy mix).
- Presentation and reproducibility
  - Numerous formatting issues hinder readability: “.4 UNREAL AGENT” (section numbering), typos (“doconvolutional”), inconsistent capitalization (e.g., “id software”), and multiple broken/incomplete references (e.g., “Barreto et al.,? \Q2016\E”). References require a thorough cleanup to meet ICLR standards.
  - Important implementation choices are ad hoc: different λPC ranges for Atari/Labyrinth to “fit normalization”; no report on reward normalization or control of pixel intensity distributions; missing details on gradient clipping, target network updates, and optimization schedules beyond RMSprop with 32 threads.
  - Code and models are not provided; given the complexity, reproducibility is doubtful without significantly more detail.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Specify all evaluation protocols in detail for both Labyrinth and Atari, including frame budgets, compute budgets, test-time policies, and normalization formulas; report averages and confidence intervals across fixed seeds, and include complete distributions without best-k filtering as primary results.
- Equalize compute/update budgets across methods; alternatively, report performance as a function of wall-clock time and total gradient steps to disentangle algorithmic improvements from extra updates.
- Provide comprehensive ablations across all Labyrinth tasks and a representative Atari subset: UNREAL minus each component; each auxiliary component alone; value replay alone; reward prediction without skew; pixel control with different grids/crops/normalizations; feature control beyond a single level.
- Analyze the induced representation: do auxiliary tasks improve attribution/saliency on reward-relevant regions? Are learned policies more sample-efficient due to faster discovery of high-reward states?
- Justify architectural choices: ReLU on Qaux, central crop, cell size, dueling parametrization for aux heads. Include sensitivity analyses.
- Fix all reference and formatting issues; standardize citation formats and ensure all references are complete and correct.

Questions for the authors
1. What are the exact training frame budgets, wall-clock times, and total optimization steps per method on Atari and Labyrinth? Are compute and update counts matched between A3C and UNREAL?
2. Provide full ALE evaluation details (sticky actions/no-op starts, life-loss termination, reward clipping, evaluation episodes, test-time exploration). Are the reported baselines reproduced under the same protocol?
3. How are human-normalized scores computed exactly? Please report human protocols and variance. How are negative human scores handled?
4. For auxiliary Q-learning: what is the target network update rule/frequency for θ−? Any gradient clipping or stability tricks? Did you observe divergence?
5. Why the 80×80 central crop and ReLU on Qaux? What happens without cropping and with linear Q outputs?
6. How sensitive are results to replay buffer size, replay ratio, and reward skew in reward prediction?
7. Can you provide full ablations on all 13 Labyrinth levels and a subset of Atari games isolating each auxiliary component’s contribution?
8. Are improvements still present when performance is measured at fixed wall-clock time or fixed total updates across methods?
