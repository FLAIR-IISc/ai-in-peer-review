1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, a deep RL agent that augments a recurrent A3C backbone with auxiliary tasks designed primarily to shape the representation rather than directly affect the policy. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control via off-policy Q-learning on pseudo-rewards: pixel control (maximize local pixel change per cell) and feature control (maximize activations of internal units), implemented with deconvolutional Q-heads shared with the policy/value network.
- Reward-focused auxiliary task: immediate reward prediction trained on skewed replay (balancing zero vs non-zero rewards) to combat reward sparsity while not biasing the policy/value learning.
- Replay-enhanced value regression: extra value regression from recent experience to exploit newly shaped features.
These components are combined in a single loss and trained asynchronously. On 13 3D Labyrinth levels, UNREAL achieves large gains in final score, data efficiency (≈10× fewer frames to reach A3C performance), and hyperparameter robustness. On 57 Atari games, UNREAL improves mean/median human-normalized performance and robustness relative to A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. Ablations indicate each auxiliary component helps; learning to control pixel changes outperforms reconstruction and simple change prediction.

2) Decision
Accept. Primary reasons:
- Novel, bold direction: learning to control generic pseudo-rewards (pixel/feature changes) as an auxiliary objective for representation shaping, trained off-policy alongside an on-policy recurrent actor-critic. This idea is both elegant and broadly applicable.
- Strong, diverse empirical gains: consistent improvements across many 3D navigation/laser-tag tasks and Atari, with ablations, robustness analyses, and clear architectural details.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper addresses the challenge of learning effective representations for deep RL in sparse-reward, partially observed, visually rich settings—improving data efficiency and robustness without handcrafting domain-specific signals.

Is the approach well motivated and situated in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly contrasts with prior temporal abstraction (options), Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and model-based prediction. Unlike prior work that uses pseudo-rewards to define options or transfer across reward functions, UNREAL uses pseudo-rewards purely as auxiliary control signals for representation learning, trained efficiently off-policy with replay. The link to depth prediction and emulator-feature prediction in 3D RL is also made, with a distinct focus here on control-oriented auxiliary tasks.

Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. The Labyrinth experiments demonstrate substantial final performance gains and a 10× median data-efficiency improvement; ablations (pixel control vs prediction vs reconstruction) support the core hypothesis that “learning to control” produces better features than “learning to predict/reconstruct.” The Atari results show broad improvements and robustness. The methodology is sound (shared trunk, off-policy auxiliary heads, rebalanced replay for reward prediction), and implementation details are provided.

Caveats: Fairness vs compute is not fully disentangled—UNREAL performs extra updates via replay/aux tasks. While data-efficiency is emphasized (frames to a score), equal wall-clock or equal-gradient-budget comparisons would strengthen claims. Statistical reporting (confidence intervals across seeds) is limited though hyperparameter sweeps partially address variability. Feature control results are less comprehensive than pixel control. Nonetheless, the magnitude and consistency of improvements and the conceptual novelty offset these limitations.

4) Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Compute fairness: Report wall-clock comparisons and gradient-update counts per environment frame, and reproduce key gains under a fixed compute budget to address potential confounds from additional auxiliary updates.
- Statistical rigor: Include confidence intervals or interquartile ranges over multiple seeds per hyperparameter setting; report significance for headline improvements.
- Feature control: Expand results for feature control across more levels/games and analyze complementarity with pixel control.
- Sensitivity analyses: Provide sensitivity to λVR, λPC, λRP, replay buffer size, grid resolution for pixel control, and reward-rebalancing ratio; include ablations that vary these systematically.
- Exploration effects: Analyze whether auxiliary control implicitly improves exploration (e.g., visitation metrics, coverage, or time-to-first-reward).
- Generalization/transfer: Evaluate whether the learned representation transfers to new reward functions or goals (e.g., zero-shot or few-shot transfer), given the connection to UVFA/successor features.
- Policy bias discussion: Clarify possible leakage of reward-prediction skew into the policy via shared convolutional features; demonstrate empirically that policy/value estimates remain unbiased (e.g., on-policy evaluation, off-policy correction analysis, or control experiments with detached gradients).
- Comparisons: Where possible, add baselines with prioritized replay integrated into A3C, and auxiliary model-based prediction (e.g., action-conditional video prediction) to isolate the unique benefits of control-based auxiliary tasks.

5) Questions for the authors
- Compute parity: How many auxiliary/replay updates are executed per environment step relative to the baseline? Do the gains persist under matched wall-clock and matched update budgets?
- Reward balancing: What is the precise sampling scheme for reward prediction (e.g., proportions, mini-batch composition), and how sensitive are results to this ratio and buffer partitioning?
- Off-policy stability: Did you observe instabilities from off-policy Q-learning on replayed on-policy data? Any target network, clipping, or other stability tricks beyond n-step Q-learning?
- Pixel control design: How sensitive is performance to the grid size (n×n), cell size, or the choice of absolute pixel differences as pseudo-reward? Did you try more semantic changes (e.g., optical flow, learned feature deltas)?
- Feature control scope: Which layers/units were most beneficial to control? Does controlling higher-level features help more than early features? Any risk of pathological maximization of internal activations that harms policy learning?
- Replay buffer size/horizon: How do buffer size and the length of sequences used for value replay affect results?
- Evaluation details: For Labyrinth, how are human-normalized scores computed? Please provide variance across seeds for headline numbers and learning curves.
- Future directions: You note not using the learned pseudo-reward controllers directly. Did you attempt using them as options/skills for hierarchical control, or for exploration bonuses?

Overall, this is a bold and influential idea, well executed and empirically compelling. I recommend acceptance.
