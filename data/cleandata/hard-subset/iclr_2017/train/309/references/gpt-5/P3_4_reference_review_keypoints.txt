- Proposal: UNREAL augments on-policy recurrent A3C with off-policy auxiliary tasks (pixel/feature control, reward prediction, value replay) from a short replay buffer to improve representation learning.
- Architecture: Shared CNN-LSTM trunk with auxiliary heads; auxiliary losses computed off-policy with rebalanced sampling for reward prediction.
- Core idea: Use pseudo-reward control tasks (n-step Q-learning) to learn controllable features and reward-focused tasks to de-sparsify rewards and stabilize value learning.
- Empirical claim: UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance on DeepMind Lab (13 levels) and Atari (57 games).
- Ablations: Pixel/feature control outperform input reconstruction/change prediction; value replay and reward prediction add further gains.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason 1: Strong, consistent empirical gains in both data efficiency and final performance across diverse domains with reasonable ablations.
- Key reason 2: Well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks distinct from prior work by using pseudo-rewards purely for representation learning.
- Problem addressed: Improving representation learning and sample efficiency in visually complex, partially observed environments with sparse rewards.
- Placement in literature: Differentiates from options/Horde/UVFA by using auxiliary pseudo-rewards to shape shared representations rather than drive behavior; novel combo with recurrent A3C and deconv spatial heads.
- Lab results: UNREAL doubles mean/median human-normalized Lab performance vs A3C and achieves ~10× speedups to A3C’s final score across many levels.
- Atari results: Gains over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean/median and robustness; pixel control less uniformly helpful but non-pixel variants still improve A3C.
- Methodological soundness: Mixing on-policy policy gradients with off-policy Q-learning for auxiliaries is reasonable; policy/value objectives insulated from replay bias.
- Pixel control head: Deconv/dueling design is well-motivated for spatial tasks; short 2k replay buffer limits non-stationarity.
- Rigor/reporting: Architecture, training details, hyperparameter sampling, and robustness sweeps provided; multiple seeds via asynchronous threads.
- Reporting concerns: Some choices (e.g., “top-3” curves, small replay buffer, human normalization details) need clearer transparency.
- Fairness/evaluation suggestion: Clarify compute budgets and ensure baselines receive comparable resources and tuning budgets.
- Atari protocol request: Specify no-op starts, sticky actions, frame-skip, life-loss handling, and training frames per game to confirm consistency with prior work.
- Human normalization request: Detail human training time, skill level, number of trials, and variance for Labyrinth normalization.
- Ablation/sensitivity request: Provide granular ablations isolating each component across levels/games, confidence intervals across seeds, and sensitivity to λPC/λRP/λVR and replay size.
- Negative-transfer analysis: Report cases where pixel control hurts and whether gating/scheduling auxiliary losses mitigates harm.
- Representation diagnostics: Run linear probes, CCA/CKA similarity, and visualize what feature control units learn to control.
- Generalization tests: Evaluate transfer to held-out procedural seeds/textures and check if reward-prediction rebalancing overfits short-horizon cues.
- Comparison baselines: Add intrinsic motivation baselines (count-based, forward/inverse models) to contextualize benefits.
- Questions for authors (condensed): Sensitivity to replay buffer size/prioritization; exact Atari evaluation settings and frames; human-normalization variance and significance; evidence of auxiliary control biasing behavior and mitigation; per-game/level results with independent-seed CIs; role/benefit of feature control in full UNREAL; stability of reward-prediction balancing for extremely sparse rewards; provide wall-clock times, hardware, and code/pseudocode for reproducibility.
