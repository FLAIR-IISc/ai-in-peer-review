Summary
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks designed to learn richer representations and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key idea is to leverage off-policy auxiliary control objectives—“pixel control” (policies that maximize local pixel change) and “feature control” (policies that maximize selected hidden-unit activations)—together with a reward prediction task trained on a rebalanced replay buffer, plus value function replay. The auxiliary heads share early network layers with the policy and are trained from recent experience stored in a replay buffer using n-step Q-learning. The authors show strong improvements over A3C on 13 challenging 3D Labyrinth levels (2x human-normalized performance, up to 10–18x data-efficiency speedups) and across 57 Atari games (substantial gains over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN), with ablations indicating that each auxiliary component helps and their combination helps most. The paper emphasizes robustness to hyperparameters and includes comparisons to simpler auxiliary baselines (reconstruction and immediate input-change prediction).

Decision: Accept
Reason: The central idea—learning with unsupervised control objectives that shape the representation, trained off-policy in parallel with on-policy actor-critic—is novel, bold, and impactful, and the empirical gains are broad and sizable. Some evaluation details could be tightened, but the conceptual contribution and demonstrated effectiveness outweigh these concerns.

Supporting arguments
- Novelty and conceptual clarity: The paper unifies on-policy policy-gradient learning with off-policy auxiliary control objectives via shared representation and replay. Pixel/feature control tasks are a compelling, domain-agnostic way to densify learning signals without directly biasing the policy. Reward prediction with skewed sampling provides an effective way to counter reward sparsity while avoiding policy/value bias. This is a crisp, general design pattern for representation learning in RL.
- Breadth and strength of results: Extensive experiments on 3D Labyrinth and 57 Atari games show consistent improvements in final performance, learning speed, and hyperparameter robustness. Ablations (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction, value replay) support the claim that each auxiliary task contributes and the combination yields the best results. Comparisons to reconstruction and input-change prediction underscore the importance of control (not just prediction) as an auxiliary signal.
- Practical importance: The method is simple to integrate with existing actor-critic agents, scales with asynchronous training and replay, and is likely to benefit many domains where reward is sparse and observations are high dimensional.
- Methodological soundness: Losses and training protocols are clearly specified. The off-policy handling for auxiliary Q-learning and the architectural details for deconvolutional heads are described, and the replay mechanism is used judiciously (balanced sampling for reward prediction; standard sampling for value replay and control).

Concerns and limitations (not blocking)
- Evaluation protocol details on Atari could be more standardized (e.g., sticky actions, full disclosure of seeds and statistical tests, consistent compute budgets). Reporting top-3 hyperparameter averages is informative but nonstandard; reporting mean±std across all seeds/hyperparams per game would strengthen claims.
- The off-policy/recurrent interaction could use further clarification (e.g., how LSTM states are reconstructed for replayed sequences; whether importance sampling or target networks fully mitigate bias).
- Pixel-control reward design is somewhat handcrafted (crop, grid size, absolute difference, normalization), which may bias toward domains with frequent motion; broader analysis of sensitivity would be useful.
- Feature control is promising but evaluated only preliminarily; a deeper comparison to other intrinsic motivation or representation-learning baselines (e.g., curiosity/prediction error, count-based exploration, model-based auxiliary tasks) would contextualize gains further.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a standardized Atari evaluation table with mean/median human-normalized scores, 95% CIs across seeds, and details on sticky actions/no-ops. Include wall-clock time and environment steps to chosen thresholds to support efficiency claims.
- Disaggregate ablations per game/level to highlight where each auxiliary task helps most (e.g., sparse vs dense reward).
- Clarify the handling of recurrent state during replayed updates (how histories are reconstructed, unroll length, whether bootstrap values use θ−).
- Add sensitivity analyses: pixel-control grid resolution/crop, λ weights, replay buffer size, and reward rebalancing ratio.
- Discuss why control (vs prediction) provides superior representation shaping; a short theoretical or empirical lens (e.g., controllability/empowerment connections) would strengthen the narrative.
- Consider a brief experiment executing auxiliary policies intermittently (options-style) to probe whether learned control heads can also improve exploration.

Questions for the authors
- How are LSTM states computed for replayed sequences in auxiliary control and value replay? Are hidden states re-initialized or reconstructed from preceding context? Any observed instability from off-policy updates through recurrence?
- How sensitive are results to the pixel-control design choices (grid size, crop region, absolute difference metric, dueling head, ReLU on Qaux)? Does feature-control alone recover most benefits, especially in domains with little pixel motion?
- What is the relative contribution of value replay vs pixel control vs reward prediction on Atari? Can you report a per-game breakdown?
- Does reward rebalancing for prediction ever harm the shared features for policy/value? Did you try alternative rebalance ratios or curriculum schedules?
- How does UNREAL fare on very sparse-reward Atari games (e.g., Montezuma’s Revenge)? If not tested, what do you expect to happen and why?
- What is the compute budget (actors, frames, wall-clock) compared to A3C and Dueling DQN? Are speedups in data efficiency matched by wall-clock speedups?

Overall, this paper advances a fresh and practical direction—learning to control unsupervised signals to shape representations—that delivers large and robust gains. I recommend acceptance.
