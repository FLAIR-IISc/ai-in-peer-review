Summary
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks intended to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The key additions are: (i) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel-control and feature-control) using a deconvolutional head; (ii) reward prediction trained on a skewed replay buffer to over-sample rare rewards; and (iii) value-function replay. These losses are combined with the on-policy A3C loss. Experiments on 13 Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games claim substantially faster learning (up to 10× in Labyrinth) and higher final performance (e.g., mean human-normalized 87% on Labyrinth; on Atari, mean 880% and median 250%), along with increased robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations suggest auxiliary control outperforms reconstruction or simple change prediction on a few Labyrinth levels; reward prediction and value replay also help.

Decision: Reject (borderline), due to evaluation methodology and insufficiently controlled ablations
Key reasons:
- Non-standard and potentially optimistic evaluation protocol on Atari (selection/reporting of “top-3” hyperparameter runs per game; unclear training budgets and evaluation settings), making it hard to assess fair improvements over strong baselines.
- Insufficient isolation of contributions across the full benchmark: ablations for each component are limited (e.g., control vs reward prediction vs replay vs larger capacity) and largely confined to subsets (Labyrinth), leaving confounds unresolved.

Supporting arguments
- Fairness/rigor of evaluation:
  - Atari results are reported as averages over the top-3 hyperparameter settings per method/game; this risks overfitting to the test environments and complicates comparison to published baselines that typically use a fixed protocol (e.g., fixed training frames, evaluation with 30 no-ops, sticky actions, single hyperparameter per game). It is unclear whether baselines received equivalent hyperparameter sweeps and selection criteria.
  - The total training budget (frames/updates) per game is not clearly specified for Atari; without matched budgets and compute, it is difficult to ascribe gains to the method rather than more training or better tuning.
  - Statistical rigor is limited: while robustness plots are shown, there are few confidence intervals or per-game significance analyses; the “mean human-normalized” metric can be dominated by a few extreme games, and the unusually high 880% mean warrants careful, standardized reporting.

- Attribution of gains:
  - Multiple changes are introduced simultaneously: auxiliary control, reward prediction with class rebalancing, value-function replay, off-policy updates, and additional network heads. The ablations on Labyrinth indicate benefits of pixel-control vs reconstruction/change prediction on three levels, but there is no comprehensive, controlled study across Atari isolating each component.
  - Replay alone (with A3C) and larger network capacity controls are not thoroughly evaluated, making it unclear how much improvement comes from better optimization/data reuse vs the specific auxiliary objectives.
  - The method mixes on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (Q-learning) updates sharing parameters. There is no theoretical or empirical stability analysis (e.g., gradient interference, divergence), and no comparisons to simpler, established alternatives (e.g., prioritized replay with value-only auxiliary losses) across the full suite.

- Generality and design choices:
  - Pixel control requires ad hoc normalization/tuning (λPC ranges differ across domains due to pixel intensity changes), which may undermine the claim of generality. The chosen pseudo-reward (absolute pixel change) could incentivize behavior that does not align with task reward in some games; broader evaluation of this failure mode is missing.
  - Reward prediction uses a heavily skewed sampling strategy that intentionally biases the auxiliary distribution. Although the authors argue the policy/value are unaffected, shared encoders can still introduce representational bias; this is not probed.

- Reproducibility/details:
  - Several implementation details are domain-specific (e.g., 2k replay buffer size, action repeats, thread counts) and may affect outcomes; code availability and standardized evaluation adherence are not discussed.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Report Atari results under a standard, fixed protocol: training frames (e.g., 200M), sticky actions, 30 no-op starts, single hyperparameter per game selected via a validation process separated from test scores; provide mean/median with confidence intervals across seeds.
- Provide per-component ablations across the full Atari suite and Labyrinth: A3C+replay only; A3C+reward prediction only; A3C+pixel control only; A3C+feature control only; combinations; and matched-capacity controls.
- Match compute and network capacity across baselines; include wall-clock comparisons.
- Analyze stability/gradient interference between on-policy and off-policy losses; include learning dynamics diagnostics and sensitivity to λ weights.
- Examine negative-transfer cases (games with low visual change or where pixel change is anti-correlated with reward).
- Clarify if improvements persist with larger replay buffers and different prioritization schemes; compare to prioritized replay baselines directly.
- Clean up references and ensure complete, consistent citations.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact training budget per Atari game (frames, updates), evaluation protocol (no-ops, sticky actions), and number of evaluation episodes used to compute final scores? Are all methods trained/evaluated identically?
- How are the “top-3” runs chosen, and are baselines (A3C, Dueling DQN) afforded identical tuning and selection? Can you report results for a fixed hyperparameter setting per game plus mean/median across random seeds?
- How much of the gain comes from value replay alone? Please provide A3C+replay vs A3C+auxiliary control vs A3C+reward prediction across all 57 Atari games.
- Does pixel control ever hurt performance? Please report per-game deltas and characterize when auxiliary control is beneficial or detrimental.
- How sensitive is UNREAL to λVR, λPC, λRP? Are there stable ranges that generalize across games without per-game tuning?
- Can you provide evidence that the shared encoder is not biased by the skewed reward-prediction sampling (e.g., by measuring representation drift or performance when disabling reward prediction after pretraining)?
- Do the reported Atari gains hold under sticky actions and the current ALE evaluation best practices?

Overall, the idea of leveraging auxiliary tasks to shape representations is interesting and plausibly useful, but the current empirical evidence and evaluation protocol are not yet rigorous enough for me to endorse acceptance.
