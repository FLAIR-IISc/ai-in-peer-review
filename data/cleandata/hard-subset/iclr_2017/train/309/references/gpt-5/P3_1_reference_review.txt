1) Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks trained from a small replay buffer to accelerate and stabilize representation learning. The key components are: (i) auxiliary control tasks defined by pseudo-rewards, especially pixel control (maximize local pixel-change in an n×n grid) and feature control (maximize hidden unit activations), trained via off-policy n-step Q-learning from replay; (ii) a reward prediction task trained on rebalanced replay that oversamples rewarding transitions to combat sparsity, using a feedforward head on stacked CNN features; and (iii) value function replay to further stabilize/accelerate learning. These are combined in a joint loss with shared CNN/LSTM backbone, where on-policy A3C updates the main policy/value while auxiliary objectives shape the representation from off-policy sampled recent history. Experiments on 13 Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games report substantial gains in data efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameters relative to A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. Ablations indicate pixel control and reward prediction both contribute; predicting pixel change or reconstruction alone is weaker than control.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (1) Strong, consistent empirical improvements in challenging 3D environments and Atari, with careful ablations and robustness analyses. (2) A well-motivated and novel integration of off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction into an on-policy recurrent agent, demonstrating a practical and general recipe for representation shaping in deep RL.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the long-standing issue of sparse rewards and slow representation learning in high-dimensional, partially observed settings. Using auxiliary tasks to densify learning signals is compelling and aligned with prior ideas (Horde, UVFA, successor representations, model prediction), but here the auxiliary signals are trained specifically to improve the shared representation of a performant recurrent policy rather than to construct reusable goal-conditioned policies.

- Methodology novelty and soundness:
  • Auxiliary control via pixel/feature control: Defining pseudo-rewards for controllability and learning Q-values off-policy from replay is a practical way to leverage a single stream of experience for many tasks. The deconvolutional dueling head that outputs spatial Q-values for pixel control is architecturally appropriate and exploits spatial structure.
  • Reward prediction with rebalanced sampling: Oversampling rare rewarding transitions to shape CNN features while not biasing the policy/value targets is a nice design choice; it addresses reward sparsity without off-policy bias in the actor-critic.
  • Value function replay: Simple but effective; complements on-policy A3C updates.
  • The combined loss and training protocol (on-policy A3C + off-policy replay for auxiliary tasks) are clearly articulated and practically feasible.

- Relation to prior work: Differentiates from Horde (separate value functions, no representation sharing), UVFA/successor representations (goal/reward-factorization for transfer, not auxiliary shaping), and model-prediction auxiliaries (here, control tasks outperform pixel prediction/reconstruction). Cites recent 3D aux tasks (depth, feature prediction) and positions contributions appropriately.

- Empirical support:
  • Labyrinth: Large gains in human-normalized performance and 10× median data-efficiency improvements; robustness across hyperparameters; ablations showing control > prediction > reconstruction, supporting the central claim that control-based auxiliaries better shape useful features.
  • Atari: Broad improvements in mean and median human-normalized scores and robustness across 57 games. The study explores multiple hyperparameter settings per game.

4) Additional feedback for improvement (not necessarily affecting the decision)
- Evaluation protocol clarity on Atari: Please specify training frame budgets, evaluation setting (number of evaluation episodes, no-op or random starts, life-loss termination, sticky actions), and score aggregation. Comparisons to Prioritized Dueling DQN should match evaluation protocols as closely as possible or include caveats.

- Handling recurrence in replayed updates: Elaborate how LSTM states are reconstructed for value replay and off-policy Q-learning (sequence length, state initialisation, target bootstrapping). This is important for reproducibility and to understand credit assignment stability.

- Compute and sample efficiency fairness: Report wall-clock and frame budgets per method, including auxiliary loss computation overhead, and ensure baselines receive comparable compute. Provide per-level sample-efficiency curves (frames to reach given thresholds) for Atari, analogous to Labyrinth.

- Sensitivity to auxiliary weights and design: Include a small study on λPC, λRP, λVR, grid resolution for pixel control, and normalization of pixel-change rewards. Clarify why output ReLU on Qaux is chosen and how negative advantages/values are handled; pixel-change rewards are nonnegative, but explicit rationale helps.

- Analysis of learned representations: Add qualitative/quantitative analysis showing how auxiliaries change feature activations, attention/saliency, or value estimation error. This would strengthen the representation-learning claim beyond performance metrics.

- Generality of auxiliaries: While feature control shows promise, most results hinge on pixel control. Discuss applicability to non-visual domains or object-centric signals (if available), and whether the approach transfers to tasks with dense rewards.

Questions to the authors
- Recurrent replay: How exactly are LSTM hidden/cell states initialized for replayed sequences used in Q-learning and value replay? Do you unroll over the sampled window only, and how do you handle target networks θ− for the auxiliary heads?

- Atari evaluation details: What are the precise evaluation settings (no-op starts vs random human starts, sticky actions, life-loss as terminal, number of eval episodes, frame budgets)? How many total frames are used for training per game? Are results averaged over seeds?

- Distribution shift from rebalanced reward prediction: Did you observe any adverse effects on the main policy/value learning from oversampling rewarding transitions in the shared CNN? Any regularization to prevent overfitting to rare patterns?

- Auxiliary/task update ratios: What fraction of total gradient updates and compute is spent on auxiliary losses vs A3C? How sensitive are gains to the frequency of auxiliary updates and replay buffer size (2k)?

- Pixel control design choices: How sensitive are results to grid size (e.g., 10×10 vs 20×20), crop, and pixel-change normalization across games (especially Atari with variable intensity scales)?

- Transfer/generalization: Have you tested whether representations learned with auxiliaries transfer across levels or reward functions (e.g., freezing CNN and retraining policy), akin to UVFA/SR transfer?

Overall, this is a strong, practically impactful paper with a clear contribution to improving deep RL via auxiliary learning. The additional details above would further solidify the empirical claims and facilitate reproduction.
