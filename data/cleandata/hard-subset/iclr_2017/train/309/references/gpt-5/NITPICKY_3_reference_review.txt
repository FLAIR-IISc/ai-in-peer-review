Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning and data efficiency: (i) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control), (ii) reward prediction from short CNN-encoded observation stacks using rebalanced replay, and (iii) value function replay from recent trajectories. A joint loss combines on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary losses.
- Experiments on 13 Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games indicate substantial gains in final performance, robustness to hyperparameters, and sample efficiency relative to A3C; reported Atari mean/median human-normalized performance is 880%/250%. Labyrinth mean performance increases from 54% to 87% with up to 10x speed-up to reach A3C’s best.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Methodological clarity and rigor are insufficient for the breadth of empirical claims, particularly in the mixing of on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary updates without importance sampling or a stability analysis, while simultaneously asserting “no bias” from reward prediction.
2) Evaluation and reporting shortcomings (inconsistent run counts; selection of top-3 hyperparameters; unclear fairness to PD-DQN; limited statistical evidence; small replay buffer; non-public environment) undermine confidence in the claimed state-of-the-art performance and robustness.

Supporting arguments
- Off-policy auxiliary learning and bias: The paper states the reward prediction task can be trained from a skewed distribution “without biasing the value function or policy,” but its gradients flow through shared encoders into the policy/value networks. This does influence the representation used by the on-policy learner, hence can bias learning. No theoretical or empirical justification is given to support the “no bias” claim.
- Lack of off-policy correction: Auxiliary control uses n-step Q-learning from replay without importance sampling or target-network details beyond θ− “previous parameters.” Given joint training with recurrent A3C, the stability and convergence behavior of this combination is unclear. There is no ablation showing sensitivity to off-policy degree, no study of replay ratio, and no analysis of the effect on the on-policy estimator’s variance/bias.
- Evaluation methodology issues:
  - Hyperparameter selection: The main figures present curves for the “top 3 agents” chosen post hoc from 50 hyperparameter settings, which risks optimistic bias. While robustness plots are provided, the primary headline results emphasize top configurations rather than averages with error bars. Later, Appendix C states 45 random hyperparameter settings (inconsistent with 50).
  - Statistical reporting: The Labyrinth “10x” speed-up is computed relative to “A3C best performance,” a post hoc benchmark; there are no confidence intervals or tests of significance. The figures are referenced, but error bars and precise aggregation protocol (median over seeds vs hyperparameters vs episodes) are not clearly specified.
  - Fairness of Atari comparisons: It’s not stated whether PD-DQN results use the same evaluation protocol (frame-skip, sticky actions, episode termination, number of training frames, hyperparameter search budget). The paper reports “across all levels” UNREAL surpasses A3C/PD-DQN but then gives only aggregate mean/median numbers; per-game comparisons and training budget parity are not shown.
  - Human normalization: For Labyrinth, human-normalized scores are used, but the number of human testers, their training, variance, and evaluation protocol are not described. The YouTube link is not a substitute for reproducible evaluation.
- Reproducibility and completeness:
  - Labyrinth environment appears non-public; without release, the main claims are difficult to verify. Appendix B provides many details, but critical choices remain ambiguous (reward clipping, target network update schedule, gradient clipping, normalization of pixel-change rewards across games).
  - Replay buffer size “2k observations” (≈500 agent steps with action repeat 4) seems small for stable replay-driven learning, yet no sensitivity analysis is provided. Sequence lengths of 20 further reduce the number of distinct training sequences.
  - Architecture choices: A final ReLU on Qaux forces nonnegative Q-values; while pixel-change rewards are nonnegative, discounted returns could be zero only if no change occurs; for feature control, details about the reward sign (and whether the target features are ReLU-bounded) are missing. The choice is not justified nor ablated.
  - Several references and formatting are broken: numerous entries render as “Schaul et al.,? \Q2015\E,” “Barreto et al\.,” lowercased proper nouns (id software), typos (“doconvolutional,” “hyperparamer,” “stateof-the-art,” “The Figure 1 (b)”). Equation punctuation is inconsistent; some symbols are introduced without definitions (e.g., θ− usage in this A3C context).
- Ablations: While some ablations are reported (pixel reconstruction vs input change prediction), comprehensive ablations are missing: the contribution of value replay vs reward prediction vs pixel control vs feature control on both domains; effect of replay prioritization; replacing Q-learning with actor-critic for auxiliary control; removing shared LSTM vs shared CNN only.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Provide a principled discussion of representational bias introduced by auxiliary tasks, including either theoretical framing or targeted experiments showing minimal policy bias (e.g., frozen encoder vs trainable encoder comparisons, or isolating gradient contributions).
- Clarify and justify off-policy design choices: importance sampling or retrace-style corrections, target network usage and update schedule, replay ratios, and stability diagnostics. Include sensitivity analyses for replay buffer size and sampling distribution.
- Standardize evaluation: fixed training budget (frames), identical protocols to baselines (sticky actions; no-op starts), report per-game and aggregate results with confidence intervals. Avoid post hoc top-k selection as the main result; provide averages across seeds/hyperparameters with error bars.
- Reproducibility: release code, hyperparameter search ranges and selection criteria, and the Labyrinth environment (or an open equivalent), including human evaluation protocol details and raw scores.
- Clarify architectural choices: rationale for final ReLU on Qaux, reward scaling/normalization for pixel control across games, details of feature-control reward (sign, normalization), and whether rewards are clipped.
- Improve writing and formatting: fix references; proofread for typos; provide algorithm pseudocode; ensure section numbering consistency; disambiguate “2k” as 2,000.

Questions for the authors
1) How do you justify the claim that reward prediction trained on a skewed distribution does not bias the policy/value when sharing the encoder? Can you provide experiments isolating this effect?
2) What off-policy corrections (if any) are used for the auxiliary control Q-learning? How are target networks maintained and updated? Did you observe instability without them?
3) Why impose a ReLU on Qaux? For feature control, are target activations guaranteed nonnegative? Did you try leaving Q-values unconstrained?
4) Please clarify the Atari evaluation protocol and ensure parity with PD-DQN (training frames, sticky actions, life-loss termination, preprocessing). Provide per-game results and CIs.
5) The replay buffer is only 2,000 frames. How sensitive are results to buffer size and sampling frequency? What is the ratio of replay to on-policy updates?
6) In Labyrinth human-normalization, how many human subjects, what training, and what variance? How are episode lengths controlled across agents and humans?
7) There is an inconsistency: 50 vs 45 hyperparameter settings. Which is correct? How many random seeds per hyperparameter? How were top-3 selected?
8) Can you provide ablations separating contributions of value replay, reward prediction, pixel control, and feature control on both domains?
