- Proposes UNREAL: an A3C agent augmented with off-policy auxiliary control and reward-prediction tasks to improve representations and speed/stabilize policy learning.
- Introduces two auxiliary classes: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control via n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards) and reward-focused tasks (skewed immediate reward prediction and value-function replay).
- Auxiliary tasks are trained from a small replay buffer, decoupled from on-policy actor-critic updates, sharing convolutional and recurrent encoders.
- Empirical results: on 13 Labyrinth levels UNREAL more than doubles A3C’s mean human-normalized performance and achieves ≈10× data-efficiency gains; also shows improved robustness.
- On 57 Atari games UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean/median human-normalized performance and robustness.
- Ablations indicate pixel control outperforms input-change prediction and reconstruction, with feature control providing additional benefit.
- Decision: Accept.
- Key reason: well-motivated idea to use off-policy auxiliary control and reward prediction to shape shared representations without biasing policy/value targets.
- Key reason: convincing empirical evidence across diverse domains with thorough ablations and robustness analyses showing large performance and efficiency gains.
- Addresses the problem of sparse/complex visual rewards by densifying learning signals for representation learning and safely using skewed reward sampling for prediction.
- Carefully situated relative to prior work (options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, model/auxiliary prediction) with novelty in the combined auxiliary approach.
- Methodologically clear: joint loss, shared CNN+LSTM, deconvolutional Q-head with dueling parameterization, and replay-driven off-policy updates are documented.
- Empirical support is broad: Labyrinth and Atari suites, hyperparameter robustness plots, many hyperparameter runs, and implementation details for reproducibility.
- Caveat: fairness of compute/sample comparison—UNREAL performs many auxiliary updates per environment step, so wall-clock vs. sample-efficiency claims need clarification.
- Caveat: ablation breadth—more systematic quantification of each component’s marginal contribution and confidence intervals across seeds would strengthen claims.
- Caveat: sensitivity—pixel control requires different λPC across domains, suggesting sensitivity to reward/pixel scaling and other hyperparameters.
- Suggested improvement: report both environment frames and wall-clock time and normalize auxiliary update counts across methods.
- Suggested improvement: provide per-game or aggregated Atari ablation summaries to show which tasks help which game types.
- Suggested improvement: include confidence intervals or interquartile ranges for final performance across seeds.
- Suggested improvement: expand feature control analysis (combinations with other auxiliaries and interpretability of controlled units).
- Suggested improvement: analyze interference/compatibility between on-policy actor-critic and off-policy auxiliary Q-learning (e.g., gradient similarity, shared vs. separate LSTM).
- Suggested improvement: clarify evaluation protocol for human-normalized scores (evaluation episodes, stochasticity treatment, life termination handling).
- Questions for authors: how many auxiliary optimization steps per environment frame and how does this compare to A3C’s update count and wall-clock time?
- Questions for authors: sensitivity of performance to λPC, λRP, λVR, replay buffer size, and pixel grid resolution; guidance for defaults on new domains?
- Questions for authors: does reward prediction help in dense-reward or visually simple games, and is class balance adjusted per domain?
- Questions for authors: is there an A3C + value-replay-only baseline to isolate replay effects on value learning?
- Questions for authors: which layer/units are best for feature control and does combining feature and pixel control yield additive gains?
- Questions for authors: can auxiliary control policies induce harmful representation biases (e.g., overemphasizing motion) that impair certain tasks, and how is this mitigated?
