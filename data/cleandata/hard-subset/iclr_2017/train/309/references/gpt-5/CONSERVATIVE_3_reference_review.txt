1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The auxiliary tasks include: (i) off-policy control heads trained via n-step Q-learning to maximize pixel changes (and preliminarily, feature activations), (ii) reward prediction trained on a replay buffer rebalanced to over-sample rewarding events, and (iii) replayed value function updates. A combined loss jointly trains the shared representation while keeping the policy/value updates on-policy. Empirically, the authors report large gains over A3C on 13 DeepMind Labyrinth levels (mean human-normalized 87% vs 54%, with ~10x data-efficiency improvements) and improved Atari performance across 57 games (880% mean, 250% median human-normalized), with several ablations suggesting that control-style auxiliary tasks are more effective than reconstruction or immediate change prediction.

2. Decision: Accept (with reservations)
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical improvements across two domains using established RL components, with ablations and some robustness analyses.
- The auxiliary-task framing is well-motivated as a representation-learning aid for sparse rewards, and leverages off-policy learning for data efficiency without directly biasing the on-policy policy gradient.

3. Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Motivation and placement: The work connects clearly to UVFA, successor representations, Horde, and model/prediction-based auxiliary tasks. The contribution—control-style auxiliary tasks optimized off-policy and reward-focused auxiliary prediction—is a coherent, incremental advancement grounded in prior literature.
- Methodological soundness: The integration of off-policy Q-learning heads with an on-policy A3C core is reasonable; the loss decomposition (on-policy vs. replay) is explicit; architectural choices are described in detail.
- Empirical scope: Results on both 3D partially observed environments and Atari, with ablations (pixel control vs. prediction vs. reconstruction; preliminary feature control) and a hyperparameter sweep analysis for robustness, are valuable.
- Data efficiency: The reported >10x reduction in steps to reach A3C’s best performance on Labyrinth is compelling for practitioners.

Weaknesses/concerns
- Evaluation rigor and baseline fairness:
  - Atari protocol details are insufficient: number of training frames, evaluation regime (no-op/human starts, sticky actions), number of evaluation episodes, and statistical uncertainty are not fully specified. Comparisons to Prioritized Dueling DQN may not be apples-to-apples without matched training budgets and evaluation settings.
  - Human-normalized means can be dominated by outliers; while medians are reported, additional robust metrics (e.g., capped or log-normalized) would strengthen claims.
- Ablations across domains: While Labyrinth ablations are informative, the contribution of each auxiliary component on Atari is not isolated. Feature control is only preliminarily evaluated on one level; yet it is positioned as a core auxiliary task.
- Sensitivity to auxiliary losses: Robustness analyses focus on learning rate and entropy cost; sensitivity to λPC, λVR, λRP is not systematically explored (authors note λPC effects). Given the added complexity, this is important for reproducibility.
- Potential unintended incentives: Pixel-control may encourage indiscriminate motion (maximizing visual change) rather than task-relevant behaviors; more analysis (e.g., behavior diagnostics, counterexamples where motion is detrimental) would increase confidence that auxiliary control doesn’t harm base-task performance.
- Reproducibility: Asynchronous training, replay buffers, and multiple heads introduce many implementation degrees of freedom; code release or stricter reporting of seeds/variability would help.

4. Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the decision)
- Report standardized Atari evaluation (training frames, no-op vs. human starts, sticky actions), number of test episodes, and confidence intervals; include per-game plots with mean±SE over multiple seeds for top configurations.
- Provide per-component ablations on Atari and a sensitivity study over λPC, λVR, λRP (and buffer size). Consider an analysis of compute/parameter overhead to ensure gains aren’t primarily due to capacity.
- Clarify how many off-policy updates per environment step are used and whether this is matched across baselines; discuss stability (e.g., divergence checks) when mixing on- and off-policy updates.
- Analyze whether pixel-control gains persist in low-motion or flicker-prone games, and whether the auxiliary reward normalization differs across games.
- Consider additional auxiliary tasks that are reward-aligned (e.g., value or advantage prediction at multiple horizons) and compare to depth/semantic prediction baselines in Labyrinth.
- Provide failure cases where auxiliary tasks hurt; discuss mitigation (e.g., scheduling or uncertainty-gated auxiliary updates).

Questions for the authors
- Atari protocol: What is the exact training budget (total frames), evaluation protocol (no-op/human starts, sticky actions), number of evaluation episodes per run, and aggregation across seeds? Are the Dueling DQN numbers from matched budgets/settings?
- Off-policy update schedule: How many replay/auxiliary updates per 20-step on-policy update? Is the wall-clock compute matched to A3C baselines?
- Auxiliary weight sensitivity: How sensitive are results to λPC, λVR, λRP? Can you provide per-domain sweeps and guidance for practitioners?
- Pixel control behavior: Do agents learn to induce gratuitous motion to increase pixel change? Any diagnostics on action distributions or environment-specific degradation?
- Feature control: Can you provide broader results (Labyrinth and Atari) and clarify which hidden layers/units are controlled? How does feature-control compare/compose with pixel-control?
- Replay buffer: Why 2k most recent steps? How do results vary with buffer size and prioritization strategy?
- Generalization: On procedural Labyrinth levels, can you show performance on held-out seeds/maps to demonstrate generalization beyond training distributions?
- Reproducibility: Will code or sufficient pseudo-code and hyperparameter details be released to replicate the results?
