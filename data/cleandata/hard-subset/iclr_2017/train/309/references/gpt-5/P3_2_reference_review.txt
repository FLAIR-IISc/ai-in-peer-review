Summary
The paper proposes UNREAL, an actor-critic agent that augments A3C with three auxiliary objectives trained from a short replay buffer: (1) off-policy auxiliary control via n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards defined over pixel-change (and, in one experiment, feature control), (2) reward prediction of immediate reward using skewed sampling that over-represents rewarding events, and (3) value function replay. All objectives share a common convolutional encoder and LSTM with the base policy/value networks; auxiliary heads can be feedforward with deconvolutional architecture for spatial control. The paper claims that these auxiliary tasks shape representations to improve data efficiency, robustness, and final performance. Empirically, UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C on 13 Labyrinth tasks (mean human-normalized from 54% to 87%, ~10x fewer steps to reach A3C’s best performance) and improves over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN on 57 Atari games. Ablations suggest pixel/feature control and reward prediction each contribute; reconstruction or simple change prediction underperform control.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Well-motivated and novel integration of off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused auxiliary prediction into an on-policy recurrent actor-critic, with strong empirical gains in challenging 3D partially observable domains and Atari.
- Evidence of improved data efficiency and robustness, with ablations and multiple seeds/hyperparameters, addressing common RL concerns around instability and sensitivity.

Supporting arguments
- Problem framing: Sparse reward and partial observability require strong representation learning; auxiliary tasks can densify training signals without biasing the policy if used only for representation. The paper operationalizes this through pixel/feature control and skewed reward prediction, trained off-policy from recent experience, and shows how to combine on-policy and off-policy losses coherently.
- Positioning: The work is well situated relative to options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model-based/auxiliary prediction approaches. The distinction that pseudo-reward policies are not executed but used as auxiliary learning targets is clear and practically important.
- Methodology: The auxiliary control formulation is clear (cell-wise pixel-change rewards, dueling deconv heads; analogous setup for feature control). Reward prediction’s skewed sampling is justified for sparsity, and decoupling it from policy/value avoids direct policy bias. Value replay is simple and synergistic with new features. Architecture details and hyperparameters are sufficiently specified.
- Empirical support: Labyrinth is a challenging benchmark for POMDP control with rich visuals; the 10x speed-up and robustness improvements are meaningful. Atari results show broad improvements and hyperparameter-robust behavior. Ablations (pixel control vs change prediction vs reconstruction) support the claim that learning to control is superior to passive prediction; reward prediction and replay further help.
- Rigor: The use of many random hyperparameter settings and seeds is commendable; additional plots on robustness across hyperparameters/seeds help address variance concerns.

Concerns and limitations (impact the strength but not the validity of the contribution)
- Fairness of comparisons and isolation of effects: The base A3C baseline does not use replay, whereas UNREAL includes value replay and off-policy auxiliary learning. A control baseline “A3C + value replay (no auxiliary)” is needed to quantify how much improvement stems from replay alone. Similarly, a version with reward prediction but no control would help attribute gains on Atari.
- Ablation coverage: Feature control is only shown preliminarily on a subset of Labyrinth tasks; the main Atari results omit it. Full factorial ablations across components (PC, FC, RP, VR) on a broader set would strengthen the causal story.
- Stability and interference: Combining on-policy actor-critic with off-policy Q-learning in a shared network can introduce gradient interference and instability. While results suggest it works, analysis of gradient scales, loss normalizations, and stability metrics (e.g., divergence rates, sensitivity to λ weights) is missing.
- Potential bias from skewed reward prediction: Over-sampling rewarding transitions for the reward predictor may shape features in ways that could hurt dense-reward tasks; sensitivity analyses across dense vs sparse tasks are not reported.
- Evaluation details on Atari: Important protocol details are missing (training frames per game, evaluation protocol—no-op starts, sticky actions, number of evaluation episodes). Reporting these would improve reproducibility and fairness relative to prior work.
- Environment availability: Labyrinth seems proprietary or nonstandard; reproducibility would benefit from releasing environments and code.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all affect the accept decision)
- Include an A3C+replay baseline and a full component ablation on a representative subset of Atari.
- Report training frames, wall-clock time, and evaluation settings (no-op, sticky actions, life termination) for Atari; align to ALE best practices.
- Provide sensitivity to λPC, λVR, λRP and to the frequency/ratio of auxiliary updates.
- Analyze representation changes: e.g., how pixel/feature control affects feature activations and saliency around reward-predictive cues; quantify whether auxiliary tasks reduce value approximation error.
- Consider off-policy corrections (e.g., Retrace) or target networks for auxiliary Q-learning to discuss/ensure stability.
- Discuss generalization: do learned auxiliary-control representations transfer across tasks with different reward functions/maps? A small transfer experiment would be informative.
- Explore unifying auxiliary control under a parameterized pseudo-reward embedding (UVFA/successor features) to reduce the hand-design of pixel grids and scale to richer pseudo-reward families.

Questions for the authors
- How much of the gain is attributable to value replay alone? Can you report an A3C+VR baseline and A3C+RP (no PC/FC) on Labyrinth and Atari?
- What are the exact Atari evaluation protocols (training frames, test episodes, no-op starts, sticky actions), and how do your training budgets compare to Prioritized Dueling DQN?
- How sensitive is performance to λPC, λVR, λRP and the number/frequency of auxiliary updates? Do you normalize auxiliary losses to balance gradients?
- Did you observe instability from the off-policy Q-learning heads? Any mitigations (target networks, gradient clipping per head)?
- Does reward prediction’s skewed sampling degrade performance on dense-reward games? Have you tried adaptive skewing?
- For pixel control, how robust is performance to grid resolution/crop choices and to environments with high exogenous flicker/dynamic textures?
- Can you release the Labyrinth levels and code to facilitate reproducibility?
