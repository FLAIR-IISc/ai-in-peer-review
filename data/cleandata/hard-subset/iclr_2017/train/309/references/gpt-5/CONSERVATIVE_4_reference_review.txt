Summary
This paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks trained off-policy from a small replay buffer to improve representation learning and data efficiency. The auxiliary tasks include (i) auxiliary control via Q-learning to maximize pixel-change (and preliminarily, feature activations), (ii) immediate reward prediction trained on rebalanced replay to over-sample rare rewards, and (iii) value function replay. The approach keeps the primary policy/value learning on-policy while leveraging off-policy updates for auxiliary objectives, sharing visual and recurrent features. Experiments on 13 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games show substantial gains over A3C in final performance, data efficiency (up to 10× fewer frames to reach A3C final score), and robustness to hyperparameters; the method also surpasses Prioritized Dueling DQN on Atari. Ablations suggest pixel-control is more effective than input reconstruction or simple change prediction, with preliminary evidence that feature-control is competitive.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical improvements across diverse domains (3D partial observability and Atari), including robustness and data-efficiency gains, with ablations supporting the core design choices.
- Methodologically conservative extension: builds on established A3C and Q-learning with a clean auxiliary-task formulation, making it practical and impactful.

Supporting arguments
- Scope and rigor of evaluation: 13 Labyrinth tasks spanning navigation, exploration, and combat; 57 Atari games with broad hyperparameter sweeps. Results consistently favor UNREAL, including median and mean human-normalized scores and learning speedups.
- Clear architectural rationale: On-policy A3C handles partial observability; off-policy auxiliary tasks efficiently train perceptual and control-relevant features using recent experience and simple feedforward heads, without entangling auxiliary targets with policy/value updates.
- Ablations: Pixel-control > change prediction > reconstruction, matching the hypothesis that learning to control informative aspects of the perceptual stream shapes more useful features than passive prediction or reconstruction. Preliminary feature-control is promising and coherent with the representation-learning motivation.
- Practicality: Replay is small and recent (2k), simplifying stability concerns; losses are combined with simple weights; implementation details provided.

Concerns and requests for clarification (impact not decisive)
- Fairness and compute: Auxiliary heads add parameters and learning updates. Are comparisons compute-matched (e.g., equal wall-clock budget) or frame-matched only? Please report both environment frames and wall-clock training time; clarify whether all baselines and UNREAL use identical hardware/thread counts and optimization settings.
- Statistical rigor: Most plots appear to show top-k hyperparameter settings, with aggregate plots in the appendix. Please provide confidence intervals/standard errors for the main reported numbers (final performance and sample efficiency) using multiple seeds per chosen hyperparameter, and report mean/median across all seeds/hparams prominently (not only in the appendix).
- Evaluation protocol details:
  - Atari: training frames, evaluation episodes/length, sticky actions, life-loss handling, and score aggregation rules. Ensure baselines are tuned under the same protocol with comparable hyperparameter sweeps.
  - Labyrinth human normalization: define human evaluation protocol (number of participants, training time, episode budget, variance).
- Off-policy with recurrence: Auxiliary Q-learning heads rely on LSTM features. How is the recurrent state handled in replay updates? Do you replay sequences to reconstruct hidden states or store and reuse hidden states? Please specify truncation lengths and target network update cadence (θ−) for stability.
- Reward prediction rebalancing: While the predictor is auxiliary, its shared CNN/LSTM features may bias representation. Provide evidence that rebalancing does not degrade performance when rewards are extremely sparse or when non-rewarding dynamics dominate; a “no-RP” ablation is partially shown but more systematic results would help.
- Sensitivity and generality: λPC ranges differ across Labyrinth and Atari and are sampled from broad log-uniform ranges. Please report sensitivity analyses for λPC, λVR, λRP; and discuss portability to continuous control or non-visual domains where pixel change is ill-defined.
- Baseline breadth: Consider stronger unsupervised/auxiliary baselines (e.g., curiosity/intrinsic motivation, forward dynamics prediction) and compute-matched comparisons. The reconstruction baseline here appears disadvantaged and might not reflect the best current predictive-model auxiliaries.
- Dynamic textures: Labyrinth has animated textures; pixel-change reward could be dominated by irrelevant visual flicker. Provide diagnostics showing learned pixel-control focuses on task-relevant changes (e.g., saliency or cell-level Qaux visualizations) and does not induce harmful behavior.

Questions for the authors
1. Compute parity: Are results matched in wall-clock and parameter count? What is the relative training time per frame for UNREAL vs A3C?
2. Exact training budgets: How many frames per game on Atari? How many episodes/frames per Labyrinth level? Evaluation episode counts and selection criteria?
3. Recurrent replay: How are LSTM states reconstructed during off-policy Q-learning and value replay? Sequence lengths used and impact on stability?
4. Target networks: What is θ− update frequency for auxiliary Q-learning heads? Any double Q or other stabilizers?
5. Sensitivity: How do results vary with replay buffer size (2k), grid size for pixel control, and weighting coefficients? Any failure modes when rewards are extremely sparse?
6. Robustness across domains: Have you tried feature-control without pixel-control broadly across Atari? Does it close the gap to pixel-control? Any results in non-visual or continuous-control tasks?
7. Human-normalization: Precise human evaluation methodology and variance estimates?
8. Code/data: Will you release code and training/evaluation scripts to ensure reproducibility?

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper)
- Promote the aggregate-over-seeds/hyperparameters results to the main text and include confidence intervals for key claims (final score, sample efficiency).
- Add compute/time efficiency analysis and parameter counts to address fairness concerns.
- Expand ablations to include feature-control more broadly and stronger predictive-model auxiliaries; report per-game/per-level tables alongside human-normalized summaries.
- Clarify implementation details around recurrent replay and target networks for the auxiliary heads.
- Improve reference formatting and ensure all citations are complete and consistent.

Overall, the paper is a well-motivated and carefully engineered extension of established methods with broad empirical validation. With clarifications and stronger statistical and fairness reporting, it would make a solid contribution.
