1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of an A3C agent with a suite of auxiliary tasks designed to shape representations and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key ideas are: (i) auxiliary control via off-policy n-step Q-learning for pseudo-rewards (pixel control and, preliminarily, feature control), (ii) an auxiliary reward-prediction task trained with replay and class rebalancing to densify learning signals without biasing the policy/value updates, and (iii) value-function replay. These are combined into a single loss optimized end-to-end while keeping the main policy/value updates on-policy (A3C). Empirically, UNREAL significantly improves performance, learning speed, and hyperparameter robustness on 13 visually rich Labyrinth 3D tasks, and also achieves strong gains on 57 Atari games. Ablations in Labyrinth support the claim that control-driven auxiliary tasks outperform purely predictive ones, and that combining auxiliary tasks yields further gains.

2) Decision: Accept
Primary reasons:
- Strong empirical improvements in both performance and data efficiency on challenging 3D tasks, with evidence of robustness across hyperparameters, and competitive (state-of-the-art) results on Atari.
- Well-motivated and carefully integrated methodology that connects representation learning (via auxiliary tasks and replay) with on-policy actor-critic, with ablations that isolate the value of key components.

3) Supporting arguments
What is the problem? The paper tackles how to learn better representations for deep RL agents in sparse-reward, visually complex environments so as to improve data efficiency, stability, and ultimate performance.

Motivation and placement in literature: The use of pseudo-reward–driven auxiliary control connects to options/temporal abstraction, Horde/UVFA, and successor representations, but differs in that auxiliary policies are not used directly for behavior; rather they serve to shape shared representations. Reward prediction relates to model-based/predictive auxiliary tasks, but here is deliberately de-biased from policy learning by training off the behavior distribution with rebalanced replay. The paper clearly positions these choices against prior work on predictive modeling, reconstruction, and auxiliary predictions in 3D RL, and provides empirical comparisons (e.g., control vs prediction vs reconstruction).

Do the results support the claims? On Labyrinth, UNREAL roughly doubles mean human-normalized performance vs A3C and achieves up to 10–18× fewer environment steps to A3C’s best performance. Ablations indicate: pixel control > input change prediction > reconstruction; preliminary feature control is close to pixel control. Robustness analyses across learning rate/entropy show smoother performance landscapes and improved median outcomes. On Atari, the reported mean/median human-normalized improvements over A3C and dueling DQN are substantial, with additional robustness to hyperparameters. Implementation details are sufficiently specified to understand the approach (architectures, replay, loss weights, action repeat). Overall, evidence is compelling that auxiliary control and reward prediction materially improve representation learning and sample efficiency.

4) Additional feedback and questions to help improve the paper (not all affect the accept decision)
- Compute budget and fairness:
  - The method performs extra replay updates (value replay, control Q-learning, reward prediction). Please report wall-clock and total gradient-update budgets versus A3C to disentangle data efficiency from compute efficiency. A controlled ablation that equalizes update counts would strengthen the claim.
- Atari evaluation protocol:
  - Clarify the evaluation details (no-op starts, terminal-on-life-loss vs game-over, sticky actions, frame-skip, number of evaluation episodes, score aggregation). Ensure comparisons to prior A3C and dueling DQN use the same protocol.
- Hyperparameter selection and reporting:
  - You report averages over the top-3 agents and also show robustness plots over all hyperparameters. Please add summary statistics across all runs (mean/median ± std) and reconcile the “50” vs “45” hyperparameter counts mentioned. Consider reporting best-single-seed vs median-over-seeds to reduce selection bias.
- Ablations on Atari:
  - The Labyrinth ablations are convincing. Could you also include Atari ablations for pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay (and their combinations), to show generality beyond 3D tasks?
- Sensitivity and design choices:
  - Pixel control: How sensitive are results to the grid resolution, reward normalization, and the use of absolute frame differences? Why ReLU on Qaux (non-negativity is reasonable given the reward definition, but please comment on potential downsides)?
  - Replay: The buffer is relatively small (2k). How sensitive are results to buffer size and sampling ratios? How frequently are target parameters θ− updated?
  - Loss weights: λPC is sampled; λVR and λRP are fixed to 1. Please provide a brief sensitivity analysis and rationale for these choices.
  - Reward prediction rebalancing: Do you oversample both positive and negative rewards to 50% vs zero? In tasks with dense negative rewards, does this skew harm representations?
- Off-policy stability:
  - You use n-step Q-learning off-policy for auxiliary control with shared representation. Any observed instability or interference with on-policy A3C? Did you try importance sampling or retrace-style corrections, and are they necessary?
- Feature control:
  - Preliminary results suggest it is competitive with pixel control. Including more systematic feature-control experiments (and possibly combining feature and pixel control) would broaden the contribution.
- Generalization and transfer:
  - Since auxiliary control tasks encourage learning controllable aspects of the environment, do the learned representations transfer across levels or tasks (e.g., unseen Labyrinth maps or altered visuals)? Even a small study would be valuable.
- Additional analysis:
  - Qualitative visualizations of what the auxiliary heads attend to are helpful; more quantitative diagnostics (e.g., correlation between reward-prediction accuracy and policy learning speed) could illuminate mechanisms.

Questions for the authors
1) How many auxiliary updates are performed per environment step on average, and how does performance change if you constrain total updates to match A3C?
2) What exact Atari evaluation protocol did you follow, and is it identical to the baselines you compare to?
3) Can you provide ablations on Atari isolating pixel control, value replay, and reward prediction?
4) How sensitive is UNREAL to replay buffer size and sampling skew for reward prediction?
5) What is the update schedule for θ− in the auxiliary Q-learning heads, and did you observe instability without target networks?
6) Did you try auxiliary control rewards other than absolute pixel change (e.g., optical flow magnitude, temporal-difference of learned features), and how do they compare?
7) Can you comment on representation transfer to new layouts or visuals, or to tasks with non-visual inputs?
