Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, a deep RL architecture that augments an on-policy recurrent A3C agent with off-policy auxiliary tasks optimized from a short replay buffer. The central idea is to use pseudo-reward control tasks (pixel control and feature control) trained via n-step Q-learning to learn representations that are sensitive to controllable aspects of the environment, together with reward-focused auxiliary tasks (immediate reward prediction and value replay) to de-sparsify reward features and stabilize value learning. A shared CNN-LSTM trunk supports both the base policy/value and auxiliary heads; auxiliary losses are computed off-policy from recent experience, with rebalanced sampling for reward prediction. Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance on DeepMind Lab (13 levels) and Atari (57 games), with ablations showing pixel/feature control outperform input reconstruction or simple change prediction. The paper’s pitch is a unifying recipe that reliably improves A3C in visually rich, partially observed settings.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains in both data efficiency and final performance across diverse domains, with reasonable ablations and robustness analyses.
- Clear, well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks that is distinct from prior work (Horde/UVFA) by using pseudo-reward policies purely as auxiliary objectives for representation learning.

Supporting arguments
1) Problem tackled: How to improve representation learning and sample efficiency for deep RL agents in visually complex, partially observed environments with sparse rewards. The approach promotes learning features that are both controllable and reward-predictive, without biasing the base policy’s objective.

2) Motivation and placement in literature: The paper situates itself relative to options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations, emphasizing a different use-case: auxiliary pseudo-rewards are not executed as behavior, but shape shared representations. It also contrasts with model-based auxiliary predictions and recent auxiliary prediction tasks (e.g., depth, feature detection). The framing as multi-task/auxiliary objectives trained off-policy on recent experience is appropriate and novel at the time in combination with recurrent A3C and deconv spatial control heads.

3) Do results support claims? Largely yes.
- DeepMind Lab: UNREAL doubles mean/median human-normalized performance over A3C and achieves ~10x speedups to A3C’s final score, with improvements on most levels, including challenging navigation and laser-tag. Robustness to hyperparameters improves, as shown by hyperparameter sweeps and aggregate performance plots.
- Atari: Gains over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean/median performance and hyperparameter robustness. While pixel control is less uniformly helpful on Atari, the variant without pixel control still improves A3C.
- Ablations: Pixel control > change prediction > reconstruction; feature control performs comparably to pixel control on at least one level. Value replay and reward prediction provide additional benefits.

Methodological soundness: Mixing on-policy policy gradients with off-policy value-based auxiliary updates is a reasonable engineering choice; the paper isolates policy optimization from skewed replay by not feeding reward prediction directly into the policy/value objectives. The auxiliary control Q-learning trains on the same shared features, leveraging data efficiency. The pixel control head’s deconv/dueling design is well motivated for spatial tasks. The replay buffer is short and recent (2k), limiting non-stationarity.

Rigor and reporting: The paper provides architecture/training details, hyperparameter sampling, and multiple seeds via asynchronous threads; robustness plots average over large sweeps. However, some reporting choices (e.g., “top-3” curves, small replay buffer, and human normalization details) could be more transparent.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Fairness and evaluation protocol:
  - Clarify compute budgets (number of frames, wall-clock, number of actors/CPUs/GPUs) and ensure baselines receive comparable resources and hyperparameter tuning budgets.
  - For Atari, specify evaluation details (no-op starts, sticky actions, frame-skipping, life-loss handling) and confirm consistency with DQN/A3C protocols.
  - Human-normalized score computation in Labyrinth: detail human training time, skill level, and variance.
- Ablations and sensitivity:
  - Provide a more granular ablation isolating each component (value replay, reward prediction, pixel control, feature control) across several Labyrinth levels and a subset of Atari games, with confidence intervals across seeds.
  - Report sensitivity to λPC, λRP, λVR and replay buffer size. Are improvements robust for smaller buffers or different replay skews?
  - Show outcomes where pixel control hurts (if any), and whether gating or scheduling auxiliary losses mitigates negative transfer.
- Understanding representation effects:
  - Provide diagnostics: e.g., linear probes for reward-related features with and without auxiliary tasks; CCA/CKA similarity between representations.
  - Visualize what feature control units learn to “control”; does this align with semantically meaningful factors (e.g., goal objects, doors, enemies)?
- Generalization:
  - Test transfer/generalization: train on subset of procedural seeds or textures and evaluate on held-out seeds/textures. Does auxiliary control improve out-of-distribution performance?
  - Assess whether reward-prediction rebalancing leads to overfitting to short-horizon cues.
- Comparisons:
  - Include additional intrinsic motivation baselines where feasible (e.g., count-based bonuses in pixel space or forward/inverse dynamics prediction), even if preliminary, to contextualize benefits of control-based auxiliary tasks.

Questions for the authors
1) How sensitive are results to the replay buffer size (2k) and the recency of samples? Would larger buffers or prioritized replay by TD-error change outcomes?
2) What are the exact Atari evaluation settings (sticky actions, no-op starts, frame skip, life termination)? How many training frames per game? Are the gains still present under sticky actions?
3) For Labyrinth human normalization, how many human trials per level and what variance? Are improvements significant across seeds?
4) Do auxiliary control losses bias behavior toward motion (due to pixel change rewards)? Any evidence of undesirable behaviors or interference with the base policy? How is this mitigated (e.g., λPC schedules)?
5) Can you report per-game/level performance with confidence intervals across independent seeds (not just hyperparameter sweeps)? How often does UNREAL underperform A3C?
6) Feature control: when combined with pixel control and reward prediction, does it add further gains? If not included in the main UNREAL, why?
7) Does reward prediction balancing at P(r≠0)=0.5 hold across games with very sparse rewards? Any instability when rewards are extremely rare?
8) Compute and reproducibility: please provide wall-clock training times, hardware details, and, if possible, code or pseudocode to facilitate replication.

Overall, this is a well-motivated, empirically strong contribution that demonstrates a practical recipe for improving deep RL agents via off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks.
