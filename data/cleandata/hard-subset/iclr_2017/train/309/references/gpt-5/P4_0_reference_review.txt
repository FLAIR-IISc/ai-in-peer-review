1. Summary of claims and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an RL agent that augments an on-policy A3C learner with off-policy auxiliary tasks designed to shape representations for faster and more robust learning. The main auxiliary components are:
- Auxiliary control tasks: policies trained via n-step Q-learning from a replay buffer to maximise pseudo-rewards, notably pixel control (maximise local pixel change) and feature control (maximise selected hidden unit activations).
- Reward-focused auxiliary task: immediate reward prediction trained on skewed replay that oversamples rare reward events to densify supervision without biasing the policy/value learning objectives.
- Value function replay: additional value regression from replayed sequences to exploit improved features.
These are combined in a single loss with shared perception and recurrent state. The approach is evaluated on 13 DeepMind Lab (Labyrinth) levels and 57 Atari games, with extensive ablations. Reported outcomes include substantially higher final performance, up to ~10x data efficiency improvements, and greater robustness to hyperparameters relative to A3C and, on Atari, superior mean/median human-normalized scores versus Prioritized Dueling DQN.

2. Decision
Accept.
Key reasons: (i) Strong empirical gains across diverse domains with careful ablations and robustness analyses; (ii) A clear and well-motivated integration of auxiliary control and reward prediction with on-policy RL that advances representation learning for sparse-reward, visually rich environments.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the three key questions:

- Problem tackled: Improve data efficiency, stability, and final performance of deep RL in partially observed, high-dimensional visual environments by learning representations through auxiliary tasks that increase the density and diversity of learning signals.

- Motivation and placement in the literature: The work is well positioned relative to options, Horde/GVFs, UVFAs, successor representations, and model/auxiliary prediction approaches. The paper’s emphasis is not on using learned pseudo-reward policies for hierarchical control, but on using control-based auxiliary objectives (pixel/feature control) and reward prediction to shape shared features. Combining off-policy auxiliary control with an on-policy recurrent agent and replay is clearly motivated and distinguishes this work from prior prediction-only auxiliary tasks.

- Support for claims and rigor:
  - Experimental breadth: Results on 13 Lab levels (including static/randomized navigation and laser-tag) and 57 Atari games are broad. Human-normalized scores, learning speed comparisons, and robustness to hyperparameters (learning rate, entropy cost) are presented.
  - Ablations: The paper isolates contributions of pixel control, feature control (preliminary), reward prediction, and value replay. Baselines like reconstruction and input-change prediction are included, supporting the claim that learning to control pixel changes is more effective than merely predicting or reconstructing input.
  - Methodological details: Losses, architectures (including deconvolutions for spatial Q-values), replay setup, and optimization are described with sufficient specificity to enable reproduction.
  - Soundness: Off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control from replay is appropriate. Reward prediction trained on skewed replay does not directly bias the policy/value updates. Sharing the convolutional encoder between auxiliary heads and the main agent is standard practice for representation learning.

Potential weaknesses/clarifications:
- Fairness/details of evaluation: While robustness across hyperparameters is shown, the selection of “top-3” curves per game and the comparison to external baselines (e.g., Prioritized Dueling DQN) could be sensitive to different training budgets or evaluation protocols. A single fixed training budget across methods is not explicitly quantified in the provided text.
- Theoretical grounding: There is limited theoretical analysis of stability when combining on-policy policy gradients with multiple off-policy auxiliary Q-learning losses. The approach is empirical but appears stable in practice.
- Auxiliary objective design: Pixel-change rewards may emphasize camera or texture motion rather than task-relevant changes; the paper partially addresses this via cropping and grid-based control but does not analyze failure modes.
- References and formatting: Some reference formatting is inconsistent; minor typos exist.

Overall, the breadth and strength of empirical evidence, together with ablations and robustness analyses, substantively support the main claims.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily decisive)
- Report standardized training budgets across all methods (e.g., environment frames, wall-clock, and compute) and uniform evaluation protocols (number of evaluation episodes, stochasticity such as sticky actions on Atari, life-loss handling, no-op/random starts).
- Provide results averaged over multiple seeds for each selected hyperparameter setting, and clarify how hyperparameters were chosen per game; include aggregate statistics (mean ± std) at fixed budgets to complement “top-k” plots.
- Include an ablation removing replay (i.e., on-policy-only auxiliary control) to quantify the importance of off-policy updates, and an ablation varying replay buffer size.
- Analyze the sensitivity to the auxiliary loss weights λPC, λVR, λRP, including potential gradient scaling/normalization to stabilize multi-task training.
- Expand feature-control experiments and consider combining pixel and feature control; provide insights into when each is most beneficial.
- Add qualitative analyses linking auxiliary performance to main-task learning (e.g., feature visualizations, correlation between auxiliary value improvements and main-task returns).
- Discuss computational overhead of auxiliary heads and replay, and the speed-performance trade-off.

Questions for the authors
- What is the exact training budget (environment frames per game/level, wall-clock, and hardware) for UNREAL vs A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in Atari and Labyrinth? Are comparisons done under identical budgets?
- What evaluation protocol is used on Atari (no-op or random starts, life termination as episode end, action-repeat, sticky actions)? How many evaluation episodes per checkpoint?
- How sensitive are results to replay buffer size (2k steps) and to the frequency of auxiliary updates? Did larger buffers help or hurt?
- How are auxiliary losses balanced during training? Were any gradient normalization or loss-scaling techniques used beyond fixed λ’s sampled from ranges? How stable is training across seeds for a fixed hyperparameter setting?
- For pixel control on Atari, how is normalization handled across games with different motion statistics beyond λPC ranges? Is there per-game reward scaling?
- Does skewing reward prediction to P(r ≠ 0) = 0.5 materially alter the learned representation in ways that could bias the policy indirectly? Any ablation with unskewed sampling?
- Can you provide results with feature control combined with other auxiliaries on more levels, and any qualitative evidence that feature control targets more task-relevant features than pixel control?
- Did you observe cases where auxiliary control emphasized irrelevant motion (e.g., animated textures) and degraded performance? Any mitigation strategies?
