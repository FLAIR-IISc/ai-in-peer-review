Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, a deep RL architecture that augments a strong on-policy agent (A3C with CNN-LSTM) with several auxiliary tasks trained off-policy from a short replay buffer to shape the shared representation and improve data efficiency and robustness. The key auxiliary tasks are: (1) auxiliary control via Q-learning over pseudo-rewards, notably pixel-control (maximize per-cell frame differences over a deconv head) and feature-control (maximize activations of internal units); (2) reward prediction from short history with a rebalanced replay sampler that over-represents rare rewarding events; and (3) value function replay (additional off-policy value regression). The overall loss combines on-policy A3C with these off-policy losses. Experiments on 13 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games show substantial gains over A3C in final score, learning speed, and hyperparameter robustness; also surpassing Prioritized Dueling DQN on Atari. Ablations demonstrate that control-based auxiliary tasks outperform mere prediction/reconstruction signals. The approach is simple, general, and computationally compatible with existing architectures.

Decision: Accept
Reasons:
1) Strong empirical improvements in both data efficiency and robustness across diverse domains; results are clear, consistent, and practically important.
2) A well-motivated and novel integration of off-policy auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction with an on-policy recurrent agent; ablations support the design choices.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper addresses learning better representations for RL in visually rich, partially observed, and often sparse-reward settings. The motivation to exploit auxiliary tasks that are aligned with controllable aspects of the environment (pixel/feature control) and with rewarding events (rebalanced reward prediction) is compelling and well-connected to prior work (UVFA, successor representations, Horde, model prediction, intrinsic motivation).
- Methodology: The joint loss and separation of on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (auxiliary Q-learning and replayed value/reward tasks) are sound and practically effective. Using deconv heads for spatially local auxiliary control is a smart architectural choice. The small replay buffer and prioritization for reward prediction are straightforward and efficient.
- Empirical support: On Labyrinth, UNREAL more than doubles human-normalized mean performance versus A3C (54% to 87%) and achieves up to 10×–18× speedups to reach A3C’s best score. On Atari, UNREAL achieves large mean/median human-normalized improvements and better hyperparameter robustness, while using the same base architecture and hyperparameter sweep protocol. Ablations indicate: pixel control > predicting immediate pixel change > reconstruction; preliminary feature-control results are also promising.
- Rigor and fairness: The paper evaluates over many seeds/hyperparameters, reports both mean and median, and includes ablations. Implementation details are thorough. The design avoids directly biasing the policy/value learning by confining skewed sampling to the auxiliary reward prediction head.

Weaknesses and concerns (do not preclude acceptance)
- Baselines and fairness: While A3C baselines are fairly treated, comparisons to Prioritized Dueling DQN rely on published numbers with different architectures/protocols (recurrent vs feedforward, on-policy vs off-policy); more careful discussion of compute budgets and evaluation protocols would help. Additionally, an ablation isolating “A3C + value replay only” and “A3C + reward prediction only” across all Labyrinth tasks would strengthen attribution (some are present, but a consolidated table would help).
- Novelty: The core novelty is in the practical integration and the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel/feature control) rather than in new theory. The paper is still impactful, but the conceptual ingredients build on established ideas (Horde, UVFA, intrinsic prediction).
- Generality and failure modes: Pixel control may over-emphasize visually dynamic but reward-irrelevant textures; the paper’s strong results suggest this is not harmful, but a more systematic analysis of representation focus would be useful.
- Statistical reporting: While robustness analyses are provided, clearer confidence intervals on final scores and sample efficiency metrics would aid scientific rigor.

Suggestions to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Provide a consolidated ablation table across Labyrinth showing A3C, +VR, +RP, +PC, +PC+RP, full UNREAL, and (if possible) feature control variants, with mean/median, speedup, and robustness metrics.
- Clarify off-policy stability details: Are target networks used for auxiliary Q-learning (θ−) per task head? How often are they updated?
- Discuss compute: wall-clock training time, number of environment frames per method, and overhead of auxiliary heads.
- Analyze representation: qualitative/quantitative evidence that auxiliary control focuses features on task-relevant dynamics rather than texture flicker (e.g., saliency, ablations with static/dynamic textures, or using grayscale/temporal smoothing for pixel control).
- Extend comparisons: include curiosity/intrinsic motivation baselines that predict next-frame features or novelty (given the related motivation), and depth-prediction or other auxiliary tasks where applicable.
- Report statistical uncertainty (95% CIs) on final performance and speedup metrics.
- Expand feature-control results and consider combining it with pixel control on more tasks.

Questions for the authors
1) Target networks and stability: For the auxiliary Q-learning losses, do you maintain separate target networks θ−? What update cadence? Did you observe instability without them?
2) Value replay vs reward prediction: What fraction of the gains (speed/score/robustness) comes from value replay alone versus reward prediction versus pixel control? Can you provide a full ablation table across all 13 levels?
3) Replay buffer size/sensitivity: Why 2k steps? How sensitive are results to buffer size and prioritization scheme for reward prediction? Did you try prioritized replay for value replay or pixel control?
4) Pixel control reward design: Dynamic textures in Labyrinth could dominate pixel-change rewards. Did you experiment with normalized or background-subtracted pixel change, optical flow, or downweighted high-frequency flicker? Any negative cases where pixel control hurts?
5) Fairness to DQN baselines: On Atari, are training frames and action-repeat protocols matched, and how does wall-clock compare? Did you evaluate A3C and UNREAL under the same compute/time budgets?
6) Feature control: Can you elaborate on which layer/units you control, and whether combining feature and pixel control yields complementary gains?
7) Reproducibility: Will you release code and environment configurations to facilitate replication?
