1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks designed to learn better representations and thereby improve data efficiency and robustness. The key ideas are: (i) auxiliary control tasks defined by pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control), trained off-policy with n-step Q-learning on replayed experience; (ii) a reward prediction task trained on rebalanced replay to oversample rare rewarding events; and (iii) value function replay to exploit improved features. These tasks share the encoder (and LSTM) with the base policy/value, shaping the representation without directly biasing the on-policy updates. The combined loss jointly trains all components. Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves learning speed and final performance over A3C on 13 3D Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, with extensive ablations showing the contribution of each component and comparisons to alternative auxiliary signals (reconstruction, change prediction). The paper reports up to 10× data-efficiency gains on Labyrinth, improved robustness to hyperparameters, and state-of-the-art Atari performance relative to A3C and Dueling DQN at the time.

2. Decision
Accept. Reasons:
- Strong empirical gains across two challenging domains with broad coverage and careful ablations substantiate the central claim that auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks greatly improve data efficiency and robustness.
- The approach is well-motivated and distinct from prior work: it uses off-policy auxiliary control purely for representation learning, combined with reward-focused rebalanced replay, in a clean and practical architecture.

3. Supporting arguments
On the questions:

- Problem tackled: How to learn representations for deep RL that mitigate sparse reward and improve data efficiency and robustness without changing the base policy optimization. The paper aims to exploit auxiliary predictions/control to shape features that support faster and more stable learning.

- Motivation and placement: The related work is well-covered (options, Horde, UVFA, SR, model-based prediction, auxiliary predictions in 3D RL). The novelty is not in introducing pseudo-rewards per se, but in using many off-policy control heads and reward prediction as auxiliary tasks for representation learning, decoupled from the base policy, and trained from replay while the base agent remains on-policy and recurrent. The pixel/feature control heads and reward rebalancing provide a specific, well-justified instantiation that differs from UVFA/SR (which typically target transfer across goals) and from Horde (which learned separate value functions without sharing representation for a primary task).

- Do results support claims? Empirical support is compelling:
  - Labyrinth: clear, sizeable gains in both speed and final performance, including human-normalized scores exceeding baseline A3C, with per-level breakdown and ablations (pixel control vs change prediction vs reconstruction; reward prediction; value replay). The unsupervised baselines show that “control” outperforms mere prediction or reconstruction, supporting the hypothesis.
  - Atari: improved mean/median human-normalized scores and robustness across 57 games; learning curves and hyperparameter sweeps support the robustness claim.
  - The architecture and training details are specified (loss weights, replay size, grid design, network heads), improving reproducibility.
  - Caveats: Theoretical guarantees are limited, and some confounds (additional compute via replayed updates; extra parameters) are not fully controlled. Nonetheless, sample-efficiency gains are measured in environment steps and not just wall-clock, and ablations isolate components reasonably well.

Strengths:
- Clear, simple idea with strong practical impact; broad and rigorous empirical evaluation and ablations.
- Sensible use of replay for auxiliary tasks while keeping the base policy on-policy, reducing bias concerns.
- Careful discussion of robustness to hyperparameters and randomness.

Weaknesses / concerns:
- Fairness of compute budget: UNREAL performs more gradient updates per environment step than vanilla A3C due to auxiliary and replayed updates. Wall-clock and update-normalized comparisons are not reported.
- The reward predictor is trained on a skewed distribution; though not used directly in the policy loss, its shared encoder could indirectly bias exploration. Empirical evidence suggests benefits, but more analysis would help.
- Limited exploration of feature control beyond a preliminary result; pixel control dominates.
- Evaluation protocols on Atari (e.g., sticky actions, number of training frames, evaluation seeds) could be detailed more rigorously; variance across seeds for fixed hyperparameters is not thoroughly analyzed.

4. Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful suggestions, not essential to my decision)
- Report wall-clock time, total gradient updates, and FLOPs to contextualize sample efficiency vs compute. Consider an A3C+replay baseline (value replay without auxiliary tasks) matched for update budget.
- Provide variance across multiple seeds for fixed hyperparameters on a subset of representative tasks. Significance tests for reported improvements would strengthen claims.
- Analyze the learned representation: e.g., visualize attention/activations, measure linear separability of reward-relevant states, and quantify how auxiliary tasks change state visitation.
- Extend the ablation on feature control (vary which layers are controlled; combine with pixel control) and study sensitivity to pixel-grid size and reward normalization.
- Clarify Atari evaluation details (training frames, no-op starts, sticky actions, frame-skip). If possible, include results under sticky actions.
- Discuss failure modes: do auxiliary control heads ever encourage degenerate features (e.g., favoring camera motion) and how is this mitigated?
- Release code and, if possible, the Labyrinth tasks or comparable open environments to facilitate replication.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget: How many total optimization steps per environment step does UNREAL perform versus A3C? What are the wall-clock speedups/slowdowns? Can you provide a compute-matched comparison?
- Stability: Did you observe instabilities from mixing on-policy A3C with off-policy n-step Q-learning on shared recurrent features? Any tricks beyond target networks and short replay buffers?
- Reward prediction skew: How sensitive are results to the 50/50 rebalancing ratio and replay buffer size? Does rebalancing harm performance in tasks with frequent negative rewards?
- Pixel control specifics: How sensitive are gains to the grid resolution, cropping choice, and reward normalization across games (especially Atari with heterogeneous pixel dynamics)? Any evidence of the agent learning representations that overemphasize motion?
- Feature control: Could you share fuller results for feature control across more levels/games and in combination with pixel control?
- Generality: Have you tried UNREAL in continuous control or non-visual domains? If not, what modifications would you anticipate (e.g., defining control targets over proprioceptive features)?
- Robustness analysis: For a fixed hyperparameter setting, what is the performance variance across seeds? Do the robustness benefits persist when controlling for this?
- Evaluation protocol: Please clarify Atari training steps, evaluation seeds, sticky actions usage, and any life-loss or episode termination handling to enable exact replication.
