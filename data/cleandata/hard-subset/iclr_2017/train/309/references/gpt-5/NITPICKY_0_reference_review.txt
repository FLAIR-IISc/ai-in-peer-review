Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks trained largely off-policy from a short replay buffer: (1) auxiliary control via Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel-control and feature-control), (2) immediate reward prediction with class rebalancing to mitigate sparse rewards, and (3) value-function replay. A unified loss combines on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary losses.
- The motivation is to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness without directly using the auxiliary policies for control.
- Empirically, the paper reports substantial gains on 13 3D Labyrinth levels (mean human-normalized score from ~54% to ~87%, with ~10× data-efficiency) and strong results on 57 Atari games (880% mean, 250% median), together with ablations indicating benefits of each auxiliary task.

Decision: Reject
Key reasons:
1) Evaluation rigor and fairness: The reporting relies on top-k hyperparameter selection, lacks standard Atari evaluation details, uses non-comparable protocols to prior work, and emphasizes data-efficiency in steps but not wall-clock, while adding significant extra computation. Human-normalization procedures for Labyrinth are insufficiently specified. These issues materially limit confidence in the strength and generality of the claims.
2) Methodological clarity and reproducibility: Several important technical details are ambiguous or inconsistent (e.g., the formal objective vs. implementation, off-policy corrections, state definition including network activations, target networks, auxiliary loss interactions), and the reference/formatting quality is poor. Collectively, these make the work hard to replicate and interpret.

Supporting arguments
- Protocol and comparability:
  - Atari evaluation lacks critical details: evaluation seeds/episodes, no-op or sticky actions, life-loss handling, frame-skip conventions, and score aggregation. Comparisons to Prioritized Dueling DQN may be unfair if protocols differ. Reporting “top 3” hyperparameters for learning curves risks optimistic bias; distributions across all settings are shown but are not the primary headline.
  - Labyrinth human baselines: number of human subjects, instructions, training, variance, and evaluation protocol are not described; hence “human-normalized” improvements are hard to interpret.
  - Data efficiency is reported in environment steps to reach A3C’s best, but auxiliary tasks add substantial learning updates and extra nets (deconv heads, reward predictor), likely affecting wall-clock. Without wall-clock comparisons, “10×” efficiency can be misleading for practical settings.

- Methodological clarity:
  - Objective mismatch: Eq. (1) uses Eπc[R(c)] although auxiliary policies are not used for behavior; training is off-policy from π. Clarify whether πc is learned purely for representation, and whether the stated objective corresponds to the implemented training (it currently does not).
  - State definition includes the agent’s hidden activations, making the MDP non-stationary as parameters change; off-policy Q-learning on such a non-stationary internal-state space is nontrivial. No discussion of stability or convergence implications.
  - Off-policy details: For Q-learning on auxiliary tasks, is θ− a separate target network per task? How often updated? For value replay, targets are computed wrt the (evolving) current policy but without importance sampling; justify why this does not bias value estimation.
  - Pixel-control definition: Reward is average absolute pixel change on a grid. This may encourage camera motion over task-relevant behavior; the paper shows benefits, but no systematic analysis of pathological behaviors or normalization across games (λPC is tuned per domain, and “fit this normalization factor” reads as post hoc).
  - Q-values with ReLU final activation: for pixel control, nonnegative rewards make this less problematic, but feature-control rewards or discounted returns could (in principle) be negative; justify this design and its effect on learning stability and value ranges.
  - Replay buffer size (2k) and sampling: With 20-step returns and periodic updates, effective sample diversity may be limited; provide sensitivity analyses.
  - Reward prediction: heavy class rebalancing is used, yet these features are shared with the policy/value head; while you argue no bias to the policy/value, the shared encoder can induce representational bias. This deserves ablation (e.g., reward-prediction-only vs control-only vs both, with/without rebalancing).

- Ablations and robustness:
  - While some ablations are reported, key factors are missing: effects of λVR, λPC, λRP individually and jointly; sensitivity to the pixel grid size/crop; effect of removing replay rebalancing; contribution of value replay alone; effect of using only feature control across more levels; and whether adding only replay (without auxiliary tasks) helps A3C.
  - The “top 3” hyperparameter results emphasize best-case behavior; median-over-seeds for the best hyperparameter and wall-clock performance would be more informative.

- Presentation and references:
  - Numerous formatting errors, typos, and malformed references (e.g., “Barreto et al.,? \Q2016\E”), as well as unclear figure references not present in the text excerpt. Such issues detract from clarity and polish.

Additional feedback (to improve the paper; not all are decision-critical)
- Provide a rigorous, standardized Atari evaluation (sticky actions, no-ops, fixed evaluation episodes, no learning during evaluation), and compare to baselines under identical protocols.
- Report wall-clock as well as sample efficiency, and normalize compute budgets across agents.
- Expand ablations isolating each auxiliary component and the replay mechanisms; include sensitivity to λ’s and buffer size, and remove top-k hyperparameter selection for headline metrics.
- Analyze potential degenerate behaviors encouraged by pixel-control, and experiment with alternative pseudo-rewards (e.g., optical flow magnitude, prediction error, feature-change norms).
- Justify off-policy choices theoretically or empirically (e.g., target networks, stability diagnostics); clarify the role and update rules for θ− for each auxiliary head.
- Clarify whether the LSTM input uses previous reward (rt−1) or “current reward” at time t; disambiguate temporal indexing.
- Improve reference formatting and ensure all citations are complete and consistent.

Questions for the authors
1) Exact Atari evaluation protocol: number of eval episodes, no-op or sticky actions, episode termination, life-loss handling, deterministic vs stochastic evaluation, and whether learning is paused.
2) How are target networks θ− maintained for each auxiliary Q-function? Update frequency? Shared across tasks?
3) For value replay, what is the exact target and policy used? Any off-policy correction? How do you prevent drift due to policy lag?
4) Why ReLU on Qaux outputs? How do you handle potentially negative returns for feature control?
5) Pixel-control specifics: effect of crop/grid size; normalization of pixel-change rewards across games; evidence that agents do not exploit camera motion; ablations removing camera-rotation actions.
6) Human baseline protocol for Labyrinth: number of participants, training, variance, evaluation horizon; how is human-normalization computed?
7) Wall-clock comparisons: how much extra compute do auxiliary losses add relative to A3C, and do the data-efficiency gains persist in time-to-target benchmarks?
8) Reproducibility: will code and the Labyrinth environments be released? If not, can you provide full hyperparameter tables and seeds for the reported top runs?
