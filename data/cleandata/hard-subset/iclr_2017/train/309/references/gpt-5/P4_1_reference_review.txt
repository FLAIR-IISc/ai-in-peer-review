Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper proposes augmenting deep RL agents with auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, stability, and final performance. The main agent, UNREAL, combines on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary tasks trained from recent experience replay: (i) auxiliary control tasks via n-step Q-learning on pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control), (ii) a reward prediction task trained on rebalanced replay emphasizing rare rewards, and (iii) value function replay.
- The approach is motivated by representation learning: pseudo-control and immediate reward prediction are used solely to shape shared features, not to directly influence the policy/value targets, aiming to avoid biasing the policy update while improving the learned representation.
- Extensive experiments on 13 Labyrinth 3D tasks and 57 Atari games show substantial gains over A3C in final performance, sample efficiency, and hyperparameter robustness. Ablations show that each auxiliary component helps; pixel/feature control outperform prediction-only or reconstruction baselines. The paper reports human-normalized performance gains and large speedups to reach A3C’s final scores.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong empirical evidence across diverse domains with careful ablations supporting the central claim that auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction significantly improve performance and robustness.
- The approach is well-motivated and reasonably positioned in the literature, combining ideas from options/UVFA/Horde with a practical, scalable integration into modern deep RL (A3C + replay + auxiliary heads).

Supporting arguments
1) Problem addressed: The paper targets the challenge of sparse rewards and inefficient representation learning in visually rich, partially observed environments. It proposes using auxiliary control and prediction tasks to densify learning signals without directly biasing the policy/value learning.

2) Motivation and literature placement: The work is clearly situated with respect to options, successor representation, UVFAs, and Horde. The novelty lies in using many auxiliary pseudo-reward control tasks and reward prediction as representation-shaping objectives, trained off-policy from a shared stream of experience, and integrated with an on-policy recurrent A3C agent. The contrast with prior uses of pseudo-rewards (as goals for behavior) and with model-based prediction-only objectives is explicit. The use of rebalanced replay for reward prediction is a practical contribution.

3) Support for claims and rigor:
- Technical soundness: Losses are specified (A3C, n-step Q-learning for each auxiliary control task, cross-entropy for reward prediction, value replay), with architectural details (shared CNN/LSTM, deconv heads, dueling parametrization). Off-policy training is confined to auxiliary tasks and value replay; a target network is used for Q-learning. The design choice to use a feedforward reward predictor over short sequences is reasonable to decouple temporal credit assignment from feature shaping.
- Empirical evidence: Labyrinth results show large gains in human-normalized scores and significant sample-efficiency improvements (up to 10× mean/median speedups). Robustness across hyperparameters is demonstrated by sweeps and aggregate plots. Ablations against pixel reconstruction and change prediction baselines support the central claim that learning to control pixel/feature changes is more effective than mere prediction or reconstruction. Atari results report higher mean/median scores than A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN and improved robustness, with 50 hyperparameter settings per method.
- Caveats: Some evaluation details that affect fairness are not fully specified (e.g., total training frames, evaluation protocol, compute budget, no-op/termination settings for Atari). Mixing off-policy Q-learning with recurrent state is complex; while a target network and replay are used, no importance sampling or off-policy correction is described for recurrent hidden states. Nevertheless, empirical stability appears good.

Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily decisive for acceptance)
- Clarify evaluation protocols on Atari (number of training frames, no-op starts, sticky actions if used, evaluation episodes, life-loss termination) to ensure comparability with prior work.
- Report compute budgets and updates-per-environment-step. Since UNREAL performs additional auxiliary updates, distinguish wall-clock vs sample efficiency and ensure baselines receive comparable compute or report both.
- Provide variance across random seeds for key results (not only across hyperparameters) and statistical tests where feasible.
- Discuss the potential for representation bias introduced by rebalanced replay for reward prediction, given shared convolutional features; consider an experiment showing minimal policy/value bias (e.g., compare with/without rebalancing using the same auxiliary architecture).
- Elaborate on stability measures: target network update frequency for auxiliary Q, replay buffer size sensitivity, and λ weights sensitivity. A small hyperparameter sensitivity table for λPC, λVR, λRP would help.
- Expand feature control experiments beyond a single level and/or analyze learned features (e.g., saliency or unit activation controllability) to support interpretability claims.
- Consider adding limited off-policy corrections for recurrent auxiliary control (e.g., resetting LSTM state from replay or using burn-in) and report whether it affects performance.

Questions for the authors
- Training budget: How many environment frames were used for Atari and Labyrinth? Are wall-clock times comparable across A3C and UNREAL?
- Evaluation protocol on Atari: no-op/sticky actions settings, terminal on life loss, number of evaluation episodes, and whether training used frame-skip=4. How do these choices compare to the Prioritized Dueling DQN results you cite?
- Fairness of hyperparameter sweeps: Did A3C and baselines receive the same number of hyperparameter samples and training frames? For SOTA comparisons, were baselines reproduced under your setup or taken from the literature?
- Off-policy auxiliary control with LSTM: How are LSTM states handled during replayed Q-learning updates (e.g., burn-in, truncation)? Any observed instability or divergence?
- Reward prediction rebalancing: Have you measured whether rebalanced sampling degrades value/policy estimates (e.g., via policy evaluation on held-out trajectories)? Any effect on exploration behavior?
- Sensitivity: How sensitive are results to λPC, λVR, and λRP, and to the replay buffer size (2k)? Any systematic guidance for choosing these?
- Pixel control details: How is the pixel-change reward normalized across games with different intensity dynamics (especially Atari), and how critical is this normalization to performance?
- Target network: What is the update schedule for θ− in auxiliary Q-learning? Did you try double Q-learning to reduce overestimation?
- Generalization: Do features learned by auxiliary control/prediction transfer across tasks (e.g., pretrain on one Labyrinth level, fine-tune on another)?
