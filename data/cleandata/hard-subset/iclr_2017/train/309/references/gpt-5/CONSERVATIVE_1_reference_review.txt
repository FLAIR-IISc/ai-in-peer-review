1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks intended to improve representation learning and data efficiency. The auxiliary tasks include: (i) off-policy control of pixel changes (and optionally network feature activations) via n-step Q-learning using a deconvolutional head; (ii) reward prediction trained on rebalanced replay to counter reward sparsity; and (iii) value function replay. The approach combines on-policy A3C updates with off-policy auxiliary losses from a local replay buffer, optimizing a single joint objective. Empirically, the authors report substantially improved sample efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameters on 13 Labyrinth 3D tasks and 57 Atari games, with ablations indicating contributions from each auxiliary component.

2) Decision
Accept. Key reasons: (a) strong empirical gains over established baselines across diverse domains, with ablations; (b) the method builds on well-established components (A3C, replay, Q-learning) and the proposed auxiliary tasks are straightforward to implement and plausibly motivated.

3) Supporting arguments
- Problem addressed: Representation learning for RL under sparse reward and partial observability. The paper’s question—can auxiliary control and reward-focused prediction tasks accelerate and stabilize RL without changing the base objective?—is clear and relevant.
- Motivation and placement: The work is positioned against options/UVFAs/successor representation, model-based prediction, and recent auxiliary-prediction work in 3D. The choice to use auxiliary control purely for representation shaping (not as separate policies used at execution) is a reasonable, distinct design point.
- Methodological soundness: The learning objective is well specified; off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control with a replay buffer is standard; the rebalanced reward prediction is decoupled from policy/value to avoid bias, with shared low-level features. Using a deconv head to exploit spatial structure is sensible. The combined loss and training schedule are clearly described.
- Empirical support: On Labyrinth, UNREAL improves mean human-normalized scores from 54% to 87% and achieves 10x median data-efficiency gains, with consistent hyperparameter robustness improvements. Ablations (pixel control, value replay, reward prediction) show additive benefits; comparisons to reconstruction and simple change prediction favor control-based auxiliaries. On Atari, the approach yields strong mean/median gains and improved robustness. The breadth of domains and ablations lend weight to the claims.
- Rigor caveats: While the evidence is compelling, some controls are missing (see below), but the magnitude and consistency of improvements across many tasks offset these limitations.

4) Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Compute/accounting fairness: Report wall-clock time, total optimizer updates, and FLOPs for each method. Auxiliary heads and replay updates increase compute; equalizing (or at least reporting) compute would clarify whether gains are sample- or compute-driven.
- Capacity/control ablations: Add controls that match parameter count and update frequency (e.g., A3C with an extra head trained on a dummy supervised task; A3C with the same number of additional gradient steps from replay but without auxiliary losses).
- Baseline parity: Ensure Atari comparisons use identical training budgets, evaluation protocols (no-op starts, life termination, sticky actions), and hyperparameter sweep budgets. Clarify whether Prioritized Dueling DQN results are re-run under your setup or taken from the literature with potentially different frame budgets.
- Statistical reporting: Provide confidence intervals or interquartile ranges across seeds for key aggregate metrics, and significance testing for major claims.
- Human baselines (Labyrinth): Describe the human evaluation protocol (training time allowed, instructions, number of episodes, and variability).
- Sensitivity analyses: Report sensitivity to replay buffer size, λ weights, grid size for pixel control, and n-step horizon for auxiliary Q-learning.
- Generality: Include more results on feature control alone and in combination with pixel control to substantiate the general “control of learned features” claim.

5) Questions for the authors
- Compute and budget:
  - What are the relative wall-clock times and total gradient updates for A3C vs UNREAL to reach the reported performance? If we constrain total compute, do the gains persist?
  - How many frames were used per Atari game and per Labyrinth task? Are DQN/A3C baselines run with the exact same frame budgets and evaluation protocol?
- Fairness of comparisons:
  - For Atari, do you use sticky actions and no-op starts? Are the reported Dueling DQN numbers obtained under the same protocol and frame counts, or taken from prior work?
  - How many hyperparameter settings were explored per method, and is this matched across methods (e.g., number of trials, search ranges)?
- Ablations/controls:
  - What is the performance of “A3C + value replay only” and “A3C + replayed actor-critic updates” (no auxiliary losses), matching the number of replay updates used by UNREAL?
  - Does an A3C baseline with matched parameter count (extra heads trained with random labels or autoencoding) close the gap?
  - How sensitive are results to λPC, λVR, λRP and to the pixel-control grid resolution?
- Stability/off-policy issues:
  - Did you observe instability from mixing on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning updates on shared representations? Any tricks (target networks, update ratios) critical for stability?
- Reward prediction:
  - How much does the rebalanced sampling (P(r≠0)=0.5) contribute versus training reward prediction on-policy? Any evidence that this biases the representation in ways that hurt long-horizon performance?
- Feature control:
  - Beyond the preliminary result, does feature control consistently help across Atari/Labyrinth? How do feature-control and pixel-control interact?

Overall, this is a strong empirical paper that leverages established ingredients in a well-motivated way and demonstrates substantial, robust gains across challenging benchmarks. Addressing the fairness and compute-accounting concerns would further strengthen the case.
