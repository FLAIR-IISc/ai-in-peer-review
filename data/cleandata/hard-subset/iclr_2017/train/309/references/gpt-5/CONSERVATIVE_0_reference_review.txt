Summary and contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning and data efficiency. The key components are: (i) auxiliary control tasks trained off-policy with n-step Q-learning to maximize pseudo-rewards such as pixel-change (and preliminarily feature-control); (ii) reward prediction trained with class-rebalanced replay to alleviate reward sparsity; and (iii) value-function replay for additional value regression. These losses are combined into a single objective, with experience replay used for the auxiliary updates.
- Empirically, the method is evaluated on 13 challenging 3D Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games. The authors report substantial gains in both final performance and data efficiency over A3C, and improvements in robustness to hyperparameters. Ablations indicate the benefit of each auxiliary component.

Decision: Accept (weak accept)
Key reasons: (1) Strong empirical gains across two domains using largely established ingredients (A3C, replay, auxiliary losses), with ablations and domain breadth that suggest practical significance. (2) The contribution is methodologically incremental yet pragmatic and well-motivated; despite some confounds in evaluation, the improvements appear sizable and robust.

Supporting arguments
Strengths
- Sound high-level motivation: shaping representations via unsupervised/auxiliary predictions that are related to environment dynamics and reward sparsity is consistent with a long line of work (Horde, UVFA, SR, model prediction).
- Principled integration: A3C handles on-policy policy/value learning; off-policy replayed Q-learning handles many auxiliary tasks efficiently; reward prediction uses rebalanced sampling without biasing the policy/value head.
- Breadth of evidence: Results on 13 visually complex 3D tasks and 57 Atari games, with consistent improvements, including large speedups in learning and higher human-normalized scores.
- Ablations: Pixel-control vs. reconstruction vs. input-change prediction baselines on Labyrinth; reward prediction and value replay ablations; some sensitivity to hyperparameters is reported, with improved robustness.
- Practicality: The approach uses straightforward architectural additions and training regimes that can be adopted by practitioners.

Concerns
- Compute and update budget fairness: UNREAL performs additional replay-based updates and adds heads/parameters. It is unclear whether gains are due in part to strictly more optimization steps/compute than the A3C baseline. A compute- or update-matched baseline (e.g., A3C with equivalent extra value-head updates/replay but without auxiliary targets) is only partially addressed via “value function replay”; a more systematic control is needed.
- Evaluation methodology on Atari: Selecting the “top-3 agents” over 50 hyperparameter settings per game complicates fairness vs. prior results that often use fixed, tuned settings. The report would benefit from standardized ALE evaluation protocols (e.g., no-op/human starts), uniform training frame budgets, and summary statistics over seeds for a fixed hyperparameter choice. Statistical uncertainty (means/medians without variance/CIs) is not shown.
- Ablation coverage: While pixel-control ablations are informative on three Labyrinth levels, more extensive comparisons are needed. For example: (i) “replay only” control; (ii) “reward-prediction only with class rebalancing”; (iii) parameter-matched baselines; (iv) auxiliary losses with on-policy-only training to isolate the effect of off-policy replay vs. auxiliary targets.
- Generality and transfer: The paper frames auxiliary tasks as representation learning, yet does not evaluate transfer to new tasks or pseudo-rewards, nor generalization to novel levels within Labyrinth beyond performance curves. This limits claims about representation quality.
- Theoretical grounding: The approach is largely heuristic; no guarantees or analyses are provided for stability of off-policy auxiliary Q-learning combined with recurrent on-policy A3C. Empirically it works, but this mixing could be fragile.

Suggestions to improve the paper
- Provide compute-normalized comparisons: match total gradient steps, wall-clock, and parameter counts across baselines; report throughput and training budgets (frames and updates).
- Add a “replay-only” A3C baseline and a “parameter-matched extra-heads with random targets” baseline to control for extra capacity and updates.
- Expand ablations: per-domain contribution of each loss component (λPC, λRP, λVR) with sensitivity curves; grid-size and normalization choices for pixel control; impact of buffer size.
- Standardize Atari evaluation: fixed training frames, no-op/human starts; report mean±std across multiple seeds per fixed hyperparameter; avoid picking top-k hyperparameters post hoc for the main claim.
- Provide more analysis of failure cases and qualitative behaviors to understand when pixel- or feature-control helps or hurts.
- If feasible, add transfer experiments (e.g., training auxiliary heads on a subset of tasks and evaluating data efficiency on new tasks).

Questions for the authors
1. How many additional gradient updates per environment frame does UNREAL perform relative to A3C? Can you report compute-normalized results (same updates/wall-clock) to isolate the effect of auxiliary targets?
2. What is the exact parameter increase from the auxiliary heads? Have you compared to a parameter-matched A3C baseline?
3. Does “value function replay” alone (without pixel/reward prediction) close a significant fraction of the gap to UNREAL? Please provide full curves for this control.
4. On Atari, what evaluation protocol was used (no-op or human starts)? How many training frames per game? Please report mean±std over seeds for a fixed hyperparameter choice, not only top-3.
5. How sensitive are results to λPC, λRP, λVR? Please include per-game sensitivity plots and justify the different λPC ranges for Atari vs. Labyrinth beyond scale considerations.
6. Can you show ablations where auxiliary tasks are trained on-policy only (no replay) to separate the effect of replay vs. the auxiliary objectives?
7. For pixel control, how were pixel-difference rewards normalized across games with different intensity dynamics? Did you observe any exploitation of flicker or rendering artifacts?
8. How stable is the combined on-policy A3C with off-policy auxiliary Q-learning in recurrent settings? Any signs of divergence or instabilities?
9. Do auxiliary features transfer? For example, pretrain on a set of Labyrinth levels and fine-tune on held-out levels; does UNREAL pretraining reduce sample complexity?
10. Could you release code and hyperparameters to facilitate reproducibility?

Overall, despite methodological caveats, the empirical gains are substantial and consistent across domains. With stronger controls and standardized evaluation, this would be a clear accept; as is, I lean to accept given practical impact.
