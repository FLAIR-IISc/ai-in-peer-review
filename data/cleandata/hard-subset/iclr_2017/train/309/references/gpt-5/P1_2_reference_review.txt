Summary of contributions
- Problem: The paper targets sample inefficiency and brittle learning in deep RL, especially in visually rich, partially observable 3D environments where rewards are sparse.
- Approach: It augments a strong on-policy baseline (A3C with CNN-LSTM) with off-policy auxiliary tasks trained from recent experience replay. The auxiliary tasks are:
  1) Auxiliary control via Q-learning on pseudo-rewards: pixel control (maximize per-cell pixel change) and feature control (maximize hidden-unit activations). A deconvolutional head predicts per-action spatial Q-values.
  2) Reward-focused auxiliary tasks: reward prediction of immediate reward from short stacks of encoded frames with class-rebalancing to oversample rare rewards.
  3) Value function replay: additional value regression from replay in addition to on-policy A3C value updates.
- Algorithm: UNREAL optimizes a combined loss (A3C + replayed value + Q-learning for control + reward prediction) with shared representations and separate heads, trained asynchronously.
- Empirics: On 13 Labyrinth tasks, UNREAL more than doubles human-normalized performance over A3C and achieves ~10x speedup to reach A3C’s best score. Robustness across hyperparameters improves. On 57 Atari games, UNREAL surpasses A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN in mean/median human-normalized scores. Ablations indicate pixel control > predicting input change > reconstruction, and preliminary evidence that feature control is also beneficial.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical gains in both final performance and data efficiency across diverse environments, supported by ablations and robustness analyses.
- Well-motivated and simple-to-implement idea—using off-policy auxiliary tasks to shape shared representations—positioned clearly relative to options, UVFAs, SR, model learning, and prior auxiliary prediction work.

Supporting arguments
- Motivation and placement: The paper makes a compelling case that auxiliary tasks can densify learning signals without directly biasing the policy/value targets. It differentiates itself from prior work: options (control-free representation focus here), Horde (many GVFs but not for shared representation), UVFAs/SR (factorization for transfer vs. representation shaping here), and model-learning (predictive tasks; here control-oriented auxiliary signals).
- Technical soundness: The integration of on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning heads and replay is clear, and the architectural choices (spatial deconv head, dueling parametrization, immediate reward prediction with rebalanced sampling) are justified. The design exploits spatial structure and task locality. The separation of uses—reward prediction only for shaping shared features—helps avoid policy/value bias while allowing skewed sampling.
- Empirical support: 
  - Labyrinth: Significant improvements in human-normalized scores and large speedups. The ablations (pixel control vs. change prediction vs. reconstruction) are particularly convincing and align with the hypothesis that learning to control produces richer, more task-relevant representations than passive prediction or reconstruction.
  - Atari: Broad gains and improved hyperparameter robustness across 57 games suggest generality beyond the 3D setting.
  - Robustness: The paper systematically explores hyperparameter sensitivity, showing smoother performance landscapes for UNREAL.
- Novelty and impact: While auxiliary tasks are not new, the specific formulation of auxiliary control via pixel/feature control and its effective off-policy integration with an on-policy recurrent agent is a substantial and influential contribution. The practicality (simple losses and heads) and strong results are likely to have significant impact.

Additional feedback to improve the paper
- Statistical rigor: Report means and standard errors across seeds for headline numbers. While hyperparameter sweeps are informative, include fixed-seed averages for the chosen hyperparameters to separate robustness from central tendency. Provide statistical tests where appropriate.
- Evaluation protocol details:
  - Specify number of environment frames used for Atari and Labyrinth, evaluation episodes, and whether evaluations use the same stochasticity as training (e.g., no-ops, frame skip). Clarify comparability with Dueling DQN (training budget, evaluation protocol).
  - For human-normalization: detail the human testing protocol (number of subjects, trials, variance).
- Fairness and selection: You evaluate 50 hyperparameter settings and often plot the top-3 per method; also include plots averaging across all settings and/or a single tuned setting chosen via validation to guard against selection bias. The appendix hints at this, but making the main-text comparison unambiguous would strengthen claims.
- Ablations breadth: 
  - Provide broader ablations on Atari (pixel control only, reward prediction only, value replay only, combinations), not just Labyrinth, to test generality.
  - Feature control is only preliminarily tested on one level; a more systematic study or dropping it from the main claims would avoid overstatement.
- Computational cost: Quantify wall-clock overhead from auxiliary tasks (training time, memory), and sample-efficiency gains in frames to target scores. This helps practitioners assess trade-offs.
- Stability: Off-policy Q-learning with shared recurrent features can be delicate. Include details on target network updates (θ−), update frequencies, and any stabilizing tricks. Provide sensitivity to replay buffer size and prioritization.
- Analysis: Add qualitative/quantitative analysis of learned representations (e.g., saliency/attention maps, correlation between pixel-control value maps and reward-relevant regions) to strengthen the mechanistic story.

Questions for the authors
1) Training budget and evaluation:
- How many environment frames were used for Atari and Labyrinth per run? Are results reported at a fixed frame budget? How many evaluation episodes and with what stochasticity (e.g., no-ops, sticky actions)?
2) Hyperparameter selection and seeds:
- For the headline Atari numbers, are they averages over the top-3 hyperparameters or over all 50? How many random seeds per hyperparameter? Can you report per-game mean±SE across seeds for a single chosen configuration?
3) Off-policy control details:
- How often are target parameters θ− updated? What is the n for n-step Q-learning in auxiliary control? Did you observe instability from off-policy updates with shared LSTM, and if so, how did you mitigate it?
4) Contribution of each component:
- On Atari, what are the gains from (a) value replay alone, (b) reward prediction alone, (c) pixel control alone, and their combinations? Is value replay doing most of the work?
5) Reward prediction bias:
- Although reward prediction does not directly feed into policy/value targets, could feature sharing induce bias? Did you test variants without sharing lower layers or with different rebalance ratios?
6) Generality of control tasks:
- How sensitive is pixel control to grid size, crop region, or using more semantically meaningful pseudo-rewards (e.g., optical flow magnitude)? How does feature control compare when targeting different layers?
7) Compute/resources:
- What is the training wall-clock for UNREAL vs. A3C on Labyrinth and Atari (with identical hardware)? What is the memory overhead of the replay and auxiliary heads?
8) Transfer:
- Can a UNREAL-trained representation transfer to new reward functions or tasks (e.g., different goals in the same environment) without retraining auxiliary tasks?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed paper with strong empirical evidence and a simple, impactful idea. I recommend acceptance.
