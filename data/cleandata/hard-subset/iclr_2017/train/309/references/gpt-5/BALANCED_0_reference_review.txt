1) Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an augmentation of deep RL agents with unsupervised auxiliary tasks aimed at improving representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. Concretely, the method adds: (i) auxiliary control tasks defined by pseudo-rewards (not used for behavior directly) such as pixel control and feature control, trained off-policy with n-step Q-learning from a small replay buffer; (ii) a reward prediction task that rebalances rare rewarding events and shapes the shared convolutional features without biasing the policy/value heads; and (iii) value function replay to further exploit recent experience. These losses are combined with an on-policy A3C agent with an LSTM core. Extensive experiments show strong gains on 13 challenging 3D Labyrinth levels and on 57 Atari games, with improved final scores, markedly faster learning (up to 10x fewer frames to reach A3C’s best performance on Labyrinth), and increased hyperparameter robustness. Ablations suggest pixel control is superior to simple prediction or reconstruction, and feature control is promising.

2) Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, consistent empirical improvements in two distinct domains (3D partially observed and Atari), with careful ablations and robustness analyses.
- Well-motivated approach that leverages auxiliary control and reward-prediction to focus representation learning on dynamics and reward-relevant features, while preserving the base policy’s on-policy training.

3) Supporting arguments
What problem is tackled? The paper addresses data inefficiency and brittle representation learning in deep RL—especially under sparse reward and complex visual dynamics—by introducing auxiliary tasks that densify learning signals without directly biasing the behavior policy.

Is the approach well-motivated and placed in the literature? Yes. The paper clearly situates itself with respect to options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and prior auxiliary prediction/modeling efforts, articulating the distinctive choice to use pseudo-reward control solely for representation shaping. The rationale for off-policy training from recent replay (efficiency) and rebalancing reward prediction (counteracting sparsity) is compelling.

Do the results support the claims? Largely yes. On Labyrinth, UNREAL improves mean/median human-normalized performance (87/92% vs 54/45% for A3C) and accelerates learning by ~10x, with per-level breakdowns and ablations (pixel control vs prediction vs reconstruction; value function replay; with/without pixel control). On Atari-57, the method outperforms A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN on mean and median human-normalized scores and shows hyperparameter robustness. The implementation details are thorough and reproducible in principle (network heads, replay buffer sizes, update schedules). The evidence aligns with the claims that auxiliary control and reward prediction shape features beneficial for policy/value learning.

That said, a few aspects temper the strength of the evidence:
- Fairness and compute parity: UNREAL adds replay and extra loss heads (more parameters/updates). The baseline A3C has no replay. A control baseline matching model capacity and update budget (e.g., A3C + extra heads with random losses, or A3C + value replay only) would isolate the effect of auxiliary control vs extra capacity/off-policy updates.
- Evaluation protocol details on Atari (training frames, evaluation episodes, sticky actions, score normalization choices, life-loss handling) are crucial for comparing to prior SOTA; some of these specifics are not fully documented in the provided text.
- Statistical rigor: while many hyperparameter settings/seeds are included, the reporting often highlights top-k configurations and human-normalized summaries; confidence intervals and tests for key comparisons would strengthen the claims.

Overall, despite these caveats, the results are strong and consistent across varied tasks, and the methodological contribution is clear and impactful.

4) Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not all points affect the decision)
- Add compute- and parameter-matched baselines:
  - A3C + replay only (value replay, no auxiliary control/reward prediction).
  - A3C + extra heads of similar size trained on non-informative or self-supervised tasks (e.g., random labels, jigsaw) to control for capacity/regularization.
  - A3C + off-policy updates on the same transitions without pseudo-rewards.
- Clarify Atari evaluation protocol: training frame budget, evaluation episode count, sticky actions, terminal-on-life-loss, reward clipping, human baselines, and any differences from prior reports. Include raw scores in the main text or a compact appendix table.
- Report wall-clock time and update counts to reach performance milestones to contextualize the 10× sample-efficiency gains versus added computation.
- Analyze sensitivity: ablate λPC, λRP, λVR and replay buffer size; quantify how much each contributes on representative tasks.
- Provide more diagnostics on representations: e.g., visualize what pixel/feature control learns, measure how reward prediction shifts activation selectivity, and whether auxiliary policies become meaningful behaviors.
- Explore generalization/transfer: does the learned representation transfer across Labyrinth levels or to new reward functions?
- Discuss potential interference: could biased reward-prediction sampling negatively bias features for the main task? Any observed failure cases or negative transfer?
- Minor: fix typos and citation formatting; ensure the environment (Labyrinth) and code for auxiliary heads are accessible to aid reproducibility.

Questions for the authors
- Atari protocol: How many training frames per game? Were sticky actions used? What evaluation protocol (no-op starts or human starts), number of evaluation episodes, and life-loss handling? Are rewards clipped? How are human-normalized scores computed?
- Compute parity: How does wall-clock training time and total number of gradient updates (including auxiliary) compare between A3C and UNREAL? Could you report results with equalized compute?
- Capacity control: How many additional parameters do the auxiliary heads add? Have you tried an A3C baseline with matched parameter count but no auxiliary losses?
- Replay ablation: What is the performance of A3C + value function replay (no pixel/feature control, no reward prediction)? And UNREAL without replay but with on-policy auxiliary updates?
- Robustness: How consistent are results across seeds when keeping hyperparameters fixed? Could you report mean ± std over multiple seeds for selected games/levels?
- Auxiliary rewards: How sensitive is pixel control to the normalization of pixel-change magnitudes across games (you mention different λPC ranges for Atari vs Labyrinth)? Any failure modes where pixel control hurts?
- Transfer: Do representations learned via pixel/feature control transfer to new goals or modified reward functions without retraining the policy?

Overall, this is a strong and timely paper with a clear idea and compelling empirical evidence. I recommend acceptance.
