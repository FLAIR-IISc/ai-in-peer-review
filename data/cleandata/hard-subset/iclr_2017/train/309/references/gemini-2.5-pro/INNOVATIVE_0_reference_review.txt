### Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning" (UNREAL)

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel and compelling framework for dramatically improving the performance and data efficiency of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a strong on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained off-policy using experience replay. These auxiliary tasks are designed to facilitate the learning of rich, general-purpose representations. Specifically, the authors propose two main classes of auxiliary objectives: auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns policies to maximize changes in its sensory input (pixel control) or its own internal network activations (feature control); and auxiliary reward tasks, where the agent predicts the onset of immediate rewards from recent experience, with a skewed sampling strategy to emphasize rare reward events. By combining these auxiliary objectives with value function replay in a single, unified architecture, the paper demonstrates a massive leap in performance. The claims are validated through extensive experiments on a new, visually complex 3D environment called Labyrinth, as well as the standard 57-game Atari suite, showing state-of-the-art results, a 10x speedup in learning, and significantly improved robustness to hyperparameters over the A3C baseline.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that I am enthusiastic to support. It presents a bold, creative, and highly effective approach to a central problem in reinforcement learning: efficient representation learning. My recommendation is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The paper introduces a powerful and general framework for representation learning by framing it as a multi-objective optimization problem that includes auxiliary *control*. The idea of tasking an agent with learning to actively control its own perceptual stream (pixel control) is a significant and exciting departure from more passive auxiliary tasks like reconstruction or prediction. This concept of self-supervised control provides a rich training signal for understanding environment dynamics and affordances, and opens up a promising new direction for research into intrinsic motivation and unsupervised skill discovery.

2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The demonstrated performance gains are not incremental but constitute a major step forward for the field. Doubling the mean human-normalized score on the challenging new Labyrinth environment and achieving a 10x learning speedup over a state-of-the-art baseline like A3C is a remarkable achievement. The fact that the same architecture also achieves state-of-the-art results across the Atari suite underscores the generality and power of the proposed method.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The primary strength of this work lies in its creative reframing of representation learning. While auxiliary tasks have been explored before, the UNREAL agent's specific formulation is novel and powerful. The combination of an on-policy main objective (A3C) with off-policy auxiliary objectives trained from a replay buffer is an elegant architectural choice that gets the best of both worlds: the stability of on-policy policy-gradient learning for the main task, and the superior data efficiency of off-policy Q-learning for the representation-shaping auxiliary tasks.

The auxiliary control task is the paper's most significant conceptual contribution. The experiment in Figure 5, which directly compares learning to *control* pixel changes versus simply *predicting* them or *reconstructing* the input, is particularly insightful. The finding that control is superior to prediction, which is in turn superior to reconstruction, provides strong evidence for the central hypothesis and suggests that active interaction is a more potent signal for representation learning than passive observation. This is a bold claim, well-supported by the evidence provided.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The introduction and use of the Labyrinth environment, with its rich visuals and more complex physics, provides a challenging and valuable new benchmark for the community. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate that each component of the UNREAL agent contributes meaningfully to the final performance. Furthermore, the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is a crucial, practical contribution, as deep RL methods are notoriously sensitive. Showing that the UNREAL agent is not only better but also easier to train is a significant result.

**4. Additional Feedback and Suggestions for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Theoretical Grounding:** While empirically successful, the paper could benefit from a deeper discussion on the principles for designing effective auxiliary tasks. Why this specific combination? Is there a theoretical framework that could guide the selection or even automatic generation of such tasks in the future? A brief discussion on this could strengthen the paper's long-term impact.
*   **Nomenclature:** The term "Unsupervised" in the UNREAL acronym, while catchy, might be slightly misleading. The auxiliary tasks are more accurately described as "self-supervised," since the agent generates its own labels (pseudo-rewards) from its experience stream. This is a minor terminological point.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on data efficiency (in terms of environment steps). It would be helpful to include a brief analysis of the computational overhead. How much does the wall-clock time per training step increase with the addition of the auxiliary tasks and off-policy updates?
*   **Clarity on Feature Control:** The role of "feature control" was slightly unclear. It is introduced as a key idea in Section 3.1, but the main results in Figure 3 seem to focus on "pixel control" (PC). Section 4.1.1 discusses a preliminary experiment with feature control, calling it a "promising future direction." It would be beneficial to clarify whether feature control was part of the final UNREAL agent used for the main reported results or if it was explored as a separate, alternative control task.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

*   Could you clarify the exact composition of the auxiliary tasks in the final UNREAL agent that generated the headline results in Figures 3 and 4? Was it A3C + Pixel Control + Reward Prediction + Value Replay, or did it also include Feature Control?
*   The use of a deconvolutional network for the pixel control Q-values is an interesting architectural choice. Have you experimented with simpler architectures (e.g., separate MLPs for each grid cell)? How critical is this spatially-aware architecture to the success of pixel control?
*   The skewed sampling for reward prediction is noted as a simple form of prioritized replay. Did you experiment with applying the more general prioritized experience replay algorithm (Schaul et al., 2015b) to the other off-policy updates (value replay, pixel control)? If so, how did it compare?
