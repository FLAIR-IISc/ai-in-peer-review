### Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

#### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper proposes a novel deep reinforcement learning agent, named UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), designed to improve learning speed, final performance, and robustness. The core idea is to augment a strong on-policy baseline agent, Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), with a set of off-policy auxiliary tasks that share the same core network representation (CNN and LSTM). These auxiliary tasks are not used for direct control but serve to shape the agent's features, forcing it to learn a more general and useful representation of the environment.

The key contributions are:
1.  **A Hybrid On-Policy/Off-Policy Architecture:** The agent learns its main policy on-policy via A3C, while simultaneously learning from a replay buffer in an off-policy manner to train the auxiliary tasks. This combines the stability of on-policy learning for the main task with the data efficiency of off-policy learning for representation.
2.  **Novel Auxiliary Tasks:** The authors introduce two main classes of auxiliary tasks:
    *   **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions (Q-learning) for pseudo-rewards. Two specific instances are proposed: *Pixel Control*, where the agent learns to maximize pixel changes in different regions of the input image, and *Feature Control*, where it learns to maximize the activation of hidden units in its own network. These tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment.
    *   **Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate next reward from a short sequence of observations. To handle sparse rewards, this task is trained on data from a replay buffer with a skewed sampling strategy that over-represents rewarding transitions.
3.  **State-of-the-Art Empirical Results:** The UNREAL agent is extensively evaluated on two challenging domains: the visually rich 3D Labyrinth environment and the standard 57-game Atari benchmark. The results demonstrate dramatic improvements over the A3C baseline, achieving more than double the human-normalized score in Labyrinth (87% vs. 45%), a 10x speedup in learning, and superior performance and robustness on Atari, setting a new state-of-the-art.

#### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that makes significant and well-supported contributions to the field of deep reinforcement learning. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Effective Method for Representation Learning:** The paper introduces a principled and highly effective framework for improving representation learning in RL agents. The insight to combine stable on-policy policy learning with data-efficient off-policy auxiliary learning within a single architecture is powerful. The specific auxiliary tasks proposed are intuitive and well-motivated, addressing key challenges like sparse rewards and learning environmental dynamics.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are outstanding. The authors not only demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the standard Atari benchmark but also introduce and test their agent in the Labyrinth environment, which presents more complex perceptual and control challenges. The thoroughness of the evaluation—including ablation studies, hyperparameter robustness analysis, and comparisons to alternative unsupervised methods—provides compelling evidence for the efficacy of the proposed components.

#### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's strength lies in its clear motivation, clever architectural design, and convincing empirical results.

**Methodological Soundness:** The UNREAL architecture is a thoughtful synthesis of several powerful ideas in deep RL: A3C's asynchronous training, experience replay's data efficiency, and the use of self-supervised auxiliary tasks for representation learning. The distinction between using on-policy updates for the main task and off-policy updates for the auxiliary tasks is crucial. This allows the representation to be enriched by replaying and re-analyzing past experiences without directly biasing the main policy with off-policy data, which can be unstable.

The proposed auxiliary tasks are well-chosen. Pixel Control directly incentivizes the agent to learn a model of cause-and-effect in the environment—what actions cause what visual changes. This is a more active form of learning than passive prediction or reconstruction tasks, as convincingly shown in the ablation study in Figure 5. Reward Prediction with biased replay is a simple yet effective solution to the sparse reward problem, ensuring that the feature extractor learns to identify reward-predictive states even if they are rare.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental section is a model of clarity and thoroughness. The use of the Labyrinth environment is a significant plus, as it moves beyond the established Atari benchmark into 3D first-person environments, which are more representative of real-world robotics and navigation tasks. The reported gains are not marginal; a 10x speedup and a doubling of performance are highly significant.

The ablation studies (Figure 3, top left) are critical as they systematically demonstrate that each auxiliary component (Pixel Control, Reward Prediction, Value Replay) contributes positively to the final performance, and their combination yields the best results. Furthermore, the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters (Figure 3, right column) addresses a critical practical concern in deep RL. Showing that UNREAL not only performs better but is also easier to train is a major practical contribution.

#### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is excellent, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Computational Overhead:** The UNREAL agent is significantly more complex than the A3C baseline, with multiple additional network heads and loss terms. A brief discussion of the computational overhead (e.g., increase in training time per step, GPU memory usage) would be valuable for researchers aiming to replicate or build upon this work.
*   **Deeper Analysis of Pixel Control:** The use of a deconvolutional network for the pixel control Q-values is an interesting architectural choice. A brief justification or analysis of this choice would be welcome. For example, does this structure allow the agent to learn spatially coherent policies for affecting the screen? A visualization of a learned pixel control policy (e.g., an action-value map for a specific state) could provide valuable insight.
*   **"Unsupervised" Terminology:** The term "Unsupervised" in the UNREAL acronym is slightly misleading. The auxiliary tasks are more accurately described as "self-supervised," as they generate their own labels (pixel changes, rewards) from the agent's interaction stream. This is a minor terminological point but worth clarifying for precision.

#### 5. Questions for the Authors

1.  **Task Interference:** The overall objective function (Eq. 2) is a weighted sum of multiple losses. Did you observe any negative interference between the tasks during training? For instance, could the objective of maximizing pixel change (e.g., by creating an explosion) ever directly conflict with the main objective (e.g., avoiding damage)? How does the agent learn to balance these potentially competing objectives?
2.  **Generality of Auxiliary Tasks:** The proposed auxiliary tasks seem broadly applicable. Can you speculate on scenarios or environment types where they might be less effective or even detrimental? For example, in an environment with significant stochastic and uncontrollable visual noise, could pixel control mislead the agent into focusing on irrelevant dynamics?
3.  **Feature Control Details:** The results for feature control are presented as preliminary but promising. Could you elaborate on which hidden layer was used for control and why? Does controlling earlier versus later layers in the network have a different effect on the learned representation?
