### Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

**Summary of Contributions:**
This paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, which enhances the A3C framework by incorporating several auxiliary tasks designed to accelerate and stabilize learning. The core idea is that by forcing the agent's representation (learned via a shared CNN-LSTM encoder) to solve additional problems simultaneously, it will develop more general and useful features. The auxiliary tasks include: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where off-policy Q-learning is used to learn policies for maximizing pixel changes ("pixel control") or internal network activations ("feature control"), 2) an auxiliary reward prediction task, which predicts the immediate next reward from a short sequence of past observations, and 3) value function replay, which performs additional value function updates from an experience replay buffer. The authors demonstrate that this combined approach leads to substantial improvements in performance, data efficiency, and hyperparameter robustness over a strong A3C baseline on a challenging 3D environment (Labyrinth) and the 57-game Atari suite.

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons for Decision:**
1.  **Significant Lack of Technical Precision and Clarity:** The paper suffers from several ambiguous or potentially problematic formalisms in its methodology section. Key definitions are non-standard, and the overall objective function is poorly explained, making it difficult to fully assess the technical soundness of the approach.
2.  **Pervasive Formatting and Presentation Errors:** The manuscript is marred by a substantial number of inconsistencies and errors, most notably a completely corrupted bibliography. This level of carelessness is unacceptable for a top-tier publication and critically undermines confidence in the rigor of the work as a whole.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Regarding Technical Precision:**
    *   In Section 3.1, the state space `S` for the auxiliary control tasks is defined to include "the history of observations and rewards as well as the state of the agent itself, i.e. the activations of the hidden units of the network." This is a highly unusual and problematic definition. Making the reward function `r(c)` dependent on the agent's internal network activations, which are a function of the parameters `θ` being optimized, introduces a non-stationarity that is not addressed. How does this agent-dependent reward function affect the convergence properties of Q-learning? This requires significant justification.
    *   Equation (1) presents the overall objective as `argmax θ Eπ[R1:∞] + λC ∑ c∈C Eπc [R(c) 1:∞]`. This formulation is confusing as it mixes expectations over different policies (`π` and `πc`). The agent follows a single behavior policy `π`, so experience is generated on-policy. The paper then states that off-policy Q-learning is used for the auxiliary tasks, which is the correct way to learn about `πc` from `π`'s experience. However, the objective function as written is not what is being optimized. The actual optimization is on a combined loss function (Equation 2), not a combined expectation of returns. This section should be rewritten to clearly state that multiple losses, derived from different objectives, are being jointly minimized.
    *   The explanation of on-policy versus off-policy updates is muddled. Section 3.4 states that `LVR` (the value replay loss) is optimized from replayed data in *addition* to the on-policy A3C loss, of which `LVR` is already a component (as defined in Section 2). This implies the value function is updated twice with the same loss term but on different data distributions (on-policy and replayed). This is a critical detail that needs to be clarified. Is the on-policy value loss still computed, or is it replaced entirely by the replayed loss?

*   **Regarding Presentation and Formatting:**
    *   The reference section is completely broken, with BibTeX formatting errors (`Barreto et al\.,? \Q2016\E, Barreto et al\.,`) rendering it unusable. It is impossible to properly verify the paper's placement in the literature or look up cited works. This is a critical failure of manuscript preparation.
    *   There is a major structural error in Section 3. The paper describes Section 3.4 in the text, but the section numbering jumps from 3.2 to a subsection-like ".4" after presenting section 3.3. It appears sections 3.3 and 3.4 were swapped and mislabeled.
    *   Citation style is inconsistent throughout the paper. For instance, `(Sutton et al., 1999b)` is used, but also `Lin & Mitchell (1992)` and `Lample & Chaplot (2016)`. A consistent style (e.g., `\citep` vs `\citet`) should be used.
    *   The title of the subsection "Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning" in Section 4.1.1 is misleading. The auxiliary tasks are self-supervised, but the agent is still fundamentally driven by an external reward signal from the environment. "Unsupervised RL" typically refers to learning in the complete absence of extrinsic rewards.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement:**

*   In Section 4.1, the paper claims Labyrinth has "considerably richer visuals and more realistic physics" than VizDoom or Minecraft. This is a subjective claim that should be substantiated with quantitative metrics or removed.
*   The replay buffer size is stated as "2k observations". This is an ambiguous notation (2000?) and seems exceptionally small for modern deep RL. Please clarify if this is a typo.
*   The hyperparameter search seems incomplete. In Appendix B, `λRP` and `λVR` are fixed to 1, while `λPC` is tuned. The choice of these weights is crucial. Why were these not included in the random search? The fixed values could be a major confounding factor in the ablation studies.
*   In the Background (Section 2), the definition of the n-step Q-learning loss `LQ` uses `s'` without defining it. It should be explicitly stated that `s'` is `s_{t+n}`.
*   The term `doconvolutional` is used in Appendix B. The more common and precise term is "transposed convolution."
*   Notation `Nact` is used in Appendix B without definition. It is presumably the number of actions, but all notation should be explicitly defined.

While the empirical results are strong and the high-level idea of using auxiliary tasks is promising, the paper is not ready for publication in its current form. The combination of technical ambiguities and a profound lack of care in the manuscript's preparation makes it fall well below the standard expected at ICLR.
