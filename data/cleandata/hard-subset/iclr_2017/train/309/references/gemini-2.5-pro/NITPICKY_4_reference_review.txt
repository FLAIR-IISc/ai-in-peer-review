### **Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"**

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, an architecture that augments a base A3C agent with several auxiliary tasks to accelerate and improve learning. The central claim is that by learning these auxiliary tasks simultaneously with the main policy, the agent develops a richer, more robust state representation, leading to significant improvements in data efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter stability. The proposed auxiliary tasks include: 1) Auxiliary control tasks, where off-policy Q-learning is used to learn policies for maximizing pseudo-rewards like pixel changes ("pixel control") or hidden unit activations ("feature control"). 2) A reward prediction task, where the agent predicts the immediate future reward from a short sequence of past observations, trained on a balanced dataset of rewarding and non-rewarding transitions sampled from a replay buffer. 3) Value function replay, where additional value function updates are performed on transitions sampled from the replay buffer. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of the UNREAL agent on the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and on 57 Atari games, showing state-of-the-art results and substantial gains over the A3C baseline.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper is marred by a significant number of formatting errors, notational inconsistencies, and instances of unclear technical writing, which collectively obscure critical methodological details and detract from the rigor of the presentation.
2.  Key architectural details are either missing or poorly explained, making it difficult to fully understand the implementation and, consequently, to reproduce the work. The paper feels rushed and lacks the polish expected for a top-tier conference.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

While the empirical results are impressive and the high-level ideas are well-motivated, the execution of the paper is insufficiently rigorous. My decision is based on the accumulation of many "minor" flaws that together form a major barrier to confident assessment.

*   **Structural and Formatting Issues:** The paper's structure is confusing. The introduction to Section 3 promises a description of sections 3.1, 3.2, and 3.4, omitting the presented Section 3.3. Furthermore, Section 3.4 is incorrectly numbered as ".4". Most egregiously, the bibliography is completely broken, with BibTeX artifacts (`et al\.,? \Q2016\E,`) scattered throughout the reference list. This level of carelessness is unacceptable and suggests a lack of thorough proofreading.

*   **Lack of Clarity in Methodology:**
    *   **Combined Loss Function:** The definition of the final loss function in Section .4 and Equation (2) is ambiguous. It states that `LVR` is optimized from replayed data "in addition to the A3C loss (of which it is one component)". This is contradictory. It is unclear if `LA3C` in Eq. (2) contains its own on-policy value loss component, or if the `LVR` term in Eq. (2) is the *only* value loss, or if there are two separate value losses (one on-policy, one off-policy). This is a crucial detail of the algorithm that is not clearly specified.
    *   **State Space Definition:** In Section 3.1, the state space `S` for the auxiliary tasks is defined to include "the activations of the hidden units of the network." This definition is circular and non-standard. The state is typically a function of the environment and history, not the internal parameters of the function approximator that is meant to be processing the state. This needs formal clarification.
    *   **Misleading Section Title:** The "Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning" subsection in 4.1.1 is a misnomer. The experiments described are ablations of self-supervised auxiliary tasks in a standard RL setting, not unsupervised RL in the typical sense of skill discovery without extrinsic rewards.

*   **Missing Architectural Details:**
    *   A critical detail is missing in Appendix B regarding the deconvolutional head for pixel control. The text states, "The LSTM outputs are first mapped to a 32 × 7 × 7 spatial feature map". The LSTM output is a 256-dimensional vector. How is this vector reshaped into a 3D tensor? This is a non-trivial architectural choice (e.g., a linear layer followed by a reshape) that is completely omitted, making reproducibility difficult.
    *   The term "doconvolutional layer" is used in Appendix B, which is a typo for "deconvolutional".

*   **Inconsistent Notation and Typesetting:**
    *   Mathematical notation is inconsistent throughout the paper. For example, the expectation operator is sometimes italicized (`*E*`) and sometimes upright (E). The n-step return `Rt:t+n` is defined in Section 2 but the value function is then defined using `Rt:∞`.
    *   The typesetting of equations is poor. In Equation (1), the `argmax` argument `θ` should be placed underneath the operator for readability.

While any single one of these issues might be minor, their sheer number suggests a rushed and careless preparation. For a paper presenting a complex architecture with many moving parts, this lack of precision and clarity is a fatal flaw.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The empirical results are genuinely strong. A revised version of this paper with meticulous attention to the issues raised above would be very compelling. I strongly encourage the authors to thoroughly revise and resubmit.
*   The "feature control" auxiliary task is an interesting idea but feels underdeveloped. It is only briefly tested in the ablation study. Why was this not included in the final UNREAL agent? A more thorough investigation and discussion of its effects would be valuable.
*   The discussion could be strengthened by more explicitly connecting the components of UNREAL to related concepts in the literature, such as intrinsic motivation, curiosity, and empowerment. The connection to hippocampal replay is also hinted at but not explored.
*   The rebalancing strategy for reward prediction is simple and effective, but its limitations are not discussed. An analysis of how this performs in extremely sparse reward settings, where the "rewarding" buffer might be very small and lead to overfitting, would add depth to the work.

**5. Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you please provide a precise, unambiguous equation for the full UNREAL loss, clarifying how the on-policy value loss from A3C and the off-policy value replay loss `LVR` are distinct and how they are combined?
2.  Can you please specify the exact operation used to transform the 256-dimensional LSTM output vector into the 32x7x7 spatial feature map that serves as input to the deconvolutional heads for the auxiliary control tasks?
3.  What was the rationale for not including the promising "feature control" task in the final UNREAL agent configuration, given it performed almost as well as pixel control in the ablation?
