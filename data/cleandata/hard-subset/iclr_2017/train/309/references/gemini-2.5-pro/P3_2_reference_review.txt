**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent architecture designed to improve sample efficiency, final performance, and robustness. The core idea is to augment a strong on-policy actor-critic agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned concurrently from the same stream of experience. These tasks are not used for control directly but serve to shape the agent's internal representation, making it more effective for the main RL objective. The authors propose three specific auxiliary tasks: (1) Auxiliary Control Tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions to maximize local pixel changes (pixel control) or internal network activations (feature control); (2) Auxiliary Reward Prediction, where the agent predicts the immediate next reward from a short sequence of observations, trained on a reward-balanced replay buffer; and (3) Value Function Replay, which provides additional off-policy updates for the main task's value function. The UNREAL agent intelligently combines on-policy A3C for the main policy with off-policy Q-learning and experience replay for the auxiliary tasks and value function bootstrapping. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach through extensive experiments on a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth and the standard 57-game Atari suite, showing state-of-the-art results that significantly outperform the A3C baseline in terms of learning speed and final score.

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The key reasons for my decision are:
1.  **Novel and Principled Method:** The UNREAL architecture is a clever and well-motivated combination of state-of-the-art on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (DQN-style replay) learning. The use of auxiliary tasks for representation learning is not new in principle, but its specific instantiation here—particularly the off-policy pixel control and reward-balanced reward prediction—is novel and highly effective.
2.  **Exceptionally Strong and Thorough Empirical Evaluation:** The paper's claims are backed by rigorous and extensive experiments. The introduction and evaluation on the Labyrinth environment is a valuable contribution in its own right. Crucially, the authors provide comprehensive ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each component of their system. The comparison of pixel *control* versus pixel *prediction* and *reconstruction* provides deep insight into why the proposed method is effective. The scale of the Atari evaluation and the analysis of hyperparameter robustness further strengthen the paper's conclusions.

**Supporting Arguments**

The primary strength of this work lies in its thoughtful architectural design and the meticulous experiments that validate it. The paper addresses a critical challenge in deep RL: data efficiency. By forcing the agent's shared representation (the CNN and LSTM) to learn features useful for a diverse set of objectives, the authors demonstrate a powerful method for accelerating learning.

The distinction between how different components are trained is particularly insightful. The main policy is trained on-policy with A3C, which is well-suited for continuous control and recurrent architectures. In parallel, the auxiliary tasks and value function are trained off-policy from a replay buffer. This hybrid approach gets the best of both worlds: the stability and exploratory nature of on-policy learning for the main objective, and the superior data efficiency of off-policy learning for the representation-shaping auxiliary tasks. The specific choice of tasks is also well-justified. Pixel control encourages the agent to learn about cause and effect in its environment—an elemental form of model learning. Reward prediction, especially with skewed replay, directly focuses the representation on reward-salient features, which is critical in sparse-reward settings.

The experimental results are impressive. In the challenging Labyrinth environment, UNREAL achieves more than double the performance of a strong A3C baseline, while demonstrating a 10x median speedup. The ablation studies in Figure 3 clearly show that each component adds value, with the full UNREAL agent performing best. The results in Figure 5 are particularly illuminating, demonstrating that learning to *control* changes is more beneficial than merely *predicting* them or *reconstructing* the input, providing strong evidence for the "unsupervised reinforcement learning" aspect of the work. The state-of-the-art results on Atari confirm the generality of the approach beyond a single domain.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is a strong paper, the following points could be addressed to further improve its clarity and impact:

*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper convincingly demonstrates improvements in sample efficiency. However, adding multiple auxiliary heads (especially the deconvolutional network for pixel control) and performing off-policy updates from a replay buffer must incur significant computational cost per environment step. A brief discussion of the trade-off between sample efficiency and wall-clock training time/computational complexity would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practicality.
*   **Feature Control:** The idea of using network features as control objectives is very compelling. However, it is presented as a preliminary result in the "Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning" subsection and seems to be absent from the main ablation studies and the final UNREAL agent tested on Atari. It would be beneficial to clarify if feature control was part of the final UNREAL agent configuration or if it was only explored in isolation. If it was excluded, a short explanation of why (e.g., less effective than pixel control, computationally prohibitive) would be helpful.
*   **Interplay of Auxiliary Tasks:** The losses for the auxiliary tasks are simply summed with weighting coefficients. It would be interesting to include a brief discussion on the potential for negative interference or synergy between the tasks. For instance, could the objective of maximizing pixel change ever conflict with the main objective of maximizing environmental reward in a way that harms the agent's policy?

**Clarifying Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks compared to the A3C baseline? Specifically, what is the approximate increase in GPU/CPU time per training step?
2.  The hyperparameter for the pixel control loss weight (λPC) was sampled from different ranges for Labyrinth and Atari. Could you provide some intuition as to why this was necessary? Is the method sensitive to this parameter, or is there a principled way to set it based on environment characteristics (e.g., the typical magnitude of pixel changes)?
3.  For the reward prediction task, you used a 50/50 sampling split between zero and non-zero reward transitions. How was this specific ratio chosen, and did you experiment with other ratios? How crucial is this balanced sampling to the performance gains from this auxiliary task?
