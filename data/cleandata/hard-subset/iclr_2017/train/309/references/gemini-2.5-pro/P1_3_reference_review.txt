### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, robustness, and overall performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a strong baseline agent, Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), with a suite of auxiliary tasks that are trained jointly with the main policy. These tasks are designed to promote the learning of a richer and more versatile state representation.

The contributions are threefold:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards, such as maximizing pixel changes in different regions of the input (pixel control) or maximizing the activation of hidden units in its own network (feature control). These tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of past observations. To handle sparse rewards, this task is trained on experience replayed from a buffer with a skewed sampling strategy that over-represents rewarding transitions.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** The agent re-trains its value function on recently experienced trajectories stored in a replay buffer, allowing for additional value iteration steps and better leveraging of features learned via the other auxiliary tasks.

The paper demonstrates the effectiveness of this combined approach through extensive experiments on two challenging domains: the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment and the 57-game Atari suite. The UNREAL agent is shown to drastically outperform the A3C baseline, achieving state-of-the-art results, significantly faster learning (a 10x speedup on Labyrinth), and improved robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a very strong paper that presents a novel and impactful contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The UNREAL agent achieves substantial, not just incremental, performance gains over a state-of-the-art baseline (A3C) on two distinct and challenging benchmark suites. The reported 10x improvement in data efficiency on Labyrinth and the new state-of-the-art mean score of 880% on Atari are highly significant.
2.  **Rigorous Scientific Methodology:** The paper supports its claims with thorough experimentation, including comprehensive ablation studies that demonstrate the individual and combined benefits of each auxiliary task. The comparison of pixel control against simpler baselines like reconstruction and prediction provides strong evidence for the specific design choices made.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's strength lies in its clear hypothesis—that auxiliary tasks can improve representation learning—and the compelling evidence provided to support it. The architecture cleverly combines on-policy learning for the main task (A3C) with off-policy learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach allows the agent to benefit from the stability of A3C while leveraging the data efficiency of experience replay for representation learning.

The choice of auxiliary tasks is well-motivated. Pixel control encourages the agent to understand cause and effect in its visual environment, a general skill that is useful beyond any specific game objective. The reward prediction task, combined with prioritized sampling, directly addresses the common challenge of sparse rewards by forcing the network to become sensitive to reward-predictive states.

The experimental setup is exemplary. Using the novel and visually rich Labyrinth environment is a contribution in itself, pushing the boundaries of what current agents can handle. The results are not just cherry-picked; the paper shows performance curves averaged over multiple levels and demonstrates robustness across a wide range of hyperparameters (Figure 3), which is a crucial aspect for practical applications. The ablation study (Figure 3, top left) clearly shows a cumulative improvement as each component is added, which convincingly argues that the final UNREAL agent's success is not due to a single trick but a synergistic combination of well-designed components. The comparison in Figure 5, showing that learning to *control* pixel changes is more effective than just *predicting* them or *reconstructing* the input, provides deep insight into why the proposed method works and is a highlight of the paper's analysis.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could further strengthen it:

*   **Computational Cost:** The UNREAL agent adds several network heads and loss computations. A discussion of the computational overhead (in terms of training time or FLOPs) compared to the A3C baseline would be valuable. While the agent is more data-efficient in terms of environment steps, it would be useful to know the trade-off in terms of computational resources.
*   **Intuition on Hyperparameter λPC:** The paper notes that the weight for the pixel control loss (λPC) was sampled from different ranges for Labyrinth and Atari. It would be helpful to provide more intuition on why this was necessary and how sensitive the agent is to this parameter. Is it primarily related to the general scale of pixel changes in each domain?
*   **Generalizability of Auxiliary Tasks:** The chosen auxiliary tasks are well-suited for first-person visual environments. A brief discussion on how one might design analogous tasks for other domains (e.g., robotics with proprioceptive inputs, language-based tasks) could broaden the paper's impact and guide future work.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The A3C agent learns on-policy, while the auxiliary tasks learn off-policy from a replay buffer storing the "most recent 2k observations." Does this mean the replay buffer is essentially a sliding window of the most recent experience generated by the current policy? If so, have you considered the effects of using a larger buffer that contains data from older policies?
2.  Could you provide more detail on the performance of the "feature control" auxiliary task? It is mentioned as a promising future direction and shown on one level in Figure 5, where it performs very well. Was it evaluated more broadly, and if so, how did it compare to pixel control across the full set of environments?
3.  The deconvolutional architecture for the pixel control Q-values is an interesting choice that leverages spatial locality. Did you experiment with simpler architectures (e.g., a single fully-connected layer outputting Q-values for each grid cell independently) and find the deconvolutional approach to be superior?
