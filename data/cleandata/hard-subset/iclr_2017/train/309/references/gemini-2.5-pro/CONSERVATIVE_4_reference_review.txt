**Summary**

The paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, which augments the state-of-the-art A3C agent with a set of auxiliary tasks designed to accelerate representation learning and improve data efficiency. The core idea is to train the agent's shared feature extractor (a CNN-LSTM) on multiple objectives simultaneously. These auxiliary tasks include: 1) off-policy auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns Q-functions to maximize pixel changes in the input ("pixel control") or activations in a hidden layer ("feature control"); 2) a reward prediction task, where a separate network predicts immediate rewards from recent history, trained on a balanced replay buffer to handle sparse rewards; and 3) value function replay, which performs additional value function updates from the replay buffer. The authors evaluate UNREAL on a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth and on the 57-game Atari suite. The empirical results are impressive, showing that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline in terms of final performance, learning speed (a claimed 10x speedup), and robustness to hyperparameters.

**Decision: Accept**

While I am skeptical of the "kitchen sink" approach of combining multiple, seemingly disparate auxiliary tasks, the empirical results are too strong to ignore. The paper demonstrates a substantial improvement over a very strong baseline (A3C) on two complex and distinct domains. The thorough ablation studies provide convincing evidence that the proposed components are indeed beneficial.

**Key Reasons for Decision:**

1.  **Strong Empirical Performance:** The primary strength of this paper is its results. Achieving an average human-normalized score of 87% on the new Labyrinth environment (up from 54% for A3C) and 880% mean on Atari is a significant achievement. The claimed 10x improvement in data efficiency is a particularly important contribution for deep RL, where sample efficiency remains a major challenge. These results are well-documented with clear learning curves and ablation studies.
2.  **Thorough Ablation Study:** The authors systematically evaluate the contribution of each auxiliary task (Pixel Control, Reward Prediction, Value Replay) in Figure 3. The results clearly show that each component adds value, and their combination yields the best performance. This addresses the immediate concern that the performance gain might be attributable to just one of the introduced mechanisms. The comparison of pixel control against pixel prediction and reconstruction further strengthens the methodological claims.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper is methodologically sound in its implementation. It builds upon A3C, a well-established and powerful baseline, ensuring that the comparisons are relevant. The use of a replay buffer to facilitate off-policy training of the auxiliary tasks while the main policy learns on-policy is a reasonable design choice that leverages the benefits of both approaches. The introduction of Labyrinth is a valuable contribution, providing a new challenging benchmark for the community.

However, the work is not without its weaknesses. The main concern is the lack of a unifying principle or deep justification for the specific combination of auxiliary tasks. The approach feels more like an engineering solution—bolting together several good ideas that were found to work—rather than a principled advancement. The paper proposes learning to *control* pixel changes and internal features, which is an interesting idea. However, the motivation for why control is superior to simpler predictive tasks is not fully fleshed out beyond the empirical comparison in Figure 5. It feels like an assortment of techniques (off-policy Q-learning, supervised prediction, experience replay) has been added to A3C, increasing complexity substantially. While the results justify the final agent, it is unclear if this is the most elegant or efficient way to achieve this performance boost.

Furthermore, the complexity of the UNREAL agent is a significant concern. It introduces several new architectural components (e.g., the deconvolutional head for pixel control, the separate network for reward prediction) and hyperparameters (λVR, λPC, λRP). While the paper claims improved robustness to the A3C hyperparameters (learning rate, entropy), it does not adequately discuss the sensitivity to these new weighting parameters, which are critical for balancing the various losses.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The paper could be strengthened by providing more intuition or analysis on *why* these tasks are so effective. For example, does pixel control force the agent to learn about causality and object interactions in the environment? Does reward prediction help the agent identify reward-salient features that the value function might otherwise take longer to discover? Visualizations of the learned features or attention maps would be highly beneficial.
*   The justification for some architectural choices is thin. For instance, why is the reward prediction task handled by a separate feedforward network rather than being an additional output of the main LSTM? The paper states this is to "simplify the temporal aspects," but a more detailed explanation would be welcome.
*   A discussion on the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks would be useful. While data efficiency is improved, what is the cost in terms of wall-clock time or computational resources per update step?

**Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you provide a more cohesive motivation for why this specific set of auxiliary tasks (control, reward prediction, value replay) is the right combination? Was this set derived from a theoretical standpoint, or was it the result of empirical trial and error?
2.  The paper combines on-policy A3C updates with off-policy updates from a replay buffer, all of which update a shared representation. This can potentially lead to conflicting gradients and instability. Did you observe any such issues? How does the UNREAL agent balance the on-policy and off-policy nature of its updates to the shared parameters?
3.  Regarding the loss weighting hyperparameters: λVR and λRP were fixed to 1.0, while λPC was sampled from a log-uniform distribution. What is the justification for this difference in treatment? How sensitive is the agent's performance to the choice of λVR and λRP? A sensitivity analysis for all three lambda terms would significantly strengthen the paper's claims of robustness.
