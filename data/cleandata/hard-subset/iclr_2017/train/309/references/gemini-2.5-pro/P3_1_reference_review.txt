### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel agent architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong baseline agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained jointly with the main policy. These tasks are designed to shape the agent's internal representation, forcing it to learn features that are broadly useful for understanding and interacting with the environment, especially when the main reward signal is sparse.

The authors propose three specific auxiliary tasks:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy Q-functions to maximize two types of pseudo-rewards: changes in pixel intensity within a grid overlaying the input ("pixel control") and activations of its own hidden units ("feature control"). These tasks encourage the agent to learn how its actions affect its perception and internal state, promoting active exploration and interaction.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent predicts the immediate reward from a short sequence of recent observations. To combat reward sparsity, this task is trained on experience sampled from a replay buffer in a skewed manner, over-representing rewarding transitions. This helps the representation quickly learn to identify reward-predicting states.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** In addition to the on-policy A3C value updates, the agent performs extra value function regression on data sampled from the replay buffer, improving data efficiency through off-policy value iteration.

The paper presents a compelling empirical evaluation on two challenging domains: the visually complex 3D "Labyrinth" environment and the standard 57 Atari games. The results demonstrate that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline, achieving a 10x speedup in learning, more than doubling the final human-normalized score on Labyrinth, and setting a new state-of-the-art on the Atari suite.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of deep reinforcement learning. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents a novel and coherent framework for integrating auxiliary tasks into a state-of-the-art RL agent. The specific choice of tasks—particularly the "control" objectives—is well-justified and provides a powerful, unsupervised signal for representation learning that goes beyond simple prediction or reconstruction. The hybrid on-policy/off-policy architecture is an elegant solution to efficiently train these diverse objectives from a single stream of experience.
2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experiments are thorough and provide strong evidence for the claimed benefits. The use of the new, challenging Labyrinth environment demonstrates the method's applicability to complex 3D worlds. Crucially, the authors include extensive ablation studies that clearly isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is a particularly valuable and often-neglected aspect of DRL research, which strongly supports the practical utility of the proposed method.

### Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in how it thoughtfully combines several ideas into a single, effective agent. While the concept of auxiliary tasks is not entirely new, the specific instantiation here is novel and powerful. The distinction made in the related work section is key: unlike prior work on options or pseudo-rewards, the auxiliary policies are used *solely* for representation learning, not for high-level planning. This simplifies the framework while retaining the representational benefits.

The design of the auxiliary tasks is particularly insightful. The pixel control task provides a dense, intrinsic motivation signal that encourages interaction with dynamic elements of the environment, a crucial behavior for exploration. The reward prediction task, coupled with skewed replay, directly addresses the credit assignment problem in sparse reward settings by forcing the network to become sensitive to reward-predictive features. The ablation study in Figure 3 (top left) convincingly shows that each of these components contributes positively to the final performance, with their combination yielding the best results.

The experimental results are impressive. A 10x speedup to reach the baseline's peak performance is a dramatic improvement in data efficiency. Achieving 87% mean human-normalized score on Labyrinth, up from 54% for A3C, is a step-change in performance. The comparison against alternative unsupervised tasks like input reconstruction (Figure 5) is important, as it demonstrates that learning to *control* the environment provides a richer training signal than simply learning to passively observe or predict it. This finding has significant implications for future work in unsupervised reinforcement learning. The Atari results confirm that the benefits are not limited to a single domain, establishing UNREAL as a generally applicable and state-of-the-art method.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is excellent, the following points could further strengthen it:

*   **Analysis of Computational Cost:** The addition of multiple auxiliary network heads and off-policy training from a replay buffer inevitably increases the computational cost per training step. It would be beneficial to quantify this overhead. While the massive gains in data efficiency (sample complexity) likely outweigh the increased computational complexity, a brief discussion of wall-clock time trade-offs would provide a more complete picture.
*   **Deeper Intuition on Control Tasks:** The paper provides a high-level motivation for pixel and feature control, but a qualitative analysis could offer deeper insight. For instance, visualizing the agent's behavior while it follows a specific pixel control policy (e.g., maximizing change in the top-left grid cell) could be very illustrative. Does it learn to turn towards moving objects or actively manipulate parts of the scene?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper admirably tests for robustness by sampling hyperparameters. However, the combined loss function includes several weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP). While λPC is sampled, λVR and λRP are fixed to 1. A brief discussion on the sensitivity to these specific weights would be helpful. Are they robust, or were they carefully tuned?
*   **Choice of Replay Buffer Size:** The replay buffer is notably small (2000 transitions), focusing the off-policy updates on very recent experience. This is an interesting design choice that differs from the large buffers typically used in DQN. A sentence justifying this choice—for example, that it balances stability with keeping the off-policy data distribution close to the current on-policy one—would clarify the rationale.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  The deconvolutional architecture for the pixel control Q-values is an interesting choice that leverages spatial locality. How crucial is this specific architecture to the performance of pixel control? Have you experimented with a simpler, non-spatial head (e.g., a fully-connected layer) for these auxiliary targets?
2.  Could you elaborate on the choice of a small (2k frames) replay buffer? Does performance degrade significantly with larger buffers, perhaps due to the off-policy data becoming too stale relative to the on-policy A3C updates?
3.  The pixel control task seems universally applicable, but can you foresee any environments where it might be a distracting or even detrimental signal? For example, in a game with significant background animations or visual noise that are irrelevant to the task, could the agent waste capacity learning to control these noisy elements?
