### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a deep reinforcement learning architecture designed to improve learning speed, final performance, and robustness. The core idea is to augment a strong baseline agent, Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained jointly with the main policy. These auxiliary tasks are designed to facilitate more efficient and effective representation learning, particularly in visually complex environments with sparse rewards.

The key contributions are the introduction and combination of three main auxiliary components:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy to maximize pseudo-rewards, such as maximizing pixel changes in different regions of the screen (pixel control) or maximizing the activations of its own hidden units (feature control). These tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment and its own internal representations, purely for the benefit of feature learning.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained on a supervised task to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of recent observations. To combat reward sparsity, training samples are drawn from an experience replay buffer in a balanced way, over-sampling rewarding transitions. This helps the agent’s representation become more sensitive to reward-predictive states.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** The agent utilizes an experience replay buffer to perform additional off-policy updates to its value function, accelerating the propagation of value information through the state space.

The paper demonstrates through extensive experiments on a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth and the 57-game Atari suite that the UNREAL agent significantly outperforms the A3C baseline. The results show dramatic improvements in data efficiency (up to a 10x speedup), higher final scores, and greater robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This paper is a clear accept for two primary reasons:
1.  **Significant Performance Improvement:** The proposed UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art results on the challenging Atari benchmark and demonstrates a massive improvement over a strong A3C baseline on the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment. The claimed 10x speedup in data efficiency is substantial and empirically well-supported.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Evaluation:** The claims are supported by an extensive set of experiments, including comprehensive ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task. The comparison of pixel control against simpler objectives like prediction and reconstruction provides strong evidence for the central hypothesis that learning to control the environment is a powerful unsupervised objective for representation learning.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

**Clarity:** The paper is exceptionally well-written and easy to follow. The motivation for using auxiliary tasks is clearly articulated, and each component of the UNREAL architecture is introduced logically and described in sufficient detail. The diagrams are helpful in understanding the overall architecture and the flow of information. The experimental setup and results are presented clearly, allowing for straightforward interpretation of the agent's performance gains.

**Technical Soundness:** The proposed method is technically sound. It cleverly combines an on-policy algorithm (A3C) for the main objective with off-policy learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach allows the agent to benefit from the stability of A3C while leveraging the data efficiency of off-policy updates for representation learning. The specific choice of auxiliary tasks is well-motivated: pixel control encourages learning about environment dynamics, while reward prediction focuses the representation on salient, task-relevant events. The implementation details provided in the appendix appear sufficient for reproducibility.

**Novelty:** While the concept of auxiliary tasks is not new in machine learning, this paper makes several novel contributions. The primary novelty lies in the specific combination of these particular auxiliary objectives within a single, coherent agent architecture. The idea of using "pixel control"—learning off-policy value functions for maximizing local pixel changes—is a creative and effective method for unsupervised exploration and representation learning. Furthermore, the empirical demonstration that this control-based objective is superior to more passive objectives like reconstruction or prediction (Figure 5) is an important and novel finding for the field. The overall UNREAL framework provides a strong and compelling blueprint for how to effectively integrate auxiliary tasks into modern deep RL agents.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are intended as constructive feedback to help strengthen the paper further and are not critical to the decision.
*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper focuses on data efficiency in terms of environment steps. A brief discussion on the computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time, GPU/CPU usage) of adding the auxiliary tasks would provide a more complete picture. While the agent learns faster, it also performs more computation per update, which is a relevant trade-off for practitioners.
*   **Sensitivity to Loss Weights:** The final loss function includes several weighting hyperparameters (λVR, λPC, λRP). While the paper shows impressive robustness to learning rate and entropy cost, an analysis of the agent's sensitivity to these λ weights would be valuable. The appendix notes they were sampled from a distribution, but understanding their impact on performance could offer further insights.
*   **Feature Control:** The "feature control" task is introduced as a promising direction but is only evaluated preliminarily on a single level. While the results are positive, this component feels less developed than the others. Either expanding this section with more results or framing it more explicitly as preliminary future work would improve clarity.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   Could you elaborate on the choice of a deconvolutional network for the pixel control Q-values? What is the intuition behind this architectural choice, and did you experiment with simpler alternatives (e.g., a fully connected layer outputting a flattened grid)?
*   The value replay component (LVR) is ablated together with reward prediction (RP) in Figure 3 (top left), as "A3C + VR + RP". Could you clarify the individual contribution of value replay? Is it synergistic with reward prediction, as suggested in the text, and does it provide benefits on its own?
*   In reward prediction, the sampling is balanced between zero and non-zero rewards. For environments with varied reward scales (e.g., small positive vs. large positive), did you consider a more nuanced sampling or a regression-based loss instead of classification? How do you think that might affect the learned representation?
