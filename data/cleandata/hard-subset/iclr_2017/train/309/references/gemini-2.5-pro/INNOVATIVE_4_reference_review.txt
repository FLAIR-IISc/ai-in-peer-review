### Review of "Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning"

**Summary of Claims and Contributions**
This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to accelerate and improve deep reinforcement learning by augmenting a standard agent with a suite of auxiliary tasks. The core idea is that by forcing an agent to learn about its environment in multiple ways simultaneously, it can develop a richer and more versatile representation, which in turn benefits the primary goal of reward maximization. The authors build upon a strong A3C baseline and add three main auxiliary components: (1) Auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions to maximize local changes in pixel intensity (pixel control) or its own internal network activations (feature control); (2) An auxiliary reward prediction task, which predicts immediate future rewards from a short sequence of past observations, using a biased replay scheme to oversample rare reward events; and (3) Value function replay, which performs additional value function updates from the replay buffer. The key contribution is the combination of these elements into a single, cohesive architecture that synergistically improves performance. The paper claims this approach leads to drastically improved data efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameters, backing this up with extensive experiments on the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and the 57-game Atari suite.

**Decision: Strong Accept**

This paper presents a bold, creative, and highly effective approach to a critical problem in reinforcement learning: efficient representation learning in complex environments with sparse rewards. The two key reasons for my strong recommendation for acceptance are:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The paper introduces a compelling framework for representation learning by reframing it as a multi-objective control problem. The concept of "auxiliary control"—learning policies specifically to manipulate the agent's own perceptual input or internal state—is a significant and inspiring idea. It moves beyond passive, predictive auxiliary tasks (like reconstruction or future frame prediction) and toward active, purposeful interaction as a mechanism for learning. This is a powerful conceptual shift that opens up numerous avenues for future research.

2.  **Overwhelmingly Strong Empirical Evidence:** The experimental results are not merely incremental; they are transformative. The UNREAL agent achieves a staggering leap in performance over a state-of-the-art A3C baseline, more than doubling the mean human-normalized score in the visually rich Labyrinth environment and setting a new state-of-the-art on Atari. Perhaps more importantly, the claimed 10x improvement in data efficiency addresses one of the most significant bottlenecks in deep RL. The thorough ablation studies convincingly demonstrate that each auxiliary component contributes to the final performance, and the explicit analysis of hyperparameter robustness provides strong evidence of the method's practical utility.

**Supporting Arguments**

The core strength of this work lies in its thoughtful architectural design, which elegantly combines on-policy learning for the main task with more data-efficient off-policy learning for the auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach leverages the stability of A3C while using a replay buffer to efficiently train the dense, self-supervised signals from the auxiliary objectives. This is a sophisticated and effective engineering choice.

The comparison of pixel control against pixel prediction and reconstruction in Section 4.1.1 is particularly insightful. It provides strong evidence for the central hypothesis that learning to control is a more powerful objective for representation learning than simply learning to predict or reconstruct. This result alone is a valuable contribution to the field. While one could argue that some of the specific design choices (e.g., the grid size for pixel control) are heuristic, the underlying principle is sound and the results speak for themselves. This is exactly the kind of bold, high-risk, high-reward research that the community should encourage. I am willing to overlook minor questions about methodological specifics in light of the groundbreaking results and the significance of the core ideas.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While these points do not detract from my decision, they could help strengthen the paper further:

*   **Further Intuition on Auxiliary Tasks:** The paper could benefit from a deeper discussion of *why* these specific auxiliary tasks are so effective. For instance, does pixel control encourage the agent to learn about object boundaries and movable entities? Does reward prediction help disentangle reward-relevant visual features from distractors? Providing more qualitative analysis or visualizations (beyond the linked video) could strengthen the reader's intuition.
*   **Interplay between Tasks:** The ablation shows that the components are synergistic. It would be interesting to speculate on the nature of this synergy. For example, does the rich gradient from pixel control help create features that the sparse reward prediction task can then more easily leverage?
*   **A Note on "Unsupervised":** The term "unsupervised" in the title and agent name might be slightly misleading, as the auxiliary tasks are self-supervised, and the main task is still driven by an external reward. While this is a common convention, clarifying this distinction could prevent confusion.

**Questions for the Authors**

To deepen my understanding, I would be interested in the authors' thoughts on the following:

1.  The policies learned for the auxiliary control tasks are used only to generate gradients for the shared representation. Have you considered using these policies directly, for example, as a set of intrinsic options for exploration or hierarchical planning? This seems like a natural and exciting next step.
2.  Feature control appeared highly effective in the comparison shown in Figure 5, nearly matching pixel control. Why was it not included in the final UNREAL agent that was benchmarked on Atari? Is it more computationally demanding, less stable across many tasks, or simply left for future work?
3.  The replay buffer size is set to 2k, which is relatively small. This suggests the agent benefits most from learning on very recent data. Did you experiment with larger buffer sizes, and if so, how did it impact the performance of the off-policy auxiliary tasks?
