### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a primary reinforcement learning agent with a set of auxiliary tasks that share a common representation (a convolutional and recurrent neural network). This forces the shared representation to learn general, useful features about the environment, which in turn accelerates learning on the main task.

The authors build upon the strong Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) baseline. They propose a hybrid on-policy/off-policy training scheme where the main A3C policy is trained on-policy, while the auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy using experience sampled from a replay buffer. This is a key architectural insight, allowing the data-hungry auxiliary tasks to learn efficiently without being constrained by the on-policy nature of the main algorithm.

The specific auxiliary tasks introduced are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy Q-functions to maximize pseudo-rewards. Two variants are proposed: *Pixel Control*, which incentivizes the agent to learn policies that maximally change pixel values in different grid cells of the input image, and *Feature Control*, which does the same for activations in its own hidden layers.
2.  **Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate reward from a short sequence of recent observations. Critically, training samples are drawn from the replay buffer in a skewed manner to over-represent rare rewarding events, helping the agent learn features relevant to the sparse primary reward signal.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** In addition to the on-policy value updates of A3C, value function regression is also performed on sequences sampled from the replay buffer, effectively performing additional value iteration steps.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their UNREAL agent through extensive experiments on two challenging domains: the visually rich, first-person 3D Labyrinth environment and the standard 57-game Atari benchmark. The results are impressive, showing that UNREAL achieves a 10x speedup to reach the baseline's peak performance, more than doubles the human-normalized score on Labyrinth, and establishes a new state-of-the-art on Atari.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Clear Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Novel and Effective Combination of Ideas:** The paper presents a well-motivated and elegant framework that thoughtfully combines on-policy learning for the main objective with off-policy learning for auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach effectively leverages the stability of policy gradients for the main task while using the data efficiency of experience replay for representation learning. The specific choices of auxiliary tasks, particularly pixel *control* over mere prediction, are insightful and empirically validated.
2.  **Extensive and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are comprehensive and compelling. The authors not only demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the standard Atari benchmark but also introduce and test on Labyrinth, a challenging 3D domain that pushes the boundaries of current methods. The inclusion of thorough ablation studies, which clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary component, and robustness analysis across hyperparameters, significantly strengthens the paper's claims.

### 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary strength lies in its principled approach to representation learning in RL. While using auxiliary tasks is not a new idea, the specific implementation here is novel and highly effective. By decoupling the auxiliary learning into an off-policy process using a replay buffer, the agent can learn about environmental dynamics and reward structures from each piece of experience multiple times, a significant advantage over purely on-policy methods.

The choice of auxiliary tasks is well-justified. The reward prediction task with skewed sampling directly addresses the problem of sparse rewards by forcing the network's early layers to become sensitive to reward-predictive states. The auxiliary control tasks are particularly innovative. The authors' experiment comparing pixel *control* against pixel *prediction* and pixel *reconstruction* (Figure 5) provides strong evidence for their hypothesis that learning to influence the environment is a more powerful representation learning signal than passively modeling it. This is a significant finding for the field.

The empirical results are outstanding. On Labyrinth, achieving an average of 87% human-normalized score (up from 54% for A3C) is a massive leap forward for RL in complex 3D environments. The 10x data efficiency gain is of huge practical importance, as training these large models is computationally expensive. The fact that the same architecture also achieves state-of-the-art results on Atari demonstrates its generality. The robustness analysis (Figure 3, right panels) is another major contribution, showing that UNREAL is not only higher-performing but also easier to tune, which is a critical, often-overlooked aspect of RL algorithms.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While this is an excellent paper, a few points could be clarified or expanded upon to further increase its impact:
*   **Computational Cost:** The UNREAL architecture adds several new components (replay buffer, multiple network heads, off-policy updates). It would be beneficial to include a brief discussion of the increase in computational overhead (e.g., wall-clock training time, memory usage) compared to the A3C baseline. This would give practitioners a clearer picture of the trade-offs involved.
*   **Sensitivity to λ Hyperparameters:** The combined loss function in Equation 2 includes several weighting terms (λ). The paper mentions these are sampled during the hyperparameter search, but a short discussion on the sensitivity of the final performance to these weights would be valuable. For instance, is the performance robust across a wide range of λ values, or is careful tuning required?
*   **Feature Control Details:** The results for the feature control task are presented as preliminary and only on a subset of environments. While pixel control is clearly the main focus, the concept of an agent learning to control its own internal representations is fascinating. Expanding slightly on the motivation or potential advantages/disadvantages of feature control versus pixel control would be interesting, even if the empirical results are not as complete.
*   **Justification for Deconvolutional Head:** The use of a deconvolutional network for the pixel control Q-values is an interesting architectural choice. A sentence or two in Section 3.1 or Appendix B justifying this decision (e.g., to efficiently produce spatially-corresponding Q-values while sharing parameters across the grid) would strengthen the description of the method.

### 5. Questions for Authors

*   The replay buffer is populated by the on-policy A3C agent. As the A3C policy improves and changes, the distribution of experience in the buffer will also shift. Did you observe any stability issues or challenges related to training the off-policy auxiliary Q-learners from this non-stationary behavior distribution?
*   The pixel control task seems particularly well-suited for first-person navigation tasks where the agent's actions have direct and local visual consequences. How do you see this specific auxiliary task generalizing to other types of environments, such as those with a static top-down view or more abstract state representations where the link between action and pixel change is less direct?
*   Could you comment on the interaction between the different auxiliary tasks? The results show that combining them is beneficial, but did you observe any interesting emergent behaviors? For example, does the reward prediction task guide the exploratory policies learned by the pixel control task towards more rewarding areas of the state space?
