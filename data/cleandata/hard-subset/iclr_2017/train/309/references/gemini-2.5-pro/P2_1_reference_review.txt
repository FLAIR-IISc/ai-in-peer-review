### **1. Summary**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a strong on-policy baseline agent, Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), with a suite of auxiliary tasks that are trained off-policy from a replay buffer. These tasks are designed to promote the learning of a rich and general state representation in the shared lower-level network layers.

The proposed auxiliary tasks are of two main types:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns policies to maximize internally-generated pseudo-rewards. The paper explores "pixel control," where the agent learns to maximize pixel changes in different regions of the screen, and "feature control," where it learns to maximize the activations of its own hidden units. These tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment and its own representation.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Tasks:** To combat reward sparsity, the agent is trained on a "reward prediction" task, where it predicts the immediate reward from a recent sequence of observations. This is trained using a skewed replay strategy that oversamples rewarding experiences, helping the network's features become more sensitive to reward-predictive states without biasing the main policy.

Additionally, the replay buffer is used for extra "value function replay" updates, further improving the learning of the main value function. The paper provides an extensive empirical evaluation on the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment and the 57-game Atari benchmark, demonstrating state-of-the-art results and showing through thorough ablation studies that each component contributes significantly to the overall improvement.

### **2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The key reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents a novel and effective synthesis of on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (Q-learning from replay) methods. This hybrid approach intelligently leverages the strengths of both paradigms: the stability of on-policy learning for the main task and the superior data efficiency of off-policy learning for training the representation via auxiliary tasks. The choice of auxiliary tasks is well-justified and tackles fundamental challenges in RL, such as feature learning and sparse rewards.

2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results and Rigorous Evaluation:** The paper demonstrates not just incremental gains but a dramatic improvement over a state-of-the-art baseline on two challenging and distinct domains. The reported 10x speedup and doubling of performance on the Labyrinth environment are particularly impressive. The evaluation is scientifically rigorous, including comprehensive ablation studies that clearly isolate the benefits of each component, comparisons to alternative auxiliary tasks, and a crucial analysis of the method's robustness to hyperparameters.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper's claims are very well supported by the evidence provided.

*   **Placement in Literature and Motivation:** The related work section is clear and correctly positions the work relative to prior approaches like Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. It accurately identifies its unique contribution: using pseudo-reward policies *solely* for representation learning, rather than for hierarchical control. The motivation for each auxiliary task is compelling and directly addresses known weaknesses of deep RL agents.

*   **Technical Soundness:** The architecture is sensible and well-described. The use of a deconvolutional network for the spatially-aware pixel control task is a clever architectural choice. The combined loss function (Equation 2) provides a clear framework for integrating the various learning signals. The use of a small replay buffer for off-policy updates on recent experience is a practical way to combine these different learning schemes.

*   **Empirical Strength:** The experimental results are the main strength of this paper.
    *   **Labyrinth Environment:** Demonstrating success on a new, visually rich 3D environment with complex dynamics adds significant weight to the claims, showing the method is not just tailored to 2D arcade games.
    *   **Atari Benchmark:** Achieving state-of-the-art results on the standard Atari benchmark proves the general applicability and power of the UNREAL agent.
    *   **Ablation Studies:** Figure 3 provides a clear and convincing story. The performance of the A3C baseline is successively and substantially improved as each auxiliary component (Value Replay, Reward Prediction, Pixel Control) is added. This leaves little doubt that the proposed combination is effective and that each part plays a role.
    *   **Comparison to Alternatives:** The experiment in Figure 5, which compares auxiliary *control* to auxiliary *prediction* and *reconstruction*, is crucial. It supports the paper's central hypothesis that learning to control the environment is a more powerful objective for representation learning than passively predicting or reconstructing observations.
    *   **Robustness Analysis:** The analysis showing that UNREAL is significantly more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C is a major practical contribution. This makes the algorithm more reliable and easier to use, which is a highly valuable property.

### **4. Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   The name "Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning" is slightly ambiguous. While the auxiliary tasks do not use the extrinsic environment reward, they are still supervised by internally-generated pseudo-rewards within an RL framework. Perhaps a term like "Self-Supervised" would be more precise, but this is a minor terminological point.
*   The paper presents "feature control" as a promising but less-explored alternative to "pixel control." A more direct comparison or combination of the two in the main ablation study would be very insightful. Do they learn complementary features, or is one generally superior?
*   The weightings (λ) for the auxiliary losses are critical hyperparameters. While the paper explores robustness to learning rate and entropy, a brief sensitivity analysis or discussion on how these λ values were set (beyond being sampled for the hyperparameter search) would strengthen the paper's practical guidance.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  The replay buffer size is set to 2k transitions, which seems relatively small for off-policy learning. What was the rationale for this choice? Did you experiment with larger buffers, and if so, how did it affect performance and computational overhead?
2.  For the reward prediction task, you employ a simple rebalancing strategy to ensure a 50/50 split of rewarding and non-rewarding samples. How does this compare to using a more standard prioritized experience replay mechanism (e.g., based on TD-error) for this specific auxiliary task?
3.  The pixel control task discretizes the screen into a 20x20 grid. How sensitive is the agent's performance to this grid resolution? Is there a trade-off between the granularity of control learned and the computational complexity of the additional network heads?
