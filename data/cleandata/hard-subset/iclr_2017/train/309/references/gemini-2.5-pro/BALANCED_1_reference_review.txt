### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel agent architecture aimed at significantly improving the data efficiency, robustness, and overall performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong on-policy baseline agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned in parallel and off-policy. These tasks are designed to facilitate the development of a richer and more effective state representation.

The authors propose three main auxiliary components:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards. Two such tasks are explored: "pixel control," where the agent learns to maximize changes in pixel intensity across a grid of the visual input, and "feature control," where it learns to maximize the activations of its own hidden units. These tasks encourage the agent to learn about controllable aspects of its environment.
2.  **Reward Prediction:** To combat reward sparsity, the agent is trained on a supervised task to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a sequence of recent observations. This task is trained using a skewed experience replay mechanism that oversamples rewarding transitions, helping to focus the representation on reward-relevant features.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** The agent performs additional value function updates by replaying recent sequences of experience, which helps to propagate value information more rapidly through the improved representation.

The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation on two challenging domains: the standard 57-game Atari benchmark and a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results demonstrate that the UNREAL agent dramatically outperforms the A3C baseline and achieves new state-of-the-art performance on both benchmarks, showing remarkable improvements in learning speed (up to 10x faster) and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper presenting a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The decision to accept is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Significant Performance and Efficiency Gains:** The UNREAL agent demonstrates substantial and compelling improvements over a very strong baseline (A3C). The reported results—doubling the mean human-normalized score on the challenging Labyrinth environment and achieving a new state-of-the-art on Atari-57—are highly significant. The drastic improvement in data efficiency is a crucial step forward for making deep RL more practical.
2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The paper is supported by an exceptionally strong and well-executed set of experiments. The authors not only show superior performance but also conduct detailed ablation studies to demonstrate the value of each component. Furthermore, their analysis of robustness to hyperparameters provides convincing evidence for another key claim, and their comparison of pixel *control* to simpler alternatives like pixel prediction and reconstruction solidifies the novelty of their approach.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper excels in its clear motivation, novel architecture, and rigorous evaluation.

*   **Motivation and Novelty:** While the idea of using auxiliary tasks for representation learning is not entirely new, this paper presents a novel and highly effective synthesis of several ideas. The combination of an on-policy main policy (A3C) with off-policy learning of auxiliary tasks from a replay buffer is a key architectural insight. The specific auxiliary control tasks, especially pixel control implemented with a deconvolutional head, are creative and well-motivated. The paper does an excellent job of placing this work in the context of prior literature (e.g., Horde, UVFA, successor representations) while clearly distinguishing its own contributions.
*   **Technical Soundness:** Each component of the UNREAL agent is well-justified. Auxiliary control helps the agent build an understanding of environmental dynamics. Reward prediction, trained on a biased sample of experience, directly tackles the challenge of sparse rewards. Value replay allows the improved representation to be more efficiently leveraged for the main task. Combining these off-policy updates with the stable, on-policy A3C framework results in a coherent and powerful algorithm.
*   **Experimental Strength:** The experiments are exemplary. The introduction of the Labyrinth environment is a valuable contribution in its own right, offering a testbed with greater visual complexity and more realistic physics than many standard benchmarks. The ablation study (Figure 3) clearly isolates the positive impact of each auxiliary task. The comparison in Figure 5, which shows that learning to *control* pixel changes is more effective than merely predicting them or reconstructing the input, provides strong support for the central hypothesis of the paper. Finally, the analysis of hyperparameter robustness addresses a critical practical issue in deep RL and convincingly shows that UNREAL is not just higher-performing but also easier to use than its baseline.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions and do not detract from the paper's overall strength.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The UNREAL agent adds several loss components and network heads. While the gains in sample efficiency are clear, it would be helpful for the authors to briefly discuss the trade-off in terms of computational cost per training step compared to the vanilla A3C baseline.
*   **Analysis of Feature Control:** Feature control is presented as a promising idea and shows strong performance on the one level where it is tested (Figure 5). However, it is not included in the final UNREAL agent or the main ablation studies. A more thorough comparison between pixel control and feature control, or an analysis of their potential combination, would be a valuable addition. For instance, are certain network layers better to control than others?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The appendix mentions that the weight for the pixel control loss (λPC) was sampled from different ranges for Labyrinth and Atari. This suggests some domain dependency. Could the authors comment on the sensitivity to this hyperparameter and offer any intuition for why this tuning was necessary? This could inform future work on more adaptive or generalizable methods.
*   **Connection to Model-Based RL:** The pixel control objective, where an agent learns to effect changes in its environment, is conceptually related to learning an implicit model of environment dynamics. The related work section touches on model-based RL, but a deeper discussion of this connection could enrich the paper. For example, how does learning to control observations compare to explicitly predicting future frames as a representation learning objective?

### 5. Questions for Authors

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to solidify my understanding:

1.  The reward prediction task uses a rebalanced replay buffer to ensure P(r≠0) = 0.5. How sensitive is the agent's performance to this specific ratio? Did you experiment with other balancing schemes?
2.  For pixel control, the reward is the L1 distance between frames in a cell. Did you consider alternative, perhaps more perceptual, distance metrics (e.g., based on SSIM or a pre-trained VGG network)?
3.  The deconvolutional architecture for the pixel control Q-values is an elegant solution. Did you experiment with a simpler baseline, such as having a separate, non-spatial MLP head for each grid cell, to quantify the benefits of exploiting this spatial structure?
4.  The value function replay is described as resampling from the "behaviour policy distribution." Could you clarify if this is effectively on-policy replay (i.e., re-learning from recent on-policy trajectories) or if an off-policy correction like importance sampling is used? The current description is slightly ambiguous.
