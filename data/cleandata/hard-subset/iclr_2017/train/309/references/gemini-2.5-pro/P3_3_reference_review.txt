### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a standard on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained off-policy using experience replayed from a local buffer. These tasks are designed to promote the learning of a rich and general-purpose representation in the shared network layers.

The proposed auxiliary tasks are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions (Q-learning) for maximizing pseudo-rewards. Two specific tasks are explored: *pixel control*, where the goal is to maximize changes in pixel intensity within different regions of the input image, and *feature control*, where the goal is to maximize the activation of hidden units in the network.
2.  **Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate reward from a short sequence of past observations. To handle sparse rewards, training samples are drawn from a replay buffer in a skewed manner to over-represent rewarding transitions.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** In addition to the on-policy value function updates in A3C, the agent performs extra value function regression on sequences sampled from the replay buffer.

The authors evaluate UNREAL extensively on the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and on 57 Atari games. The results demonstrate substantial improvements over a strong A3C baseline, achieving state-of-the-art performance on both benchmarks. Notably, the paper claims a 10x median speedup in learning on Labyrinth and shows significantly improved robustness to hyperparameter choices.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The core ideas are well-motivated, the proposed architecture is technically sound, and the empirical results are both extensive and highly compelling.

1.  **Novel and Effective Architecture:** The paper's primary strength lies in its elegant integration of on-policy learning for the main task with off-policy learning for auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach effectively leverages the stability and strong performance of A3C for policy learning, while exploiting the data efficiency of experience replay for representation learning. This is a powerful and generalizable architectural pattern.

2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Evaluation:** The experimental validation is exemplary. The introduction and evaluation on the visually rich Labyrinth environment is a valuable contribution in itself, pushing research beyond the now-standard Atari benchmark. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the individual and combined benefits of the auxiliary tasks. Crucially, the analysis of hyperparameter robustness (Figure 3) provides strong evidence that the improvements are not due to fine-tuning, but are a fundamental property of the method.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The motivation for using auxiliary tasks to accelerate representation learning, especially in sparse reward settings, is well-established. This paper builds on this foundation by proposing a particularly effective set of tasks and a novel training scheme.

The distinction made in the related work section is important: unlike prior work on options or pseudo-rewards, the auxiliary control policies are used *solely* for representation learning and are not executed. This simplifies the framework and focuses its benefits entirely on shaping the shared features for the main A3C agent. The comparison against simpler auxiliary objectives like input reconstruction or change prediction (Figure 5) provides strong evidence for the superiority of learning to *control* environmental features rather than just passively predicting them.

The results are striking. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves a mean human-normalized score of 87% compared to A3C's 54%, a massive improvement. The claimed 10x speedup to reach A3C's peak performance is a testament to the agent's data efficiency. The Atari results are similarly impressive, achieving a mean score of 880% over A3C, establishing a new state of the art at the time of publication. The robustness analysis, showing a much tighter performance distribution across hyperparameter settings, is a critical result for the practical application of deep RL.

The paper is well-written and clear. The architecture is described in sufficient detail, and the appendix provides necessary implementation specifics. The combination of control-based and prediction-based auxiliary tasks appears to provide complementary benefits, enriching the learned representation from different perspectives.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. The UNREAL agent trains four separate heads (policy/value, pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) in addition to managing a replay buffer. A brief analysis of the wall-clock time or computational overhead per training step compared to the A3C baseline would be a valuable addition for practitioners seeking to implement this method.
*   **Clarification on Feature Control:** The results for "feature control" are presented as preliminary and are only shown for one level in an ablation study (Figure 5). Its contribution relative to pixel control is substantial but slightly lower. It would be useful to clarify whether feature control was included in the main UNREAL agent tested across all 57 Atari games and 13 Labyrinth levels, or if the main agent only used pixel control. The text in Section 3.4 and the loss function in Equation 2 seem to imply a general control loss `LPC`, but the experiments largely focus on pixel control.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (λPC):** The appendix notes that the hyperparameter `λPC` (weighting for pixel control loss) was sampled from different ranges for Labyrinth and Atari. This suggests some sensitivity to the environment's visual characteristics (e.g., magnitude of pixel changes). A brief discussion in the main text could provide valuable intuition on how to set this hyperparameter for new environments.

### **Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you elaborate on the computational overhead of UNREAL? What is the approximate increase in training time per environment step compared to the A3C baseline, given the additional network heads and replay buffer operations?
2.  In the main experiments on Labyrinth and Atari (Figure 3), did the UNREAL agent's auxiliary control component consist of pixel control only, or a combination of pixel control and feature control?
3.  The reward prediction task is trained on a rebalanced dataset where P(r≠0) = 0.5. Did you experiment with other rebalancing ratios? How sensitive is the performance of the reward prediction task, and its downstream effect on the main policy, to this specific choice?
