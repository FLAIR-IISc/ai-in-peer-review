### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent designed to significantly improve data efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter robustness. The core idea is to augment a strong asynchronous advantage actor-critic (A3C) agent with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned concurrently from the same stream of experience. These tasks are designed to promote the learning of a richer, more effective state representation.

The key contributions are threefold:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks**: The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing two types of pseudo-rewards: changes in pixel intensity across different regions of the input image (Pixel Control) and activations of hidden units in its own network (Feature Control). This encourages the agent to learn about the environment's dynamics and which aspects of the world it can influence.
2.  **Reward Prediction**: To combat the challenge of sparse rewards, the agent is trained on an auxiliary supervised task of predicting the immediate future reward from a short sequence of past observations. This task is trained using a separate replay buffer where rewarding transitions are oversampled, forcing the network to learn features predictive of rewards without biasing the main policy.
3.  **Value Function Replay**: To improve data efficiency, the agent replays recent sequences of experience to perform additional updates to the value function of the main A3C agent.

The UNREAL architecture elegantly combines on-policy learning for the main task with off-policy learning for the auxiliary tasks, leveraging experience replay for the latter. The authors provide an extensive empirical evaluation on the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment and on 57 Atari 2600 games. The results demonstrate that UNREAL achieves state-of-the-art performance, substantially outperforming A3C with a reported 10x speedup in learning and doubling the mean human-normalized score in Labyrinth.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Performance and Efficiency Gains:** The paper presents a novel and well-motivated architecture that achieves substantial improvements in both final performance and data efficiency over a very strong baseline (A3C). The results are not incremental; achieving a 10x speedup and state-of-the-art scores on both a complex 3D environment and the standard Atari benchmark is a major contribution.

2.  **Thorough and Rigorous Empirical Evaluation:** The strength of the paper lies in its exceptionally rigorous experimental validation. The authors include comprehensive ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task. Furthermore, the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is exemplary and addresses a critical but often overlooked aspect of deep RL research. The comparison of pixel control against alternative unsupervised objectives like reconstruction provides strong support for the specific design choices.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling:

*   **Novelty and Insight:** The hybrid architecture combining on-policy A3C for the main policy with off-policy Q-learning and supervised learning for auxiliary tasks is an insightful way to integrate the benefits of different learning paradigms. It allows for stable policy updates while using data-efficient off-policy methods to rapidly shape the representation. The idea of using control as an unsupervised objective, rather than just prediction or reconstruction, is a key insight that is well-supported by the experiments.

*   **Strength of Results:** The empirical results are impressive. In the challenging Labyrinth environment, UNREAL not only learns much faster but also reaches a final performance level (87% mean human-normalized score) that is significantly higher than the A3C baseline (54%). The results on Atari (880% mean) also demonstrate the generality of the approach and establish a new state-of-the-art.

*   **Methodological Rigor:** The experimental design is a model for the field. The ablation studies (Figure 3, top left) are crucial for understanding the synergy between the different components. The specific experiment comparing pixel control to pixel change prediction and pixel reconstruction (Figure 5) is particularly valuable, as it provides clear evidence for why learning to *control* features is more effective than simply predicting or reconstructing them. The hyperparameter robustness analysis (Figure 3, right column) is a significant strength that increases confidence in the method's practical applicability.

*   **Clarity and Reproducibility:** The paper is well-written and easy to follow. The methods are explained clearly, and the architecture is well-illustrated. The appendix provides sufficient implementation details to facilitate reproducibility.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive suggestions for strengthening the paper and are not critical for my decision to accept.

*   **Connection to Skill Discovery:** The paper briefly mentions that the auxiliary policies are not used directly, but the concept of "pixel control" feels closely related to unsupervised skill discovery or learning options. It would be beneficial to expand on this connection in the discussion. A short paragraph speculating on how these learned control policies could be leveraged for exploration or hierarchical planning in future work would add depth.

*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity of λPC:** The appendix notes that the sampling range for the pixel control loss weight (λPC) was different for Labyrinth and Atari. This suggests the raw pixel change pseudo-reward may not be consistently scaled across different environments. Have you considered normalizing this pseudo-reward (e.g., by running statistics of pixel changes) to make λPC more transferable and less sensitive?

*   **Feature Control Evaluation:** The feature control experiments are presented as "preliminary" and only tested in isolation on a few levels. While the results are promising, it would be interesting to see how a full UNREAL agent with feature control (instead of or in addition to pixel control) performs. This would provide a more complete picture of its utility.

*   **Architectural Justification:** The use of a deconvolutional network for the pixel control Q-function is an elegant choice. A brief sentence in the main text justifying this choice (e.g., "to efficiently exploit the spatial structure of the control task and share parameters across grid cells") would strengthen the architectural description.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate it if you could clarify the following points in your author response:

1.  **Off-Policy Corrections:** The auxiliary control tasks are trained with off-policy Q-learning on experience generated by the main A3C policy. Could you clarify if any off-policy correction methods (e.g., importance sampling, Retrace) were used or considered? Given that the behavior policy is non-stationary, were any instabilities observed from using vanilla Q-learning updates?

2.  **Reward Prediction Skewing:** In the reward prediction task, you use a 50/50 split between zero and non-zero rewards. How sensitive is the performance to this ratio? Did you experiment with other skewing strategies, and what was the impact?

3.  **Choice of Layer for Feature Control:** For the feature control task, you chose to control activations in the second convolutional layer. What was the rationale behind this specific choice? Have you experimented with controlling activations at different depths (e.g., earlier layers for low-level features, or the LSTM output for more abstract features), and how did this affect learning?
