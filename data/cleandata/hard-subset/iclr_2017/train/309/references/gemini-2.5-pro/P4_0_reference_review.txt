### 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper proposes a novel deep reinforcement learning agent, named UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), designed to improve learning speed, final performance, and robustness. The core idea is to augment a strong baseline agent (A3C) with a suite of auxiliary tasks that promote the development of a richer and more effective feature representation. The contributions are threefold:

1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing internally-generated pseudo-rewards. Two specific tasks are proposed: "pixel control," where the agent learns to maximize pixel changes in different regions of the input image, and "feature control," where it learns to maximize the activations of its own hidden units. These tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction Task:** To combat reward sparsity, the agent is trained to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of recent observations. This task is trained using a separate replay buffer where rewarding events are oversampled, allowing the agent's feature extractors to learn about reward-predictive states more efficiently without biasing the main policy.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** In addition to the standard on-policy A3C updates, experience replay is used to perform additional off-policy updates to the value function, allowing it to benefit more rapidly from the improved representations shaped by the auxiliary tasks.

The authors demonstrate the efficacy of this combined approach through extensive experiments on a visually complex 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth and on the 57-game Atari benchmark. The UNREAL agent is shown to significantly outperform the A3C baseline, achieving a 10x median speedup on Labyrinth and setting a new state-of-the-art on Atari.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Empirical Improvement:** The paper presents exceptionally strong and well-supported empirical results on multiple challenging domains. The UNREAL agent not only achieves state-of-the-art performance but also demonstrates dramatic improvements in data efficiency and robustness to hyperparameters, addressing key weaknesses of contemporary deep RL algorithms.
2.  **Novel and Sound Combination of Methods:** The paper introduces a novel and coherent framework for integrating auxiliary tasks into an actor-critic agent. The specific choices of tasks (pixel control, reward prediction) and the way they are implemented (e.g., off-policy Q-learning for control, rebalanced replay for prediction) are well-motivated and technically sound. The ablation studies convincingly show that each component contributes to the overall performance gains.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's claims are rigorously supported by its experimental methodology and results.

First, the technical approach is sound. It builds upon A3C, a state-of-the-art baseline, and intelligently combines on-policy learning for the main objective with off-policy learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks. This separation allows each component to be trained in the most data-efficient manner. For example, using off-policy Q-learning for the pixel control tasks allows the agent to learn about environment dynamics from a single stream of experience, while the rebalanced replay for reward prediction effectively addresses the common problem of sparse rewards. The architectural choices, such as using a deconvolutional head for the spatially-structured pixel control task, are sensible and well-justified.

Second, the empirical evaluation is thorough and compelling. The choice of Labyrinth as a primary testbed is excellent, as its rich visuals and complex navigation and interaction tasks showcase the benefits of improved representation learning. The reported results—doubling the mean human-normalized score from 54% to 87% and achieving a 10x speedup—are highly significant. The ablation studies presented in Figure 3 clearly isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task, demonstrating their additive benefits. Furthermore, the analysis of hyperparameter robustness (Figure 3, Top Right) is a valuable contribution, showing that the UNREAL agent is not only better but also easier to train in practice.

The inclusion of results on the 57 Atari games confirms that the benefits are not specific to the Labyrinth environment. Achieving a new state-of-the-art on this widely-used benchmark (880% mean, 250% median human-normalized score) solidifies the paper's claims of general applicability. The comparison in Section 4.1.1 against alternative unsupervised objectives, such as input reconstruction and pixel change prediction, provides strong evidence for the specific choice of an auxiliary *control* objective.

### 4. Additional Feedback and Questions for Authors

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision.

**Feedback:**
*   The term "Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning" for the subsection in 4.1.1 could be slightly misleading. While the auxiliary tasks do not require external labels, they are formulated as standard RL problems with internally defined reward functions. "Self-Supervised Control" or "Comparison of Auxiliary Objectives" might be a more precise heading.
*   The paper introduces "feature control" and provides a promising preliminary result (Figure 5), but this task is not included in the final UNREAL agent tested on Atari or in the main Labyrinth results. Clarifying why it was not fully integrated, and whether it could offer further benefits in combination with the other tasks, would be interesting.
*   A brief discussion on the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks would be valuable. While data efficiency is drastically improved, understanding the trade-off in terms of wall-clock training time would provide a more complete picture.

**Questions for Authors:**
1.  The comparison between pixel *control* and pixel change *prediction* (Figure 5) is very insightful. Could you provide some qualitative intuition as to why learning to control these changes is so much more effective for representation learning than simply predicting them? Does the agent learn qualitatively different behaviors (e.g., actively seeking out dynamic stimuli) under the control objective?
2.  The loss function in Equation 2 includes the A3C loss (which contains a value loss component) and a separate value replay loss `L_VR`. Section 3.4 states that the main A3C loss is minimized on-policy while the additional `L_VR` is optimized from replayed data. Could you please confirm if the value head is updated from two separate sources (on-policy rollouts and off-policy replay samples) within a single optimization step?
3.  The reward prediction task uses a feedforward network over a small stack of three frames, while the main policy/value network uses an LSTM over the full history. What was the reasoning behind this architectural separation? Was a version where reward prediction was also conditioned on the LSTM's hidden state considered?
