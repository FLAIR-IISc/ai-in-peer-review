### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a method for improving the performance, data efficiency, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a strong asynchronous actor-critic baseline (A3C) with several auxiliary tasks that are trained concurrently from the same stream of experience. These tasks are designed to facilitate better representation learning. The proposed auxiliary tasks include: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pixel changes ("pixel control") or hidden unit activations ("feature control"); 2) a reward prediction task, where a feedforward network predicts the immediate future reward from a sequence of frames, trained on a reward-balanced dataset sampled from a replay buffer; and 3) value function replay, which performs additional value function updates on re-sampled data. The authors demonstrate that this combined agent achieves state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games and drastically outperforms the A3C baseline on a new, visually complex 3D environment called Labyrinth.

### Decision: Reject

While the empirical results presented in this paper are impressive, I am recommending rejection at this time. My primary concerns are twofold. First, the proposed method combines a multitude of existing techniques (A3C, experience replay, auxiliary tasks, Q-learning) into a highly complex system, but the paper lacks a clear, principled justification for this specific combination over other possibilities. It reads more as a successful but intricate engineering effort than a fundamental contribution to reinforcement learning methodology. Second, the strength of the empirical claims is weakened by the comparison to a baseline (A3C) that lacks key components, such as experience replay, which are known to significantly improve data efficiency and are integral to the proposed UNREAL agent.

### Supporting Arguments

1.  **Lack of Methodological Principle:** The UNREAL agent is a complex amalgamation of on-policy policy-gradient learning (A3C) and off-policy value-based learning (Q-learning for auxiliary tasks) using experience replay. While the ablation study demonstrates that each component contributes to the final performance, the paper does not provide a strong argument for why this particular set of auxiliary tasks (pixel control, reward prediction) is the right one. The tasks themselves are not entirely novel concepts; self-supervised objectives are a common tool for representation learning. The contribution here lies in their specific application and combination, yet the justification feels post-hoc and driven by empirical success rather than a priori theoretical or conceptual grounding. For instance, why is learning to *control* pixel changes superior to learning a forward dynamics model that *predicts* them? The paper provides a single empirical comparison but lacks a deeper analysis, leaving the reader to wonder if other, perhaps more established, self-supervised methods would perform similarly.

2.  **Insufficiently Strong Baselines:** The primary baseline for comparison is the standard A3C algorithm. A core feature of UNREAL is the use of an experience replay buffer to train the auxiliary tasks and perform additional value function updates. The original A3C method was designed specifically to avoid experience replay by using asynchronous actors. By re-introducing a replay buffer, UNREAL gains the well-known benefits of data reuse and decorrelation. A significant portion of the reported 10x speedup could be attributed to this component alone. To properly isolate the contribution of the novel auxiliary tasks, a much stronger baseline would be an A3C agent that is also augmented with experience replay for its value updates (akin to methods like ACER). Without this crucial comparison, it is difficult to ascertain how much of the performance gain comes from the proposed auxiliary objectives versus the simple, well-established technique of experience replay.

3.  **Novelty of the Evaluation Domain:** A large part of the paper's results are showcased on the Labyrinth environment, which is introduced in this work. While the environment appears challenging and visually rich, its novelty makes it difficult to contextualize the magnitude of the reported improvements. Results on a new, internal benchmark, however impressive, are less convincing than similar results on established, widely-understood benchmarks where the performance of prior methods is well-documented. While Atari results are also presented, the focus and most dramatic gains are on Labyrinth.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

*   The term "Unsupervised" in the agent's name feels like a misnomer. The auxiliary tasks are self-supervised, relying on labels (pixel changes, rewards) generated directly from the agent's experience stream. Using more precise terminology would be appropriate.
*   The complexity of the final UNREAL agent is substantial, involving multiple network heads, separate loss functions with weighting hyperparameters, and a mix of on-policy and off-policy updates. A discussion of the computational overhead and implementation complexity compared to the baseline would be a valuable addition.
*   The section on "feature control" is presented as preliminary and only evaluated on a single level. This component feels underdeveloped compared to the others and detracts from the paper's focus. It might be better to either flesh it out with a more complete evaluation or remove it in favor of a more streamlined presentation.

### Questions for the Authors

1.  To better isolate the benefits of the proposed auxiliary tasks from those of experience replay, could you provide results for a baseline that incorporates experience replay for value function updates but does not include the pixel control or reward prediction tasks?
2.  Could you elaborate on the selection process for your auxiliary tasks? Have you experimented with other well-known self-supervised objectives, such as learning an inverse dynamics model or using contrastive learning, and how do they compare?
3.  Regarding the Labyrinth environment, can you provide any performance metrics for standard baseline algorithms on more established 3D platforms like VizDoom to help calibrate the difficulty of your new environment and the significance of your agent's performance?
