### Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent designed to improve learning speed, final performance, and robustness. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained off-policy using a shared replay buffer. These auxiliary tasks are designed to shape the agent's representation, forcing it to learn more general and useful features about the environment's dynamics and reward structure.

The proposed auxiliary tasks are of two main types:
1.  **Auxiliary Control:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards. Two specific instances are proposed: "pixel control," where the agent learns to maximize pixel changes in different regions of the input, and "feature control," where it learns to maximize the activations of its own hidden units. These tasks encourage the agent to learn a model of controllable aspects of its environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent learns to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of recent observations. This task is trained on samples from the replay buffer with a skewed sampling strategy that over-represents rewarding transitions, helping the agent learn reward-predictive features even when rewards are sparse.

The authors evaluate the UNREAL agent extensively on two challenging domains: the 57-game Atari suite and Labyrinth, a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment. The results demonstrate that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline and achieves state-of-the-art performance, showing dramatic improvements in data efficiency (up to a 10x speedup), final scores, and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a strong paper with significant contributions to the field of deep reinforcement learning. My decision is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper presents an elegant and effective architecture that intelligently combines on-policy learning for the main task with off-policy learning for auxiliary tasks. The specific choice of auxiliary tasks, particularly using "control" as a mechanism for representation learning, is a compelling and novel idea that is well-differentiated from prior work on options or intrinsic motivation. The motivation for each component is clear and directly addresses known challenges in RL, such as sparse rewards and sample inefficiency.
2.  **Comprehensive and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The experimental results are outstanding. The authors not only demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on the standard Atari benchmark but also introduce a new, challenging 3D environment (Labyrinth) where UNREAL achieves superhuman performance. The claims of drastically improved data efficiency and robustness are thoroughly supported by extensive experiments, including detailed ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component and insightful comparisons to alternative auxiliary tasks like input reconstruction.

### Supporting Arguments

The strengths of this paper are numerous. The central idea of using auxiliary tasks that share a representation with the main policy is powerful. By training these tasks off-policy from a replay buffer, the agent can learn about the environment's general dynamics far more efficiently than from on-policy data alone.

The design of the auxiliary tasks is particularly clever.
*   **Pixel Control:** This task forces the agent to learn an intuitive model of physics and cause-and-effect (e.g., "what action causes this part of the screen to change?"). The use of a deconvolutional network to produce spatially-aware Q-values for different grid cells is a well-suited architectural choice. The ablation study showing that *controlling* pixel changes is superior to merely *predicting* them or *reconstructing* the input provides strong evidence for the efficacy of this control-based approach to representation learning.
*   **Reward Prediction:** The problem of sparse rewards is a major hurdle in RL. The reward prediction task, combined with skewed replay, is a simple yet highly effective solution that focuses the representation on identifying reward-relevant states without biasing the agent's main value function or policy.

The empirical validation is exemplary. The reported 10x speedup in reaching A3C's peak performance on Labyrinth is a striking result with significant practical implications. Furthermore, the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is a welcome and important contribution, as it addresses a key challenge in applying deep RL methods in practice. The plots showing performance across a wide range of learning rates and entropy costs clearly demonstrate that UNREAL is not just a high-performing agent but also a more reliable one.

### Additional Feedback for Improvement

The paper is excellent, but the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Computational Cost:** The UNREAL architecture adds several network heads and loss computations to the A3C baseline. While the improvement in *data* efficiency is massive, a discussion of the trade-offs in terms of computational overhead (e.g., wall-clock time per training step, GPU memory) would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practicality.
*   **Feature Control:** The feature control task is a fascinating idea, essentially encouraging the agent to learn to control its own internal representations. However, it is only presented as a "preliminary" result on a single level. Expanding on this—or at least discussing the challenges or reasons why it was not evaluated more broadly—would be very valuable. Is it as general or as stable as pixel control?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (λPC):** The paper notes that the weight for the pixel control loss, λPC, was sampled from different ranges for Atari and Labyrinth. This suggests that this hyperparameter may need to be tuned for new domains. Some intuition on why this is the case (perhaps related to the general magnitude of pixel changes in different environments) and whether any normalization scheme could alleviate this would be helpful for future practitioners.

### Questions for Authors

1.  Could you comment on the computational overhead (e.g., in terms of wall-clock training time or FLOPs per update) of the full UNREAL agent compared to the A3C baseline?
2.  The "feature control" auxiliary task yielded a substantial improvement in the single experiment shown. Could you elaborate on why this promising approach was not evaluated more extensively across the other Labyrinth levels or Atari games? Did you encounter any stability or performance issues with it?
3.  Regarding the pixel control weight λPC, what is your intuition for why different ranges were required for Labyrinth and Atari? Does this dependency on the visual statistics of the environment suggest a need for an adaptive weighting scheme?
