### **1. Summary**
This paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, an architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a state-of-the-art on-policy agent, A3C, with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned in parallel to shape the agent's internal representation. These tasks are trained off-policy from a replay buffer, allowing for efficient use of past experience.

The paper introduces three main auxiliary tasks:
1.  **Auxiliary Control:** The agent learns policies to maximize pseudo-rewards, such as causing maximal change in pixel intensity in different regions of the input (pixel control) or maximally activating hidden units in its own network (feature control). These tasks are learned via off-policy Q-learning.
2.  **Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate reward from a short sequence of recent observations. To address reward sparsity, training samples are drawn from a replay buffer in a skewed manner to over-represent rewarding events.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** The agent performs additional value function updates by replaying sequences from the buffer, similar to experience replay in DQN, to further accelerate value function learning.

The UNREAL agent combines the on-policy A3C algorithm for the main task with these off-policy auxiliary tasks, which share the initial convolutional layers. The authors claim this hybrid approach leads to substantially faster learning and higher final performance. They provide empirical validation on the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and the standard Atari-57 benchmark suite.

### **2. Decision: Accept**
The decision is to **Accept** this paper. The key reasons for this decision are:
1.  **Significant Empirical Contribution:** The paper presents substantial and state-of-the-art performance improvements over a very strong baseline (A3C) on two demanding and distinct benchmark domains. The reported gains in data efficiency (a claimed 10x speedup) and final performance are highly compelling.
2.  **Methodological Rigor:** The claims are well-supported by a rigorous and thorough experimental evaluation. This includes comprehensive ablation studies that clearly demonstrate the cumulative benefit of the proposed auxiliary tasks, as well as a robust analysis of performance across a wide range of hyperparameters, which reinforces the claim of improved stability.

### **3. Supporting Arguments**
The paper is technically sound and makes a valuable contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning.

**Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture is a novel and well-motivated combination of existing concepts. Combining an on-policy policy gradient method (A3C) for the main objective with off-policy value-based methods for auxiliary tasks is a technically sound approach. It allows the main policy to benefit from the stability of on-policy updates while the representation learning benefits from the data efficiency of off-policy learning with experience replay. The specific auxiliary tasks are well-chosen: pixel control provides a dense, intrinsic motivation signal that encourages interaction with the environment, while reward prediction directly tackles the challenge of sparse extrinsic rewards by focusing the representation on reward-relevant state features.

**Novelty:** While the concepts of auxiliary tasks, intrinsic motivation, and experience replay are not new in isolation, their synthesis in the UNREAL agent is novel. Specifically, the combination of off-policy Q-learning for *control-based* auxiliary tasks (pixel control) with on-policy A3C is a key contribution. The paper effectively positions this work relative to prior art, distinguishing its use of pseudo-rewards for representation learning from earlier work on options or Horde.

**Empirical Support:** The empirical evidence is the strongest aspect of the paper.
*   **Performance:** The results on both Labyrinth and Atari are state-of-the-art. Doubling the mean human-normalized performance on the visually complex Labyrinth environment is a significant achievement. The 880% mean score on Atari also represents a substantial advance.
*   **Ablation Studies:** Figure 3 clearly illustrates that adding each auxiliary task provides a consistent and cumulative performance boost. This provides strong evidence that the overall improvement is not due to a single component but to the combination of all proposed ideas.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** The hyperparameter robustness analysis (Figure 3, right column) is exemplary. By showing the performance distribution over many randomly sampled hyperparameter settings, the authors convincingly demonstrate that UNREAL is not only higher-performing but also easier to tune than the A3C baseline, which is a crucial practical consideration.
*   **Comparison of Auxiliary Tasks:** The experiment in Section 4.1.1, which compares pixel control against pixel prediction and reconstruction, provides valuable insight. It supports the hypothesis that learning to *control* aspects of the environment is a more powerful representation learning objective than passive prediction or reconstruction.

### **4. Additional Feedback**
The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not central to the decision.
*   The term "Unsupervised" in the UNREAL acronym could be slightly misleading. The auxiliary tasks are not unsupervised in the traditional sense; they are self-supervised, with pseudo-rewards providing the supervision. A more precise term might have been "self-supervised" or "intrinsic."
*   The computational cost of the auxiliary tasks is not discussed. While the improvement in sample complexity is clear, an analysis of the increase in wall-clock training time or computational overhead per environment step would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved.
*   Feature control is introduced as a promising auxiliary task and is compared favorably to pixel control in one experiment (Figure 5). However, it is not included in the final UNREAL agent evaluated on the main benchmarks. It would be beneficial to clarify why this was the case and whether it presents challenges (e.g., instability, higher computational cost) that prevented its inclusion.

### **5. Questions for the Authors**
To help clarify my understanding, I would appreciate answers to the following questions:
1.  Could you provide more intuition behind the choice of a deconvolutional architecture for the pixel control auxiliary heads? How does this architecture enforce spatial consistency in the Q-values, and how did it compare to simpler alternatives (e.g., a shared MLP followed by independent outputs for each grid cell)?
2.  The reward prediction task is trained on a skewed distribution from the replay buffer, while the value function replay is not. Was there a specific reason for not using prioritized or skewed replay for the value function updates as well?
3.  Regarding the comparison in Section 4.1.1: The paper hypothesizes that input reconstruction hurts performance by focusing on irrelevant details. Have you considered whether a different reconstruction architecture, for instance one with an information bottleneck, might mitigate this issue and yield better results?
4.  How sensitive is the UNREAL agent's performance to the loss weights (λVR, λPC, λRP)? The experiments show robustness to learning rate and entropy cost, but a brief analysis of the interplay between the different loss components would be very informative.
