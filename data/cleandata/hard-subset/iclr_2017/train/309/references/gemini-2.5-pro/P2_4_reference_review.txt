### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel agent architecture designed to improve the sample efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong, on-policy actor-critic agent (A3C) with a suite of auxiliary tasks that are trained in an off-policy manner from a replay buffer. These auxiliary tasks are intelligently designed to foster a more robust and general-purpose representation. Specifically, the authors propose:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy Q-values to maximize pseudo-rewards, such as causing maximal pixel changes in different regions of the input (pixel control) or maximally activating hidden units (feature control). These tasks encourage the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of past observations. To combat reward sparsity, the training data for this task is rebalanced to oversample rewarding events.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** Additional value function updates are performed on replayed data, accelerating the propagation of value information through the learned representation.

The paper provides extensive empirical evidence on two challenging domains: a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth, and the standard 57-game Atari benchmark. The results demonstrate that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline, achieving a 10x speedup in learning, doubling the human-normalized score on Labyrinth, and setting a new state-of-the-art on Atari.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept.

This is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. The two primary reasons for my decision are:

1.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The UNREAL agent presents a principled and elegant method for combining the stability of on-policy policy-gradient methods (A3C) for the main task with the data efficiency of off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer for auxiliary representation learning. The choice of auxiliary tasks is well-justified, addressing distinct challenges like learning environment dynamics (control tasks) and focusing on sparse reward signals (reward prediction).

2.  **Exceptionally Strong and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The paper's claims are supported by comprehensive experiments that are a model of scientific rigor in RL research. The authors not only show massive performance gains over a state-of-the-art baseline on two diverse and challenging domains but also include crucial ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each component and a thorough analysis of hyperparameter robustness, a critical but often overlooked aspect of RL.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The core idea of using auxiliary tasks for representation learning is not new, but this paper's execution is excellent. The architectural decision to decouple the main on-policy learner from the off-policy auxiliary learners is a key insight. This allows the representation to benefit from the high data efficiency of experience replay without introducing the instability that off-policy updates can cause in actor-critic methods.

The auxiliary tasks themselves are well-conceived. **Pixel control** is an intuitive, self-supervised objective that encourages the agent to understand cause and effect within its environment ("what can I control?"). Using a deconvolutional network to produce a spatial map of Q-values for these tasks is a clever architectural choice that leverages the spatial nature of the problem. The **reward prediction** task directly tackles the problem of credit assignment with sparse rewards by forcing the network to develop features sensitive to reward-predictive states. The use of a skewed replay buffer for this task is a simple yet highly effective way to amplify the rare reward signal.

The experimental results are compelling. A 10x speedup to reach the A3C baseline's final performance is a dramatic improvement in data efficiency. Achieving 87% mean human-normalized performance on the visually complex Labyrinth environment and 880% mean on Atari demonstrates a substantial leap in capability. The ablation studies in Figure 3 clearly show that each component contributes positively to the final performance. Furthermore, the hyperparameter robustness analysis (Figure 3, right column) is particularly convincing. Showing that UNREAL not only achieves a higher peak performance but also maintains a high level of performance across a wider range of hyperparameters is a result of significant practical importance, suggesting the method is more reliable and easier to deploy than its predecessors.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper convincingly demonstrates improvements in data efficiency (number of environment steps), but it does not discuss the increase in computational cost (wall-clock time, GPU/CPU usage) from the additional network heads and off-policy updates. A brief comment on the computational trade-offs would provide a more complete picture of the method's practicality.
*   **Justification of Design Choices:** Some design choices, while effective, seem somewhat arbitrary. For example, the 20x20 grid for pixel control or the sequence length of three for reward prediction. A short discussion on the sensitivity to these parameters, or the intuition behind their selection, would be beneficial.
*   **Weighting of Auxiliary Losses:** The paper notes that the weight for the pixel control loss (λPC) was sampled from different ranges for Labyrinth and Atari. This suggests some domain-specific tuning is required. It would be helpful if the authors could provide some intuition as to why this was necessary (e.g., differences in pixel intensity dynamics between the domains) and whether they have any general heuristics for setting this important hyperparameter.

### **Questions for Authors**

1.  Could you quantify the computational overhead of UNREAL compared to the A3C baseline? For instance, what is the approximate increase in training time per million environment steps?
2.  How sensitive is the performance of the pixel control task to the granularity of the control grid? Have you experimented with coarser or finer grids (e.g., 10x10 or 40x40), and how did it affect learning?
3.  Regarding the different ranges for the λPC hyperparameter between Labyrinth and Atari, is this due to fundamental differences in the environments' visual statistics? Does this imply that this hyperparameter may need careful tuning when applying UNREAL to new domains?
