### Review of "Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning"

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, an architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a standard A3C agent with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned jointly with the main policy. These auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy using experience replayed from a buffer, allowing for more efficient use of data. The proposed tasks include: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns policies to maximize pixel changes (pixel control) or hidden unit activations (feature control), and 2) auxiliary reward tasks, where the agent predicts the immediate next reward from a sequence of observations, with samples biased towards rewarding events. The agent is evaluated on 57 Atari games and a visually complex 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth, demonstrating significant improvements over the A3C baseline and achieving state-of-the-art results.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The proposed UNREAL agent is a highly complex system that combines numerous existing techniques (A3C, experience replay, Q-learning for auxiliary tasks) without a clear, principled motivation for their specific integration. The work appears to be more a feat of successful system engineering than a fundamental contribution to reinforcement learning methodology.
2.  The primary auxiliary task, pixel control, while empirically effective, is heuristically motivated and its general applicability is not convincingly demonstrated. The strong results on the new, proprietary Labyrinth environment, which features dynamic textures, may not generalize to environments with different visual characteristics.

**3. Supporting Arguments**

The paper presents impressive empirical results, but the underlying methodology lacks the rigor and conceptual novelty expected for a publication at ICLR.

First, the UNREAL agent is an amalgamation of several components: an on-policy A3C loss, off-policy Q-learning for pixel control, a supervised reward prediction loss, and an off-policy value function regression loss. The final objective is a weighted sum of these four disparate losses. While the ablation study shows that each component contributes positively, the paper provides little insight into *why* this specific combination is the right one, nor does it offer a theoretical framework to justify the mixing of on-policy and off-policy updates in this manner. The agent's design feels ad-hoc, raising concerns that its success may be contingent on careful tuning and the specific nature of the chosen environments. The contribution seems to be "these things work well together" rather than a novel, generalizable learning principle.

Second, the design of the auxiliary tasks is based on intuition rather than a solid theoretical foundation. The pixel control task, for example, is motivated by the heuristic that "changes in the perceptual stream often correspond to important events." While plausible, this assumption is not universally true and could be counterproductive in environments with significant irrelevant dynamic elements. The evaluation relies heavily on the Labyrinth environment, which the paper notes has "dynamic (animated)" textures. It is plausible that the pixel control task is particularly well-suited to this specific challenge, and its impressive performance may not carry over to a broader class of problems. The comparison against pixel reconstruction and prediction is a good first step, but it doesn't fully address the question of whether pixel control is a generally robust auxiliary objective. A stronger case would require either a more formal justification for the objective or evaluation across a more diverse set of environments with varying visual properties.

Finally, while the performance gains are substantial, the complexity of the resulting agent is a significant drawback. The introduction of multiple auxiliary heads, a replay buffer, and several new hyperparameters (λVR, λPC, λRP) moves away from the relative simplicity and elegance of algorithms like A3C. Without a clear understanding of the principles governing the interaction of these components, it is difficult to see how the UNREAL architecture can be reliably adapted to new problems. The work feels more like a specific, highly-engineered solution than a general advance in reinforcement learning.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   **Ablation Study:** A more detailed ablation study would strengthen the paper. For instance, what is the performance of A3C + Experience Replay (for value updates) alone? This would help disentangle the benefits of off-policy value learning from the novel auxiliary tasks. Similarly, combining just reward prediction with the base A3C agent would be an informative data point.
*   **Analysis of Interactions:** The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of the interplay between the on-policy A3C updates and the various off-policy updates. How does the off-policy learning on replayed data affect the on-policy policy gradient? Are there potential stability issues or competing objectives that arise from this hybrid approach?
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational overhead is missing. The auxiliary tasks add significant computation (e.g., deconvolutional networks for pixel control). What is the increase in wall-clock training time or computational cost per environment step compared to the A3C baseline? This is a crucial factor for assessing the claimed improvements in data efficiency.

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Could you provide further justification for the choice of Q-learning for the auxiliary control tasks? The goal is representation learning, not to execute these auxiliary policies. Would a simpler, direct policy gradient approach for maximizing the pseudo-rewards yield similar benefits for the representation with less complexity?
2.  The Labyrinth environment's dynamic textures seem like a potential confounder for the pixel control task. Have you investigated the agent's performance on a visually complex but largely static 3D environment? Is it possible that the pixel control task's main benefit in Labyrinth comes from learning to ignore the irrelevant background flicker?
3.  The paper claims a 10x speedup in terms of "number of steps." Does this refer to environment interactions? Given the added computational load of the auxiliary tasks and off-policy updates, how does this translate to wall-clock time?
4.  How sensitive is the agent's performance to the choice of the replay buffer size (2k observations seems quite small) and the frequency of auxiliary updates (every 20 steps)?
