### Summary

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent architecture designed to improve data efficiency, robustness, and overall performance. The core idea is to augment a primary on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks trained off-policy from a replay buffer. The paper makes several key contributions:
1.  It proposes a novel class of **auxiliary control tasks**, where the agent learns policies to actively maximize change in its sensory input (pixel control) or in its own internal network activations (feature control). This encourages the agent to learn a rich representation of environment dynamics and the consequences of its actions.
2.  It introduces an **auxiliary reward prediction** task, which uses a skewed experience replay mechanism to oversample rare rewarding events, helping the agent learn reward-relevant features even in sparse settings.
3.  It combines these auxiliary components with the A3C algorithm in a single, cohesive architecture that leverages the stability of on-policy learning for the main task and the data efficiency of off-policy learning for the auxiliary objectives.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of UNREAL through extensive experiments on a new, visually complex 3D environment called Labyrinth, as well as on the 57-game Atari benchmark. The results are exceptional, showing that UNREAL dramatically outperforms a strong A3C baseline, achieving a 10x learning speedup on Labyrinth and setting a new state of the art on Atari.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is an outstanding paper that I believe will have a significant impact on the field. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Exceptional Novelty and a Bold New Direction:** The paper's central contribution—the introduction of auxiliary *control* tasks—is a genuinely novel and powerful idea. It reframes the role of auxiliary tasks from passive prediction to active, goal-directed exploration of the agent's sensory and internal space. This is a significant conceptual leap that opens up a compelling new direction for representation learning in RL.
2.  **Impressive Empirical Results Validating the Core Idea:** The proposed UNREAL agent not only presents a fascinating new concept but also backs it up with state-of-the-art performance. The massive improvements in both learning speed and final performance on the challenging Labyrinth environment and the Atari suite provide unequivocal evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed methods.

### Supporting Arguments

This paper excels in its conceptual boldness and rigorous execution. The idea of learning policies for maximizing pixel changes or hidden unit activations is a creative and inspired way to force an agent to build a causal understanding of its environment. The ablation study in Figure 5, which shows that "control" is superior to simpler prediction or reconstruction objectives, provides strong support for this central claim. This is not merely an incremental improvement but a paradigm shift in how we can use self-supervised signals.

The architectural design of UNREAL, which elegantly combines on-policy A3C for the main policy with off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks, is a sophisticated and highly effective piece of engineering. It successfully marries the strengths of both approaches: the stability of on-policy updates for the sparse external reward signal, and the superior data efficiency of off-policy learning for the dense, internally generated auxiliary signals.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and convincing. The introduction of the Labyrinth environment is a welcome contribution in itself, providing a more challenging and visually rich domain than Atari. The fact that UNREAL achieves a nearly 10x speedup and doubles the final performance of A3C on this domain is a powerful testament to its capabilities. The new state-of-the-art results on Atari further cement the paper's claims. Furthermore, the analysis showing improved robustness to hyperparameters is a crucial, often overlooked, result that speaks to the practical utility of the proposed method.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

While I am very enthusiastic about this work, the following points could help further improve the paper and clarify some details for the reader. These are intended as constructive suggestions and do not detract from my strong recommendation to accept.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**
*   The concept of "feature control" is incredibly intriguing, as it suggests an agent can learn to introspectively manipulate its own representations. However, it is only presented as a preliminary result in one figure. Given its novelty and potential, I would encourage the authors to expand on this, perhaps by providing more analysis in the appendix. It feels like a powerful idea that is currently overshadowed by the more extensively evaluated pixel control.
*   The paper combines several components (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay). A deeper analysis of the interplay between these tasks would be fascinating. For instance, do pixel control and reward prediction learn complementary representations? An analysis of feature similarity (e.g., using CKA) between different parts of the network could provide valuable insights.
*   A brief discussion on the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks would be helpful for practitioners looking to implement this method.

**Questions for the Authors:**
1.  For the pixel control task, a deconvolutional network is used to generate the Q-values for the grid of cells. Could you provide more intuition for this architectural choice? How critical is it to exploit this spatial structure compared to using a simpler architecture, such as a separate MLP head for each cell's policy?
2.  In the feature control experiments, you chose to control the activations of the second hidden layer. What was the reasoning behind this specific choice? Did you experiment with controlling other layers (e.g., the LSTM state or the final layer before the policy head), and did you observe different learning dynamics?
3.  The skewed replay for reward prediction uses a 50/50 split between zero and non-zero rewards. How sensitive is the agent's performance to this ratio? Have you considered alternative rebalancing schemes, for example, based on reward magnitude?
