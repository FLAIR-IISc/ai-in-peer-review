### Summary

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture that significantly enhances the performance and data efficiency of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a base A3C agent with a suite of auxiliary tasks designed to accelerate the learning of a rich and robust state representation. These tasks are cleverly designed to be self-supervised and are trained in parallel with the main policy. The primary contributions are the introduction of two novel classes of auxiliary tasks: 1) Auxiliary control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions for pseudo-rewards corresponding to maximizing pixel changes (pixel control) or internal network feature activations (feature control). 2) An auxiliary reward prediction task, where the agent predicts the immediate next reward from a short sequence of observations, trained on a rebalanced experience replay buffer to better handle sparse rewards. The authors demonstrate that this combined approach leads to dramatic improvements over a strong A3C baseline, achieving state-of-the-art results on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and the 57-game Atari suite, with a reported 10x median speedup in learning.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

This is a clear accept. The paper presents a bold and highly effective new direction for representation learning in RL. My decision is based on two key factors:

1.  **Significant Conceptual Novelty:** The introduction of auxiliary *control* tasks is a powerful and genuinely new idea. Moving beyond passive prediction (e.g., image reconstruction, depth prediction) to learning policies that actively seek to control aspects of the perceptual stream or the agent's own internal state is a fundamental conceptual leap. This provides a much richer, more agent-centric learning signal that directly relates to understanding environmental dynamics and affordances.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims of the paper are backed by overwhelmingly strong and well-conducted experiments. The performance gains are not minor or incremental; UNREAL achieves more than double the performance of A3C on Labyrinth and sets a new state-of-the-art on Atari. The reported 10x speedup in data efficiency is a massive practical contribution. The thorough ablation studies clearly demonstrate the value of each component, and the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is a welcome and important addition.

### Supporting Arguments

The central thesis of this paper—that an agent can learn a better representation of its world by simultaneously learning how to control it in diverse ways—is both intuitive and powerful. The implementation of this idea via pixel and feature control is creative and effective. Instead of relying on hand-crafted auxiliary tasks that might be domain-specific (e.g., predicting enemy presence), pixel control provides a general, unsupervised objective for learning about cause and effect in any visual environment. The agent must learn which actions cause which changes on screen, a fundamental aspect of environmental understanding.

The empirical validation is exemplary. Testing on both a visually rich, modern 3D environment (Labyrinth) and the standard Atari benchmark demonstrates the generality of the approach. The ablation study in Figure 3 is particularly compelling, showing that each auxiliary task contributes positively to performance and that their combination is synergistic. The specific comparison in Figure 5, which shows that learning to *control* pixel changes is superior to merely *predicting* them or reconstructing the input, provides strong evidence for the paper's core conceptual claim. This kind of targeted experiment, which pits the novel idea directly against more conventional alternatives, is exactly what is needed to validate a bold new approach.

While the UNREAL agent is a complex system combining on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning and experience replay, this complexity feels justified by the results. The authors have effectively combined the strengths of different learning paradigms: the stability of A3C for the main task, and the data-efficiency of off-policy learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks. This hybrid approach is a significant engineering contribution in its own right and could serve as a blueprint for future complex agents.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Further Exploration of Feature Control:** The concept of feature control is fascinating, as it suggests an agent can improve by learning to control its own "thoughts". However, the results presented are preliminary. A more in-depth analysis would be welcome. For example, which layers are most effective to control? Does combining feature control with pixel control yield further gains?
*   **Qualitative Analysis of Learned Representations:** The paper convincingly shows *that* the auxiliary tasks improve performance, but provides little insight into *how* they shape the representation. It would be illuminating to include some qualitative analysis. For example, could t-SNE visualizations of the LSTM state show that the UNREAL agent learns more distinct representations for semantically different game states? What kinds of behaviors do the pixel control policies learn? Do they learn to spin in place to maximize visual change, or do they learn to interact with specific objects?
*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper focuses on data efficiency (in terms of environment steps), but what is the cost in terms of wall-clock time and computational resources? Adding multiple auxiliary heads and off-policy updates must increase the computational load per training step. A brief discussion of this trade-off would make the work more complete from a practical standpoint.

I have a few questions for the authors to clarify my understanding:

1.  Regarding the pixel control task, the instantaneous reward is defined as the average absolute pixel difference. Does this mean the optimal policy for a given cell is simply to move the camera as fast as possible to induce large optic flow? Or does the agent learn more nuanced behaviors, such as seeking out and interacting with dynamic objects that might appear in that cell?
2.  The reward prediction task uses a rebalanced replay buffer to ensure a 50% probability of sampling a non-zero reward. How does this interact with the value function replay, which samples from the buffer without this skew? Are they sampled from the same buffer, or are separate buffers or sampling schemes used?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of pixel control to the grid size (20x20)? Was this parameter tuned, or is the method generally robust to this choice?
