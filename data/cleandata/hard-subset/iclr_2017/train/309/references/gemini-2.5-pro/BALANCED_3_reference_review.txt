### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a novel deep reinforcement learning agent that significantly enhances the performance, data efficiency, and robustness of a strong baseline agent (A3C). The core idea is to enrich the learning signal for the agent's representation (a shared CNN-LSTM network) by training it on a set of auxiliary tasks in addition to the main policy optimization task. The paper proposes three complementary auxiliary tasks: 1) auxiliary control tasks, where off-policy Q-learning is used to learn policies that maximize environmental changes (e.g., pixel changes, or "pixel control") or internal network activations; 2) an auxiliary reward prediction task, where the agent predicts upcoming rewards from a short sequence of past observations, with training data rebalanced to emphasize sparse reward events; and 3) value function replay, which re-applies value function updates on recent experience. The UNREAL agent elegantly combines on-policy A3C for the main objective with off-policy learning for the auxiliary tasks, all from a single stream of experience. The authors provide an exceptionally thorough empirical evaluation on both the challenging, visually rich 3D Labyrinth environment and the 57-game Atari benchmark, demonstrating state-of-the-art results and dramatic improvements over the A3C baseline in all evaluated metrics.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Powerful and Well-Motivated Method:** The paper presents a compelling and well-motivated framework for improving deep RL through auxiliary tasks. The specific choice of complementary tasks—promoting control over the environment and sensitivity to sparse rewards—is insightful and effectively addresses key challenges in RL, such as representation learning from sparse signals. The architectural design that combines on-policy and off-policy learning is both elegant and highly effective.
2.  **Extensive and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The experimental results are a major strength of this work. The authors not only demonstrate state-of-the-art performance on two demanding benchmarks but also provide a comprehensive suite of ablations and analytical experiments. These experiments convincingly show the individual contributions of each component, validate the design choice of "control" over simpler "prediction" or "reconstruction," and establish the agent's improved robustness to hyperparameters, making the claims of the paper highly credible.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The central thesis of the paper—that auxiliary tasks can substantially improve representation learning in deep RL—is convincingly demonstrated. The authors build upon prior work in representation learning and intrinsic motivation but synthesize these ideas into a single, cohesive, and remarkably effective agent. The synergy between the different components is clear: the main A3C policy drives exploration, while the off-policy auxiliary tasks efficiently extract rich information from the resulting experience stream.

The empirical evaluation is exemplary. Introducing the new Labyrinth environment, with its rich visuals and complex tasks, is a valuable contribution in itself. The performance gains on this benchmark are not incremental but transformative; doubling the mean human-normalized score (from 54% to 87%) and achieving a 10x speedup in learning are truly significant results. This success is mirrored on the standard Atari benchmark, where UNREAL surpasses previous state-of-the-art results.

Crucially, the paper goes beyond simply showing a high score. The ablation studies (Figure 3) clearly dissect the contribution of each auxiliary task, showing that they are all beneficial and complementary. The comparison in Section 4.1.1 between pixel control, pixel change prediction, and pixel reconstruction is particularly insightful. It provides strong evidence that actively learning to *control* the environment provides a much richer learning signal for representation than passively predicting or reconstructing observations, validating a core hypothesis of the work. The analysis of hyperparameter robustness is also a welcome and important contribution, addressing a significant practical challenge in applying deep RL.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

The following points are intended as constructive suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical for the acceptance decision.

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper rightly emphasizes the massive gains in data efficiency. It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of the trade-off in terms of computational cost. What is the wall-clock time overhead or increase in FLOPs per update when adding the auxiliary tasks? This information would provide a more complete picture of the agent's overall efficiency.
*   **Feature Control:** The feature control task is an intriguing idea but is only presented with preliminary results. Expanding on this, even just in the appendix, would be valuable. For instance, what challenges were encountered? Is it more difficult to tune than pixel control? Providing more detail could inspire future work in this direction.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity of λPC:** The appendix notes that the weight for pixel control (λPC) was sampled from a different range for Atari vs. Labyrinth. This suggests it might be a sensitive hyperparameter that requires domain-specific tuning. A short discussion on the intuition behind this choice and guidance on how one might set it for a new environment would be beneficial for reproducibility and future applications of the method.
*   **Labyrinth Environment Availability:** The Labyrinth environment appears to be a fantastic new benchmark for the community. If there are plans to release it, mentioning this would be a great service to the research community.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to deepen my understanding of your work.

1.  In the reward prediction task, you state that biasing the feature shaping via skewed sampling does not bias the value function or policy. Could you elaborate on this? Since the policy and value functions are direct outputs of the LSTM, which itself receives input from the biased shared CNN features, it seems some bias would inevitably propagate. Is the argument that the on-policy A3C updates are strong enough to overcome this, or that the induced bias is actually a helpful inductive bias for the main task?
2.  The deconvolutional architecture for the pixel control Q-function is an interesting choice that leverages spatial structure. Did you experiment with simpler, non-spatial architectures (e.g., a single MLP head)? How critical was this specific architectural choice to the success of pixel control?
3.  In your comparison of control vs. prediction vs. reconstruction, you hypothesize that reconstruction hurts performance by focusing on irrelevant details. This is a very plausible explanation. An alternative hypothesis could be related to gradient dynamics (e.g., the reconstruction loss overwhelming the RL loss). Did you observe any particular challenges in balancing the loss weights for the reconstruction task compared to the control task?
