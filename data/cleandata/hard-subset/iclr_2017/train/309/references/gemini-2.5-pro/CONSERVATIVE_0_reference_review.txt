**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent architecture that augments a standard A3C agent with a suite of auxiliary tasks designed to accelerate and improve representation learning. The primary contributions are the introduction of two main classes of auxiliary tasks: 1) Auxiliary control tasks, specifically pixel control and feature control, where the agent learns off-policy to maximize local changes in its perceptual input or internal network activations. 2) Auxiliary reward prediction, where the agent learns to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of past observations, using a rebalanced experience replay buffer to focus on rare reward events. The proposed UNREAL agent combines these tasks with value function replay within an A3C framework. The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on a visually complex 3D environment (Labyrinth) and the Atari 57 suite that their approach significantly outperforms the A3C baseline in terms of final performance, data efficiency, and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

Decision: Weak Reject

1.  **High Algorithmic Complexity without Sufficient Justification:** The proposed UNREAL agent is a complex system that combines an on-policy actor-critic algorithm (A3C) with multiple off-policy, value-based auxiliary tasks trained from an experience replay buffer. While the empirical results are strong, the paper does not provide a sufficiently principled motivation for this specific combination of components over other, potentially simpler, approaches. The work appears to be a successful, but highly engineered, solution whose general applicability is not yet established.

2.  **Insufficient Ablation and Analysis of Component Interactions:** The paper lacks a thorough investigation into the relative contributions and interactions of its various components. While some ablations are provided, it is difficult to disentangle the benefits of experience replay itself from the specific auxiliary tasks that use it. It is unclear whether the performance gains stem from a genuine synergy between the proposed tasks or simply from adding more computation and a form of off-policy learning to the A3C baseline.

**Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The core idea of using auxiliary tasks to shape representations is well-established. The novelty here lies in the specific tasks proposed and their integration. However, this integration raises significant concerns about methodological soundness and generality.

The UNREAL agent combines the on-policy A3C algorithm with off-policy Q-learning updates for pixel control and value function replay. Training a shared network with such disparate update rules and data distributions (on-policy vs. off-policy from a buffer) can be unstable, yet the paper does not analyze or discuss these potential negative interferences. The impressive results may be due to careful, domain-specific tuning rather than a fundamentally robust combination of methods.

Furthermore, the auxiliary tasks themselves seem heuristic. The "pixel control" task, which involves learning policies to maximize pixel changes in a 20x20 grid, feels arbitrary. While effective in the visually rich Labyrinth environment, it is not obvious why this is a generally useful objective. In environments with subtle state changes or significant distractors, this objective could easily misguide representation learning by focusing on irrelevant dynamics. The paper compares control to prediction and reconstruction, which is a good step, but fails to motivate this specific control objective over other established auxiliary tasks, such as learning an environment dynamics model.

The claim of improved robustness to hyperparameters is also questionable. The UNREAL agent introduces several new hyperparameters (λVR, λPC, λRP) in addition to those of A3C. The analysis in Figures 3 and 8 only shows robustness with respect to A3C's learning rate and entropy cost. A true test of robustness would require analyzing the sensitivity to the newly introduced weights, which could be just as critical to tune for optimal performance.

Finally, while the performance gains are impressive, they come at the cost of a much more complex system. Before endorsing such a complex architecture, it is essential to ensure that a more rigorously tuned baseline could not achieve similar results. For instance, how would a standard A3C agent perform if augmented with a simple experience replay mechanism for value function updates (A3C + VR), without the additional control and prediction tasks? The current ablation studies do not sufficiently address these simpler alternatives, making it hard to attribute the gains specifically to the novel contributions of the paper.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

*   The paper would be significantly strengthened by a more thorough ablation study. Specifically, evaluating A3C combined with each auxiliary component individually (A3C + Pixel Control, A3C + Reward Prediction, A3C + Value Replay) would provide clearer insight into where the performance gains originate.
*   The justification for the pixel control task could be improved. Visualizing the learned pixel control policies or analyzing the features that emerge from this task could provide more concrete evidence that it encourages the agent to learn meaningful interactions with its environment.
*   A discussion of the computational overhead is missing. The auxiliary tasks add significant computation (e.g., a deconvolutional network for pixel control). Quantifying this cost relative to the baseline would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved.
*   The connection to prior work on intrinsic motivation could be made more explicit. Learning to control features of the environment is a core concept in that field, and positioning this work more firmly within that context would be beneficial.

**Questions for the Authors**

1.  How sensitive is the performance of the pixel control task to the grid discretization (e.g., 20x20 vs 10x10) and the choice of metric for pixel change (e.g., L1 vs L2 norm)? Does this require careful tuning for each new environment?
2.  Have you observed any instabilities from combining on-policy A3C updates with off-policy Q-learning updates from the replay buffer on the shared representation? Is there a risk of catastrophic forgetting or representation drift due to the different data distributions?
3.  Could you provide an analysis of the agent's sensitivity to the new loss weights (λVR, λPC, λRP)? How was the range for sampling these hyperparameters determined?
4.  The reward prediction task samples from the replay buffer to create a balanced dataset of zero and non-zero rewards. How does this perform in environments where all rewards are of the same sign (e.g., only positive), or where rewards are dense? Does the benefit of this task diminish as reward sparsity decreases?
