### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel deep reinforcement learning architecture designed to improve data efficiency, final performance, and hyperparameter robustness. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong on-policy agent (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks that are learned in parallel from the same stream of experience. These tasks are designed to accelerate the development of a rich and useful feature representation.

The key auxiliary tasks proposed are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks**: The agent learns off-policy value functions (using Q-learning) for pseudo-rewards that encourage control over the environment. Two specific tasks are explored: *pixel control*, where the agent learns to maximize changes in different regions of the input pixels, and *feature control*, where it learns to maximize the activations of its own hidden units.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction**: The agent is trained to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of past observations. This task is trained on a replay buffer with skewed sampling to over-represent rare rewarding events, helping the feature representation become more sensitive to reward-predictive states.
3.  **Value Function Replay**: The agent uses experience replay to perform additional updates to its value function, further stabilizing and accelerating value learning.

The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment and 57 Atari games. The results demonstrate that UNREAL substantially outperforms the A3C baseline, achieving more than double the human-normalized score in Labyrinth (87% vs 54%), reaching the A3C baseline performance with 10x fewer environment steps, and setting a new state-of-the-art on the Atari benchmark (880% mean human-normalized score).

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

1.  **Significant Performance Improvement and Data Efficiency:** The paper introduces a well-motivated architecture that yields substantial, not just incremental, improvements over a very strong baseline (A3C). The demonstrated 10x speedup in learning on the challenging Labyrinth domain is a significant contribution to addressing the critical issue of data efficiency in deep RL. The state-of-the-art results on both Labyrinth and Atari underscore the effectiveness and generality of the approach.
2.  **Excellent and Thorough Empirical Validation:** The experimental methodology is rigorous and convincing. The authors evaluate their agent on two diverse and challenging domains. Crucially, they provide extensive ablation studies that clearly isolate the benefits of each auxiliary component. The comparison between learning to *control* pixel changes versus simply *predicting* them provides strong evidence for their specific formulation of auxiliary control tasks. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is a valuable and often overlooked contribution that strengthens the practical applicability of the method.

### **Supporting Arguments for Decision**

The paper's strength lies in its elegant combination of on-policy and off-policy learning to achieve a "best of both worlds" architecture. The main policy is learned on-policy via A3C, which is well-suited for continuous control and recurrent architectures. Simultaneously, the auxiliary tasks are learned off-policy from a replay buffer, which allows for much greater data efficiency and techniques like prioritized sampling (as used for reward prediction). This hybrid approach is a key architectural insight.

The design of the auxiliary tasks themselves is novel and well-motivated. While the general idea of auxiliary tasks has been explored, the specific formulation of *pixel control* as a set of parallel Q-learning problems with a deconvolutional output is a clever way to generate a dense, self-supervised training signal that encourages the agent to learn about the consequences of its actions. The results in Figure 5, showing that controlling pixels is superior to predicting pixel changes or reconstructing the input, strongly support this design choice and offer valuable insights for future work in representation learning for RL.

The empirical results are outstanding. Achieving a mean score of 87% of human performance on the visually rich and complex Labyrinth environment is a major achievement. The consistency of improvement across both Labyrinth and the 57-game Atari suite demonstrates that the benefits are not domain-specific but rather stem from fundamental improvements to the learning process. The robustness plots (Figure 3, right column) are particularly compelling, showing that UNREAL not only achieves a higher peak performance but also has a much higher probability of performing well across a random sweep of hyperparameters, which is of great practical importance.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on data efficiency in terms of environment steps. However, the UNREAL agent performs significantly more computation per environment step than the A3C baseline due to the multiple auxiliary losses and replay-based updates. A brief discussion of the trade-off between sample efficiency and computational cost (e.g., wall-clock time or FLOPs per update) would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's efficiency.
*   **Clarity on State Space Definition:** In Section 3.1, the state space S is defined to include "the activations of the hidden units of the network." This is a non-standard definition, as S typically refers to the state of the environment. While it is clear what is meant in the context of feature control (the reward is a function of the agent's internal state), this phrasing could be confusing. It would be clearer to define the pseudo-reward function as being dependent on the agent's internal activations, rather than redefining the environment's state space.
*   **Interaction Between Auxiliary Tasks:** It would be interesting to see a brief discussion on the potential interplay between the different auxiliary tasks. For example, does feature control learn similar things to pixel control, given that features are derived from pixels? Do the control tasks and the reward prediction task learn complementary representations? While a full empirical study is not necessary, some qualitative discussion would add depth.

### **Questions for the Authors**

*   For the feature control task, the second hidden layer was chosen. What was the rationale behind this specific choice? Have you experimented with controlling features from other layers (e.g., the final LSTM layer), and how sensitive is the performance to this architectural decision?
*   The pixel control task uses a 20x20 grid over the input image. How was this granularity determined? Is there a trade-off where a finer grid might provide a richer signal but be computationally more expensive or harder to learn, while a coarser grid might be less informative?
*   The reward prediction task uses a separate feedforward network on a stack of recent frames. The motivation given is to simplify the temporal aspects of the task. Did you experiment with predicting the reward directly from the main A3C agent's LSTM state? If so, how did that compare to the separate network? This choice seems to imply that the raw recent visual information is more predictive of immediate reward than the LSTM's compressed history.
