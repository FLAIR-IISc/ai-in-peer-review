### Summary

This paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to dramatically improve the data efficiency, robustness, and overall performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a standard on-policy agent (A3C) with a suite of auxiliary tasks that are trained off-policy using a replay buffer. These tasks are designed to accelerate and enrich the learning of the agent's internal representation (the shared convolutional and recurrent layers). The authors introduce two main classes of auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control, where the agent learns policies to maximize local changes in its perceptual input (pixel control) or its own network activations (feature control); and reward prediction, where the agent predicts the immediate future reward from a short sequence of observations, with training data rebalanced to oversample rewarding events. The entire system is trained end-to-end, combining the on-policy A3C loss with off-policy Q-learning and prediction losses for the auxiliary tasks. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of UNREAL on two challenging domains: a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth, and the 57-game Atari suite. The results are exceptionally strong, showing massive improvements over the A3C baseline in learning speed (up to 10x faster), final performance (doubling human-normalized scores on Labyrinth and achieving a new state-of-the-art on Atari), and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

### Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision: Accept**

This is an outstanding paper that introduces a powerful and conceptually novel framework for representation learning in RL. It is a bold step away from incremental improvements and offers a new direction for tackling the core challenges of data efficiency and sparse rewards. My decision is based on two key reasons:

1.  **High Novelty and Conceptual Significance:** The paper's primary contribution is the idea of using auxiliary *control* tasks, learned off-policy, for the sole purpose of shaping a better representation for a primary on-policy agent. The concept of "pixel control"—learning thousands of micro-policies to maximally change different parts of the visual input—is a creative, non-obvious, and highly effective way to force the agent to learn about the dynamics and controllable aspects of its environment. This is a significant conceptual leap beyond simpler auxiliary tasks like reconstruction or feature prediction, as convincingly shown in the ablation studies. The UNREAL architecture, which elegantly combines the strengths of on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (Q-learning with replay) methods, is itself a novel and compelling contribution.

2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results and Rigorous Validation:** The performance gains demonstrated are not minor; they represent a dramatic improvement over a very strong state-of-the-art baseline. The reported 10x speed-up and doubling of mean human-normalized score on the challenging Labyrinth environment are remarkable. The fact that the same architecture also achieves state-of-the-art results across the Atari suite speaks to its generality. The paper's empirical rigor is further strengthened by extensive ablation studies that clearly isolate the contribution of each auxiliary component, as well as a thorough analysis of robustness to hyperparameters, which is a critical but often overlooked aspect of RL research.

### Supporting Arguments

The central thesis of the paper—that an agent can learn more effectively by simultaneously trying to control its environment and predict rewards in addition to maximizing the main task reward—is convincingly validated. The authors smartly decouple the training schemes for the different objectives: the main policy is trained with stable on-policy A3C, while the dense auxiliary signals are learned with the more data-efficient off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer. This architectural choice is key to the method's success.

The comparison in Figure 5 is particularly illuminating. The authors show that learning to *control* pixel changes is substantially better than merely *predicting* them, which in turn is better than simply *reconstructing* the input. This provides strong evidence that active interaction and control are more powerful signals for representation learning than passive observation and prediction, a finding with potentially broad implications beyond this specific paper.

Furthermore, the introduction of the Labyrinth environment is a valuable contribution in its own right. It presents a more complex and visually rich challenge than many standard benchmarks, pushing the boundaries of what current methods can achieve and providing an excellent testbed for evaluating progress in areas like navigation, exploration, and control in 3D worlds.

### Additional Feedback and Questions

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   The selection of auxiliary tasks, while effective, feels somewhat handcrafted. Could the authors speculate on whether there might be a more general principle for generating or selecting auxiliary tasks? For example, could one formulate tasks based on maximizing information-theoretic quantities or discovering bottlenecks in the environment's state space?
*   The "feature control" task is extremely promising, as it suggests the agent can learn to introspectively control its own internal representations. The paper presents this as a preliminary result. A deeper analysis comparing the effects of controlling different layers or types of features would be a fascinating direction for future work.
*   The use of a deconvolutional network for the pixel control Q-values is a clever architectural choice to exploit spatial locality. Did you experiment with a simpler baseline, such as an independent MLP head for each grid cell? Understanding the performance difference would help clarify the importance of this specific architectural detail.
*   The replay buffer is relatively small (2k transitions), focusing the auxiliary learning on very recent experience. What was the rationale for this choice, and have you investigated the effect of larger buffer sizes? One could imagine that a larger buffer might provide more diverse data for representation learning, but perhaps at the cost of stability due to greater off-policy divergence.
