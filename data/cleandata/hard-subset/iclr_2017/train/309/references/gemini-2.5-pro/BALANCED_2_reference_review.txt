### **Summary of the Paper**

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, learning stability, and overall performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the augmentation of a powerful on-policy agent, A3C, with a set of auxiliary tasks trained off-policy using experience replay. These tasks are not intended to be solved for their own sake but to provide dense, diverse learning signals that accelerate the formation of a rich and robust state representation in the shared neural network encoder. The authors propose three main auxiliary tasks: (1) Auxiliary Control, where the agent learns policies to maximize pixel changes in different parts of the visual input (pixel control) or activations in its own hidden layers (feature control); (2) Reward Prediction, where the agent predicts the immediate reward from a short sequence of past observations, using a skewed sampling strategy to focus on sparse rewarding events; and (3) Value Function Replay, which performs additional value function learning on replayed data. The paper presents a comprehensive empirical evaluation on the visually complex 3D Labyrinth environment and 57 Atari games, demonstrating that UNREAL dramatically outperforms the A3C baseline, achieving a 10x speedup in learning, doubling the final performance on Labyrinth, and setting a new state of the art on the Atari suite.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision: Accept**

1.  The paper proposes a novel and principled method for representation learning in RL that effectively combines the strengths of on-policy (A3C) and off-policy (Q-learning with experience replay) methods. The suite of auxiliary tasks is well-motivated and targets distinct aspects of representation learning, from environmental dynamics to reward-centric features.

2.  The claims are substantiated by an exceptionally strong and thorough experimental evaluation. The results show massive improvements over a state-of-the-art baseline not only in final performance but also in data efficiency and hyperparameter robustness. The inclusion of careful ablation studies and comparisons to alternative auxiliary tasks provides convincing evidence for the efficacy of the proposed components.

### **Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's strengths are numerous and significant.

*   **Novelty and Significance:** While the idea of using auxiliary tasks is not entirely new, this work makes several key contributions. The architectural design, which integrates an online, on-policy A3C agent for the main task with off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary tasks via a shared replay buffer, is a powerful and elegant synthesis. The proposed auxiliary tasks themselves, particularly "pixel control," are creative and effective. By framing the task as learning to control environmental features rather than just passively predicting them, the agent is encouraged to learn a causal understanding of its environment. The sheer magnitude of the performance gains (e.g., from 54% to 87% human-normalized score on Labyrinth) demonstrates the high impact of this approach on challenging RL problems.

*   **Methodological Soundness and Rigor:** The experimental design is a model of rigor in deep RL research. The authors evaluate their method on two distinct and challenging domains: a novel, visually rich 3D environment and the standard Atari benchmark. The primary baseline, A3C, is a strong, contemporary method. The ablation study (Figure 3) is crucial, as it clearly demonstrates that the performance gains are not due to a single trick but are an additive effect of the different auxiliary tasks. Furthermore, the comparison of pixel control against pixel prediction and reconstruction (Figure 5) directly supports the central hypothesis that learning to control is a more powerful objective for representation learning than passive prediction.

*   **Robustness and Practical Relevance:** A standout feature of the evaluation is the analysis of robustness to hyperparameters. By running many experiments with randomly sampled hyperparameters and showing the sorted performance curves (Figure 3, right panels), the authors convincingly argue that UNREAL is not only higher-performing but also more stable and easier to tune than the A3C baseline. This is an immensely valuable property for practical applications and is too often overlooked in RL research.

*   **Clarity:** The paper is very well-written. It clearly motivates the problem of representation learning in sparse-reward settings and explains the rationale behind each component of the UNREAL agent. The diagrams and detailed appendices provide the necessary information for a full understanding of the architecture and experimental setup.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to be constructive and are not critical to my assessment of the paper's quality.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The UNREAL agent is considerably more complex than the A3C baseline, with multiple additional network heads and loss functions computed from a replay buffer. A brief discussion of the computational overhead (in terms of wall-clock time or FLOPs per update) would be beneficial for practitioners seeking to implement or build upon this work.
*   **Analysis of "Control" Tasks:** The paper demonstrates that pixel control is effective, but it would be interesting to see more analysis. For instance, is the agent sensitive to the grid size for pixel control? A brief discussion of the preliminary "feature control" results would also be interesting. Why was it not included in the final UNREAL agent, given its strong performance? Is it more or less stable than pixel control?
*   **Deeper Representation Analysis:** The paper argues that auxiliary tasks improve the agent's representation. While the performance gains strongly support this, a more direct analysis of the learned representations would be fascinating. For example, visualizations (e.g., t-SNE) of the LSTM state or CNN features could provide qualitative insight into how the auxiliary tasks shape the representation space.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to solidify my understanding of the work.

1.  The pixel control task rewards the agent for maximizing the absolute difference in pixel intensity. The Labyrinth environment is described as having dynamic textures that "shimmer and pulse." Does this inherent environmental dynamism create a noisy reward signal for pixel control? How does the agent learn to distinguish between changes it causes and changes that are part of the environment's background dynamics?
2.  The reward prediction (RP) task samples from the replay buffer with a 50/50 split between rewarding and non-rewarding transitions. Have you explored other ratios or more adaptive schemes? For instance, could a prioritization based on the magnitude of the reward or the TD-error of the RP loss itself yield further benefits?
3.  You mention that Value Function Replay (LVR) "exploits newly discovered features shaped by reward prediction." Could you elaborate on this mechanism? Given that LVR samples uniformly while LRP samples are skewed, is the shared convolutional encoder the sole pathway for this feature transfer, or is there a more direct interaction?
4.  Figure 5 shows impressive preliminary results for the "feature control" auxiliary task. What was the rationale for not including it in the final UNREAL agent that was evaluated across all Labyrinth and Atari games? Was it less stable, more computationally expensive, or did it simply not provide additive benefits when combined with pixel control?
