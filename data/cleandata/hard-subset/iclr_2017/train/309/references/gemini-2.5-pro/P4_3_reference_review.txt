### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), an agent architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a base asynchronous advantage actor-critic (A3C) agent with several auxiliary tasks that are trained concurrently to promote the learning of a more effective shared representation.

The contributions are threefold:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing pseudo-rewards. Two specific tasks are proposed: "pixel control," where the agent learns to maximally change pixel intensities in different regions of the input image, and "feature control," where it learns to activate units in its own hidden layers.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction Task:** To combat reward sparsity, the agent learns to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a sequence of recent observations. This task is trained using a skewed sampling of experience from a replay buffer to over-represent rewarding events.
3.  **A Hybrid On-Policy/Off-Policy Architecture:** The agent combines the on-policy A3C algorithm for the main task with off-policy Q-learning updates from an experience replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks and for additional value function training.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through extensive experiments on two challenging domains: a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth, and the 57-game Atari suite. The results show that the UNREAL agent significantly outperforms the A3C baseline, achieving a 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth and establishing a new state-of-the-art on Atari.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The paper presents a novel and well-motivated combination of on-policy learning with a suite of off-policy auxiliary tasks. This hybrid architecture effectively addresses the critical challenge of data efficiency in deep RL.
2.  The claims of improved performance, data efficiency, and robustness are substantiated by an exceptionally thorough and rigorous set of experiments, including strong baseline comparisons, comprehensive ablation studies, and state-of-the-art results on standard benchmarks.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's technical approach is sound and well-situated within the literature. While the use of auxiliary tasks for representation learning is not new, the specific instantiation in UNREAL is novel and effective. The combination of an on-policy policy-gradient method (A3C) for the main objective with off-policy value-based methods (Q-learning) for auxiliary tasks trained from a replay buffer is a key contribution. This allows the agent to benefit from the stability of A3C while leveraging the data efficiency of experience replay for representation learning.

The choice of auxiliary tasks is well-justified. "Pixel control" encourages the agent to learn about the environment's controllable dynamics, a more active learning signal than passive prediction or reconstruction. The authors support this with a direct empirical comparison in Section 4.1.1, which shows that learning to control pixel changes is superior to merely predicting them or reconstructing the input. "Reward prediction," coupled with skewed replay, directly targets the problem of sparse rewards by forcing the representation to become sensitive to reward-predictive states.

The empirical evaluation is a major strength of this work.
*   **Performance:** The UNREAL agent achieves dramatic performance gains over a strong A3C baseline on the visually complex Labyrinth environment (mean human-normalized score improves from 54% to 87%). It also achieves state-of-the-art results on the Atari benchmark, surpassing previous methods with a mean score of 880%.
*   **Data Efficiency:** The claim of a 10x learning speedup on Labyrinth is clearly supported by the learning curves in Figure 3, demonstrating a significant practical benefit.
*   **Robustness:** The analysis of performance across a wide range of hyperparameters (Figure 3, top right) provides convincing evidence that the addition of auxiliary tasks makes the learning algorithm more stable and less sensitive to hyperparameter tuning, which is a significant practical advantage.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The authors systematically evaluate the contribution of each component (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay), demonstrating that each part provides a tangible benefit and that their combination yields the best results. This rigorous analysis validates the design choices of the UNREAL architecture.

In summary, the paper introduces a technically sound and novel method, supports it with clear motivation, and validates its effectiveness with an extensive and convincing set of experiments.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

The following points are suggestions to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to the decision.
*   **Clarity on Training Flow:** While Section 3.4 describes the combined loss, the interplay between the on-policy A3C updates and the off-policy updates from the replay buffer could be made even clearer. A diagram or algorithm box illustrating a full training step (e.g., perform on-policy A3C update, add experience to buffer, sample from buffer for off-policy auxiliary and value updates) would enhance readability.
*   **Analysis of Feature Control:** The results for the "feature control" task are presented as preliminary and only on one level. While promising, a more complete analysis, including its combination with the other auxiliary tasks as mentioned, would be a valuable addition.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** The paper provides excellent quantitative results. A brief qualitative analysis could provide further insight. For example, visualizing the learned value functions for some pixel control tasks (i.e., the Q_aux maps) could help illustrate what the agent learns about controlling its environment.
*   **Labyrinth Environment Details:** As Labyrinth is a new evaluation platform introduced in this paper, providing a few more key details in the main text (e.g., typical episode length, frequency of rewards) would be helpful for readers to better contextualize the results, rather than relying solely on the appendix.

### 5. Questions for the Authors
*   **Pixel Control Reward:** The reward for pixel control is the average absolute pixel difference. How sensitive is the performance to this choice of metric? Have you considered alternative metrics, such as those more robust to global illumination changes or focused on structural differences, and do you hypothesize they might perform better or worse? Furthermore, how was the 20x20 grid size chosen, and have you studied the sensitivity to this discretization?
*   **Replay Buffer Size:** The replay buffer stores the 2000 most recent transitions. This seems relatively small, meaning the off-policy updates are performed on very recent, and thus highly correlated, data. Could you comment on the sensitivity of the results to the buffer size? Is there a specific benefit to using such a "short-term" memory for these auxiliary tasks compared to a larger, more conventional buffer that stores more diverse experiences?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity (λ):** The paper excellently demonstrates robustness to learning rate and entropy cost. The loss weights (λ_VR, λ_PC, λ_RP) are also critical hyperparameters. Could you provide some insight into the sensitivity of the final performance to these weights? The appendix mentions they are sampled from a log-uniform distribution, but an ablation or sensitivity plot for these weights would be very informative.
