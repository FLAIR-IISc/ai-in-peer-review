### Paper Review

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, overall performance, and hyperparameter robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to augment a strong baseline agent, Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), with a set of auxiliary tasks that are trained concurrently to shape a more effective and general representation within a shared neural network.

The main contributions are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions for maximizing two types of pseudo-rewards: changes in pixel intensity in different regions of the input (pixel control) and activations of hidden units in its own network (feature control). These tasks encourage the agent to learn controllable aspects of its environment.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction:** To combat reward sparsity, the agent is trained on a supervised task to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a short sequence of recent observations. This is enhanced by using a replay buffer with skewed sampling to over-represent sequences leading to non-zero rewards.
3.  **Value Function Replay:** The agent leverages the replay buffer to perform additional off-policy updates on the main task's value function, effectively performing extra value iteration steps on recent experience to accelerate learning.
4.  **A Hybrid On-Policy/Off-Policy Architecture:** UNREAL effectively combines the on-policy A3C algorithm for the main policy with off-policy Q-learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks and value function replay, capitalizing on the strengths of both approaches.

The authors provide a comprehensive empirical evaluation on two challenging domains: 57 Atari games and a visually rich, first-person 3D environment called Labyrinth. The results demonstrate that UNREAL significantly outperforms the A3C baseline and other state-of-the-art methods, achieving dramatic improvements in learning speed (up to 10x faster), final performance, and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Clear Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Performance and Efficiency Gains:** The paper presents a method that achieves a substantial and impressive improvement over a very strong state-of-the-art baseline (A3C). The claims of improved data efficiency (10x speedup on Labyrinth) and final performance (doubling human-normalized scores on Labyrinth and setting a new SOTA on Atari) are well-supported by rigorous experiments.
2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Combination of Ideas:** The paper effectively integrates several powerful concepts—on-policy and off-policy learning, representation learning, and auxiliary tasks—into a single, cohesive agent. The specific choice of auxiliary tasks, particularly pixel control and reward prediction with skewed replay, is novel, well-motivated, and demonstrated to be highly effective through thorough ablation studies.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The decision to accept is based on the paper's novelty, the strength of its empirical results, and the quality of the experimental design.

*   **Methodological Soundness and Novelty:** The UNREAL architecture is a thoughtful combination of existing and new ideas. While auxiliary tasks have been explored before, this paper's contribution lies in the specific tasks chosen and their integration into a modern deep RL framework. The concept of "pixel control"—learning policies to maximize visual change—is an intuitive and powerful unsupervised objective that forces the agent to learn about cause and effect in its environment. The reward prediction task, coupled with skewed sampling, is a very clever and practical solution to the common problem of sparse rewards, allowing the agent to learn reward-relevant features much more quickly without biasing the main policy. The resulting hybrid on-policy/off-policy agent is a significant architectural contribution.

*   **Empirical Rigor and Strength:** The experimental evaluation is exemplary.
    *   **Challenging Benchmarks:** The use of the new Labyrinth environment is a major strength. It presents a more complex perceptual and control challenge than Atari, making the strong results there particularly compelling. Testing across the full suite of 57 Atari games demonstrates the generality of the approach.
    *   **Strong Baselines and Ablations:** The paper compares against A3C, a top-tier algorithm. Crucially, the ablation studies (Figures 3 & 4) clearly break down the contribution of each auxiliary task, showing that each component adds value and their combination is synergistic. The comparison against alternative unsupervised tasks like input reconstruction further strengthens the claim that *control* is a more effective objective than prediction or reconstruction for representation learning in this context.
    *   **Robustness Analysis:** The analysis of hyperparameter robustness (Figure 3, Top Right and Bottom Right) is a critical and often overlooked contribution. Demonstrating that the method is not only higher-performing but also easier to tune significantly increases its practical value for other researchers and practitioners.

**4. Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

This is an excellent paper that is well-written and presents a significant advance. The following points are intended as constructive feedback for further improvement and are not critical to the decision to accept.

*   **Clarity on Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The paper demonstrates robustness to learning rate and entropy cost. However, the combined loss function introduces three new weighting hyperparameters (`λVR`, `λPC`, `λRP`). In the implementation details, `λVR` and `λRP` are set to 1, while `λPC` is sampled from a log-uniform distribution. Could you clarify the rationale for fixing two of these and sampling the third? A brief discussion on the sensitivity of the agent's performance to these new hyperparameters would strengthen the paper further.

*   **Feature Control vs. Pixel Control:** The paper introduces both pixel control and feature control, but the main UNREAL agent in the experiments appears to use pixel control. The unsupervised learning comparison in Figure 5 shows feature control is also very effective, nearly matching pixel control. Was pixel control found to be consistently superior, or was it chosen for another reason? A short comment on the trade-offs or relative performance of these two control tasks would be insightful.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The UNREAL agent adds several new components (multiple Q-learning heads, a reward prediction network, experience replay) on top of A3C. Could you comment on the computational overhead of these additions? For example, what is the approximate increase in wall-clock training time or required FLOPs per environment step compared to the A3C baseline? While the data efficiency is vastly improved, understanding the computational cost is important for practical applications.

*   **Minor Presentation Suggestion:** Section 3.3, "Experience Replay," describes the mechanism used by the auxiliary tasks. The flow of the methods section might be slightly improved by integrating the discussion of replay directly into the sections describing the tasks that use it (i.e., mentioning replay in Sections 3.1, 3.2, and for the value function replay), rather than having it as a standalone section. This is a minor point, and the current structure is still clear.
