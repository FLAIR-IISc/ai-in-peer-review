### Review of "Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning" (UNREAL)

**1. Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to improve the performance, data efficiency, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is the augmentation of a strong on-policy baseline agent, Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C), with a set of off-policy auxiliary tasks that share a common representation (a CNN-LSTM network). These auxiliary tasks are trained from a replay buffer to maximize data usage and are designed to help the agent build a richer, more general representation of its environment. The specific auxiliary tasks proposed are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks:** The agent learns off-policy value functions to maximize pseudo-rewards, such as maximizing pixel changes in different regions of the input (pixel control) or maximizing the activation of hidden units (feature control). This encourages the agent to learn how its actions affect the environment and its own internal state.
2.  **Reward Prediction:** The agent is trained to predict the immediate upcoming reward from a sequence of past observations. To handle sparse rewards, training samples are drawn from a replay buffer in a balanced way (equal chance of zero and non-zero rewards).
3.  **Value Function Replay:** The agent performs additional value function regression on sequences sampled from the replay buffer, effectively performing more value iteration steps to accelerate learning.

The authors demonstrate through extensive experiments on a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth and the standard Atari-57 benchmark that the UNREAL agent significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art A3C baseline in terms of final performance, learning speed (up to 10x faster), and robustness to hyperparameter choices.

**2. Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is a very strong paper that presents a novel and impactful contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Significant Methodological Innovation:** The paper introduces a compelling and well-motivated framework for representation learning in RL. The idea of using auxiliary *control* tasks, as opposed to purely predictive ones, is a powerful concept that forces the agent to learn a model of environmental dynamics in an actionable way. The hybrid architecture, which cleverly combines the stability of on-policy A3C for the main task with the data efficiency of off-policy learning from a replay buffer for the auxiliary tasks, is an elegant and effective engineering solution.

2.  **Exceptionally Thorough and Convincing Empirical Evaluation:** The claims made in the paper are supported by extensive and rigorous experiments. The authors not only show massive performance gains over a state-of-the-art baseline on the standard Atari benchmark but also introduce and evaluate on Labyrinth, a new and challenging 3D domain. The inclusion of comprehensive ablation studies, a direct comparison between their proposed control task and more standard prediction/reconstruction tasks, and a detailed analysis of robustness to hyperparameters provides overwhelming evidence for the efficacy of their approach.

**3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper's core hypothesis is that learning to control aspects of the environment and the agent's own feature activations leads to better representations for reinforcement learning. This is a powerful idea that moves beyond passive, predictive auxiliary tasks. The experimental results strongly validate this hypothesis. In Figure 5, the comparison showing that pixel *control* is significantly better than pixel *prediction*, which in turn is better than pixel *reconstruction*, provides a crucial piece of evidence that directly supports the paper's central claim.

The empirical results are not merely incremental; they are a substantial leap forward. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves a mean human-normalized score of 87% compared to A3C's 54%, while requiring less than 10% of the data to do so. On Atari, it achieves a mean score of 880%, well above the previous state-of-the-art. These results are highly significant and demonstrate the practical value of the proposed method.

The paper is also very well-written and clear. The motivation for each auxiliary task is well-explained, the architecture is described in sufficient detail (especially with the appendices), and the experimental setup is transparent. The related work section does an excellent job of situating the work, clearly distinguishing it from prior methods like Horde, UVFAs, and successor representations by highlighting that the auxiliary policies are used exclusively for representation learning.

**4. Additional Feedback for Improvement**

While this is an excellent paper, the following points could be addressed to further strengthen it:

*   **Computational Cost:** The addition of multiple auxiliary tasks presumably increases the computational overhead per training step compared to the A3C baseline. A brief discussion of this trade-off (e.g., wall-clock time vs. sample efficiency) would provide a more complete picture of the algorithm's practical utility.
*   **Feature Control:** The feature control task is introduced as a promising idea but is only evaluated on one level in a preliminary fashion. It would be beneficial to either integrate it more fully into the main results or frame it more explicitly as a direction for future work. The current presentation leaves its contribution to the final UNREAL agent somewhat ambiguous.
*   **Interplay of Tasks:** The paper demonstrates that combining the tasks leads to the best performance. A deeper analysis or discussion on *why* these tasks are complementary would be insightful. For example, does reward prediction help the agent focus on salient events while pixel control provides a general understanding of physics and object interaction?

**5. Questions for the Authors**

1.  Regarding the pixel control task, the reward is the average absolute pixel difference. Did you experiment with other metrics for change, and how sensitive is the performance to the grid size (20x20 in this case)?
2.  The paper shows that control is superior to prediction. What is your primary intuition for this? Is it because learning a control policy requires more exploration and a deeper understanding of cause-and-effect than simply making a one-step prediction?
3.  Was the feature control task included in the final UNREAL agent that produced the main results on Labyrinth and Atari, or were those results generated using only pixel control, reward prediction, and value replay? Section 4.1.1 refers to feature control results as "preliminary," which suggests it might not have been part of the full agent tested in Figures 3 and 4.
