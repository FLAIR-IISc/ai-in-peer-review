**Summary of the paper**

This paper introduces UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that augments the Asynchronous Advantage Actor-Critic (A3C) algorithm with several auxiliary tasks aimed at improving representation learning. The goal is to enhance data efficiency, learning speed, and final performance, particularly in visually complex environments with sparse rewards. The core idea is to train a shared network (CNN-LSTM) on the main policy-gradient objective concurrently with off-policy auxiliary objectives. The proposed auxiliary tasks are: 1) Auxiliary Control tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions for pseudo-rewards corresponding to maximizing pixel changes ("pixel control") or hidden unit activations ("feature control"); 2) An Auxiliary Reward Prediction task, which predicts the immediate next reward from a short sequence of past observations, trained on rebalanced samples from a replay buffer; and 3) Value Function Replay, which performs additional value function updates on re-sampled experiences. The authors evaluate their UNREAL agent on the standard Atari 2600 benchmark and a new, visually rich 3D environment called Labyrinth, demonstrating significant performance gains, faster learning, and improved hyperparameter robustness over the A3C baseline.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Reject

**Key Reasons:**
1.  The primary novel contributions—the auxiliary control tasks—are heuristic and lack a strong, generalizable motivation. The paper does not provide sufficient evidence to suggest that maximizing pixel changes is a fundamentally sound principle for representation learning, rather than an ad-hoc engineering trick that happens to work on the tested environments.
2.  The overall UNREAL architecture combines multiple, largely disconnected techniques (on-policy A3C, off-policy Q-learning for control, reward prediction, and value function replay) into a single, complex system. The empirical evaluation is not thorough enough to disentangle the contributions of these components, making it difficult to assess the true source of the performance gains. Much of the improvement may stem from the more established practice of incorporating experience replay, rather than the novel auxiliary tasks.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper presents an impressive engineering effort, and the results on the Labyrinth environment are striking. However, the work feels more like a collection of effective techniques bundled together than a principled advance in reinforcement learning methodology.

My main concern lies with the auxiliary control tasks, specifically "pixel control." The justification provided is that "changes in the perceptual stream often correspond to important events." While this may be true, it does not automatically follow that learning a policy to *maximize* such changes is a universally beneficial objective for building a good state representation. In many scenarios, an optimal agent might need to minimize visual change (e.g., in a stealth-based game) or focus on subtle cues that are uncorrelated with large-scale pixel changes. The paper presents this as a general method, but its motivation is entirely empirical and lacks theoretical grounding. The comparison in Section 4.1.1, showing pixel control is better than prediction or reconstruction, is limited to only three navigation levels in the new Labyrinth environment. This is insufficient evidence to validate such a core claim. It is an interesting idea, but without more rigorous justification and broader validation, it remains an unproven, environment-specific heuristic.

Furthermore, the UNREAL agent is a highly complex system that combines at least four distinct learning mechanisms. The ablation study in Figure 3 (top left) on Labyrinth is a good start, but it is not comprehensive enough. A crucial missing baseline is A3C augmented with only value function replay (A3C+VR). Experience replay is a well-established technique for improving data efficiency in off-policy algorithms like DQN. Its application here to the value function component of an on-policy algorithm is a non-trivial contribution in itself, and it is plausible that this component alone accounts for a large portion of the reported gains in sample efficiency. Without isolating this effect, especially on the standard Atari benchmark, the claims about the novel control and reward tasks are significantly weakened. The paper essentially bundles a known technique (experience replay) with several new, unproven ones, and attributes the collective success to the entire package.

Finally, while the introduction of the Labyrinth environment is welcome, leaning heavily on it to demonstrate the method's effectiveness is problematic. As the benchmark is new and unpublished, the community has no established reference point for performance, making the impressive scores difficult to contextualize. Key ablation experiments, such as the comparison to reconstruction and prediction, should have been conducted on the well-understood Atari suite to provide more convincing and verifiable evidence.

**Additional Feedback and Questions for the Authors**

The paper is clearly written and the proposed ideas are creative. The following points are intended to help strengthen the work for a future submission.

*   **Architectural Complexity:** The final agent is a "kitchen sink" of recent RL ideas. A more focused study on a smaller subset of these contributions, with more extensive ablations, would likely be a stronger scientific contribution. For instance, a thorough investigation into combining on-policy policy updates with off-policy value function replay from a buffer would be valuable on its own.
*   **Justification for Control Tasks:** To make the pixel control task more compelling, could you connect it more formally to principles of intrinsic motivation, such as empowerment or curiosity? As it stands, it feels arbitrary. The paper dismisses learning environment models too quickly; a more direct comparison or discussion of how pixel control relates to model learning would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Robustness:** The paper claims improved robustness to hyperparameters, but also introduces several new weighting parameters (λVR, λPC, λRP) that must be tuned. The λPC parameter in particular was sampled from a wide range, suggesting it requires careful tuning for different domains (Labyrinth vs. Atari). This seems to contradict the claim of increased robustness. Could you clarify how the overall tuning effort of UNREAL compares to that of A3C?

**Questions to be addressed:**

1.  Could you provide an ablation study on the Atari benchmark that isolates the performance of A3C augmented only with Value Replay (A3C+VR)? This is critical to understanding how much of the gain is attributable to the more established technique of experience replay versus your novel auxiliary tasks.
2.  The pixel control objective seems suited for action-packed games. Have you considered environments where this objective would be detrimental? For example, a game requiring stealth or minimal interaction with the environment. A discussion of the limitations or failure modes of this auxiliary task would strengthen the paper.
3.  The paper reports a 10x speedup in terms of "number of steps." Given the significant computational overhead of the deconvolutional heads for pixel control, the replay buffer, and multiple loss calculations, what is the approximate speedup (or slowdown) in terms of wall-clock time required to reach A3C's final performance?
