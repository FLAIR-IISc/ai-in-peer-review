This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control tasks (pixel changes and network features) and a reward prediction task to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to learn a better representation by incentivizing the agent to control various aspects of its environment and predict reward, ultimately leading to faster learning and more stable performance in both 3D (Labyrinth) and 2D (Atari) environments.

**Reject.** While the idea of leveraging auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning in RL is not novel, the paper lacks sufficient justification for the specific choices made and, critically, doesn’t provide a convincingly rigorous comparison to well-established baselines beyond A3C. The substantial performance gains reported feel inflated given the relative simplicity of the proposed additions, and the experiments, while extensive, don’t fully isolate the contributions of each component.

The primary concern lies with the limited benchmarking. The paper primarily compares against A3C, a competent but not state-of-the-art algorithm, particularly in the Atari domain. The absence of comparison to algorithms like distributional DQN, Rainbow, or even more recent advancements, makes it difficult to assess the true significance of the proposed UNREAL architecture. Asserting superiority over "current state-of-the-art" without including them in the evaluation is misleading. The claim of improved robustness is also not sufficiently supported; a more thorough hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, perhaps using automated tuning methods, would be more convincing. 

Furthermore, the justification for *how* the auxiliary tasks lead to improved performance remains somewhat superficial. While the authors acknowledge the benefit of learning environmental dynamics, the precise mechanism by which pixel control and network feature control contribute to better overall performance isn’t clearly articulated. The ablation studies are helpful, but the improvement from the pixel control task feels surprisingly large, suggesting a potential issue with hyperparameter optimization or implementation details across the different variants. The argument for using reward prediction as a shaping mechanism, while reasonable, is also not thoroughly validated; demonstrating that it truly avoids biasing the primary policy would require more targeted experiments.

The description of the experimental setup is reasonably detailed, but lacks clarity on the computational resources used. Training deep RL agents can be very resource-intensive, and information about hardware and training time would be valuable for reproducibility and assessing the practicality of the approach. The extensive appendix is appreciated, but serves to highlight the sheer number of experimental details that weren't included in the main body of the paper, making it harder to get a concise understanding of the methodology.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Stronger Baselines:** Include comparisons to more recent and competitive RL algorithms, especially on Atari.
*   **Ablation Study Refinement:** Perform more targeted ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task and ensure fair comparisons (e.g., tuning hyperparameters separately for each variant).
*   **Mechanism Clarification:** Provide a more detailed explanation of *why* the auxiliary tasks are effective. Visualizations of the learned features or activations could be helpful.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Conduct a more robust hyperparameter search, potentially using automated methods like Bayesian optimization.
*   **Computational Details:** Report the hardware and training time used for each experiment.
*   **Statistical Significance:**  Report statistical significance tests for the performance differences observed.
*  **Explain the use of deconvolutional layers** Why are these used? What do they do? Why are they appropriate in this context?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   What specific architectural choices related to the deconvolutional layers were made, and why were they chosen?
*   Were any attempts made to tune the weighting factors (λVR, λPC, λRP) using automated hyperparameter optimization techniques? If so, what were the results?
*   Can you provide more insights into the features learned by the auxiliary tasks (e.g., visualizations of the pixel control activations)?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of the replay buffer size and the sampling strategy?
