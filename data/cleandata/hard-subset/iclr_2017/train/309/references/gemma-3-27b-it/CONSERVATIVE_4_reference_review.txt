This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that augments a standard A3C architecture with auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) and a reward prediction task. The core idea is to learn a more robust and efficient representation by forcing the agent to solve these additional, related objectives during training. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements in both data efficiency and final performance on the Labyrinth 3D environment and on a suite of Atari games.

**Reject.** While the experimental results are promising, the paper presents a rather complex system without sufficiently justifying the design choices or thoroughly establishing the contribution beyond a substantial engineering effort. The improvement is attributed to the *combination* of auxiliary tasks, but the individual contributions and interplay between these tasks aren’t clearly elucidated. The reliance on empirical results, without a deeper theoretical understanding of *why* these auxiliary tasks work, leaves me unconvinced.

The primary concern is the lack of rigorous justification for the chosen auxiliary tasks. The authors state the motivation for pixel and feature control, and reward prediction, but fail to convincingly demonstrate *why* these specific tasks are beneficial, and why they are better than other possible choices. Simply showing that they work well empirically isn’t sufficient.  The choice of pixel control, for example, feels somewhat arbitrary. Why focus on changing pixels rather than, say, predicting the next frame? The ablation study is helpful, but doesn’t fully disentangle the effects of each component. The claim of improved robustness to hyperparameters is interesting, but the analysis provided (Figures 7 & 8) lacks detail. A more comprehensive hyperparameter search, with clear visualizations of the performance landscape, would be needed to substantiate this claim.

The connection to existing work is adequate, but leans towards simply citing related areas rather than demonstrating a clear advancement *built upon* previous research. The work builds on UVFAs and successor representations, but doesn't clearly articulate how UNREAL improves or diverges from these approaches. It is not clear how UNREAL avoids the pitfalls that have plagued these earlier methods.  The authors claim to avoid biasing the policy with the reward prediction task, but don't explain in sufficient detail how the specific training procedure (skewed sampling, separate architecture) achieves this.

Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):

*   **Theoretical Analysis:** Providing some theoretical justification for the effectiveness of the auxiliary tasks would significantly strengthen the paper. Even a simple analysis of how these tasks influence the learned representation could be valuable.
*   **Ablation Study Expansion:**  A more detailed ablation study is needed. For example, testing different combinations of auxiliary tasks, varying the weighting of each loss component (λ values), and examining the effect of different architectures for the auxiliary tasks.
*   **Representation Visualization:** Visualizing the learned representations (e.g., activations of hidden layers) could provide insights into how the auxiliary tasks influence the agent’s internal state.
*   **Comparison to Baselines:** A more comprehensive comparison to stronger baselines, particularly those incorporating recent advances in reinforcement learning, is needed.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Show the full hyperparameter search space and the results of each configuration tested for the robustness claim.
*   **Computational Cost:** The computational overhead of adding these auxiliary tasks should be quantified.

Questions for the Authors:

*   What is the theoretical justification for choosing to control pixel changes specifically, rather than other visual properties?
*   How does the choice of the skewed sampling ratio (0.5 for reward prediction) affect performance, and how was this value determined?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of how the separate architecture for reward prediction prevents biasing the main policy?
*   What is the computational overhead of adding the auxiliary tasks compared to a standard A3C agent?
*   Have you explored other auxiliary tasks and, if so, why were these particular tasks selected?
