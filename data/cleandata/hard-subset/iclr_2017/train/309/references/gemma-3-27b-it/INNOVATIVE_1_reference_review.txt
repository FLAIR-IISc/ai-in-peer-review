This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that augments a standard A3C architecture with auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) and a reward prediction task. The core idea is to learn a richer representation by simultaneously optimizing for multiple, diverse objectives, leading to faster learning, improved performance, and enhanced robustness. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements on both Labyrinth (a complex 3D environment) and Atari games compared to a baseline A3C agent, and show that UNREAL is less sensitive to hyperparameter choices.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are promising, the paper feels more like a successful engineering effort than a fundamentally novel theoretical contribution. The core concept – using auxiliary tasks for representation learning – is well-established, and the specific auxiliary tasks chosen feel somewhat ad-hoc, lacking a strong theoretical justification beyond “they seem to work.” The improvements are significant, but don’t quite reach the level of a breakthrough that would warrant acceptance despite minor methodological shortcomings.

The strongest supporting argument for rejection is the lack of deep analysis regarding *why* UNREAL works so well. The authors demonstrate *that* it works, but provide limited insight into *how* the auxiliary tasks contribute to better representation learning. While ablation studies are included, they don’t clearly delineate the relative importance of each auxiliary task or shed light on the interplay between them. The comparison to other auxiliary task approaches (pixel reconstruction vs. pixel control) is a step in the right direction, but further investigation into the learned representations would be beneficial. The discussion of the robustness to hyperparameters is welcome, but could be strengthened with a more quantitative analysis (e.g., variance of performance across hyperparameter settings).

Further supporting arguments:

* **Limited Novelty:** The paper builds on a well-trodden area of research (auxiliary tasks in RL). While combining existing techniques can be valuable, the contribution feels incremental.
* **Ad-hoc Task Selection:** The choice of pixel and feature control as auxiliary tasks seems somewhat arbitrary. The paper doesn't provide a compelling rationale for why these tasks are particularly effective, beyond empirical observation.
* **Lack of Representation Analysis:** The paper lacks a detailed analysis of the learned representations. Visualizing the activations of different layers or quantifying the information content of the representations would provide valuable insights.
* **Computational Cost:** The increased complexity of UNREAL (multiple auxiliary tasks, additional loss terms) likely comes with a significant computational cost. This trade-off isn't adequately discussed.

Additional feedback (for potential improvement, should the authors wish to resubmit):

* **Theoretical Grounding:** Explore potential theoretical connections between the chosen auxiliary tasks and desirable properties of the learned representations (e.g., disentanglement, invariance).
* **Representation Visualization:** Include visualizations of the learned representations (e.g., t-SNE plots of feature activations) to demonstrate the effect of the auxiliary tasks.
* **Ablation Study Analysis:** Provide a more in-depth analysis of the ablation study results, focusing on the relative contributions of each auxiliary task.
* **Computational Complexity:** Discuss the computational cost of UNREAL compared to A3C.
* **Generalization:** Explore the generalization capabilities of the learned representations. Can they be transferred to new tasks or environments?

Questions for the authors:

* What is the intuition behind choosing pixel and feature control as auxiliary tasks, specifically? Were other potential auxiliary tasks considered and rejected, and if so, why?
* Can you provide any insights into *how* the auxiliary tasks contribute to better representation learning? Are there any specific patterns or features that emerge in the learned representations as a result of the auxiliary tasks?
* How does the performance of UNREAL scale with the number of auxiliary tasks? Is there a point of diminishing returns or even negative interference?
* What is the computational overhead of UNREAL compared to A3C? How does this overhead affect the practical applicability of the approach?
* Did you observe any specific scenarios where UNREAL failed to outperform A3C, and if so, what were the characteristics of those scenarios?
