This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) and reward prediction to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to train the agent not only to maximize the primary reward but also to control internal representations (pixels, network activations) and predict immediate rewards, shaping a better feature space for learning. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over A3C on both the Labyrinth 3D environment and a suite of Atari games, showing both faster learning and higher final performance, alongside increased robustness to hyperparameter choices.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are promising, and the overall idea is intriguing, the paper feels more like a successful engineering effort showcasing a powerful combination of existing techniques than a truly novel theoretical contribution. The improvements, while substantial, feel incremental given the complexity added, and lack a strong theoretical justification for *why* these auxiliary tasks work so well together. 

The core strength of the paper lies in the strong empirical performance. The Labyrinth results are particularly impressive, demonstrating a clear advantage over A3C. The ablation studies help to understand the contribution of each component, however, the description of *how* these components interact to produce such gains remains somewhat superficial. The Atari results are positive, but less striking, and don’t offer the same level of insightful analysis as the Labyrinth experiments. 

The paper is well-written and clearly organized. The related work section is comprehensive and accurately positions the work within the existing literature. However, the motivation behind choosing specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) feels somewhat ad-hoc. While the authors provide some rationale, it lacks a deeper theoretical grounding. The choice of these tasks seems driven by their feasibility and ease of implementation rather than a principled derivation from reinforcement learning theory. Further, the method requires significant hyperparameter tuning, which is acknowledged, but the robustness gains achieved don’t fully compensate for this added complexity. The impact of the weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) seems critical, and a more systematic investigation of their influence would strengthen the paper. Finally, the claim of "more than doubling the previous state-of-the-art results" on Labyrinth feels overstated - while the improvement *is* substantial, simply quoting the ratio of final scores glosses over the initial performance difference.

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Theoretical Justification:**  The paper would greatly benefit from a more in-depth discussion of *why* these auxiliary tasks should improve learning. Is there a connection to information bottleneck principles? Can the auxiliary tasks be framed as regularization terms that promote desirable properties in the learned representation?
*   **Ablation Study Details:**  Provide more detail on the hyperparameter tuning process for each ablated variant. It's important to ensure that the performance differences are not simply due to different levels of optimization.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** A more thorough analysis of the sensitivity of the performance to the weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) is required. Perhaps a sensitivity analysis or a method for automatically tuning these weights.
*   **Computational Cost:**  Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks.  Is the improvement in data efficiency offset by increased computational cost?
*   **Generalization:** Explore the generalization ability of the learned representations. Can the agent transfer its learned skills to new levels or tasks in Labyrinth?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the theoretical motivation behind choosing to control pixel changes and network features specifically? Are there other auxiliary tasks that you considered and rejected, and if so, why?
2.  Can you provide more details on the process of hyperparameter tuning, particularly for the weighting terms? How did you ensure that the ablation studies were fair and that the different variants were optimized to a comparable degree?
3.  You mention that the auxiliary tasks shape the features of the agent. Can you provide a visualization or quantitative analysis of the learned features before and after incorporating the auxiliary tasks?  Are there specific features that are consistently improved?
4.  The reward prediction task is trained on a skewed distribution of samples. What is the impact of this skew on the learned representation, and how does it affect the agent’s policy?
5.  How does the computational cost of UNREAL compare to A3C? Is the improvement in data efficiency sufficient to offset the increased computational burden?
