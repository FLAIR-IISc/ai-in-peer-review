This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to learn useful representations by simultaneously maximizing performance on multiple tasks – the primary RL task and several carefully designed auxiliary tasks related to controlling pixels and network features, as well as predicting immediate rewards. The results demonstrate significant improvements over A3C on both the Labyrinth 3D environment and a suite of Atari games, particularly in terms of sample efficiency and hyperparameter robustness.

**Reject**

The paper presents a substantial amount of experimental work and achieves impressive results. However, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and detail in certain areas, particularly regarding the design and justification of specific architectural choices and hyperparameters. While the broad idea is sound, a more rigorous and transparent description of the methodology is needed to convince the reviewer of the novelty and generalizability of the proposed approach.

The main arguments supporting the rejection are:

1. **Lack of Ablation Detail & Justification:** While the paper presents ablation studies, the explanation of *why* specific components contribute to performance is insufficient. For example, while pixel control improves performance, the paper doesn't deeply analyze *what* aspects of pixel control are most impactful or how they differ from, say, input reconstruction (which is shown to be detrimental). Similarly, the choice of the feedforward network for reward prediction, separate from the LSTM, lacks a detailed rationale.
2. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity and Search Space:** The paper claims improved robustness to hyperparameters. However, the description of the hyperparameter search space and the methods used to find optimal values are vague. The claim of robustness needs to be supported by a more thorough analysis demonstrating consistent performance across a wider range of hyperparameter settings, and a clear articulation of how UNREAL reduces the need for extensive tuning.

Supporting Arguments:

The paper is well-motivated, building upon existing work in temporal abstraction, auxiliary tasks, and representation learning. The connection to prior art is clearly established. The experimental setup seems rigorous, involving multiple levels in Labyrinth and a comprehensive evaluation on Atari. The reported improvements in sample efficiency and performance are substantial.

However, several areas require further clarification. The details of the deconvolutional network used for pixel control are mentioned but lack a compelling justification for the specific architecture. The weighting of the different loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP) is treated as a hyperparameter without a strong theoretical or empirical basis for the chosen ranges. The success on Labyrinth is encouraging, but the Atari results warrant more in-depth analysis. The paper mentions that Atari games differ in pixel intensities which influences λPC, but lacks a clear explanation of how this was accounted for. The description of how hyperparameters were tuned is insufficient. A table or figure showing the range of hyperparameters tested and the resulting performance variations would be highly valuable. The raw Atari scores (mentioned in section C) would allow a more detailed analysis of the results.

Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):

*   **Clarify the Design Choices:** Provide a more detailed justification for specific architectural choices, such as the use of a separate feedforward network for reward prediction and the structure of the deconvolutional network for pixel control.
*   **Expand the Ablation Studies:** Conduct more in-depth ablation studies to isolate the contributions of each auxiliary task and identify the key components driving the performance improvements. Analyze *why* certain tasks are more beneficial than others.
*   **Provide a Detailed Hyperparameter Search Analysis:** Clearly define the hyperparameter search space, the search method used, and the resulting performance distributions. Quantify the robustness of UNREAL to hyperparameter variations.
*   **Provide more insights on Labyrinth levels:** Include a more detailed analysis of the performance on individual Labyrinth levels, explaining why UNREAL performs better or worse on certain levels.
*   **Provide the raw Atari scores:** Allow for a closer examination of performance across the games.
*   **Discuss limitations**: What types of environments might UNREAL struggle in? Are there potential drawbacks to using auxiliary tasks?

Questions for the Authors:

1.  Regarding the feedforward network for reward prediction: Why was a separate network chosen instead of integrating the reward prediction task into the LSTM? What is the trade-off between these approaches?
2.  What are the computational costs associated with the auxiliary tasks? Does adding these tasks significantly increase the training time or memory requirements?
3.  Can you provide more details on the hyperparameter search process for both Labyrinth and Atari? What methods were used, and what was the range of values explored for each hyperparameter?
4.  You mention that the weight (λPC) for pixel control differs across Atari games. Can you elaborate on how this difference was quantified and addressed during the experiments?
5.  What are the potential limitations of the UNREAL approach, and in what types of environments might it be less effective?
