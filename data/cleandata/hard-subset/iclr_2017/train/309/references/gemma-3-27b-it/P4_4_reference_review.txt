The paper proposes a reinforcement learning agent, UNREAL, that combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel changes and network feature activations) with reward prediction to improve learning efficiency and robustness in both 3D (Labyrinth) and 2D (Atari) environments. The authors leverage A3C as a base agent and augment it with additional losses related to these auxiliary tasks, optimizing a combined loss function. The core idea is to learn a richer representation by encouraging the agent to explore and control various aspects of the environment, even beyond maximizing the primary reward.

**Reject** with a strong encouragement to revise and resubmit. While the results demonstrate significant improvements in certain scenarios, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and rigorous justification for some design choices, hindering a full assessment of the contribution. The jump in performance is significant, but the paper doesn't thoroughly convince *why* these auxiliary tasks are so effective beyond stating that they lead to richer representations.

The approach is well-motivated in the context of representation learning in RL, building on prior work in temporal abstraction, auxiliary tasks, and successor representations. The authors correctly position their work relative to existing literature, acknowledging related architectures like Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. The use of A3C as a backbone provides a strong baseline and allows for a relatively straightforward integration of the auxiliary tasks. The experimental setup, including both Labyrinth and Atari environments, is also a positive aspect, showcasing the generality of the approach. However, the detailed ablation studies, while present, aren't comprehensive enough to disentangle the contributions of each auxiliary task completely.

The paper supports its claims with empirical results. The substantial performance gains in Labyrinth, particularly the improved robustness to hyperparameter settings, are noteworthy. The Atari results are also promising, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. However, the presentation of these results could be improved. For instance, the scatter plots of performance vs. hyperparameters (Figure 8) are difficult to interpret without clear markers for each hyperparameter setting. More importantly, the ablation study in Labyrinth (Figure 3) doesn't provide statistical significance tests. The claim of "substantial improvement" needs to be backed by statistical validation. Additionally, the rationale behind the specific choices for the auxiliary tasks—pixel control and feature control—isn’t fully explained. Why these specifically, and why not others? The choice of using a simpler feedforward network for reward prediction, while justified, needs more elaboration. The benefit of *not* using the reward predictor for value estimation is mentioned, but a deeper discussion of potential drawbacks or alternative approaches would strengthen the argument. The lack of error bars or standard deviations on the learning curves also diminishes the rigor of the evaluation.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarity:** The description of the deconvolutional network used for auxiliary value generation could be more detailed. A visual diagram illustrating the network architecture would be beneficial.
*   **Ablation Study:** Conduct a more thorough ablation study to understand the individual and combined effects of each auxiliary task. Consider removing the reward prediction task, the pixel control task, and the feature control task individually and in combination. Report statistical significance tests for all comparisons.
*   **Justification of Auxiliary Tasks:** Provide a more compelling argument for why pixel control and feature control were selected. Explore other potential auxiliary tasks and discuss why they were not considered.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Investigate the sensitivity of the UNREAL agent to the weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) and provide guidelines for setting these parameters.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks.
*   **Reward Prediction:** Clarify the choice of a multi-class cross-entropy loss for reward prediction. Compare its performance to a mean-squared error loss.
*   **Explain Figure 5 better:** What does “learned on” mean in figure 5’s caption? It’s unclear what the baselines are learning and what “UNREAL” is learning.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a statistical analysis (e.g., t-tests, ANOVA) to demonstrate the significance of the performance improvements reported in Figures 3 and 4?
2.  What other auxiliary tasks were considered, and why were pixel control and feature control ultimately selected?
3.  How sensitive is the performance of UNREAL to the weighting terms λVR, λPC, and λRP? Can you provide guidelines for setting these parameters?
4.  What is the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks compared to the baseline A3C agent?
5.  Could you elaborate on the reasoning behind using a separate, simpler network for reward prediction, rather than integrating it directly into the main A3C network?
6.  What impact does the replay buffer size have on the performance of the algorithm?
7.  Figure 5: Please clarify what the different baselines are learning. What is UNREAL learning in that experiment? What does “learned on” mean?
