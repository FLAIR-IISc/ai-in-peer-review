## Review of "AUXILIARY TASKS FOR REINFORCEMENT LEARNING"

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes the UNREAL algorithm, a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning that combines A3C with auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction. The core idea is to learn a more robust and efficient representation by simultaneously optimizing for multiple objectives – the primary task reward and a set of auxiliary tasks designed to encourage exploration, feature learning (pixel changes and network activations), and reward anticipation.  The authors demonstrate significantly improved performance and data efficiency on both the Labyrinth 3D game and a suite of Atari games compared to standard A3C and other baseline methods. The paper provides a clear articulation of the motivation, a detailed algorithmic description, and comprehensive experimental results.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a compelling and well-supported contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The performance gains observed on both complex 3D environments and the Atari benchmark are substantial and demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The thorough experimental evaluation and ablation studies further strengthen the paper's claims.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, showing more than a doubling of performance compared to A3C, alongside a significant speedup in learning. Achieving human-level performance on challenging levels (like lt hallway slope) is a notable achievement. The Atari results also demonstrate a clear advantage over existing methods.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The motivation for using auxiliary tasks is clearly articulated and grounded in relevant literature (options, UVFA, successor representations, etc.).  The authors correctly identify the need for more efficient and robust representations in complex RL environments.
* **Comprehensive Evaluation:** The ablation studies systematically investigate the contribution of each auxiliary task, providing valuable insights into their individual and combined effects. The comparison to related baselines (pixel reconstruction, input change prediction) further solidifies the benefits of the proposed control-based approach.
* **Clear and Detailed Description:** The paper provides a detailed explanation of the UNREAL algorithm, including the architecture, loss functions, and training procedures. The implementation details are also sufficiently described to facilitate replication.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The presented analysis demonstrating increased robustness to hyperparameters is a valuable addition, highlighting the practical advantages of the proposed approach.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper demonstrates increased robustness, a more detailed discussion of the specific hyperparameters that significantly impact performance, and their optimal settings across different environments, would be beneficial.
* **Computational Cost:** A brief discussion of the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks (e.g., increased training time, memory requirements) would provide a more complete picture of the trade-offs involved.
* **Visualization of Learned Features:**  While the paper mentions learning useful features, providing some visualizations of the learned features (e.g., activations of hidden units, pixel control outputs) could offer further intuition into the effectiveness of the auxiliary tasks.
* **Further Exploration of Feature Control:** The results on feature control are promising. Exploring different hidden layers and the selection of features for control could lead to even greater improvements.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  The paper mentions that the weighting term λPC (for pixel control) needs to be fitted for Atari games due to varying pixel intensities. Could you elaborate on the method used for fitting this parameter and whether it could be automated or learned during training?
2.  You note that simply predicting input changes yielded worse results than learning to *control* pixel changes. Do you have a hypothesis as to why predicting changes, while informative, doesn't contribute to learning as effectively as active control?
3.   Regarding the choice of LSTM for A3C, did you experiment with other recurrent architectures (e.g., GRU) and if so, what were the results?
4.  The paper primarily focuses on value-based auxiliary tasks (Q-learning). Have you considered exploring policy gradient-based approaches for optimizing the auxiliary tasks? Could a combination of value and policy-based methods be advantageous?
