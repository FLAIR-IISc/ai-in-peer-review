The paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction. The core idea is to improve representation learning by adding these auxiliary objectives, leading to faster training, more robust learning, and higher performance. Specifically, the agent learns to control pixel changes, network features, and predict immediate rewards. The authors demonstrate significant improvements on both the Labyrinth and Atari benchmark environments compared to A3C and other baselines.

**Reject**

The paper presents a compelling idea and shows promising empirical results. However, the presentation lacks sufficient clarity and justification for several key design choices, hindering a full assessment of the technical soundness and novelty of the approach. While the results are strong, the lack of detailed ablation studies and analysis makes it difficult to disentangle the contributions of each auxiliary task and fully understand *why* UNREAL performs so well.

The primary concerns are related to the lack of a rigorous justification for the specific auxiliary tasks chosen and their implementation details. For example, the motivation for choosing pixel changes and specific network features is not thoroughly explained. How were the specific layers chosen for feature control? Why 8x8 or 4x4 cells for pixel control?  Further, the benefits of the specific deconvolutional network architecture used for Qaux aren't adequately explained. The paper also describes using a skewed sampling strategy for reward prediction based on a 50/50 split of zero/non-zero rewards. The rationale behind this specific ratio isn't clear and feels somewhat arbitrary. 

The experiments, while extensive, could be strengthened. While the paper shows that adding *any* auxiliary task improves performance, it would be useful to understand the interactions between them. For instance, is the combination of pixel control *and* reward prediction significantly better than pixel control alone? The paper touches upon this in the Labyrinth results, but a more systematic ablation study across both Labyrinth and Atari would be valuable. Additionally, the claim of improved robustness to hyperparameters is demonstrated but the figures provided don’t offer much insight. Showing a scatter plot of performance vs. each hyperparameter, conditioned on auxiliary tasks, would improve interpretability. Finally, the Atari results are presented without per-game breakdowns making it difficult to assess performance on individual games.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify the motivation:** Provide a more detailed theoretical justification for the choice of auxiliary tasks. Why are pixel changes and network features expected to be beneficial for representation learning in the context of reinforcement learning?
*   **Detailed Ablation Studies:** Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task. What is the performance impact of removing each task individually and in combination?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis:** Provide more detailed visualizations of the hyperparameter sensitivity analysis. Scatter plots showing performance as a function of each hyperparameter, conditioned on auxiliary task configurations, would be helpful.
*   **Atari Game-Specific Results:** Include a table or figure showing the performance of UNREAL on individual Atari games.
*   **Computational Cost:** Briefly discuss the increased computational cost associated with training UNREAL compared to A3C.
*   **Replay Buffer Details:**  How is the replay buffer split when skewing towards rewarding events? Is this based on the immediate reward or the n-step return?
*   **Deconvolutional Network Justification:** Explain the rationale behind choosing the specific deconvolutional network architecture used to generate Qaux.
*  **Explain the choice of n-step return:** Why was an n-step return of 20 chosen?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you elaborate on the theoretical reasoning behind choosing pixel changes and specific network features as auxiliary tasks? What properties of these tasks make them particularly well-suited for improving representation learning in RL?
2.  Could you provide a more detailed analysis of the interaction between the different auxiliary tasks? Is the combination of all three tasks essential for achieving the reported performance, or are some tasks more important than others?
3.  What is the computational overhead of training UNREAL compared to A3C, and how does this overhead scale with the complexity of the environment?
4.  How sensitive is the performance of UNREAL to the weighting parameters (λVR, λPC, λRP) used to combine the different loss components?
5.  You mention using a 50/50 split of zero/non-zero rewards for the reward prediction task. How did you determine this specific ratio, and what impact does it have on performance?
