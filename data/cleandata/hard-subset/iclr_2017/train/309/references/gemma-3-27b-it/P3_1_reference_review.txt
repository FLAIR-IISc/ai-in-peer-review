## ICLR 2024 Review: UNREAL - Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes the UNREAL agent, a novel reinforcement learning architecture that leverages auxiliary tasks – pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction – to enhance learning speed, robustness, and performance. The authors argue that these auxiliary tasks promote a more effective representation, and demonstrate significant improvements over a baseline A3C agent on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games. The core idea is to combine on-policy learning (A3C) with off-policy learning on auxiliary tasks using experience replay, fostering both exploration and efficient feature learning. The results are impressive, demonstrating a substantial boost in sample efficiency and consistently higher scores.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept**. This is a strong paper with compelling results. The combination of auxiliary tasks, particularly the cleverly designed pixel and feature control, demonstrably improves performance and robustness in challenging reinforcement learning environments. The experimental validation is thorough, and the improvements observed are significant. 

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The work builds upon existing research in temporal abstraction and auxiliary task learning but distinguishes itself by integrating *multiple* auxiliary tasks, alongside reward prediction, into a unified framework. The motivation for using auxiliary tasks to learn better representations is well-established, and the specific choices made (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) are well-justified.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on Labyrinth are particularly convincing. The substantial speedup in learning (10x faster to reach A3C best performance) and the significant performance improvement (over 2x final score) are remarkable. The Atari results, while less dramatic in relative terms, still demonstrate consistent improvement and robustness.  The extensive hyperparameter sweeps and reporting strengthens the findings.
* **Sound Methodology:** The architecture and training procedure are clearly described and appear well-designed. The use of experience replay to facilitate off-policy learning on auxiliary tasks is a sensible choice, and the combination with on-policy A3C appears to be a good balance. The ablation studies (comparing UNREAL to A3C and variants with different auxiliary tasks removed) provide crucial insights into the contribution of each component.
* **Comprehensive Related Work:** The paper provides a good overview of relevant literature, covering temporal abstraction, auxiliary tasks, and representation learning. This situates the work effectively within the broader field.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Clarify the interplay between tasks:** While the paper describes *what* auxiliary tasks are used, it could benefit from a more detailed explanation of *how* they interact during learning. Are there any observed synergies or conflicts between the different tasks? A visualization of the learned features or policies for each auxiliary task would be insightful.
* **Discussion of computational cost:** The UNREAL agent introduces additional complexity. A discussion of the computational cost associated with training and running the agent (e.g., memory requirements, training time) would be helpful, especially in comparison to the baseline A3C agent.
* **Further analysis of reward prediction:**  The reward prediction auxiliary task seems particularly important.  A more detailed analysis of *what* features the reward predictor is learning, and how these features contribute to the overall performance, would be valuable. Show examples of what the reward predictor focuses on during gameplay.
* **Generalization to other environments:** While the Labyrinth and Atari results are strong, exploring the performance of UNREAL on a wider range of environments (e.g., MuJoCo, other 3D game platforms) could further strengthen the claims of generalizability.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* **Regarding the pixel control task:** Why specifically an 80x80 crop and a 20x20 grid? Was this determined empirically, or is there a theoretical justification for these parameters?
* **Regarding feature control:**  Which hidden layer was chosen for feature control and why? Was this layer chosen based on any specific properties or representational characteristics?
* **Regarding the weighting of losses (λVR, λPC, λRP):** How were these weights selected?  Did you perform a systematic hyperparameter search over these values, or were they chosen based on intuition?  Could you provide more details on the sensitivity of the results to these weights?
* **Regarding robustness:** You mention increased robustness to hyperparameters. Could you quantify this robustness more precisely, e.g., by measuring the variance in performance across different hyperparameter settings?
* **Could you explain more about why reconstructing the input led to *worse* performance in the unsupervised comparison?** This counterintuitive result deserves further investigation and explanation.



In conclusion, this is a high-quality paper that presents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The UNREAL agent is a promising approach for improving the efficiency and robustness of RL agents, and the authors have provided compelling evidence to support their claims. I strongly recommend acceptance.
