This paper proposes UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that leverages auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction to improve learning efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to augment the standard A3C agent with additional objectives – controlling pixels and network features, as well as predicting immediate rewards – all learned concurrently with the primary task. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in both the Labyrinth and Atari environments, showcasing faster learning, higher final performance, and greater robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**Decision: Reject**

Despite the promising results and clear presentation, I believe the paper falls short of the ICLR standard primarily due to a lack of rigorous statistical analysis and a somewhat limited exploration of the design choices. While performance gains are reported, the significance of these gains isn’t definitively established, and the ablation studies, while present, don’t fully justify all the components of the UNREAL architecture.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Statistical Significance:** The reported performance improvements, especially in Atari, are presented as average scores. However, the variance across different hyperparameter settings is not fully explored.  While Figure 8 shows scatter plots, a more formal statistical test (e.g., t-test or ANOVA) comparing the performance of UNREAL and A3C across multiple random seeds and hyperparameter settings is crucial to establish whether the observed improvements are statistically significant. The claim of "significantly improved" performance needs to be backed by statistical evidence.

2. **Ablation Study Depth:** While the authors perform ablation studies, the rationale behind the specific choices of ablations isn't always clear. For example, why are specific combinations of auxiliary tasks tested, and not others?  Furthermore, the ablation studies don’t fully dissect the contribution of each component. The performance gains from reward prediction, while noted, are not explicitly quantified in isolation.  The interplay between different auxiliary tasks is also not thoroughly investigated. It would be helpful to understand if certain auxiliary tasks are complementary or redundant.

3. **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** The authors mention improved robustness to hyperparameters. However, the hyperparameter search space isn't fully detailed. Knowing the bounds of the search space and the number of samples would provide better context to the robustness claim. A sensitivity analysis could also pinpoint the hyperparameters that most significantly impact performance.

4. **Limited Theoretical Justification:** While the motivation for using auxiliary tasks is reasonable (improving representation learning), the paper lacks a strong theoretical framework explaining *why* these specific tasks are effective.  A more in-depth discussion on the information-theoretic benefits of controlling pixels or network features could strengthen the contribution.



**Additional Feedback (For Improvement):**

*   **Clarify the connection to prior work:** The related work section is a bit descriptive but lacks a critical analysis of how UNREAL builds upon or diverges from existing approaches. Specifically, the relationship to related work on intrinsic motivation and curiosity-driven exploration should be discussed.
*   **Provide more details on the Labyrinth environments:** The description of the Labyrinth levels is somewhat vague. A clearer explanation of the challenges posed by each level would help readers understand the significance of the results.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Are there any trade-offs between performance gains and computational complexity?
*   **Visualization of Learned Features:** Providing visual examples of the features learned by the agent (e.g., activations of hidden layers or the output of the reward prediction network) could provide further insight into the mechanism by which UNREAL achieves its improvements.
*   **Generalization to other environments:** While the Atari results are encouraging, it would be beneficial to evaluate UNREAL on a wider range of environments, including those with different state and action spaces.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide a statistical analysis (e.g., t-test) to demonstrate the statistical significance of the performance improvements reported in the Atari experiments?
*   What is the rationale behind choosing pixels and network features as auxiliary control tasks? Were other potential tasks considered, and why were they not selected?
*   Could you elaborate on the interplay between the different auxiliary tasks? Are there any scenarios where certain tasks are more or less beneficial than others?
*   How does the computational cost of UNREAL compare to that of a standard A3C agent?
*   Have you explored alternative methods for combining the losses from the different auxiliary tasks (e.g., dynamic weighting schemes)?
