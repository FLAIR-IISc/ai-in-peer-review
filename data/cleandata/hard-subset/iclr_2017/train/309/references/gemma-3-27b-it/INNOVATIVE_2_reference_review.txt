This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that leverages auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to train the agent not only to maximize the primary reward but also to control low-level aspects of the environment (pixel changes, network activations) and predict immediate rewards. This multifaceted learning approach leads to a more effective representation, facilitating faster learning and improved performance across both 3D (Labyrinth) and 2D (Atari) environments.

**Reject.** While the idea is compelling, and the experimental results are promising, the paper feels like a collection of well-executed ideas rather than a truly novel, cohesive contribution. The improvements demonstrated, while substantial, feel incremental given the complexity added to the system. The paper lacks a strong theoretical underpinning to explain *why* these auxiliary tasks are so effective, relying heavily on empirical validation.

The primary concern stems from the lack of a truly unifying principle. The combination of pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction feels somewhat ad-hoc. While each component is justified with references to related work, their synergistic effect isn’t deeply explored theoretically. It's not immediately clear why *these* specific tasks are chosen, and a more principled approach to auxiliary task selection would significantly strengthen the paper. The results on Labyrinth are impressive, but the environment is somewhat bespoke. The Atari results are stronger, but given the already extensive literature on Atari, achieving state-of-the-art performance, while valuable, doesn't necessarily constitute groundbreaking work. The observed robustness to hyperparameters is a positive signal, but isn't enough to compensate for the lack of a deeper conceptual advance. The comparison to reward prediction *versus* simply predicting changes is a good step, but more analysis of *what* is being learned by each auxiliary task would be highly valuable.

**Additional Feedback (for potential improvement):**

*   **Theoretical Justification:** Explore a more theoretical basis for the choice of auxiliary tasks. Can information-theoretic principles (e.g., maximizing mutual information) guide task selection? Is there a connection to intrinsic motivation or curiosity-driven exploration?
*   **Ablation Study Depth:** While ablation studies are present, expanding them to analyze the *interaction* between tasks would be useful. Are some tasks redundant when combined? Do certain tasks benefit more from others? A more granular analysis of the loss weights (λVR, λPC, λRP) and their sensitivity would also be helpful.
*   **Visualization of Learned Representations:** Providing more qualitative insights into the learned representations would be beneficial. Visualize the activations of network layers during different auxiliary tasks. This could reveal whether the tasks are encouraging the agent to learn meaningful features.
*   **Generalization to Other Environments:** Testing the UNREAL agent on a wider range of environments, particularly those with different characteristics (sparse vs. dense rewards, continuous vs. discrete action spaces), would strengthen the generalization claims.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. Is the increased data efficiency enough to offset the added complexity?

**Questions for the authors:**

*   What specific criteria were used to select the pixel control and feature control tasks? Was there any exploration of other potential auxiliary tasks?
*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* you believe reward prediction is particularly beneficial, beyond simply alleviating perceptual sparsity?
*   How sensitive are the results to the choice of hyperparameters (e.g., the weighting terms λVR, λPC, λRP)?
*   Do you observe any negative transfer or interference between the auxiliary tasks?
*   What is the computational cost overhead of adding the auxiliary tasks compared to the baseline A3C agent?
