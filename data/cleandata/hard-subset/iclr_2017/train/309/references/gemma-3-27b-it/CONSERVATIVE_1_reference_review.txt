This paper proposes a reinforcement learning agent, UNREAL, which augments a standard A3C architecture with auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) and a reward prediction task. The core idea is to improve representation learning through these auxiliary objectives, ultimately leading to faster learning, improved performance, and greater robustness to hyperparameter settings. The authors demonstrate improved results on both the Labyrinth 3D environment and a suite of Atari games.

Reject. While the experimental results demonstrate performance gains, the paper presents a rather complex system without sufficient justification for *why* each component contributes to the overall improvement, and relies heavily on empirical validation without a strong theoretical grounding. The plethora of ablated results, while extensive, don’t coalesce into a clear understanding of the mechanisms at play.

The primary concern is the lack of a compelling argument for the chosen auxiliary tasks beyond “they seem to help.” Pixel control, while common, feels somewhat ad-hoc. Feature control, though a more interesting idea, requires a justification for *which* features are beneficial to control. The reward prediction task is presented as a means to alleviate reward sparsity, but its specific impact beyond that remains unclear. The authors claim it biases the feature representation without biasing the policy/value functions, which feels like a delicate balance that isn’t rigorously demonstrated. Simply showing that the combined approach works better than A3C isn't sufficient; it doesn’t isolate the effect of each component. 

The paper is heavily empirical. While the results are positive, the large number of experiments and ablations doesn’t provide a clear and concise explanation of *why* UNREAL succeeds. A more focused analysis—perhaps with ablation studies specifically designed to test hypotheses about the interplay between the auxiliary tasks—would be more convincing. Furthermore, the robustness claim, though supported by figures, feels more like a description of the results than a theoretically motivated outcome. The comparison to other auxiliary task baselines (reconstruction vs. control) is a step in the right direction but feels limited. 

Additional feedback:

*   **Theoretical Justification:** The paper would benefit from a more formal connection to existing theory on representation learning, auxiliary tasks, or intrinsic motivation.
*   **Ablation Study Design:** The ablation studies feel somewhat exhaustive but lack clear hypotheses. Design experiments that specifically test the interaction between the auxiliary tasks. For example, what happens when pixel control *conflicts* with feature control?
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While showing robustness is good, exploring the hyperparameter space more systematically (e.g., using a grid search or Bayesian optimization) could reveal more insights into the sensitivity of the algorithm to different settings.
*   **Computational Cost:** The UNREAL agent introduces significantly more complexity. A discussion of the computational overhead compared to A3C would be valuable.
*   **Code Release:** Releasing the code would be extremely beneficial for reproducibility and allow the community to build upon this work.

Questions for the authors:

1.  Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* you chose pixel control and feature control as auxiliary tasks, beyond just observing that they improved performance? What theoretical or empirical basis guided these choices?
2.  How do you ensure that the reward prediction task doesn’t inadvertently bias the policy or value function, as you claim? Can you provide any evidence supporting this claim?
3.  The paper states that the reward prediction task uses a simpler feedforward network than the main agent. Why was this design choice made, and what is the justification for using different architectures for the main agent and the auxiliary task?
4.  You mention that input reconstruction harmed performance. Can you elaborate on why you believe this occurred, and what implications does this have for the selection of appropriate auxiliary tasks?
5.  Can you provide a more detailed analysis of the learned features in the agent’s representations, and how these features differ between the A3C baseline and the UNREAL agent?
