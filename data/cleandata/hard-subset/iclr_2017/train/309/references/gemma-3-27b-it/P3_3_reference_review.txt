## ICLR 2024 Review: UNREAL - Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents UNREAL, a novel deep reinforcement learning architecture that leverages auxiliary tasks – pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction – to improve data efficiency and robustness. The authors argue that these auxiliary tasks promote better representations, leading to faster learning and more stable performance. They demonstrate significant improvements over standard A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and a suite of Atari games. The core idea is compelling: instead of just focusing on reward maximization, the agent learns useful skills and representations by concurrently tackling related, self-supervised objectives. The results are quite strong, showing both improved final performance and significantly faster learning.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** (with minor revisions). The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, demonstrating a clear and impactful improvement over existing methods. The experimental results are compelling and well-supported. The idea of combining multiple auxiliary tasks in this manner is novel and offers a promising direction for future research.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Novelty and Motivation:** The combination of auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) with reward prediction is a novel approach. The motivation for learning better representations through these tasks is well-articulated and grounded in the existing literature on temporal abstraction, successor representations, and auxiliary prediction.  The authors convincingly argue why these tasks would be beneficial for RL.
* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive.  Outperforming A3C by a significant margin on such a complex 3D environment speaks to the effectiveness of the UNREAL approach.  The Atari results further validate the generalizability of the method.  The reported speedups in learning are a major benefit.
* **Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies systematically demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task, reinforcing the claim that their combined effect is crucial. The comparison to pixel reconstruction and direct pixel change prediction is insightful.
* **Robustness Analysis:** The analysis of robustness to hyperparameters, showing UNREAL’s smoother performance landscape, is a valuable contribution. This suggests the approach is less sensitive to careful tuning, making it more practical.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology. The diagrams (Figure 1) are helpful for understanding the architecture.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Discussion of Computational Cost:** While the paper demonstrates improved *data* efficiency, it would be helpful to briefly discuss the increased *computational* cost of training with these auxiliary tasks. How does the increased complexity impact training time or resource requirements?
* **Reward Prediction Details:** The choice of a three-class reward prediction (zero, positive, negative) seems reasonable, but a brief justification for this choice would be helpful.  Why not a regression task predicting the reward value directly?
* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity Analysis (Expanded):** While the paper demonstrates robustness, presenting the hyperparameter search space and the distributions of hyperparameters used across the successful runs would provide further insight.
* **Qualitative Analysis of Learned Features:** While the quantitative results are strong, some qualitative examples of the features learned by the auxiliary tasks (e.g., visualizing the activation patterns from feature control) would be beneficial.
* **Connection to Intrinsic Motivation:** The work draws parallels to intrinsic motivation, but a more explicit connection to related work in that area could strengthen the framing.
* **Clarify "Human Normalised Scores":** Provide a clearer explanation of how these scores are calculated.

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

* Regarding the pixel control task, did you experiment with different grid sizes (e.g., smaller or larger cells)? How sensitive is performance to this parameter?
* You mention the feature control auxiliary task is “slightly worse” than pixel control on Labyrinth.  Can you elaborate on what differences you observed in the learned representations between these two tasks?
*  The Labyrinth levels are described in terms of categories. Could you provide a table summarizing the average performance across each category for A3C, UNREAL, and the ablated versions? This would provide a more granular understanding of the improvements.
*  In the Atari experiments, were the hyperparameters shared across all games, or tuned individually for each game? If tuned individually, how much effort was involved in this process?
* How much memory is required to store the experiences used for experience replay, and how does this scale with the complexity of the environment?



In conclusion, this is a strong paper with a well-motivated approach, compelling results, and a clear presentation. The suggested revisions are minor and intended to further enhance the paper's clarity and impact.  I recommend acceptance to ICLR 2024.
