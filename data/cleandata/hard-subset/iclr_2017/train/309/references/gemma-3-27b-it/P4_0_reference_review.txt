This paper presents UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to leverage auxiliary tasks – pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction – to learn a richer representation of the environment, thereby accelerating learning and improving performance in both Labyrinth and Atari environments.

**Decision:** Accept. The paper tackles a relevant problem in RL – improving sample efficiency and robustness – and proposes a novel approach that demonstrably improves performance on challenging environments. The results are compelling and the methodology appears sound.

**Supporting Arguments:**

*   **Novelty:** While auxiliary tasks have been used in RL before, the combination presented in UNREAL – specifically the nuanced approach to reward prediction and the integration with A3C and experience replay – appears novel. The paper clearly differentiates itself from existing work like UVFA and successor representations by emphasizing the use of auxiliary tasks *solely* for representation learning, rather than directly for planning or control.
*   **Technical Soundness:** The proposed architecture is clearly described and grounded in established RL principles (A3C, Q-learning). The use of off-policy learning for auxiliary tasks alongside on-policy A3C is a sensible choice. The ablation studies provide strong evidence that each component contributes to the overall improvement. The experimental setup, with a significant number of hyperparameter settings evaluated, strengthens the claim of robustness.
*   **Results:** The results presented are impressive. Achieving more than twice the performance of A3C in Labyrinth and significant improvements across a broad range of Atari games is a strong indicator of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The reported speedup in learning is also a valuable contribution. The inclusion of human-normalized scores adds context and demonstrates the practical significance of the performance gains. The robustness analysis further supports the method’s practicality.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Reward Prediction Details:** The description of the reward prediction task could be more detailed. While the paper mentions skewed sampling, more specifics on the implementation (e.g., the exact sampling ratio, the architecture details) would be beneficial.
*   **Hyperparameter Search:** While the paper mentions a large number of hyperparameter settings were tried, a brief description of the search space and method (e.g., random search, grid search) would be helpful.
*   **Computational Cost:** A discussion of the computational cost associated with UNREAL compared to A3C would be valuable.  The addition of auxiliary tasks likely increases the computational burden, and understanding this trade-off is important.
*   **Ablation Study Depth:** The ablation study demonstrates the contribution of each component, but it would be interesting to see ablations combining multiple components. For example, what is the performance when removing both pixel and feature control, but keeping reward prediction?
*   **Figure clarity:** Some figures (e.g., Figure 4) are difficult to read due to the small font size and dense plotting. Improving figure legibility would aid comprehension.

**Questions for Authors:**

1.  Could you elaborate on the rationale behind using a separate, simpler feedforward network for reward prediction compared to leveraging the LSTM within the A3C agent?
2.  What specific strategies were employed for hyperparameter tuning, and what was the search space for each hyperparameter?
3.  What is the approximate increase in computational cost (e.g., training time, memory usage) associated with UNREAL compared to a baseline A3C agent?
4.  The paper mentions a skew in the replay buffer. Is the skew maintained throughout training, or does it change over time? If it changes, how is it adjusted?
5.  Could you provide further details on the implementation of the deconvolutional network used for pixel control? What are the specific filter sizes, strides, and activation functions used in each layer?
