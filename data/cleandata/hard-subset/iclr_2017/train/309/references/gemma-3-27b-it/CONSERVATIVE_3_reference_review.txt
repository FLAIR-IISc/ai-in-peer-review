This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) with reward prediction to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to learn useful representations by incentivizing the agent to control various aspects of its environment and predict rewards, thereby accelerating learning and improving generalization. The authors demonstrate improvements over A3C on both Labyrinth and Atari environments.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are promising, the paper presents a rather complex architecture with many moving parts without sufficiently justifying the design choices or providing comprehensive ablation studies. The improvements, while statistically significant, aren’t drastic enough to warrant adopting this considerably more complex approach over well-established methods like A3C, particularly given the lack of rigorous analysis explaining *why* it works.

The main concern is the lack of clarity regarding the contribution of each component. While the authors demonstrate that combining auxiliary tasks improves performance, they don't adequately dissect the impact of each individual task and its interaction with others.  For example, the improvement attributed to reward prediction seems largely based on the authors’ intuition about sparse rewards, but lacks a strong justification or comparison to simpler alternatives. The significant gains on Labyrinth are encouraging, but the limited explanation of the level design and the potential for overfitting to these specific environments raises concerns about the generalizability of the findings.  The claim of improved robustness to hyperparameters is supported by figures, but a more quantitative analysis (e.g., variance of performance across hyperparameter settings) would be more convincing.  Finally, the paper relies heavily on ablation studies, but doesn't provide enough detail about the search space used for hyperparameter optimization for each ablated variant. Were the optimal hyperparameters for each ablated version aggressively tuned, potentially giving them an unfair advantage?

Additional feedback:

*   **Motivation:** The motivation for combining *these specific* auxiliary tasks is somewhat weak. While auxiliary tasks have been shown to be beneficial, the rationale for pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction isn’t convincingly presented. A more thorough literature review exploring why these particular choices were made would be valuable.
*   **Ablation Study Details:** Provide more details on the hyperparameter tuning process for each ablated version.  Were the same search ranges used for all variants?  Report the number of trials performed for each configuration.
*   **Statistical Significance:**  While the paper reports improvements, more rigorous statistical testing should be performed to assess the significance of the differences between UNREAL and the baselines.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead of UNREAL compared to A3C.  The increased complexity likely necessitates more resources, which should be acknowledged.
*   **Level Design:** Provide more details about the Labyrinth levels. Are they randomly generated or hand-crafted? What specific challenges do they pose to the agent? This context is crucial for interpreting the results.
*   **Reward Prediction Details**:  Explain why a multi-class cross-entropy loss was chosen for reward prediction. What alternatives were considered, and why was this the preferred approach? How sensitive is performance to the 0.5 reward balance used for sampling?

Questions for the authors:

*   Could you provide a more detailed explanation of why you chose pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction as the auxiliary tasks? What specific hypotheses were you testing with each task?
*   How sensitive are the results to the specific weighting factors (λVR, λPC, λRP) used in the combined loss function?
*   What is the computational cost of UNREAL compared to A3C? How much more memory and processing power are required?
*   How do the performance gains of UNREAL scale with the complexity of the environment? Would the benefits still be significant in even more challenging domains?
*   You mention that learning to reconstruct pixels can be detrimental. Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and how it differs from learning to *control* pixels?
