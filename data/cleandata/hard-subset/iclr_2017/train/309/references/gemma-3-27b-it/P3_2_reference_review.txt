## ICLR 2024 Review: UNREAL - Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper presents UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent that leverages auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction to improve learning efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to learn useful representations by simultaneously optimizing for multiple objectives: the primary task, controlling low-level aspects of the environment (pixel changes, network activations), and predicting immediate rewards. The authors demonstrate substantial performance gains on both complex 3D Labyrinth environments and a suite of Atari games, showing improved sample efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and overall performance compared to A3C and other baselines. The work is well-motivated, building upon existing research in temporal abstraction, representation learning, and auxiliary task learning, and proposes a compelling integration of these ideas.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** with minor revisions. The paper presents a strong contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, demonstrating a significant improvement in both performance and sample efficiency through a well-designed and well-executed approach. The results are compelling, and the analysis is thorough.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The results on both Labyrinth and Atari are impressive. The consistent improvement over A3C across a diverse set of environments strongly suggests the effectiveness of the UNREAL framework. The reported speedups in learning (10x on average) are particularly noteworthy, addressing a critical challenge in deep RL.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind using auxiliary tasks – to learn better representations and improve exploration – and effectively connects this motivation to relevant prior work (options, UVFA, successor representations, auxiliary prediction). The combination of control tasks and reward prediction appears synergistic, addressing both perceptual sparsity and facilitating learning of valuable features.
* **Thorough Ablation Studies:** The ablation studies performed in the Labyrinth environment are well-designed and provide insights into the contributions of each auxiliary task. Showing that pixel control outperforms simply predicting pixel changes, and that feature control is also beneficial, strengthens the arguments for the proposed approach.
* **Robustness Analysis:** Demonstrating increased robustness to hyperparameters is a significant finding. It highlights a key advantage of UNREAL - its reduced sensitivity to tuning, a common pain point in deep RL.
* **Clear Presentation:** The paper is generally well-written and clearly explains the methodology and experimental setup. The figures and tables are informative and effectively communicate the results.

**4. Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** While the paper highlights robustness, a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter space could be beneficial. A visualization (e.g., heatmap) showing performance across a wider range of learning rates and entropy costs would be insightful.
* **Computational Cost:**  The paper focuses on sample efficiency. Discussing the *computational* cost of adding the auxiliary tasks would provide a more complete picture. Does it significantly increase training time per step?  Is the added computational burden justified by the gains in sample efficiency?
* **Reward Prediction Details:** The reward prediction task is presented briefly. Expanding on the rationale behind the specific reward classification scheme (zero, positive, negative) and exploring alternative prediction targets (e.g., magnitude of reward) could be valuable.
* **Generalization Across Environments:**  While the results are strong on Labyrinth and Atari, discussing potential limitations in generalizing to other environments (e.g., sparse reward settings beyond those tested) would add nuance.
* **Figure Clarity:** Figure 1 (architecture diagrams) are a bit crowded. Simplifying them, or providing zoomed-in views of specific components, would improve readability.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1. **Computational Overhead:** What is the approximate percentage increase in training time per environment step due to the addition of the auxiliary tasks in both Labyrinth and Atari?
2. **Reward Prediction Rationale:** Why was a three-class reward prediction scheme (zero, positive, negative) chosen over other alternatives like predicting the reward magnitude directly?
3. **Feature Control Selection:** Why was the second hidden layer selected for feature control, and were other layers explored? What properties of that layer made it particularly suitable for this purpose?
4. **Generalization Concerns:** Do you anticipate that UNREAL would perform as well in extremely sparse reward environments where rewards are received only after very long sequences of actions? If not, what modifications might be needed to address such challenges?
5. **Code Availability:** Is the code for the UNREAL agent planned for release?  Making the code available would significantly enhance the reproducibility and impact of this work.



Overall, this is a high-quality paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The proposed UNREAL agent demonstrates a significant improvement in learning efficiency and robustness, and the thorough analysis provides strong support for the claims. I recommend acceptance with minor revisions.
