This paper tackles the problem of sample efficiency and robustness in deep reinforcement learning, proposing the UNREAL agent which combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) with reward prediction to learn more effective representations. The core idea – leveraging multiple, diverse auxiliary tasks to shape the agent's understanding of the environment – is a compelling and potentially high-impact approach. The paper is well-motivated, building on existing work in temporal abstraction, representation learning, and auxiliary tasks, and clearly positions itself within that landscape.

**Reject.** While the idea is strong, and the empirical results impressive, the paper feels somewhat overblown in its claims and lacks crucial ablations to fully justify the complexity of the proposed system. The jump to claiming "significant improvement" and "more than doubling" previous state-of-the-art *without* more detailed analysis of the contribution of each component is a concern.

The primary argument for rejection rests on the lack of clarity regarding the *relative* contribution of each auxiliary task. While the authors demonstrate that adding auxiliary tasks *helps*, they don’t convincingly demonstrate *which* tasks are most important or if the gains are synergistic. The ablation study presented in the Labyrinth section, while a step in the right direction, is limited. Showing improvements over baselines with *only* reward prediction or *only* pixel reconstruction is good, but doesn't explain why combining them yields such substantial gains.  Is the reward prediction task truly crucial, or is the improvement primarily driven by the more sophisticated pixel control? The Atari results, while impressive, suffer from the same issue; it’s difficult to discern the independent impact of each component.  Additionally, the hyperparameter sensitivity analysis, while demonstrating robustness, doesn’t pinpoint specific parameter settings responsible for the observed performance, leaving the practicality of deployment unclear.

Further bolstering my concerns, the discussion of the auxiliary control tasks feels somewhat hand-wavy. While the intuition behind controlling pixel changes and network features is reasonable, a deeper theoretical justification for *why* these tasks are effective for representation learning would be beneficial. The choice of specific architectures for the auxiliary tasks (e.g., the deconvolutional network for pixel control) is also not thoroughly explained.

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Detailed Ablation Studies:** Conduct more granular ablation studies. Remove *combinations* of tasks (e.g., pixel control + reward prediction, feature control + reward prediction) and compare their performance. Quantify the synergistic effects, if any.
*   **Task Importance Analysis:** Investigate methods for determining the relative importance of each auxiliary task during training. Perhaps introduce dynamic weighting schemes that adjust task contributions based on their impact on the primary task performance.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** Provide a more rigorous theoretical argument for why controlling pixel changes and network features leads to better representations. Connect these tasks to existing theories of representation learning or information maximization.
*   **Hyperparameter Tuning Details:** Provide a more detailed analysis of the hyperparameter tuning process. What search space was used? What metrics were optimized? How sensitive are the results to specific hyperparameter choices?
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks. How does the training time and memory usage of UNREAL compare to A3C?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the contribution of each auxiliary task to the overall performance gains observed in both the Labyrinth and Atari experiments? Specifically, can you quantify the increase in performance attributable to each task individually, and any synergistic effects?
2.  What is the rationale behind using a deconvolutional network for processing the auxiliary pixel control tasks? Are there alternative architectures that you considered, and if so, why were they rejected?
3.  The paper mentions that the reward prediction task biases the data distribution. Could you elaborate on how this bias affects the learned representation, and how you mitigated any potential negative consequences?
4.  How does the computational cost (training time and memory usage) of UNREAL compare to that of A3C? Are there any strategies for reducing the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks?
