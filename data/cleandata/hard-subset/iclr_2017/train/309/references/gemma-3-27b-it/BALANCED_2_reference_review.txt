This paper proposes UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve data efficiency and robustness. The core idea is to learn useful representations by simultaneously optimizing for multiple objectives, including both intrinsic (auxiliary tasks) and extrinsic (primary task) rewards. The authors demonstrate strong results on both Labyrinth and Atari environments, significantly outperforming A3C and other baselines, particularly in terms of sample efficiency.

**Decision: Reject**

While the results are promising and the motivation is sound, the paper falls short of convincingly demonstrating a novel and significant contribution to the field due to a lack of sufficient ablation and a somewhat unclear articulation of *why* these specific auxiliary tasks work so well. The empirical evidence, while positive, feels more like an engineering demonstration than a deep scientific investigation.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Limited Ablation Study:** The ablation studies presented (Figure 3) show improvements from adding auxiliary tasks, but don't provide enough granularity to understand the relative importance of each component. Specifically, the contribution of reward prediction appears less clear. Is the benefit primarily driven by pixel control, or is reward prediction simply helping to accelerate learning of the pixel control policies? A more systematic ablation, removing combinations of tasks and varying their weighting (λ values) would be crucial. The comparison to pixel reconstruction and input change prediction is helpful, but doesn’t fully justify the specific choices made in UNREAL.

2. **Lack of Mechanistic Insight:** The paper primarily focuses on *what* works (UNREAL performs better) but offers limited insight into *why*.  While the authors mention learning useful features, they don't delve into *what* features are being learned, or *how* those features contribute to improved performance.  Visualizations of the learned representations (e.g., activations of hidden layers, the Qaux outputs) are mentioned as being available online, but are not included in the paper itself. Including some qualitative or quantitative analysis of the learned features would significantly strengthen the paper.  Are the pixel control tasks learning to attend to important objects? Are the network features capturing task-relevant abstractions?

3. **Overly Engineering-Focused Presentation:** The description of the architecture and implementation details (Appendix B) is extensive, bordering on overwhelming. While thoroughness is appreciated, the paper reads more like an implementation manual than a scientific investigation. The focus should be on the core ideas and their theoretical justification, rather than the intricate details of the network architecture.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

* **Clarify the role of λ parameters:** The authors mention sampling λ values from a log-uniform distribution. Provide a justification for this choice. Is there a principled way to tune these parameters, or is it purely empirical?
* **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests for the performance improvements observed in the experiments. Are the differences between UNREAL and the baselines statistically significant?
* **Computational Cost:** Discuss the computational cost associated with adding the auxiliary tasks. Does UNREAL require significantly more compute than A3C?
* **Generalizability:** While the results are promising on Labyrinth and Atari, discuss the potential for generalizing these findings to other environments. Are there any limitations to the approach?
* **Connection to Existing Work:** While the related work section is comprehensive, consider more deeply connecting UNREAL to the broader literature on intrinsic motivation and representation learning. How does UNREAL differ from existing approaches like curiosity-driven exploration or variational autoencoders?
* **Address the "Why":** The biggest weakness is the lack of insight into *why* these auxiliary tasks work. Explore the learned representations. Are the pixel control tasks learning to detect edges, corners, or objects? Are the network features capturing task-relevant abstractions?

**Questions for the Authors:**

1. Can you provide a more detailed ablation study that systematically evaluates the contribution of each auxiliary task, including different weighting schemes for the loss function?
2.  What specific types of features are being learned by the pixel control and network feature tasks? Can you provide visualizations or quantitative analysis of these learned features?
3.  Why did you choose these specific auxiliary tasks (pixel changes and network features)? What theoretical justification supports this choice?
4. How does the computational cost of UNREAL compare to A3C? Does the improved sample efficiency outweigh the increased computational cost?
5.  Do you observe any negative transfer from the auxiliary tasks? Are there any cases where adding auxiliary tasks degrades performance?
