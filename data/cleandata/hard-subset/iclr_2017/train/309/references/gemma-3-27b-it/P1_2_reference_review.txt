## ICLR 2024 Review: UNREAL - Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

**1. Summary:**

This paper proposes UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning architecture that integrates auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction into a standard A3C framework. The core idea is to leverage these auxiliary objectives to learn a richer, more robust representation of the environment, ultimately leading to improved sample efficiency and performance. The authors demonstrate substantial gains in both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment and across a suite of Atari games, showcasing significant improvements in both final performance and learning speed compared to baseline A3C. They also demonstrate robustness to hyperparameter choices.

**2. Decision:**

**Accept** - This paper presents a compelling and well-supported approach to improving reinforcement learning through the strategic incorporation of auxiliary tasks. The empirical results are strong, demonstrating clear advantages over a strong baseline (A3C) in complex environments. The analysis of robustness and the ablation studies further strengthen the claims.

**3. Supporting Arguments:**

* **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance improvements on both Labyrinth and Atari are significant and statistically convincing. The substantial speedup in learning, particularly in Labyrinth, is a key strength. Achieving human-level performance in several Labyrinth levels is particularly notable.
* **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper clearly articulates the motivation behind using auxiliary tasks – to address perceptual sparsity and promote effective representation learning.  The connection to existing work on temporal abstractions, UVFAs, and successor representations is well-established.
* **Rigorous Evaluation:** The authors conduct thorough ablation studies, demonstrating the contribution of each auxiliary task. The comparison to other auxiliary task approaches (pixel reconstruction, input change prediction) highlights the benefits of their specific choices (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction). The robustness analysis is a valuable addition, showcasing the stability of the approach.
* **Clear Presentation:**  The paper is generally well-written and organized, with a clear description of the architecture and experimental setup.  The inclusion of implementation details and supplemental material is helpful.

**4. Additional Feedback (Suggestions for Improvement):**

* **Reward Prediction Details:** While the reward prediction task is described, elaborating on the specific architecture used (beyond mentioning a simpler feedforward network) and its hyperparameters would be beneficial. It would be helpful to understand *why* this specific architecture was chosen.
* **Feature Control Insights:** The results show feature control is promising.  A deeper dive into *which* features are being learned and *how* they contribute to performance would provide valuable insights. Visualizations of activated features during gameplay could be impactful.
* **Generalization to Other RL Algorithms:** The work focuses on A3C. While well-motivated, it would be interesting to briefly discuss the potential for integrating these auxiliary tasks with other RL algorithms (e.g., PPO, SAC).
* **Computational Cost:** While the sample efficiency is improved, a discussion of the *computational* cost of adding these auxiliary tasks would be helpful.  Are there trade-offs between sample efficiency and computational overhead?

**5. Questions for the Authors:**

*  You mention that input reconstruction *hurt* final performance. Is this consistently observed across all Atari games? Do you have a hypothesis for *why* reconstructing irrelevant parts of the image is more detrimental than learning to control pixel changes?
* In the Labyrinth experiments, the performance improvement is most pronounced in the navigation levels. Do you believe this is because these levels particularly benefit from improved state representation, or is there another factor at play?
* Regarding the reward prediction task, what metric (other than classification accuracy) was used to monitor the quality of the predictions during training?  Did you observe any correlation between the quality of reward prediction and the performance of the main policy?
* Could you elaborate on the choice of the specific weighting coefficients (λVR, λPC, λRP) used in the combined loss function? Were these determined through extensive hyperparameter tuning, or is there a more principled approach to their selection?



Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a valuable contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The authors have presented a well-motivated, rigorously evaluated, and empirically successful approach to improving representation learning through auxiliary tasks. I recommend acceptance.
