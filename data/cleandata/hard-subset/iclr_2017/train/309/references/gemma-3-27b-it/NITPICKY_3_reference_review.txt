This paper proposes the UNREAL agent, an extension to A3C incorporating auxiliary control tasks (pixel changes and network features) and reward prediction, aiming to improve data efficiency and robustness in reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate substantial performance gains on the Labyrinth 3D environment and Atari games, showcasing both faster learning and improved final performance compared to A3C and other baselines.

**Reject.** While the presented results are promising, the sheer number of minor flaws and inconsistencies throughout the paper, particularly in the experimental details and presentation, significantly detract from the overall confidence in the findings. The lack of clarity and precision undermines the impact of the potentially valuable contributions.

The paper suffers from several issues. Firstly, the experimental setup lacks sufficient detail for reproducibility. While implementation details are provided in Appendix B, they are still somewhat vague. For example, the exact hyperparameter ranges explored are stated, but the search method (e.g., random search, grid search) isn’t. Furthermore, the statement that hyperparameters were “retuned” is insufficient – details of the retuning process are needed. The weighting of the loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP) is described as being sampled from log-uniform distributions, but the specific distributions used for Labyrinth versus Atari differ, requiring justification. This difference is alluded to but not fully explained. 

Secondly, the discussion of related work, while comprehensive, is sometimes awkwardly phrased and lacks a critical assessment of the existing methods. For example, the dismissal of environment model learning in rich visual environments feels unsubstantiated. The comparison with Lample & Chaplot (2016) and Mirowski et al. (2016) lacks depth, failing to highlight any limitations or specific reasons why their approaches differ significantly from the proposed UNREAL agent.

Thirdly, the presentation of results, especially in Figures 3, 4, 5, 6, and 7, requires improvement. Figure 3’s comparison is cluttered, and the difference between mean and median performance isn’t clearly explained. Figure 5 lacks error bars making it difficult to determine statistical significance. The scatter plots in Figure 8 are useful, but the axes labels are small and difficult to read. In addition, referencing figures in the text that are located in the appendix (e.g. Figure 9, Figure 10) is awkward.

Fourthly, the paper contains several minor technical inconsistencies. The equation for LQ (n-step lookahead loss) includes θ− representing previous parameters but doesn’t explicitly state how these are obtained (e.g., target network update). The explanation of deconvolutional network usage is somewhat convoluted. The reward prediction task uses a three-class classification, but the motivation for this choice (versus, e.g., regression) isn't adequately explained. Also, the statement about splitting the replay buffer equally for rewarding/non-rewarding events is unclear – does this apply to all experiences, or just those used for reward prediction?

Finally, the formatting throughout is inconsistent. Citations are sometimes missing, and the writing style occasionally lacks precision. The use of abbreviations (e.g., A3C) without first defining them is a minor but noticeable issue.



**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Reproducibility:** Provide a more detailed account of the experimental setup, including the hyperparameter search strategy, the precise hyperparameters used for the reported results, and a clear description of the training procedure. Consider releasing code to facilitate reproducibility.
*   **Related Work:** Offer a more critical and nuanced discussion of related work, highlighting the strengths and weaknesses of existing approaches and clearly positioning the UNREAL agent within the broader landscape of reinforcement learning research.
*   **Presentation of Results:** Improve the clarity and readability of the figures. Add error bars, provide informative axis labels, and ensure consistent referencing.
*   **Technical Details:** Clarify any ambiguous technical details, such as the target network update mechanism, the rationale for the three-class reward prediction task, and the specifics of the replay buffer splitting strategy.
*   **Formatting:** Proofread the paper carefully to eliminate any formatting inconsistencies and ensure consistent citation style.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Can you provide the specific hyperparameters (learning rate, entropy cost, λVR, λPC, λRP) used to generate the results presented in Figure 3?
*   What search method was used to identify the optimal hyperparameters?
*   Why was a three-class classification used for reward prediction instead of regression?
*   How are the “previous parameters” (θ−) obtained in the n-step lookahead loss (LQ)?
*   Could you clarify the exact procedure for splitting the replay buffer into rewarding and non-rewarding subsets?
*   What is the rationale for using different ranges for the λPC parameter in Labyrinth versus Atari?
