This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve data efficiency and robustness. The authors introduce auxiliary tasks based on pixel changes and network feature activations, alongside a reward prediction task, and demonstrate substantial performance gains on the Labyrinth 3D environment and Atari games.

**Reject.** While the presented ideas are interesting and the results are promising, the paper suffers from a plethora of minor, yet cumulatively substantial, flaws in presentation, methodological rigor, and clarity. The sheer number of these issues leads me to believe the work, while potentially valuable, isn't ready for publication at ICLR.

The primary concern lies in the lack of meticulous detail regarding experimental setup and results presentation. The authors state significant speedups (e.g., "10× speedup") but fail to define *how* speedup is measured. Is it time to reach a specific performance threshold? Steps to convergence? The lack of clarity makes these claims difficult to evaluate. Similarly, the assertion of increased robustness is supported by figures showing smooth curves, but statistical significance isn’t addressed. A smooth curve doesn't inherently demonstrate robustness. The hyperparameter sweep details are vague. The phrase “sampled from a log-uniform distribution” is insufficient. What ranges were used? How many samples were taken? Were these ranges chosen based on a principled approach or simply by trial and error? 

Further, the ablation study, while presented, lacks sufficient detail. It is not immediately clear what specific architectural changes were made for each ablated version beyond the removal of a task. This makes it difficult to isolate the contribution of each component. The discussion of the reward prediction task feels superficial; the justification for the skewed sampling of rewards (P(rτ != 0) = 0.5) is not convincing. Why this specific ratio? Was it tuned? What happens if reward is *extremely* sparse, making this distribution unrealistic?

The writing quality also detracts from the paper. Numerous grammatical errors and awkward phrasing impede understanding. For example, the sentence “However, in comparison, Labyrinth has considerably richer visuals and more realistic physics” is unnecessarily verbose. The paper also relies heavily on jargon without sufficient explanation, assuming a level of familiarity that may not be universal among ICLR reviewers. The structure within section 4 is problematic. The breakdown of Labyrinth levels and descriptions is extensive but reads as extraneous detail rather than crucial information supporting the core argument. The descriptions are very granular, detailing the color of horseshoes!

Finally, the appendix contains figures labeled "Raw Atari Scores" and "Labyrinth Levels" but no content is provided. The reference list is also formatted inconsistently.

**Additional Feedback:**

*   **Clarify Speedup Metric:** Define precisely how speedup is calculated and provide confidence intervals.
*   **Statistical Analysis:** Perform statistical significance tests to support claims of robustness.
*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Provide complete details of the hyperparameter search space and sampling strategy.
*   **Ablation Study Details:** Explicitly state the architectural changes made for each ablated version.
*   **Reward Prediction Justification:** Provide a more rigorous justification for the skewed reward sampling strategy. Explore alternative approaches.
*   **Writing & Editing:** Thoroughly proofread the paper for grammatical errors, awkward phrasing, and jargon.
*   **Appendix Completion:** Populate the missing figures in the appendix.
*   **Reference Formatting:** Ensure consistent formatting of all references.
*   **Section 4 Refocus:** Streamline section 4, focusing on the key aspects of the experimental setup directly relevant to evaluating the core contribution.



**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational resources used for training (e.g., number of CPUs/GPUs, memory)?
2.  For the pixel control task, what is the justification for the 20x20 grid size? Was this parameter tuned?
3.  How sensitive are the results to the choice of the γ value used in the n-step returns?
4.  Can you provide the code for the experiments, including the exact hyperparameter settings used for the reported results?
5.  The paper states that the reward prediction task is not used for anything other than shaping the features. However, how is this ensured in practice, given that the reward prediction loss contributes to the overall loss function and therefore influences the gradients? Is there a mechanism to isolate the effect of this auxiliary task on the primary policy?
