This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines A3C with auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control) and reward prediction. The core idea is to learn a richer representation by simultaneously optimizing for multiple objectives, leading to faster learning, improved data efficiency, and greater robustness to hyperparameter settings. The results on both Labyrinth and Atari demonstrate significant performance gains over existing methods, particularly in terms of sample efficiency.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are promising, and the idea of combining auxiliary tasks with A3C is intriguing, the paper feels somewhat overblown in its claims and lacks sufficient depth in analysis to justify the strong conclusions drawn. The improvements, while statistically significant, feel more like engineering wins stemming from a well-tuned combination of existing techniques rather than a fundamentally novel breakthrough in RL.

The primary justification for rejection lies in the lack of ablation studies that definitively isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task. While the paper *presents* ablation results, they are largely presented as aggregated curves (Fig 3) and don't delve into *why* certain combinations work better than others. The authors claim that learning to control pixels is superior to simply predicting pixel changes, but a detailed mechanistic explanation for this observation is absent. Similarly, the benefits of feature control are asserted but not rigorously demonstrated beyond improved overall performance. It's unclear if the success is due to the specific auxiliary tasks chosen, the way they’re implemented (e.g., the deconvolutional network architecture), or a synergistic effect arising from their combination. The paper lacks a more granular analysis of what features are being learned and how they contribute to improved performance in the primary task. The claim of improved robustness also needs more rigorous backing; the provided figures show some robustness but lack a formal statistical evaluation.

Furthermore, the presentation is a bit uneven. The descriptions of the auxiliary tasks (pixel control, reward prediction) are detailed, but the connection to existing work on representation learning feels superficial. The related work section acknowledges relevant papers but doesn’t fully integrate them into the narrative, leaving the reader wondering how UNREAL builds upon or diverges from these prior efforts. The authors briefly mention UVFA and Successor Representations but don’t sufficiently contrast them with their approach. Are the learned representations fundamentally different? If so, how?

**Additional Feedback (for potential revision):**

*   **Ablation Analysis:** Provide a more detailed ablation study with individual task performance broken down across levels, and a clear explanation of *why* certain tasks are beneficial. Include error bars.
*   **Feature Visualization:** Visualize the learned features – what are the networks actually learning to control? Show examples of activations and demonstrate how these activations correlate with meaningful events in the environment.
*   **Representation Analysis:** Compare the learned representations (e.g., using PCA or t-SNE) with those learned by standard A3C. This will provide concrete evidence of the benefits of the auxiliary tasks.
*   **Theoretical Justification:** While empirical results are important, a more theoretical discussion of why these auxiliary tasks lead to improved representation learning would strengthen the paper.
*   **Connection to Prior Work:** Expand the related work section to provide a more thorough comparison with existing representation learning techniques.
*   **Hyperparameter Sensitivity:** Quantify the robustness claim. Show statistical analysis (e.g., variance in performance across hyperparameter settings) to support the assertion.
*   **Computational Cost:** Mention the additional computational cost of incorporating the auxiliary tasks. Is the increase in sample efficiency worth the added compute?

**Questions for the authors:**

*   Can you provide a more detailed explanation of *why* learning to control pixels is superior to predicting pixel changes? What specific aspects of the control task lead to improved performance?
*   What is the computational overhead of adding the auxiliary tasks?
*   Have you experimented with different architectures for the auxiliary tasks (e.g., different deconvolutional network structures)?
*   How do the learned representations in UNREAL differ qualitatively from those learned by standard A3C?
*   What are the limitations of UNREAL? Are there environments or tasks where it is unlikely to be effective?
