

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), a novel reinforcement learning agent that incorporates auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters. The authors propose three key auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that train the agent to maximize changes in specific perceptual features, (2) auxiliary reward prediction tasks that predict immediate reward onset, and (3) experience replay techniques for these auxiliary tasks. The authors demonstrate that UNREAL significantly outperforms standard A3C agents across two challenging domains: the 3D Labyrinth environment (achieving 87% human-normalized mean performance, more than double A3C's 54%) and 57 Atari games (achieving 880% mean human-normalized performance, surpassing state-of-the-art methods). The paper makes a clear contribution to the field by showing that auxiliary tasks can effectively shape representation learning without directly biasing the main policy, and that this approach substantially improves learning efficiency and robustness.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The paper makes a significant contribution to reinforcement learning with strong empirical results that clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed auxiliary tasks. The work is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper excels in all three key review criteria:

**1. Specific question/problem tackled**: The paper clearly identifies the problem of sparse rewards and data inefficiency in deep reinforcement learning, particularly in complex visual environments. It proposes a well-defined solution: incorporating auxiliary tasks that shape the agent's representation without directly biasing the main policy. This is a well-framed problem with clear significance to the field.

**2. Approach well-motivated and properly situated in literature**: The related work section is thorough and well-structured, demonstrating a deep understanding of the field. The authors correctly identify that previous work on auxiliary tasks (like environment models or successor representations) has either focused on direct reward maximization or used separate representations. The authors position their work as a clear advancement that uses auxiliary tasks specifically for representation learning, with the key insight that these tasks can be trained off-policy and share representations with the main agent. This placement in the literature is accurate and compelling.

**3. Claims supported by rigorous results**: The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and convincing. The authors provide:
- Strong results across two different domains (3D Labyrinth and Atari)
- Thorough ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary task
- Comparison to appropriate baselines (including A3C and prior work on auxiliary tasks)
- Analysis of learning speed (10x faster than A3C to reach best performance)
- Analysis of hyperparameter robustness (UNREAL is significantly more robust than A3C)
- The results are statistically sound, with multiple hyperparameter settings evaluated and averaged performance reported

The paper's claim that UNREAL "more than doubled the previous state-of-the-art results on the challenging set of 3D Labyrinth levels" is strongly supported by the data in Figure 3 and Figure 4. The Atari results (880% mean human-normalized performance) also convincingly demonstrate superiority over prior work.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, these suggestions would further improve it:

**Theoretical insights**: The paper would benefit from more theoretical discussion on *why* these auxiliary tasks work. For example, the paper shows pixel control outperforms input reconstruction (Figure 5), but doesn't explain why. A brief theoretical analysis of how these tasks shape the representation would strengthen the paper.

**Limitations discussion**: The paper could be enhanced by including a discussion of potential limitations of the UNREAL approach. For instance, are there specific types of environments where the auxiliary tasks might not provide significant benefits? How does the approach scale to even more complex visual domains?

**Implementation clarity**: While the implementation details are provided in the appendix, a brief clarification in the main text about how the reward prediction task avoids biasing the main policy would help readers. The paper states: "the reward predictor is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent," but a more concrete explanation of how this is achieved would be valuable.

**Comparison to successor representations**: The paper mentions successor representations in the related work but doesn't discuss how UNREAL relates to or differs from these approaches. A brief comparison would help position the work more precisely.

## Questions for the Authors

1. In Figure 5, the paper shows that feature control is slightly worse than pixel control. Could you elaborate on why pixel control appears to be more effective than feature control for shaping the representation? Is this due to the spatial structure of the environment?

2. The paper states that UNREAL is "substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C." Could you provide more specific details about this robustness? For instance, what is the range of hyperparameter values that still yield strong performance?

3. How do the auxiliary tasks interact with the LSTM's ability to maintain long-term memory? Does the use of auxiliary tasks affect the agent's ability to solve tasks that require long-term planning?

4. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task uses a skewed sampling to overrepresent rewarding events. How did you determine the optimal sampling ratio (50% rewarding vs. 50% non-rewarding)? Did you experiment with different ratios?

5. Could you provide more details on how the deconvolutional network for pixel control is trained? Specifically, how does the network learn to predict the optimal action for changing the pixels in each cell?
