

# Peer Review: UNREAL - Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contribution

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness by incorporating auxiliary tasks. The authors propose two main types of auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that learn to maximize changes in specific parts of the input or network features, and (2) reward prediction tasks that learn to predict immediate rewards. Unlike previous work that used auxiliary tasks for prediction or as direct policy improvements, UNREAL uses these tasks solely as representation learning mechanisms to shape the agent's internal features. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (achieving 87% human-normalized performance compared to A3C's 54%) and 57 Atari games (880% mean human-normalized performance), while also being 10-18x more data-efficient and significantly more robust to hyperparameter choices.

## 2. Decision

Accept with minor revisions.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Acceptance

The paper makes a strong empirical contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The UNREAL approach is well-motivated and positions itself effectively within the literature, distinguishing itself from prior work on temporal abstractions (options, Horde), UVFAs, successor representations, and environment models. The authors demonstrate a clear understanding of the challenges in RL (particularly sparse rewards) and provide a thoughtful solution that focuses on representation learning rather than directly modifying the policy.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The ablation studies clearly show that each auxiliary task contributes to the overall improvement, and the comparison to baselines (like pixel reconstruction) provides strong evidence that the authors' approach is fundamentally different and superior. The paper's results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, with UNREAL doubling performance over A3C while being substantially more data-efficient. The Atari results also show consistent improvements across all 57 games, outperforming prior state-of-the-art methods.

The experimental design is rigorous, with multiple hyperparameter settings evaluated for each game, and the authors provide clear visualizations of learning curves and performance across different environments. The paper's focus on both performance and data efficiency addresses a critical practical challenge in RL.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be enhanced:

- **Theoretical Justification**: The paper states that reward prediction "shapes the features" but doesn't provide a clear theoretical connection between reward prediction and the main task. A brief discussion of why predicting immediate rewards improves representation learning would strengthen the paper's foundation.

- **Task Interactions**: The paper would benefit from more insight into how the different auxiliary tasks interact. For example, what is the relationship between feature control and reward prediction? How do they complement each other in shaping the representation?

- **Spatial Structure Explanation**: The paper mentions that pixel control and feature control are "spatially structured" tasks but doesn't fully explain why this spatial structure matters for representation learning. A short explanation would help readers understand this design choice.

- **Classification vs. Regression**: The reward prediction task uses a three-class classification loss (positive, negative, zero reward) rather than a regression loss. The paper should explain the reasoning behind this design choice and whether it was tested against alternatives.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "the reward predictor is not used for anything other than shaping the features," but how exactly is the reward prediction task integrated into the agent's representation? Is it a separate head that shares weights with the main policy, or is it used in some other way?

2. In the reward prediction task, why was a classification loss (three classes) chosen over a regression loss for the actual reward value? What were the trade-offs considered in this design choice?

3. The paper mentions that feature control was "only slightly worse than pixel control" but doesn't explore why pixel control was more effective. Could you provide more insight into the relative strengths of these two approaches?

4. How might the UNREAL approach be extended to continuous action spaces or environments where pixel control might not be well-defined?

5. The paper demonstrates significant improvements in data efficiency, but what is the computational overhead of maintaining these auxiliary tasks? How does the total training time compare to A3C?

The UNREAL approach represents a significant advancement in making reinforcement learning more data-efficient and robust. With minor clarifications on the theoretical connections and design choices, this paper would make an excellent contribution to ICLR.
