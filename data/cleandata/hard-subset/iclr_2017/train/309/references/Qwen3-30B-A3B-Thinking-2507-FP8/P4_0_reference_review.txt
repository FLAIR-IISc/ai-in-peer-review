

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent to Play Multiple Games"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that significantly improves upon standard A3C by incorporating three auxiliary tasks: pixel control (learning to maximize changes in specific image regions), feature control (learning to maximize activations of specific network units), and reward prediction (learning to predict immediate rewards). The authors claim these auxiliary tasks dramatically improve data efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameter settings. The approach is validated on both a challenging 3D environment (Labyrinth with 13 levels) and 57 Atari games, demonstrating substantial improvements over state-of-the-art baselines.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper presents a well-motivated, technically sound approach with strong empirical evidence supporting its claims. The contributions are novel, well-validated, and make a significant advancement in the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

**Technical Soundness and Novelty:** The paper's core contribution—combining multiple auxiliary tasks for representation learning in RL—represents a meaningful advancement. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work (options, UVFAs, successor representations) by emphasizing that the auxiliary tasks are not used for direct reward maximization but solely to develop better representations. The specific implementation of pixel control (using a deconvolutional network) and reward prediction (with rebalanced replay) is technically sound and well-justified.

**Empirical Rigor:** The experimental validation is comprehensive and well-designed. The Labyrinth experiments cover multiple difficulty levels (simple navigation, maze exploration, laser-tag), providing nuanced insights into the agent's capabilities. The Atari experiments use the standard 57-game evaluation protocol and compare against strong baselines (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN). The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary component, and the comparison against baselines like input reconstruction and immediate reward prediction provides strong evidence for the specific design choices.

**Impact and Significance:** The results are compelling: UNREAL more than doubles performance on Labyrinth (87% vs 54% human-normalized mean), achieves 880% mean and 250% median performance on Atari, and requires less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. The improved robustness to hyperparameters (shown through extensive hyperparameter sweeps) is particularly valuable for practical applications.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further improve it:

- The paper could provide more theoretical justification for why the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control) work better than alternatives. For instance, why do these particular choices lead to better representation learning compared to other potential auxiliary tasks?

- The discussion of feature control could be expanded. The paper mentions feature control was "only slightly worse than pixel control" but doesn't provide comparative metrics across multiple levels. A more detailed analysis would strengthen this claim.

- The computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks (both in training and inference time) should be quantified, as this is an important practical consideration.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." Could you provide specific quantitative comparisons between these two approaches across multiple environments?

2. The reward prediction task uses a skewed sampling strategy (50% non-zero rewards). Could you elaborate on how this affects the learned representations and whether the performance would be sensitive to the exact sampling ratio?

3. The paper mentions that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout" in the procedurally-generated navigation levels. How does UNREAL's learning process differ from a purely exploration-based approach, and what specific aspects of the auxiliary tasks enable this faster exploration?
