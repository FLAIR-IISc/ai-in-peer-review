

# Review of "UNREAL: End-to-End Learning and Planning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) with reward prediction tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements over A3C on both Labyrinth (a 3D first-person navigation environment) and Atari games, with UNREAL achieving 87% human-normalized mean performance on Labyrinth and substantially outperforming previous state-of-the-art methods on Atari.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper contains multiple substantial technical and presentation flaws that undermine its credibility and scientific rigor, including a critical numerical error and insufficient evidence for key claims.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Rejection

### Critical Numerical Error
The paper claims UNREAL "achieves more than twice the final human-normalised mean performance of A3C, increasing from 54% to 87%." This is mathematically incorrect: 54% to 87% represents a 61% increase (87/54 = 1.61), not "more than twice" (which would require 108% or higher). This error appears in the main text, abstract, and conclusion, suggesting a fundamental lack of attention to detail in the paper's presentation.

### Ambiguous Performance Claims
The paper states "UNREAL surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents, i.e. A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN (Wang et al., 2016), across all levels attaining 880% mean and 250% median performance." The 880% mean performance figure is nonsensical for Atari games, where human-normalized scores typically range from 0-200%. This suggests either a misunderstanding of the metric or a serious error in calculation.

### Methodological Ambiguity
The paper states "the reward predictor is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent," but fails to explain how this avoids introducing bias to the policy. The distinction between reward prediction and value learning with γ=0 is not clearly articulated, making the contribution less novel than claimed.

### Inconsistent Terminology and Presentation
- Inconsistent capitalization of "Labyrinth" (sometimes "Labyrinth," sometimes "labyrinth")
- Inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., "Barreto et al.,? \Q2016\E" instead of standard citation format)
- Ambiguous description of "speedup" (10× faster learning or 10× fewer steps?)
- Unclear mathematical definition of "maximally changing pixels" in Section 3.1
- Confusing loss function description (LVR is listed separately from LA3C despite being part of A3C)

### Insufficient Evidence for Key Claims
The paper claims UNREAL is "substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings" but provides only scatter plots (Figure 8) without quantitative analysis of robustness. The claim that "UNREAL without pixel control" is a meaningful ablation is not justified, as pixel control appears to be the most effective component.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

1. **Correct the critical numerical error**: The claim about "more than twice the performance" must be corrected to accurately reflect the 61% improvement (87% vs 54%).

2. **Clarify all performance metrics**: Provide a clear definition of "human-normalized scores" and explain why 880% is a meaningful figure for Atari performance.

3. **Revise methodology descriptions**: 
   - Precisely define "maximally changing pixels" mathematically
   - Clearly distinguish reward prediction from standard value learning
   - Explain why pixel control was chosen for ablation rather than other components

4. **Improve presentation consistency**:
   - Standardize capitalization of "Labyrinth"
   - Fix all reference formatting issues
   - Clarify ambiguous terms like "speedup" and "data efficiency"

5. **Strengthen theoretical justification**: Provide a more rigorous explanation for why these auxiliary tasks improve representation learning, rather than simply stating the claim.

## Questions for Authors

1. Can you clarify exactly how human-normalized scores were calculated for Labyrinth? The paper states these are "normalized to human performance," but doesn't specify the normalization procedure.

2. What is the exact definition of "maximally changing pixels" used in the pixel control auxiliary task? Is it maximizing the absolute difference in pixel values, or some other metric?

3. The paper claims UNREAL achieves "880% mean performance" on Atari. Given that the maximum human-normalized score on Atari is typically 200%, can you clarify how this figure was calculated?

4. Why was pixel control specifically removed in the ablation study rather than feature control or reward prediction? What evidence suggests pixel control was the most important component?

5. The paper states that the reward predictor "does not introduce bias to the agent's policy" because it's not used for anything other than shaping features. Can you provide a theoretical argument for why this is true, given that the reward predictor is trained on a skewed distribution of rewarding events?
