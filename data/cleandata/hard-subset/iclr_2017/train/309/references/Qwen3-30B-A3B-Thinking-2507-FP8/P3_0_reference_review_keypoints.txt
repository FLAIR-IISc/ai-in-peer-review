- Introduces UNREAL: a deep RL agent using auxiliary control (pixel/feature control) and reward-prediction tasks to shape representation without biasing the main policy.  
- Claims major gains: >2x A3C on Labyrinth (87% vs 54% human-normalized), 10x learning speedup, and state-of-the-art performance on Atari (880% mean, 250% median human-normalized).  
- Well-situated in literature with clear distinction from Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and prior auxiliary-task work.  
- Theoretical motivation: controlling aspects of the environment yields better representations than mere prediction.  
- Experimental evaluation is comprehensive: Labyrinth and 57 Atari games, plus ablations and hyperparameter-robustness analyses.  
- Ablation results show pixel-control outperforms pixel prediction/reconstruction and each auxiliary task contributes to final performance.  
- Demonstrates substantially greater robustness to hyperparameter choices compared to A3C, with lower variance in learning curves.  
- Decision: Accept with minor revisions.  
- Revision request 1: Provide analysis/visualizations of learned representations to substantiate the representation-learning claim.  
- Revision request 2: Justify and provide evidence for using three-class reward prediction versus binary classification or regression.  
- Revision request 3: Quantify the computational overhead and scaling cost of adding the auxiliary tasks.  
- Revision request 4: Correct the mathematically inconsistent human-normalized score claim (87% is not “more than twice” 54%).  
- Revision request 5: Discuss failure cases and limitations, especially on the hardest Labyrinth levels.  
- Specific questions for authors: why control beats prediction (dynamics vs pattern recognition), evidence for 3-class superiority, choice of sequence length 3, how UNREAL’s exploration differs from A3C, and a quantitative metric for hyperparameter robustness (e.g., variance).
