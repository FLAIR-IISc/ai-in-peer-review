

# Review of "UNREAL: Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Rewards"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning framework that combines auxiliary tasks with the standard A3C architecture to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness. The authors claim that by incorporating three types of auxiliary tasks—pixel control (learning to maximize changes in specific image regions), feature control (learning to maximize activations of specific hidden units), and reward prediction (predicting immediate rewards from visual inputs)—the agent develops a more effective representation of the environment. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over A3C on the 3D Labyrinth environment (increasing human-normalized scores from 54% to 87%) and on 57 Atari games (achieving 880% mean human-normalized performance). The paper claims UNREAL substantially improves data efficiency (requiring less than 10% of the data needed by A3C) and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

## 2. Decision

Reject.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

While the paper presents thorough experiments and reasonable placement in the literature, I have significant concerns about the methodological soundness and the strength of the evidence supporting the claims. As a reviewer who prefers established methods and is skeptical of unproven approaches, I find the paper lacks sufficient evidence to justify the novelty and impact of its proposed auxiliary tasks.

First, the paper's core innovation—using control tasks (pixel and feature control) as auxiliary objectives—appears to be a natural extension of existing work rather than a truly novel approach. The related work section properly cites similar efforts like the Horde architecture, UVFAs, and successor representations, but the paper fails to convincingly demonstrate why these specific control tasks provide significantly better representation learning than simpler alternatives. The comparison to pixel reconstruction (Kulkarni et al., 2016) and input change prediction is limited, and the paper does not provide a strong theoretical justification for why learning to "control" these aspects (rather than simply predicting them) leads to better representations.

Second, the paper claims "more than twice the final human-normalised mean performance" but provides insufficient detail about what "human-normalized" means. Is this normalized against human players' performance, or against some human baseline? The paper does not clarify this critical point, making it difficult to assess the significance of the claimed improvements.

Third, while the ablation studies show that each auxiliary component contributes to performance, the paper does not sufficiently analyze why these specific auxiliary tasks work better than alternative approaches. The paper states that "learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes," but it doesn't provide evidence that the control tasks actually learn meaningful representations rather than simply memorizing patterns. The paper lacks analysis of the representations learned by the auxiliary tasks and how they differ from those learned by standard RL.

Fourth, the paper claims UNREAL "removes the perceptual sparsity of rewards," but it does not provide evidence that the reward prediction task actually learns to identify meaningful reward events rather than merely overfitting to the skewed sampling method. The paper's approach of oversampling rewarding events (to make non-zero rewards equally represented) is a form of data augmentation that could explain some of the performance gains without requiring the auxiliary tasks to be particularly insightful.

Finally, the paper's claims of "more than twice the final performance" and "data efficiency" are not sufficiently supported by the experimental design. The paper uses a single replay buffer and a fixed sampling strategy, which could introduce biases that aren't properly accounted for in the results. The paper doesn't compare against other state-of-the-art methods beyond A3C, and the results on Atari games are presented without sufficient error bars or statistical significance testing.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Provide a clearer definition of "human-normalized" performance and how it's calculated.
2. Conduct more thorough ablation studies comparing UNREAL to simpler, more interpretable auxiliary tasks.
3. Include analysis of the representations learned by the auxiliary tasks (e.g., t-SNE visualizations of the features).
4. Provide more evidence that the auxiliary tasks are learning meaningful representations rather than merely memorizing patterns.
5. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods beyond A3C, especially those using similar auxiliary tasks.
6. Include statistical significance testing for all performance claims.
7. Address the theoretical justification for why control tasks would outperform prediction tasks.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. What is the precise definition of "human-normalized" performance used in the Labyrinth and Atari experiments? How is this normalization calculated, and how does it compare to other standard normalization approaches used in the RL literature?

2. The paper claims that reward prediction "removes the perceptual sparsity of rewards," but it doesn't provide evidence that the reward predictor is actually learning to recognize meaningful reward events rather than simply memorizing patterns. Can the authors provide examples of reward prediction examples where the agent correctly predicts rewards in novel situations?

3. The paper states that "learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes," but it doesn't provide a theoretical justification for this claim. Why would a control task be fundamentally better than a prediction task for representation learning? Can the authors provide evidence that the control tasks are learning different representations than prediction tasks?

4. The paper uses skewed sampling for reward prediction (equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards), which is a form of data augmentation. How much of the performance gain is due to this data augmentation technique rather than the specific auxiliary task design?

5. The paper claims UNREAL is "more data efficient," requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. However, the paper doesn't provide error bars or statistical significance testing for these claims. Can the authors provide confidence intervals for the data efficiency claims?
