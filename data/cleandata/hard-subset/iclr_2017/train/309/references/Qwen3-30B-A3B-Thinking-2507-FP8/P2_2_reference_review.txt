

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contribution

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Rewards), a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning that incorporates auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The authors propose two main types of auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that learn to maximize changes in specific visual regions or network activations, and (2) auxiliary reward prediction tasks that predict the onset of immediate rewards. Unlike previous work that used pseudo-reward maximization for high-level planning, the authors use these auxiliary tasks solely as representation learning mechanisms. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL significantly outperforms standard A3C agents on both a challenging 3D first-person game environment (Labyrinth) and the Atari 57 game benchmark, achieving more than double the human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (87% vs. 54%) and substantially improved data efficiency (10× faster learning) and robustness to hyperparameters.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper addresses an important and well-motivated problem in reinforcement learning (sparse rewards) with a novel yet well-grounded approach that builds effectively on prior literature.
2. The empirical results are strong, scientifically rigorous, and demonstrate significant improvements over multiple strong baselines across diverse environments.

## 3. Supporting arguments for the decision

The paper's contribution is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The related work section thoroughly contextualizes the approach in relation to temporal abstractions (options), Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and other auxiliary task approaches. The authors clearly articulate how their work differs from previous approaches (e.g., "our agents do not make any direct use of the pseudo-reward maximising policies that they learn... instead, they are used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation").

The empirical evaluation is comprehensive and scientifically sound. The authors conduct experiments on two distinct environments (Labyrinth and Atari) with appropriate baselines (A3C and ablated UNREAL variants), multiple metrics (human-normalized scores, learning speed, robustness to hyperparameters), and careful ablation studies to isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task. The results consistently show that UNREAL outperforms baselines by significant margins across both environments.

The paper provides sufficient detail about the implementation to allow for reproducibility, including specific network architectures, hyperparameter ranges, and training procedures. The ablation studies (particularly the comparison between pixel control, input change prediction, and input reconstruction) provide compelling evidence for the authors' claims about why their approach works better.

The results are impressive: UNREAL achieves 87% mean human-normalized performance on Labyrinth compared to 54% for A3C (more than doubling performance), with a 10× speedup in learning and less than 10% of the data required to reach A3C's final performance. On Atari, UNREAL achieves 880% mean and 250% median performance, surpassing previous state-of-the-art methods.

## 4. Additional feedback to improve the paper

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance it:

1. **Theoretical justification**: The paper provides strong empirical results but lacks a more detailed theoretical explanation for why these specific auxiliary tasks work well. A brief discussion of the underlying principles (e.g., how the auxiliary tasks relate to the information bottleneck or representation learning theory) would strengthen the contribution.

2. **Computational cost analysis**: The paper emphasizes data efficiency but doesn't quantify the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks. It would be valuable to include a comparison of wall-clock time per step or total training time to demonstrate whether the computational cost is justified by the performance gains.

3. **Feature visualization**: The paper mentions "visualizations of the activations and auxiliary task outputs" in the video link, but it would be helpful to include some representative visualizations in the paper itself. This would provide more concrete evidence of how the auxiliary tasks shape the representation.

4. **Feature control details**: The paper states that feature control was "only slightly worse than for pixel control" but doesn't elaborate on why. A more detailed comparison of when feature control might be preferred over pixel control (e.g., in environments where specific network features are more informative) would be valuable.

5. **Human-normalized performance**: The paper uses "human-normalized" scores but doesn't clearly define how these are calculated. Providing a brief explanation (e.g., "performance relative to human performance on the same task") would improve clarity for readers.

## Questions for the authors

1. The paper states that UNREAL is "more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C." Could you provide more detail on how this robustness manifests (e.g., what specific hyperparameters are most sensitive, and by how much)? A sensitivity analysis would strengthen this claim.

2. The reward prediction task uses a skewed sampling (50/50 non-zero/zero rewards). How sensitive is the performance to the exact skewing ratio (e.g., 60/40, 70/30)? Is there an optimal ratio, and does it depend on the environment?

3. In the Labyrinth experiments, the paper mentions "procedurally-generated navigation levels requiring effective exploration." How does UNREAL's exploration strategy differ from standard exploration methods (e.g., entropy regularization), and how does the auxiliary reward prediction task specifically enhance exploration?

4. The paper claims that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." Could you elaborate on why input reconstruction is harmful to final performance, and whether this is a general phenomenon or specific to the environments tested?

5. The paper states that UNREAL "surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents, i.e. A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN." Could you provide a more detailed comparison with Prioritized Dueling DQN, including specific performance metrics on the Atari games where UNREAL outperforms it?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the reinforcement learning literature. The results are compelling, the approach is well-motivated, and the experiments are rigorous. I believe this paper would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
