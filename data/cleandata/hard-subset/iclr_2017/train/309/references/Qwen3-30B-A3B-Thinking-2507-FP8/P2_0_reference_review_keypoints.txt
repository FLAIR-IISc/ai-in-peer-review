- Introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning) that uses auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and hyperparameter robustness.  
- Proposes three auxiliary task types: auxiliary control (pixel and feature control), reward prediction, and experience replay for auxiliary tasks.  
- Shows strong empirical gains: 3D Labyrinth mean human-normalized 87% vs A3C 54%, and 57 Atari games mean 880% human-normalized performance.  
- Claims auxiliary tasks shape representations without directly biasing the main policy, improving learning efficiency and robustness.  
- Recommendation: accept.  
- Targets the problem of sparse rewards and data inefficiency in deep RL, especially in complex visual environments.  
- Approach is well-motivated and properly situated in related work, leveraging off-policy auxiliary tasks that share representations with the main agent.  
- Empirical evaluation is comprehensive: two domains, thorough ablations, appropriate baselines, speed analysis (≈10x faster to best performance), hyperparameter robustness tests, and statistical averaging.  
- Strong evidence supports claims of more than doubling prior state-of-the-art on Labyrinth and outperforming prior work on Atari.  
- Suggest adding theoretical analysis explaining why auxiliary tasks (e.g., pixel control over input reconstruction) shape representations effectively.  
- Recommend discussing limitations: environments or settings where UNREAL might not help and scalability to more complex domains.  
- Ask for clearer main-text explanation of how the reward prediction task avoids biasing the main policy.  
- Recommend a brief comparison to successor representations to better position the contribution.  
- Question: Why does pixel control outperform feature control—does spatial structure explain the difference?  
- Question: Provide specific ranges of hyperparameters where UNREAL remains robust compared to A3C.  
- Question: How do auxiliary tasks interact with the LSTM’s long-term memory and planning capabilities?  
- Question: How was the skewed sampling ratio for reward prediction (50% rewarding vs 50% non-rewarding) chosen, and were other ratios tested?  
- Question: Provide details on training the deconvolutional network for pixel control and how it learns to predict actions that change pixels in each cell.
