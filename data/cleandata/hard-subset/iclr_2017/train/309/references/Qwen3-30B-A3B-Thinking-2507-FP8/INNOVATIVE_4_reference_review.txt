

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning architecture that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness through the strategic incorporation of multiple auxiliary tasks. The authors make a compelling case that learning to control pixel changes and network features (as auxiliary control tasks) and learning to predict immediate rewards (as an auxiliary reward task) can collectively shape a more effective representation for the agent, without directly biasing the main policy. The key novelty lies in their specific implementation and combination of these auxiliary tasks, which builds on but significantly extends previous work on representation learning (UVFA, successor representations) and auxiliary tasks (Lample & Chaplot, Mirowski et al.). The paper demonstrates that UNREAL more than doubles the performance of A3C on challenging 3D Labyrinth levels (87% vs 54% human-normalized performance) and significantly improves performance across 57 Atari games compared to state-of-the-art baselines.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper makes a bold and novel contribution to reinforcement learning by demonstrating that a specific combination of auxiliary tasks can dramatically improve representation learning without compromising the main policy. This is a significant conceptual advance that goes beyond incremental improvements to existing methods.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's contribution is genuinely novel in how it conceptualizes and implements auxiliary tasks for representation learning. Unlike previous work that used auxiliary tasks for planning (options) or transfer (UVFA), the authors position their auxiliary tasks as purely for representation learning, which is a fresh perspective that justifies the approach. The empirical results are compelling: UNREAL achieves more than double the performance of A3C on Labyrinth, with 10x faster learning and 18x data efficiency gains on some levels. The ablation studies (Figure 5) provide strong evidence that learning to control pixel changes is superior to simpler baselines like input reconstruction, which aligns with their theoretical motivation.

The paper's novelty is not just in the specific tasks but in the holistic approach to combining them. The authors recognize that different auxiliary tasks serve different purposes (pixel control for perceptual learning, feature control for internal representation learning, reward prediction for shaping features) and that their combination creates synergistic benefits. This represents a significant conceptual leap beyond previous work that typically focused on a single auxiliary task.

While the paper doesn't provide deep theoretical justification for why these specific auxiliary tasks work, the empirical evidence is strong enough to support the claim, and the reviewer prioritizes novel concepts over theoretical completeness. The authors have carefully positioned their work in the literature, clearly distinguishing their approach from related work on options, UVFA, and successor representations.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: While the empirical results are strong, adding a brief theoretical discussion of why these specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) might shape better representations would strengthen the conceptual contribution.

2. **Connection to Representation Learning**: Explicitly connect UNREAL's representation learning to more formal representation learning frameworks (e.g., successor features, UVFA) to show how UNREAL advances the field beyond previous representation learning approaches.

3. **Implementation Details**: The paper mentions "deconvolutional networks" for auxiliary tasks but doesn't fully explain why this architecture choice is optimal for spatially structured auxiliary tasks. A brief discussion of this design choice would help readers understand the innovation.

4. **Limitations**: Briefly discuss the limitations of UNREAL - for example, whether the approach scales to even more complex environments or if there are specific types of environments where it might not work as well.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the auxiliary tasks are not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent." Could you elaborate on how the reward prediction task specifically shapes the features without introducing bias to the main policy? What evidence do you have that the reward predictor doesn't inadvertently bias the main policy?

2. In the ablation studies (Figure 5), feature control shows slightly better performance than pixel control on some levels. Could you explain why feature control might be more effective for certain tasks, and what this implies about the nature of the representations being learned?

3. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task is trained to predict "immediate reward" (γ=0). How does this differ from simply using the reward as a supervised signal for the feature extractor, and why is this specific formulation beneficial?

4. The authors note that UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters than A3C. Could you provide insight into which aspects of the auxiliary tasks contribute most to this robustness, and whether this robustness generalizes to other RL algorithms beyond A3C?
