

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces UNREAL, an agent architecture that enhances standard A3C reinforcement learning with two types of auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize changes in specific parts of the visual input (pixel control) or network features (feature control), and (2) reward prediction tasks that predict immediate rewards from sequences of observations. The authors claim that these auxiliary tasks significantly improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters across both 3D Labyrinth environments and 57 Atari games.

## 2. Decision
I recommend rejection.

Key reasons for rejection:
1. The core idea of using auxiliary tasks for representation learning is not novel and builds directly on previous work without sufficient distinction.
2. The empirical evaluation lacks sufficient analysis to understand why the auxiliary tasks work, particularly the pixel control and feature control tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution appears to be combining existing auxiliary task ideas (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) with A3C. However, this combination is not novel and lacks clear articulation of how it advances beyond prior work.

The related work section correctly cites prior work on UVFAs, successor representations, and auxiliary tasks (Lample & Chaplot, 2016; Mirowski et al., 2016), but fails to clearly distinguish UNREAL from these approaches. For instance:
- The pixel control task closely resembles the "predicting internal features" work of Lample & Chaplot (2016), but the paper doesn't explain how UNREAL differs or improves upon this.
- The reward prediction task is similar to the "immediate reward prediction" mentioned in the related work.
- The feature control task relates to UVFAs and successor representations, but the paper doesn't adequately explain how their implementation differs.

The empirical evaluation shows that UNREAL outperforms A3C on Labyrinth and Atari, but lacks sufficient analysis to understand why these auxiliary tasks work. The ablation study (Figure 5) shows that pixel control outperforms input reconstruction, but doesn't explain why. The paper also doesn't provide visualizations of the features learned by the auxiliary tasks or how they relate to the main task.

The implementation details in Appendix B are sparse, particularly regarding the deconvolutional network for pixel control. It's unclear how this network was trained or why it's necessary for the auxiliary tasks. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient analysis of the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks versus the data efficiency gains.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
- Clearly articulate the novelty of UNREAL compared to prior auxiliary task work. What specific improvements does UNREAL bring over previous methods?
- Provide more detailed ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task to the final performance.
- Include visualizations of the features learned by the auxiliary tasks and how they relate to the main task.
- Add more detail on the implementation of the auxiliary tasks, particularly the deconvolutional network for pixel control.
- Analyze the trade-off between the additional computation required for the auxiliary tasks and the data efficiency gains.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How does UNREAL specifically differ from UVFAs and successor representations (cited in related work)? What unique advantages does it offer?
- Can you provide more details on the deconvolutional network used for pixel control? How was it trained, and why was this architecture chosen over simpler alternatives?
- Why does feature control work well, and how does it differ from pixel control? What specific features of the network are being controlled, and why are these features useful for the main task?
- The paper claims UNREAL is more data-efficient, but doesn't provide a clear analysis of the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks. Can you provide this analysis?
- In Figure 5, the authors show that pixel control outperforms input change prediction. Can you provide more insight into why learning to control pixel changes is better than predicting them?
