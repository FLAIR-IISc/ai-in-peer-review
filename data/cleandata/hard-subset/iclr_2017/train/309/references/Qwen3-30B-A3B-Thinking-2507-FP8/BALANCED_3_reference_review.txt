

# Review of "UNREAL: End-to-End Learning and Planning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces the UNREAL agent, which enhances deep reinforcement learning by incorporating auxiliary tasks (auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction) into the standard A3C framework. The authors claim these auxiliary tasks help develop more effective representations, leading to faster learning, higher performance, and greater robustness to hyperparameters. Specifically, they propose:
- Pixel control: Learning to maximize changes in specific regions of the input image
- Feature control: Learning to maximize activations of specific hidden units
- Reward prediction: Predicting immediate reward from historical observations

The paper demonstrates significant performance improvements on 13 Labyrinth levels (increasing human-normalized scores from 54% to 87%) and 57 Atari games (achieving 880% mean performance), with UNREAL learning 10-18× faster than A3C and showing greater robustness to hyperparameter choices.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The paper's key contribution is insufficiently novel, and the empirical results don't fully support the claims about the specific mechanism of representation improvement.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The main reasons for rejection are:

First, the paper's novelty is limited. The auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) are largely incremental extensions of existing work. Pixel control resembles previous work on predicting future states or image reconstruction (e.g., Kulkarni et al., 2016), while reward prediction is similar to value learning focused on immediate rewards. The paper cites UVFA and successor representations but doesn't clearly articulate how UNREAL advances beyond these approaches. The paper states "the auxiliary tasks are used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation," but this is not substantially different from previous work that used auxiliary tasks for representation learning.

Second, the paper doesn't provide sufficient evidence to support its core claim that these specific auxiliary tasks are the primary reason for performance gains. The ablation study (Figure 4) shows that removing pixel control hurts performance, but the paper doesn't isolate the contribution of each component or rule out alternative explanations. For instance, the use of experience replay (which is also part of UNREAL) could be responsible for some of the improvements. The paper states that "the UNREAL agent, which combines all three auxiliary tasks, achieves more than twice the final human-normalized mean performance of A3C," but doesn't demonstrate that the auxiliary tasks are the key factor beyond the overall architecture changes.

The paper also lacks theoretical justification for why these specific auxiliary tasks would be beneficial for representation learning. While the authors claim these tasks help with sparse reward problems, they don't explain how the pixel control or feature control tasks specifically address this challenge in a way that previous methods didn't.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more detailed comparison with UVFA and successor representation approaches. Specifically, explain why UNREAL's approach to using auxiliary tasks for representation learning is superior to these existing methods, which also learn representations for multiple reward functions.

2. Conduct a more thorough ablation study that isolates the contribution of each auxiliary task. For example, how much does pixel control contribute versus feature control versus reward prediction? The paper mentions that combining tasks leads to better results, but doesn't quantify the individual contributions.

3. Include analysis of the representations learned by the auxiliary tasks. Do the features learned by pixel control or feature control correlate with features useful for the main task? How do these representations differ from those learned by standard A3C? A visualization or statistical analysis of these representations would strengthen the paper's claim about "more effective representation."

4. Provide a more detailed discussion of the theoretical basis for why these auxiliary tasks should help with sparse reward problems. The paper states that these tasks help "remove the perceptual sparsity of rewards," but doesn't explain the mechanism by which this happens.

## 5. Clarifying Questions
To better assess the paper, the authors should address:

1. How do the representations learned by the auxiliary tasks (pixel control and feature control) differ from those learned by standard A3C? What specific features do they capture that are useful for the main task?

2. The paper claims that "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." What is the theoretical or empirical basis for this claim? How does the "control" aspect specifically help beyond just prediction?

3. The paper states that UNREAL "more than doubled the previous state-of-the-art results" on Labyrinth. What was the previous state-of-the-art, and how does UNREAL compare to it beyond just the numbers? Are there any other papers that achieved similar performance on Labyrinth?

The paper presents strong empirical results, but the lack of clear novelty and insufficient analysis to support the core claims about representation learning prevent it from meeting ICLR's standards for acceptance. With the suggested revisions, particularly a more thorough comparison to related work and a deeper analysis of the representations, this could become a stronger contribution to the field.
