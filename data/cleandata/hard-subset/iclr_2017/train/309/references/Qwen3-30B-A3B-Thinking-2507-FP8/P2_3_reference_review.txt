

# ICLR Review: UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning architecture that incorporates auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters. The authors propose three key auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control), (2) auxiliary reward prediction, and (3) experience replay mechanisms. The UNREAL agent combines these components with the A3C framework to create a unified training approach. The paper demonstrates significant improvements across two challenging domains: Labyrinth (a 3D first-person game platform) and 57 Atari games. The results show UNREAL achieves more than double the human-normalized performance of A3C on Labyrinth (87% vs. 54%) and surpasses state-of-the-art agents on Atari with 880% mean and 250% median performance. The paper provides thorough ablation studies and comparisons to relevant baselines, making a strong case for the effectiveness of their approach.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper. The key reasons for this decision are: (1) the paper makes a clear, significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by demonstrating substantial improvements through well-designed auxiliary tasks, and (2) the empirical evidence supporting the claims is rigorous and comprehensive across multiple environments and hyperparameter settings.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in addressing three critical aspects of the reviewing criteria:

**Problem Definition and Motivation (Strong):** The paper clearly identifies the problem of sparse rewards and inefficient learning in reinforcement learning, particularly in complex visual environments. The literature review (Section 1) is exceptionally thorough, placing the work in context with related approaches like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. Crucially, the authors distinguish their work from previous approaches by emphasizing that auxiliary tasks are used *solely as auxiliary objectives for developing better representations*, not for high-level planning. This precise positioning strengthens the paper's contribution.

**Methodological Rigor (Strong):** The experimental design is robust and comprehensive. The authors evaluate on two distinct domains (Labyrinth and Atari), conduct extensive ablation studies (UNREAL without pixel control, etc.), and compare against relevant baselines (pixel reconstruction, input change prediction). The use of multiple hyperparameter settings (50 for Atari, 45 for Labyrinth) across multiple random seeds (Figure 7-8) provides strong evidence of statistical significance and robustness. The paper demonstrates not just better final performance but also dramatically improved data efficiency (10× speedup in learning) and hyperparameter robustness—key metrics for practical RL applications.

**Results and Claims Support (Strong):** The empirical results directly support the claims made in the paper. The Labyrinth results (Figure 3-4) show clear performance gains across multiple level types, with the full UNREAL agent significantly outperforming all baselines. The Atari results (Figure 3-6) demonstrate consistent outperformance across all 57 games, with substantial improvements in both mean and median performance. The ablation studies (Figure 5) provide important insights into why certain auxiliary tasks are effective (e.g., learning to *control* pixel changes is better than simply predicting them), which strengthens the paper's theoretical contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance its quality:

1. **Clarify human-normalized scoring:** The paper mentions "human-normalized scores" but doesn't explain the calculation method. For reproducibility, please clarify how human performance was measured and normalized for each environment (Labyrinth and Atari).

2. **Elaborate on representation benefits:** The paper claims auxiliary tasks improve representation learning but doesn't provide concrete evidence of better representations (e.g., t-SNE visualizations of learned features, or analysis of feature similarity across tasks). Adding such analysis would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

3. **Explain computational overhead:** While the paper demonstrates performance gains, it doesn't quantify the additional computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. A brief discussion of inference time or memory requirements would be valuable for practical implementation.

4. **Clarify reward prediction mechanism:** The paper states that reward prediction is "not used for anything other than shaping the features" but doesn't fully explain how this avoids biasing the main policy. A more detailed explanation of the training procedure would strengthen the methodological contribution.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To ensure full confidence in my assessment, I'd appreciate clarification on these points:

1. In the Labyrinth experiments, you report "human-normalized scores" but don't specify how these were calculated. Could you provide the exact formula used to normalize against human performance?

2. Your ablation study (Figure 5) shows feature control is slightly worse than pixel control. Could you elaborate on why feature control might be less effective than pixel control, and whether you've explored combining these tasks in different ways?

3. The paper mentions that input reconstruction hurt final performance because "it puts too much focus on reconstructing irrelevant parts of the visual input." Can you provide more specific evidence for this claim (e.g., visualizations of what the agent was focusing on)?

4. The reward prediction task uses a skewed sampling to overrepresent rewarding events. How did you determine the optimal sampling ratio (0.5 probability of non-zero reward), and did you test other ratios?

The paper makes a significant contribution to reinforcement learning with clear, well-supported results. The UNREAL architecture represents a meaningful advancement in making RL more data-efficient and robust, with practical implications for real-world applications. The thorough experimental evaluation and strong theoretical foundation make this a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
