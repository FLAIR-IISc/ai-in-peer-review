

# ICLR Peer Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper introduces UNREAL (UNsupervised REinforcement learning), an agent architecture that significantly enhances standard reinforcement learning algorithms through the incorporation of auxiliary tasks. The authors propose two main types of auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks, including "pixel control" (learning to maximize changes in specific parts of the input image) and "feature control" (learning to maximize activation of specific hidden units in the neural network), and (2) auxiliary reward tasks, specifically "reward prediction" (predicting immediate reward from observation sequences). 

The UNREAL agent combines these auxiliary tasks with the A3C reinforcement learning algorithm, optimizing a single combined loss function. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over standard A3C across two domains: Labyrinth (a complex 3D first-person game) and Atari 57 games. UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized score on Labyrinth (vs 54% for A3C), surpasses state-of-the-art on Atari (880% mean human-normalized score), learns 10-18× faster, and shows improved robustness to hyperparameter settings. The paper includes thorough ablation studies showing individual contributions of each auxiliary task, and a detailed comparison to related work.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons for this decision:
1. The paper makes a significant empirical contribution with strong, consistent results across multiple challenging environments.
2. The proposed auxiliary tasks are well-motivated and the ablation studies convincingly demonstrate their individual and combined benefits.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper delivers on its promise with high-quality empirical results that are both substantial and scientifically rigorous. The improvements over A3C on Labyrinth (87% vs 54% human-normalized scores) and Atari (880% mean vs prior state-of-the-art) are significant and consistent across multiple environments. The data efficiency gains (10-18x faster learning) and robustness to hyperparameters are particularly noteworthy, as these are critical practical considerations for reinforcement learning.

The literature review is thorough and well-placed, clearly distinguishing the authors' approach from previous work on temporal abstractions, UVFAs, and successor representations. The authors correctly note that their auxiliary tasks are used for representation learning rather than for high-level planning, which is a key distinction from related work.

The ablation studies (Figures 4-5) provide strong evidence that each auxiliary task contributes to the overall performance. The comparison to baselines (reconstruction vs. control) is particularly convincing - the paper shows that learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes, which in turn is better than simple input reconstruction. This is a valuable insight that advances the field beyond previous work.

The paper provides sufficient implementation details (Appendix B) for reproducibility, and the experimental setup is well-described. The use of 50 hyperparameter settings across 57 Atari games, and the thorough analysis of robustness to hyperparameters (Figure 8), adds significant scientific rigor to the claims.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong, there are a few areas where it could be enhanced:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper would benefit from a more detailed theoretical discussion of why these specific auxiliary tasks are beneficial. While the empirical results are strong, a deeper analysis of how these tasks shape the representation would strengthen the paper. For example, visualizations of the representations learned with and without the auxiliary tasks would be valuable.

2. **Comparison to UVFA and Successor Representations**: The paper mentions UVFAs and successor representations in the related work but doesn't provide a detailed comparison of how UNREAL relates to these approaches. Given that the paper's auxiliary tasks are similar in spirit to UVFAs (which also use multiple value functions), a more thorough comparison would help position the work more precisely.

3. **Analysis of Representation Learning**: While the paper shows that auxiliary tasks improve performance, it doesn't provide a detailed analysis of how the representations learned with UNREAL differ from those learned with A3C. It would be interesting to see how well the representations transfer to new tasks or how the internal activations change during training.

4. **Computational Efficiency**: The paper claims improvements in data efficiency (learning faster with less data), but doesn't provide a clear analysis of the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. It would be helpful to know whether the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks is negligible or significant.

5. **Broader Impact**: The paper could benefit from a brief discussion of the broader implications of this work, particularly regarding how auxiliary tasks might be used in other domains beyond reinforcement learning.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help me better understand and assess the paper, I would appreciate clarification on the following points:

1. The paper mentions that "the agent's state st is a function of its experience up until time t." In the UNREAL architecture, how does the LSTM handle the fact that the auxiliary tasks (especially reward prediction) require processing sequences of observations? Is there any special handling for the temporal structure of the auxiliary tasks compared to the main policy?

2. In the reward prediction task, the paper states that "we sample such that zero rewards and non-zero rewards are equally represented." How does this sampling strategy affect the generalization of the reward predictor to unseen environments? Have the authors tested this on environments with different reward distributions?

3. The paper claims that "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." Could the authors provide some intuition for why this is the case? Is it because the control task encourages the agent to actively interact with the environment to create changes, whereas prediction is passive?

4. In the Labyrinth experiments, the paper mentions that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout." How does the UNREAL agent's behavior on procedural navigation levels (nav maze all random 0{1,2,3}) compare to an agent that has explicitly learned the maze layout? Could the authors provide qualitative analysis of the agent's behavior on these levels?

5. The paper uses a deconvolutional network for pixel control. How sensitive is the performance to the specific architecture of this deconvolutional network? Have the authors tried simpler or different architectures for this component?

These questions would help me better understand the technical details and the broader implications of the work. The paper is already strong, and these additions would further enhance its contribution to the field.
