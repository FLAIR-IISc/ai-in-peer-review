

# Review of "UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

## Summary
The paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that incorporates auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) to improve data efficiency and robustness. The authors claim that these auxiliary tasks help develop better representations, leading to faster learning and higher performance on both 3D Labyrinth environments and Atari games. The paper makes a well-motivated contribution by building on prior work on auxiliary tasks while introducing novel methods for incorporating them into reinforcement learning frameworks.

## Decision
Reject. While the paper presents an interesting extension to A3C with promising results, the presence of multiple minor but significant technical inconsistencies, ambiguous methodological details, and implausible numerical claims prevents me from recommending acceptance.

## Supporting Arguments

1. **Implausible Performance Claims**: The paper claims "880% mean and 250% median performance" on Atari games (Section 4.2). Human-normalized scores typically range from 0-200%, with 100% representing human performance. An 880% score is implausible and suggests a possible typo (likely 88.0% or 88%). This is a critical error that undermines the credibility of the results.

2. **Inconsistent Notation and Formatting**: The paper contains numerous minor but persistent inconsistencies:
   - Inconsistent capitalization of "UNREAL" (sometimes "UNREAL", sometimes "unreal")
   - Inconsistent spacing in "LPC" vs "L PC" in equation 2
   - Inconsistent use of "Q-learning" vs "Q learning" (Section 3.1)
   - Inconsistent reference formatting (e.g., "Barreto et al.,? \Q2016\E" appears multiple times)
   - Inconsistent use of "λPC" vs "λPC" in the loss function

3. **Ambiguous Performance Metrics**: The paper states UNREAL "achieves a mean speedup of the number of steps to reach A3C best performance of 10×" (Section 4.1.1). It's unclear whether this means:
   - UNREAL reaches A3C's final performance in 1/10th the steps
   - UNREAL reaches a better final performance in 1/10th the steps
   - UNREAL reaches its own best performance in 1/10th the steps of A3C's best performance

4. **Lack of Justification for Technical Choices**: The paper states "we use a multiclass cross-entropy classification loss across three classes (zero, positive, or negative reward)" but provides no justification for choosing three classes over two (reward or no reward). This is a non-trivial design choice that requires explanation.

5. **Insufficient Methodological Detail**: The paper mentions "the full implementation details can be found in Section B" but Section B is not included in the provided text. The main paper lacks sufficient detail to reproduce the experiments, particularly regarding the grid size for pixel control (20×20) and the selection of this specific size.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Clarify Human-Normalized Scores**: The paper claims "human-normalised score of 116% on lt hallway slope" (Section 4.1.1). While it's possible to exceed 100% human-normalized performance, this should be explicitly defined in the methodology section. The paper should clarify whether this means the agent achieved 1.16× the human score.

2. **Justify Three-Class Reward Prediction**: Provide a clear rationale for choosing three classes (zero, positive, negative reward) rather than two classes (reward or no reward). The authors should include ablation studies comparing these approaches.

3. **Fix Ambiguous Speedup Metric**: Clarify exactly how "speedup" is measured. Is it the number of steps to reach a specific performance threshold, or to reach the agent's own best performance? This is crucial for understanding the data efficiency claim.

4. **Improve Reference Formatting**: The references section contains numerous formatting inconsistencies that need to be fixed. For example, "Barreto et al.,? \Q2016\E" appears multiple times in the reference list.

5. **Correct the Atari Performance Claim**: The 880% score is likely a typo. It should be 88.0% or 88% to be credible. This must be corrected before the paper can be considered for acceptance.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact definition of "human-normalized score" used in the paper, particularly for scores exceeding 100%? How is this score calculated relative to human performance?

2. What was the specific reason for choosing a 20×20 grid for pixel control? Was this chosen through hyperparameter tuning, or is it a fixed design choice?

3. Why was the reward prediction task designed as a three-class classification (zero, positive, negative reward) rather than a two-class classification (reward or no reward)? Could you provide results comparing these two approaches?

4. How exactly was the "speedup" metric calculated? Specifically, what performance threshold was used to determine "reaching A3C best performance"?

5. Could you confirm whether the Atari performance claim of "880% mean human-normalized performance" is a typo, and if so, what the correct value should be?
