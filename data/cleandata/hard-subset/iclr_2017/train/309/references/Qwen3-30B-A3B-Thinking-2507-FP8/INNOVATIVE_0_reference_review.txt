

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent architecture that significantly improves data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness by incorporating two types of auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) and reward prediction tasks. The key insight is that these auxiliary tasks are not used for direct reward maximization but solely as auxiliary objectives to develop a more effective representation for the primary task. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL outperforms A3C by more than 50% on Labyrinth (reaching 87% human-normalized performance) and achieves state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games (880% mean human-normalized performance). The approach is novel in its specific use of auxiliary control tasks (learning to maximize pixel changes or feature activations rather than just predicting them) and the careful design of reward prediction as a representation-shaping mechanism without biasing the policy.

## 2. Decision

Accept

Key reasons for acceptance: 
1. The paper introduces a novel conceptual approach to auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning that significantly outperforms previous methods and represents a meaningful new direction in the field.
2. The empirical results are compelling and demonstrate substantial improvements across multiple environments, with clear evidence of both performance gains and data efficiency.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's core contribution is the conceptual novelty of using auxiliary control tasks (learning to maximize specific perceptual or feature changes) rather than just predicting them. This is a bold and well-motivated departure from previous work on auxiliary tasks, which typically focused on prediction rather than control. The paper clearly positions this approach within the literature, showing how it differs from related work on UVFA, successor representations, and other auxiliary methods.

The empirical results are strong and well-supported. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs. A3C's 54%), with a 10x speedup in learning and requiring less than 10% of the data. The ablation studies demonstrate that each component (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) contributes meaningfully to the overall performance, with the combination being particularly effective. The Atari results are equally impressive, showing UNREAL surpassing state-of-the-art agents across all 57 games.

What makes this paper stand out is that it doesn't just incrementally improve existing methods but introduces a fundamentally new perspective on how to use auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning. The paper's claim that "they are used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation" is not just a technical detail but a conceptual shift that has significant implications for the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, consider the following:

1. While the ablation studies show the benefits of each component, a more detailed analysis of why pixel control works better than input reconstruction or immediate reward prediction would be valuable. The paper mentions that input reconstruction hurt performance because it focuses on irrelevant visual elements, but a more concrete explanation would help readers understand this key insight.

2. The paper demonstrates robustness to hyperparameters, but it would be useful to see how the UNREAL agent performs with different network architectures or on even more challenging environments beyond Labyrinth and Atari.

3. The paper mentions that "this is an interesting direction for future research" regarding pseudo-reward maximization policies, but it would be helpful to elaborate on why this isn't pursued in the current work and what challenges might be involved.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the agent must balance improving its performance with respect to the global reward rt with improving performance on the auxiliary tasks." Could you elaborate on how this balance is achieved in practice, particularly regarding the weighting parameters λPC and λRP? How sensitive are the results to these hyperparameters?

2. The reward prediction task is designed to predict "the onset of immediate reward given some historical context." Could you provide more detail on how the training data is constructed for this task, particularly regarding the temporal window used for the input sequences?

3. The paper mentions that feature control was "only slightly worse than for pixel control." Could you share any insights into why feature control might be less effective than pixel control, or under what circumstances one might be preferred over the other?
