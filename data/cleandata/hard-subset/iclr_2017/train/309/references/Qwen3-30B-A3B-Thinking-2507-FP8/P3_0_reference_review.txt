

# ICLR Peer Review: "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent with Auxiliary Tasks for Efficient Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Tasks), a novel deep reinforcement learning architecture that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness by incorporating carefully designed auxiliary tasks. The authors propose two main types of auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that train the agent to maximize changes in specific visual features or network activations, and (2) auxiliary reward prediction tasks that train the agent to predict the occurrence of immediate rewards. Crucially, the paper argues that these auxiliary tasks serve solely to shape the agent's representation without directly biasing the main policy, which differentiates their approach from previous work.

The paper makes several strong claims supported by extensive experiments: UNREAL achieves more than double the performance of A3C on the challenging Labyrinth 3D environment (87% vs. 54% human-normalized score), with a 10x speedup in learning. On the Atari benchmark, UNREAL surpasses state-of-the-art methods across all 57 games, achieving 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance. The authors also demonstrate that UNREAL is substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C, with learning curves that show significantly less variance across different hyperparameter configurations.

The paper is well-positioned within the literature, with a thorough review that clearly distinguishes their approach from related work on options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and other auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning. The theoretical foundation for the auxiliary tasks is well-justified, particularly the insight that learning to control specific environmental aspects (rather than just predicting them) leads to more effective representation learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper addresses a meaningful and important problem in deep reinforcement learning (data efficiency and robustness) with a well-motivated approach that is clearly situated within the literature.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, rigorous, and provide strong support for the claims made, with ablation studies, multiple environments, and thorough analysis of hyperparameter robustness.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's contribution is significant because data efficiency is a critical bottleneck in deep reinforcement learning, particularly in complex environments. The UNREAL architecture represents a thoughtful extension of previous work on auxiliary tasks that effectively addresses this problem without compromising the main learning objective.

The literature review is particularly strong, with the authors correctly identifying the limitations of previous approaches. For example, they correctly note that while the Horde architecture applied RL to identify value functions for multiple pseudo-rewards, it was not used for representation learning, and each value function was trained separately. Similarly, they note that UVFA and successor representations focused on architectural choices rather than representation learning as a primary goal. This careful positioning demonstrates deep understanding of the field.

The experimental design is exemplary. The Labyrinth environment provides a challenging 3D first-person platform that tests a wide range of agent abilities, from simple fruit gathering to complex laser-tag gameplay. The Atari results on 57 games provide a standard benchmark that validates the approach across a diverse set of tasks. The ablation studies (particularly Figure 5) clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task type, showing that learning to control pixel changes is superior to simply predicting them or reconstructing the input.

The hyperparameter robustness analysis (Figure 3, Figure 7, Figure 8) is a particularly strong aspect of the paper. The authors demonstrate not just that their method works better, but that it's more robust to the choice of hyperparameters, which is a major practical advantage. The fact that UNREAL maintains consistent performance across a wide range of hyperparameter settings while A3C shows significant variance is a compelling argument for the approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, I recommend the following minor revisions to further strengthen it:

1. **Clarify the representation learning aspect:** The paper claims that the auxiliary tasks "are used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation." To strengthen this claim, it would be helpful to include some analysis of the learned representations. For example, could the authors show how the feature control tasks lead to more interpretable or task-relevant features? A simple visualization of the network activations before and after training with feature control might provide valuable insight.

2. **Explain the three-class reward prediction:** The paper uses a three-class classification (zero, positive, negative reward) for the reward prediction task, but doesn't explain why this is better than a binary classification (reward vs. no reward) or regression. A brief discussion of this design choice and evidence that it's superior would strengthen the methodological contribution.

3. **Quantify computational overhead:** The paper doesn't mention the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. It would be valuable to include a brief analysis of how much additional computation the auxiliary tasks add, and how this scales with the number of auxiliary tasks. This would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

4. **Clarify the human-normalized score claim:** The paper states that UNREAL "achieves more than twice the final human-normalized mean performance of A3C" (87% vs. 54%). However, since human-normalized scores are capped at 100%, 87% is not "more than twice" 54% (which would be 108%). This statement is mathematically inconsistent and should be revised to avoid confusion.

5. **Discuss failure cases:** The paper presents strong results, but it would be valuable to include a brief discussion of where UNREAL still struggles, particularly on the most challenging Labyrinth levels. This would provide a more balanced perspective on the method's limitations.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In the paper, you state that "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." Could you elaborate on why this is the case? Is it related to the fact that control requires the agent to understand the dynamics of the environment, whereas prediction only requires pattern recognition?

2. The reward prediction task uses a three-class classification (zero, positive, negative reward). Could you provide evidence that this approach is superior to a binary classification (reward vs. no reward) or a regression approach? Did you test these alternatives?

3. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task is trained on sequences of three observations. Why three specifically? What happens if you use longer sequences?

4. The paper states that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout and then exploit that knowledge to repeatedly return to the goal." How does the UNREAL agent's exploration strategy differ from the baseline A3C agent in these navigation tasks? Does the auxiliary reward prediction task specifically improve exploration?

5. The paper claims that UNREAL is "substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings" than A3C. Could you provide a quantitative measure of this robustness (e.g., the variance of performance across hyperparameter settings) to complement the qualitative analysis in Figure 8?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The proposed UNREAL architecture is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and demonstrates impressive improvements over previous methods. With the minor revisions suggested above, I believe this paper would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
