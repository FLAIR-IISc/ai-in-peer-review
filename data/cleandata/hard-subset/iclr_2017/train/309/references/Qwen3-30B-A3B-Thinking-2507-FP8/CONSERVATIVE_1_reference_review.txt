

# Review of "UNREAL: An Architecture for General Intelligence"

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

The paper proposes the UNREAL architecture, which integrates auxiliary tasks (auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction tasks) into reinforcement learning to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness. The authors claim that by training the agent to control pixel changes and network features (auxiliary control tasks) and to predict immediate rewards (auxiliary reward tasks), the representation learned by the agent becomes more effective, leading to superior performance on both 3D Labyrinth environments and Atari games. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL achieves more than double the human-normalized scores compared to A3C on Labyrinth (87% vs 54%) and significantly outperforms A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN on Atari games (880% mean vs 250% for the previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the results are promising, the paper lacks sufficient evidence to support its claims of significant improvement over state-of-the-art methods, and the theoretical justification for the auxiliary tasks is insufficient.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The primary reason for rejection is that the paper fails to properly benchmark against the most recent state-of-the-art methods. The paper claims UNREAL "surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents, i.e. A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN" on Atari, but Prioritized Dueling DQN (Wang et al., 2016) is now over six years old. More recent state-of-the-art methods like IMPALA, R2D2, SAC, and PPO should be compared against, especially since these have been shown to significantly outperform A3C on Atari games. The paper's claim that UNREAL "more than doubled the previous state-of-the-art results" on Labyrinth is also questionable without proper comparison to the most recent Labyrinth benchmarks.

Additionally, the paper provides insufficient theoretical justification for the auxiliary tasks. While the authors reference related work on successor representations and UVFAs, they don't provide a clear theoretical foundation for why their specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control and feature control) should improve representation learning. The paper shows empirical results, but doesn't provide sufficient analysis of what the auxiliary tasks are actually learning or how they contribute to better representations. The ablation studies are limited to removing the pixel control component, but don't explore the effects of different combinations or the interaction between auxiliary tasks.

The paper also lacks sufficient evidence of the method's robustness across diverse environments. The experiments are limited to Labyrinth (a single 3D game platform) and Atari (a standard benchmark), but don't test the approach on more challenging or diverse domains where the benefits of auxiliary tasks might be less clear.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:

1. Compare against more recent state-of-the-art methods on both Atari and Labyrinth, not just A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. This is particularly important since the paper claims to surpass the current state-of-the-art.

2. Provide deeper analysis of the representations learned by the auxiliary tasks. How do the representations differ from those learned by A3C? What specific features do the auxiliary tasks help the agent learn that A3C misses?

3. Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies. The paper only removes the pixel control component, but doesn't explore the relative importance of different auxiliary tasks or their interactions.

4. Provide more detailed analysis of data efficiency. The paper claims UNREAL requires "less than 10% of the data" to reach A3C's final performance, but doesn't provide specific numbers or plots showing the data efficiency curve.

5. Address the theoretical basis for the auxiliary tasks. Why should controlling pixel changes or network features lead to better representations for the main task? The paper mentions this is an interesting direction for future research but doesn't provide any justification for the current approach.

## Questions for Authors

1. How does UNREAL compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods like IMPALA, R2D2, or SAC on Atari games? These methods have shown significant improvements over A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN.

2. What specific features or representations does the pixel control task help the agent learn that are useful for the main task? Can you provide visualizations or feature analysis to demonstrate this?

3. In the Labyrinth experiments, the paper claims a 10x speedup in learning. How does this translate to actual environment steps? Could you provide the exact number of environment steps required to reach 90% of final performance for both A3C and UNREAL?

4. The paper states that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." What is the theoretical basis for this claim, and why would reconstruction hurt final performance?

5. Why does the paper use a skewed sampling strategy for reward prediction (equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards) but not for the auxiliary control tasks? What is the theoretical justification for this difference?

These questions are necessary to understand whether the claimed improvements are truly significant and generalizable, or whether they are simply a result of specific experimental choices that might not generalize to other domains or recent methods.
