

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims/Contribution

The paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), a novel reinforcement learning architecture that significantly improves data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters by incorporating multiple auxiliary tasks. The authors propose a unified framework that combines three specific auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that learn to maximize changes in visual input or network features, (2) reward prediction tasks that predict immediate rewards to shape the agent's representation, and (3) value function replay that leverages experience replay to improve data efficiency. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that these auxiliary tasks, when combined effectively, substantially outperform the A3C baseline across two challenging environments: a complex 3D first-person game platform called Labyrinth and the Atari 57 game suite. The results show that UNREAL achieves more than double the human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (54% to 87%) and significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on Atari, while being substantially more data-efficient (requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance) and more robust to hyperparameter settings. The paper provides a thorough literature review, clear methodology, and extensive ablation studies to support their claims.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

The paper should be accepted for ICLR. The key reasons for this decision are:
1. The paper addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning (data efficiency and robustness) with a well-motivated approach that builds on and extends previous work in a clear and principled way.
2. The empirical results are strong, comprehensive, and scientifically rigorous, showing significant improvements across multiple environments and metrics.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper makes a compelling case for the effectiveness of auxiliary tasks in improving reinforcement learning. The authors have carefully positioned their work within the literature, showing how their approach differs from previous methods like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. They specifically note that previous work has focused on using temporal abstractions for high-level learning and planning, whereas their approach uses auxiliary tasks solely for representation learning.

The experimental evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The Labyrinth environment provides a challenging 3D first-person platform with rich visual complexity, and the Atari experiments cover 57 games, providing a broad benchmark. The ablation studies (e.g., Figure 5 showing that learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting them) provide strong evidence that the specific auxiliary tasks proposed are indeed beneficial.

The results are statistically significant: UNREAL achieves more than double the human-normalized performance on Labyrinth, with a mean speedup of 10x in learning (requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance). The robustness to hyperparameters is particularly impressive, with UNREAL outperforming A3C across a wide range of hyperparameter settings (Figure 3 Top Right).

The paper also provides a clear explanation of how the auxiliary tasks are implemented and integrated into the A3C framework, with Figure 1 providing a helpful visual overview. The technical details in Appendix B are sufficiently complete for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the relationship between auxiliary tasks and representation learning**: While the paper states that auxiliary tasks are used "solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation," it would be helpful to provide more explicit analysis of how the representation changes as a result of these auxiliary tasks. For example, showing feature visualization or dimensionality reduction of the representation at different training stages could provide stronger evidence for this claim.

2. **Discuss computational overhead**: The paper mentions that UNREAL is more data-efficient, but doesn't discuss the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks. For real-world applications, it's important to know if the additional computation per step is justified by the improved data efficiency. A comparison of wall-clock time (not just environment steps) would be valuable.

3. **Expand on the reward prediction task**: The paper describes reward prediction as predicting "the onset of immediate reward," but it's not entirely clear how this differs from standard value learning. A more detailed discussion of the theoretical relationship between reward prediction and value learning would strengthen the paper.

4. **Include more discussion of failure cases**: While the paper shows strong positive results, it would be helpful to discuss any environments or tasks where UNREAL did not perform well, and why. This would provide a more balanced view of the approach's limitations.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that the reward prediction task is trained with a skewed sampling approach (to overrepresent rewarding events), but doesn't discuss how this might affect the agent's exploration strategy. Could this potentially lead to suboptimal exploration in some environments, and if so, how does UNREAL mitigate this?

2. The paper claims that UNREAL requires "less than 10% of the data" to reach A3C's final performance. Could the authors clarify whether this is measured in terms of environment steps, training updates, or another metric? This would help readers better understand the data efficiency claim.

3. In the Labyrinth experiments, the authors report human-normalized scores of 116% on lt hallway slope and 100% on nav maze random goal 02. Could the authors provide more context about what these scores mean in terms of actual human performance? For example, is 100% meaning the agent performed as well as the best human player, or is it normalized relative to the best human performance in some way?

4. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task uses a multiclass classification loss across three classes (zero, positive, or negative reward), but doesn't discuss why they chose this approach over a regression loss. What was the rationale behind this choice, and would a different loss function yield similar results?
