

# Review of "UNREAL: Curriculum Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contribution
This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning architecture that incorporates auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) alongside reward prediction tasks to significantly improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness. The key contribution is the insight that learning to actively control aspects of the environment (rather than simply predicting them) creates more useful representations for the main task. The authors demonstrate that UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (achieving 87% human-normalized performance, doubling previous results) and 57 Atari games (880% mean human-normalized performance), while also being significantly more robust to hyperparameter choices.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper presents a novel approach to auxiliary task design in reinforcement learning that is well-motivated and strongly supported by empirical results. The core idea of using auxiliary control tasks (rather than just prediction tasks) represents a significant conceptual advance over previous work on auxiliary tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper makes a compelling case for the novelty and significance of its approach. Unlike previous work that used auxiliary tasks for direct reward maximization (e.g., options, UVFA), UNREAL uses these tasks solely as representation learning tools, which is a fresh perspective. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work in the literature, particularly highlighting how their pixel control tasks differ from pixel reconstruction (Kulkarni et al., 2016) and input change prediction.

The empirical results are impressive and rigorously presented. The paper includes comprehensive ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary task component, with the full UNREAL agent achieving 10-18x faster learning speed and requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. The robustness analysis across hyperparameters is particularly convincing, showing UNREAL consistently outperforms A3C across different learning rates and entropy costs.

The paper also makes an important conceptual contribution by addressing how to incorporate auxiliary tasks without biasing the main policy. By using reward prediction as a feature-shaping tool rather than a direct reward signal, the authors avoid the common pitfall of auxiliary tasks introducing bias to the main learning process.

## 4. Additional Feedback and Questions
To further strengthen this already strong paper, I'd suggest:

- A more detailed theoretical justification for why control tasks work better than prediction tasks. The ablation study shows pixel control > input change prediction > input reconstruction, but a deeper explanation of why active control leads to better representations would be valuable.

- More concrete analysis of the computational cost of UNREAL versus A3C. While the paper demonstrates significant performance gains, it would be helpful to know if the additional computation is justified in practical applications.

- A more thorough comparison of feature control versus pixel control across different environments. The paper mentions feature control was "only slightly worse" than pixel control but doesn't provide a detailed breakdown of when one might be preferable to the other.

Questions for authors:
1. Can you provide more theoretical justification for why auxiliary control tasks (learning to change pixels) work better than simple prediction tasks (predicting pixel changes)? Is there a connection to intrinsic motivation or curiosity mechanisms in reinforcement learning?

2. The paper states that UNREAL achieves "more than twice the final human-normalised mean performance of A3C" on Labyrinth, but doesn't specify if this is measured against the same evaluation protocol as previous work. Could you clarify if these results are directly comparable to previous state-of-the-art on Labyrinth?

3. In the reward prediction task, you use a multiclass classification loss across three classes (zero, positive, or negative reward). Could you explain why you chose this approach rather than a regression-based loss, and how it compares to alternative formulations?

4. The paper mentions that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout" in procedural navigation levels, but doesn't provide evidence that UNREAL actually learns this exploration strategy. Could you elaborate on how the auxiliary tasks specifically enable this kind of efficient exploration?

This paper represents a significant advance in the field of reinforcement learning with a novel approach that has been rigorously validated across multiple environments. The core idea of using auxiliary control tasks for representation learning is bold, well-motivated, and clearly superior to previous methods, making it a strong candidate for acceptance at ICLR.
