

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Rewards), a novel framework that significantly enhances reinforcement learning by incorporating three specific auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize changes in specific visual regions (pixel control) or network activations (feature control), (2) reward prediction tasks that anticipate immediate rewards from visual sequences, and (3) experience replay mechanisms tailored to these auxiliary tasks. The authors demonstrate that this combination substantially improves data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance across both 3D Labyrinth environments and 57 Atari games. The key contribution is not merely adding auxiliary tasks, but designing them specifically to shape the agent's internal representation in ways that benefit the main task, rather than using them as direct value function approximations.

## 2. Decision

Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments

This paper presents a significant contribution to reinforcement learning by introducing a novel combination of auxiliary tasks that fundamentally changes how representations are shaped. The authors clearly distinguish their approach from related work on temporal abstractions (options), value function approximation (UVFAs, successor representations), and auxiliary tasks (Lample & Chaplot, 2016; Mirowski et al., 2016), positioning their work as a new direction in representation learning rather than an incremental improvement.

The empirical evidence is exceptionally strong, with clear demonstrations of substantial gains across multiple environments. On Labyrinth, UNREAL more than doubles the performance of A3C (87% vs. 54% human-normalized score) and achieves a 10x speedup in learning. On Atari, UNREAL significantly outperforms both A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN across all 57 games. The ablation studies (Figure 5) are particularly compelling, showing that learning to control pixel changes is substantially better than simply predicting them, and that the feature control auxiliary task provides significant benefits.

As a reviewer who prioritizes novelty over methodological perfection, I find this paper's core idea—using auxiliary tasks specifically designed to shape the agent's internal representation through control rather than prediction—to be bold and conceptually significant. The paper demonstrates a clear new direction in reinforcement learning that could influence future work, rather than merely making incremental improvements to existing approaches.

## 4. Additional Feedback

- The paper would benefit from a more detailed comparison with the successor representation work by Barreto et al. (2016), as the authors mention their approach is related but don't clearly distinguish how their representation learning approach differs in a fundamental way.

- Including visualizations of the representations learned through the auxiliary tasks (e.g., what specific visual features the pixel control task learns to manipulate) would strengthen the claims about representation learning.

- While the computational cost is mentioned in the implementation details, a more explicit discussion of the trade-offs between the benefits of the auxiliary tasks and the increased computational requirements would be valuable.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper shows that feature control is slightly less effective than pixel control (Figure 5). Could you elaborate on why this might be the case, and whether you believe there are specific scenarios where feature control would be preferable?

2. The authors state that the reward prediction task "does not introduce bias to the agent's policy" because it's not used for anything other than shaping features. How do you reconcile this with the fact that the reward prediction task is trained on a skewed distribution (equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards)?

3. The paper mentions that "reward is encountered very sparsely" in many environments, which motivates the auxiliary tasks. How would the UNREAL approach perform in environments where rewards are not sparse but still challenging (e.g., continuous control tasks with dense rewards but high-dimensional state spaces)?
