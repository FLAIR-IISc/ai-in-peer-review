

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Rewards), a novel reinforcement learning framework that significantly improves the data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness of deep RL agents through the incorporation of auxiliary tasks. The authors make a compelling argument that current deep RL methods struggle with sparse rewards, slow learning, and hyperparameter sensitivity, and propose that auxiliary tasks can help shape the agent's representation without biasing the main policy.

The key contributions include:
- Introduction of two novel auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that learn to maximize changes in visual input or network activations
- A reward prediction task that predicts immediate rewards to overcome perceptual sparsity
- A unified architecture (UNREAL) that combines these auxiliary tasks with the standard A3C algorithm
- Comprehensive empirical validation across two challenging domains: Labyrinth (a 3D first-person game platform) and 57 Atari games

The paper demonstrates that UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C in both environments, achieving 87% human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (compared to 54% for A3C) and 880% mean human-normalized performance on Atari (surpassing state-of-the-art methods). UNREAL also shows dramatic improvements in data efficiency (requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's performance) and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

The authors provide a thorough literature review that properly situates their work within the context of previous approaches like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations, while clearly articulating the limitations of these approaches and how UNREAL addresses them.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

The paper makes a strong, well-motivated contribution to reinforcement learning that is clearly articulated and well-supported by empirical evidence. The results are impressive and the approach is both novel and practical for implementation. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a significant contribution to the field.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper strongly supports its claims through rigorous experimentation across two distinct environments with comprehensive ablation studies. The results are statistically significant, with clear improvements in both performance and data efficiency. The paper's claims are well-placed in the literature, with a thorough review that accurately identifies the limitations of previous work and positions UNREAL as a meaningful advancement.

The most compelling aspects of the paper are:
1. The clear demonstration that auxiliary tasks can substantially improve data efficiency (10x faster learning) without requiring any changes to the core RL algorithm
2. The convincing evidence that the auxiliary tasks improve robustness to hyperparameter choices, a critical practical concern in RL
3. The careful design of auxiliary tasks that don't bias the main policy (unlike many previous approaches)
4. The thorough ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each component

The paper's experimental design is strong, with proper baselines, hyperparameter tuning, and multiple evaluation metrics. The human-normalized scores provide a meaningful benchmark for comparison.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong as is, the following points could further enhance its quality:

1. **Clarify the distinction between auxiliary control and prediction**: The unsupervised reinforcement learning section (4.1.1) shows that learning to control (pixel control) is better than simply predicting (input change prediction), which in turn is better than reconstruction. However, the paper could better explain *why* this is the case. What specific properties of the control task make it more beneficial for representation learning than prediction? This would strengthen the theoretical foundation of the approach.

2. **More detail on the hyperparameter robustness**: The paper claims UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters, but doesn't provide quantitative metrics on this robustness (e.g., standard deviation of performance across hyperparameter settings). Including this would make the claim more concrete.

3. **Clarify the human-normalized scoring**: The paper uses "human-normalized scores" throughout but doesn't explain how these scores are calculated (e.g., what is the human baseline, how is it normalized). A brief clarification would improve reproducibility.

4. **Compare with UVFA and successor representations**: While the paper mentions these approaches in the related work, it would be helpful to have a more direct comparison of how UNREAL differs from these methods, especially since the authors claim their approach is "not used for representation learning" unlike UVFA.

5. **Address potential limitations**: The paper could briefly discuss potential limitations of the approach, such as whether the auxiliary tasks might become too specialized or whether the approach would scale to more complex environments with different types of sparsity.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

To help clarify some aspects of the paper and strengthen the claims, I would like the authors to address the following questions:

1. In the Labyrinth experiments, you note that "rewarding objects are rarely visible" and that input reconstruction hurts performance. Could you elaborate on why this is the case and whether this is a general limitation of reconstruction-based approaches in visual RL?

2. The paper states that feature control is "only slightly worse than pixel control" but doesn't provide specific quantitative comparisons. Could you provide the exact performance difference between these two auxiliary tasks on the Labyrinth levels?

3. In Figure 5, the input change prediction baseline (which predicts immediate pixel changes) outperforms reconstruction but is still worse than pixel control. What specific properties of the pixel control task make it more effective for representation learning than simply predicting the immediate change?

4. The paper mentions that UNREAL "surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents, i.e. A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN" on Atari. Could you provide more specific comparisons between UNREAL and Prioritized Dueling DQN, including any potential implementation differences that might affect the comparison?

5. The reward prediction task uses a multiclass classification loss (zero, positive, negative rewards) rather than a regression loss. What were the results of trying a regression loss instead, and why did the authors choose classification?

These questions would help clarify the theoretical underpinnings of the approach and provide additional evidence for the claims made in the paper.
