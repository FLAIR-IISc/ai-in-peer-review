

# Review of "UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL, an extension of the A3C reinforcement learning algorithm that incorporates multiple auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and performance. The authors claim that by adding auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) and reward prediction tasks, their approach significantly outperforms A3C on both the 3D Labyrinth environment and 57 Atari games. The paper presents empirical results showing UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized mean performance on Labyrinth (compared to 54% for A3C) and 880% mean performance on Atari (compared to previous state-of-the-art).

## 2. Decision

Reject. While the paper presents some promising empirical results, the contribution lacks sufficient novelty, and the evidence provided does not conclusively demonstrate the value of the specific auxiliary tasks proposed.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main contribution appears to be a combination of established techniques rather than a fundamentally new method. The auxiliary tasks described (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) have all been explored in previous work (Kulkarni et al., 2016; Lample & Chaplot, 2016; Mirowski et al., 2016). The paper's claim of "unsupervised" learning is misleading since the auxiliary tasks are specifically designed to be informative for the main task.

The experimental evidence provided is insufficient to support the paper's claims. While UNREAL outperforms A3C in the reported experiments, the paper lacks a thorough ablation study to isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task. Section 4.1.1 does provide some comparison between auxiliary tasks, but the results are limited to three Labyrinth levels and do not conclusively demonstrate that the specific auxiliary tasks proposed are superior to other existing approaches.

Furthermore, the claim that UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters is not sufficiently supported. The paper shows that UNREAL achieves better performance across a wider range of hyperparameters, but the evidence is limited to a single metric (human-normalized scores) without proper statistical analysis. The authors do not provide confidence intervals, p-values, or other statistical measures to validate their robustness claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To strengthen this paper, the authors should:

1. Provide a more thorough ablation study that isolates the contribution of each auxiliary task across multiple environments. This would help determine whether the improvements are due to the specific auxiliary tasks or simply better hyperparameter tuning.

2. Clarify the "unsupervised" aspect of their approach. If the auxiliary tasks are designed to be informative for the main task, they are not truly unsupervised. The authors should either revise their terminology or provide a stronger theoretical justification for their claims.

3. Include more detailed statistical analysis to support their robustness claims. Simply showing performance curves across hyperparameters is insufficient; the authors should provide statistical significance tests.

4. Address why their specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control) are better than alternatives like input reconstruction or input change prediction. The current evidence in Section 4.1.1 is limited and does not conclusively demonstrate superiority.

5. Provide a more detailed discussion of how their approach differs from the UVFA and successor representation methods they cite in the related work. The paper positions itself as building on these methods, but the specific innovation is not clearly articulated.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper claims that the auxiliary tasks are "unsupervised," but they're specifically designed to be informative for the main task. Can you clarify the theoretical basis for this claim and how it differs from standard auxiliary tasks used in RL?

2. In Section 4.1.1, the authors compare their approach to input reconstruction and input change prediction. However, the paper doesn't provide a clear explanation for why learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting them. Can you provide more theoretical or empirical justification for this design choice?

3. The paper claims that UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters, but the evidence provided is limited to a single metric (human-normalized performance) across hyperparameter settings. Can you provide more detailed statistical analysis to support this claim, such as confidence intervals or p-values for the robustness comparisons?

4. The paper states that the UNREAL agent "more than doubled the previous state-of-the-art results" on Labyrinth. However, the previous state-of-the-art is not clearly established. What was the previous best result on Labyrinth before this work, and how does it compare to the results reported in this paper?
