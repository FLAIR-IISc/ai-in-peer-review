

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contribution

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that incorporates auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, robustness, and performance in challenging environments. The authors propose three key auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control), auxiliary reward prediction, and value function replay. The paper demonstrates that these auxiliary tasks significantly enhance learning in both 3D environments (Labyrinth) and Atari games, with the UNREAL agent achieving more than double the human-normalized performance of the A3C baseline on Labyrinth (87% vs. 54%) and surpassing state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution with scientifically rigorous experiments that clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed auxiliary tasks. The results are impressive and well-supported by thorough ablation studies and robustness analyses.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning: the challenge of sparse rewards in complex environments. The authors provide a strong literature review that situates their work within the broader context of auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning, clearly distinguishing their approach from prior work (e.g., UVFAs, successor representations, Horde). Their key innovation is the use of auxiliary tasks as representation learning tools rather than for direct reward maximization.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors present comprehensive evaluations on two distinct benchmark environments (Labyrinth and Atari) with multiple metrics (mean performance, median performance, data efficiency, hyperparameter robustness). The ablation studies provide clear evidence that each auxiliary task contributes to the overall improvement, and the robustness analysis across hyperparameters strengthens the claims about the approach's practical utility.

The paper demonstrates a 10x speedup in learning on Labyrinth (11x median) and requires less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. The Atari results are particularly compelling, showing UNREAL surpassing state-of-the-art agents across all 57 games with 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, the authors could:
- Provide more theoretical justification for why these specific auxiliary tasks are effective, beyond empirical results.
- Include a deeper analysis of the representations learned by the agent with and without auxiliary tasks to better understand how they improve the agent's ability to recognize rewarding states.
- Clarify the relationship between the auxiliary tasks and the underlying mechanisms that lead to improved performance.
- Discuss the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks, which would be valuable for practical implementation considerations.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper shows that learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes, which in turn is better than input reconstruction. Can the authors provide more insight into why these specific auxiliary tasks work better than alternatives, beyond empirical results?

2. The paper demonstrates significant improvements on Labyrinth and Atari, but how do the authors think the proposed approach would generalize to environments with different reward structures or sparsity patterns beyond those tested?

3. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task is trained with a skewed sampling to overrepresent rewarding events. How sensitive are the results to the exact sampling strategy, and what would happen if the sampling were not skewed?
