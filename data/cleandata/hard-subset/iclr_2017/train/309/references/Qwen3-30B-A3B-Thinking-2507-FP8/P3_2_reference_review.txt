

# Peer Review: UNREAL: Auxiliary Reinforcement Learning

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), a novel reinforcement learning architecture that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness by incorporating auxiliary tasks focused on representation learning rather than direct reward maximization. The authors propose three key auxiliary tasks: (1) pixel control (learning to maximize changes in specific image regions), (2) feature control (learning to maximize activation of specific network units), and (3) reward prediction (predicting immediate rewards to shape representations). The paper makes a clear and well-motivated contribution to the field, demonstrating that auxiliary tasks can be used to develop more effective representations, which in turn improves learning on both 3D environments (Labyrinth) and Atari games. The empirical results are compelling, showing UNREAL achieves over 87% human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (more than double A3C's performance) and significantly outperforms prior state-of-the-art on 57 Atari games.

## 2. Decision

**Accept with minor revisions.**

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by demonstrating how auxiliary tasks can be effectively used to improve representation learning, which is a fundamental challenge in RL.
2. The experimental results are comprehensive, rigorous, and well-designed, with thorough ablation studies and multiple hyperparameter settings evaluated.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper is well-positioned in the literature and makes a clear contribution to the field. The authors provide an excellent literature review that clearly differentiates their approach from prior work on auxiliary tasks (e.g., Horde, UVFA, successor representations) by emphasizing their focus on representation learning rather than direct reward maximization.

The empirical evidence strongly supports the claims:
- On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized mean performance (vs. 54% for A3C), with a 10× speedup in learning and requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance.
- On Atari, UNREAL achieves 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, surpassing both A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN.
- The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task component.
- The robustness analysis shows UNREAL is significantly less sensitive to hyperparameter choices than A3C.

The paper's main contribution is its clear demonstration that auxiliary tasks can be used to shape representations in a way that benefits the primary task without introducing reward bias, which is a novel and important perspective in the field.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, I recommend the following improvements to enhance its impact:

1. **Clarify the relationship with successor representations**: The paper references Barreto et al. (2016) on successor representations, but doesn't clearly explain how UNREAL relates to or differs from this work. Successor representations also factor continuous sets of value functions, but UNREAL uses auxiliary tasks for representation learning rather than directly computing successor features. A more detailed comparison would strengthen the paper's positioning.

2. **Provide more insight into the representation learning**: The paper claims UNREAL develops better representations, but doesn't provide evidence of what these representations look like or how they differ from A3C's representations. A simple visualization of feature activations or t-SNE plots of the learned representations would greatly strengthen the argument.

3. **Clarify the computational cost**: The paper doesn't discuss the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks. How much more compute time does UNREAL require per step compared to A3C? The paper mentions faster learning, but if the computational cost per step is significantly higher, this could be a limiting factor for practical applications.

4. **Expand the discussion of feature control**: The paper mentions feature control as a promising direction but doesn't provide enough detail on how it differs from pixel control. It would be helpful to clarify what specific features are being controlled (e.g., are they high-level features or low-level features?) and why this approach is valuable.

5. **Include more comparison to prior work on auxiliary tasks**: While the literature review is good, the paper could better position itself against other recent work on auxiliary tasks (e.g., Lample & Chaplot, 2016; Mirowski et al., 2016) by explaining how UNREAL's approach differs.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. The paper states that "input reconstruction hurts final performance because it puts too much focus on reconstructing irrelevant parts of the visual input." Could you provide more specific evidence for this claim? For example, could you share a visualization showing what parts of the input the reconstruction task focuses on versus what the pixel control task focuses on?

2. In the reward prediction task, the paper uses a multiclass classification with three classes (zero, positive, negative reward). Why was this chosen over a regression approach? How would the results change if you used a regression loss instead?

3. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task is trained on sequences with skewed sampling (equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards). Could you provide more detail on how this sampling was implemented? Specifically, did you use the same replay buffer for both the primary task and reward prediction, or did you maintain separate replay buffers?

4. The paper states that "feature control was only slightly worse than pixel control" but doesn't provide specific numbers. Could you include the exact performance metrics for feature control on Labyrinth levels in the main paper or supplementary material?

5. How does the UNREAL agent perform on environments with even sparser rewards than those tested in the paper (e.g., environments where rewards occur only once every 1000 steps)? The paper mentions sparse rewards as motivation, but doesn't test environments with extreme sparsity.

The paper is strong and makes a valuable contribution to the field. With these minor clarifications and additions, it would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
