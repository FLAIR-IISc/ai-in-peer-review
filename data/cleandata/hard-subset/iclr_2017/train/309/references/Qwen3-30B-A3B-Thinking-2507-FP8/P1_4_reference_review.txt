

# Peer Review: UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), an approach that significantly enhances deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters. The authors propose a novel architecture that combines three key auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks that train agents to maximize pixel changes or network feature activations, (2) reward prediction tasks that anticipate immediate rewards, and (3) experience replay mechanisms to optimize learning from both rewarding and non-rewarding states. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that these auxiliary tasks, when properly integrated with the main reinforcement learning objective, substantially improve performance without directly optimizing for the main reward. The authors show that UNREAL achieves over 87% human-normalized performance on challenging 3D Labyrinth environments (more than doubling A3C's performance) and achieves state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games, with 880% mean human-normalized performance.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**

Key reasons: (1) The paper presents a well-motivated approach with strong empirical evidence across two distinct domains (Labyrinth and Atari), and (2) the results demonstrate substantial improvements in both performance and data efficiency that are scientifically rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in several critical areas. First, it provides a thorough and well-structured literature review that clearly positions the work within the broader context of reinforcement learning, distinguishing UNREAL from prior approaches like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. The authors effectively argue that their approach differs fundamentally from previous work by using auxiliary tasks solely as representation learning tools rather than as direct policy optimizers.

Second, the experimental design is robust and comprehensive. The authors conducted extensive ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary component, compared against meaningful baselines (including input reconstruction and immediate reward prediction), and evaluated performance across multiple hyperparameter settings. The Labyrinth experiments are particularly impressive, with 13 diverse levels testing different aspects of agent capability (fruit gathering, navigation, exploration, and laser-tag gameplay). The Atari results are equally strong, with UNREAL outperforming both A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN across all 57 games.

Third, the paper demonstrates scientific rigor in how it measures improvements. The human-normalized scores provide a meaningful benchmark, and the data efficiency analysis (requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance) is particularly compelling. The robustness analysis across hyperparameters (shown in Figure 3 top right and Figure 8) is especially valuable for practical applications, as it demonstrates UNREAL's stability across different training conditions.

The authors also provide a thoughtful explanation for why their approach works, suggesting that auxiliary control tasks help shape representations to focus on relevant visual features rather than irrelevant ones (as seen in the comparison with input reconstruction). This theoretical insight is well-supported by the empirical results.

## 4. Additional Feedback

To further strengthen the paper, I recommend:

1. **Clarify the relationship between auxiliary control tasks and successor representations**: The paper mentions successor representations in the related work but doesn't explicitly connect how UNREAL's auxiliary control tasks relate to this line of work. A brief discussion would help position UNREAL within this established framework.

2. **Provide more detail on the computational overhead**: The paper mentions that the auxiliary tasks are trained on very recent sequences from the replay buffer, but it would be helpful to quantify the additional computational cost (e.g., percentage increase in training time) compared to the base A3C agent.

3. **Discuss limitations**: While the paper is strong in demonstrating improvements, it would be valuable to briefly acknowledge potential limitations of the approach (e.g., whether it generalizes to even more complex environments, or whether there are specific task types where auxiliary tasks might be less beneficial).

4. **Expand on the reward prediction task**: The paper mentions that reward prediction is trained with a multiclass loss across three classes (zero, positive, negative reward), but doesn't elaborate on why this specific formulation was chosen over alternatives (e.g., regression). A brief justification would strengthen this component.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. In the Labyrinth experiments, the authors note that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." What specific aspects of the visual input do you think the reconstruction task focused on that were detrimental to performance? Could you elaborate on your hypothesis about "rewarding objects being rarely visible"?

2. The paper states that "the same UNREAL architecture also significantly improved both the learning speed and the robustness of A3C over 57 Atari games." Could you provide more specific details about how robustness was measured (beyond what's shown in Figure 3 and 8)? For instance, was it measured by the standard deviation of performance across hyperparameter settings, or by the percentage of hyperparameter configurations that achieved at least X% of the best performance?

3. The paper mentions that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout and then exploit that knowledge." How does the UNREAL agent's learning process differ from a naive exploration strategy, and what specific aspects of the auxiliary tasks enable this more efficient exploration?

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the approach has clear potential for broader application. I recommend acceptance.
