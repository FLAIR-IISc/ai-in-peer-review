

# Review

## Summary of Claims and Contribution

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness by incorporating auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) and reward prediction tasks. The authors demonstrate that combining these auxiliary tasks with A3C leads to substantial performance improvements across both Labyrinth (a 3D first-person game) and Atari 57 games, achieving 87% human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (more than doubling A3C's performance) and 880% mean human-normalized performance on Atari. The paper makes a compelling case for how auxiliary tasks can shape representations more effectively than traditional approaches.

## Decision

Reject

## Key Reasons for Rejection

1. **Consistent formatting and typographical errors throughout the paper** - The paper contains numerous inconsistent formatting issues, including inconsistent citation styles (e.g., "Sutton et al., 1999b" in text vs. "Sutton et al., 1999" in references), inconsistent spacing around mathematical symbols (e.g., "γ n" instead of "γ^n"), inconsistent use of British vs. American spelling ("maximise" vs. "maximize"), and inconsistent hyphenation ("human-normalised" vs. "human-normalized"). These are not merely cosmetic but undermine the paper's credibility as a carefully written academic contribution.

2. **Mathematical notation inconsistencies** - The paper contains multiple inconsistent mathematical notations, including "γ n" instead of "γ^n", "θ−" instead of "θ^−", "P (rτ 6= 0)" instead of "P(r_τ ≠ 0)", and inconsistent spacing in summation indices (e.g., "∑n i=1" instead of "∑_{i=1}^n"). These inconsistencies make the paper appear sloppy and could lead to misinterpretation of the technical content.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's technical content is strong and presents a novel approach with promising results. However, as a perfectionist reviewer, I must emphasize that the consistent presentation of mathematical notation and formal writing style is a fundamental expectation for ICLR. The numerous minor formatting inconsistencies across the paper (including in the references section where entries have strange formatting like "\Q2016\E" instead of "2016") suggest a lack of careful proofreading that is unacceptable for a top-tier conference like ICLR.

The paper's claims about the UNREAL agent's performance are supported by empirical results, but the presentation issues make it difficult to assess the technical details with confidence. For instance, the inconsistent mathematical notation could lead to misinterpretation of the loss function (Equation 2) or the reward prediction task (Section 3.2). The paper's contribution is significant, but these minor issues collectively undermine the paper's overall quality and presentation.

## Additional Feedback

1. **Formatting consistency**: Please ensure consistent use of British/American spelling throughout the paper. The paper currently uses a mix of "maximise" and "maximize," "focussed" and "focused," and "normalised" and "normalized." Choose one style and apply it consistently.

2. **Mathematical notation**: Standardize all mathematical notation. Use proper superscripts (e.g., γ^n), consistent spacing around operators, and proper subscripts/superscripts. For example:
   - "γ n" should be "γ^n"
   - "θ−" should be "θ^-"
   - "P (rτ 6= 0)" should be "P(r_τ ≠ 0)"
   - "∑n i=1" should be "∑_{i=1}^n"

3. **Reference formatting**: The references contain inconsistent and incorrect formatting (e.g., "Barreto et al\.,? \Q2016\E"). Please ensure all references follow standard academic formatting with consistent punctuation and date presentation.

4. **Figure references**: Change "Figure 1 (a)" to "Figure 1(a)" for consistency with academic writing standards.

5. **Clarify technical claims**: In Section 3.2, the phrase "extremely efficiently" is vague. Please quantify or provide a reference for this claim.

## Questions for Authors

1. Could you clarify the exact meaning of "extremely efficiently" in Section 3.3? Is there a specific metric or comparison that demonstrates this efficiency?

2. The paper uses both "human-normalised" and "human-normalized" - which spelling convention are you following, and why is there inconsistency?

3. In the references section, the formatting contains strange elements like "\Q2016\E" - could you please confirm the correct formatting for these entries?

4. The paper states "We use an action repeat of four" for Labyrinth, but does this affect the interpretation of the results compared to Atari (where action repeat is not specified)? If so, please clarify.

5. In Section 3.1, the paper states "the activations of the hidden units of the network" as part of the state space. This appears to be a technical inaccuracy since the activations are not part of the state space but rather part of the agent's internal representation. Could you clarify this point?
