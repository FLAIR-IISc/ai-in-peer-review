

# Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of Contribution

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Rewards), a novel reinforcement learning architecture that significantly improves data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness through the strategic incorporation of auxiliary tasks. The authors propose a bold approach that combines two key innovations: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that train the agent to manipulate specific aspects of the perceptual stream and network features, and reward prediction tasks that help shape the agent's representation to better recognize rewarding states. Unlike previous work that used auxiliary tasks as separate value functions or for transfer learning, UNREAL uses these tasks as auxiliary objectives for developing more effective representations, without directly using the pseudo-reward maximizing policies they learn. The paper demonstrates that this combination leads to substantial improvements on challenging 3D environments (Labyrinth) and 57 Atari games, with UNREAL achieving over 87% human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (more than doubling the previous state-of-the-art) and significantly outperforming A3C on Atari.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper introduces a novel and impactful direction for reinforcement learning research that successfully combines auxiliary tasks in a way that significantly outperforms previous approaches. The key innovation is not merely adding auxiliary tasks but strategically using them to shape the agent's representation for more effective learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in novelty and conceptual contribution. While prior work has explored auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning (e.g., Horde, UVFA, successor representations), the authors identify a critical gap: most prior approaches either used these tasks for transfer learning or as separate value functions, rather than as representation-shaping mechanisms. UNREAL's core innovation is using these tasks as "auxiliary control" mechanisms to develop better representations for the main task, which is a significant conceptual shift.

The paper is well-motivated and properly positioned in the literature. The related work section clearly distinguishes UNREAL from prior approaches and establishes a strong theoretical foundation for the proposed methodology. The authors effectively argue that sparse rewards make traditional reinforcement learning challenging, and that their approach addresses this by removing perceptual sparsity without biasing the agent's policy.

The empirical results are compelling and scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct thorough experiments on both the challenging Labyrinth environment (with 13 levels testing different abilities) and 57 Atari games. The results consistently show UNREAL outperforming A3C by significant margins (more than doubling performance on Labyrinth, achieving 880% mean human-normalized score on Atari), with substantial improvements in data efficiency (10-18× faster learning) and robustness to hyperparameter settings. The ablation studies provide clear evidence that each auxiliary component contributes meaningfully to the overall performance.

The paper's novelty and impact outweigh minor methodological concerns. The authors appropriately acknowledge that they are not claiming to solve the general reinforcement learning problem, but rather introducing a specific, effective approach to representation learning that can be combined with existing RL methods.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

- The paper could clarify the relationship between UNREAL and "unsupervised" reinforcement learning, as the approach is more accurately self-supervised rather than unsupervised. This distinction would help position the work more precisely in the literature.

- The paper mentions that "learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes," but could provide more intuition about why this is the case. What specific properties of the control task make it more effective for representation learning than prediction?

- The computational cost of UNREAL compared to A3C is not discussed in detail. A brief analysis of the additional memory and compute requirements would help readers understand the practical trade-offs.

- The paper could explore whether the success of feature control is due to the specific hidden layer chosen or if it's a general phenomenon across different network architectures.

## 5. Questions for Authors

- Can you provide more theoretical justification for why controlling network features (rather than simply predicting them) leads to better representations? What properties of the control task make it particularly effective?

- How would you expect UNREAL to perform in environments with different reward sparsity patterns or different types of visual complexity?

- The paper mentions that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." Could you elaborate on why this occurs, and whether this is specific to the Labyrinth environment or a more general phenomenon?

- The paper focuses on discrete action spaces. How would you expect UNREAL to generalize to continuous action spaces, and what modifications might be necessary for this extension?
