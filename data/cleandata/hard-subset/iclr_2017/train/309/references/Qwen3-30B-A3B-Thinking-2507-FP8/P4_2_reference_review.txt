

# Review of "UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning for Efficient Agent Learning), an agent architecture that incorporates auxiliary tasks to improve reinforcement learning performance. The authors propose two types of auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize changes in pixels or network features, and (2) auxiliary reward prediction tasks that predict immediate rewards. These auxiliary tasks are designed to shape the agent's representation without directly biasing the main policy. The UNREAL agent combines these with the A3C algorithm, resulting in significant improvements in data efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameters across Labyrinth (a 3D game platform) and Atari environments. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL more than doubles the state-of-the-art performance on Labyrinth (from 54% to 87% human-normalized scores) and achieves superior results on Atari games compared to prior methods.

## 2. Decision

Accept

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper makes a strong case for the effectiveness of auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning. The three key reasons for acceptance are:

First, the paper addresses a well-motivated problem: the sparsity of rewards in many reinforcement learning environments, which hinders efficient learning of effective representations. The authors position their work effectively within the literature, distinguishing their approach from related work on options, Horde, UVFAs, and successor representations. Their key insight that auxiliary tasks can shape representations without biasing the main policy is well-justified and represents a meaningful contribution to the field.

Second, the experimental results are scientifically rigorous and compelling. The paper provides thorough evaluations across multiple environments (Labyrinth and Atari), multiple baselines (A3C and ablated versions), and multiple hyperparameter settings. The results show consistent and significant improvements: UNREAL achieves more than twice the final performance of A3C on Labyrinth (87% vs 54% human-normalized score), with a 10-18x speedup in learning. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task component. The Atari results are particularly strong, with UNREAL achieving 880% mean human-normalized performance, significantly surpassing prior state-of-the-art methods.

Third, the paper provides strong evidence for the robustness of the approach. The authors demonstrate that UNREAL is substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C, as shown through careful hyperparameter sweeps and visualizations of performance across different settings. This is an important practical consideration that adds significant value to the contribution.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong overall, a few clarifications would strengthen it:

- A more detailed analysis of the learned representations would help clarify why the auxiliary tasks improve performance. Specifically, how do the features learned by the UNREAL agent differ from those learned by the baseline A3C agent? Providing metrics or visualizations of these features would strengthen the theoretical contribution.

- The paper mentions using a deconvolutional network for pixel control tasks but doesn't explain why this architecture was chosen over alternatives (e.g., fully connected layers). A brief discussion of the design choices for the auxiliary task architectures would be helpful.

- The reward prediction task uses a three-class classification (zero, positive, negative reward). It would be valuable to understand how the authors determined the optimal number of classes and how performance varies with different class configurations.

## 5. Clarifying Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "reward prediction is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent." How do you ensure that the reward prediction task doesn't inadvertently bias the main policy, especially since it's trained on skewed data (equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards)?

2. In the reward prediction task, you mention using a sequence of three observations. What was the rationale for this specific sequence length, and how sensitive is the performance to this choice?

3. The paper states that the pixel control task uses a 20×20 grid. How did you determine this grid size, and how does performance vary with different grid configurations?

4. The paper mentions that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." Could you provide more details on why input reconstruction hurts final performance, particularly regarding the "irrelevant parts of the visual input" hypothesis?
