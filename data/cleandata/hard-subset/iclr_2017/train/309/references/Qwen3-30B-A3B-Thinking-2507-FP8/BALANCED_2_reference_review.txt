

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent to Play Multiple Games"

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) with reward prediction tasks to improve representation learning in reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate that these auxiliary tasks, which do not directly maximize the main reward, significantly improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness across a challenging 3D environment (Labyrinth) and 57 Atari games. The paper makes a strong empirical case for the effectiveness of these auxiliary tasks, showing UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (more than doubling A3C's performance) and surpasses state-of-the-art results on Atari.

## 2. Decision
Reject. Key reasons: The paper lacks sufficient theoretical justification for why these auxiliary tasks improve representation learning, and the experimental design has limitations in ablation studies and hyperparameter analysis.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
While the empirical results are impressive, the paper falls short on two critical dimensions. First, the paper makes strong claims about representation learning but provides no evidence that the representations are actually better. The authors state that auxiliary tasks are "used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation," but they don't measure or analyze the quality of the representations learned. For instance, they don't show that representations from UNREAL are more informative, discriminative, or transferable than those from A3C. Without this evidence, it's unclear whether the performance gains are due to better representations or some other mechanism.

Second, the ablation studies are insufficient to isolate the contribution of each component. The paper shows that combining all auxiliary tasks works best but doesn't clearly demonstrate how much each individual task contributes. For example, the paper mentions that feature control was "only slightly worse than pixel control," but doesn't provide enough detail to understand why this difference exists or how to optimize the combination of these tasks. The comparison between pixel control and feature control is particularly underdeveloped.

Additionally, while the paper claims UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters, it doesn't provide sufficient analysis of how the hyperparameters for the auxiliary tasks specifically affect performance. The paper mentions hyperparameter sensitivity in Figure 8 but doesn't explain the "inconsistency around (−3,−3) point" in the UNREAL plot, which appears to be related to the λPC hyperparameter. This lack of clarity undermines the robustness claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Add representation analysis to demonstrate how the auxiliary tasks improve the quality of learned representations. This could include linear evaluation on the representations, visualization of feature activations, or comparison of representation similarity across tasks.
2. Conduct more thorough ablation studies that isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task. For example, show performance when only pixel control is used, only feature control, and their combination.
3. Provide a more detailed analysis of hyperparameter sensitivity, particularly for the weights of the auxiliary tasks (λPC, λRP, etc.), including how these weights interact with the main task.
4. Include a discussion of the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks and whether the performance gains justify the additional computational burden.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- How exactly do the auxiliary tasks improve the representation learning? Can you provide evidence (e.g., through representation analysis) that the representations learned with UNREAL are indeed better than those learned with A3C?
- The paper mentions that "feature control" was "only slightly worse than pixel control." Can you provide more details about this comparison, including the specific levels where this difference was observed and why feature control might be slightly less effective?
- The paper states that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." Can you provide more details about why this is the case? Is it because the reconstruction task focuses on irrelevant visual features?
- How does the computational cost of UNREAL compare to A3C? Is the improvement in data efficiency sufficient to offset the additional computational cost of the auxiliary tasks?

The paper presents interesting empirical results that could be valuable for the community, but the lack of theoretical grounding and insufficient analysis of the representation learning mechanism prevents it from meeting ICLR's standards for theoretical and empirical rigor. Addressing these issues would significantly strengthen the paper's contribution.
