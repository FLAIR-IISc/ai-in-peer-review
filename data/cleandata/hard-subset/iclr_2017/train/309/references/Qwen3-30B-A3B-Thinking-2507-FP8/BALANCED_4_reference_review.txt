

# Review of "UNREAL: Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning"

## Summary
This paper proposes UNREAL, a reinforcement learning architecture that incorporates auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) into the A3C framework. The authors claim these auxiliary tasks improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters without directly biasing the main policy. The approach is evaluated on Labyrinth (a 3D first-person game platform) and 57 Atari games, with results showing substantial improvements over A3C baselines, including doubling the human-normalized performance on Labyrinth and achieving state-of-the-art results on Atari.

## Decision
Reject. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution but lacks sufficient theoretical grounding and suffers from conceptual ambiguities that undermine its core claims about representation learning.

## Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is empirical, showing that auxiliary tasks improve RL performance. However, the theoretical motivation is weak. The authors claim their auxiliary tasks "are used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation," yet the results suggest they directly improve the main task performance. The distinction between representation learning and direct task learning is blurred throughout the paper. For instance, the reward prediction task predicts immediate rewards, which is directly related to the main task, contradicting the claim that it "does not introduce bias to the agent's policy."

The ablation study in Figure 5 shows pixel control outperforms feature control, but the paper provides no theoretical or empirical explanation for this difference. This is critical for understanding the contribution, as it suggests not all auxiliary tasks are equally valuable for representation learning.

The paper also fails to address how these auxiliary tasks might interfere with each other or the main task. For example, does the pixel control task cause the agent to focus on irrelevant visual features? The authors claim that "input reconstruction hurts final performance because it puts too much focus on reconstructing irrelevant parts of the visual input," but they don't provide evidence for this claim beyond the comparison to their own method.

The paper's strongest contribution is empirical, but the lack of conceptual clarity and theoretical grounding makes it difficult to assess whether this is a meaningful advance in representation learning or simply another effective auxiliary task design.

## Additional Feedback
1. **Clarify the theoretical framework**: The paper needs to establish a clear theoretical basis for why these specific auxiliary tasks improve representation learning. How do they relate to existing representation learning theory (e.g., the role of intrinsic motivation in representation learning)?

2. **Provide evidence of representation learning**: To support the claim that the auxiliary tasks improve representation learning (rather than directly improving task performance), include visualizations of hidden layer activations or use dimensionality reduction techniques to show how the representations differ between UNREAL and A3C.

3. **Expand the ablation study**: The current ablation (Figure 5) shows pixel control outperforms feature control, but the authors don't explain why. A more detailed analysis of how each auxiliary task affects different aspects of the agent's behavior would strengthen the paper.

4. **Reframe the contribution**: If the auxiliary tasks primarily improve the main task directly (rather than through representation learning), the authors should reframe their contribution to focus on effective auxiliary task design rather than representation learning.

## Questions for Authors
1. The paper claims that reward prediction "does not introduce bias to the agent's policy" because it's "not used for anything other than shaping the features." However, the reward prediction task predicts immediate rewards, which is directly related to the main task. How do you ensure that the features shaped by reward prediction don't become biased toward immediate rewards rather than the long-term rewards of the main task?

2. In Figure 5, pixel control outperforms feature control. What is the theoretical or empirical basis for this difference? Why would controlling pixel changes be more beneficial for representation learning than controlling feature activations?

3. The paper states that "learning to control pixel changes is indeed better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes, which in turn is better than simply learning to reconstruct the input." How does this align with the claim that the auxiliary tasks are "used solely as auxiliary objectives for developing a more effective representation" rather than directly improving the main task?

4. The authors note that "reward prediction is trained to minimise a loss LRP" and "the reward predictor is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent." However, the reward prediction task is trained on sequences where non-zero rewards are equally represented. How does this skewed sampling affect the representation learned by the agent, and why is this not considered a form of bias?

This paper has strong empirical results but needs to clarify its theoretical contribution and address the conceptual ambiguities before it can be considered for publication at ICLR. With the suggested revisions, it could become a valuable contribution to the field.
