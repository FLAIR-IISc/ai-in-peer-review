

# Review of "UNREAL: End-to-End Learning and Planning"

## Summary of Claims
The paper proposes UNREAL, an agent architecture that augments standard deep reinforcement learning (specifically A3C) with auxiliary tasks including pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction. The authors claim these auxiliary tasks significantly improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters. They demonstrate that UNREAL outperforms A3C on 13 Labyrinth levels (achieving 87% human-normalized performance versus A3C's 54%) and on 57 Atari games (880% mean performance versus A3C's state-of-the-art). The paper positions these auxiliary tasks as fundamentally different from prior work on temporal abstractions and successor representations, arguing they improve representation learning without directly using pseudo-rewards for planning.

## Decision
Reject. While the results appear promising, the paper fails to provide sufficient statistical evidence to support its strong claims of "more than doubling" performance and "substantial improvements" in data efficiency and robustness.

## Supporting Arguments
1. **Lack of Statistical Significance Testing**: The paper presents mean performance curves but provides no statistical evidence that the reported improvements are significant. The performance differences shown in Figures 3-6 appear large, but without proper statistical testing (e.g., t-tests, confidence intervals), it's impossible to determine if these differences are meaningful or simply due to random variation. The authors claim "more than doubled" performance but don't provide any statistical validation for this claim.

2. **Incomplete Baseline Comparison**: The Atari results compare UNREAL only to A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN (Wang et al., 2016), which is now outdated. The paper should compare to more recent state-of-the-art methods like Rainbow, D4PG, or IMPALA, which have since established new benchmarks on Atari. The claim that UNREAL "surpasses the current state-of-the-art" is misleading without these comparisons.

3. **Insufficient Analysis of Computational Cost**: The paper doesn't quantify the additional computational burden of the auxiliary tasks. The UNREAL agent has more parameters and requires more compute than A3C, which might explain some of the performance gains. Without this analysis, it's impossible to determine if the improvements are due to the auxiliary tasks themselves or simply because the agent has more capacity.

4. **Overstated Claims about Novelty**: The paper claims their auxiliary control tasks are "not used for representation learning" (contrasting with UVFA and successor representations), but the approach is fundamentally similar to prior work on auxiliary tasks (e.g., Lample & Chaplot, 2016; Mirowski et al., 2016). The novelty is overstated given these prior connections.

## Additional Feedback
- **Statistical Rigor**: Provide formal statistical significance testing for all performance comparisons. The paper should include p-values, confidence intervals, and effect sizes for all reported improvements.

- **Computational Analysis**: Include a detailed breakdown of the computational cost (training time, memory usage, etc.) of UNREAL compared to A3C. Without this, the data efficiency claims are unsubstantiated.

- **Ablation Study Improvement**: The ablation study (Figure 4) is insufficient. It should show not just that removing auxiliary tasks hurts performance, but that the performance difference is statistically significant.

- **Hyperparameter Sensitivity**: While the paper claims UNREAL is "more robust to hyperparameter settings," the evidence is limited to a single figure (Figure 3 top right). A more thorough analysis of how performance varies across hyperparameters would strengthen this claim.

- **Comparison to Recent Work**: Update the Atari results to include comparisons with state-of-the-art methods published after 2016, such as Rainbow (Hessel et al., 2018) or IMPALA (Espeholt et al., 2018).

## Questions for Authors
1. What statistical tests did you perform to confirm the significance of the performance improvements reported in Figures 3, 4, and 6?
2. Why did you choose to compare to Prioritized Dueling DQN (2016) instead of more recent state-of-the-art methods on Atari (e.g., Rainbow, 2018)?
3. Can you provide a detailed breakdown of the computational cost (training time, memory, etc.) of UNREAL compared to A3C?
4. How did you ensure that the improvements were due to the auxiliary tasks and not simply because UNREAL has more parameters than A3C?

The paper presents an interesting approach with potentially valuable insights, but the lack of statistical rigor and incomplete baseline comparisons prevent me from being confident in the claims. The paper would benefit significantly from more thorough statistical analysis and a more complete comparison to the current state of the art.
