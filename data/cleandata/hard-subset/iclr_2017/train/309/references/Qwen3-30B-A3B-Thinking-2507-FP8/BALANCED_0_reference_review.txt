

# Review of "UNREAL: End-to-End Learning and Planning"

## 1. Summary
The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that incorporates auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) and reward prediction to improve representation learning. The authors claim that this approach significantly improves data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters compared to standard A3C. The paper presents empirical results on the Labyrinth 3D environment and 57 Atari games, reporting that UNREAL achieves over 87% human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (more than doubling previous results) and surpasses state-of-the-art on Atari.

## 2. Decision
Reject. The primary reasons are insufficient statistical evidence to support the claims of significant performance improvements and insufficient novelty relative to existing work on auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main claim - that UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C - lacks statistical rigor. The results in Figure 3 show mean human-normalized scores across hyperparameter settings, but no statistical significance testing is provided. The paper reports performance improvements without confidence intervals, p-values, or analysis of variance. For instance, the claim that UNREAL "more than doubled the previous state-of-the-art results" is based on mean scores without any indication of whether these differences are statistically meaningful. This is particularly concerning given the authors' claim that UNREAL is "more robust to hyperparameter settings," which would require statistical evidence to support.

Furthermore, the novelty of the approach is questionable. The paper builds on several existing ideas: pixel control is closely related to the "input change prediction" baseline in Figure 5, which the authors acknowledge is "only slightly worse than pixel control." Reward prediction is similar to work by Lample & Chaplot (2016) and Mirowski et al. (2016). Feature control is also related to UVFA and successor representation work. The paper's literature review is thorough, but it fails to clearly articulate what is novel about the specific combination of these auxiliary tasks and how it differs from prior work. The paper also doesn't provide sufficient evidence that the combination is better than using these tasks individually.

The ablation studies are limited. The paper compares UNREAL to A3C and UNREAL without pixel control, but doesn't explore the impact of different combinations of auxiliary tasks or investigate why certain tasks are more effective than others. The paper states that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse," but without statistical evidence or detailed analysis to support this claim.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- **Statistical Rigor:** The authors should provide statistical significance testing for all reported performance improvements. This could include confidence intervals for mean performance across hyperparameter settings, p-values from t-tests comparing UNREAL and A3C, and analysis of variance to determine if the improvements are consistent across environments.

- **Novelty Clarification:** The paper needs to more clearly distinguish its contribution from prior work. The authors should explicitly state what makes their specific combination of auxiliary tasks novel and why it's better than existing approaches. A table comparing UNREAL to related methods (e.g., UVFA, successor representations, auxiliary prediction tasks) would help clarify the novelty.

- **Mechanistic Understanding:** The paper would benefit from a deeper analysis of *why* the auxiliary tasks work. For example, why does feature control work better than pixel control in some cases? What specific aspects of the representation are improved by these tasks? The current paper is largely descriptive rather than explanatory.

- **Computational Cost:** The authors should report the computational overhead of implementing the auxiliary tasks. How much additional time or memory does UNREAL require compared to A3C? This is particularly important for a paper claiming improved data efficiency.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. How were the hyperparameters for the auxiliary tasks (λPC, λRP, etc.) chosen? Were they tuned per environment or kept consistent across environments? If tuned, how many hyperparameter settings were evaluated for each task?

2. The paper claims that "reward prediction is not used for anything other than shaping the features." Can you provide evidence that the reward predictor does not introduce bias to the policy? What mechanisms prevent the reward predictor from directly influencing the policy's action selection?

3. The paper states that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse." Can you provide more details about this comparison, including the statistical significance of the final performance differences?

4. How does UNREAL compare to more recent work on auxiliary tasks in reinforcement learning, such as the work by Jaderberg et al. (2016) on "Learning to Play via Deep Meta-Reinforcement Learning" or the "Dense Reward" approach by Guo et al. (2017)?

The paper has a strong foundation with a good literature review and interesting experimental results, but the lack of statistical rigor and insufficient novelty make it difficult to support the claims of significant improvement over prior work. With the suggested improvements, particularly in statistical analysis and clearer articulation of novelty, this could become a strong contribution to the field.
