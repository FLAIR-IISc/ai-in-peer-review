

# Review of "Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning"

## 1. Summary of Claims
The paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), an architecture that enhances deep reinforcement learning agents through auxiliary tasks. The authors propose two novel auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that learn to maximize changes in specific visual regions or network features, and auxiliary reward prediction tasks that predict immediate rewards. These tasks are designed to shape the agent's representation without directly biasing the main policy. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL significantly improves data efficiency, final performance, and robustness to hyperparameter settings across two challenging domains: 3D Labyrinth environments and 57 Atari games. The authors claim UNREAL more than doubles the state-of-the-art performance on Labyrinth (from 54% to 87% human-normalized mean performance) and achieves 880% mean human-normalized performance on Atari, surpassing previous methods.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to reinforcement learning by providing a well-motivated and rigorously evaluated approach to representation learning through auxiliary tasks. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the approach is well-placed in the literature and addresses a clear problem in deep RL (data inefficiency and hyperparameter sensitivity), and (2) the empirical results are compelling and scientifically rigorous.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's approach is well-motivated by a thorough analysis of related work, clearly distinguishing UNREAL from prior methods like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. The authors correctly identify that previous approaches either used auxiliary tasks for planning (not representation learning) or did not effectively transfer representations across different reward functions. The proposed auxiliary tasks directly address the problem of sparse rewards by shaping the agent's representation without biasing the main policy.

The empirical evidence is scientifically rigorous and comprehensive. The authors conduct experiments across two distinct domains (3D Labyrinth and Atari) with appropriate baselines (A3C and ablated UNREAL variants). They provide multiple metrics of success: final performance (human-normalized scores), learning speed (steps to reach A3C's best performance), and robustness to hyperparameters. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task component. The Labyrinth results are particularly impressive, showing a 10x speedup and more than double the performance of A3C. The Atari results also show consistent improvement over state-of-the-art methods.

The methodology is sound and well-documented in the appendix. The authors use standard evaluation protocols and provide sufficient detail about implementation and hyperparameters. The comparison with baselines like pixel reconstruction and input change prediction provides valuable insight into why their approach works better.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, a few improvements could enhance its clarity and impact:
- The paper could provide more insight into why the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control and feature control) work better than alternatives like pixel reconstruction. The current explanation that "input reconstruction hurts final performance because it puts too much focus on reconstructing irrelevant parts" could be strengthened with visualizations of what the agent is actually focusing on.
- The connection between the auxiliary tasks and the theoretical properties of the representation learning would be strengthened with a brief discussion of how these tasks relate to information theory or representation learning principles.
- The paper mentions that "feature control" was slightly worse than "pixel control" but still beneficial. A more detailed analysis of when feature control might be preferable (e.g., in environments where specific features are more predictive of reward) would be valuable.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. In the Labyrinth experiments, the paper shows that UNREAL achieves 116% human-normalized score on lt hallway slope. How does this compare to the human baseline? Is it possible that the human-normalized scoring system is not capturing the true difficulty of this level?
2. The paper states that UNREAL is "substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C" (Figure 3 Top Right). Could the authors provide a more quantitative measure of this robustness, such as the percentage of hyperparameter configurations that yield performance within 10% of the best configuration for each method?
3. In the reward prediction task, the authors use a three-class classification (zero, positive, negative reward). Why not use a regression approach to predict the exact reward value instead? How would this change the performance or efficiency of the agent?
4. The paper mentions that UNREAL requires less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance. Could the authors provide more detail on the data efficiency metric, including whether this is measured in terms of environment steps or wall-clock time?
