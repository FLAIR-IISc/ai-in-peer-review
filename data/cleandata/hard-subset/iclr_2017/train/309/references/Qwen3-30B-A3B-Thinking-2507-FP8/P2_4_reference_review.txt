

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent with Auxiliary Rewards"

## 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning framework that significantly improves data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness by incorporating multiple auxiliary tasks into the standard A3C architecture. The authors make a compelling contribution by proposing two specific types of auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that are not directly reward-maximizing but serve to develop more effective representations. They also introduce an auxiliary reward prediction task that helps overcome sparse reward challenges. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL achieves more than double the human-normalized performance of A3C on 13 challenging 3D Labyrinth levels (87% vs. 54%) and significantly outperforms state-of-the-art methods on 57 Atari games (880% mean human-normalized performance). The work is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a clear contribution to the field of representation learning in reinforcement learning.

## 2. Decision

**Accept**. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field with well-motivated, well-executed research that addresses a significant challenge in reinforcement learning (sparse rewards and poor representation learning). The empirical results are robust, comprehensive, and clearly demonstrate the value of the proposed auxiliary tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper excels in all three key criteria for evaluation:

**1. Clear problem statement and motivation:** The paper clearly identifies the challenge of sparse rewards making representation learning difficult in RL. It effectively situates its work within the literature, distinguishing itself from related approaches like options, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations by emphasizing that their auxiliary tasks are used *solely* for representation learning rather than as primary reward-maximizing objectives. The motivation is exceptionally strong, particularly in explaining why their approach differs from prior work on auxiliary tasks.

**2. Rigorous empirical evaluation:** The authors provide a thorough empirical evaluation across two distinct domains (3D Labyrinth and Atari) with multiple baselines and ablations. The Labyrinth experiments are particularly impressive, testing a diverse set of 13 levels that cover different challenges (fruit gathering, navigation, exploration, and laser-tag). The Atari results are equally compelling, showing consistent improvement across 57 games with both mean and median performance metrics. The paper demonstrates not just better final performance but also significantly faster learning (10× speedup) and greater robustness to hyperparameters.

**3. Scientific rigor and clarity:** The methodology is clearly described, with sufficient implementation details to reproduce the experiments. The ablation studies (e.g., Figure 5 showing that learning to control pixel changes is better than predicting them, which is better than input reconstruction) provide strong evidence for the authors' claims. The paper also includes thorough analysis of hyperparameter robustness (Figure 3 Top Right and Figure 8), which is a critical aspect of RL research that is often overlooked. The authors carefully address potential concerns about biasing the policy through the auxiliary tasks, explaining why reward prediction doesn't introduce bias.

The paper's main strength is its focus on *why* these auxiliary tasks work as representation learning tools rather than as direct reward maximizers, which is a nuanced but critical distinction in the literature. The empirical results are convincing, with clear statistical evidence of improvement across multiple metrics.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions could further enhance its impact:

1. **Theoretical justification:** While the empirical results are compelling, adding a brief theoretical discussion of why these auxiliary tasks lead to better representations would strengthen the paper. For instance, discussing how pixel control and feature control might encourage the network to learn features that are causally related to state changes could provide deeper insight.

2. **Computational cost analysis:** The paper mentions the benefits of UNREAL but doesn't quantify the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks. A brief analysis of the additional training time or memory required would be helpful for practitioners considering implementation.

3. **Broader implications:** The paper could briefly discuss how these findings might generalize to other domains beyond video games, such as robotics or natural language processing, where representation learning is similarly challenging.

## 5. Questions for Authors

To help clarify my assessment and ensure confidence in the results, I'd appreciate answers to these questions:

1. In the reward prediction task, you mention that you sample sequences to ensure equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards. Could you elaborate on how this skewed sampling might affect the distribution of states that the agent encounters during training, and whether this could potentially bias the representation in unintended ways?

2. The paper states that feature control was "only slightly worse than pixel control" (Section 4.1.1). Given that feature control uses a different type of auxiliary task (network features rather than pixel changes), could the authors provide more insight into why feature control might be less effective than pixel control, and whether there are specific types of network features that would be more beneficial?

3. In the Atari experiments, you report "880% mean and 250% median performance." Could you clarify whether this is mean human-normalized performance across all 57 games (as would be standard), or if this is normalized differently? The paper states they use "human-normalized scores" but the exact normalization method would be helpful for interpretation.

4. The paper mentions that "the same UNREAL architecture also significantly improved both the learning speed and the robustness of A3C over 57 Atari games." Could the authors provide more specific numbers about the data efficiency improvement on Atari (similar to the 10× speedup reported for Labyrinth)?

This paper makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, and I believe it would be a valuable addition to ICLR. The work is well-executed, well-motivated, and provides clear evidence of substantial improvements over existing methods.
