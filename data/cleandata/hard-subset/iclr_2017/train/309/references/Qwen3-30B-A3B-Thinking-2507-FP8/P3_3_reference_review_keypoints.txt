- Introduces UNREAL: augment A3C with three auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) to improve representation learning rather than directly optimize main reward.  
- Demonstrates strong empirical gains: large improvements in data efficiency, final performance, and robustness on Labyrinth and Atari.  
- Key contribution: auxiliary tasks focused on representation learning can dramatically boost RL performance without using pseudo-rewards for planning.  
- Decision: Accept — strong empirical contribution with compelling results on challenging environments.  
- Empirical strength: UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized on Labyrinth vs. 54% for A3C, 10–18× speedup, and uses <10% of the data.  
- Ablation studies show each auxiliary task helps, with pixel control giving the largest performance boost.  
- Atari results strong (880% mean human-normalized), indicating generalizability across domains.  
- Well-situated in literature; distinguishes UNREAL from prior work by using auxiliaries solely for representation learning.  
- Methodology sound: off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary tasks combined with on-policy main task; skewed sampling for reward prediction addresses sparse rewards.  
- Improvement 1: Lacks theoretical justification linking chosen auxiliary tasks to representation learning principles (e.g., predictive coding, information bottleneck).  
- Improvement 2: Needs comparison to more recent Atari SOTA methods (e.g., Rainbow, IMPALA).  
- Improvement 3: Feature control is under-explained—clarify unit selection and why it outperforms using features directly.  
- Improvement 4: Discuss limitations and scalability to more complex environments or cases where auxiliaries could hurt.  
- Question 1: How does reward prediction not bias representations toward training-environment reward structure and harm generalization?  
- Question 2: What evidence supports the chosen 50% oversampling of rewarding events; were other sampling schemes tested?  
- Question 3: Claims of hyperparameter robustness need statistical analysis (e.g., p-values) to show significance.  
- Question 4: Why use a 3-frame sequence for reward prediction and how would different sequence lengths affect performance?  
- Question 5: Why does input reconstruction hurt performance compared to pixel control—focus on irrelevant details or deeper representation reasons?  
- Overall: Strong, well-motivated empirical paper that would benefit from theoretical grounding, updated comparisons, clarifications, and discussion of limitations.
