

# Peer Review: UNREAL

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contribution

This paper proposes the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning) agent, which enhances deep reinforcement learning agents through auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency and robustness. The key contributions are:

- Introducing auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize changes in specific visual regions (pixel control) or network features (feature control)
- Proposing reward prediction as an auxiliary task to shape the agent's representation without biasing the main policy
- Combining these auxiliary tasks with A3C in a unified framework that significantly improves performance

The authors demonstrate that UNREAL outperforms the A3C baseline by more than doubling human-normalized scores on Labyrinth (from 54% to 87%) and achieves state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games (880% mean human-normalized performance). The framework also learns 10x faster than A3C and is more robust to hyperparameter settings.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **acceptance** of this paper.

Key reasons:
1. The paper makes a significant empirical contribution by demonstrating that auxiliary tasks can substantially improve data efficiency and robustness in deep RL.
2. The UNREAL framework is well-motivated, clearly articulated, and provides a strong foundation for future work in representation learning for RL.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper's contribution is highly relevant to current challenges in deep reinforcement learning, where data efficiency remains a critical bottleneck. The authors have carefully positioned their work within the literature, distinguishing their approach from previous auxiliary task methods (like Horde, UVFA, and successor representations) by emphasizing their use of auxiliary tasks for representation learning rather than direct reward maximization.

The empirical results are compelling and well-supported. The paper demonstrates consistent improvements across two diverse environments (Labyrinth and Atari), with ablation studies clearly showing the contribution of each auxiliary task component. The authors provide thorough analysis of learning speed (10x faster than A3C) and robustness to hyperparameter settings, which is particularly valuable for practical applications.

The methodology is sound. The reward prediction task is specifically designed to avoid biasing the main policy while still shaping the representation, a thoughtful consideration that addresses a key challenge in auxiliary task design. The authors' careful sampling procedure for reward prediction (equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards) effectively addresses the sparsity of rewards in many environments.

The paper's architecture (Figure 1) is clearly presented, and the implementation details in Appendix B provide sufficient information for reproducibility.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these additions would further enhance it:

- **Computational cost analysis**: The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational overhead of the auxiliary tasks. How much additional computation does UNREAL require compared to A3C? This is important for practical deployment considerations.

- **Task-specific performance analysis**: A more detailed analysis of which specific Atari games saw the largest gains would provide deeper insights into the framework's strengths and limitations.

- **Limitations discussion**: The authors should explicitly discuss potential limitations of the UNREAL framework, such as scalability to more complex environments or sensitivity to network architecture choices.

- **Feature control insights**: The paper mentions that feature control was slightly worse than pixel control but doesn't explore why. More insight into this comparison would strengthen the paper.

## 5. Questions for the Authors

1. In the reward prediction task, the authors sample sequences to ensure equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards. Could you elaborate on the exact sampling procedure, especially how you handle environments with extremely sparse rewards (where non-zero rewards might be very rare)?

2. The paper uses a three-class classification (zero, positive, negative reward) for reward prediction. Could you explain why you chose this approach rather than a regression-based method, and how this choice affects the learning dynamics?

3. In Figure 5, feature control shows improvement but is slightly worse than pixel control. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case? Is it related to the specific network layer chosen, or are there other factors at play?

4. The paper states that UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters but doesn't quantify this robustness beyond the visualizations. Could you provide a more formal measure of robustness (e.g., standard deviation across hyperparameters) to support this claim?

5. The authors use different ranges for λPC in Labyrinth (0.01-0.1) versus Atari (0.0001-0.01). Could you explain the reasoning behind this difference, and whether it's due to the nature of the environments or the pixel change characteristics in Atari games?

## Conclusion

The UNREAL framework represents a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning, demonstrating that carefully designed auxiliary tasks can substantially improve data efficiency and robustness without requiring fundamental changes to the underlying RL algorithm. The paper is well-written, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a compelling case for the utility of auxiliary tasks in representation learning for RL. I believe this work will be of significant interest to the ICLR community and will likely inspire follow-up research on auxiliary task design in reinforcement learning.
