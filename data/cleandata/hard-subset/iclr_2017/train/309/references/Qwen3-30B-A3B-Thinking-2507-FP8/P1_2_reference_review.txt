

# Peer Review for ICLR

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning), a novel reinforcement learning agent architecture that incorporates auxiliary tasks to significantly improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameter settings. The authors propose a framework that combines three key components: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that learn to maximize changes in visual features or network activations, (2) an auxiliary reward prediction task that learns to predict immediate rewards to shape the agent's representation, and (3) an experience replay mechanism that prioritizes rewarding events for the reward prediction task.

The UNREAL agent builds upon the A3C framework but adds these auxiliary tasks to enhance representation learning. The paper demonstrates significant improvements across two domains: a challenging 3D first-person game platform called Labyrinth (where UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized mean performance compared to A3C's 54%, with a 10x speedup in learning), and 57 Atari games (where UNREAL achieves 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, surpassing state-of-the-art results). The authors provide thorough ablation studies showing that each auxiliary component contributes to the overall performance, and they demonstrate that UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameter choices than A3C.

The paper makes a clear and significant contribution to the field by showing how auxiliary tasks can be effectively used to improve representation learning in deep reinforcement learning, rather than just as separate value functions for planning. The motivation is well-grounded in the literature, and the experimental results are compelling and well-documented.

## 2. Decision

I recommend **Accept** for this paper.

**Key reasons for acceptance:**
1. The paper makes a significant contribution to the field by demonstrating how auxiliary tasks can substantially improve data efficiency and robustness in reinforcement learning, which addresses a critical practical limitation of current deep RL methods.
2. The experimental results are strong, with clear improvements across multiple environments, thorough ablation studies, and compelling evidence of robustness to hyperparameter choices.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper addresses a fundamental challenge in deep reinforcement learning: the need for more data-efficient agents. Current methods often require enormous amounts of data to learn, making them impractical for real-world applications. UNREAL tackles this problem by using auxiliary tasks to develop better representations, which is a promising direction that builds on but significantly extends prior work in the field.

The experimental results are particularly impressive. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs 54% for A3C), with a 10x speedup in learning. This is not just a marginal improvement but a substantial leap forward. On Atari, UNREAL achieves 880% mean human-normalized performance, which is a significant improvement over previous state-of-the-art results.

The paper's ablation studies are thorough and convincing. Figure 5 clearly shows that learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate changes or reconstructing inputs, and that feature control provides substantial benefits. This helps establish that the authors' approach is not just adding arbitrary auxiliary tasks but is specifically designed to improve representation learning.

The robustness analysis (Figure 3 top right) is particularly noteworthy. Many RL papers focus only on the best hyperparameter settings, but the authors show that UNREAL is more robust across a wide range of hyperparameters, which is a critical practical consideration that many papers overlook.

The literature review is well-organized and effectively positions the work within the existing body of research. The paper clearly distinguishes its approach from prior work on temporal abstractions, Horde, UVFA, and successor representations by focusing on representation learning rather than just using auxiliary tasks for planning or separate value functions.

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

I have some suggestions to strengthen the paper further:

1. **Clarify the representation unification**: The paper mentions "unified" representation learning but doesn't fully explain how the representation is unified across the different tasks. A diagram or more detailed explanation of how the shared representation works would help readers better understand the architecture.

2. **Detail the loss function interaction**: The paper states that "the reward prediction task is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent." It would be helpful to more explicitly explain how the reward prediction loss interacts with the main A3C loss during training. What prevents the reward prediction from biasing the policy, as the authors claim?

3. **Expand on feature control benefits**: The paper mentions that "feature control was only slightly worse than for pixel control" but doesn't provide a clear explanation of why feature control is effective. A more detailed analysis of what features are being controlled and why they're useful would strengthen the paper.

4. **Add visualizations**: Including visualizations of the auxiliary tasks (e.g., showing what parts of the image the pixel control task is focusing on) would help readers better understand the auxiliary tasks and their effects.

5. **Clarify the "twice" claim**: The paper states that UNREAL "achieves more than twice the final human-normalised mean performance of A3C" but then cites 87% vs 54%. Could you clarify how this is a "twice" improvement? Is it specifically comparing the mean performance (87% vs 54%) or something else?

## Questions for the Authors

1. In Section 4.1.1, you compare to "A3C augmented with a pixel reconstruction loss" and "A3C augmented with an input change prediction loss." Could you provide more details on how these baselines were implemented, particularly how the input change prediction loss differs from your reward prediction task?

2. The paper states that "reward prediction is trained to minimise a loss LRP" but doesn't clearly explain how the reward prediction task is actually used during training. Could you provide more detail on the exact training procedure for the reward prediction task?

3. In Section 3.2, you state that "the reward prediction task is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent." How does this differ from simply using the reward prediction as an additional loss term in the main RL objective? What specific mechanisms prevent the reward prediction from biasing the policy?

4. The paper mentions that "the optimal policy would begin by exploring the maze to rapidly learn its layout and then exploit that knowledge to repeatedly return to the goal." How does UNREAL's behavior compare to this optimal policy in the procedurally-generated navigation levels?

5. For the Atari experiments, could you provide more detail on how the human-normalized scores were calculated? This is important for understanding the significance of the 880% mean performance claim.

Overall, this is a strong paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The results are compelling, the methodology is sound, and the paper is well-written. I believe it would be a valuable addition to the ICLR proceedings.
