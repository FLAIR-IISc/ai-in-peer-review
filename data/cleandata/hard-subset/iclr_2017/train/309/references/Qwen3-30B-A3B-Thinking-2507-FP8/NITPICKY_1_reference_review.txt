

# Review

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that incorporates auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) to improve data efficiency and robustness. The authors present a comprehensive evaluation across 13 Labyrinth levels and 57 Atari games, demonstrating significant improvements over A3C in both final performance and learning speed. The work builds on relevant literature about auxiliary tasks and representation learning, with a clear connection to previous work on UVFAs and successor representations.

## 2. Decision
Reject

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper presents an interesting approach that appears to deliver strong empirical results, but I must reject it due to multiple significant technical inconsistencies and methodological ambiguities that undermine the paper's scientific rigor. Specifically:

First, there is a critical technical inconsistency between the paper's stated methodology and the actual implementation. In Section 3.1, the authors state: "We focus on value-based RL methods that approximate the optimal action-values by Q-learning. Specifically, for each control task c we optimise an n-step Q-learning loss..." However, the UNREAL agent is built on A3C (a policy gradient method), not Q-learning. The paper claims to use Q-learning for auxiliary tasks but then describes using A3C's policy gradient approach. This fundamental misunderstanding of RL methods is unacceptable in a paper claiming to make contributions to reinforcement learning.

Second, the paper contains multiple inconsistent notations and formatting errors that suggest poor attention to detail. In Equation (2), the loss function is written as "LUNREAL(θ) = LA3C + λVRLVR + λPC ∑ c L(c)Q + λRPLRP" - the terms λVRLVR and λRPLRP are confusingly written with the weight multiplied by the loss name, rather than as separate terms. Similarly, in Equation (1), the spacing around "∑ c∈C" is inconsistent with standard mathematical notation.

Third, the paper makes several unsubstantiated claims without proper justification. For example, in Section 3.2, they claim "a multiclass cross-entropy classification loss across three classes (zero, positive, or negative reward)" is used for reward prediction, but then state "a mean-squared error loss is also feasible." However, reward values are typically continuous (not categorical), making classification inappropriate for this task. The authors provide no justification for choosing classification over regression, which would be the standard approach for predicting continuous rewards.

Fourth, the paper lacks methodological justification for key design choices. The choice of a 20×20 grid for pixel control (Section 3.1) is presented without explanation. Why 20×20? Why 4×4 cells? The authors provide no ablation study or rationale for this specific grid size, making it appear arbitrary. Similarly, the choice of 2k for the replay buffer size (Section 4) is not justified.

## 4. Additional Feedback
To improve the paper, the authors should:

1. Correct the fundamental misunderstanding of RL methods - either clarify that the auxiliary tasks are policy-based (consistent with A3C) or switch to a proper Q-learning framework for the auxiliary tasks.

2. Provide clear justification for all key design choices, particularly the 20×20 grid for pixel control and the classification approach for reward prediction.

3. Fix all inconsistent notations and formatting issues throughout the paper, particularly in equations and reference citations.

4. Clarify the relationship between the auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction tasks to eliminate conceptual ambiguity.

5. Provide statistical significance tests for the comparisons between different auxiliary task configurations in Section 4.1.1.

## Questions for Authors
1. Given that UNREAL is built on A3C (a policy gradient method), how exactly are the auxiliary control tasks being learned? If they're not using Q-learning as stated, what method is being used for the auxiliary tasks?

2. Why was a classification approach chosen for reward prediction rather than regression, given that reward values are continuous quantities?

3. What was the rationale for choosing a 20×20 grid for pixel control? Was this tested against other grid sizes?

4. In the Atari experiments, how does UNREAL compare to the most recent state-of-the-art methods at the time of submission (e.g., Rainbow, DQN with prioritized experience replay)?

5. The paper claims "UNREAL is significantly faster at learning... achieving a mean speedup of the number of steps to reach A3C best performance of 10×". Is this measured in environment steps or wall-clock time? The distinction is important for understanding data efficiency claims.
