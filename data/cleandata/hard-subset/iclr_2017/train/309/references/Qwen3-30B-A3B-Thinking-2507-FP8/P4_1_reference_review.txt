

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent to Play Multiple Games"

## 1. Summary of the paper's claims and contributions

The paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that incorporates auxiliary tasks to improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness. The authors propose two types of auxiliary tasks: auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that train the agent to maximize changes in specific parts of the visual input or network activations, and reward prediction tasks that train the agent to predict immediate rewards. Unlike previous work that used these as primary learning objectives, UNREAL uses them solely for representation learning. The authors claim their approach significantly outperforms baseline A3C agents across multiple environments, including a challenging 3D first-person game platform called Labyrinth and 57 Atari games.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution with well-designed experiments that convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of their auxiliary tasks approach. The key reasons for acceptance are: (1) the paper provides clear, rigorous empirical evidence that the proposed auxiliary tasks substantially improve performance and data efficiency, and (2) the approach is well-motivated and properly placed in the literature, with clear differentiation from previous work.

## 3. Supporting arguments

The paper's approach is well-motivated and properly situated within the literature. The related work section thoroughly reviews previous methods for temporal abstractions (options, Horde), continuous representations (UVFA, successor representations), and auxiliary tasks (environment models, internal feature prediction). The authors clearly differentiate their work from these prior approaches by emphasizing that their auxiliary tasks are used solely for representation learning rather than as primary learning objectives.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized mean performance (compared to A3C's 54%), representing a more than twofold improvement. The learning speed is dramatically improved, requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance (10× speedup on average). On Atari, UNREAL achieves 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, surpassing previous state-of-the-art agents. The ablation studies (Figure 4) clearly show that each auxiliary task contributes to performance, and the comparison with baselines (Figure 5) demonstrates that their approach is superior to simple alternatives like input reconstruction or predicting immediate pixel changes.

The experimental design is rigorous, with proper evaluation protocols including multiple hyperparameter settings, random seeds, and comprehensive ablation studies. The authors also provide a thorough implementation section that would allow for replication of their results. The paper's claims are well-supported by both the quantitative results and the qualitative analysis of learning curves.

## 4. Additional feedback

The paper would benefit from a more detailed theoretical discussion of why these auxiliary tasks improve representation learning. While the empirical results are strong, a deeper analysis of the underlying mechanisms would strengthen the contribution. The authors could also consider providing more information about the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks, particularly for the pixel control and feature control components, to help readers understand the trade-offs.

The paper could be strengthened by including a more thorough analysis of failure cases or environments where UNREAL does not perform as well as expected. While the results are impressive, understanding the limitations of the approach would provide a more complete picture of its applicability.

## 5. Questions for authors

1. In Figure 5, the paper shows that feature control is slightly less effective than pixel control. Could you provide more insight into why this might be the case, and whether there are specific characteristics of the feature control tasks that could be adjusted to improve their performance?

2. The paper mentions that the reward prediction task is trained with a multiclass cross-entropy loss across three classes (zero, positive, or negative reward). How does the performance of UNREAL change when using a different loss function (e.g., mean-squared error), and what are the implications for the reward prediction task's effectiveness?

3. The paper states that UNREAL is "more robust to hyperparameter settings" than A3C. Could you provide a more detailed analysis of which hyperparameters are most sensitive, and how the robustness might vary across different environments (Labyrinth vs. Atari)?
