

# ICLR Review

## 1. Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions

This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning architecture that significantly improves data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameter settings by incorporating auxiliary tasks into a standard A3C agent. The authors propose two novel auxiliary task types: (1) auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) that train the agent to maximize changes in specific visual regions or activate specific neural network features, and (2) reward prediction tasks that predict immediate rewards from visual input. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL substantially outperforms standard A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (achieving 87% human-normalized mean performance, more than double A3C's 54%) and 57 Atari games (achieving 880% mean human-normalized performance). The authors provide thorough ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary component, and demonstrate that UNREAL is significantly more data-efficient (requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance) and more robust to hyperparameter choices than baseline methods.

## 2. Decision

I recommend acceptance of this paper.

Key reasons for acceptance:
1. The paper presents strong empirical evidence for the effectiveness of the proposed auxiliary tasks, with significant improvements across multiple environments.
2. The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough related work section that clearly differentiates it from prior work on auxiliary tasks and representation learning.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for the Decision

The paper makes a significant contribution to the field of reinforcement learning by demonstrating that carefully designed auxiliary tasks can substantially improve deep RL agents. The Labyrinth experiments are particularly compelling as they demonstrate the approach on a challenging 3D environment with rich visual inputs, where previous work has struggled. The Atari results are also impressive, with UNREAL surpassing state-of-the-art methods across all 57 games.

The ablation studies (Figure 4 and Figure 5) provide strong evidence that each component of the UNREAL architecture contributes to the performance gains. The comparison with unsupervised baselines (pixel reconstruction and input change prediction) is particularly convincing in showing that the specific auxiliary control tasks are crucial for performance. The paper demonstrates that learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes, which in turn is better than simply learning to reconstruct the input.

The paper is scientifically rigorous in its experimental design. The authors provide results across multiple hyperparameter settings (50 different random hyperparameter settings on Atari, 45 on Labyrinth), showing that UNREAL consistently outperforms A3C across all settings. The robustness to hyperparameters (Figure 3 Top Right) is a particularly valuable practical contribution, as it addresses a major pain point in deep RL research.

The paper's explanation of why these auxiliary tasks work is compelling: they help the agent develop better representations of the environment that are more relevant to reward, without directly biasing the policy (for the reward prediction task) or requiring the agent to learn to control specific aspects of the environment (for the auxiliary control tasks).

## 4. Additional Feedback to Improve the Paper

While the paper is strong as is, the following improvements would enhance its impact:

- **Clarify the theoretical motivation**: The paper provides excellent empirical results but lacks a theoretical justification for why these specific auxiliary tasks work. A brief discussion of the relationship between the auxiliary tasks and the underlying representation learning would strengthen the paper.

- **Visualize the learned representations**: Including visualizations of how the representations differ between A3C and UNREAL (e.g., feature activation maps for different tasks) would help readers understand the mechanism behind the performance improvements.

- **Expand on the reward prediction task**: The paper states that "the reward predictor is not used for anything other than shaping the features," but it's not clear why this is different from other auxiliary tasks that also shape features. A more detailed explanation of this distinction would be helpful.

- **Discuss limitations**: The paper could benefit from a discussion of potential limitations, such as how the approach might perform on environments with very different visual characteristics or reward structures, and how it might scale to more complex environments.

- **Clarify human-normalized scores**: The paper refers to "human-normalized scores" but doesn't explain how these scores are calculated. A brief explanation would help readers unfamiliar with the Labyrinth environment.

- **Provide more detailed performance breakdown**: A more detailed breakdown of the performance improvements across the different Labyrinth level categories (simple fruit gathering, navigation, etc.) would help understand which aspects of the environment benefit most from the auxiliary tasks.

## Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the reward predictor is not used for anything other than shaping the features of the agent." How does this differ from the role of the auxiliary control tasks, which also shape the features? Why is this distinction important for the approach to work?

2. The paper uses a 20×20 grid for pixel control. What was the rationale for choosing this specific grid size? How would the performance change with different grid sizes?

3. In the reward prediction task, the paper samples sequences to ensure equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards. How does this compare to other sampling strategies, such as prioritized replay based on the magnitude of reward?

4. The paper claims UNREAL is "substantially more robust to hyperparameter settings than A3C." Can you provide more details on how this robustness manifests across different hyperparameter values, particularly for the task weights (λVR, λPC, λRP)?

5. The paper states that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." It would be valuable to see a more detailed analysis of why input reconstruction hurts final performance, as this could provide insights into the design of future auxiliary tasks.
