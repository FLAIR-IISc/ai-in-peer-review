

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a Single Agent to Master Multiple Tasks"

## Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces the UNREAL agent, which combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) and reward prediction tasks with the standard A3C reinforcement learning framework. The authors claim that these auxiliary tasks significantly improve data efficiency, learning speed, and robustness to hyperparameters on both Labyrinth and Atari benchmarks. The paper presents empirical results showing the UNREAL agent achieves more than double the human-normalized mean performance of A3C on Labyrinth and substantial improvements on Atari.

## Decision
Reject.

## Supporting Arguments

The paper's primary weakness lies in its insufficient empirical validation of the proposed auxiliary tasks against established baselines. While the authors claim to "more than double" previous results, the paper fails to provide adequate comparison with other auxiliary task approaches that have been thoroughly studied in the literature. For example:

1. The paper compares against a basic A3C baseline but fails to compare against other established methods that use auxiliary tasks, such as the UVFA and successor representation approaches cited in the related work. The paper claims their auxiliary control tasks are better than "simply predicting immediate pixel changes" but doesn't establish how their approach differs from or improves upon prior work in the field.

2. The ablation studies are insufficient. The paper shows that combining all three auxiliary tasks works best, but it doesn't properly isolate the contribution of each component. For instance, the "pixel control" task is described as controlling "the central 80×80 crop of the inputs" with a "20×20 grid of non-overlapping 4×4 cells" but doesn't establish why this specific grid size or spatial structure is optimal. The paper also doesn't provide statistical significance testing for the reported performance gains.

3. The comparison with unsupervised learning baselines (pixel reconstruction and input change prediction) is limited to just three Labyrinth levels, which is insufficient to draw general conclusions about the effectiveness of the proposed approach.

4. The paper makes strong claims about "more than doubling" performance but doesn't provide a clear comparison to the most recent state-of-the-art methods on Labyrinth. The paper states UNREAL "surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents, i.e. A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN," but doesn't provide evidence that Prioritized Dueling DQN was previously the state-of-the-art on Labyrinth.

The paper's approach appears to be a combination of existing ideas (pixel changes, feature control, reward prediction) rather than a fundamentally new contribution. The authors cite related work but fail to establish how their specific implementation differs meaningfully from previous approaches that have already explored similar auxiliary tasks.

## Additional Feedback

To strengthen the paper, the authors should:
1. Conduct more comprehensive ablation studies comparing each auxiliary task against established baselines (UVFA, successor representations, etc.) across multiple environments.
2. Provide statistical significance testing for all performance claims.
3. Include a more thorough comparison with other auxiliary task approaches that have been previously studied in the literature.
4. Clarify the theoretical motivation for the specific design choices (e.g., why 20×20 grid for pixel control, why feature control uses the second hidden layer).
5. Better contextualize the claimed performance gains within the current state of the art, rather than simply stating "more than double."

## Questions for Authors

1. How do your pixel control and feature control tasks specifically differ from the UVFA and successor representation approaches cited in Section 1? Why are your implementations superior?

2. The paper states "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made the final scores worse when compared to vanilla A3C." What is the theoretical basis for this claim, and why would input reconstruction hurt final performance? The paper provides no explanation for this counterintuitive finding.

3. The paper claims UNREAL "surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents" on Atari, but the paper doesn't cite a specific state-of-the-art method for comparison. Could you clarify which method is considered the current state-of-the-art on Atari, and provide a direct comparison to that method?

4. The paper states UNREAL achieves "more than twice the final human-normalised mean performance of A3C" on Labyrinth, but the human-normalized scores are 54% to 87%. Could you clarify what the "twice" claim refers to, as 54% to 87% is not a doubling?

The paper presents an interesting combination of auxiliary tasks but fails to provide sufficient evidence to support its strong claims. The results are not rigorously validated against established baselines, and the paper does not clearly establish why this specific combination of auxiliary tasks represents a meaningful advance over existing work. Without more thorough validation and comparison, the paper's claims cannot be confidently accepted.
