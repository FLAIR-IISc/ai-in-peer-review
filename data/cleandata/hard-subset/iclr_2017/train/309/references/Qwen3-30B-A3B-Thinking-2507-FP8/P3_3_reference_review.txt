

# Peer Review: UNREAL - Auxiliary Tasks for Reinforcement Learning

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution

This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Rewards), a novel architecture that enhances deep reinforcement learning agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks designed to improve representation learning rather than directly optimizing for the main reward. The authors propose three specific auxiliary tasks: (1) pixel control (maximizing changes in specific image regions), (2) feature control (maximizing activation of specific hidden units), and (3) reward prediction (predicting immediate rewards from recent observations). The UNREAL agent combines these with A3C, demonstrating significant improvements in data efficiency, final performance, and robustness across both 3D Labyrinth environments and Atari games. The key contribution is showing that auxiliary tasks focused solely on representation learning can dramatically improve RL performance without directly optimizing for the main reward.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution with compelling results on challenging environments, and the auxiliary task approach is well-motivated within the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments

The paper's main strength lies in its comprehensive empirical validation. The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive: UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs. 54% for A3C), with a 10-18x speedup in learning and requiring less than 10% of the data. The ablation studies convincingly show that each auxiliary task contributes to the final performance, with pixel control providing the most significant boost. The Atari results are also strong (880% mean human-normalized score), demonstrating the approach's generalizability.

The paper is well-placed within the literature. The related work section thoroughly contextualizes the contributions against temporal abstractions (options), Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and recent auxiliary tasks. The key distinction is made clear: UNREAL uses auxiliary tasks solely for representation learning, not for direct reward maximization, which differentiates it from prior work like UVFAs that use pseudo-rewards for planning.

The methodology is sound and well-executed. The use of off-policy learning for auxiliary tasks (Q-learning) while maintaining on-policy learning for the main task is a reasonable design choice. The reward prediction task's skewed sampling (50% rewarding events) is a practical solution to sparse reward problems, and the experiments properly evaluate the robustness to hyperparameters.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper makes a strong contribution, several improvements would strengthen it:

1. **Theoretical Justification**: The paper lacks theoretical grounding for why these specific auxiliary tasks improve representation learning. The authors should discuss how these tasks relate to known representation learning principles (e.g., predictive coding, information bottleneck). This would make the contribution more generalizable beyond the specific tasks tested.

2. **Comparison to Recent Work**: The Atari results claim to surpass "state-of-the-art," but the paper doesn't compare to more recent methods like Rainbow or IMPALA. The authors should update their comparisons to include the most recent SOTA for Atari.

3. **Feature Control Clarification**: The "feature control" task is less well-explained than pixel control. The paper should provide more details about how they select which hidden units to control, and why this approach works better than simply using the features directly.

4. **Limitations Discussion**: The paper should discuss potential limitations of UNREAL, such as how the approach might scale to even more complex environments or whether the auxiliary tasks could become detrimental in certain settings.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper states that "the agents do not make any direct use of the pseudo-reward maximising policies they learn." However, the reward prediction task directly predicts the immediate reward. How does this not bias the agent's representation toward the specific reward structure of the training environments, potentially harming generalization?

2. In the reward prediction task, the authors sample sequences to overrepresent rewarding events (P(rτ ≠ 0) = 0.5). What evidence do you have that this specific sampling strategy is optimal? Have you tested other sampling distributions?

3. The paper claims UNREAL is more robust to hyperparameters, but the figures show some variance. Could you provide a statistical analysis (e.g., p-values) showing that the robustness improvement is statistically significant?

4. The reward prediction task uses a 3-frame sequence to predict the next reward. Why was this specific sequence length chosen? How would the performance change with longer or shorter sequences?

5. The paper mentions that "input reconstruction hurt final performance" compared to pixel control. Could you provide more insight into why this is the case? Is it simply that reconstruction focuses on irrelevant visual details, or is there a deeper representation learning principle at play?

In summary, this is a strong paper that makes a significant empirical contribution to the field of reinforcement learning. The results are impressive, the methodology is sound, and the approach is well-motivated. With the suggested clarifications and theoretical grounding, this would be an excellent contribution to ICLR.
