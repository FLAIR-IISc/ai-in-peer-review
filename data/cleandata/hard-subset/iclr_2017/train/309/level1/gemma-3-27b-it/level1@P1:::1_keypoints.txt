- Decision: Accept.

- Strong empirical results: large gains on Atari (880% of human) and Labyrinth (10× speedup, 87% human), showing clear advancement.

- Well-motivated approach: positions work within RL literature, links to temporal abstractions/auxiliary tasks, and uses an intuitive multi-skill learning analogy.

- Rigorous experimental evaluation: comparisons to baselines, ablations, multiple seeds, hyperparameter sensitivity, visualizations, and evaluation on both 2D and 3D environments.

- Clarify hyperparameter tuning: provide details on search method, search space, and computational cost of tuning.

- More detailed ablation study: quantify individual contributions of each auxiliary task, especially in Labyrinth, ideally in a granular table.

- Report computational cost: specify CPUs/GPUs, wall-clock training time, and resource requirements for practical feasibility.

- Report negative results: disclose cases where auxiliary tasks did not help or harmed learning to present limitations.

- Theoretical justification: include more formal reasoning for why learning multiple pseudo-rewards produces better representations.

- Question 1: Why is learning to control pixels better than predicting pixel changes — is it because policies directly influence the environment?

- Question 2: Can you quantify hyperparameter robustness beyond scatter plots (e.g., variances across settings for A3C vs UNREAL)?

- Question 3: How do weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) affect performance — was sensitivity analysis done and what are optimal ranges?

- Question 4: For the replay buffer, is the rewarding vs non-rewarding split done before or after any TD-error prioritization, and could prioritization skew representation?

- Question 5: How does the choice of auxiliary tasks (pixel change, network features, reward prediction) influence performance — were other tasks considered and why were they rejected?
