This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that learns to maximize cumulative reward alongside a multitude of pseudo-reward functions simultaneously. The core idea is to develop a more effective representation by leveraging reinforcement learning to predict and control features of the sensorimotor stream. The approach combines auxiliary control tasks (pixel changes, network features) with auxiliary reward prediction, coupled with an experience replay mechanism. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both Atari and a 3D Labyrinth environment, exceeding state-of-the-art results and exhibiting faster learning and improved robustness.

**Reject.** While the empirical results are impressive, the paper lacks sufficient clarity in describing the interplay between the various components and the specific rationale behind several design choices. The novelty, while present, is somewhat incremental, building upon existing work (UVFA, Horde, Successor Representations) without a clearly articulated novel contribution beyond the specific combination of techniques and the scale of performance gains.

The paper's primary weakness lies in the lack of detailed justification for the specific auxiliary tasks chosen and their relative weighting. While the intuition behind predicting reward and controlling pixels/features is plausible, the paper doesn’t provide a compelling argument for *why* these tasks are particularly effective, or how their selection impacts the learned representation. The ablation studies offer some insight, but a more systematic investigation into the contribution of each component would be beneficial. Furthermore, the weighting of the different loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP) is determined empirically, and the sensitivity of the results to these hyperparameters is not adequately explored beyond the robustness testing shown in Figure 8. The improvements reported on the Labyrinth environment are substantial, but the complexity of that environment makes it difficult to assess whether the gains are attributable to the UNREAL architecture or specific characteristics of the game. The paper would benefit from demonstrating similar improvements on a wider range of Atari games or more diverse environments.

The description of the deconvolutional network used for pixel control is somewhat opaque. While the architecture is outlined, the motivation for the specific choices (e.g., filter sizes, stride) is unclear. Similarly, the rationale for using a separate feedforward network for reward prediction, rather than integrating it into the LSTM, could be better explained. The connection between the auxiliary tasks and the final policy is not fully elucidated. How does learning to control pixels or network features ultimately translate into improved performance on the primary task?

**Additional Feedback (for improvement):**

*   **Clarify the novelty:** Explicitly state how UNREAL differs from and improves upon existing methods like UVFA and Horde. A table summarizing the key differences would be helpful.
*   **Provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations:** Visualize the learned features or provide quantitative measures of their quality. Are the features learned by UNREAL more disentangled or informative than those learned by baseline agents?
*   **Conduct a more thorough ablation study:** Systematically evaluate the impact of each auxiliary task and hyperparameter on performance.
*   **Investigate the transferability of the learned representations:** Can the representations learned by UNREAL be transferred to new tasks or environments more effectively than those learned by baseline agents?
*   **Expand the experimental evaluation:** Include results on a wider range of environments and tasks.
*   **Provide a more detailed explanation of the deconvolutional network architecture and its motivation.**
*   **Explain why a separate feedforward network is used for reward prediction.**
*   **Discuss the computational cost of UNREAL compared to baseline agents.**

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  What is the theoretical basis for choosing the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction)? Are there any guiding principles or criteria used to select these tasks?
2.  How sensitive are the results to the weighting of the different loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP)? Could an automatic weighting scheme be used to optimize these parameters?
3.  Can you provide examples of the learned features and demonstrate how they relate to the primary task?
4.  What is the computational overhead of UNREAL compared to a standard A3C agent? Does the increased complexity impact training time or resource requirements?
5.  The paper mentions that learning to reconstruct inputs can be detrimental. Is there a point where reconstruction *could* be beneficial (e.g., when dealing with noisy or incomplete observations)?
