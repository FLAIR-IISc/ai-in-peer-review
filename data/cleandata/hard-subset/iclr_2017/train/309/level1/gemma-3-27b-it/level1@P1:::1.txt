This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting a standard A3C agent with auxiliary tasks designed to learn a richer representation of the environment. The core idea is to simultaneously train policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions, alongside the primary task reward, fostering a more adaptable and robust agent. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Decision: Accept**

The paper presents a compelling and well-executed idea with strong empirical results. The improvements over existing methods, particularly the substantial gains in data efficiency observed in the Labyrinth environment, are significant and warrant acceptance. The approach seems well-motivated and grounded in existing literature, and the experimental setup appears rigorous.

**Supporting Arguments:**

1. **Strong Empirical Results:** The performance gains on both Atari and Labyrinth are substantial. Achieving 880% expert human performance on Atari and a 10x speedup in learning with 87% human performance on Labyrinth demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach. These results are not marginal improvements; they represent a clear advancement in the field.

2. **Well-Motivated Approach:** The paper successfully positions the work within the existing landscape of reinforcement learning research. It clearly connects the ideas to related work on temporal abstractions, auxiliary tasks, and representation learning, correctly identifying gaps in previous approaches and explaining how UNREAL addresses them. The analogy of a baby learning multiple skills to achieve various goals is particularly insightful and motivates the multi-objective learning framework.

3. **Rigorous Experimental Evaluation:** The authors present a thorough experimental evaluation, including comparisons to baselines, ablation studies, and hyperparameter sensitivity analysis. The use of multiple random seeds and reporting average performance across different settings strengthen the validity of the findings. The inclusion of visualizations and a video further enhances the understanding and reproducibility of the results. The choice to evaluate on both Atari and a complex 3D environment demonstrates the generalizability of the proposed method.

**Additional Feedback:**

* **Clarification of Hyperparameter Tuning:** While the authors mention hyperparameter tuning, more details regarding the search space and method used (e.g., random search, grid search, Bayesian optimization) would be beneficial. Specifying the computational cost associated with the tuning process would also be helpful.
* **Ablation Study Details:**  A more detailed breakdown of the individual contributions of each auxiliary task in the Labyrinth environment would be valuable. For example, how much does each auxiliary task contribute to the overall performance gain when combined with A3C? This could be presented in a more granular table format.
* **Computational Cost:** Providing more details regarding the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent (e.g., number of CPUs, GPUs, training time) would give readers a better understanding of the practical feasibility of the approach.
* **Negative Results:** Reporting any instances where the auxiliary tasks *didn't* improve performance or even hindered learning would provide a more balanced view of the method's limitations.
* **Theoretical Justification:** While the empirical results are strong, a more formal theoretical justification for why learning multiple pseudo-rewards leads to better representations could further strengthen the paper.

**Questions for the Authors:**

1.  You mention that learning to control pixels is better than simply predicting pixel changes. Can you elaborate on *why* you believe this is the case? Is it related to the policy directly influencing the environment, rather than simply observing it?
2.  In the Labyrinth experiments, you state that the robustness to hyperparameters improved with UNREAL. Can you quantify this improvement beyond the scatter plots in Figure 8? For example, what is the variance in performance across different hyperparameter settings for A3C versus UNREAL?
3.  What is the impact of the weighting terms (λVR, λPC, λRP) on performance? Did you perform a sensitivity analysis of these weights, and if so, what are the optimal ranges for each weight?
4.  Regarding the replay buffer: Is the split between rewarding and non-rewarding subsets performed *before* or *after* prioritization based on TD error (if any)? Could prioritization impact the equal representation of zero and non-zero rewards?
5.  How does the choice of the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction) influence the performance? Were other auxiliary tasks considered and rejected, and if so, why?
