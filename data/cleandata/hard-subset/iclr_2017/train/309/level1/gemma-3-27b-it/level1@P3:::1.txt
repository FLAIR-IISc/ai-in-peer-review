This paper introduces UNREAL, a deep reinforcement learning agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously, alongside the primary task reward. The core idea is to create a richer, more informative representation by encouraging the agent to control various features of the sensorimotor stream. The authors demonstrate significant improvements in performance and learning speed on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, surpassing state-of-the-art results. The approach appears well-motivated and the experimental results are compelling.

**Reject** – While the paper presents a strong contribution, the substantial performance gains are reported with limited detail on hyperparameter tuning and ablation studies *within* the Atari experiments. The Labyrinth results are well-documented and convincing, but the leap in Atari performance, claiming to surpass existing SOTA by a considerable margin, warrants a more thorough investigation of the hyperparameter space and a more granular breakdown of the contribution of each auxiliary task in that domain.

The paper is strongly motivated. The authors correctly identify the limitations of solely maximizing extrinsic reward and propose a sensible approach to address them.  The connection to related work (UVFA, Successor Representations, auxiliary prediction tasks) is well-established and clearly positioned. They convincingly argue that learning to control various aspects of the environment fosters a more useful and adaptable representation.  The architectural design, incorporating A3C with auxiliary tasks and experience replay, seems well-considered and justified. The experimental setup in Labyrinth is comprehensive, showcasing clear improvements over A3C and various ablations. The reported 10x speedup is particularly noteworthy.

However, the Atari results require more scrutiny. While the paper states that hyperparameters were retuned, the details are sparse. The claim of 880% expert human performance is striking, and I'm concerned that the hyperparameters may have been significantly optimized specifically for this set of games. The performance breakdown for individual Atari games isn’t provided, making it difficult to assess the robustness of the gains. The relatively small table displaying the average performance curves offers limited insight into the variability of results. A more detailed ablation study *within* the Atari framework – systematically removing each auxiliary task and reporting performance – is needed to understand their individual contributions and potential interactions. Furthermore, a statistical significance test comparing UNREAL's performance against baselines in Atari would strengthen the findings. The impact of the replay buffer weighting (skewing towards rewarding events) on the Atari results should be investigated more explicitly. Did this weighting significantly influence performance compared to a standard replay buffer?

**Additional Feedback (for improvement, not part of the decision):**

*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Provide a more detailed account of the hyperparameter search process for both Labyrinth and Atari. Include the ranges explored for each hyperparameter and the methodology used for selection (e.g., random search, grid search).
*   **Ablation Studies (Atari):** Perform more targeted ablation studies on Atari, systematically removing each auxiliary task (pixel control, reward prediction) and reporting the resulting performance.
*   **Statistical Significance:** Report statistical significance tests (e.g., t-tests) to demonstrate the significance of the performance gains on both Labyrinth and Atari.
*   **Individual Game Performance (Atari):**  Present a table or plot showing the performance of UNREAL and the baselines on individual Atari games. This will provide a clearer picture of the robustness of the approach.
*   **Replay Buffer Analysis (Atari):** Quantify the impact of the skewed replay buffer on performance. Compare results with and without the weighting.
*   **Computational Cost:** Discuss the increased computational cost associated with training UNREAL compared to A3C. How does the added complexity affect training time and resource requirements?
*   **Visualization of Learned Features:** Provide visualizations of the features learned by the auxiliary tasks (e.g., the pixel change policies, the activated network features). This could offer valuable insights into how these tasks contribute to the overall representation.

**Questions for the authors:**

1.  Could you provide a more detailed breakdown of the hyperparameter search process for the Atari experiments, including the ranges explored for each hyperparameter and the selection methodology?
2.  How much additional computation (e.g., training time, GPU hours) was required to train UNREAL compared to the baseline A3C agent, both for Labyrinth and Atari?
3.  Did you observe any cases where an auxiliary task *degraded* performance on specific Atari games? If so, how did you address this?
4.  Can you elaborate on the choice of the three reward prediction classes (zero, positive, negative)? Were other classification schemes considered?
5.  The paper mentions a skewing of the replay buffer towards rewarding events.  What percentage of samples in the replay buffer were derived from rewarding events?
