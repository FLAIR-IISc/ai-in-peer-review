This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL with auxiliary tasks focused on controlling different features of the sensorimotor stream. The core idea – that learning to control various aspects of the environment can lead to more robust and efficient learning – is well-motivated and intuitively appealing. The authors demonstrate impressive results on both Atari and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, significantly outperforming existing state-of-the-art methods. The strong empirical validation is a major strength of this work.

**Reject.** While the paper presents a compelling approach and strong results, the description of the experimental setup, particularly regarding hyperparameter tuning and the contribution of each auxiliary task, lacks sufficient detail for complete confidence. The robustness claim, while supported by figures, requires more rigorous justification given the complexity of the system.

The primary reason for rejection is the lack of clarity around the hyperparameter search process. The authors state they used 50 random hyperparameter settings, but don’t detail *how* these were sampled or the range explored for each hyperparameter. Reporting best performance across these settings is useful, but doesn't fully address whether the gains are sensitive to specific parameter choices. The performance improvements could be driven by lucky hyperparameter selections rather than inherent robustness. 

Secondly, while the ablation studies show that each auxiliary task contributes to performance, the relative contribution of each task is not entirely clear. The paper doesn't quantify the impact of, for example, removing pixel control versus reward prediction. A more granular analysis would strengthen the argument for the effectiveness of each component.

The presented results are very strong, and the idea of learning a diverse set of skills to improve representation learning is promising. However, without more clarity on the experimental details and a more precise understanding of the contribution of each component, it’s difficult to confidently accept the paper.

**Additional Feedback (to improve the paper):**

*   **Hyperparameter Details:** Provide a detailed description of the hyperparameter search space used for both Atari and Labyrinth. Include the ranges explored for learning rates, entropy coefficients, and weighting factors for the auxiliary losses. Consider reporting the standard deviation of the results across the 50 runs to further demonstrate robustness.
*   **Ablation Study Granularity:**  Present a more detailed ablation study, showing the performance impact of removing each auxiliary task individually *and* in combination. A table summarizing the results would be beneficial.
*   **Qualitative Analysis:** Include more qualitative examples illustrating what the agent learns through the auxiliary tasks. Visualizing the activations or policies learned for specific pixel control or feature control tasks could provide valuable insight.
*   **Computational Cost:**  Discuss the increased computational cost associated with training the UNREAL agent compared to standard A3C. Are the gains in performance worth the added computational burden?
*   **Clarify “Robustness”:** Define more precisely what is meant by "robustness" in this context. Is it robustness to hyperparameter settings, initial conditions, or perturbations in the environment?

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   Could you provide more details about the hyperparameter search process? Specifically, how were the ranges for each hyperparameter determined, and what sampling method was used?
*   Can you quantify the relative contribution of each auxiliary task (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) to the overall performance improvement?
*   What is the computational overhead of the UNREAL agent compared to a standard A3C agent, in terms of training time and memory usage?
*   In the Labyrinth experiments, how were the levels chosen, and is there any bias in the level selection that might favor the UNREAL agent?
*   Are there cases where adding certain auxiliary tasks *hurt* performance, and if so, why might this be the case?
