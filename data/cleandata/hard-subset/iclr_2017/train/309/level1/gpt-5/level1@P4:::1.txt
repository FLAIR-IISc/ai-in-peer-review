Summary of claims and contributions
- The paper introduces UNREAL, an A3C-based deep RL agent augmented with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The agent jointly optimizes: (i) auxiliary control tasks that learn Q-functions for pseudo-rewards (pixel control and feature control) off-policy from a replay buffer; (ii) a reward prediction task that oversamples rewarding events to densify reward-related features; and (iii) replayed value regression to enhance value learning.
- The design aims to exploit richer training signals from the sensorimotor stream beyond sparse extrinsic rewards, with the auxiliary tasks shaping shared representations without directly biasing the policy/value estimates.
- Empirically, UNREAL achieves substantial gains: on 13 Labyrinth levels it improves mean human-normalized performance from 54% to 87% and provides ~10× speedup to reach A3C’s best score; on 57 Atari games it reaches 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance, surpassing prior SOTA (A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN). Ablations show each auxiliary component provides benefits, and that “control” tasks outperform pixel reconstruction or immediate-input-change prediction baselines.

Decision
Accept, due to strong empirical improvements with thorough ablations and a well-motivated, technically plausible design that advances representation learning in deep RL.

Supporting arguments
- Problem and motivation: The paper targets the challenge of learning effective representations in environments with sparse extrinsic reward. The central idea—leveraging multiple pseudo-reward control tasks and reward-focused auxiliary signals—is clearly motivated and well positioned with respect to options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, model-based prediction, and recent auxiliary prediction work in 3D RL.
- Methodology and technical soundness:
  - The auxiliary control tasks are implemented as off-policy n-step Q-learning with a replay buffer, sharing the convolutional and recurrent trunk with the base A3C policy/value network. Pixel control via a spatial deconvolutional head (with dueling parametrization) is a concrete, novel instantiation; feature control is a plausible variant.
  - Reward prediction oversamples rare positive/negative rewards from replay while not directly influencing the policy or value estimate, mitigating bias concerns. Value function replay further stabilizes/improves value estimates by additional supervised regression.
  - The overall training objective is a weighted sum of on-policy A3C, off-policy control Q-losses, replayed value regression, and reward prediction. Although convergence guarantees are not provided (as is typical in deep RL with function approximation), the design choices are standard and reasonable; the paper acknowledges off-policy learning and replay tradeoffs, and the implementation includes limited-size recent replay to align with non-stationarity.
  - The use of a recurrent agent and replay for off-policy auxiliary tasks on sequences is briefly justified by considering the hidden activations as part of state; more implementation details are provided in the appendix.
- Empirical support and rigor:
  - Strong results on diverse domains (Labyrinth and Atari) with comprehensive ablations. The Labyrinth evaluation categorizes tasks (navigation, procedural generalization, laser-tag), and reports both final scores and data efficiency improvements. The Atari evaluation spans 57 games with hyperparameter sweeps; robustness plots across hyperparameters support claims of stability.
  - Ablations directly compare control vs prediction vs reconstruction, supporting the claim that learning to control aspects of perception is more useful for representation than reconstructive objectives in these settings.
  - Implementation details for architectures, losses, replay, and hyperparameter ranges are given, aiding reproducibility.
- Novelty:
  - While related to Horde/UVFA/successor features and auxiliary prediction works, the paper’s combination of off-policy auxiliary control tasks for representation shaping, reward-focused prediction with rebalanced sampling, and value replay into a single, end-to-end RL agent is novel and impactful for 2017. Pixel control formulated as many parallel spatial pseudo-rewards is particularly distinctive.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not central to the decision)
- Clarify the evaluation protocol details for Atari: total training frames per run, number of environment steps/updates for selection of “top 3” seeds per game, and whether early stopping or fixed budgets were used. Report mean/median across all seeds at a fixed frame budget alongside “top-k” results to minimize selection bias (you partially do this; consolidating in the main text would strengthen claims).
- Recurrent off-policy training: Elaborate on how LSTM states are handled for replayed sequences (e.g., sequence length, burn-in, truncation) and whether the auxiliary updates backpropagate through time to previous hidden states. Discuss any observed instability and mitigation.
- Reward prediction oversampling: Provide a sensitivity analysis to the rebalance ratio (you fix P(r ≠ 0)=0.5); show performance as a function of skew to verify robustness and avoid overfitting to reward-adjacent features.
- Pixel control design: Discuss the effect of dynamic textures and visually noisy environments (e.g., Labyrinth’s animated textures). A brief analysis showing that the agent does not learn degenerate “chase flicker” behaviors would be useful, and/or variants of pixel control that normalize for exogenous motion.
- Broaden ablations on Atari: Include per-game ablations (pixel control on/off, reward prediction on/off, value replay on/off) to show component contributions in 2D domains.
- Comparisons: Consider including results against A3C with prioritized replay or A3C with model-based auxiliary prediction (e.g., action-conditional video prediction) to further isolate the benefit of control-oriented auxiliary tasks versus predictive ones.
- Reproducibility: Summarize compute resources (number of actors, wall-clock time) and release pseudo-reward definitions/code if possible.

Questions for the authors
- How many total environment frames were used for Atari training per game and per method? Are the reported scores at a fixed budget or best across training?
- How exactly are LSTM hidden states initialized and unrolled for replayed auxiliary updates? Do you use burn-in to reconstruct hidden state from replayed sequences before computing losses?
- What is the contribution of value function replay versus reward prediction when pixel control is disabled on Atari? An ablation table for Atari would help.
- How sensitive is performance to λPC, λVR, and λRP? Beyond the hyperparameter sweeps, is there evidence that UNREAL is robust to these weights across tasks?
- For pixel control, did you evaluate variants that discount or normalize dynamic background changes (e.g., optical flow normalization)? Do such variants change outcomes on Labyrinth with animated textures?
- What human baseline (“expert human”) was used for normalization on Atari (number of trials, guidelines, score variance)? Is the same human baseline used as in prior work to ensure comparability?
- Have you tested UNREAL on extremely sparse-reward Atari titles (e.g., Montezuma’s Revenge)? If not, do you anticipate the current auxiliary tasks suffice, or would additional exploration bonuses be required?
- Do the learned auxiliary control policies have utility at test time (e.g., as options)? Any preliminary results on composing them for hierarchical control?
