1. Summary of contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with unsupervised auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness. The key ideas are:
- Auxiliary control tasks learned off-policy via n-step Q-learning from a replay buffer: pixel control (maximizing local pixel change in a spatial grid) and feature control (maximizing internal unit activations). These tasks are used solely to shape shared representations, not as options for action.
- Reward-focused auxiliary tasks: immediate reward prediction trained on a skewed replay distribution to counteract reward sparsity; and value function replay, providing extra value regression updates off-policy.
- A combined loss that mixes on-policy A3C updates with off-policy auxiliary updates, all sharing a CNN-LSTM trunk. The pixel/feature control heads are deconvolutional, exploiting spatial structure.

Empirically, UNREAL substantially improves over strong baselines on 13 3D Labyrinth levels (mean human-normalized score 87%, 10× speedup to A3C best performance) and on 57 Atari games (mean 880% human, median 250%), with compelling ablations showing the contribution of each auxiliary component. The paper is well written, thoughtfully motivated, and places its ideas in context with options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations, and model-based auxiliary predictions.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons:
- Strong, broad empirical evidence that auxiliary control and reward-focused tasks significantly improve data efficiency and robustness over A3C and Dueling DQN, with careful ablations.
- Clear, well-motivated, and novel integration of off-policy auxiliary tasks with on-policy actor-critic that yields pragmatic and meaningful performance improvements in visually rich, partially observed domains.

3. Supporting arguments
Addressing the ICLR criteria:

a) Specific question/problem:
How can an RL agent exploit abundant non-reward signals in the sensorimotor stream to learn representations that accelerate and stabilize learning of sparse extrinsic rewards? The paper tackles this by defining pseudo-reward control tasks and reward-focused prediction tasks and optimizing them jointly with the main policy.

b) Motivation and placement in literature:
- Motivation is compelling: pure reward-maximization struggles with sparsity; unsupervised reconstruction may align poorly with control; predicting and controlling features of experience plausibly aligns with downstream goals.
- Prior works (options, Horde, UVFAs, successor representations) learn across many reward functions, often for transfer or temporal abstraction. The key differentiator here is using many auxiliary RL tasks purely to sculpt the representation shared with the extrinsic policy.
- The design choice to use off-policy Q-learning for the auxiliary control tasks while keeping the base policy on-policy (A3C) is well reasoned and addresses data efficiency without biasing the policy gradient. Reward prediction’s skewed sampling is justified as it shapes features without biasing the policy or value update.

c) Do results support claims and are they rigorous?
- Evidence breadth: 13 diverse 3D Labyrinth tasks and 57 Atari games is strong for 2017 standards.
- Quantitative gains: large speed-ups and human-normalized score improvements; improved hyperparameter robustness; ablations showing pixel control > predicting pixel change > reconstruction, and added gains from reward prediction and value replay.
- Correctness: The method is described in sufficient detail (network, losses, replay, hyperparameter ranges). Mixing on- and off-policy updates is nontrivial but empirically stable with small replay windows and clear separation of objectives.
- Caveats:
  • The Atari evaluation protocol details (training frames, evaluation regime, no-op starts/human starts, life-loss termination, reward clipping) are not fully specified; fairness against Dueling DQN and A3C hinges on such details.
  • Reporting top-3 hyperparameter averages per game could advantage UNREAL if baselines were not equally tuned, though robustness plots mitigate this concern.
  • The mean 880% human score can be dominated by a few outlier games; medians are reported and look solid, but a more granular per-game table (some shown) and significance analysis would strengthen the claim.
Despite these, the overall empirical case is persuasive and materially advances state of the art.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve the paper; not necessarily decision-critical)
- Clarify Atari evaluation protocol: training budget in frames, evaluation episodes and seeds, no-op/human starts, sticky actions (if any), reward clipping, frame skip, and termination on loss of life. This aids reproducibility and fairness.
- Calibrate contributions: include a full ablation on Atari (e.g., only reward prediction, only value replay, only pixel control), not just Labyrinth, to confirm generality across domains.
- Analyze gradient interference: report if and how auxiliary losses interfere with the policy/value gradients (e.g., gradient cosine similarity, per-loss learning dynamics) and whether adaptive loss weighting helps.
- Report sensitivity to λPC, λRP, λVR and replay buffer size; discuss general guidelines for setting these across heterogeneous games.
- Provide per-game Atari results alongside human and A3C baselines in the main paper (or a consolidated appendix table) and mark outliers to temper the mean.
- Discuss computational cost: wall-clock time and throughput relative to A3C. Speedup in data efficiency is great; compute efficiency would add practical context.
- Consider transfer: does the representation learned via auxiliary control facilitate faster adaptation to new reward functions (e.g., UVFA-like generalization)?
- Minor: fix some reference formatting glitches and typos; clarify that “deconvolution” refers to transposed convolution.

Questions for the authors
- What are the exact Atari evaluation settings (training frames, no-op/human starts, life-loss termination, sticky actions, reward clipping, evaluation episodes), and are they identical across A3C, Dueling DQN, and UNREAL?
- How many frames were used to train on Atari/Labyrinth, and how many evaluation episodes/seeds were averaged for the reported scores?
- How were the “top 3” hyperparameters selected per game? Were baselines (A3C, Dueling DQN) given the same hyperparameter search budget and selection criterion?
- How sensitive are results to λPC and the choice of pixel-control grid/cell size? Does feature control match or surpass pixel control when combined with other tasks?
- Do auxiliary tasks ever hurt final performance on specific games? If so, can you characterize when and why (e.g., visually static games, reward-dense tasks)?
- Can you report wall-clock speedups and compute overhead of auxiliary heads and replay?
- Does the representation learned via auxiliary control transfer to new reward functions within the same environment, or to new but similar environments?
