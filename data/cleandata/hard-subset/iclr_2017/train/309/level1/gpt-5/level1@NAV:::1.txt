1. Summary and contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with off-policy auxiliary tasks that shape shared representations to improve data efficiency and robustness, especially under sparse extrinsic rewards. Two families of auxiliary objectives are introduced: control tasks that learn policies to maximize pseudo-rewards (not executed at test time), notably pixel-control and feature-control; and reward-focused tasks, notably immediate reward prediction trained with replay skewed toward rewarding events. The agent also employs value function replay. A single joint loss combines on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary control, producing a representation that continues learning in the absence of extrinsic rewards but can be focused toward reward-relevant features. UNREAL substantially outperforms strong baselines: on Labyrinth it yields ~10× speedup and 87% human-normalized performance; on 57 Atari games it achieves very strong mean/median human-normalized scores and improved hyperparameter robustness. Ablations indicate control > prediction > reconstruction.

2. Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (i) Significant, consistent empirical gains in both performance and data efficiency across diverse domains, supported by ablations; (ii) Well-motivated and well-situated approach that unifies auxiliary control and reward-centric prediction with on/off-policy learning in a simple, general framework.

3. Supporting arguments
- Problem clarity: addressing representation learning and exploration under sparse rewards via multi-task pseudo-rewards is compelling and timely.  
- Literature placement: distinguishes from options/Horde/UVFA/successor representations and prior auxiliary prediction work by using learned control purely to shape shared features.  
- Methodological soundness: combining on-policy A3C with off-policy Q-learning on replay is plausible and carefully engineered (task-specific heads, deconv for spatial control, prioritized sampling for reward prediction).  
- Evidence: Strong aggregate metrics, robustness sweeps, and informative ablations; control-based auxiliaries provide clear added value over prediction/reconstruction baselines.  
- Minor concerns: clarity on evaluation protocol details (e.g., Atari evaluation settings, seeds, compute), fairness of capacity vs baselines, and stability/justification of off-policy auxiliary updates.

4. Additional feedback (to help improve; not central to decision)
- Report compute/training time and sample efficiency curves against matched-capacity baselines (e.g., A3C+replay, A3C+prioritized replay) for fairness.  
- Provide sensitivity analyses for λPC, λRP, λVR and replay buffer size.  
- Expand feature-control ablations and analyze representation (e.g., probing tasks).  
- Discuss failure modes and domain dependence of pixel-change pseudo-rewards; consider transfer to novel tasks/goals.

Questions for authors
- What Atari evaluation protocol was used (no-op starts vs human starts, sticky actions, training frames, evaluation episodes)? How many seeds per game and how are “top-3” models selected?  
- Do auxiliary off-policy updates use any correction (e.g., importance sampling), and how sensitive is performance to this choice?  
- How is pixel-change reward normalized across games with different visual dynamics, and how critical is λPC scaling?  
- Does reward-prediction replay skew risk overfitting or unintended policy bias? Any diagnostics?  
- Can the learned auxiliary control policies be leveraged for exploration or transfer, beyond representation shaping?
