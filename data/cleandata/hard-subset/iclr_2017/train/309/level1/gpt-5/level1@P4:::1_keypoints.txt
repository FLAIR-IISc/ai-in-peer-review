- Introduces UNREAL: an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning, data efficiency, and robustness.
- Jointly optimizes off-policy auxiliary control tasks (pixel and feature control), reward prediction with oversampling, and replayed value regression.
- Aims to exploit sensorimotor stream signals to shape shared representations without directly biasing policy/value estimates.
- Shows strong empirical gains: Labyrinth mean human-normalized from 54% to 87% and ~10× speedup; Atari (57 games) reaches 880% mean and 250% median human-normalized performance.
- Ablations indicate each auxiliary component contributes and control tasks outperform reconstruction/prediction baselines.
- Decision: accept due to strong empirical improvements, thorough ablations, and a well-motivated, technically plausible design.
- Targets sparse-reward representation learning and is well positioned relative to options, Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and auxiliary prediction work.
- Auxiliary control implemented as off-policy n-step Q-learning with replay, sharing conv+recurrent trunk with A3C.
- Pixel control uses a spatial deconvolutional head with dueling parametrization; feature control is a plausible variant.
- Reward prediction oversamples rare rewards to avoid biasing policy/value but densify reward-related features.
- Value function replay provides additional supervised regression to stabilize/improve value estimates.
- Training objective is a weighted sum of on-policy A3C, off-policy control Q-losses, value replay, and reward prediction.
- Convergence guarantees are not provided but design choices are standard and reasonable for deep RL.
- Recurrent agent + replay rationale: hidden activations considered part of state; implementation details given in appendix.
- Empirical evaluation is rigorous: diverse domains, task categorization in Labyrinth, hyperparameter sweeps, and robustness plots.
- Ablations compare control vs prediction vs reconstruction, supporting control-oriented objectives for representation shaping.
- Implementation and hyperparameter details are provided to aid reproducibility.
- Novelty: combining off-policy auxiliary control, reward-focused prediction, and value replay end-to-end is novel; pixel control as parallel spatial pseudo-rewards is distinctive.
- Suggest clarifying Atari evaluation protocol: frames per run, selection procedure for top seeds, and reporting fixed-budget means/medians.
- Request more detail on recurrent off-policy training: handling of LSTM states, sequence lengths, burn-in, and stability mitigation.
- Ask for sensitivity analysis on reward-oversampling ratio (P(r ≠ 0)=0.5) to show robustness.
- Recommend analyzing pixel control in visually noisy/dynamic-texture environments to rule out degenerate behaviors and consider normalization variants.
- Suggest broader per-game ablations on Atari to quantify each component’s contribution in 2D domains.
- Recommend additional comparisons (e.g., A3C with prioritized replay or model-based prediction) and sharing compute/resources and pseudo-reward code for reproducibility.
- Questions: total environment frames and whether scores are at fixed budgets or best across training.
- Questions: how LSTM hidden states are initialized/unrolled for replayed updates and whether burn-in is used.
- Questions: ablation of value replay vs reward prediction when pixel control is disabled on Atari.
- Questions: sensitivity of performance to auxiliary loss weights (λPC, λVR, λRP).
- Questions: whether pixel control variants that normalize dynamic background changes were evaluated and their effects.
- Questions: which human baseline was used for Atari normalization and whether it matches prior work.
- Questions: applicability to extremely sparse games (e.g., Montezuma’s Revenge) and whether extra exploration bonuses are needed.
- Questions: whether learned auxiliary control policies are useful at test time (e.g., as options) or for hierarchical control.
