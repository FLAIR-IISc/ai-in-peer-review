Summary of contributions
- The paper proposes UNREAL, an A3C-based agent augmented with auxiliary tasks that shape the representation while the main policy is trained on extrinsic rewards. Two key families of auxiliary tasks are introduced: (i) control tasks that learn to maximize pseudo-rewards off-policy (pixel-control and feature-control), and (ii) reward-focused tasks that densify the signal without biasing the policy (reward prediction with rebalanced replay, plus value-function replay).
- Architecturally, the agent shares a CNN-LSTM backbone with separate deconvolutional “heads” for pixel/feature control and a separate feedforward head for reward prediction. Off-policy n-step Q-learning is used for auxiliary control with a short replay buffer; on-policy A3C is used for the main policy/value.
- Empirically, UNREAL achieves substantial gains in data efficiency, robustness to hyperparameters, and final performance on 13 visually rich 3D Labyrinth tasks, and surpasses prior state-of-the-art results on 57 Atari games (mean 880% human, median 250%), while also more than doubling A3C’s average human-normalized performance on Labyrinth (to 87%).
- The paper provides ablations (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay), comparisons to reconstruction and immediate change prediction baselines, and analyses of robustness across hyperparameters.

Decision: Accept
Key reasons: (1) Strong, consistent empirical improvements across diverse domains with careful ablations; (2) Well-motivated and relatively novel use of auxiliary control tasks to learn representations, with a clear and effective training recipe (off-policy replay for auxiliary tasks, on-policy A3C for the main objective).

Supporting arguments
1) What problem is tackled?
- How to improve data efficiency and robustness of deep RL, especially under sparse rewards, by exploiting richer training signals beyond extrinsic rewards. The central hypothesis is that learning to predict and control features of the sensorimotor stream yields representations better aligned with downstream control.

2) Is the approach well-motivated and well-placed in the literature?
- Motivation is clear: pixel-level reconstruction can emphasize irrelevant variation; control-oriented pseudo-tasks should encourage features that encode how actions change observations. This bridges ideas from auxiliary prediction (Horde), UVFAs/successor representations, and model learning, but departs by using many pseudo-reward–maximizing policies solely to shape a shared representation, not for direct option execution. The connection to prior art is appropriately discussed; the paper makes a distinct contribution by: (i) defining spatially grounded pixel/feature control with deconv heads; (ii) combining off-policy auxiliary Q-learning with on-policy A3C; (iii) rebalanced reward prediction to combat sparsity; and (iv) demonstrating gains in 3D FP environments.

3) Do the results support the claims, and are they rigorous?
- Evidence is strong: large, consistent gains in final performance and learning speed on 13 Labyrinth tasks with human-normalized scores and clear ablations; nontrivial gains on Atari with broad hyperparameter sweeps and robustness analyses. The comparison to alternative unsupervised objectives (reconstruction and immediate change prediction) is convincing and isolates the benefit of control-based auxiliary tasks. Reporting includes human-normalized scores, per-task breakdowns, and raw Atari scores in the appendix.
- Some caveats remain regarding compute fairness and evaluation protocol details (see feedback), but the core claims (data efficiency, robustness, improved final performance) are well-supported.

Additional feedback to improve the paper (helpful, not decisive)
- Compute and update fairness: The auxiliary tasks add off-policy updates per environment step. Please report wall-clock training time, total number of parameter updates, and an apples-to-apples compute budget comparison with A3C and Dueling DDQN. The sample-efficiency gains are compelling; quantifying compute overhead will strengthen the claims.
- Atari evaluation details: Clarify the evaluation protocol (no-op starts, life-loss termination, evaluation episode length, action repeat, and whether “expert human” differs from typical “human” baselines). Ensure consistency with prior work to contextualize the 880% figure.
- Ablations on Atari: Provide per-component ablations (pixel control, reward prediction, value replay) on a representative subset of Atari to verify that the improvements generalize beyond Labyrinth.
- Feature control: Results are preliminary; expanding coverage across Labyrinth and Atari would better establish its value and interaction with pixel control.
- Off-policy stability: Elaborate on why sharing parameters between on-policy A3C and off-policy auxiliary Q-learning is stable. Any special tricks (e.g., target networks, gradient clipping, loss scales) beyond those described?
- Hyperparameter tuning: You sample learning rate, entropy cost, and λPC. Please report how λPC impacts stability and performance, and whether the auxiliary loss weights were tuned per domain/game (some results mention different sampling ranges for Atari vs Labyrinth).
- Replay buffer sensitivity: Analyze the impact of buffer size, reward rebalancing ratio for reward prediction, and n-step targets on performance and stability.
- Generalization: Consider experiments that test whether the learned representation transfers to new tasks/pseudo-rewards in the same environment, or to modified reward functions, to substantiate the representation-learning hypothesis.
- Visualization/interpretability: The video/figures are helpful. Additional qualitative analyses (e.g., saliency for reward prediction or pixel control success maps) could make the representation-shaping effect more tangible.

Questions for the authors
- Compute budget: How much extra wall-clock time and number of gradient steps do the auxiliary tasks add compared to A3C for both domains? If constrained to equal compute, do the gains persist?
- Evaluation protocol on Atari: What exact evaluation settings were used (no-op starts, sticky actions if any, life-loss termination, frame skip, episode caps)? How are “expert human” scores defined?
- Off-policy/on-policy interplay: Did you observe interference between A3C and auxiliary Q-learning gradients? Were different learning rates or gradient normalization used per loss?
- Reward prediction balancing: You rebalance to P(r ≠ 0)=0.5 with three classes. How sensitive are results to this choice? Did you try cost-sensitive losses or different class balances?
- Feature control: Which layer(s) were most effective to control? How does feature control combine with pixel control—additive or redundant?
- Robustness across seeds: For a fixed hyperparameter setting, how variable are final scores across random seeds for UNREAL vs A3C?
- Transfer: If you freeze the shared CNN after auxiliary pretraining (no extrinsic rewards), how quickly can a policy be learned on a downstream task versus learning from scratch?
- Replay buffer: Why 2k steps? Does a larger/smaller buffer help or hurt, especially for value replay and control?

Overall, this is a well-motivated, carefully executed paper with strong empirical impact. I recommend acceptance.
