- Introduces UNREAL, an RL agent that uses auxiliary tasks (pixel/feature control, reward prediction) to improve representation learning and policy training.
- Demonstrates large empirical gains: 10× faster learning on Labyrinth, 87% of human expert, and 880% mean human-normalized score on Atari.
- Decision: Accept — paper merits publication.
- Primary reason 1: Significant contribution by using auxiliary tasks to address sparse rewards and data inefficiency.
- Primary reason 2: Strong empirical results across challenging benchmarks with rigorous validation.
- Primary reason 3: Methodology is well-motivated, grounded in prior work, and experimentally rigorous.
- Supporting argument 1: Novel approach to representation learning distinct from prior temporal abstraction or environment modeling work.
- Supporting argument 2: Comprehensive experiments and ablations show importance of each auxiliary task.
- Supporting argument 3: Clear descriptions of methods and setup enable reproducibility and fair baseline comparisons.
- Suggestion 1: Add deeper theoretical discussion on why auxiliary tasks improve representations and align with long-term objectives.
- Suggestion 2: Discuss computational overhead and scalability of auxiliary tasks to larger or real-world environments.
- Suggestion 3: Address generalization to other domains (continuous control, multi-agent) beyond Labyrinth and Atari.
- Suggestion 4: Provide ablation/analysis on task weighting hyperparameters (λPC, λRP) and their effect on performance.
- Question 1: How do auxiliary tasks affect generalization and transfer to unseen environments or tasks?
- Question 2: What is the computational cost of auxiliary tasks and how does it scale with environment complexity?
- Question 3: Could auxiliary tasks introduce unintended biases in learned representations, and how to mitigate them?
- Question 4: How sensitive is performance to the specific choice of auxiliary tasks (e.g., replacing pixel control)?
