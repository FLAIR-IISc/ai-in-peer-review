## Review of "Deep Reinforcement Learning with Auxiliary Control Tasks"

This paper introduces a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning by augmenting standard RL with auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction. The core idea is that learning to control various features of the sensorimotor stream, beyond just maximizing extrinsic reward, leads to more robust and data-efficient learning. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both Atari games and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, achieving state-of-the-art results and a substantial speedup in learning. The approach appears well-motivated by the observation that real-world environments offer a wealth of learning signals beyond sparse extrinsic rewards.

I **accept** this paper. The results are compelling, demonstrating a clear and substantial improvement over existing methods on challenging benchmarks. The significant speedup in learning is particularly noteworthy, suggesting a more efficient use of data.

The paper provides strong empirical evidence to support its claims. The results on Labyrinth are particularly impressive, showcasing a large performance gain and improved robustness. The ablation studies help to isolate the contribution of each auxiliary task, and the comparison to unsupervised learning baselines (pixel reconstruction) is insightful. The consistent performance improvement across a diverse set of Atari games further strengthens the validity of the findings. The authors clearly articulate the methodology and provide sufficient detail for reproducibility.

However, the paper could benefit from further clarification on several points. While the intuition behind the auxiliary tasks is clear, a more detailed explanation of *why* specific tasks (e.g., pixel changes, network features) are effective would be valuable. It would be helpful to understand the relationship between the choice of auxiliary tasks and the characteristics of the environment. Additionally, the weighting of the different loss components (λVR, λPC, λRP) seems crucial; a more systematic exploration of these hyperparameters and their impact on performance would be beneficial. Finally, the computational cost of adding these auxiliary tasks is not explicitly addressed. 

Specifically, I would like to know: 

*   How sensitive are the results to the specific grid size used for pixel control? Would a different grid size significantly impact performance?
*   What is the computational overhead introduced by the auxiliary tasks, and how does it scale with the complexity of the environment?
*   Could the authors provide a more detailed analysis of the learned representations? For example, are the learned features interpretable, and do they align with human intuition about the environment?
