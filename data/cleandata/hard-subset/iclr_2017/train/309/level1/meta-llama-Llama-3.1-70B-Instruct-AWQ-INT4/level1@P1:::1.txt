**Summary of the paper's contributions**

The authors introduce an agent that learns separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning. The agent shares a common representation that continues to develop in the absence of extrinsic rewards. The authors also introduce a novel mechanism for focusing this representation upon extrinsic rewards, allowing learning to rapidly adapt to the most relevant aspects of the actual task. The proposed UNREAL agent significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art on Atari, averaging 880% expert human performance, and a challenging suite of first-person, three-dimensional Labyrinth tasks, leading to a mean speedup in learning of 10× and averaging 87% expert human performance on Labyrinth.

**Decision and key reasons**

I decide to **Accept** this paper. The two key reasons for this choice are:

1. The authors provide a well-motivated approach that tackles a specific problem in deep reinforcement learning, namely, the need for more efficient and robust learning in complex environments. The proposed UNREAL agent addresses this problem by learning separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously, which leads to improved performance and data efficiency.
2. The paper supports its claims with extensive experimental results on two challenging domains, Atari and Labyrinth. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed approach, showing significant improvements over the state-of-the-art in terms of performance, data efficiency, and robustness to hyperparameter settings.

**Supporting arguments**

The authors provide a clear and well-structured presentation of their approach, which is well-placed in the literature. The related work section provides a thorough overview of existing approaches to deep reinforcement learning, highlighting the limitations of current methods and the need for more efficient and robust learning.

The experimental results are extensive and well-presented, providing a clear demonstration of the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The authors also provide ablation studies, which help to understand the contribution of each component of the UNREAL agent to its overall performance.

**Additional feedback**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

* Provide more details on the computational resources required to train the UNREAL agent, as this information is essential for reproducing the results.
* Consider adding more visualizations of the learned policies and the evolution of the pseudo-reward functions during training, which could help to better understand the behavior of the agent.
* Provide more insights into the choice of hyperparameters and the sensitivity of the results to these choices, as this information is essential for practitioners who want to apply the proposed approach to their own problems.

**Questions for the authors**

* Can you provide more details on the choice of pseudo-reward functions and how they are selected for each task?
* How do you handle the case where the pseudo-reward functions are not well-defined or are highly correlated with each other?
* Can you provide more insights into the trade-offs between the different components of the UNREAL agent, such as the balance between the main policy and the auxiliary tasks?
