**Summary of the paper's claims and contributions**

This paper introduces a novel deep reinforcement learning agent that learns to control a wide range of pseudo-reward functions simultaneously, in addition to the traditional extrinsic reward. The agent, called UNREAL, uses a combination of auxiliary control tasks, reward prediction tasks, and experience replay to learn a more effective representation of the environment. The authors claim that UNREAL significantly outperforms the previous state-of-the-art on Atari and Labyrinth tasks, achieving 880% expert human performance on Atari and 87% expert human performance on Labyrinth.

**Decision: Accept**

I decide to accept this paper because it presents a novel and well-motivated approach to deep reinforcement learning, and the experimental results demonstrate significant improvements over the state-of-the-art on challenging tasks. The paper is well-written, and the authors provide a clear and thorough explanation of their methodology and results.

**Supporting arguments**

1. **Well-motivated approach**: The authors provide a clear and compelling motivation for their approach, highlighting the limitations of traditional reinforcement learning methods that focus solely on extrinsic rewards. They argue that by learning to control a wide range of pseudo-reward functions, the agent can develop a more effective representation of the environment and improve its performance on a variety of tasks.
2. **Significant experimental results**: The authors present experimental results on Atari and Labyrinth tasks, demonstrating significant improvements over the state-of-the-art. The results are thorough and well-presented, with clear comparisons to baseline methods and ablation studies to evaluate the contributions of different components of the UNREAL agent.

**Additional feedback**

1. **More discussion of related work**: While the authors provide a good overview of related work in the introduction, I would like to see a more detailed discussion of how UNREAL relates to other approaches in the field. For example, how does UNREAL compare to other methods that use auxiliary tasks or reward prediction?
2. **More analysis of the learned representation**: While the authors provide some visualizations of the learned representation, I would like to see more analysis of what the agent has learned. For example, can the authors provide more insights into what features of the environment the agent is learning to control, and how these features relate to the extrinsic reward?
3. **More discussion of the limitations of UNREAL**: While the authors present impressive results, I would like to see more discussion of the limitations of the UNREAL approach. For example, are there any tasks or environments where UNREAL performs poorly, and if so, why?

**Questions for the authors**

1. Can you provide more details on how the auxiliary control tasks are selected and designed? Are there any specific criteria or heuristics used to choose the tasks?
2. How do the authors plan to extend UNREAL to more complex environments or tasks, where the number of possible pseudo-reward functions may be much larger?
3. Can you provide more insights into how the learned representation of UNREAL relates to the extrinsic reward, and how the agent is able to generalize to new tasks or environments?
