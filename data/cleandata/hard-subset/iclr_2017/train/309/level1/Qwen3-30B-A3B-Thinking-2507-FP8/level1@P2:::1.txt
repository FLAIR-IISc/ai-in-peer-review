

# Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Paper Claims

The paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously through reinforcement learning, while also developing a shared representation that continues to develop even in the absence of extrinsic rewards. The authors propose two key auxiliary tasks: (1) auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize changes in pixel values or network features, and (2) reward prediction tasks that learn to anticipate immediate rewards. By combining these with standard A3C reinforcement learning, the UNREAL agent achieves significant performance improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The authors claim that this approach not only improves final performance but also substantially increases data efficiency (10x speedup in learning) and robustness to hyperparameters.

## 2. Decision

Accept. The paper makes a strong empirical contribution with scientifically rigorous results that clearly demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed auxiliary tasks. The approach is well-motivated and well-positioned in the literature.

## 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper successfully addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning: the sparsity of extrinsic rewards and the limitations of traditional unsupervised learning for representation development. The authors provide a compelling rationale for their approach by contrasting it with prior work on unsupervised learning (which typically focuses on reconstruction) and showing how their method of treating features as pseudo-rewards leads to more useful representations.

The empirical evaluation is thorough and well-designed. The authors present comprehensive results on both Atari (57 games) and Labyrinth (13 levels), with multiple ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary task. The results are not only impressive in terms of final performance (880% human-normalized performance on Atari, 87% on Labyrinth) but also demonstrate substantial improvements in learning speed (10x faster) and robustness to hyperparameters. The ablation studies (Figures 4-5) clearly show that each component of the UNREAL architecture contributes to performance, with pixel control and reward prediction being particularly important.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thorough related work section that carefully distinguishes their approach from prior work on Horde, UVFA, successor representations, and auxiliary prediction tasks. The authors clearly articulate how their work builds upon and improves previous methods.

The methodological approach is sound and well-explained. The combination of A3C for the main policy with off-policy Q-learning for auxiliary tasks is well-justified, and the experience replay mechanism for reward prediction is a clever solution to the sparsity problem. The implementation details are sufficiently clear for reproducibility, and the hyperparameter sensitivity analysis (Figures 3, 7-8) demonstrates the robustness of their approach.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

While the paper is strong, these suggestions would further enhance it:

1. The paper could provide more insight into why the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control) work better than alternatives. The comparison with input reconstruction (Fig. 5) is helpful, but a more detailed analysis of the representation learning process would be valuable.

2. The authors claim that the representation continues to develop in the absence of extrinsic rewards. It would be useful to include a visualization or analysis showing how the representation evolves during training with and without extrinsic rewards.

3. The paper mentions that the auxiliary control tasks do not directly use the pseudo-reward maximizing policies (though this is a future direction). It would be helpful to include a brief discussion of why this design choice was made and potential trade-offs.

4. The paper could more explicitly connect the auxiliary tasks to specific cognitive functions or learning mechanisms observed in biological systems, as the baby analogy in the abstract suggests.

## 5. Questions for Authors

1. The paper shows that pixel control outperforms feature control (Fig. 5), but the difference is small. Could you elaborate on why pixel control might be slightly more effective in the environments tested, and whether this difference would persist in more complex environments?

2. The paper mentions that the auxiliary reward prediction task uses a skewed sampling approach to overrepresent rewarding events. How sensitive are the results to the exact sampling ratio (currently set to 50% non-zero rewards)? Would a more aggressive skewing lead to further improvements?

3. The UNREAL agent uses a deconvolutional network to produce the auxiliary value functions. How would the performance change if the authors used a different architecture for these auxiliary networks, such as a fully connected network or a different type of convolutional architecture?
