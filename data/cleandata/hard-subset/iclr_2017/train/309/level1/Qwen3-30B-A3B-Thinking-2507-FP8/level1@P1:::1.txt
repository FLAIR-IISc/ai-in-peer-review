

# Peer Review for ICLR 2017

## 1. Summary of Claims and Contribution
This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent that significantly improves upon state-of-the-art results by simultaneously learning multiple policies for maximizing various pseudo-reward functions. The authors argue that environments contain abundant training signals beyond extrinsic rewards, and that learning to control these signals (rather than simply reconstructing them) develops more useful representations. The UNREAL architecture integrates auxiliary control tasks (pixel control and feature control) with a reward prediction mechanism, all built upon the A3C framework. The paper demonstrates impressive empirical results: UNREAL achieves 880% human performance on Atari (surpassing previous state-of-the-art) and 87% human performance on the challenging Labyrinth environment with a 10× speedup in learning. The work is well-motivated, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a compelling case for the value of auxiliary control tasks in representation learning for reinforcement learning.

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution by demonstrating that auxiliary control tasks significantly improve both data efficiency and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The results are impressive and well-supported by thorough experiments across multiple domains.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's main contribution is well-motivated and well-positioned in the literature. The authors clearly identify the limitation of traditional reinforcement learning (focusing solely on extrinsic rewards) and provide a strong justification for their approach by contrasting it with related work (Horde, UVFA, successor representations). Their key insight—that learning to control features of the sensorimotor stream develops more useful representations than simply reconstructing them—is well-supported by their experiments.

The empirical results are robust and compelling. The paper provides thorough ablation studies (Figure 4) showing that each component of the UNREAL architecture contributes to the overall performance improvement. The comparison to unsupervised baselines (Figure 5) is particularly convincing, demonstrating that learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting or reconstructing them. The Labyrinth results are especially impressive, showing not just higher final performance (87% human performance vs. 54% for A3C) but also significantly faster learning (10× speedup).

The paper also demonstrates strong robustness to hyperparameter choices (Figure 3 top right, Figure 8), which is a critical practical consideration. This robustness is particularly valuable given the well-known sensitivity of deep reinforcement learning methods to hyperparameters.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
- The paper could benefit from a more detailed discussion of the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. How much additional computation does UNREAL require compared to A3C? Is the speedup in learning worth the additional computational overhead?
- While the paper mentions that feature control can be applied to any hidden unit in the network, it would be helpful to discuss how the choice of which units to control affects performance. A brief analysis of different hidden layer choices would strengthen the paper.
- The paper states that the UNREAL agent uses a "deconvolutional network" for auxiliary value prediction but doesn't provide sufficient detail about this architecture. A brief description or diagram would improve clarity.
- The paper mentions that the reward prediction task is trained with a multiclass cross-entropy loss across three classes (zero, positive, or negative reward), but it's unclear how the "negative reward" class is defined. Clarifying this would improve the methodological description.

## 5. Questions for Authors
- The paper states that the reward prediction task is trained to predict "zero, positive, or negative reward," but it's not clear how negative rewards are defined. Is this specifically for rewards that are negative (not zero), or is it a separate category of reward?
- The paper mentions "skewed sampling" for the reward prediction task to overrepresent rewarding events. Given that the reward predictor is used only to shape features (not for policy learning), how do you ensure that the bias introduced by this skewed sampling doesn't negatively impact the agent's policy learning?
- The paper claims 880% human performance on Atari. Could you clarify how this metric is calculated? Is it the average performance across all 57 games, normalized by human performance on the same games?
