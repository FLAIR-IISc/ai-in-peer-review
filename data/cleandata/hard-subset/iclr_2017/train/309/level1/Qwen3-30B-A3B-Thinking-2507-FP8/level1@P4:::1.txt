

# Review for ICLR_2017

## 1. Summary of Contribution
The paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that simultaneously learns separate policies for maximizing multiple pseudo-reward functions (such as pixel changes and network feature activations) alongside the main extrinsic reward task. The authors argue that learning to control these features through reinforcement learning, rather than simply predicting or reconstructing them, develops more useful representations that transfer to the main task. The approach uses a shared representation across all tasks and introduces a novel mechanism for focusing this representation on extrinsic rewards. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over previous state-of-the-art methods on both Atari (880% human-normalized performance) and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (87% human-normalized performance with 10× faster learning).

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to reinforcement learning through a well-motivated approach that is clearly positioned within the literature and supported by rigorous empirical evidence.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper effectively addresses a meaningful problem in reinforcement learning: how to develop more data-efficient agents that learn useful representations in sparse reward environments. The authors provide a compelling motivation by contrasting their approach with related work on unsupervised learning and auxiliary tasks (Horde, UVFA, successor representations), clearly articulating how UNREAL differs by using reinforcement learning to develop control policies for auxiliary tasks rather than just using them for representation learning.

The empirical results are scientifically rigorous and well-supported. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary component (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) and demonstrate consistent improvements across multiple environments. The Labyrinth results are particularly impressive, with UNREAL achieving 87% human-normalized performance (up from 54% for A3C) while learning 10× faster. The Atari results (880% mean human-normalized performance) significantly surpass previous state-of-the-art methods. The experimental design includes multiple hyperparameter settings (50 for Atari, 45 for Labyrinth), careful comparison with relevant baselines, and detailed analysis of robustness to hyperparameters.

The paper's claims about the benefits of learning to control features rather than predict them are well-supported by the comparison between auxiliary control tasks and simpler prediction/reconstruction baselines. The results show that learning to control pixel changes substantially outperforms simply predicting immediate pixel changes or reconstructing inputs, with the latter actually harming final performance compared to vanilla A3C.

## 4. Additional Feedback
- The paper could provide a more detailed theoretical justification for why learning to control features leads to better representations, beyond the intuitive baby analogy. A clearer explanation of the underlying mechanisms would strengthen the contribution.
- While the paper describes the architecture and auxiliary tasks well, it would benefit from a more systematic discussion of how to select appropriate auxiliary tasks for new environments, rather than relying on the specific choices of pixel control and feature control.
- The connection between the sensorimotor stream premise and the specific auxiliary tasks chosen could be made more explicit to strengthen the theoretical foundation.
- The authors might consider discussing potential limitations of the approach, such as whether the method scales to environments with extremely sparse rewards or very high-dimensional state spaces.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "the sensorimotor stream contains an abundance of other possible learning targets" but doesn't provide a principled way to select which targets would be most beneficial for a given environment. Could the authors elaborate on how to systematically identify or select the most effective auxiliary tasks for new domains?
2. The ablation study shows that feature control is nearly as effective as pixel control, but the paper doesn't explain why. Is there a theoretical or mechanistic reason why controlling network features might be as effective as controlling pixel changes, and would this hold across different architectures or environments?
3. The paper mentions that "the same UNREAL architecture also significantly improved both the learning speed and the robustness of A3C over 57 Atari games." What specific component of UNREAL contributes most to this increased robustness to hyperparameters? Is it the auxiliary control tasks, the reward prediction, or the experience replay mechanism?
