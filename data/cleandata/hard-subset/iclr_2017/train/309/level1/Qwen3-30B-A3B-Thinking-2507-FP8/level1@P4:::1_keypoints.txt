- Introduces UNREAL: jointly learns policies for multiple pseudo-rewards (pixel/feature control) alongside the main task using a shared representation and a mechanism to focus on extrinsic reward, improving transfer and learning.  
- Strong accept decision: makes a significant, well-motivated contribution with rigorous empirical support.  
- Tackles data-efficiency in sparse-reward RL by using auxiliary control tasks learned via reinforcement learning rather than prediction/reconstruction.  
- Differentiates from prior work (Horde, UVFA, successor representations) by learning control policies for auxiliary tasks instead of only using them for representation learning.  
- Empirical results are robust: thorough ablation studies, comparisons to baselines, and multiple hyperparameter settings across environments.  
- Labyrinth results: 87% human-normalized performance and 10× faster learning compared to A3C baseline.  
- Atari results: 880% mean human-normalized performance, substantially surpassing prior state-of-the-art.  
- Learning-to-control auxiliary tasks outperforms prediction/reconstruction baselines; reconstruction can harm final performance versus vanilla A3C.  
- Suggests need for deeper theoretical justification for why control-based auxiliary tasks produce better representations.  
- Recommends systematic guidance for selecting auxiliary tasks for new environments rather than relying on pixel/feature control choices.  
- Advises making the sensorimotor stream premise and its connection to chosen auxiliary tasks more explicit to strengthen theory.  
- Notes potential limitations to discuss: scaling to extremely sparse rewards and very high-dimensional state spaces.  
- Asks authors to clarify how to systematically identify effective auxiliary tasks for new domains.  
- Questions why feature control is nearly as effective as pixel control and whether this holds across architectures/environments.  
- Requests clarification on which UNREAL component (auxiliary control, reward prediction, or replay) most improves learning speed and robustness to hyperparameters.
