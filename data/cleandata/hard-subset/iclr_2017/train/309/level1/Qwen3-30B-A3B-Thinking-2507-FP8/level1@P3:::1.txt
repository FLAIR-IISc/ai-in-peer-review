

# Review of "UNREAL: Training a General Reinforcement Learning Agent"

## 1. Summary of Contributions
The paper proposes UNREAL, an agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously through reinforcement learning, alongside the standard extrinsic reward. The key contributions include: (1) introducing auxiliary control tasks that learn to maximize pixel changes and network features; (2) developing reward prediction as an auxiliary task to address sparse rewards; and (3) integrating these with experience replay for more efficient learning. The authors demonstrate significant performance improvements on both Atari (880% expert human performance) and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (87% expert human performance with 10× faster learning).

## 2. Decision
Accept. The paper makes a compelling contribution to reinforcement learning by demonstrating how auxiliary tasks can substantially improve data efficiency and robustness. The key reasons for acceptance are the strong empirical results across multiple domains and the clear theoretical motivation for the auxiliary tasks.

## 3. Supporting Arguments
The paper's approach is well-motivated by the observation that environments contain abundant learning signals beyond extrinsic rewards, and the authors provide a strong justification for why their auxiliary tasks are better suited for representation learning than traditional unsupervised approaches. The comparison to pixel reconstruction and immediate reward prediction in Section 4.1.1 is particularly convincing, showing that learning to *control* features (rather than predict them or reconstruct inputs) leads to better performance.

The experimental results are scientifically rigorous. The authors conduct thorough ablation studies, compare to multiple baselines, and demonstrate consistent improvements across both Atari (57 games) and Labyrinth (13 levels). The human-normalized performance metrics provide a clear benchmark for comparison. The results show not just higher final performance (87% vs 54% on Labyrinth), but also significantly faster learning (10× speedup) and greater robustness to hyperparameters.

The paper is well-placed in the literature, with a thoughtful discussion of related work on Horde, UVFA, and successor representations. The authors clearly articulate how their approach differs from these methods (e.g., using shared representations rather than separate value functions for each auxiliary task).

One particularly strong aspect is the integration of experience replay with the auxiliary tasks, which enables the efficient learning of these tasks without disrupting the main policy learning. The authors' insight that reward prediction can shape features without biasing the policy is theoretically sound and empirically validated.

## 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement
While the paper is strong, a few improvements would enhance its impact:

1. The theoretical justification for why pixel control and feature control specifically lead to better representations could be strengthened. A more detailed discussion of the relationship between the auxiliary tasks and the properties of the optimal representation would be valuable.

2. The paper mentions that feature control was "only slightly worse than pixel control" but doesn't provide a clear explanation for why one might be preferred over the other. A deeper analysis of the specific network features being controlled would be helpful.

3. The paper could more explicitly address potential limitations, such as whether the approach scales to even more complex environments or whether the auxiliary tasks become less effective as the main task becomes more complex.

## 5. Questions for Authors
1. The paper states that "learning to control pixel changes is better than simply predicting immediate pixel changes." Could you elaborate on why the control objective (learning a policy to maximize pixel changes) leads to better representations than the prediction objective (simply predicting pixel changes)? Is this a fundamental difference between control and prediction tasks?

2. In Figure 4, the performance of UNREAL without pixel control is significantly worse on some Labyrinth levels (e.g., nav maze all random 01). Could you explain why pixel control appears particularly crucial for these exploration-heavy tasks, and whether the feature control auxiliary task could compensate for the absence of pixel control in these scenarios?

3. The paper mentions that the UNREAL agent achieved 116% human-normalized score on lt hallway slope. Given that this exceeds 100%, could you provide more context about what this means in terms of actual performance relative to human players? Is this a meaningful improvement over human performance, or does it reflect a limitation in the human-normalization procedure?
