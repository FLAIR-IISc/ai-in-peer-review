

This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that simultaneously learns multiple policies for maximizing various pseudo-rewards (such as pixel changes and network feature activations) while also predicting extrinsic rewards. The authors argue that this approach develops more robust and useful representations than standard RL, leading to faster learning and better performance. The paper is well-motivated by the observation that environments contain abundant learning signals beyond the primary reward, and it clearly differentiates itself from related work on auxiliary tasks (Horde, UVFA, successor representations) by using these tasks solely to shape the representation rather than as direct policy components.

I recommend acceptance. The key reasons are: (1) The approach is novel and well-justified by both theoretical reasoning and empirical evidence, and (2) The results demonstrate significant improvements over strong baselines across diverse environments.

The empirical evidence is robust and scientifically rigorous. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human performance (vs. 54% for A3C) with a 10× speedup in learning, while on Atari it achieves 880% human performance—substantially outperforming prior work. The ablation studies convincingly show each auxiliary component contributes meaningfully to performance, and the authors carefully address hyperparameter sensitivity. The comparison to unsupervised approaches (pixel reconstruction vs. control) provides particularly compelling evidence for their core hypothesis.

For improvement, the paper could more explicitly explain why controlling features (rather than simply predicting them) leads to better representations. A deeper analysis of which specific pseudo-rewards were most beneficial for different tasks would strengthen the insights. Additionally, while the architecture is well-described, a clearer justification for the specific choice of pseudo-rewards (beyond pixel changes and features) would be helpful.

I'd like to ask: How do you expect the UNREAL approach to scale to environments where the number of potential pseudo-rewards becomes very large? Also, could the agent learn to identify which pseudo-rewards are most relevant to the current task, rather than using all of them equally? These questions would help clarify the generalizability and potential limitations of the approach.
