This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to improve the data efficiency, robustness, and overall performance of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core contribution is to augment a strong on-policy baseline (A3C) with a set of auxiliary tasks trained off-policy from a replay buffer. These tasks include auxiliary control, where the agent learns policies to maximize changes in pixel inputs or network features, and auxiliary reward prediction, where it predicts the onset of extrinsic rewards from short sequences of experience. The authors claim this combination of on-policy policy learning with efficient off-policy representation learning allows the agent to develop a richer understanding of its environment, leading to state-of-the-art results on both the 57-game Atari suite and a new, challenging 3D Labyrinth environment.

Decision: Accept. This paper presents a compelling and well-executed idea that yields significant empirical gains. The primary reasons for acceptance are (1) the elegant and effective combination of on-policy and off-policy learning for different objectives, and (2) the extensive and convincing experimental validation, including strong ablation studies.

The UNREAL architecture is a well-motivated synthesis of recent advances in deep RL. By using A3C for the main policy, it retains the benefits of stable on-policy updates. Simultaneously, it leverages the data efficiency of off-policy Q-learning on replayed experience to train the auxiliary tasks, which serve as a powerful representation learning engine. The experimental results are impressive. The agent not only achieves a new state-of-the-art on Atari, averaging 880% human performance, but it also demonstrates a massive 10x learning speedup on the visually complex Labyrinth domain. The ablation studies are crucial, as they clearly show that each auxiliary component contributes to the final performance, validating the paper's core claims.

For improvement, I would suggest a more detailed discussion on the sensitivity to the auxiliary loss weights (λ). While they are part of the hyperparameter search, understanding their relative importance and stability would be beneficial. Furthermore, the comparison between pixel control and pixel reconstruction is very insightful; expanding on the hypothesis that control objectives encourage a more causal understanding of the environment could further strengthen the paper's motivation.

I have a few questions for the authors:
1.  Could you quantify the computational overhead (in terms of wall-clock time and/or FLOPs) of the auxiliary tasks compared to the A3C baseline?
2.  The paper presents pixel control and feature control separately. Have you experimented with combining them, and if so, do they provide complementary benefits?
