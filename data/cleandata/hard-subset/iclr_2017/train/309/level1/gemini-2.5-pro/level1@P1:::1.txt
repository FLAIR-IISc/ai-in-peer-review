### **Summary of Contributions**
This paper introduces UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning), a deep reinforcement learning agent designed to learn more efficiently and achieve higher performance by leveraging a richer set of training signals from the environment beyond just the extrinsic reward. The core contribution is the introduction of auxiliary tasks that are learned simultaneously with the main goal of reward maximization. These tasks are cleverly designed to improve the agent's representation learning.

The key innovations are:
1.  **Auxiliary Control Tasks**: The paper frames representation learning as a set of auxiliary *control* problems. The agent learns off-policy value functions (and corresponding policies) for maximizing pseudo-rewards, such as causing maximal change in pixel intensity in different regions of the screen ("pixel control") or maximally activating hidden units in its own network ("feature control"). This forces the representation to capture the controllable aspects of the environment, a powerful learning objective.
2.  **Auxiliary Reward Prediction**: To help the agent focus on important, reward-relevant states, an auxiliary task of predicting the immediate next reward from a short sequence of observations is introduced. This is trained on a balanced replay buffer to counteract the sparsity of rewards.
3.  **A Hybrid On-policy/Off-policy Architecture**: The UNREAL agent elegantly combines a primary on-policy A3C agent for the main task with off-policy Q-learning for the auxiliary tasks and for value function regression, all trained from a shared experience replay buffer.

The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach through extensive experiments on 57 Atari games and a new, visually rich, and challenging 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The UNREAL agent is shown to drastically outperform the state-of-the-art A3C baseline in terms of final performance, data efficiency (a 10x speedup on Labyrinth), and robustness to hyperparameter choices.

### **Decision and Key Reasons**

**Decision:** Accept

This is an excellent paper that makes a significant contribution to the field of deep reinforcement learning. My recommendation for acceptance is based on two primary reasons:

1.  **Novel and Impactful Approach to Representation Learning:** The core idea of using auxiliary control tasks—learning policies to control features of the agent's sensory input—is a novel and highly effective method for self-supervised representation learning in RL. It shifts the paradigm from passive prediction/reconstruction to active control, leading to demonstrably better and more useful features.
2.  **Comprehensive and Rigorous Empirical Validation:** The paper's claims are supported by an exceptionally strong and thorough set of experiments. The evaluation on both the standard Atari benchmark and the new, challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, complete with detailed ablation studies and direct comparisons against alternative unsupervised methods, provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness and generality of the proposed techniques.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous. The motivation for using auxiliary tasks to overcome reward sparsity and improve data efficiency is very clear and compelling. The proposed UNREAL architecture is a thoughtful combination of existing state-of-the-art components (A3C, experience replay, Q-learning) into a novel and synergistic whole.

The distinction from prior work is well-articulated. While the Horde architecture also learned multiple value functions, it did not use them for shared representation learning. The comparison with standard unsupervised methods is particularly insightful. The experiment shown in Figure 5, which directly compares pixel control against pixel change prediction and pixel reconstruction, provides strong evidence for the central hypothesis that learning to *control* is more beneficial for the agent's performance than merely learning to *predict* or *reconstruct*. This is a key result that strengthens the paper's main claim.

The empirical results are outstanding. Achieving an average of 880% expert human performance on Atari and 87% on the complex Labyrinth tasks represents a substantial leap over prior state-of-the-art. Furthermore, the reported 10x speed-up in learning and increased robustness to hyperparameters are of great practical importance, making the approach more viable for complex problems. The ablation studies clearly demonstrate that each of the proposed auxiliary components contributes positively to the final performance, validating the design of the UNREAL agent.

### **Additional Feedback for Improvement**

These points are intended to help strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision.

*   **Computational Overhead:** The paper does not discuss the computational cost of the UNREAL agent relative to the A3C baseline. Adding multiple auxiliary heads and performing off-policy updates from a replay buffer presumably increases training time and memory requirements. A brief analysis of this overhead would provide a more complete picture for practitioners.
*   **Guidance on Auxiliary Task Weighting:** The weights for the auxiliary losses (λVR, λPC, λRP) are important hyperparameters. The paper notes that λPC was set differently for Atari and Labyrinth. It would be helpful to include a short discussion on the sensitivity to these weights and provide some intuition or guidance on how they might be set for new environments.
*   **"Feature Control" Status:** The results for the feature control task are mentioned as being preliminary. To strengthen the paper, it would be beneficial to either expand on these experiments or clarify that this is presented as a promising direction for future work rather than a fully validated component of the main UNREAL agent.

### **Questions for the Authors**

I would appreciate clarification on the following points to solidify my understanding:

1.  **Pixel Control Granularity:** The pixel control task uses a 20x20 grid. What was the reasoning behind this choice? Have you investigated the effect of different grid sizes on performance and computational cost?
2.  **Non-Stationarity of Auxiliary Tasks:** The state space for auxiliary tasks is defined to include the agent's own hidden unit activations. This makes the auxiliary task policies dependent on the agent's main network, which is non-stationary as it learns. How does the learning process cope with this non-stationarity? Is it simply managed by the slow-moving target network in the Q-learning updates?
3.  **Reward Prediction Sampling:** For the reward prediction task, you sample such that zero and non-zero rewards are equally represented. Did you compare this simple balancing scheme to more sophisticated prioritized sampling methods (e.g., based on TD-error)?
4.  **Pixel Reconstruction Performance:** The finding that pixel reconstruction hurts final performance is interesting, as other works have sometimes found it beneficial. Do you have a hypothesis for why it was detrimental here, beyond focusing on irrelevant details? Could it be an artifact of the specific architecture or loss weighting used?
