### **Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces a novel and powerful agent architecture, UNREAL, designed to accelerate and improve deep reinforcement learning by leveraging auxiliary tasks. The central idea is to learn a rich internal representation not just by maximizing the main extrinsic reward, but also by simultaneously learning to predict and control various aspects of the agent's sensorimotor stream. The authors make several key contributions. First, they introduce a set of auxiliary "control" tasks, such as maximizing pixel changes in different regions of the screen (pixel control) or maximizing the activation of hidden units (feature control). These tasks are framed as separate reinforcement learning problems and are trained off-policy using Q-learning. Second, they propose an auxiliary reward prediction task, trained on rebalanced experience from a replay buffer, which helps the agent's representation become more sensitive to reward-relevant states, a crucial benefit in sparse reward settings. Third, the paper presents an elegant architecture that combines an on-policy policy-gradient method (A3C) for learning the main task with off-policy learning from a replay buffer for training the auxiliary tasks and performing additional value function updates. This hybrid approach capitalizes on the strengths of both paradigms. The efficacy of UNREAL is demonstrated through extensive experiments on 57 Atari games and a new, visually complex 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results are state-of-the-art, showing massive improvements in final performance, data efficiency (e.g., a 10x speedup on Labyrinth), and robustness to hyperparameters over strong baselines.

### **Decision: Accept**

I strongly recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons are:

1.  **Novel and Impactful Method:** The paper presents a well-motivated and conceptually significant framework for representation learning in RL. The introduction of auxiliary control tasks and the principled combination of on-policy and off-policy learning address the critical challenges of data efficiency and sparse rewards in a novel way.
2.  **Exceptional Empirical Results:** The claims are backed by extremely strong and comprehensive experimental evidence. The UNREAL agent achieves state-of-the-art performance by a large margin on two diverse and challenging benchmarks, with thorough ablation studies and robustness analyses that convincingly demonstrate the effectiveness of each component of the proposed method.

### **Supporting Arguments**

The paper's strengths are numerous and compelling.

**Conceptual Soundness and Novelty:** The core idea of using the rich data stream for more than just the primary RL objective is not new, but this paper's implementation is novel and highly effective. Framing auxiliary tasks as "control" problems to be solved with RL, rather than as "reconstruction" or "prediction" problems to be solved with supervised learning, is an insightful contribution. The experiment in Figure 5, which shows that pixel control outperforms both change prediction and pixel reconstruction, provides strong evidence for this design choice. Furthermore, the hybrid architecture is a significant contribution. It allows the main policy to be learned with a stable on-policy algorithm (A3C) while the shared representation is trained with maximum data efficiency using off-policy updates on replayed experience. This is a very clever way to get the best of both worlds.

**Experimental Rigor and Strength:** The empirical evaluation is exemplary. The authors do not just test on a single domain but demonstrate success on both the standard Atari benchmark and a new, challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, which showcases the generality of the approach. The performance gains are not incremental but substantial: achieving 880% mean human performance on Atari and an average 10x learning speedup on Labyrinth are landmark results that significantly push the state of the art. The ablation studies in Figure 3 are crucial, clearly isolating the contribution of each auxiliary task and showing that they are complementary. The analysis of robustness to hyperparameters is another major strength, addressing a critical practical concern in deep RL and demonstrating that the UNREAL agent is not only higher-performing but also easier to train than the baseline.

**Clarity:** The paper is very well-written, clearly explaining the motivation, the components of the UNREAL agent, and the experimental setup. The diagrams are helpful in understanding the complex architecture.

### **Additional Feedback and Questions**

The following points are intended to help improve the paper and are not part of the decision assessment.

**Suggestions for Improvement:**

*   The term "Unsupervised" in the title and agent name might be slightly misleading. The auxiliary tasks are self-supervised, as they generate their own labels (pseudo-rewards) from the data stream, rather than being truly unsupervised. While a minor point, clarifying this distinction could prevent confusion.
*   The results for "feature control" are presented as preliminary. Given that this seems like a more general and potentially more powerful approach than pixel control (which is tied to the visual modality), a more thorough investigation and comparison would be a valuable addition.
*   The paper hypothesizes that pixel reconstruction hurts performance by focusing on irrelevant details. This is plausible, but it would be compelling to see more direct evidence, perhaps through visualizations of the learned features or attention maps, to substantiate this claim.

**Questions for the Authors:**

*   The weights for the different loss components (`λPC`, `λRP`, `λVR`) are critical hyperparameters. The paper notes they were sampled from distributions and that `λPC` was tuned differently for Atari and Labyrinth. Could you elaborate on the sensitivity of the agent's performance to these weights and whether you have any intuition or a more principled method for setting them?
*   The paper states that the policies learned for the auxiliary control tasks are not used directly. This seems like a missed opportunity. Have you considered using these policies for exploration or as a set of options for hierarchical planning, as hinted at in the related work section?
*   The replay buffer size is specified as 2,000 transitions, which is remarkably small for deep RL. Was this small size a deliberate choice to focus learning on very recent experience? How does the performance of the auxiliary tasks and the overall agent change with a larger replay buffer?
