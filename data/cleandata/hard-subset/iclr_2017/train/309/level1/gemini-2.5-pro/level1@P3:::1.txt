### 1. Summary of Contributions

This paper introduces the UNREAL (Unsupervised Reinforcement and Auxiliary Learning) agent, a novel architecture designed to significantly improve the sample efficiency, final performance, and robustness of deep reinforcement learning agents. The core idea is to enrich the sparse extrinsic reward signal from the environment with a suite of self-supervised auxiliary tasks that encourage the agent to learn a rich and general-purpose representation of its environment.

The authors make several key contributions. First, they propose a set of auxiliary *control* tasks, where the agent learns off-policy value functions to maximize internally generated pseudo-rewards, such as changes in pixel intensity ("pixel control") or activations of its own hidden units ("feature control"). This forces the agent to learn about the controllable aspects of its environment, even without external reward. Second, they introduce auxiliary *prediction* tasks, specifically reward prediction and value function replay, which use prioritized sampling from an experience replay buffer to focus the learned representation on features relevant to the main task. Third, they elegantly combine these elements into a single, cohesive architecture that leverages a hybrid on-policy/off-policy learning scheme: the main policy is trained with the on-policy A3C algorithm, while the auxiliary tasks are trained efficiently off-policy from a shared replay buffer.

The efficacy of this approach is demonstrated through extensive experiments on two challenging domains: 57 Atari games and a new, visually rich 3D first-person environment called Labyrinth. The results are exceptional, showing that UNREAL achieves a new state-of-the-art on Atari (averaging 880% of human performance) and Labyrinth (87% human performance), while achieving a remarkable 10x average speedup in learning on Labyrinth compared to a strong A3C baseline.

### 2. Decision and Key Reasons

**Decision:** Accept

**Key Reasons:**
1.  **Significant Performance and Efficiency Gains:** The paper presents a method that achieves a substantial and impressive improvement over the state-of-the-art on widely-recognized and challenging benchmarks. The 10x learning speedup on Labyrinth addresses one of the most critical challenges in deep RL: sample efficiency.
2.  **Novel and Well-Motivated Architecture:** The combination of on-policy learning for the main actor with off-policy learning for a diverse set of auxiliary tasks is a novel and powerful concept. The choice of auxiliary tasks, particularly the shift from passive prediction/reconstruction to active control, is well-justified and empirically shown to be more effective for learning useful representations.

### 3. Supporting Arguments for Decision

The paper's claims are strongly supported by its methodology and rigorous empirical evaluation.

**Well-placed in the Literature and Motivated Approach:** The paper does an excellent job of positioning its contributions relative to prior work. It correctly identifies the limitations of relying solely on sparse extrinsic rewards and builds upon ideas from unsupervised learning, temporal abstractions (options), and multi-task learning (Horde, UVFA). The key distinction and contribution lie in using the multitude of pseudo-reward policies *solely for representation learning within a shared architecture*, unlike Horde which learned separate value functions, and framing the auxiliary objective as *control* rather than simple reconstruction or prediction, which they empirically demonstrate is superior (Figure 5). This control-based objective intuitively aligns better with the agent's ultimate goal of acting effectively in the world. The architectural choice to combine stable on-policy A3C with data-efficient off-policy Q-learning for the auxiliary tasks is a clever engineering solution that leverages the strengths of both paradigms.

**Scientific Rigor and Empirical Support:** The experimental results are comprehensive and convincing.
*   **Strong Benchmarks:** The agent is tested on both the standard Atari suite and a new, challenging 3D domain (Labyrinth) with richer visuals and physics, demonstrating the generality of the approach.
*   **Ablation Studies:** The ablation study in Figure 3 (top left) is crucial, as it methodically demonstrates that each component of the UNREAL agent (Value Replay, Reward Prediction, Pixel Control) contributes positively to performance, with the full combination yielding the best results. This isolates the impact of each contribution.
*   **Direct Hypothesis Testing:** The experiment comparing pixel control against pixel prediction and reconstruction directly tests the central hypothesis of the paper. The results clearly favor control, providing strong evidence that learning to influence the environment is a more powerful representation learning objective than passively modeling it.
*   **Robustness Analysis:** The authors go beyond reporting peak performance by analyzing robustness to hyperparameters (Figure 3, top right). Showing that UNREAL is not only higher-performing but also more stable across different hyperparameter settings is a significant practical advantage and a sign of a robust method.

### 4. Additional Feedback for Improvement

These points are intended as constructive feedback to further strengthen the paper and are not critical to my decision to accept.

*   **Complexity and Hyperparameters:** The UNREAL agent is a complex system with several new loss components and corresponding weighting hyperparameters (λVR, λPC, λRP). While the robustness analysis is excellent, a brief discussion on the sensitivity to these weights or any intuition on how they might be set for new domains would be valuable for researchers aiming to build on this work.
*   **Computational Cost:** The paper focuses on improvements in data efficiency (environment steps). It would be beneficial to include a brief comment on the trade-off with computational cost. How much does the addition of the auxiliary tasks increase the wall-clock training time per update? This would provide a more complete picture of the agent's overall efficiency.
*   **Feature Control:** The results for feature control are presented as "preliminary." This is a very interesting and potentially more general idea than pixel control. If possible, expanding on these results, even in an appendix, would strengthen the paper's claims about the generality of auxiliary control tasks.

### 5. Questions for the Authors

*   The pseudo-reward for pixel control is based on the absolute difference in raw pixel intensity. In environments with dynamic textures or noise, this signal could be misleading. Have you considered alternative metrics for visual change, such as differences in early-layer CNN features, which might capture more semantically meaningful changes?
*   The reward prediction task benefits from prioritized replay by oversampling rewarding experiences. Did you experiment with applying similar prioritization schemes to the other off-policy tasks? For example, one could prioritize transitions with high TD-errors for the value replay and pixel control Q-learning updates.
*   The paper states that the learned auxiliary control policies are not used directly. This seems like a missed opportunity. Have you considered leveraging these policies for exploration (e.g., by having the agent occasionally follow a pixel-control policy to see novel states) or for building temporal abstractions/options? This appears to be a very exciting avenue for future research.
