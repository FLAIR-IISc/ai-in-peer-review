- Introduces UNREAL: an agent that learns to control multiple pseudo-reward functions (pixel changes, network features, reward prediction) to build task-useful representations.
- Claims controlling features yields more useful, rapidly adaptable representations than reconstruction or pure prediction.
- Key innovation 1: auxiliary tasks leverage the agent’s control over the environment (pixel and feature control) rather than acting as standalone objectives.
- Key innovation 2: reward prediction uses skewed sampling to balance rewarding vs non-rewarding experiences without biasing the main policy.
- Key innovation 3: strong empirical gains—880% human on Atari, 87% on Labyrinth, and ~10× learning speedup.
- Recommendation: accept, citing strong motivation, novelty, and empirical performance.
- Literature placement: clearly distinguishes from Horde, UVFA, and successor representations by focusing on representation shaping via control.
- Experimental rigor: extensive evaluation across 57 Atari games and 13 Labyrinth levels, hyperparameter robustness checks, and ablation studies showing each auxiliary task’s contribution.
- Writing/accessibility: generally clear for deep RL readers; suggests defining L_PC (pixel control loss weight) earlier.
- Suggestion 1: deeper analysis needed on why pixel control is the most impactful auxiliary task.
- Suggestion 2: provide more detailed analysis and visualizations of the reconstruction baseline, since reconstruction harmed final scores.
- Suggestion 3: investigate whether hyperparameter robustness stems from auxiliary tasks or overall architecture.
- Clarification Q1: how does skewed sampling for P(rτ ≠ 0)=0.5 differ from standard class weighting and why is it more effective?
- Clarification Q2: what are the specific limitations of feature control compared to pixel control and how might they be addressed?
- Clarification Q3: could UNREAL’s hyperparameter robustness be attributed to the auxiliary tasks themselves or is it an architectural side effect?
