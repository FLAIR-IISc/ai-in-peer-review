

This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that enhances feature learning by simultaneously optimizing multiple auxiliary tasks alongside the main reward objective. The key innovation is learning separate policies for maximizing various pseudo-reward functions (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) that share a common representation. These auxiliary tasks shape the agent's feature extractor, leading to significantly improved data efficiency and robustness compared to standard A3C. The authors demonstrate compelling results on both Atari (880% expert human performance) and Labyrinth (87% expert human performance with 10× faster learning), showing that the auxiliary tasks substantially improve both final performance and learning speed.

Decision: Accept.

Key reasons: The paper makes a strong, well-motivated contribution to reinforcement learning that is clearly supported by rigorous experiments. The auxiliary task approach is both novel and well-placed within the literature, and the empirical results are convincing and statistically significant.

Supporting arguments: The paper effectively addresses a critical limitation in deep reinforcement learning—data inefficiency—by introducing auxiliary tasks that shape the agent's representation without requiring additional reward signals. The approach is well-motivated by contrasting with unsupervised learning (which reconstructs inputs) versus the proposed method (which learns to control features). The experiments are thorough, with ablation studies showing the individual contribution of each auxiliary task, and the results on both Atari and Labyrinth consistently demonstrate significant improvements over A3C. The 10× speedup on Labyrinth and 880% average performance on Atari represent substantial advances over prior work. The paper also provides strong evidence of improved robustness to hyperparameters, which is particularly valuable for practical applications.

Additional feedback for improvement: 
1. The paper would benefit from a clearer explanation of how the agent learns multiple policies (the abstract suggests "simultaneously" but doesn't clarify that separate policies are learned for each task).
2. The computational cost of the auxiliary tasks should be explicitly addressed—how much does training time increase, and which components are most costly?
3. The feature control auxiliary task requires more empirical validation beyond the preliminary results shown in Figure 5. The paper should explore whether the feature control task provides consistent benefits across different environments and training regimes.
4. The paper mentions "the performance of our agents is still steadily improving" but doesn't explore whether longer training would yield further gains. Including this analysis would strengthen the results.
5. The choice of fixed weights for auxiliary tasks (λ = 1) should be justified more thoroughly. Were these values tuned, or were they simply set to 1 based on prior experience? A brief discussion of sensitivity to these weights would be valuable.

Questions for the authors:
1. The paper claims the agent "learns separate policies for maximising many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously." Could you clarify whether these policies are trained in parallel or sequentially, and how the sharing of representation between these policies is implemented in practice?
2. In Figure 5, the feature control task shows only marginal improvement over the baseline. Could you provide more details on the specific conditions under which feature control provides the most benefit, and whether there are particular environments where it outperforms pixel control?
3. The paper mentions "the features discovered in this manner are shared with the primary LSTM." Could you elaborate on how these shared features specifically contribute to improved performance on the main task, beyond general representation learning?
4. The supplementary materials are mentioned but not clearly referenced. Could you clarify where the additional implementation details and ablated results are located?

The paper is well-written and makes a significant contribution to the field. The results are convincing, the methodology is sound, and the presentation is clear. The minor issues identified above are not critical to the paper's core contribution but would enhance the paper's impact if addressed. The UNREAL architecture represents an important step forward in making reinforcement learning more data-efficient and robust, with clear potential for broader application.
