- Paper introduces UNREAL: an RL agent that learns auxiliary pseudo-reward tasks (pixel control, feature control) plus reward prediction alongside the main extrinsic reward.  
- Pixel control maximizes pixel-intensity changes across an image grid; feature control maximizes activations of specific hidden units; reward prediction uses skewed replay.  
- Core hypothesis: controlling auxiliary objectives yields more robust, transferable representations than reconstruction-based unsupervised learning.  
- Strong empirical gains: 87% human-normalized on Labyrinth vs 54% for A3C, 10× faster learning, and 880% mean human-normalized on 57 Atari games.  
- Decision: Accept — compelling contribution with strong empirical results and novel, well-motivated approach.  
- Strength: Clear problem statement—improving representations when extrinsic rewards are sparse/infrequent.  
- Strength: Well-motivated method that builds on prior work but focuses on control rather than reconstruction.  
- Strength: Rigorous experiments and ablations showing each auxiliary task contributes to performance.  
- Key finding: Pixel control alone outperforms A3C + reward prediction, indicating it is a critical component.  
- Robustness: Method shows insensitivity to hyperparameter choices, addressing a common RL issue.  
- Suggestion: Add a concise, concrete description of pixel control implementation (exact reward function) earlier in the paper.  
- Suggestion: Extend analysis to explain why pixel control helps (e.g., exploration vs. feature shaping; t-SNE of features).  
- Suggestion: Discuss limitation to discrete-action domains and potential behavior in continuous control (e.g., MuJoCo).  
- Question: Why use skewed replay with P(r≠0)=0.5 instead of uniform sampling with a larger replay buffer, and how sensitive is performance to the sampling ratio?  
- Question: Does the auxiliary control primarily help encode maze layouts (Labyrinth nav maze) or provide general feature learning benefits?  
- Question: Why is feature control slightly less effective than pixel control—do feature activations capture more task-irrelevant information?
