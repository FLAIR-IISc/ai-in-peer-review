

This work proposes training reinforcement learning agents to perform auxiliary tasks, arguing that this approach fosters the learning of stronger feature representations. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling activations of latent features. Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task to predict immediate rewards following a sequence of observations, learned offline via skewed sampling from an experience replay buffer to balance the probability of observing rewards to 50%. Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve significantly higher performance on discrete-action-continuous-space reinforcement learning tasks, reaching baseline performance in 10× fewer training iterations.  

The approach contrasts with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning by avoiding the need to learn potentially useless input representations or an infeasible (due to partial observability) environment model. Instead, learning to control local and internal environmental features complements the acquisition of the optimal control policy. The methodology presents a novel and compelling alternative to unsupervised learning, leveraging the agent’s inherent capacity to exert control over the environment.  

The explanation of the auxiliary tasks is presented at a high level, which aids intuitive understanding, but including explicit definitions (e.g., for L_PC) earlier in the main text—rather than deferring to the appendix—would improve accessibility. Otherwise, the paper is clearly written and accessible to readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning.  

The methodology is sound. While the optimal hyperparameter distributions may differ between A3C and UNREAL, evaluating the top three hyperparameter configurations ensures that, assuming the best hyperparameters for each method lie within the explored ranges, the per-method optimal hyperparameters are identified.  

A critical limitation, or rather a crucial direction for future work given the paper’s scope, is the lack of detailed experimental analysis into the specific effects of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong impact on overall performance. For instance, in the Labyrinth environment, A3C with pixel control (A3C+PC) outperforms all other methods (except UNREAL), suggesting pixel/feature control has disproportionate influence. It would have been valuable to isolate or analyze this effect more deeply, beyond merely measuring reinforcement learning task performance.
