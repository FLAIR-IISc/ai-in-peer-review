

**Summary**  
This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) to develop a more robust and efficient representation. The authors argue that traditional unsupervised learning (e.g., pixel reconstruction) fails to leverage the agent’s active control over the environment, whereas auxiliary tasks framed as *reinforcement learning problems* directly align with the agent’s long-term goals. By sharing representations across these tasks, UNREAL significantly accelerates learning and improves performance on both Atari (880% mean human-normalized score) and a challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (87% mean human-normalized score, 10× faster convergence than A3C). The approach is well-motivated by contrasting with prior work (e.g., Horde, UVFA) and provides a clear theoretical foundation for why control-oriented auxiliary tasks outperform passive unsupervised methods.

**Decision**  
**Accept**. The paper makes a compelling contribution to representation learning in RL, with rigorous empirical validation and a novel, well-justified approach. The key reasons are: (1) the problem of sparse-reward representation learning is well-posed, and (2) the claims are strongly supported by statistically significant results across multiple domains.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper’s core innovation—treating auxiliary tasks as *control problems* rather than reconstruction or prediction objectives—addresses a critical gap in RL representation learning. Unlike prior work (e.g., unsupervised reconstruction in Kulkarni et al., 2016), UNREAL’s pseudo-reward tasks (e.g., "maximize redness" as in the baby example) inherently shape representations toward *actionable* features, avoiding the pitfalls of irrelevant pixel reconstruction. This is validated through:  
- **Quantitative rigor**: On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs. 54% for A3C) with a 10× speedup in learning. The 57-game Atari benchmark shows consistent superiority over state-of-the-art (A3C, Prioritized Dueling DQN), with 880% mean human-normalized score.  
- **Robustness**: UNREAL’s performance is less sensitive to hyperparameters (Fig. 3, top-right), a critical practical advantage. The ablation studies (e.g., removing pixel control) demonstrate that all auxiliary components contribute meaningfully to the gains.  
- **Theoretical alignment**: The paper correctly positions UNREAL between unsupervised learning (which ignores agency) and model-based RL (which is intractable in partial-observability settings). The reward prediction task’s use of skewed replay (to balance sparse rewards) is a technically sound solution to the sparsity problem.  

The methodology is sound: Experiments use standard protocols (e.g., 50 hyperparameter runs per game), and the ablation design isolates the impact of each auxiliary task. The claim that pixel control is the dominant contributor (e.g., A3C + pixel control outperforms other combinations on Labyrinth) is well-supported by Fig. 4 and Fig. 5, which show that learning to *control* pixel changes (not just predict them) yields the largest gains.

**Additional Feedback**  
To further strengthen the paper, consider these minor improvements:  
1. **Clarify the pixel control mechanism**: The draft mentions "L_PC" without defining it. Explicitly state that L_PC weights the pixel control loss (e.g., "λ_PC = 0.01 for Labyrinth") in the main text to avoid requiring readers to consult the appendix.  
2. **Deepen the analysis of auxiliary tasks**: While the ablations show *that* pixel control is effective, a brief discussion of *why* (e.g., "Pixel control’s dominance may stem from its direct alignment with visual spatiotemporal dynamics, which are critical for navigation in Labyrinth") would add theoretical depth.  
3. **Address scalability**: The paper focuses on discrete-action domains. A sentence on potential challenges for continuous control (e.g., high-dimensional action spaces) would broaden the impact.  

**Questions for Authors**  
To ensure confidence in the assessment, please clarify:  
1. In the Labyrinth experiments, the pixel control task subdivides the image into a 20×20 grid. How were the grid boundaries determined (e.g., fixed per game, learned)? Would a coarser grid (e.g., 10×10) yield similar gains with lower computational cost?  
2. The reward prediction task uses a 3-class classifier (positive/negative/zero reward). Why not use a continuous value (e.g., MSE loss) to capture reward magnitude? Did you observe any performance differences?  
3. The paper notes that feature control was "slightly worse than pixel control" (Sec. 4.1.1). Given that feature control leverages higher-level network activations, is this due to suboptimal feature selection (e.g., layer choice), or does it suggest pixel control inherently captures more useful dynamics?  

This work is a strong contribution to RL representation learning, with clear implications for data-efficient agent design. The empirical results are thorough, the motivation is compelling, and the approach is both novel and practical. I recommend acceptance.
