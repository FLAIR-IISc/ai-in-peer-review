

This paper proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks—specifically, controlling pixel intensity changes, controlling latent feature activations, and predicting immediate rewards following action sequences—to enhance feature learning. The reward prediction task is learned offline using skewed sampling of the experience replay buffer, ensuring a 50% chance of observing rewards. Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve strong performance on discrete-action, continuous-state RL benchmarks, reaching baseline performance in 10× fewer training iterations compared to baselines.

The approach distinguishes itself from traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based methods. Instead of compelling models to learn potentially irrelevant input representations or attempt impossible environment modeling (e.g., due to partial observability), it leverages the agent’s inherent capacity to control local environmental features. This dual focus—learning control policies while manipulating internal features—complements the primary RL objective, offering a novel alternative to standard unsupervised learning strategies. The core idea is compelling and well-motivated.

The paper’s exposition is generally clear, though some technical details could be strengthened. For instance, the term "L_PC" (pixel control loss) should be explicitly defined before the appendix to aid reproducibility. Beyond this, the methodology is well-justified. The authors address hyperparameter sensitivity by reporting top-3 performance across the search space, ensuring robustness for both A3C and UNREAL. While hyperparameter distributions may differ between algorithms, this approach reliably identifies near-optimal configurations within the explored ranges.

A notable limitation is the lack of in-depth analysis into *how* the auxiliary tasks contribute to performance gains. The paper demonstrates their strong impact (e.g., A3C+PC outperforms all other combinations on Labyrinth, except UNREAL), but fails to isolate their individual effects. For instance:  
- Why does pixel control (PC) dominate feature control (FC) in Labyrinth?  
- How do these tasks interact with the primary RL objective?  
- Do they improve feature quality, exploration efficiency, or both?  
Such analysis would strengthen the claims and provide actionable insights for future work. The current focus on aggregate performance overlooks a crucial opportunity to validate the *mechanism* behind the gains. Given space constraints, this could have been addressed through ablation studies on a single environment or a brief qualitative analysis of learned features.

Overall, the work presents a novel, well-executed approach with significant empirical results. The core idea—using controllable auxiliary tasks to drive feature learning—merits attention, and the experimental validation is thorough. Addressing the limited mechanistic analysis would elevate the contribution further. The paper is accessible to Deep RL practitioners and makes a meaningful step toward more efficient and interpretable representation learning in RL.
