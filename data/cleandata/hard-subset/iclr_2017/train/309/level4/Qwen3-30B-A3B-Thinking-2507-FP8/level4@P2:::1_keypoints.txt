- Introduces UNREAL, a deep RL agent that uses auxiliary control and reward-prediction tasks to improve representation learning.  
- Shows UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C (87% vs 54% on Labyrinth) and achieves 880% of human performance on 57 Atari games.  
- Demonstrates a 10× speedup in learning, needing <10% of data to reach A3C’s final performance.  
- Decision: Accept — strong contribution with compelling, well-documented results across environments.  
- Addresses sparse extrinsic rewards by using auxiliary control tasks to learn representations aligned with long-term goals.  
- Experimental evidence is convincing: superior final performance, much faster learning, and ablations show each auxiliary component’s value.  
- Theoretical foundation and architecture design are sound, with mechanisms to balance multiple objectives without biasing primary reward maximization.  
- Suggestion: provide a more detailed discussion of how each auxiliary task (pixel control, feature control, reward prediction) drives representation improvements.  
- Suggestion: quantify computational requirements and training cost of UNREAL relative to A3C for practical assessment.  
- Suggestion: better contextualize why the auxiliary tasks outperform simpler alternatives (e.g., pixel reconstruction) within unsupervised representation-learning literature.  
- Question: specify exact computational resources and training time comparisons between UNREAL and A3C.  
- Question: will the authors release source code for UNREAL and implementations of the auxiliary tasks to aid reproducibility?  
- Question: elaborate on why feature control is especially promising and what challenges exist for broader implementation.  
- Overall: UNREAL is a meaningful advancement in deep RL with robust empirical benefits and clear applicability, meriting acceptance.
