

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent that simultaneously learns multiple auxiliary tasks to improve representation learning and task performance. The authors propose a framework where an agent learns separate policies for maximizing various pseudo-reward functions (including pixel control and feature control) while sharing a common representation. The approach also incorporates reward prediction as an auxiliary task to focus the representation on relevant aspects of the environment. The paper demonstrates significant improvements over baseline A3C agents on both Labyrinth (achieving 87% human-normalized performance with 10× faster learning) and Atari (880% mean human performance), representing a substantial advancement in deep reinforcement learning.

I recommend acceptance. The paper makes a meaningful contribution to the field by demonstrating that carefully designed auxiliary tasks can substantially improve both performance and learning efficiency in complex environments. The results are scientifically rigorous, with comprehensive experiments across multiple domains and thorough ablation studies.

The work is well-positioned in the literature, building upon relevant prior work on auxiliary tasks (Horde, UVFA, successor representations) while introducing novel mechanisms for integrating these tasks with standard reinforcement learning. The authors provide a strong theoretical foundation for their approach, demonstrating how auxiliary control tasks and reward prediction can shape useful representations without biasing the primary reward maximization objective. The empirical results are compelling, showing consistent improvements across diverse environments with clear evidence of faster learning and greater robustness to hyperparameters.

For further improvement, the authors could provide more detailed analysis of the specific features learned through the auxiliary tasks, particularly how these representations transfer across different environments. Additionally, including a more thorough discussion of the computational cost of training the UNREAL agent would be valuable for practitioners considering adoption.

I would appreciate clarification on two points: First, what are the specific computational resources (GPU hours, memory requirements) needed to train the UNREAL agent compared to baseline A3C? Second, will the authors be releasing the source code for the UNREAL implementation to facilitate replication and further research in this area?
