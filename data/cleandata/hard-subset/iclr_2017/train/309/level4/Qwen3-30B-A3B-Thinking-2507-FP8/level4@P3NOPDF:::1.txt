

This paper introduces UNREAL, an extension of the A3C framework that incorporates unsupervised auxiliary tasks—both control-based (e.g., predicting future frames) and reward-based (e.g., predicting state entropy)—to enhance deep reinforcement learning performance. The authors demonstrate that UNREAL achieves superior performance and faster learning compared to A3C across Labyrinth and Atari benchmarks. This represents a valuable contribution to the field, as it empirically validates a well-established principle: auxiliary tasks that align with the primary learning objective can significantly improve feature representation and sample efficiency. While the core idea is not entirely novel, the paper provides a compelling, systematic implementation and evaluation that solidifies its practical utility.

The work is clearly written, with a well-structured argument and accessible explanations suitable for the conference audience. The experimental design is thorough, and the results convincingly support the authors' claims. The performance gains on both challenging environments (Labyrinth’s complex mazes and Atari’s diverse games) are notable, particularly the consistent speedup in learning without compromising final performance. This reinforces the potential of auxiliary tasks as a general strategy for improving deep RL agents.

That said, the paper could strengthen its contribution by more explicitly situating its approach within existing literature. While the authors reference prior work on auxiliary tasks (e.g., Mnih et al.’s use of prediction tasks), they do not deeply engage with the theoretical rationale for why *these specific* auxiliary tasks are effective. A brief discussion of the relationship between the auxiliary objectives (e.g., frame prediction, entropy estimation) and the core RL task—particularly how they avoid interference or redundancy—would elevate the conceptual contribution. The current presentation leans heavily on empirical validation, which is sound but could be enriched by deeper analysis.

Two critical questions remain unaddressed, which would significantly aid reproducibility and resource planning for the community:  
1. **Computational Requirements**: The paper reports performance gains but does not quantify the computational cost of training UNREAL versus A3C. Given the added auxiliary tasks (e.g., multiple prediction heads), training time and hardware demands may increase substantially. The authors should clarify whether the speedup in learning translates to a net reduction in wall-clock time or merely sample efficiency, and provide details on training resources (e.g., GPU hours, cluster size).  
2. **Code Availability**: The UNREAL architecture, while well-described, is complex with multiple auxiliary components. To ensure verifiability and facilitate future work, the authors should commit to releasing the source code upon publication. This is especially important for a method targeting broad adoption in the RL community.  

The rebuttal did not address these points, suggesting they may not have been fully considered during revision. For a paper at ICLR, where reproducibility is paramount, these omissions are noteworthy.  

In summary, this is a strong, practical contribution that advances the application of auxiliary tasks in deep RL. The results are robust and well-supported, and the method is likely to be widely adopted. The minor gaps in theoretical justification and reproducibility details prevent it from being a *transformative* advance, but it is a solid, valuable addition to the literature. The authors have made a compelling case for the efficacy of their approach, and addressing the computational and code-sharing points would make this work even more impactful.  

**Recommendation**: Accept. The paper’s empirical rigor, clear presentation, and meaningful performance improvements justify acceptance, though the reviewers encourage the authors to address the reproducibility concerns in the camera-ready version.
