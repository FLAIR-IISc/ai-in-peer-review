

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel reinforcement learning agent that simultaneously learns multiple auxiliary tasks—specifically, policies for maximizing pseudo-rewards derived from pixel changes and network features—alongside the primary reward-maximization objective. The authors propose a unified framework where these auxiliary tasks develop a shared, increasingly useful representation, while a reward prediction mechanism focuses the representation on task-relevant features. The approach demonstrates significant empirical gains: UNREAL achieves 880% human-normalized performance on Atari (surpassing prior state-of-the-art), and on the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment, it attains 87% human performance with a 10× speedup in learning compared to A3C. Crucially, the authors rigorously validate that the auxiliary tasks are not merely beneficial but necessary for the observed improvements, as ablation studies show performance degrades when they are removed.

**Decision: Accept**  
The paper makes a compelling contribution to representation learning in RL by demonstrating that *systematically designed auxiliary control tasks* (not just generic unsupervised signals) significantly enhance data efficiency and robustness. The core novelty lies in the reward prediction mechanism—which avoids biasing the main policy—and the empirical validation across two diverse domains (Atari and Labyrinth), where the improvements are both substantial and statistically robust.

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Problem & Motivation**: The paper clearly identifies a critical gap in RL: sparse rewards hinder representation learning, while traditional unsupervised methods (e.g., pixel reconstruction) fail to align with task-relevant features. The authors position their work precisely within this gap, contrasting with prior efforts like Horde (which trained value functions separately) and UVFA (which focused on transfer, not representation development). Their hypothesis—that learning to *control* sensorimotor features (e.g., "maximizing redness") yields more useful representations than prediction or reconstruction—is well-motivated and validated through ablations showing pixel control outperforms input reconstruction by 20–30% on Labyrinth.

2. **Rigorous Validation**: The empirical evaluation is exceptionally thorough. On Labyrinth, UNREAL’s 87% human performance (vs. A3C’s 54%) and 10× speedup are measured across 13 diverse levels, with ablation studies isolating the contributions of each auxiliary task. The Atari results (880% mean performance) are evaluated over 57 games and 50 hyperparameter settings, demonstrating robustness superior to A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN. The authors also quantify *why* the approach works: the reward prediction task (with skewed sampling) accelerates feature learning for rewards without biasing the policy, and combining pixel/feature control with reward prediction yields synergistic gains.

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, consider these minor points:  
- Clarify the *computational cost* of UNREAL versus A3C (e.g., per-episode training time, memory overhead). The 10× speedup in learning is impressive, but does it come at a higher per-step cost?  
- Explicitly justify the choice of *specific* auxiliary tasks (e.g., why 20×20 pixel grids for control, rather than other spatial divisions?). A brief discussion of task design trade-offs would enhance the framework’s generalizability.  
- The paper notes that feature control is slightly less effective than pixel control; a short analysis of *why* (e.g., feature units may be less perceptually salient) would deepen the insight.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The reward prediction task uses a 3-frame sequence to predict *immediate* rewards. How would this design scale to environments with longer-term reward dependencies (e.g., where rewards are delayed by 10+ steps)?  
2. The ablation study shows feature control is beneficial but suboptimal to pixel control. Could the authors speculate on whether combining feature control with *learned* feature extractors (e.g., via self-supervision) would further improve performance?  

This work represents a significant step toward more data-efficient RL agents, with clear potential for broader application. The experimental rigor and novel mechanism for representation learning make it a strong fit for ICLR.
