

This paper introduces a novel approach to reinforcement learning (RL) by training agents to simultaneously solve auxiliary tasks alongside the primary control objective. The authors propose three such tasks: (1) controlling pixel intensity changes (PC), (2) controlling latent feature activations (L_PC), and (3) predicting immediate rewards following event sequences via supervised regression. The reward prediction task is learned offline using stratified sampling of the experience replay buffer to balance the ratio of reward-observing to non-reward-observing transitions to 50:50. Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve significant performance gains on discrete-action continuous-state RL benchmarks, reaching baseline performance in approximately 10× fewer training iterations than standard methods.

The core contribution lies in the conceptual shift away from traditional "passive" unsupervised learning (e.g., predicting observations or learning world models) and model-based approaches. The authors argue that forcing agents to learn representations of input data or model the environment (which may be infeasible due to partial observability) is suboptimal. Instead, they posit that learning to control *local* and *internal* features of the environment—through the proposed auxiliary tasks—complements and enhances the primary policy learning. This approach leverages the agent's inherent ability to influence the environment, offering a more actionable and effective form of self-supervision. The novelty is compelling, as it reframes unsupervised learning within the active RL framework, moving beyond static representation learning.

The paper is generally well-written and accessible to readers familiar with deep RL. However, the description of the auxiliary tasks, particularly L_PC, would benefit from earlier explicit definition (e.g., upon first mention in the main text, not relegated to the appendix), enhancing clarity without adding significant length. The methodology is sound. The authors address hyperparameter sensitivity by reporting top-3 results across the explored space for both A3C and UNREAL, ensuring robustness to variations in hyperparameter choices within the tested ranges. This mitigates concerns about method-specific optimal hyperparameters.

A critical gap, however, is the lack of experimental analysis specifically isolating the impact of each auxiliary task. While the results demonstrate that adding all auxiliary tasks yields strong performance (e.g., A3C+PC significantly outperforms other combinations in Labyrinth, even surpassing UNREAL), the paper does not dissect *why* or *how* each task contributes. For instance:
- What is the relative contribution of pixel control (PC) versus latent feature control (L_PC) to the observed gains?
- How do these tasks affect the quality or efficiency of the learned representations?
- Are there diminishing returns or interactions between the tasks?

The current analysis focuses solely on final RL performance metrics. A deeper investigation—such as ablation studies measuring feature learning dynamics, representation quality, or learning speed for individual tasks—would substantially strengthen the argument for the proposed approach. This is especially crucial given the strong empirical effect of PC (as seen in Labyrinth), which warrants dedicated analysis beyond its performance in combined settings.

In summary, this work presents a novel and promising direction for RL by integrating actionable auxiliary tasks that leverage the agent’s control capability. The core idea is well-motivated, the results are compelling, and the paper is clearly written. The primary limitation is the insufficient dissection of the auxiliary tasks' individual contributions, which represents a critical gap in the current evaluation. Addressing this through focused ablation studies would significantly enhance the paper's impact. The approach demonstrates clear potential to advance RL by moving beyond passive unsupervised learning, and the proposed tasks offer a valuable new framework for feature learning. I recommend acceptance pending minor revisions to clarify the task definitions and address the experimental analysis gap in the response.
