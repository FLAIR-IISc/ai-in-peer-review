

This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent architecture that enhances deep reinforcement learning by incorporating auxiliary tasks focused on controlling various aspects of the sensorimotor stream. The authors propose that learning multiple auxiliary control policies—specifically for pixel changes and network features—alongside reward prediction and value function replay, creates a more robust and efficient representation than traditional approaches. These auxiliary tasks share the same feature extraction network as the main policy, allowing the agent to develop representations that generalize across multiple objectives. The approach demonstrates significant improvements on both Atari and Labyrinth benchmarks, with UNREAL achieving 880% human-normalized performance on Atari (a 2.5x improvement over prior work) and 87% on Labyrinth, while reducing learning time by 10x on average.

I recommend acceptance with confidence. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field, with clear theoretical motivation, well-executed experiments, and compelling results. The three key questions are addressed thoroughly:

1. The paper tackles the problem of improving feature learning in reinforcement learning by developing a unified architecture that learns multiple auxiliary control policies simultaneously, rather than relying solely on extrinsic reward maximization.

2. The approach is well-motivated by building on related work (Horde, UVFA, successor representations) while clearly distinguishing its novel contribution: using auxiliary control policies to shape features without directly using them for decision-making. The paper provides a strong theoretical foundation for why learning to control various aspects of the environment might produce more useful representations.

3. The claims are well-supported by rigorous empirical evidence across two distinct domains (Atari and Labyrinth). The results demonstrate consistent and significant improvements over strong baselines, with careful ablation studies showing the contribution of each auxiliary task. The statistical significance of the results is well-documented.

The paper excels in clarity, with well-structured arguments and excellent presentation of results. The main contribution is clearly articulated, and the experimental design thoroughly validates the approach.

For further improvement, I suggest the following:

1. Clarify in the abstract and introduction that the agent learns *separate policies* for each auxiliary task (not a single policy optimizing all rewards simultaneously), as this was a point of confusion in the draft. The equation (1) makes this clear, but the abstract suggests a single policy.

2. Add a brief analysis of computational overhead. How much does the auxiliary training slow down the main learning process? Which components are most expensive to compute?

3. Provide more empirical validation for the feature control auxiliary task. The preliminary results in Figure 5 show only marginal initial improvements, and the paper should address whether this task provides meaningful long-term benefits.

4. Explain why the auxiliary task weights (λ parameters) were fixed at 1.0 rather than being optimized as hyperparameters. Were there experiments testing different values?

5. Clarify in the methodology that the auxiliary tasks use off-policy learning with multi-step returns, not "true" Q-learning as might be assumed from standard definitions.

6. The paper mentions "the performance of our agents is still steadily improving" but doesn't continue training the best agents to see if they can achieve even higher scores. This would provide additional confidence in the results.

7. The text refers to "L_PC" in the loss function description but this term isn't explicitly defined in the equation or surrounding text. Please ensure all notation is consistent.

8. The figure captions and text should be more precise about which plots correspond to which results (e.g., "Figure 3 Top Right" instead of "Figure 3 Top").

9. Include more ablation studies on Atari to confirm whether the same patterns of individual task contributions observed on Labyrinth hold across different domains.

These suggestions are intended to help strengthen the paper, not as requirements for acceptance. The current submission is already strong and well-supported, making it a clear candidate for acceptance at ICLR 2017.
