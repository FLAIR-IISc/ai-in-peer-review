

This paper proposes enhancing feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the primary policy's optimization with domain-independent auxiliary tasks: state-modification control (learning policies that maximize state-space changes), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. Except for value function replay, these auxiliary tasks solely serve to shape features via shared CNN+LSTM feature extraction. Experiments demonstrate significant data efficiency gains over A3C on Atari and Labyrinth environments, with results being both convincing and well-supported. The paper is well-written with sound ideas, warranting a clear acceptance. My high-level comments are minor and non-critical:

- Quantify the computational overhead of auxiliary tasks: How much training speed is lost? Which components are most costly?
- Clarify in the abstract/introduction that the agent learns *multiple distinct policies* (one per task), not a single policy optimizing all pseudo-rewards simultaneously. The current phrasing ("maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously") caused initial confusion, resolved only upon reaching Equation 1.
- The "feature control" mechanism lacks empirical validation (Figure 5's preliminary experiment shows only marginal initial benefit). I am concerned that dynamically altering the task during learning may destabilize feature extraction and convergence.
- Since agent performance continues improving steadily (as noted in the paper), extend training for the best agents to assess long-term potential.
- Justify why auxiliary task weights (λ_*) are fixed at 1 instead of being optimized as hyperparameters. Were ablation studies conducted to validate this choice?
- Note that auxiliary tasks are trained *off-policy* using multi-step empirical rewards (not true Q-learning), as clarified in OpenReview comments.
