

This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that significantly improves data efficiency and robustness by incorporating auxiliary tasks into the learning process. The key innovation lies in simultaneously training multiple policies for different "pseudo-reward" functions (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) while sharing a common representation with the main policy. By learning to control various aspects of the sensorimotor stream, the agent develops more useful representations that accelerate learning of the primary task. The authors demonstrate substantial improvements over state-of-the-art methods on both Atari (880% expert human performance) and Labyrinth (87% expert human performance), with a 10× speedup in learning.

I recommend acceptance with the following key reasons: First, the paper makes a compelling case for auxiliary tasks as a general approach to representation learning in reinforcement learning, with strong empirical results across multiple domains. Second, the paper is well-structured and provides sufficient theoretical grounding for the proposed approach.

The paper successfully supports its claims through extensive experiments on both Atari and Labyrinth environments. The results demonstrate consistent improvements across metrics, with the UNREAL agent achieving substantially better data efficiency (10× faster learning) and robustness to hyperparameter choices compared to A3C. The ablation studies in Section 4.1.1 effectively show that each auxiliary task contributes meaningfully to performance, with the combination yielding the best results. The comparison with alternative unsupervised approaches (pixel reconstruction and immediate prediction) in Figure 5 provides valuable insight into why the proposed approach works better.

I have a few minor suggestions to further strengthen the paper:

1. Clarify in the abstract and introduction that the agent learns separate policies for each auxiliary task (not a single policy optimizing all rewards simultaneously). The current wording ("maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously") could lead to misunderstanding.

2. The "feature control" auxiliary task is intriguing but lacks thorough empirical validation. The preliminary results in Figure 5 show only marginal initial improvement and no clear long-term benefits. The authors should either provide more comprehensive experiments on this task or temper their claims about its potential.

3. Consider addressing the computational cost of the auxiliary tasks. How much additional training time do these tasks require? Which components are most expensive to compute?

4. The paper states "the performance of our agents is still steadily improving" but doesn't continue training to see the asymptotic performance. It would be valuable to report final performance after extended training.

5. The auxiliary task weights (λ parameters) are set to fixed values (e.g., λRP=1) without clear justification. It would strengthen the paper to explain why these values were chosen or to include experiments showing the sensitivity of results to these weights.

6. The paper mentions that auxiliary tasks are trained off-policy with "more than one step of empirical rewards" but doesn't explicitly state they're not using "true" Q-learning. This clarification would be helpful for readers.

7. Several minor technical points need correction: the loss L_PC is referenced but not defined in Equation 2, the text around Equation 1 has a typo (λC should be inside the sum), and "is shared" should be "are shared" in the description of feature sharing.

8. The legend of Figure 3 should clarify that the percentages are for Labyrinth (not Atari), and the reference to "Figure 3 (right)" should be corrected to "Figure 3 (Top Right)" as the data appears in the top right panel.

9. More ablation studies on Atari would be helpful to verify if the contribution patterns observed on Labyrinth (where pixel control was most impactful) also hold on Atari.

10. The paper mentions "supplementary materials" in the appendix but doesn't indicate where they are located. If these materials exist, they should be properly referenced.

The paper is well-written and the core idea is sound. The experimental results are convincing, and the approach has clear potential to advance the field of reinforcement learning. The suggested improvements would make an already strong paper even more comprehensive and impactful. I believe the authors have made a significant contribution to the field that deserves acceptance.
