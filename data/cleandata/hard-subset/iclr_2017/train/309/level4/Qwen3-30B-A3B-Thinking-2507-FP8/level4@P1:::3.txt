

This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that learns to control multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously (pixel changes, network features, and immediate reward prediction) to develop more effective representations for reinforcement learning. The authors argue that learning to control these features—rather than simply reconstructing input or predicting the environment—leads to more useful representations that rapidly adapt to actual tasks. This approach contrasts with traditional unsupervised learning methods, which often produce representations that don't directly support the agent's goals.

The paper makes a significant contribution with three key innovations. First, it proposes auxiliary tasks that directly leverage the agent's ability to control the environment (pixel control and feature control), rather than using them as standalone learning objectives. Second, it introduces a reward prediction task that uses skewed sampling to balance rewarding and non-rewarding experiences, without biasing the main policy. Third, it demonstrates substantial performance gains across multiple domains: UNREAL achieves 880% expert human performance on Atari (surpassing state-of-the-art) and 87% on Labyrinth, with a 10× speedup in learning.

I recommend acceptance for these key reasons:

1. The approach is well-motivated and well-placed in the literature. The authors clearly distinguish their work from related methods (Horde, UVFA, successor representations) by emphasizing how auxiliary tasks shape representation rather than serving as standalone learning objectives. The intuitive example of a baby learning to maximize "redness" compellingly demonstrates why controlling features may yield more useful representations than reconstruction.

2. The results are scientifically rigorous. The paper provides thorough experiments across 57 Atari games and 13 Labyrinth levels, with careful analysis of hyperparameter robustness. Ablation studies demonstrate the contribution of each auxiliary task, and the 10× learning speedup on Labyrinth (with 87% human performance) is particularly impressive given the complexity of these 3D environments.

The paper is generally well-written and accessible to readers familiar with deep reinforcement learning. One minor suggestion: the paper could define L_PC (the weighting for pixel control loss) earlier rather than deferring to the appendix.

For further improvement, I suggest:

- The paper shows pixel control is the most impactful auxiliary task, but doesn't deeply analyze why. A more detailed investigation into which specific features the agent learns to control and how these relate to task success would strengthen the contribution.
- The authors note that "learning to reconstruct only led to faster initial learning and actually made final scores worse." This important claim would benefit from more detailed analysis of the reconstruction baseline, including visualizations of the features learned.
- The paper mentions UNREAL's hyperparameter robustness but doesn't explore whether this is due to the auxiliary tasks themselves or the overall architecture. A more nuanced analysis would be valuable.

To clarify my understanding:

1. The paper states reward prediction uses skewed sampling to achieve P(rτ ≠ 0) = 0.5. How does this differ from standard class weighting, and why is this particular sampling approach more effective for representation learning?

2. The authors note feature control is slightly less effective than pixel control. What are the specific limitations of feature control, and how might they be overcome in future work?

3. The paper mentions UNREAL's hyperparameter robustness but doesn't analyze the underlying mechanism. Could this robustness be attributed to the auxiliary tasks themselves, or is it a side effect of the overall architecture?

Overall, this is a strong contribution that advances reinforcement learning by proposing a more effective way to develop representations that directly support the agent's learning objectives. The results are compelling, the methodology sound, and the approach well-motivated.
