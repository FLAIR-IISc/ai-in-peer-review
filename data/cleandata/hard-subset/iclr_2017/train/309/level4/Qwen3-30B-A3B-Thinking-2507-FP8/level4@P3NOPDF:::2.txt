

This paper proposes a novel approach to enhance feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by incorporating domain-independent auxiliary tasks (state control, reward prediction, value function replay) into the main policy's optimization. Crucially, the auxiliary tasks share the CNN+LSTM feature extraction backbone to shape representations, with the exception of value function replay. The method demonstrates significant improvements in data efficiency over A3C on Atari and Labyrinth domains, with results that are generally convincing and well-supported. The writing is clear and the core ideas are sound, warranting strong acceptance.

**Major Concerns for Clarification:**
1.  **Computational Overhead:** The paper should explicitly quantify the additional computational cost of training the auxiliary tasks. How much does training speed decrease? Which components (e.g., extra network heads, additional backward passes) contribute most significantly? This is critical for practical adoption.
2.  **Policy vs. Single Policy Misunderstanding:** The abstract and introduction state the agent "maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously." This risks misinterpretation as a single policy optimizing all rewards. Clarify early that *separate policies* are learned for each auxiliary task (as implied by Eq. 1), sharing only the feature extractor. The current phrasing suggests a single policy, which is incorrect.
3.  **Feature Control Validation:** The "feature control" auxiliary task (maximizing state change) lacks strong empirical validation (Fig. 5 shows only marginal initial gains). The dynamic nature of the task during learning, where the features themselves are being modified, raises stability/convergence concerns. A more robust analysis (e.g., ablation showing its contribution to final performance, not just early steps) is needed.
4.  **Hyperparameter Justification:** Why are the weights for auxiliary tasks (λₖ) fixed to 1? Were experiments conducted to validate this choice? Optimizing λₖ as hyperparameters (e.g., via grid search or meta-learning) could yield better performance and is a standard practice.

**Minor Issues & Typos:**
*   **Equation & Notation:** Ensure λₖ is inside the sum in Eq. 1. Correct "rt^(c)" to "rₜ₊ₖ^(c)" just below Eq. 1. Define L_PC explicitly if referenced in the text around Eq. 2.
*   **Figures & Text:** Reference Figure 2 in the main text. Correct Figure 1(d) citation in Section 3.3. Clarify that Figure 3 (top-left, not "top right") shows the performance curve mentioned in 4.1.2. Specify that the % in Figure 3 legend refers to Labyrinth (not Atari) and clarify the reference to "left and middle plots" in 4.1.2 (only two levels shown).
*   **Grammar & Clarity:** "the features discovered in this manner is shared" → "are shared". "Policy gradient algorithms adjust... L_π = -..." → "Policy gradient algorithms minimize the loss L_π = -..." (as it's a loss function). "The left side shows... the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows..." → Add comma: "...methods, the right half shows...".
*   **Technical Accuracy:** Clarify in the text (or footnote) that auxiliary tasks are *not* trained with "true" Q-learning due to off-policy training with multi-step returns (addressing OpenReview comments).
*   **Supplementary Materials:** The appendix's statement "Further details are included in the supplementary materials" is redundant; supplementary materials are standard and should be referenced by name (e.g., "See Appendix B for details").
*   **Hyperparameter Value:** Confirm the value of λ_PC (assumed to be 1) in the main text or appendix.

**Additional Suggestions:**
*   **Extended Training:** Given the observation that "performance is still steadily improving," briefly reporting final performance after extended training for the top agents would provide stronger evidence of the method's potential.
*   **Atari Ablation:** Including more ablation studies on Atari (beyond Labyrinth) would help validate whether the relative contributions of individual auxiliary tasks observed on Labyrinth generalize.

**Summary:** This is a strong paper with a compelling idea and convincing results. The proposed method offers a clear, practical path to improved data efficiency in RL. The identified concerns are primarily about clarity, completeness, and minor technical details, not fundamental flaws. Addressing the computational cost, clarifying the multi-policy aspect, strengthening the validation of feature control, and justifying the λₖ choice are the most critical points for revision. The minor grammatical and presentation issues are straightforward to fix. The paper makes a valuable contribution and is recommended for acceptance.
