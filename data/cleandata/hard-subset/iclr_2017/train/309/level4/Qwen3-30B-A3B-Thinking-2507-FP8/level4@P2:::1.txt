

**Review for ICLR 2017**

**Summary of Contributions**

This paper introduces UNREAL, a novel deep reinforcement learning agent that significantly enhances representation learning through the incorporation of auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks. The authors propose that by simultaneously learning policies to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions (including pixel changes and network features) while also predicting immediate rewards, agents can develop more effective representations for the underlying environment. The paper demonstrates that UNREAL substantially outperforms A3C on both the challenging 3D Labyrinth environment (achieving 87% human-normalized performance, compared to A3C's 54%) and 57 Atari games (averaging 880% of expert human performance, compared to previous state-of-the-art). Most notably, UNREAL achieves a 10× speedup in learning, requiring less than 10% of the data to reach A3C's final performance level.

**Decision**

Accept. The paper makes a strong contribution to the field by demonstrating that carefully designed auxiliary tasks can dramatically improve both the performance and data efficiency of deep reinforcement learning agents. The results are compelling, well-documented, and supported by thorough experimentation across multiple challenging environments.

**Supporting Arguments**

The paper excels in its clear motivation and well-structured approach. The authors effectively address a critical limitation in deep reinforcement learning: the sparsity of extrinsic rewards in many environments. By introducing auxiliary control tasks that encourage the agent to learn useful representations through prediction and control (rather than mere reconstruction), they provide a principled approach to representation learning that aligns with the agent's long-term goals.

The experimental results are particularly convincing. The Labyrinth results show not only superior final performance but also dramatically faster learning, with UNREAL reaching A3C's best performance in 10× fewer steps. The ablation studies effectively demonstrate the value of each auxiliary component, with the combination of all three tasks yielding the strongest results. The Atari results further validate the approach across a diverse set of environments, with UNREAL outperforming both A3C and Prioritized Dueling DQN.

The paper's theoretical foundation is sound, with careful consideration of how auxiliary tasks relate to the primary learning objective. The architecture design is thoughtful, with appropriate mechanisms for balancing the multiple learning objectives without introducing bias to the primary reward maximization.

**Additional Feedback**

While the paper is well-written and technically sound, the following points would strengthen it further:

1. The paper would benefit from a more detailed discussion of how the specific auxiliary tasks (pixel control, feature control, and reward prediction) contribute to the improved representation learning. A more explicit connection between these tasks and the final performance gains would help readers understand the underlying mechanisms.

2. The computational requirements for training UNREAL should be quantified. How much more compute time or resources does UNREAL require compared to A3C? This information would be valuable for practitioners considering implementing the approach.

3. The paper could provide more insight into why the proposed auxiliary tasks outperform simpler alternatives like pixel reconstruction or immediate reward prediction. The results in Section 4.1.1 are promising but could be better contextualized within the broader literature on unsupervised representation learning.

**Questions for Authors**

1. Could you provide more specific details about the computational resources required to train UNREAL compared to A3C? This would help readers assess the practical feasibility of the approach.

2. Will the authors release the source code for the UNREAL agent, including the implementation details for the auxiliary tasks? This would significantly benefit the research community and facilitate reproducibility.

3. The paper mentions that "feature control" is a promising future direction. Could you elaborate on why this specific auxiliary task might be particularly valuable, and what challenges might arise in implementing it more broadly?

The UNREAL architecture represents a meaningful advancement in deep reinforcement learning, with clear empirical benefits across multiple environments. The paper is well-structured, thoroughly evaluated, and makes a strong case for the value of incorporating auxiliary tasks that align with the agent's long-term goals. The results are robust, and the approach has clear potential for application in other domains. I believe this work merits acceptance at ICLR 2017.
