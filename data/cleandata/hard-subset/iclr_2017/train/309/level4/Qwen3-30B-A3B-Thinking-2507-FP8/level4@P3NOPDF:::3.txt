

This paper introduces a novel approach to representation learning in reinforcement learning (RL) by training agents to perform auxiliary control tasks alongside their primary RL objective. The authors propose three auxiliary tasks: (1) controlling the change in pixel intensity (L_PC), (2) controlling the activation of latent features, and (3) predicting immediate reward via supervised regression. The reward prediction task leverages a skewed sampling strategy on the experience replay buffer to balance the likelihood of observing reward events (1:1 ratio). Crucially, all auxiliary tasks are learned during RL training without requiring additional data or supervision beyond the agent’s interaction with the environment.

The empirical results demonstrate significant performance gains on discrete-action, continuous-state RL benchmarks (e.g., Atari, Labyrinth). Agents incorporating the auxiliary tasks achieve baseline performance in approximately 10× fewer training iterations than standard RL baselines. The most impactful auxiliary task is pixel control (L_PC), which alone outperforms other combinations in Labyrinth (except UNREAL). This efficiency gain is particularly compelling for sample-constrained settings.

The core contribution lies in the conceptual shift from *passive* unsupervised learning (e.g., autoencoders) or *model-based* approaches (which struggle with partial observability) to *active* feature control. By leveraging the agent’s inherent ability to influence the environment, the method ensures learned representations are directly relevant to task performance. The authors convincingly argue that controlling local and internal features complements policy learning, avoiding the pitfalls of learning "useless" representations or impossible world models. This perspective presents a compelling alternative to existing unsupervised RL frameworks.

The paper is generally well-structured and accessible to readers familiar with deep RL. However, two minor issues warrant attention. First, the key auxiliary task L_PC is introduced only in the appendix without explicit mention in the main text. Defining L_PC early (e.g., "We define pixel control as L_PC, which..." in Section 3) would improve clarity and align with the paper’s high-level intuition. Second, while the methodology is sound—e.g., the top-3 hyperparameter selection strategy robustly identifies near-optimal configurations for A3C and UNREAL—the paper lacks deeper analysis of *how* the auxiliary tasks contribute to performance. The strong empirical results are well-documented, but the paper does not dissect the individual effects of each auxiliary task (e.g., via ablation studies) or explore *why* pixel control (L_PC) proves so effective. For instance, does L_PC primarily enhance state representation, reduce exploration noise, or accelerate policy convergence? Addressing this would strengthen the contribution by moving beyond performance metrics to explain *mechanistic* benefits.

A related gap is the limited discussion of the reward prediction task’s role. While it is included as a baseline, the paper does not clarify whether it is *necessary* for the observed gains or merely supplementary. A brief analysis of its relative impact versus L_PC would help readers prioritize auxiliary task design in future work.

Overall, this work offers a fresh, conceptually grounded approach to representation learning in RL. The proposed auxiliary tasks are well-motivated, the implementation is clean, and the results are robust across multiple environments. The core idea—leveraging the agent’s control capability to shape representations—is novel and potentially influential. The minor structural and analytical gaps identified above do not detract from the paper’s technical merit but represent opportunities for deeper engagement in future work.

**Recommendation**: Accept. The contribution is significant, the methodology is rigorous, and the results are compelling. Addressing the minor points (L_PC definition, task ablation) would further enhance the paper’s impact, but these are not required for acceptance. The work provides a valuable new direction for representation learning in RL, moving beyond passive unsupervised methods toward active, policy-relevant feature engineering.
