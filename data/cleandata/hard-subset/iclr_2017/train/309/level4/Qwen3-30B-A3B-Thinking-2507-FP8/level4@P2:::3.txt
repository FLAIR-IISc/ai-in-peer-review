

**Summary**  
This paper introduces UNREAL, a reinforcement learning agent that simultaneously learns to maximize multiple pseudo-reward functions alongside the primary extrinsic reward. The authors propose two auxiliary control tasks—pixel control (maximizing changes in pixel intensity across an image grid) and feature control (maximizing activations of specific hidden units)—alongside a reward prediction task that forecasts immediate rewards through skewed experience replay. The core hypothesis is that learning to control these auxiliary objectives develops a more robust and transferable representation than traditional unsupervised learning, leading to faster learning and better performance on downstream tasks. The authors demonstrate significant improvements over A3C (the strongest baseline) on both 3D Labyrinth environments (reaching 87% human-normalized performance and 10× faster learning) and 57 Atari games (880% mean human-normalized performance).

**Decision**  
Accept. The paper makes a compelling contribution to representation learning in RL, with strong empirical results and a novel, well-motivated approach that addresses key limitations of unsupervised methods.

**Supporting Arguments**  
The paper excels in addressing three critical questions: (1) The specific problem tackled is clear—how to develop more effective representations for RL agents when extrinsic rewards are sparse or infrequent; (2) The approach is well-motivated, building on related work (e.g., UVFAs, successor representations) while clearly distinguishing its focus on *control* rather than *reconstruction*; and (3) The claims are strongly supported by rigorous experiments across diverse environments, with thorough ablation studies showing that each auxiliary task contributes to performance gains.

The most compelling evidence is the Labyrinth results: UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs. 54% for A3C) with a 10× speedup in learning. Crucially, the authors show that the pixel control task alone outperforms A3C + reward prediction, suggesting this is a key component of the method. The Atari results (880% mean performance) further validate the approach's generality. The method’s robustness to hyperparameter choices (Figure 3, top right) is another strong point, as it addresses a common pain point in RL.

**Additional Feedback**  
The paper is well-written and accessible to RL researchers. To further strengthen it, consider the following:

1. **Clarify the auxiliary task mechanics**: While the high-level intuition is excellent, the paper would benefit from a brief, concrete description of how the pixel control task is implemented (e.g., the exact reward function for pixel changes) before the appendix. This would help readers immediately grasp the technical novelty.

2. **Deepen the analysis of auxiliary task contributions**: The ablation studies are strong but could be extended. For instance, the paper shows that pixel control alone is highly effective (Figure 4), yet the authors don’t explore *why* this is the case. Does pixel control primarily improve exploration, or does it shape features that directly benefit the main task? A brief analysis (e.g., t-SNE visualizations of feature spaces) would add significant value.

3. **Address potential limitations**: The paper focuses on discrete-action environments. How would UNREAL perform in continuous control settings (e.g., MuJoCo)? A short discussion of this limitation would strengthen the scope of the work.

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The reward prediction task uses skewed replay to balance positive/negative rewards (P(r≠0)=0.5). Why not use a simpler method like uniform sampling with a larger replay buffer to achieve similar balance? How sensitive is the method to the exact sampling ratio?

2. In the Labyrinth experiments, the UNREAL agent achieves 100% performance on nav maze random goal 02. Given that this level requires the agent to encode the maze layout in its weights, does the auxiliary control task primarily aid in this encoding, or is it more about general feature learning?

3. The paper notes that feature control is slightly less effective than pixel control (Figure 5). Could the authors speculate on why this is the case? For example, do feature activations correspond to more task-irrelevant information than pixel changes?
