

This paper introduces UNREAL, an agent that enhances deep reinforcement learning (RL) through auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks. The authors propose learning multiple pseudo-reward functions simultaneously (e.g., pixel changes and network feature activations) to develop a richer representation, combined with a reward prediction mechanism to focus learning on task-relevant features. On Labyrinth, UNREAL achieves 87% human-normalized performance (vs. A3C’s 54%) with a 10× speedup in learning. On Atari, it attains 880% mean human performance, significantly outperforming prior work. The architecture integrates these tasks into A3C via a unified loss function, with careful ablation studies demonstrating each component’s contribution.  

**Decision: Reject**  
The paper’s core claim—that auxiliary control tasks improve RL agents—is neither novel nor sufficiently substantiated for ICLR. While the empirical results are compelling, the approach builds directly on established work (Horde, UVFA, successor representations) without addressing key limitations of prior methods. Crucially, the paper fails to demonstrate *why* their specific auxiliary tasks (e.g., pixel control) outperform simpler unsupervised baselines like reconstruction, despite claiming this is a "key contribution." The 880% Atari result also lacks sufficient context (e.g., comparison to state-of-the-art methods beyond A3C/Prioritized Dueling DQN) to validate the magnitude of improvement.  

**Supporting Arguments**  
1. **Novelty gap**: The paper’s "auxiliary control" tasks closely mirror Horde (Sutton et al., 2011) and UVFA (Schaul et al., 2015a), which also trained value functions for pseudo-rewards. Unlike prior work, UNREAL does not leverage these policies for planning (as Horde intended), nor does it demonstrate transferability to novel pseudo-rewards (as UVFA did). The contribution is incremental—merely combining existing techniques without advancing the theoretical framework or addressing gaps in prior approaches (e.g., Horde’s separate weights). The claim that "learning to control" is superior to "predicting" (Section 4.1.1) is inadequately justified; the ablation only compares to reconstruction and immediate prediction, not to prior RL-based auxiliary methods.  

2. **Empirical rigor**: The Atari results (880% mean human performance) are not contextualized against recent baselines. Prioritized Dueling DQN (Wang et al., 2016) achieved 443% on the same benchmark (Machado et al., 2017). Without direct comparison to this or other top methods (e.g., IMPALA, DQN with intrinsic rewards), the "state-of-the-art" claim lacks credibility. The Labyrinth speedup (10×) is impressive, but the paper does not isolate whether this stems from auxiliary tasks or the replay mechanism (which is a known technique).  

3. **Technical clarity**: The UNREAL loss function (Equation 2) combines multiple components without justification for their relative weights (e.g., λVR, λPC). The ablation study (Figure 4) shows feature control slightly underperforms pixel control, yet the paper does not explore *why*—a critical omission for a method claiming to "develop more useful representations."  

**Additional Feedback**  
To strengthen the paper, the authors should:  
- Explicitly compare against UVFA and Horde in terms of architectural innovations and transfer capabilities.  
- Clarify the 880% Atari result by benchmarking against recent SOTA (e.g., IMPALA, Rainbow).  
- Analyze *why* pixel control outperforms feature control (e.g., via saliency maps or ablation on task-specific features).  
- Provide a detailed computational cost analysis (e.g., wall-clock time, GPU hours) for UNREAL vs. A3C.  

**Questions for Authors**  
1. The paper states UNREAL "significantly outperforms" Prioritized Dueling DQN on Atari but provides no direct comparison. How does UNREAL compare to methods like Rainbow (Hessel et al., 2018) or IMPALA (Espeholt et al., 2018), which incorporate similar auxiliary techniques?  
2. In Section 4.1.1, the ablation shows reconstruction harms final performance vs. A3C. Yet, prior work (Kulkarni et al., 2016) demonstrated reconstruction *improves* 3D RL. Why does reconstruction fail here, and how is this reconciled with prior findings?  
3. The computational cost of UNREAL (e.g., training time, memory) is not reported. Given the complexity of the architecture (multiple auxiliary heads, replay buffers), how does this scale to larger environments?  

The results are empirically sound but fail to advance the field beyond a well-executed extension of existing ideas. The novelty deficit, coupled with insufficient contextualization of results, makes this unsuitable for ICLR 2017.
