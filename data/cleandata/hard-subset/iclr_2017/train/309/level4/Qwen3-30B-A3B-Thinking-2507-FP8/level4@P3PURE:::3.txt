

This work proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks, arguing that this approach fosters the learning of stronger, more useful features. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: (1) controlling the change in pixel intensity and (2) controlling the activation of latent features. Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task—predicting immediate reward following a sequence of events—which is learned offline via skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance reward observation probability at 50%. Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks achieve significantly improved performance on discrete-action, continuous-state RL tasks, reaching baseline performance in 10x fewer training iterations.  

The approach contrasts with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning strategies. Rather than compelling the model to learn potentially irrelevant input representations or an infeasible task-modeling objective (due to partial observability), learning to control local and internal environmental features complements the acquisition of the optimal control policy. The method is novel and presents a compelling alternative to unsupervised learning by leveraging the agent’s inherent capacity for environmental control.  

The proposed auxiliary tasks are well-motivated at a high level, facilitating intuitive understanding. However, introducing the specific implementation of L_PC earlier (e.g., before the appendix) would enhance accessibility. Beyond this, the paper is clear and accessible to readers familiar with deep RL.  

The methodology is sound. While hyperparameter distributions may differ between A3C and UNREAL, the evaluation of the top 3 hyperparameter configurations ensures that the best-performing hyperparameters for each method are identified within the explored search space.  

A critical limitation is the lack of deeper experimental analysis into the *individual effects* of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong performance impact. For instance, in Labyrinth, A3C+PC alone outperforms all other methods (except UNREAL), yet the paper does not isolate or analyze the contribution of pixel control (PC) or feature control. A more thorough investigation—measuring beyond final RL performance (e.g., feature quality, learning dynamics, or ablation studies)—would substantially strengthen the claims. Given space constraints, this represents crucial future work rather than a flaw in the current contribution.
