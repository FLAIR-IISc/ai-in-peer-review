This paper discusses improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning, specifically focusing on optimizing the core policy with (domain-dependent) auxiliary tasks. These tasks relate to control (learning other policies that try to maximize the state space, such as pixels here), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. Except for the last one, these auxiliary tasks are only used to shape the features (by sharing CNN+LSTM feature extraction networks). Experiments show the benefits of this approach in Atari and Labyrinth problems, especially in data efficiency much higher than A3C.

The paper is well-written, the concepts are accurate, and the results are quite convincing, so I consider it a clear recognition. I have a few things to say at a high level, none of which are major concerns:

- I think you should say something about the additional computational cost of optimizing these auxiliary tasks. How much do you lose in terms of training speed? What are the most expensive components?
- If possible, please be clearer in the abstract/introduction section, stating that the agent is learning different policies for each task. When I read in the abstract that the agent "simultaneously maximizes many other pseudo-reward functions through reinforcement learning, such as pixels here," my first impression was that it was learning a single policy to optimize all the rewards together, and I realized my mistake only when I reached equation 1.
- The concept of "feature control" has not been empirically verified (the initial test in Figure 5 is not very convincing because it initially seems to help little). I like the idea but I am concerned that the task is changing during learning, as the extracted features are changing. There may be stability/convergence issues here.
- As you mentioned, "the performance of our agents is still continuously improving," so why aren't they being run to see how far they go? (at least the best agents)
- Why aren't the weight parameters (λ*) of the auxiliary tasks hyperparameters to optimize? Was there any testing to verify whether using 1 is a good choice?
- Please note that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with "real" Q-learning, as they are off-policy trained with multiple steps of empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview comment).
