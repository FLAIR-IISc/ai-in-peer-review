This paper proposes a novel approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy's optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks include control, immediate reward prediction, and value function replay, which are used to shape the features by sharing the CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth problems, with significant improvements in data efficiency compared to A3C.

The paper is well-written, and the ideas are sound. The results are convincing, making this a clear acceptance. However, I have a few minor suggestions for improvement:

1. The abstract and introduction could be clarified to indicate that the agent learns different policies for each task. Initially, it seems that the agent learns a single policy to optimize all rewards together.

2. The extra computational cost of optimizing these auxiliary tasks should be discussed. What is the impact on training speed, and which components are the most costly?

3. The "feature control" idea is not thoroughly validated empirically. While the preliminary experiment in Fig. 5 shows some initial improvement, it is not convincing. The task's changing nature during learning might lead to stability or convergence issues.

4. Since the performance of the agents is still improving, it would be interesting to continue training them to see how far they can go, at least for the best ones.

5. The auxiliary tasks' weight parameters (the lambda_*) could be considered hyperparameters to optimize. Were there any experiments to validate the choice of using 1?

6. It would be helpful to mention that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-Learning since they are trained off-policy with more than one step of empirical rewards.

Minor points:

- "Policy gradient algorithms adjust the policy to maximise the expected reward, L_pi = -..." should be rephrased as a loss to be minimized.

- In eq. 1, lambda_c should be within the sum.

- Just below eq. 1, rt^(c) should be rt+k^(c).

- Figure 2 is not referenced in the text, and Figure 1(d) should be referenced in 3.3.

- "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be rephrased as "are shared".

- The text around eq. 2 refers to the loss L_PC, but that term is not defined and is not explicitly in eq. 2.

- The legend of Figure 3 could include an explanation of what "Clip" means for dueling networks.

- More ablated versions on Atari would be helpful to see if the same patterns of individual contribution as on Labyrinth are observed.

- The legend of Figure 3 could clarify that the percentages mentioned are for Labyrinth.

- In 4.1.2, "Figure 3 (right) shows..." should be rephrased as "Figure 3 (top left) shows...".

- "Figure 5 shows the learning curves for the top 5 hyperparameter settings on three Labyrinth navigation levels" could be rephrased to indicate that it refers to the left and middle plots of the figure, showing only two levels.

- In 4.2, "The left side shows the average performance curves of the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows..." is missing a comma or something after "methods".

- The Appendix could include the supplementary materials or provide a clear indication of where they can be found.

- The value of lambda_PC could be specified (it is assumed to be 1).

Decision: Accept

Reasons for decision: The paper presents a novel approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning, with convincing results on Atari and Labyrinth problems. The ideas are sound, and the paper is well-written.

Supporting arguments:

- The approach is well-motivated, and the use of auxiliary tasks is a natural extension of existing methods.

- The results demonstrate significant improvements in data efficiency compared to A3C.

- The paper provides a clear and detailed explanation of the methods and experiments.

Additional feedback:

- Consider adding more ablated versions on Atari to further demonstrate the effectiveness of the approach.

- Provide more details on the computational cost of optimizing the auxiliary tasks.

- Clarify the abstract and introduction to indicate that the agent learns different policies for each task.

- Consider continuing to train the agents to see how far they can go, at least for the best ones.

- Provide more details on the choice of lambda_*) and whether there were any experiments to validate this choice.

Questions for the authors:

- Can you provide more details on the computational cost of optimizing the auxiliary tasks?

- How did you choose the value of lambda_*)?

- Were there any experiments to validate the choice of using 1 for lambda_*)?

- Can you provide more details on the "feature control" idea and its potential stability or convergence issues?
