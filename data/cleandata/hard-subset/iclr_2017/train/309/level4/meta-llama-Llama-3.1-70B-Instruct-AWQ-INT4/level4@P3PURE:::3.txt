This work proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks, positing that this approach will help models learn stronger features. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: controlling the change in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task to predict immediate reward following a sequence of events, learned offline through a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance the probability of seeing a reward or not to 1/2.

The proposed agents achieve significant performance on discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, reaching baseline performance in 10 times fewer iterations. This work contrasts with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning methods. Instead of forcing the model to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or an impossible task-modelling objective due to partial observability, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements learning the optimal control policy.

To me, the approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning that leverages the "possibility" of control that an agent has over the environment.

The proposed tasks are explained at a high level, making them convenient to understand intuitively. However, some lower-level details might be useful. For example, L_PC should be explicitly mentioned before reaching the appendix. Otherwise, this work is clear and easily understandable for readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology is sound. While the distribution of best hyperparameters might differ between A3C and UNREAL, measuring top-3 ensures that, assuming both best hyperparameters for A3C and UNREAL are within the explored intervals, the per-method best hyperparameters are found.

I think one weakness of the paper (or rather, a crucial area for future work, considering the limited space available) is that there is little experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks beyond their strong impact on performance. In the same vein, pixel/feature control seems to have the most impact, as evidenced by A3C+PC outperforming other methods (except UNREAL) in Labyrinth. It would be worth investigating this further, either in isolation or in more depth, by measuring more than just performance on RL tasks.
