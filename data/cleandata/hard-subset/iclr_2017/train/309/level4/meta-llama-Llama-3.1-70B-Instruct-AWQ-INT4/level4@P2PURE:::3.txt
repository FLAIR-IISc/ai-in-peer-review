This work proposes training reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks in addition to their primary objectives, hypothesizing that this will lead to the development of stronger features. The authors introduce two pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. They also propose a supervised regression task, where the agent predicts the immediate reward following a sequence of events. The latter task is learned offline through a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer, ensuring a balanced probability of seeing a reward or not.

Agents trained with these auxiliary tasks demonstrate significant performance improvements on discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in approximately 10 times fewer iterations. This approach contrasts with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning methods, which may require the model to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or an impossible task-modelling objective due to partial observability. In contrast, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements learning the optimal control policy.

The proposed approach is novel and offers an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning, leveraging the agent's ability to control the environment. The tasks are explained at a high level, making it easy to understand the intuition behind them. However, providing more details at a lower level would be beneficial. For instance, L_PC could be explicitly mentioned before the appendix. Overall, the work is clear and easily understandable for readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology is sound, and the authors' approach to measuring top-3 performance ensures that the best hyperparameters for both A3C and UNREAL are found, assuming they lie within the explored intervals. However, it is worth noting that the distribution of best hyperparameters might differ between A3C and UNREAL.

A potential weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks beyond their impact on performance. It would be beneficial to investigate this aspect further, particularly since pixel/feature control seems to have the most significant impact. For example, in the Labyrinth environment, A3C+PC outperforms other methods (except UNREAL). A more in-depth analysis of this aspect, measuring metrics beyond just performance on RL tasks, would be valuable.
