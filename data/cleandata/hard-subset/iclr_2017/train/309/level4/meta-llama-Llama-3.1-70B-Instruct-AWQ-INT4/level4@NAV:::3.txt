This paper introduces a novel approach to training reinforcement learning (RL) agents by incorporating auxiliary tasks, with the hypothesis that this will lead to the development of more robust features. The authors propose three auxiliary tasks: two pseudo-control tasks, which involve controlling changes in pixel intensity and latent feature activation, as well as a supervised regression task, which predicts immediate rewards following a sequence of events. The latter task is learned offline using a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance the probability of observing a reward or not.

The results demonstrate that agents trained with these auxiliary tasks perform significantly better on discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in substantially fewer iterations. This work diverges from traditional unsupervised or model-based learning methods, which often require the model to learn a potentially unnecessary representation of the input or an unachievable task-modelling objective. Instead, the proposed approach focuses on learning to control local and internal features of the environment, complementing the learning of the optimal control policy.

The novelty of this approach lies in its utilization of the agent's control over the environment to enhance learning. The proposed tasks are explained at a high level, making it easy to grasp the underlying intuition. However, additional details, such as explicitly mentioning L_PC before the appendix, would be beneficial for readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology employed in this study is sound, with the distribution of best hyperparameters potentially differing between A3C and UNREAL. The measurement of top-3 performance ensures that the best hyperparameters for each method are identified, assuming they fall within the explored intervals.

One limitation of this paper is the lack of in-depth experimental analysis of the impact of the auxiliary tasks on performance, beyond their significant effect on RL tasks. Notably, the pixel/feature control task appears to have the most substantial impact, with A3C+PC outperforming other methods in the Labyrinth environment. Further investigation into this aspect, either in isolation or in greater depth, would be valuable in understanding the effects of these auxiliary tasks on RL performance.
