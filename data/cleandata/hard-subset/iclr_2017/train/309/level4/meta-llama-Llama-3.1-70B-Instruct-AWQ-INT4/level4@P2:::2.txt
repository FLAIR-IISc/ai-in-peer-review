This paper proposes a novel approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy's optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks include control, immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. The paper is well-written, and the ideas are sound. The results are convincing, showing significant benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth problems, particularly in terms of data efficiency compared to A3C.

The authors' decision to use auxiliary tasks to shape the features by sharing the CNN+LSTM feature extraction network is a good one. The experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach, and the results are impressive. However, there are a few points that could be improved.

Firstly, it would be beneficial to discuss the extra computational cost of optimizing these auxiliary tasks. How much do they lose in terms of training speed? Which are the most costly components? This information would be helpful in understanding the trade-offs involved in using this approach.

Secondly, the abstract and introduction could be clarified to explicitly state that the agent is learning different policies for each task. This would avoid potential confusion, as the current wording may suggest that the agent learns a single policy to optimize all rewards together.

Thirdly, the "feature control" idea, although intriguing, is not validated empirically. The preliminary experiment in Fig. 5 is not convincing, as it only seems to help slightly initially. It would be beneficial to further investigate this idea, considering the potential stability and convergence issues that may arise due to the changing features during learning.

Fourthly, since the performance of the agents is still improving, it would be interesting to see how far they can go. Continuing the training process for the best agents could provide valuable insights into their long-term performance.

Lastly, the auxiliary tasks' weight parameters (the lambda_*) could be hyperparameters to optimize. Were there any experiments to validate that using 1 was a good choice?

In terms of minor issues, there are a few points to address:

* "Policy gradient algorithms adjust the policy to maximise the expected reward, L_pi = -..." should be rephrased to indicate that it's a loss to be minimized.
* In eq. 1, lambda_c should be within the sum.
* Just below eq. 1, rt^(c) should be rt+k^(c).
* Figure 2 is not referenced in the text, and Figure 1(d) should be referenced in 3.3.
* "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be rephrased to "are shared".
* The text around eq. 2 refers to the loss L_PC, but that term is not defined and is not explicitly in eq. 2.
* The legend of Figure 3 should explain what "Clip" means for dueling networks.
* It would be beneficial to see more ablated versions on Atari to understand the individual contribution of each auxiliary task.
* The legend of Figure 3 should clarify that the percentages mentioned are for Labyrinth.
* In 4.1.2, "Figure 3 (right) shows..." should be rephrased to "Figure 3 (top left) shows...".
* "Figure 5 shows the learning curves for the top 5 hyperparameter settings on three Labyrinth navigation levels" should be rephrased to clarify that it refers to the left and middle plots of the figure, showing results for two levels.
* In 4.2, "The left side shows the average performance curves of the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows..." should be rephrased to include a comma or other punctuation after "methods".
* The Appendix should include further details, but it's unclear where these supplementary materials are located.
* The value of lambda_PC should be specified (it's likely 1, but it's not explicitly stated).

Overall, this paper presents a promising approach to improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning. With some minor revisions and additional experiments, it has the potential to make a significant impact in the field.
