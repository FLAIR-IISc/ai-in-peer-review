This work proposes to train reinforcement learning (RL) agents to perform auxiliary tasks in addition to the primary RL objective, hypothesizing that this will lead to the development of stronger features. The authors introduce two types of pseudo-control tasks: controlling changes in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. They also propose a supervised regression task, predicting immediate reward following a sequence of events, which is learned offline via a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance the occurrence of rewarding and non-rewarding events.

The authors' approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to traditional unsupervised learning methods. By learning to control local and internal features of the environment, the agent can complement its learning of the optimal control policy. This approach is particularly useful in partially observed environments, where traditional model-based learning methods may struggle.

The proposed tasks are explained at a high level, making it easy to understand the intuition behind the approach. However, some additional details, such as the definition of L_PC, would be helpful to include in the main text, rather than relegating them to the appendix. Overall, the work is clear and easily understandable for readers familiar with deep RL.

The methodology is sound, and the authors have taken steps to ensure that the comparison between their approach, UNREAL, and the baseline, A3C, is fair. By evaluating the top-3 performing hyperparameters for each method, they account for the possibility that the optimal hyperparameters may differ between the two approaches.

One potential weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks beyond their impact on performance. For example, it would be interesting to investigate why pixel/feature control seems to have the most significant impact, particularly in the Labyrinth environment. A more in-depth analysis of the role of each auxiliary task, either in isolation or in combination, would provide valuable insights into the strengths and limitations of the UNREAL approach.

Overall, I believe that this work has the potential to make a significant contribution to the field of deep RL, and I am inclined to accept the paper. However, I would like to see the authors address the above points and provide additional experimental analysis to support their claims.

Additional feedback:

* It would be helpful to include more details about the architecture and training procedure for the auxiliary tasks, particularly the supervised regression task.
* The authors may want to consider including more visualizations or examples to illustrate the types of features learned by the UNREAL agent.
* A more detailed comparison with other state-of-the-art methods, such as Prioritized Dueling DQN, would be helpful to establish the relative performance of UNREAL.
* The authors may want to consider exploring the application of UNREAL to other domains, such as continuous control tasks or multi-agent environments.

Questions for the authors:

* Can you provide more details about the choice of hyperparameters for the auxiliary tasks, and how they were tuned?
* How do the authors plan to extend the UNREAL approach to more complex environments or tasks?
* Can you provide more insights into the types of features learned by the UNREAL agent, and how they differ from those learned by the A3C baseline?
