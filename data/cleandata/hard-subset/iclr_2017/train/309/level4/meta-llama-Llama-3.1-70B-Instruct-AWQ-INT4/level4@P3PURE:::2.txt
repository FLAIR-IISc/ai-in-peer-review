This paper proposes an approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy's optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks focus on control, immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. Except for the latter, these auxiliary tasks are used to shape the features by sharing the CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. The experiments demonstrate the benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth problems, showing significantly better data efficiency compared to A3C.

The paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are convincing, making this a clear acceptance. At a high level, I have a few minor comments:

- The authors should discuss the extra computational cost of optimizing these auxiliary tasks. Specifically, how much is lost in terms of training speed, and which components are the most costly?
- The abstract and introduction could be clarified to indicate that the agent learns different policies for each task. Initially, the phrase "also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning" in the abstract suggested that a single policy was learned to optimize all rewards together. This misunderstanding was corrected upon reaching equation 1.
- The "feature control" idea is not empirically validated, as the preliminary experiment in Figure 5 is not convincing, showing only slight initial improvements. Although the idea is intriguing, the changing task during learning, due to the modification of extracted features, raises concerns about stability and convergence issues.
- Given that the performance of the agents is still improving, it would be beneficial to continue training to see how far they can go, at least for the best-performing agents.
- The auxiliary tasks' weight parameters (the lambda_*) could be considered hyperparameters to optimize. Were there any experiments to validate the choice of using 1?
- The authors should mention that auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-Learning, as they are trained off-policy with more than one step of empirical rewards, as discussed in the OpenReview comments.

Minor issues:

- The phrase "Policy gradient algorithms adjust the policy to maximise the expected reward, L_pi = -..." should be corrected to indicate that this is a loss to be minimized.
- In equation 1, lambda_c should be included within the sum.
- Just below equation 1, rt^(c) should be rt+k^(c).
- Figure 2 is not referenced in the text, and Figure 1(d) should be referenced in section 3.3.
- The sentence "the features discovered in this manner is shared" should be corrected to "are shared."
- The text around equation 2 refers to the loss L_PC, but this term is not defined and is not explicitly included in equation 2.
- The legend of Figure 3 should explain what "Clip" means for dueling networks.
- Additional ablated versions on Atari would be beneficial to see if the same patterns of individual contribution as on Labyrinth are observed.
- The legend of Figure 3 should clarify that the percentages mentioned are for Labyrinth.
- In section 4.1.2, "Figure 3 (right) shows..." should be corrected to "the top left plot of the figure." Later, "This is shown in Figure 3 Top" should be "Figure 3 Top Right."
- The sentence "Figure 5 shows the learning curves for the top 5 hyperparameter settings on three Labyrinth navigation levels" likely refers to the left and middle plots of the figure, showing results on two levels. The preceding text may also need correction.
- In section 4.2, a comma is missing after "methods" in the sentence "The left side shows the average performance curves of the top 5 agents for all three methods the right half shows..."
- The appendix mentions "Further details are included in the supplementary materials," but the location of these materials is not specified.
- The value of lambda_PC is not provided; it is assumed to be 1.
