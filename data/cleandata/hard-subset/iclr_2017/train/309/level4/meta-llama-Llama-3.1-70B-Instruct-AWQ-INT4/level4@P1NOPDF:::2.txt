This paper proposes an approach to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning by augmenting the main policy's optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks. These tasks include control (learning other policies that attempt to maximally modify the state space), immediate reward prediction, and value function replay. Except for the latter, these auxiliary tasks are only used to help shape the features by sharing the CNN+LSTM feature extraction network. Experiments demonstrate the benefits of this approach on Atari and Labyrinth problems, with significantly improved data efficiency compared to A3C.

The paper is well-written, the ideas are sound, and the results are convincing, making this a clear acceptance. At a high level, I have a few minor comments:

- I believe it would be beneficial to discuss the extra computational cost of optimizing these auxiliary tasks. What is the impact on training speed, and which components are the most costly?
- To improve clarity, it would be helpful to explicitly state in the abstract and introduction that the agent is learning different policies for each task. Initially, the phrase "also maximises many other pseudo-reward functions simultaneously by reinforcement learning" in the abstract suggested that the agent learned a single policy to optimize all rewards together. This misunderstanding was only clarified upon reaching Equation 1.
- The "feature control" idea is not empirically validated (the preliminary experiment in Figure 5 is not convincing, as it only seems to provide a slight initial benefit). While I find this idea intriguing, I am concerned that the task is changing during learning, as the extracted features are being modified. This may lead to stability or convergence issues.
- Given that the performance of the agents is still steadily improving, it would be interesting to continue training them to see how far they can go (at least for the best-performing ones).
- It is unclear why the auxiliary tasks' weight parameters (the lambda_*) are not hyperparameters to be optimized. Were there any experiments to validate the choice of using 1?
- Please note that auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-Learning, as they are trained off-policy with more than one step of empirical rewards (as discussed in the OpenReview comments).
