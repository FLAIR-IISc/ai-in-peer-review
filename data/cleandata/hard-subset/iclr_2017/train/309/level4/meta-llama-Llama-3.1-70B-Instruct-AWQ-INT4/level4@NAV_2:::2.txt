**Summary of the Paper's Claims and Contributions**

This paper proposes an extension to deep reinforcement learning by introducing auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve feature learning. The authors augment the main policy's optimization problem with terms corresponding to domain-independent auxiliary tasks, which are only used to help shape the features. The paper presents a new architecture, UNREAL, which combines the benefits of two state-of-the-art approaches to deep reinforcement learning. The authors demonstrate the effectiveness of their approach on Atari and Labyrinth problems, achieving state-of-the-art results and improving data efficiency.

**Decision and Key Reasons**

I recommend accepting this paper. The key reasons for my decision are:

1. The paper presents a well-motivated and well-placed approach in the literature, addressing the problem of improving feature learning in deep reinforcement learning.
2. The authors provide convincing results on Atari and Labyrinth problems, demonstrating the effectiveness of their approach in achieving state-of-the-art results and improving data efficiency.

**Supporting Arguments for the Decision**

The paper is well-written, and the ideas are sound. The authors provide a clear and concise explanation of their approach, and the results are convincing. The use of auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks is a novel and effective way to improve feature learning in deep reinforcement learning. The UNREAL architecture is a significant contribution to the field, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in achieving state-of-the-art results.

**Additional Feedback for Improvement**

To further improve the paper, I suggest the following:

1. Provide more details on the extra computational cost of optimizing the auxiliary tasks and how it affects training speed.
2. Clarify in the abstract and introduction that the agent is learning different policies for each task.
3. Validate the "feature control" idea empirically, as the preliminary experiment in Figure 5 is not convincing.
4. Consider keeping the agents training to see how far they can go, at least the best ones.
5. Explain why the auxiliary tasks' weight parameters (the lambda_*) are not hyperparameters to optimize.
6. Mention that the auxiliary tasks are not trained with "true" Q-Learning since they are trained off-policy with more than one step of empirical rewards.
