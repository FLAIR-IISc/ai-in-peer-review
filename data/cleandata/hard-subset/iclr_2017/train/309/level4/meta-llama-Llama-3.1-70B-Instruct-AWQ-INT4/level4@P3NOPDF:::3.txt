This paper proposes a novel approach to training reinforcement learning (RL) agents by having them perform auxiliary tasks in addition to the primary control task. The authors posit that this multi-task learning approach will help models learn stronger features, leading to improved performance in RL tasks. They introduce three auxiliary tasks: two pseudo-control tasks, which involve controlling the change in pixel intensity and the activation of latent features, and a supervised regression task, which predicts the immediate reward following a sequence of events. The latter task is learned offline using a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance the probability of seeing a reward or not.

The proposed approach yields significant improvements in discrete-action-continuous-space RL tasks, achieving baseline performance in 10 times fewer iterations. This work contrasts with traditional "passive" unsupervised or model-based learning methods, which often require the model to learn a potentially useless representation of the input or an impossible task-modelling objective due to partial observability. In contrast, learning to control local and internal features of the environment complements learning the optimal control policy.

The authors' approach is novel and presents an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning, leveraging the agent's control over the environment. The proposed tasks are explained at a high level, making it easy to understand the intuition behind them. However, providing more details at a lower level, such as explicitly mentioning L_PC before the appendix, would be beneficial for readers. Overall, the paper is clear and easily understandable for those familiar with deep RL.

The methodology is sound, and the authors take a reasonable approach to hyperparameter tuning by considering the top-3 performing hyperparameters. This ensures that the best hyperparameters for both A3C and UNREAL are likely to be found, assuming they are within the explored intervals. However, it is possible that the distribution of best hyperparameters might differ between A3C and UNREAL.

One weakness of the paper is the limited experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks beyond their impact on performance. It would be valuable to investigate this aspect further, examining the contribution of each task to the overall performance. Notably, pixel/feature control seems to have the most significant impact, with A3C+PC outperforming other methods in the Labyrinth environment. A more in-depth analysis of this task, either in isolation or in combination with other tasks, would be beneficial, and additional metrics beyond just performance on RL tasks could provide more insights.

Overall, the paper presents a promising approach to improving RL performance, and the authors demonstrate its effectiveness in several environments. With some additional analysis and experimentation, this work has the potential to make a significant impact in the field of deep RL.
