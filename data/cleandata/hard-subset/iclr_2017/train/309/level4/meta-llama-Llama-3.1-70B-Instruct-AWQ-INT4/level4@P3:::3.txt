This paper proposes a novel approach to deep reinforcement learning (RL) by introducing auxiliary control and reward prediction tasks to improve the learning efficiency and robustness of RL agents. The authors argue that traditional unsupervised learning methods may not be effective in learning useful representations, especially in environments with sparse rewards. Instead, they suggest that learning to control local and internal features of the environment can complement learning the optimal control policy.

The paper introduces two pseudo-control tasks: controlling the change in pixel intensity and controlling the activation of latent features. Additionally, they propose a supervised regression task to predict immediate reward following a sequence of events. The latter is learned offline via a skewed sampling of an experience replay buffer to balance seeing reward or not to 1/2 chance.

The experimental results show that the proposed approach, called UNREAL, significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art RL methods on both Labyrinth and Atari games. Specifically, UNREAL achieves more than twice the final human-normalized mean performance of A3C on Labyrinth and surpasses the current state-of-the-art agents on Atari games.

The approach is novel and proposes an interesting alternative to unsupervised learning that takes advantage of the "possibility" of control that an agent has over the environment. The paper is well-written and easy to follow, even for readers not familiar with deep RL.

However, I would like to see more experimental analysis of the effect of the auxiliary tasks apart from their impact on performance. For example, it would be interesting to investigate how the auxiliary tasks affect the learning dynamics, such as the convergence rate and the stability of the learning process. Additionally, the paper could benefit from a more detailed analysis of the importance of each auxiliary task and how they interact with each other.

I would like to ask the authors to provide more details on the following:

1. How did you choose the specific auxiliary tasks, and are there any other tasks that could be used?
2. Can you provide more insights on how the auxiliary tasks affect the learning dynamics and the stability of the learning process?
3. How do the auxiliary tasks interact with each other, and are there any synergies or conflicts between them?
4. Have you considered using other types of auxiliary tasks, such as model-based learning or imitation learning?

Overall, I think this paper makes a significant contribution to the field of deep RL and has the potential to inspire new research directions. I would like to see more experimental analysis and insights into the workings of the proposed approach.
